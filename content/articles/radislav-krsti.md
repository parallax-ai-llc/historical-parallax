## Summary

Bosnian Serb general and first person convicted of genocide by the ICTY for his role in the Srebrenica massacre. Attacked and partially blinded in a UK prison.

## ICTY Case (High Level)

- Krstić was prosecuted at the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY) for crimes connected to the fall of Srebrenica in July 1995.
- The tribunal’s findings linked senior command responsibility to mass killings and the forcible removal of Bosniak civilians.
- His conviction was historically significant because it established an early genocide conviction at the ICTY, shaping later jurisprudence.

## Command Responsibility and Accountability

- International criminal law cases often examine what commanders knew (or should have known) and what steps they took to prevent or punish crimes.
- Srebrenica prosecutions became a central reference for how tribunals assess intent, participation, and the chain of command.

## Imprisonment and Attack

- After conviction, Krstić served his sentence in a UK prison.
- He was attacked by other prisoners and was reported to have been seriously injured, contributing to discussion about housing high-profile war criminals in general prison populations.

## Broader Significance

- The case remains part of the legal and historical record documenting mass atrocity in Bosnia and the international response.
- It is frequently cited in debates about the effectiveness and limits of international tribunals in delivering justice and deterrence.
- Related Srebrenica cases built a wider factual record through overlapping testimony, documents, and command-structure analysis.
