## Definition and scope

The Holocaust refers to the state-sponsored persecution and mass murder of Jews by Nazi Germany and its collaborators during World War II. Historians also document the systematic targeting of other groups, including the Roma, people with disabilities, Soviet prisoners of war, and others.

## Methods and institutions

The genocide was carried out through a combination of policies and institutions: ghettos, mass shootings by mobile killing units, forced labor, starvation, and extermination camps designed for industrial-scale killing.

## Geography

Although directed from Nazi Germany, the Holocaust unfolded across much of occupied Europe. The scale and timing varied by country depending on occupation, local collaboration, resistance, and wartime developments.

## Aftermath

The end of the war brought liberation of camps, displaced-person crises, and major war-crimes trials, including proceedings at Nuremberg. The Holocaust also shaped postwar international law, including the development of the concept of genocide and later human-rights frameworks.

## Memory and education

Public memorialization includes museums, archives, survivor testimony projects, and national remembrance days. Educational efforts emphasize evidence-based history and the dangers of antisemitism and other forms of hate.
