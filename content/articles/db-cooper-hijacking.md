## Summary

Hijacking of a Northwest Orient Airlines flight on November 24, 1971. A man calling himself D.B. Cooper extorted $200,000 in ransom then parachuted from the plane, disappearing forever.

## Incident Overview

The DB Cooper hijacking is one of the most famous unsolved crimes in U.S. aviation history. On **November 24, 1971**, a hijacker using the name “Dan Cooper” (later popularized as “D.B. Cooper”) boarded a commercial flight and claimed to have a bomb.

Key elements of the event commonly summarized:

- A demand for **$200,000** in cash and **parachutes**.
- A controlled exchange of hostages for the ransom after the plane landed.
- A later jump from the aircraft into bad weather, after which the hijacker was never conclusively identified.

## The Flight and Ransom Demand

Open-source retellings emphasize that the hijacker’s plan relied on the aircraft’s ability to fly slowly with its rear stairs deployed. The incident also highlighted security vulnerabilities of the era, when airline screening was far less stringent than in later decades.

## Investigation

The U.S. federal investigation became extensive and long-running. While many details remain uncertain, the case is often discussed in terms of:

- Large-scale interviews and suspect evaluations.
- Analysis of parachute equipment and possible jump conditions.
- The challenge of reconstructing a likely landing zone from flight-path and wind estimates.

In 2016, the FBI publicly stated it would **suspend active investigation** in order to prioritize other cases.

## Physical Evidence and Later Discoveries

A frequently cited piece of evidence is that **some of the ransom money** was later found along the Columbia River in Washington state. The discovery kept public interest alive but did not, by itself, resolve the identity or fate of the hijacker.

## Suspects and Public Theories

Over time, numerous individuals have been proposed as possible suspects. Most claims are speculative, and no theory has achieved broad consensus supported by definitive forensic proof.

## Cultural Legacy

- The story became a durable part of American true-crime folklore.
- “DB Cooper” is often referenced in films, television, books, and internet culture as a symbol of the “perfect” unsolved caper.
- The case has also been used as an example in discussions of how aviation security standards evolved.
