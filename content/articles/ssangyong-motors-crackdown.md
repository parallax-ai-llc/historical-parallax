## Summary

Ssangyong Motors laid off 2,646 workers who occupied the factory for 77 days. Police used helicopters to drop tear gas; workers were beaten and many later committed suicide.

## Background

SsangYong Motor, an auto manufacturer based in Pyeongtaek, faced a severe financial crisis in the late 2000s. In 2009 the company announced large-scale layoffs, which the union and many workers challenged as excessive and unjustified.[^1]

The dispute escalated into a prolonged occupation of the plant. The occupation became one of the most visible labor conflicts in South Korea during that period, drawing national attention to questions about restructuring, job security, and police tactics in industrial disputes.[^1]

## Key Facts (High-Level)

- **Duration**: The factory occupation lasted roughly **77 days**.[^1]
- **Layoffs**: The company’s layoff plan involved **thousands of workers** (commonly cited as 2,646).[^1]
- **Policing**: Riot police conducted an assault that included the use of **tear gas** and tactics that were widely reported as unusually forceful, including deployment from helicopters.[^1]

## Aftermath and Human Impact

Following the crackdown and the end of the occupation, the conflict continued through legal proceedings, negotiations, and public debate. Reports of injuries during the confrontation and long-term psychological and economic hardship among affected workers became a central part of how the episode was remembered.[^1]

The strike is also associated in reporting with subsequent deaths among some of those connected to the dispute, which labor advocates highlighted as evidence of lasting trauma and financial distress.[^1]

## Significance

The SsangYong case is often referenced in discussions of:

- South Korea’s approach to labor disputes during corporate restructuring
- The boundaries of police intervention in collective labor action
- The social costs of mass layoffs in a highly visible, symbolically charged industry[^1]
