## Summary

The Palitana temples are a complex of over 900 Jain temples located on Shatrunjaya Hill near the town of Palitana in the Bhavnagar district of Gujarat, India. Considered the holiest pilgrimage site in the Shvetambara sect of Jainism, Shatrunjaya Hill is believed to be a tirtha — a sacred "crossing place" — where spiritual liberation is most readily attainable. The site is so sacred that Jain tradition holds that no fewer than 23 of the 24 Tirthankaras (the omniscient spiritual teachers of Jainism) have meditated on or are in some way associated with the hill.

Climbing Shatrunjaya Hill to reach the temples is itself a pilgrimage act, involving an ascent of approximately 3,800 steps cut into the rock face of the hill, rising to a summit at approximately 600 meters. The temples at the summit, built and maintained over a millennium by Jain merchants and communities, represent one of the greatest concentrations of religious architecture anywhere in the world. The view from the summit — across the plains of Saurashtra toward the Gulf of Khambhat — is magnificent.

In 2014, Palitana made international news when the local government of Palitana city, responding to a hunger strike by Jain monks, declared the entire city of Palitana a vegetarian zone — becoming the world's first officially vegetarian city. This declaration prohibits the sale of eggs, meat, and fish within city limits, reflecting the Jain principle of ahimsa (non-violence toward all living beings).

## History & Origins

The sanctity of Shatrunjaya Hill in Jain tradition extends to prehistoric times, with texts claiming that Pundarika, a disciple of Rishabhanatha (Adinatha) — the first of the 24 Tirthankaras — attained liberation on the hill. Rishabhanatha himself is said to have visited the hill. These associations make Shatrunjaya the most ancient and important tirtha for Shvetambara Jains.

Historical evidence for temple construction on the hill dates to the 11th century CE, though texts refer to earlier structures. The earliest surviving temple is the Adishwara Temple — dedicated to Adinatha (Rishabhanatha), the first Tirthankara — which dates from approximately the 11th century. The construction of this temple marks the beginning of the historical record of the Palitana complex.

The medieval period saw intensive building activity, driven by the wealth of the Jain merchant community in Gujarat. Jain traders — known for their commercial acumen and the religious merit they accumulated through charitable giving and temple construction — donated enormous sums to construct temples at Palitana. Each temple was typically built, endowed, and managed by a particular family or business community, and the temples' dedication inscriptions often preserve the names of their donors.

The Muslim sultans who ruled Gujarat from the 14th to 16th centuries periodically damaged the temples, and much of the current structure reflects rebuilding done after periods of iconoclasm. The Mughal Emperor Akbar, who had a general policy of religious tolerance and was personally fascinated by Jainism (he had a close relationship with the Jain monk Hiravijaya Suri), reportedly visited or had interest in the Palitana temples.

The most intensive period of temple construction occurred in the 16th through 19th centuries, when Jain merchants of the Saurashtra region accumulated great wealth through trade and invested heavily in religious merit-making at Shatrunjaya. Many of the temples visible today were built or substantially rebuilt in this period.

The British colonial period brought relative stability that allowed continued temple construction. The marble-clad temples that dominate the summit complex today date largely from the 18th through early 20th centuries.

## Religious Significance

In Jainism, a tirtha is a sacred place that serves as a "ford" or "crossing place" — a location where the barriers between the earthly realm and liberation (moksha) are thinner, and where spiritual practice is especially efficacious. Shatrunjaya is considered the most important tirtha in Shvetambara Jainism, though not all Jain sects are equally devoted to the site.

Jain cosmology teaches that the universe has gone through endless cycles of time, and in each time cycle 24 Tirthankaras appear — omniscient teachers who have attained liberation and by their example and teaching show others the way. Palitana's extraordinary status derives from its association with nearly all 24 Tirthankaras, as well as with numerous other liberated souls (siddhas) who are believed to have attained moksha on the hill.

The primary deity of the Palitana complex is Adinatha (Rishabhanatha), the first Tirthankara, enshrined in the large Adishwara Temple at the center of the summit plateau. But the other Tirthankaras are also honored in numerous smaller temples across the complex. The last Tirthankara, Mahavira (24th), is honored in several temples; and the 22nd Tirthankara, Neminatha, has a special connection with Shatrunjaya as well as with the nearby sacred hill of Girnar.

Jain pilgrimage theology holds that darshan at a tirtha — the act of seeing and being seen by the sacred image — transfers spiritual merit to the pilgrim. The physical effort of climbing the steps to reach the summit temples adds to the merit accumulated. It is said that the merit of dying on Shatrunjaya Hill is so great that the dying person automatically attains liberation — a belief parallel to the Hindu teaching about dying in Varanasi.

One of the more distinctive features of Jain sacred practice at Palitana is the rule that no one — not priests, not pilgrims, not caretakers — may spend the night on the hill. This rule is strictly enforced: at dusk, all visitors must descend. The hill is left to the siddhas (liberated souls) alone at night. This prohibition has been in force throughout recorded history.

## Architecture & Description

The temples of Palitana are approached by ascending approximately 3,800 steps from the base of the hill. The steps were cut and maintained over centuries, and the ascent takes the average pilgrim one to two hours. Along the way, palanquin bearers offer to carry those unable to walk.

The summit plateau is divided into several tonks — enclosed temple precincts, each owned by a particular Jain community and managed by a trust. The approach to each tonk is through a gateway, often elaborately carved, leading into a courtyard containing multiple temples of varying sizes.

The architectural style is predominantly Maru-Gurjara (Solanki or Chaulukya style), the temple architecture developed in medieval Gujarat that is characterized by the intricate carved spires (shikharas), elaborate porches (mandapas), and exquisite stone carving that covers virtually every surface. The white marble of which most temples are constructed catches the brilliant Gujarat sunlight, creating an overwhelming visual impression of intricacy and light.

The largest and most important temple is the Adishwara Temple, dedicated to Adinatha. The main tower (meru shikhara) rises above the sanctuary in the classic north Indian style, and the exterior is covered in carved figures of celestial beings (devas), auspicious symbols, and decorative motifs. The interior contains the central marble image of Adinatha in a seated meditation posture.

The Chaumukha Temple (Temple of the Four-Faced Image) is another major landmark, containing a central pillar with four images of Adinatha facing the four cardinal directions — a form symbolizing the Tirthankara's omniscient teaching to the entire world.

The collective impression of the Palitana complex — hundreds of spires rising above the Gujarat plain as seen from a distance, and the overwhelming density of carved marble at close range — is unlike anything else in the world.

## Pilgrimage & Rituals

The annual pilgrimage to Palitana, known as the Palitana Yatra, draws hundreds of thousands of Jain pilgrims from across India and the diaspora. The pilgrimage is particularly popular during certain auspicious periods in the Jain calendar: Paryushana (the annual Jain festival of fasting and spiritual renewal), Diwali, and various Tirthankara birth anniversary celebrations.

The pilgrimage typically begins with a pre-dawn start, to ascend the steps in the cool of the morning and arrive at the summit at dawn for morning worship. The ascent is made barefoot, as footwear is not permitted on the sacred hill. Pilgrims carry offerings of flowers, saffron water, and incense to offer to the Tirthankara images in the temples.

The rituals performed at the temples are those of the Shvetambara tradition: abhisheka (ritual bathing of the image), puja (worship) with flowers, saffron, rice, and incense, and devotional chanting of stotras (hymns) in Sanskrit and Gujarati. The use of image worship (murti puja) distinguishes the Murti-pujak (image-worshipping) Shvetambaras — the majority at Palitana — from the Sthanakvasi and Terapanthi sects of Jainism, who avoid image worship.

The descent from the hill in the afternoon, completing the day's pilgrimage, is accompanied by the strict requirement to leave before sundown.

## Modern Significance

Palitana is one of the most significant pilgrimage sites in Gujarat and draws visitors from Jain communities worldwide. The town's 2014 declaration as the world's first vegetarian city was based on a decision by local politicians following a hunger strike by Jain monks who argued that the slaughter of animals for food in the shadow of a sacred site of non-violence was untenable. The decision has been controversial: non-Jain residents of Palitana argued that it violated their freedom to choose their diet, and the practical enforcement of the rule has varied.

The Palitana temples are under the care of various temple trusts, coordinated through the Jain community. Significant funds are invested in maintaining the marble temples, which require constant care due to the effects of weather, pollution, and the wear from millions of visitors.

Conservation concerns have been raised about the condition of some temples, particularly older structures dating from the 11th–13th centuries that have been affected by weathering. The balance between maintaining the temples as living religious spaces and preserving their historical fabric is an ongoing challenge.

Tourism to Palitana is increasing as awareness of the site grows both within India and internationally. The site is increasingly featured in heritage tourism circuits for Gujarat.

## Timeline

- **Prehistoric (per tradition)**: Pundarika, disciple of Adinatha, attains liberation on Shatrunjaya Hill.
- **11th century CE**: Construction of the earliest surviving temples, including the Adishwara Temple.
- **14th–16th centuries**: Periods of iconoclasm under the Gujarat Sultanate; temples damaged and rebuilt.
- **16th century**: Jain monk Hiravijaya Suri meets Mughal Emperor Akbar; increased Jain influence.
- **16th–19th centuries**: Major period of temple construction funded by wealthy Jain merchants.
- **18th–19th centuries**: Most of the current temple structures built or substantially rebuilt in marble.
- **1947**: Indian independence; Gujarat becomes part of India.
- **1960**: Gujarat state formed; Palitana is in Bhavnagar district.
- **2014**: Palitana city is declared the world's first vegetarian city following a hunger strike by Jain monks.
- **Present**: Over 900 temples on the hill; hundreds of thousands of pilgrims annually.
