## Summary

Sites across Cambodia where the Khmer Rouge executed and buried over a million victims between 1975 and 1979. The largest site at Choeung Ek contained over 8,000 skulls.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |

## Additional Context

- “Killing Fields” is a widely used term for mass-execution and burial sites associated with the Khmer Rouge era.
- The sites are discussed as part of the broader Cambodian genocide, alongside forced labor, starvation, and imprisonment policies that caused mass death.

## Memorialization and Documentation

- Several locations became memorial sites and places of remembrance, with efforts to document victims through forensic work, survivor testimony, and archival research.
- Public exhibits (including collections of recovered remains) have been used to educate visitors and to support historical accountability narratives.

## Historical Significance

- Serves as a geographic reminder of state violence at scale and the administrative logistics required for systematic mass killing.
- Often referenced in comparative genocide studies and transitional-justice discussions.

## Key Questions for Researchers
