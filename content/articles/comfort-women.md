## Summary

System of military sexual slavery by the Imperial Japanese Army during WWII. Up to 200,000 women from Korea, China, and Southeast Asia were forced into prostitution at military brothels.

## Additional Context

- **Term usage:** "Comfort women" refers to women and girls who were exploited in a system of **military brothels** organized or supported by the Imperial Japanese Army.
- **Victim origins:** Survivors and historical research describe victims from **Korea**, **China**, and other parts of **East and Southeast Asia**, among others.
- **Coercion mechanisms:** Accounts include abduction, deception with false job offers, trafficking through intermediaries, and outright force.

## Postwar Recognition and Advocacy

- **Survivor testimony:** Public testimony and documentation campaigns in the late 20th century significantly increased international awareness.
- **Historical debate:** The topic remains contentious in Japan–Korea relations and in public memory, with disputes over responsibility, wording, and education.
- **Memorialization:** Statues, museums, and annual commemorations have been created in several countries to honor survivors and document the history.

## International and Legal Themes

- **Human-rights framing:** The system is widely discussed as **sexual slavery** and a severe wartime human-rights violation.
- **Government statements:** Japanese government statements and agreements over time have been criticized by some as insufficient and defended by others as reconciliatory.
- **Documentation:** Evidence cited in public debate includes survivor testimony, military records, and contemporary reporting.
