## Summary

Supporters of President Trump stormed the US Capitol in an attempt to overturn the 2020 election results. Five people died and over 140 police officers were injured in the attack.

## Background and Lead-up

The attack occurred after weeks of false claims that the 2020 United States presidential election had been stolen. Congress met on January 6, 2021 to certify Electoral College results, a routine constitutional process.

## Key Moments (High-Level)

- A rally near the White House preceded a march toward the Capitol.
- Crowds breached security perimeters and entered the building, disrupting the certification proceedings.
- Elected officials and staff were evacuated or sheltered in secure locations while law enforcement worked to clear the complex.

## Aftermath and Accountability

The event prompted a major federal criminal investigation, including charges such as unlawful entry, obstruction-related offenses, and assault on law enforcement in some cases. Sentences varied widely based on conduct and evidence.

## Investigations and Institutional Responses

Multiple reviews examined security and intelligence failures and recommended changes to planning, inter-agency coordination, and physical security at the Capitol. The U.S. House of Representatives also convened a select committee to document events and issue findings and recommendations.

## Longer-Term Impact

The Capitol attack became a reference point in U.S. politics and civic discourse, influencing debates about political violence, extremist mobilization, election administration, and public trust in democratic institutions.
