## Summary

Mozambican officials secretly borrowed $2 billion in government-guaranteed loans that were hidden from parliament and donors. The scandal plunged Mozambique into a debt crisis.

## Background

The Mozambique hidden debts scandal refers to a set of large, state-backed loans arranged for security and maritime-related projects through state-linked companies. The loans were supported by government guarantees but were not fully disclosed to parliament, the public, or international donors at the time they were incurred.

The episode is often associated with three entities frequently cited in summaries of the case:

- Proindicus
- EMATUM
- Mozambique Asset Management (MAM)

## Disclosure and Economic Shock

When the extent of the borrowing became public in 2016, it triggered a crisis of confidence in Mozambique’s public finances. Key consequences commonly described include:

- Suspension or reduction of support by international lenders and donors, including the IMF.
- Currency depreciation, inflationary pressure, and tighter access to external financing.
- Debt restructuring negotiations and long-term fiscal strain.

## Allegations and Legal Proceedings

The scandal generated allegations of corruption, including claims that portions of the financing were diverted through kickbacks and other improper payments. Legal actions and investigations involved multiple jurisdictions because banks, investors, and intermediaries operated internationally.

In public reporting, the case is frequently referenced in connection with prosecutions and settlements involving both Mozambican officials and financial institutions tied to the loan arrangements.

## Legacy

The hidden debts scandal is often cited as a major governance and transparency failure with lasting economic consequences. It is also used in policy discussions as an example of the risks associated with opaque sovereign guarantees, weak parliamentary oversight, and complex cross-border financing structures.
