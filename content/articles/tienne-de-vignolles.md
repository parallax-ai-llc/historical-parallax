## Summary

French mercenary known as La Hire who fought alongside Joan of Arc but was also notorious for pillaging and terrorizing civilians. He epitomized the lawless routier mercenary tradition.

## Historical Context

Étienne de Vignolles, nicknamed **La Hire**, was active during the later phases of the **Hundred Years’ War**. This period featured unstable front lines, fragmented authority, and a large number of semi-professional soldiers who moved between employers and campaigns.

## Reputation and Conduct

Medieval sources and later retellings often describe La Hire in two contrasting registers:

- **Military competence and loyalty** to the French cause in major operations.
- **Routier-style violence** common among mercenary bands of the era, including pillaging of local populations when armies were unpaid or operating without strong discipline.

Because records from the period can be partisan and incomplete, specific allegations should be treated as historically contextualized rather than as modern courtroom findings.

## Role in Major Campaigns

La Hire is frequently mentioned in accounts associated with **Joan of Arc’s campaigns**, including operations around Orléans and later actions that helped shift momentum toward the French crown. He appears as one of several hard-fighting captains whose battlefield presence complemented the political and symbolic effect of Joan’s leadership.

## Legacy

La Hire’s legacy reflects a broader reality of late medieval warfare:

- commanders could be celebrated as national defenders,
- while simultaneously being feared for the collateral harm inflicted by soldiers living off the land.

This duality is why he is sometimes presented both as a heroic lieutenant and as an emblem of the era’s lawlessness.
