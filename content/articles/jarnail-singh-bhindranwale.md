## Summary

Sikh militant leader who fortified the Golden Temple in Amritsar, triggering Operation Blue Star. He was killed during the military operation.

## Political and Religious Context

Bhindranwale was associated with the Damdami Taksal, a Sikh religious seminary tradition. In many accounts of late-1970s and early-1980s Punjab politics, he became a polarizing figure amid:

- rising communal tensions,
- demands for greater Punjabi/Sikh political autonomy, and
- escalating militant violence and state countermeasures.

## Role in the Punjab Insurgency

- Supporters portrayed him as a defender of Sikh rights and a critic of perceived state repression.
- Critics and Indian authorities linked him to militant networks and killings; evaluations of his direct responsibility vary across sources and political viewpoints.

## Operation Blue Star and Death (1984)

- The Indian government launched Operation Blue Star to remove armed militants from the Golden Temple complex in Amritsar.
- The operation caused significant casualties and damage, becoming a lasting trauma in Sikh collective memory.
- Bhindranwale was killed during the operation, and his death further intensified political conflict.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- The events of 1984 were followed by major national shocks, including Indira Gandhi's assassination and subsequent anti-Sikh violence.
- Bhindranwale's legacy remains contested: he is memorialized by some as a martyr and condemned by others as a militant responsible for severe bloodshed.
