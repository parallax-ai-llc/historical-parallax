## Summary

Hugh Masekela (April 4, 1939 – January 23, 2018) was a South African jazz trumpeter, flugelhornist, cornetist, singer, and composer widely regarded as the "Father of South African jazz." Born in Witbank (now eMalahleni), he fused township jazz, Afrobeat, and American jazz into a distinctive sound that reached global audiences across six decades of performing. His 1968 instrumental hit "Grazing in the Grass" reached number one on the US Billboard Hot 100, making him one of the first African artists to achieve mainstream commercial success in the West.

Beyond music, Masekela was an unwavering voice against apartheid. He spent three decades in exile — traveling through Guinea, the United Kingdom, the United States, and Liberia — turning his displacement into a platform. He returned to South Africa in 1990 following Nelson Mandela's release and continued performing and advocating for African cultural identity until his death in 2018.

## Early Life & Background

Masekela was born in Witbank, Transvaal, on April 4, 1939. He grew up in Johannesburg's Alexandra and Sophiatown townships, vibrant cultural hubs that nurtured South Africa's jazz scene despite the oppressive conditions of apartheid. He received his first trumpet at age 14, a gift arranged by Archbishop Trevor Huddleston, who had been moved by the boy's interest in music after watching the film *Young Man with a Horn*. Masekela studied at the Huddleston Jazz Band and later at the Manhattan Brothers' orbit of musicians. He attended St. Peter's Secondary School and subsequently the Johannesburg Native Music School. In 1959, he co-founded the Jazz Epistles alongside Dollar Brand (Abdullah Ibrahim), one of the first African jazz bands to record an album.

## Rise to Prominence

Following the 1960 Sharpeville Massacre, Masekela left South Africa into exile. With help from Harry Belafonte and Miriam Makeba — whom he married in 1964 — he enrolled at the Manhattan School of Music in New York City. He signed with MGM Records and released his debut US album in 1963. His blend of township kwela rhythms with American jazz attracted critical attention, but it was the 1968 single "Grazing in the Grass," recorded for Uni Records, that brought him international stardom — selling over four million copies worldwide. He performed at the Monterey Pop Festival in 1967 and appeared at major festivals across Europe and North America throughout the 1970s.

## Key Achievements

- **"Grazing in the Grass" (1968):** Reached #1 on the Billboard Hot 100; one of the best-selling instrumental singles in history.
- **"Stimela (Coal Train)" (1974):** A landmark anti-apartheid composition depicting migrant labor conditions in Southern Africa.
- **Graceland Tour (1987):** Performed as part of Paul Simon's globally acclaimed *Graceland* concert tour, reaching audiences in over 40 countries.
- Recorded more than 40 studio albums over his career.
- Co-founded the Botswana International Music Camp to nurture young African musicians.

## Controversies & Criticism

Masekela faced criticism in the mid-1980s when, despite his anti-apartheid stance, his participation in Paul Simon's *Graceland* project drew controversy. Some African National Congress aligned groups argued the project violated the cultural boycott of South Africa, though Masekela and Simon defended it as amplifying African voices. He was also candid in interviews about personal struggles with substance abuse during his exile years, a period he addressed openly in his 2004 autobiography *Still Grazing*.

## Legacy & Influence

Masekela's integration of African rhythms into jazz helped lay the groundwork for Afrofusion as a global genre. He influenced generations of South African and diaspora musicians, and his recordings are considered foundational texts in both jazz and world music curricula. After returning to South Africa in 1990, he invested in cultural infrastructure, including the Johannesburg-based House of Tribes arts center. He received honorary doctorates from several institutions and was awarded South Africa's Order of Ikhamanga in Silver. He died of prostate cancer on January 23, 2018, in Johannesburg, mourned by heads of state, fellow musicians, and fans worldwide.
