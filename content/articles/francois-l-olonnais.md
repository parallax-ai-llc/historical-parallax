## Summary

French buccaneer known for extreme cruelty who terrorized the Spanish Main. He tortured prisoners and ate their hearts.

## Additional Context

### Historical setting

- L’Olonnais operated during the 17th-century era of Caribbean privateering and buccaneering, when European powers competed for colonial territory and maritime wealth.
- Buccaneers often shifted between quasi-legal privateering and outright piracy depending on political conditions and access to patronage.

### Reputation and sources

- Many accounts of his cruelty come from later narratives and compilations of pirate stories; such sources can mix eyewitness material with sensational embellishment.
- Even with possible exaggeration, his name became a shorthand in pirate lore for extreme violence against Spanish crews and settlements.

### Major actions (high level)

- The sack of Maracaibo is commonly cited as a prominent raid, reflecting how pirates targeted port towns for ransom, supplies, and intimidation.
- Expeditions into Central America illustrate the logistical fragility of pirate ventures: disease, supply shortages, and internal disputes could rapidly doom a campaign.

### Legacy

- L’Olonnais is remembered less for strategic success than for notoriety, serving as an example of how brutality could function as a deliberate terror tactic in maritime raiding.
- His story also shows how piracy narratives helped shape European popular images of the “Spanish Main” and colonial conflict.
