## Summary

Peter Grünberg was a German physicist who shared the 2007 Nobel Prize with Albert Fert for independently discovering giant magnetoresistance.

## Scientific Contribution: Giant Magnetoresistance (GMR)

- Giant magnetoresistance is a quantum mechanical effect observed in thin-film structures, where electrical resistance changes significantly depending on the relative alignment of magnetic layers.
- Grünberg’s work helped show how layered magnetic materials could be engineered to produce large, controllable resistance changes.

## Why It Mattered

- GMR rapidly became foundational to “spintronics,” the field that exploits electron spin (not only charge) in electronic devices.
- The discovery enabled much more sensitive read heads for hard disk drives, contributing to a step-change in storage density and lowering the cost per byte.

## Institutional and Career Notes

- He is commonly associated with German research institutes focused on solid-state and applied physics, where thin-film fabrication and precise measurement were central capabilities.

## Notes on the Nobel Prize

- The 2007 Nobel Prize in Physics was shared with Albert Fert for independent discovery and development of the GMR effect.
