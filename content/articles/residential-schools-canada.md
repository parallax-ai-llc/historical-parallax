## Summary

Canada forcibly removed 150,000 Indigenous children from their families and placed them in residential schools where thousands died from abuse, disease, and neglect. Mass graves were discovered.

## System Overview

The Canadian Indian residential school system was a network of boarding schools funded by the federal government and, for much of its history, administered in partnership with several Christian churches. The system’s stated goal was assimilation; Indigenous children were separated from their families and communities and placed in institutions often far from home.

## Living Conditions and Harms

Survivor testimony and historical investigations describe widespread harms, including:

- separation from family and suppression of Indigenous languages and cultures
- inadequate food, medical care, and sanitation
- physical, psychological, and sexual abuse in many institutions
- high mortality from disease and neglect, especially in earlier decades

## Investigations and Public Reckoning

- **Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC):** documented the system’s impacts and issued Calls to Action aimed at redress, education, and institutional reform.
- **Government apology (2008):** the federal government issued a formal apology to former students and their families.
- **Unmarked graves investigations (from 2021):** ground searches at several former school sites renewed public attention and prompted additional funding for community-led investigations.

## Legacy

The residential school system is widely recognized as a central driver of intergenerational trauma among Indigenous communities in Canada and remains a major focus of public policy, historical education, and reconciliation efforts.
