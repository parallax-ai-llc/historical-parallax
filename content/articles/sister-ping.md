## Summary

Chinese crime figure known as the 'Mother of All Snakeheads' who smuggled thousands of Chinese immigrants into the United States. She charged up to $40,000 per person for the dangerous journey.

## Additional Context

### Who she was

Cheng Chui Ping (often called “Sister Ping”) was a prominent figure associated with large-scale human smuggling networks moving migrants from China, particularly Fujian, into the United States. In many accounts she is described as a central financier and organizer rather than someone who personally transported migrants.

### “Snakehead” smuggling networks

In US law-enforcement and media reporting, “snakehead” refers to smugglers who arrange illicit migration routes. These operations can involve fraudulent documents, clandestine travel, debt bondage, and threats against migrants or their families when smuggling debts are not repaid.

### Links to high-profile incidents

Sister Ping’s name became widely known in connection with publicized immigration-smuggling cases in the 1990s, which intensified US attention on transnational smuggling rings and the risks faced by migrants in overcrowded or unsafe transport conditions.

### Prosecution and sentencing

US authorities ultimately prosecuted her for offenses related to human smuggling. The case is frequently cited as an example of how federal investigations target the financial and organizational layers of smuggling enterprises, not only the individuals conducting transport.

### Broader significance

The story is often discussed in the context of:

- Migration pressures and demand for illicit routes
- Exploitation of migrants through coercive debt arrangements
- The difficulty of dismantling networks that operate across jurisdictions
