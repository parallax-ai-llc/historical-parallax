## Summary

Horst Störmer is a German-American physicist who shared the 1998 Nobel Prize in Physics for the discovery of the fractional quantum Hall effect.

## Additional Context

### Fractional quantum Hall effect (FQHE)

- The FQHE is an emergent quantum phenomenon observed in two-dimensional electron systems under low temperatures and strong magnetic fields.
- It is associated with quantized Hall conductance at fractional values and is explained via strongly correlated electron states.
- The discovery helped establish a broader understanding of topological phases of matter and quasiparticles with fractional charge.

### Research environment

- Much of the early experimental work on quantum Hall physics took place in industrial and academic labs with advanced semiconductor fabrication and cryogenics.
- The Nobel Prize for the FQHE is shared with collaborators who contributed to the experimental discovery and theoretical interpretation.

### Impact

- Quantum Hall research influenced condensed-matter physics, precision metrology, and the development of concepts later used in topological insulators and related fields.
- The field continues to be active, including efforts to probe exotic quasiparticles and potential applications in fault-tolerant quantum computation.

### Recognition and roles

- Nobel laureates in physics often continue as senior researchers, lab leaders, or institute directors, mentoring new experimental programs.
- Störmer’s work is commonly cited in textbooks and review articles covering two-dimensional electron systems and correlated quantum phenomena.
