## Summary

Collapse of Lehman Brothers on September 15, 2008 was the largest bankruptcy filing in US history and triggered the global financial crisis. The firm had $639 billion in assets.

## Background

- Lehman Brothers was a major U.S. investment bank whose failure became a symbolic turning point in the 2007–2009 global financial crisis.
- The firm was heavily exposed to U.S. housing-related assets and the broader credit markets that seized up during the crisis.

## Why the Bankruptcy Mattered

- Lehman’s bankruptcy disrupted short-term funding markets and counterparty confidence, worsening liquidity stress across the financial system.
- It accelerated policy interventions elsewhere, including emergency lending facilities, guarantees, and broader stabilization programs.

## Mechanisms Discussed in Postmortems

- Leverage: large balance-sheet exposure relative to equity.
- Maturity mismatch: reliance on short-term funding to finance longer-term assets.
- Valuation pressure: declining prices for mortgage-related securities undermined collateral and confidence.

## Aftermath and Investigations

- The collapse led to extensive investigations and reports, including examinations of risk controls, disclosure, and accounting practices.
- “Repo 105” became a widely cited example in discussions about balance-sheet presentation and the limits of formal compliance.

## Lasting Lessons (High-Level)

- Stress-testing, liquidity buffers, and resolution planning became much more central to bank supervision after 2008.
- The event remains a case study in systemic risk: how the failure of one institution can transmit shocks through interconnected markets.
