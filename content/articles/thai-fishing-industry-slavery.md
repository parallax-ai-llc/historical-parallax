## Summary

Systematic use of forced labor in Thailands fishing industry involving trafficked men from Myanmar and Cambodia. Workers were held at sea for years, beaten, and sometimes killed.

## Background

Thailand has long been one of the world’s largest seafood exporters, with a large industrial fleet and complex supply chains that can include distant-water fishing, transshipment at sea, and processing on shore.

Migrant workers from neighboring countries have been a major part of the labor force. Recruiters and brokers operating across borders contributed to conditions where debt, document confiscation, and coercion could be used to trap workers.

## How the exploitation worked (high level)

- **Trafficking and recruitment fraud:** Men seeking work were recruited in Myanmar, Cambodia, and elsewhere, then moved through brokers into fishing crews.
- **Control at sea:** Long voyages and limited oversight made it difficult to escape or report abuse.
- **Violence and intimidation:** Reports described beatings, threats, and severe punishment for resistance or attempted escape.
- **Withholding of pay:** Wages were often delayed or denied, reinforcing dependence and preventing workers from leaving.

## Investigations and international pressure

Major media investigations in the mid-2010s highlighted forced labor and abuses linked to Thai seafood supply chains. International scrutiny increased through consumer campaigns, retailer sourcing reviews, and government attention.

The European Union’s discussions of trade measures and warnings about illegal, unreported, and unregulated fishing added economic and diplomatic pressure to demonstrate reforms.

## Policy and enforcement responses

Thailand announced reforms aimed at improving oversight, including stronger inspections, registration of workers, and monitoring of vessels and landings. Enforcement included investigations into trafficking networks and labor violations.

Outcomes varied by sector and time, and observers continued to debate the effectiveness and consistency of implementation.
