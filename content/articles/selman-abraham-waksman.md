## Scientific work

Waksman was a pioneering microbiologist associated with Rutgers University whose research emphasized **soil microbiology** and the screening of microorganisms for bioactive compounds. His laboratory helped popularize systematic approaches to finding antibiotics from actinomycetes.

## Streptomycin and antibiotics

Waksman is strongly associated with the discovery and development of **streptomycin**, one of the first effective antibiotics against tuberculosis. The work is often summarized as a collaboration within his research group, combining laboratory screening, purification, and testing that ultimately led to clinical use.

## Nobel Prize and credit controversy

Waksman received the 1952 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for the discovery of streptomycin. Historical accounts frequently note disputes about recognition within the lab, particularly involving graduate student Albert Schatz, who was closely involved in the initial isolation and testing of streptomycin. The episode is often cited in discussions of authorship, supervision, and credit allocation in science.

## Terminology and legacy

Waksman helped shape the modern usage of the term **"antibiotic"** in scientific discourse. His broader legacy includes methods for natural-product discovery and the institutional model of antibiotic screening programs that influenced later pharmaceutical research.
