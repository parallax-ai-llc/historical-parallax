## Summary

Ukrainian revolutionary who attempted to assassinate Vladimir Lenin on August 30, 1918, shooting him twice. The assassination attempt triggered the Red Terror. She was executed without trial three days later.

## Early life and radicalization (overview)

Kaplan (often described as a Ukrainian-born revolutionary active in Russia) became involved with underground political movements in the late Russian Empire.

Accounts commonly connect her to Socialist Revolutionary (SR) circles, a major revolutionary current that later opposed Bolshevik rule.

## The 1918 attempt on Lenin

On 30 August 1918, Lenin was shot after speaking at a Moscow factory. He survived but suffered serious injuries.

Kaplan was arrested soon after. The public record around the incident has long been contested, including questions about:

- The immediate evidence used to attribute responsibility
- Conflicting witness testimony and later political narratives
- The absence of a formal public trial

## Political consequences

The shooting became part of the context for a sharp escalation of political violence during the Russian Civil War era.

Bolshevik authorities linked the incident to counterrevolutionary threats and used it to justify intensified security measures, widely associated with the onset of the Red Terror.

## Execution and legacy

Kaplan was executed within days of her arrest. Because the case was handled rapidly and within an atmosphere of emergency rule, it remains a subject of historical debate.

In later historiography and political discourse, Kaplan has been portrayed variously as:

- A committed SR militant acting out of ideological opposition to Bolshevism
- A symbolic figure used to legitimize repression
- A contested historical actor whose precise role is difficult to reconstruct with certainty
