## Key Points

- **Name and titles:** Mobutu Sese Seko (formerly Joseph-Désiré Mobutu) ruled for roughly three decades and cultivated an image as the embodiment of the state.
- **System of rule:** His government is commonly described as **personalist and kleptocratic**, with patronage networks central to political control.
- **Cold War alignment:** He positioned Zaire as a key anti-communist partner for Western governments, which shaped external support despite governance concerns.
- **Economic record:** Periods of growth were followed by deepening debt, corruption, and institutional decay; public services and state capacity deteriorated over time.
- **Human rights:** Political repression, censorship, and persecution of opponents were recurring features of the regime.

## Background

Mobutu emerged from the late-colonial Belgian Congo and rose quickly in the post-independence turmoil. The early 1960s were marked by competing centers of power, secessionist movements, and intense international involvement. Mobutu’s ascent was closely tied to the security apparatus and to alliances among domestic elites.

## Rule and Governance (High-Level)

- **Party-state structure:** The MPR was treated as the nation’s sole political vehicle, and loyalty to Mobutu was a core requirement for advancement.
- **Patronage and corruption:** Access to state resources was distributed through patron-client ties; corruption became systemic rather than incidental.
- **National identity campaigns:** “Authenticité” promoted cultural and symbolic changes (e.g., place-name changes), while also reinforcing centralized authority.

## Foreign Relations

- **Regional role:** Zaire was involved in multiple regional conflicts and served as a rear base or transit point for various armed groups at different times.
- **Western support:** Strategic alignment in the Cold War brought diplomatic and material backing, even as criticism mounted over governance and rights.

## Decline and Overthrow

By the 1990s, economic weakness, elite fragmentation, and popular discontent intensified. The First Congo War (1996–1997) culminated in Mobutu’s removal, with Laurent-Désiré Kabila’s forces taking Kinshasa. Mobutu died in exile in 1997.

## Legacy

Mobutu’s legacy is widely debated but often summarized around:

- **State weakening:** Long-term institutional erosion and normalization of rent-seeking.
- **Precedent for personalist rule:** Centralization around a single leader and networked elites.
- **Regional spillover:** Zaire/DRC’s instability became intertwined with broader Great Lakes regional conflicts.
