## Summary

Elizabeth Holmes claimed to revolutionize blood testing but the technology never worked. Walgreens and Safeway offered tests to patients using unreliable equipment.

## What was allegedly misrepresented

Theranos claimed it could run a wide range of laboratory tests using only a finger-prick amount of blood, delivered quickly and at low cost. Investigations later found that the company’s proprietary devices could not reliably perform many of the tests as advertised, and that Theranos often relied on conventional analyzers from established manufacturers to generate results.

Public reporting also described internal practices such as hiding or limiting demonstrations, and presenting optimistic performance claims to partners and investors without adequate validation.

## Key actors and institutions

- **Theranos leadership:** Elizabeth Holmes (founder/CEO) and senior executives were central to fundraising and partner negotiations.
- **Business partners:** Retail and healthcare partners (including pharmacy chains) sought to provide convenient consumer testing, increasing the potential scale of harm if results were inaccurate.
- **Regulators and prosecutors:** U.S. regulators and federal prosecutors investigated the company’s lab practices and statements to investors.

## Investigation and collapse

A major turning point was investigative journalism that raised questions about the technology and company practices, which accelerated regulatory scrutiny and partner withdrawals. As scrutiny grew, the company’s valuation and ability to operate declined, and Theranos ultimately dissolved.

## Legal and financial consequences

The Theranos case involved civil enforcement actions and criminal proceedings. Holmes was convicted in U.S. federal court on fraud-related charges connected to representations made to investors, and sentencing followed in 2022. The case has been widely cited as a landmark example of "fake it till you make it" narratives crossing into alleged fraud in the context of health technology.

## Broader significance

Theranos is frequently used as a cautionary case in discussions of venture capital governance, board oversight, medical device validation, and the risks of secrecy in clinical testing. It also influenced how later biotech and diagnostics startups communicate performance claims and manage independent verification.
