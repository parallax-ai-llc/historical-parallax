## Summary

Nat King Cole (born Nathaniel Adams Coles, March 17, 1919 – February 15, 1965) was an American jazz pianist and vocalist who became one of the best-selling recording artists of the 20th century. His warm baritone voice, impeccable phrasing, and extraordinary piano technique set him apart from his contemporaries, earning him a devoted global audience across multiple decades.

Cole's significance extends beyond music: in 1956 he became the first African American to host a national television variety series in the United States, breaking a significant racial barrier in broadcast entertainment. His recordings — including "Unforgettable," "Nature Boy," and "The Christmas Song" — remain staples of the American songbook and continue to reach new audiences generations after his death.

His crossover from jazz instrumentalist to mainstream pop vocalist demonstrated that artistic excellence could transcend racial and genre boundaries, paving the way for countless Black artists in popular music and television who followed.

## Early Life & Background

Nathaniel Adams Coles was born on March 17, 1919, in Montgomery, Alabama, the fourth of five children of Edward James Coles, a Baptist minister, and Perlina Adams Coles. The family relocated to Chicago when Cole was four years old, settling in the South Side neighborhood that was a hub of African American cultural and musical life.

Cole began studying piano at age four under his mother's guidance and later took formal lessons. He was deeply influenced by Chicago's thriving jazz scene and by pianist Earl "Fatha" Hines in particular. By his mid-teens he was performing professionally, and at 17 he briefly joined a touring company of the musical *Shuffle Along* before settling in Los Angeles around 1937.

## Rise to Prominence

In Los Angeles, Cole formed the King Cole Trio in 1937 with guitarist Oscar Moore and bassist Wesley Prince — a lean, guitar-piano-bass format that became highly influential. The trio's sophisticated interplay and swing earned them a devoted following on the club circuit. Their 1943 recording of "Straighten Up and Fly Right" — based on a folk tale Cole's father used as a sermon illustration — reached No. 1 on the R&B charts and crossed over to mainstream audiences, selling over 500,000 copies.

Capitol Records signed Cole in 1943. Over the following decade he shifted progressively toward lush orchestral pop ballads. The 1948 recording of "Nature Boy" spent eight weeks at No. 1 on the pop charts. "Mona Lisa" (1950) won the Academy Award for Best Original Song and topped the charts for eight weeks, selling over three million copies. By the early 1950s Cole was one of the highest-paid entertainers in the United States.

## Key Achievements

- **"Unforgettable" (1951):** Became his signature song and later a posthumous duet hit when his daughter Natalie Cole re-recorded it in 1991, winning Record of the Year at the Grammy Awards.
- **"The Christmas Song" (1946):** One of the best-selling holiday recordings of all time, recorded four separate times with increasingly elaborate orchestration.
- **The Nat King Cole Show (1956–57):** A 15-minute NBC variety program, the first nationally broadcast American television show hosted by an African American.
- **Album *After Midnight* (1957):** A critically acclaimed return to his jazz trio roots, widely regarded as one of his finest recordings.
- He recorded over 100 charting singles and sold tens of millions of records worldwide during his lifetime.

## Controversies & Criticism

Cole faced persistent racism throughout his career. In April 1956, he was physically attacked on stage during a concert in Birmingham, Alabama, by members of a white supremacist group. Despite the incident, Cole initially continued performing in the South, though he later distanced himself from venues that enforced segregation.

His television show, *The Nat King Cole Show*, was cancelled in 1957 after 64 episodes because NBC could not secure a national sponsor — widely attributed to advertisers' reluctance to associate with a Black host in markets across the South. Cole remarked bitterly that "Madison Avenue is afraid of the dark."

Some jazz purists criticized his move away from instrumental jazz toward commercial pop balladry, viewing it as a commercial compromise. Cole consistently defended his artistic choices, arguing that songcraft and vocal performance were as legitimate as instrumental virtuosity.

## Legacy & Influence

Cole died of lung cancer on February 15, 1965, in Santa Monica, California, at age 45. His influence on popular music is vast: artists including Frank Sinatra, Tony Bennett, Harry Connick Jr., and Michael Bublé have cited him as a primary influence on phrasing and vocal delivery. His piano style shaped a generation of jazz and pop pianists.

He was inducted into the Grammy Hall of Fame multiple times, and "Unforgettable" was named one of the Songs of the Century by the Recording Industry Association of America. His pioneering role in American television remains a landmark in the history of Black representation in media.
