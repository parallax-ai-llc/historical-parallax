## Summary

The Yazd Atash Behram (Fire Temple) is a Zoroastrian fire temple located in the city of Yazd in central Iran. It is one of the nine Atash Behrams — temples housing the highest category of sacred fire — in the world, and it contains a flame that has been burning continuously since 470 CE, making it one of the oldest continuously burning fires in the world. The flame was originally lit in the province of Larestan in present-day Iran, transferred several times over the centuries to protect it, and has been at its current location in Yazd since 1940.

Yazd has historically been one of the most important centers of Zoroastrian culture and religion in Iran. While the Islamic conquest of Persia in the 7th century CE led most Iranians to convert to Islam, a significant Zoroastrian community persisted in the Yazd region, where the dry desert climate and relative isolation provided a degree of protection from persecution. Today Yazd retains the largest concentration of Zoroastrians in Iran, along with a rich legacy of Zoroastrian architecture, religious sites, and traditions.

The Atash Behram is one of several Zoroastrian sacred sites in and around Yazd that draw both pilgrims from the global Zoroastrian community (including the Parsi community of India, the descendants of Zoroastrians who fled to India during Islamic rule) and cultural tourists from around the world. Yazd itself was designated a UNESCO World Heritage City in 2017.

## History & Origins

Zoroastrianism is one of the world's oldest monotheistic religions, founded by the prophet Zarathustra (Greek: Zoroaster) who likely lived sometime between 1500 and 600 BCE (scholars debate the dates widely). Zarathustra taught the existence of one supreme God, Ahura Mazda (the "Wise Lord"), and a cosmic struggle between truth (Asha) and falsehood (Druj). The religion became the state religion of several Persian empires — the Achaemenid (550–330 BCE), the Arsacid/Parthian (247 BCE–224 CE), and the Sasanian (224–651 CE) — and shaped Persian civilization for over a millennium.

Sacred fire has been central to Zoroastrian practice since ancient times. Fire is understood not as an object of worship in itself but as the highest physical symbol of Ahura Mazda's divine truth and purity. The Avesta, the sacred scripture of Zoroastrianism, contains detailed instructions about the preparation and maintenance of sacred fires. Different grades of sacred fire exist in Zoroastrian practice, with the Atash Behram ("Victorious Fire" or "Fire of Fires") being the highest grade, created through an elaborate procedure involving the merging of fires from sixteen different sources including the fires of the four classes of society, the fire from a burning corpse, and fire from a bolt of lightning.

The flame housed in the Yazd Atash Behram was originally consecrated in 470 CE during the Sasanian period — less than two centuries before the Islamic conquest that would fundamentally transform Iranian society. It was kept in Larestan (in present-day Fars province). After the Arab conquest of Persia in the 7th century, the Zoroastrian communities began a gradual retreat, maintaining their sacred fires with tremendous dedication even as their political situation deteriorated.

The history of this particular fire is marked by repeated transfers to protect it from desecration. It was moved from its original location in Larestan to Khorasan in the 7th or 8th century, then to a series of other locations over the subsequent centuries. In the 10th century it is recorded in Sistan. By the 12th century it had made its way to Aden in the Yazd region, where it remained for centuries in a location called Narin Kushnasb.

In 1174 CE, the fire was transferred to a new location in the Yazd district called Aghda, where it remained until 1474 CE when it was moved to Ardakan, another town near Yazd. In 1686 it was brought into Yazd city itself, to a location called Sharifabad. It remained there until 1940, when it was transferred to the current Atash Behram building, which had been newly constructed for the purpose.

The current building was designed in 1934 by an Iranian architect in a style that blends Achaemenid Persian motifs — the winged symbol of Ahura Mazda (the Faravahar), flanking columns in the style of Persepolis, and a ceremonial staircase — with modern construction methods. It was completed and the fire transferred in 1940.

## Religious Significance

In Zoroastrian theology, fire (atash) is the most sacred of the physical elements because it is the purest and most luminous. As a manifestation of the divine order (Asha — truth, righteousness, cosmic order), fire is the earthly representative of Ahura Mazda's divine light. The Avestan hymn to fire, the Atash Nyayesh, addresses fire as "O Fire, of Ahura Mazda's son!" and celebrates its purity and beneficence.

The Atash Behram represents the highest possible expression of sacred fire within Zoroastrian practice. Creating an Atash Behram requires gathering fire from 16 specific sources and purifying them over an extended ritual period — a process historically taking over a year. Once established, the Atash Behram must be maintained in perpetuity: it must never be extinguished, must burn continuously, and must be fed by pure (ideally sandalwood) fuel at regular intervals throughout the day and night. The duty of maintaining the fire falls to specially trained priests (mobads).

The fire itself is understood to emit the spiritual energy (khwarr, divine glory) that benefits the entire Zoroastrian community. Pilgrims visiting the Atash Behram receive blessings from being in the fire's presence, and the fire's continued burning is understood as the continued presence of Ahura Mazda's light in the world.

Non-Zoroastrians are permitted to view the sacred fire through a glass window in the main hall; only Zoroastrians are allowed into the inner sanctuary area. The fire burns in a large urn (vase) behind the viewing window, visible as an orange-yellow flame of considerable size.

The theological significance of an unbroken flame spanning 1,500+ years is profound. In Zoroastrian understanding, this fire represents a continuous thread of divine presence, maintained through successive generations of devoted priests, through political catastrophe, persecution, displacement, and the near-extinction of the faith in Iran. That this flame has survived for fifteen centuries is understood as evidence of Ahura Mazda's protection and the dedication of his followers.

## Architecture & Description

The current Atash Behram building, completed around 1934 and opened in 1940, is a distinctive example of Achaemenid Revival architecture — a style that emerged in the late 19th and early 20th centuries as Iranian nationalists and the Zoroastrian community sought to reclaim the pre-Islamic heritage of Persia.

The facade features a large central portal flanked by columns decorated with the lotus flower capitals characteristic of Achaemenid architecture at Persepolis and Susa. Above the entrance is a relief depicting the Faravahar — the winged symbol of Zoroastrianism showing a human figure within a circle of wings, representing the human soul's aspiration toward truth and divine light. This symbol has become the most recognized icon of Zoroastrian identity worldwide.

The building is constructed of stone and brick, with the exterior clad in smooth stone. A broad ceremonial staircase leads up to the main entrance. The building is set within a garden with cypress trees — a traditional element of Persian landscaping.

The interior consists of an entrance hall and the main chamber where the sacred fire is kept. The fire is visible through a glass enclosure or window, burning in a large metal urn. The chamber is kept meticulously clean, as purity is essential to Zoroastrian ritual practice. The priests who tend the fire wear white garments and cover their mouths and noses with a cloth to prevent contamination of the sacred flame by breath.

The broader Yazd region contains numerous other Zoroastrian sacred sites. The Towers of Silence (Dakhma) — circular stone towers on hilltops outside the city where the Zoroastrian dead were traditionally exposed to be consumed by birds of prey, following the principle of not polluting the sacred elements of earth, fire, and water with a corpse — stand on the outskirts of Yazd, though the practice of aerial burial was officially prohibited in Iran in the 1970s and is no longer carried out.

## Pilgrimage & Rituals

The Yazd Atash Behram is a place of regular pilgrimage for Zoroastrians in Iran (numbering approximately 25,000–100,000 people, estimates vary), as well as for the Parsi community of India (approximately 60,000–70,000 people) and diaspora Zoroastrians from around the world.

Zoroastrians visit the fire temple for prayer (namaz), the recitation of Avestan mantras, and the experience of being in the presence of the sacred fire. Prayers are recited while facing the fire. Offerings of sandalwood — the preferred pure fuel for the sacred fire — are made by priests at prescribed intervals throughout the day.

The major festivals of the Zoroastrian calendar draw increased numbers of pilgrims. Nowruz, the Zoroastrian (and Persian) New Year celebrated at the spring equinox, is the most important annual festival, marking the triumph of light over darkness. Jashne Sadeh, celebrated in January/February, commemorates the legendary discovery of fire by the ancient hero Hoshang and involves the lighting of a large bonfire. Mehregan, the festival of the yazata (divine being) Mithra, is celebrated in autumn and is associated with loyalty, friendship, and the harvest.

For Parsi pilgrims from India, visiting Yazd and the Atash Behram is a profound journey to the ancestral homeland. The Parsis are descendants of Zoroastrians who fled Iran approximately 1,000 years ago during the early centuries of Islamic rule, establishing themselves in Gujarat on the western coast of India. Their pilgrimage to the Iranian homeland carries the emotional weight of diaspora return and reconnection with the original wellspring of their faith.

## Modern Significance

The Zoroastrian community of Iran is a recognized religious minority under the Islamic Republic, with a designated seat in the Iranian parliament (Majlis) and official protections for their religious practice. They may maintain their fire temples, observe their rituals, and follow their calendar. However, the community has shrunk dramatically over the course of the 20th century through emigration to Western countries and conversion, and the future of the community in Iran is uncertain.

The preservation of the Yazd Atash Behram and its sacred fire is a priority for the global Zoroastrian community. The fire's continued burning for over 1,500 years is understood as one of the community's most precious achievements, and its maintenance is taken with the utmost seriousness.

Yazd's designation as a UNESCO World Heritage City in 2017 has brought increased international attention to the city's extraordinary architectural heritage, including its Zoroastrian sites. The city's traditional mudbrick architecture, its qanat (underground water channel) system, its wind towers (badgirs), and its web of narrow covered alleyways represent one of the best-preserved examples of traditional Persian urban architecture.

The Atash Behram has become a site of cultural tourism as well as religious pilgrimage, as interest in pre-Islamic Persian history and civilization has grown both in Iran and internationally. The site's association with the ancient Achaemenid Empire and with Zoroastrianism's role as the spiritual foundation of Persian civilization gives it significance beyond the Zoroastrian community.

## Timeline

- **c. 1500–600 BCE**: Zarathustra (Zoroaster) founds the Zoroastrian religion; dates debated by scholars.
- **550–330 BCE**: Achaemenid Persian Empire; Zoroastrianism is the dominant religion of Persia.
- **247 BCE–224 CE**: Arsacid/Parthian Empire; Zoroastrianism continues as a major religion.
- **224–651 CE**: Sasanian Empire; Zoroastrianism is the official state religion.
- **470 CE**: The sacred flame of the Yazd Atash Behram is first consecrated in Larestan.
- **651 CE**: Arab armies conquer Persia; the Sasanian Empire falls; gradual Islamization of Persia begins.
- **7th–8th century CE**: The sacred fire is moved to Khorasan to protect it.
- **10th century CE**: The fire is recorded in Sistan.
- **1174 CE**: The fire is transferred to Aghda in the Yazd district.
- **1474 CE**: Transferred to Ardakan.
- **1686 CE**: Transferred to Sharifabad in Yazd city.
- **c. 720–740 CE**: Zoroastrian refugees begin arriving in India, eventually settling in Gujarat; they become the Parsi community.
- **1934 CE**: The current Atash Behram building is designed.
- **1940 CE**: The sacred fire is transferred to the new Atash Behram building where it burns today.
- **2017 CE**: Yazd is designated a UNESCO World Heritage City.
- **Present**: The flame continues to burn; the Zoroastrian community maintains the temple.
