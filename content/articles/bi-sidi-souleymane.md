## Context: armed groups in northwestern CAR

The Central African Republic (CAR) has experienced repeated cycles of rebellion and communal violence since the early 2000s, with numerous armed groups controlling territory outside the capital.

In the northwest, conflict dynamics have often involved competition over local power, road taxation, and livestock-related disputes, layered on top of national political instability.

## The 3R group (high level)

“3R” is commonly expanded as *Retour, Réclamation et Réhabilitation* (Return, Reclamation, and Rehabilitation).

Public reporting generally describes 3R as an armed group that claimed to represent (or defend) Fulani/Peul communities, while also engaging in predatory practices that harmed civilians, including those same communities.

Like other CAR factions, the group’s influence has been linked to control of rural routes, coercive fundraising, and the ability to mobilize fighters quickly for raids.

## Allegations of abuses

Bi Sidi Souleymane (also reported under the name “Sidiki Abbas”) has been associated in public sources with command responsibility for serious abuses attributed to 3R.

These allegations have included killings, forced displacement, and attacks on villages, including incidents cited by humanitarian and monitoring organizations.

The massacre referenced in many summaries (Ouham-Pendé, 2019) became a focal point for international attention to the group’s conduct.

## Responses, accountability, and status

CAR’s government, UN peacekeepers (MINUSCA), and international partners have periodically pursued disarmament or enforcement actions against armed groups, but outcomes have varied and are constrained by geography and limited state capacity.

Accounts of leaders’ status can change over time (arrest, flight, negotiations, or reintegration attempts), and English-language sourcing is often uneven.
