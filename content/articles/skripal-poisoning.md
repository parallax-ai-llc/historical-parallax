## Summary

Russian GRU agents poisoned former spy Sergei Skripal and his daughter with Novichok nerve agent in Salisbury. A bystander died after finding the discarded nerve agent container.

## Additional Context

### The victims and setting

Sergei Skripal was a former Russian military intelligence officer who had been convicted in Russia for espionage and later resettled in the United Kingdom as part of a spy exchange. In March 2018 he and his daughter Yulia were found critically ill in Salisbury, England, prompting a major public-health and criminal investigation.

### The agent and mode of attack

British authorities stated that a nerve agent from the “Novichok” group was used. The case drew attention because such agents are associated with state-level chemical weapons programs, and because the incident created risks for first responders and the wider public due to contamination concerns.

### Attribution and suspects

UK officials attributed the attack to Russia’s GRU and identified two individuals traveling under aliases as suspects. The attribution contributed to sustained diplomatic fallout and became a focal point in debates about state-sponsored assassination attempts, accountability, and deterrence.

### Secondary victim and public impact

Months later, a UK resident (often reported as Dawn Sturgess) died after exposure linked to a discarded container believed to have held the toxin, underscoring the broader hazards of chemical-agent incidents beyond the intended targets.

### Aftermath

The incident led to large-scale decontamination operations in Salisbury and surrounding areas, extensive media coverage, and international actions including coordinated expulsions of diplomats by multiple countries.

### Why the case mattered

It became a reference point for how governments respond to suspected state-linked attacks on their territory, especially when rare chemical agents and public safety are involved.
