## Summary

Indian tribal rebel leader who led the Rampa Rebellion against British colonial rule in the Agency Tracts of Andhra Pradesh. He was captured and shot dead by the British police.

## Historical Context

Raju is associated with resistance movements in the early 20th century that emerged in regions affected by colonial administration, policing, and restrictions on land and forest use.

The "Agency" areas (often referred to as Agency Tracts) included many tribal communities with distinct local governance traditions that came under increasing pressure during the British period.

## Rampa Rebellion (overview)

The Rampa Rebellion (1922–1924) involved raids, sabotage, and armed confrontations as part of a broader anti-colonial environment in India.

Accounts typically emphasize Raju’s role as an organizer and symbol of defiance, while noting that the conflict was marked by violence, reprisals, and the disruption of civilian life.

## Arrest and Death

Most summaries report that Raju was captured by colonial authorities and executed without a conventional public trial process, becoming a martyr figure in later narratives of the period.

## Legacy and Memory

Raju is remembered in parts of Andhra Pradesh (and in Telugu popular culture) as a freedom fighter, especially in narratives centered on tribal rights and resistance to exploitation.

Commemorations and retellings vary by source, blending historical documentation with oral tradition and regional political memory.

## Key Takeaways (high-level)

- The rebellion is often framed as both an anti-colonial uprising and a struggle connected to local governance and resource control.
- Historical evaluation depends heavily on colonial records, later nationalist histories, and regional oral accounts.
- Raju’s legacy illustrates how local revolts can be absorbed into broader national narratives over time.
