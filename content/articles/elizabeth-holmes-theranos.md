## Summary

American entrepreneur who founded Theranos and claimed to have revolutionized blood testing but the technology never worked. She defrauded investors of hundreds of millions in what was called Silicon Valley's biggest fraud.

## Additional Context

- **Theranos claim**: The company promoted the idea that many lab tests could be run from a very small blood sample using proprietary devices.
- **Investigative reporting**: Reporting by *The Wall Street Journal* (notably journalist John Carreyrou) brought sustained scrutiny to Theranos’s technology claims and business practices.

## Legal Aftermath

- Holmes was charged and later convicted on multiple counts related to defrauding investors; the case became a landmark example of how hype and secrecy can mask operational realities.
- The Theranos collapse also led to broader debate in Silicon Valley about “fake it till you make it” culture and the difference between software iteration and safety-critical biomedical claims.

## Key Takeaways

- The Theranos story is frequently cited in discussions of due diligence, governance, and the unique risks of applying startup-style messaging to regulated healthcare technology.

## Additional Details

- **Partnership narrative**: Theranos announced or pursued partnerships with major retailers and healthcare entities, which helped build credibility with the public and investors.
- **Governance**: The company’s board and leadership structure became part of later scrutiny regarding technical oversight and accountability.

## Key Takeaways (Business)

- The case is commonly used as a cautionary example in startup ecosystems: charismatic leadership and secrecy do not substitute for independently verified technical performance.
