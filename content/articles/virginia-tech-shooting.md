## Summary

Mass shooting at Virginia Tech on April 16, 2007 by Seung-Hui Cho who killed 32 people before committing suicide. It was the deadliest school shooting in US history.

## Additional Context

### Overview and Setting

The Virginia Tech shooting took place on the campus of Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University (Virginia Tech) in Blacksburg, Virginia, on April 16, 2007. The attack unfolded in multiple locations on campus over the course of the morning.

### Perpetrator and Aftermath

The perpetrator was identified as Seung-Hui Cho, a Virginia Tech student. After killing 32 people, he died by suicide. The event drew extensive public attention in the United States and internationally, and it became a reference point in subsequent discussions of mass violence.

### Emergency Response and Investigations

The shooting was followed by law-enforcement and institutional reviews, including examinations of campus notification practices and the sequence of events that day. In public reporting and official reviews, issues such as communications, timing, and coordination were widely analyzed.

### Policy and Institutional Impact

In the years following 2007, the event was frequently cited in debates about campus safety, mental-health systems, and firearm policy. Universities and public agencies referenced it when revisiting emergency-alert procedures and crisis-response planning.

### Commemoration

Virginia Tech established memorial efforts and annual remembrance practices for the victims. The shooting remains a significant historical event in the modern history of U.S. higher education and public safety.

### Public Discourse (High-Level)

The event has been repeatedly referenced in journalism, academic work, and public policy discussions as an example used to evaluate risk assessment, warning systems, and crisis communications.
