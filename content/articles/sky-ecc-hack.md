## Background

Sky ECC was marketed as a secure communications service using modified smartphones and an encrypted messaging platform. It became widely used by organized-crime groups in Europe because it promised privacy, device hardening, and controlled distribution.

## What happened

- Investigators from multiple European jurisdictions targeted Sky ECC’s infrastructure and distribution network.
- Authorities gained access to large volumes of messages and metadata associated with Sky ECC accounts.
- The intercepted communications were used to identify suspects, map criminal networks, and support coordinated raids across countries.

## Why it mattered

The operation is often cited as a turning point in European efforts against drug trafficking and other forms of organized crime because it converted day-to-day operational chatter into actionable intelligence at scale.

## Outcomes (high-level)

- Large numbers of arrests were reported across Europe.
- Significant seizures (including narcotics and cash) were attributed to the investigation.
- Prosecutors used message evidence to build cases, often combining it with physical surveillance, controlled deliveries, and financial investigations.

## Legal and evidentiary considerations

Because the investigation crossed borders and relied on encrypted-message interception, court challenges focused on issues such as: how the data was collected, chain-of-custody, disclosure obligations, and whether the interception complied with domestic and EU-level legal frameworks.

## Broader context

Sky ECC Hack is frequently discussed alongside similar law-enforcement operations targeting encrypted communication networks used by criminals (e.g., EncroChat) and illustrates an ongoing “cat-and-mouse” dynamic between secure-communications providers and investigative agencies.
