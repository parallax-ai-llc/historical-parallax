## Summary

British baby farmers known as the 'Finchley Baby Farmers' who murdered at least 18 infants placed in their care between 1900 and 1902. They chloroformed the babies. They were the first women hanged at Holloway Prison.

## Background: "Baby Farming" in Late-Victorian Britain

The term **baby farming** referred to the practice of taking in infants for payment, often from unmarried mothers facing social stigma. While some carers provided legitimate care, the system also enabled severe neglect and criminal abuse.

## Crimes and Investigation

- Sach and Walters took payments to care for infants and were accused of killing children placed with them.
- The case drew attention to how informal childcare arrangements could be exploited for profit.
- Their convictions were part of wider public concern over infant mortality and regulation of childcare at the time.

## Trial and Historical Significance

They were tried, convicted of murder, and executed in 1903. Their executions at Holloway Prison are often noted in accounts of British criminal justice history.
