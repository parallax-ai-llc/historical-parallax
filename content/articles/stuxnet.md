## Summary

Sophisticated computer worm jointly developed by the US and Israel that sabotaged Irans nuclear program in 2010. It destroyed nearly 1,000 uranium enrichment centrifuges at Natanz.

## What made it notable

Stuxnet is widely discussed as one of the first publicly known examples of malware designed to cause physical disruption to industrial equipment rather than just steal data or damage computers. It targeted industrial control systems associated with uranium enrichment.

## Technical approach (high level)

Analyses of the worm describe multiple components: propagation mechanisms to spread within networks, stealth features to avoid detection, and payload logic intended to manipulate control processes while feeding operators false signals. This combination made it unusually complex compared with typical worms of its era.

## Discovery and attribution

After it was detected and studied by security researchers in 2010, investigative reporting and later statements from officials linked the operation to state-level actors. The episode became a landmark case in debates about cyber operations, secrecy, and escalation risks.

## Broader impact

Stuxnet influenced how governments and companies thought about critical-infrastructure cybersecurity, particularly the security of industrial control systems. It also accelerated discussion of norms and legal frameworks for state behavior in cyberspace.
