## Summary
The Rwandan genocide was the mass slaughter of the Tutsi population by Hutu extremists from April to July 1994. Approximately 800,000 people were killed in 100 days, making it one of the fastest genocides in history. The international community's failure to intervene was widely condemned.

## Preconditions and escalation
The genocide is typically situated within a longer history of political violence and ethnicized identity in Rwanda, shaped by colonial-era policies, post-independence power struggles, and cycles of displacement. In the early 1990s, Rwanda experienced civil war between the government and the RPF, alongside negotiations (including the Arusha Accords) that aimed at power-sharing. The assassination of President Habyarimana on April 6, 1994 is widely cited as the immediate trigger that extremist networks used to launch coordinated mass violence.

## Organization and methods
While the genocide involved many forms of violence, high-level scholarship and reporting commonly emphasize several mechanisms:

- **Mobilization through state and local structures:** Administrative systems were used in many areas to identify targets and coordinate attacks.
- **Militia participation:** Groups such as the Interahamwe are frequently cited in accounts describing organized violence.
- **Incitement and propaganda:** Radio broadcasts and extremist media played a documented role in spreading dehumanizing messages and directing action.

## International response and aftermath
The genocide became a defining case in debates about international prevention and protection:

- **UN presence:** UNAMIR was deployed in Rwanda but was widely viewed as under-resourced and constrained by mandate and political will.
- **Justice and accountability:** The International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR) prosecuted senior figures, while Rwanda later used community-based gacaca courts for large numbers of cases.
- **Reconstruction and memory:** Post-genocide Rwanda pursued security consolidation, reconciliation policies, and annual commemorations; these efforts remain subjects of international analysis and debate.
