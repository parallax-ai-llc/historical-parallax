## Clarification

The commonly used name in historical and mathematical literature is **Srinivasa Ramanujan**. This article label appears to be a duplicate naming variant; the most complete biographical and mathematical overview is typically found under "Srinivasa Ramanujan".

## Biography (High Level)

Srinivasa Ramanujan (1887–1920) was an Indian mathematician whose intuitive and highly original results influenced number theory, analysis, infinite series, and continued fractions. Largely self-taught, he produced extensive notebooks of identities and conjectures, many of which were later proved or incorporated into mainstream mathematics.

## Collaboration with G. H. Hardy

In 1913 Ramanujan began correspondence with the British mathematician G. H. Hardy. Hardy recognized the depth of Ramanujan's work and helped bring him to Trinity College, Cambridge, where Ramanujan worked during World War I. Their collaboration produced a number of important papers and helped formalize and disseminate Ramanujan's results.

## Major Contributions

Ramanujan is associated with:

- **Partition theory**, including asymptotic formulas and congruences.
- **Modular forms** and what later became known as **mock theta functions**.
- Numerous identities for **q-series**, continued fractions, and special functions.

Many results attributed to Ramanujan were later rediscovered in his notebooks and in the "lost notebook" found decades after his death.

## Health and Death

Ramanujan's health deteriorated during his time in England and after his return to India. He died in 1920 at age 32. Despite his short life, his work continues to generate research across multiple areas of modern mathematics.
