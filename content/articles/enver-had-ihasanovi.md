## Summary

Bosnian military commander convicted by the ICTY of failing to prevent crimes committed by foreign mujahedin fighters under his command in central Bosnia. He commanded the ARBiH 3rd Corps.

## Additional Context

- **Bosnian War setting:** Hadžihasanović served as a senior officer in the **Army of the Republic of Bosnia and Herzegovina (ARBiH)** during the 1992–1995 conflict, a war marked by shifting alliances and atrocities committed by multiple parties.
- **3rd Corps responsibilities:** As commander of the **ARBiH 3rd Corps**, his area of responsibility included parts of **central Bosnia**, where fighting involved Bosnian government forces, Bosnian Croat forces, and other armed formations.
- **Foreign volunteer fighters:** The case is often discussed in connection with the presence of **foreign mujahedin/volunteer units** in Bosnia. The prosecution theory centered on whether commanders exercised effective control and took reasonable measures to prevent or punish crimes.
- **Command responsibility doctrine:** ICTY jurisprudence frequently relied on the idea that a commander can incur liability for **omissions** (failure to act) when they knew, or had reason to know, that subordinates were committing crimes and did not take necessary and reasonable steps.

## Significance

This prosecution is commonly referenced when summarizing:

- How international tribunals assess **effective control** over irregular or semi-autonomous units.
- The evidentiary challenges of linking senior commanders to crimes committed by dispersed fighters.
- The development of **command responsibility** standards applied to modern conflicts.
