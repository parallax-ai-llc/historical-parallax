## Background

Alfredo Beltrán Leyva (often known by the alias "El Mochomo") was a senior figure in Mexico’s organized-crime landscape during the period when large trafficking networks fragmented into competing factions. He is associated with the Beltrán-Leyva Organization, a group that previously operated in close alignment with the Sinaloa Cartel before a public rupture.

## Position within the Beltrán-Leyva Organization

The Beltrán-Leyva Organization functioned as a trafficking network involved in moving narcotics and coordinating logistics, protection, and corruption across multiple Mexican states. Accounts commonly describe Alfredo as one of the key family members involved in high-level operations during the organization’s rise.

## 2008 arrest and cartel split

Beltrán Leyva’s arrest in 2008 is widely cited as a catalyst for the Beltrán-Leyva faction’s break with the Sinaloa Cartel. The split contributed to a cycle of retaliatory violence and shifting alliances, with competing groups fighting for routes, local control, and revenue streams.

## Extradition and sentencing

After his capture and imprisonment in Mexico, Beltrán Leyva was extradited to the United States. In U.S. federal court, he was convicted and received a life sentence. Public reporting often frames this outcome as part of broader U.S.–Mexico efforts to prosecute major trafficking figures through extradition and federal racketeering and drug statutes.

## Wider context

The Beltrán-Leyva Organization’s conflicts with former allies and rivals are frequently discussed in analyses of Mexico’s post-2006 security environment, where leadership decapitation strategies sometimes accelerated organizational fragmentation and localized violence.
