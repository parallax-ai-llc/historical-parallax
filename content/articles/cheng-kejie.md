## Summary

Chinese politician who was vice chairman of the National People's Congress Standing Committee. He was the highest-ranking Chinese official executed for corruption, having accepted over $5 million in bribes. His execution shocked China.

## Background and offices

Cheng Kejie was a senior Chinese official who held high-ranking posts in the party-state system and later served as a vice chairman of the Standing Committee of the National People’s Congress. Prior to his national role, he was associated with regional leadership positions (including in Guangxi) in an era when local governments were increasingly involved in large-scale investment and development projects.

## Corruption case

Authorities accused Cheng of taking substantial bribes over a period of years. High-profile coverage of the case emphasized both the amount involved and Cheng’s seniority, presenting the prosecution as part of a broader anti-corruption push and a warning to other officials.

## Trial, sentencing, and execution

Cheng was convicted and sentenced to death, and he was executed in 2000. The case is frequently cited in discussions of China’s use of capital punishment for corruption offenses, and in analyses of how exemplary punishment is used to signal political resolve.

## Significance

Because of his position, Cheng’s downfall drew attention to corruption risks in the overlap between government authority, business interests, and development financing. Commentators have also noted the public messaging around the case, which combined legal condemnation with an emphasis on deterrence.

## Notes

Public discussion of the case often connects it to debates about deterrence versus due process in corruption enforcement. While the legal outcome is clear, many secondary accounts add narrative details that are not always sourced to primary court documents.
