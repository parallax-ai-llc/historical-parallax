## Summary

NKVD Major General and chief executioner who personally shot thousands of prisoners, including 7,000 Polish officers during the Katyn massacre in 28 days. Most prolific executioner in recorded history.

## Additional Context

## NKVD role

Blokhin served within the Soviet security apparatus (NKVD), an institution central to Stalin-era repression, including arrests, forced labor, and executions. Senior officials and specialized personnel carried out mass operations that targeted perceived political enemies and national groups.[^1]

## Mass execution practices

Historical accounts of Soviet mass executions describe an industrialized process: prisoners were transported, identified, executed in controlled settings, and buried in mass graves. Blokhin is frequently singled out in secondary sources as an executioner who personally performed large numbers of shootings, making his name emblematic of the system’s brutality.[^1]

## Katyn context

The Katyn massacre refers to the 1940 execution of Polish officers and other elites held by Soviet authorities. The episode remained highly contested internationally for decades and became a major historical and diplomatic issue between Poland and Russia/Soviet successor narratives.[^1]

## Aftermath and memory

As with many figures tied to state violence, details of Blokhin’s career and fate have been reconstructed from a combination of archival material and later historical writing. His reputation is primarily as a functionary of repression rather than a public political leader.[^1]
