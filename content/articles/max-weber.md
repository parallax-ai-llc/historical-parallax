## Summary

Max Weber (1864–1920) was a German sociologist, economist, and political scientist whose work fundamentally shaped the modern understanding of society, religion, capitalism, and bureaucracy. Writing at the turn of the twentieth century, he developed frameworks still central to social science, including the concepts of rationalization, ideal types, and the distinction between different forms of authority. His interdisciplinary reach made him one of the founding figures of sociology alongside Émile Durkheim and Karl Marx.

Weber's most enduring contribution, *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* (1905), argued that the religious values of Calvinism helped produce the cultural conditions for modern capitalism — a thesis that sparked debate for over a century. His insistence on value-free scholarship and interpretive social science (*Verstehen*) helped professionalize the discipline and distinguish it from natural science.

He died in 1920 at the age of 56, leaving several major works unfinished, yet his posthumously edited writings — especially *Economy and Society* — cemented his status as one of the most influential thinkers of the modern era.

## Early Life & Background

Max Weber was born on April 21, 1864, in Erfurt, Prussia (present-day Germany), the eldest of eight children. His father, Max Weber Sr., was a successful lawyer and National Liberal politician; his mother, Helene Fallenstein, was a devout Calvinist with strong moral convictions. This tension between worldly ambition and religious conscience would animate much of his later scholarship.

Weber studied law at the University of Heidelberg beginning in 1882, then continued at the University of Berlin, where he earned his doctorate in 1889 with a dissertation on medieval trading companies. He completed his habilitation in 1891 on Roman agrarian history, establishing himself as both a legal historian and economist.

## Rise to Prominence

Weber's academic career accelerated rapidly. In 1894, at age 29, he was appointed professor of political economy at the University of Freiburg, and in 1896 moved to a chair at Heidelberg. In 1897, however, he suffered a severe psychological breakdown following a confrontation with his father, who died shortly afterward. Weber spent nearly five years largely unable to work or write.

He gradually recovered and returned to scholarship around 1902, producing some of his most influential works in the years that followed. He co-founded the German Sociological Association in 1909 and played a central role in shaping German academic social science.

## Key Achievements

- **"The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism" (1905):** Linked Calvinist asceticism to the development of rational capitalism in the West.
- **Theory of Authority:** Distinguished three ideal types — traditional, charismatic, and rational-legal — still foundational in political science and organizational theory.
- **"Politics as a Vocation" and "Science as a Vocation" (1919):** Landmark lectures on the ethics of political life and scholarly objectivity.
- **"Economy and Society" (posthumous, 1922):** A systematic treatise on sociology, religion, law, and domination, edited by his wife Marianne Weber.
- **Bureaucracy theory:** His analysis of modern bureaucracy as rule-bound, hierarchical, and depersonalized remains the standard sociological account.

## Controversies & Criticism

Weber's Protestant Ethic thesis has been contested since its publication. Historians argue he oversimplified the relationship between theology and economic behavior, and that capitalism flourished in Catholic regions too. Scholars such as R. H. Tawney and Kurt Samuelsson produced detailed rebuttals.

His concept of value-free social science has also drawn criticism as an impossible ideal — critics argue that selection of research topics is itself value-laden. Additionally, some of his writings on race and nation reflect assumptions common to his era that later scholars have rightly challenged.

## Legacy & Influence

Weber's frameworks permeate contemporary sociology, political science, organizational theory, and religious studies. His ideal-type methodology and the concept of legitimacy remain essential analytical tools. Figures as varied as Talcott Parsons, Jürgen Habermas, and Anthony Giddens built directly on his foundations.

He has also shaped political practice: his analysis of charismatic authority anticipated twentieth-century movements around transformational leadership. His death on June 14, 1920 — from pneumonia during the Spanish flu pandemic — cut short a career still in full productive flow, making his completed legacy all the more remarkable.
