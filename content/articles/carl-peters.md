## Summary

German colonial administrator in East Africa known for extreme brutality who hanged his African mistress and her lover. Known as 'Mkono wa Damu' (Bloody Hand) by locals.

## Early Career

Carl Peters was trained as a German academic and political activist before turning to colonial projects during the late nineteenth-century European "Scramble for Africa." He helped organize expeditions and treaty-making efforts that were later used to justify German claims in East Africa.

## Role in German East Africa

Peters became associated with the German East Africa Company and later served in administrative roles tied to German imperial rule. His period in the region is widely described as marked by coercion, punitive expeditions, and severe violence against local populations.

## Abuses and Official Proceedings

Public controversy and internal German inquiries in the 1890s addressed allegations of brutality and unlawful killings connected to Peters’ actions. He was ultimately removed from official position, and his case became an example used in debates about colonial governance and accountability.

## Reputation and Later Memory

Peters’ image shifted over time: while criticized by contemporaries and historians for colonial violence, he was also celebrated by some nationalist circles. In the twentieth century, Nazi-era propaganda promoted him as a colonial “hero,” a portrayal later challenged in postwar scholarship and memorial debates.

## Historical Significance

Peters is frequently cited in studies of colonial atrocity, the legal ambiguity of corporate-imperial rule, and the way metropolitan politics shaped (and often failed to constrain) violence on colonial frontiers.
