## Summary

Partner of William Burke in the Edinburgh murder-for-anatomy scheme, escaped prosecution by turning king's evidence.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|

| 1828 | Participated with William Burke in murders committed to supply bodies for anatomical dissection |

## Background

- Hare was Irish; sources differ on his exact birth year (often given as **1792–1804**).
- Reporting on his early life is limited; accounts commonly describe him as an agricultural labourer before arriving in Britain.
- Before the murders, Hare worked on the **Union Canal** project and later in Edinburgh as a **coal man’s assistant**.

## The Burke and Hare Murders (1828)

- In early 19th-century Edinburgh, medical schools had high demand for cadavers for anatomy teaching, while legal supply was limited.
- Burke and Hare began by selling the body of a lodger who died at Hare’s house to anatomist **Robert Knox**.
- The scheme escalated into a series of killings; the pair are widely credited with **sixteen murders** over roughly ten months in 1828.
- Victims were typically suffocated (a method later associated with the term "burking" in popular usage), leaving fewer obvious marks.

## Investigation and Trial

- The case broke after other lodgers discovered the final known victim, **Margaret Docherty**, and alerted authorities.
- Hare accepted an offer of **immunity** in exchange for testimony, which secured a conviction of Burke.
- Burke was executed and his body was publicly dissected—an outcome that underscored public outrage and the anatomy-supply crisis.

## Later Life

- After testifying, Hare disappeared from public view; historical sources generally describe his later whereabouts and death as **unknown**.

## Legacy

- The murders became a major scandal and contributed to pressure for reform.
- The broader context—grave robbing, shortage of legal cadavers, and the Burke/Hare case—is frequently cited among the factors leading to the UK **Anatomy Act 1832**.
