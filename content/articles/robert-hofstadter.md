## Summary

Robert Hofstadter shared the 1961 Nobel Prize for pioneering studies of electron scattering from atomic nuclei.

## Scientific Work

- **Electron scattering as a probe**: Hofstadter helped demonstrate how high-energy electron beams could be used to map charge distributions inside atomic nuclei.
- **Nuclear structure measurements**: His experiments provided quantitative information about nuclear size and shape, supporting a more precise understanding of nuclear matter.
- **Methodological influence**: The approach helped set standards for using scattering experiments to infer subatomic structure, a template later applied across particle and nuclear physics.

## Broader Significance

- Precise measurements of nuclear form factors contributed to the development and testing of theoretical models of the nucleus.
- His Nobel-recognized work is often framed as part of the mid-20th-century transition to more instrument-driven, high-energy experimental physics.

## Legacy

- Hofstadter's results are frequently referenced in discussions of how experimental constraints (resolution, beam energy, detectors) shape what can be learned about unseen structure.

## Key Takeaways

- Scattering experiments convert "invisible" structure into measurable patterns, letting physicists infer spatial distributions from data.
- Hofstadter's work helped normalize the idea that improved resolution (higher energies, better detectors) can unlock qualitatively new understanding.

## Additional Note

- Nobel-recognized electron scattering work is often paired in historical summaries with parallel advances in accelerator technology and detector instrumentation.
