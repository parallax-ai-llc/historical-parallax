## Summary

Queen Elizabeth II (21 April 1926 – 8 September 2022) was the longest-reigning British monarch in history, serving as Queen of the United Kingdom and Head of the Commonwealth for 70 years. Ascending to the throne in 1952 at the age of 25, she presided over one of the most transformative periods in modern history, guiding the monarchy through decolonisation, social upheaval, and the decline of empire while maintaining its institutional relevance.

Her reign spanned the terms of 15 British Prime Ministers, from Winston Churchill to Liz Truss, and witnessed seismic global events including the Cold War, the Troubles in Northern Ireland, and the digital revolution. Known for her sense of duty, political neutrality, and personal restraint, she became a symbol of continuity and stability for millions across Britain and the Commonwealth's 56 member nations.

## Early Life & Background

Born Elizabeth Alexandra Mary Windsor at 17 Bruton Street, London, on 21 April 1926, she was the first child of Prince Albert, Duke of York, and Lady Elizabeth Bowes-Lyon. She was not originally expected to become queen. Her early education was private, supervised by her mother and governess Marion Crawford. She studied constitutional history under Henry Marten, Vice-Provost of Eton.

Her path to the throne shifted dramatically in December 1936 when her uncle King Edward VIII abdicated to marry American divorcée Wallis Simpson, making her father King George VI and Elizabeth the heir presumptive. During World War II she remained in Britain, serving in the Auxiliary Territorial Service in 1945 as a driver and mechanic — the first female member of the royal family to serve in the armed forces.

## Rise to Prominence

Elizabeth married Prince Philip Mountbatten on 20 November 1947 at Westminster Abbey. They had four children: Charles (1948), Anne (1950), Andrew (1960), and Edward (1964). Following her father's death on 6 February 1952, she acceded to the throne at age 25. Her coronation on 2 June 1953 was the first to be televised, watched by an estimated 27 million people in the UK alone.

Her early reign coincided with Britain's retreat from empire. She oversaw the transformation of the British Empire into the Commonwealth of Nations, which she championed throughout her life as a voluntary association of equal sovereign states.

## Key Achievements

- Reigned for **70 years and 214 days**, surpassing Queen Victoria's record in September 2015.
- Delivered **Christmas broadcasts** every year from 1952 to 2021, bar 1969.
- Hosted **state visits** from over 100 heads of state and made more than 260 official overseas visits.
- Awarded the **George Cross** to Malta (1942), Gibraltar (1943), and the Royal Ulster Constabulary (1999) during her reign.
- Signed the **Commonwealth Charter** in March 2013, reaffirming values of democracy and human rights across member nations.

## Controversies & Criticism

The monarchy faced significant public criticism following the death of Diana, Princess of Wales, on 31 August 1997. The Queen's delayed return to London and the palace's initial silence drew widespread condemnation. She ultimately addressed the nation on 5 September 1997, breaking with protocol.

Her reign also attracted scrutiny over the royal family's handling of Prince Andrew's association with convicted sex offender Jeffrey Epstein, leading to Andrew's withdrawal from public duties in 2019. The 2021 Oprah Winfrey interview given by Prince Harry and Meghan, Duchess of Sussex, raised allegations of racism and institutional indifference within the palace, which the Queen acknowledged required private resolution.

Critics have also pointed to the monarchy's accumulated wealth — including the Crown Estate and private holdings — as an ongoing debate about the institution's place in a modern democracy.

## Legacy & Influence

Queen Elizabeth II died at Balmoral Castle, Scotland, on 8 September 2022, aged 96. She was succeeded by her eldest son, King Charles III. Her state funeral on 19 September 2022 was attended by approximately 2,000 dignitaries from around the world and watched by an estimated global television audience of 4 billion.

Her legacy is one of steadfast institutional service. She modernised the monarchy incrementally, opening Buckingham Palace to tourists (1993), paying voluntary income tax from 1992, and adapting royal communication to include social media. She remains the most widely recognised British figure of the 20th and early 21st centuries, and her portrait appeared on the currency of more than 30 countries during her reign.
