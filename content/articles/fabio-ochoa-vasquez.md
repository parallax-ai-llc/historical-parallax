## Summary

Colombian drug lord and youngest of the Ochoa brothers in the Medellin Cartel. He was extradited to the US and sentenced to 30 years.

## Background

- The Ochoa family became prominent in Colombia during the rise of large-scale cocaine trafficking in the late 1970s and 1980s.
- Fabio Ochoa Vásquez was one of the Ochoa brothers associated with the Medellín Cartel, a group that played a central role in the cocaine trade and related violence.

## Role and Prosecution (High-Level)

- Authorities accused cartel leadership and affiliates of organizing production, transport, and distribution networks to move cocaine into the United States.
- Colombian and U.S. law enforcement pursued major trafficking figures through a mix of domestic prosecutions, cooperation agreements, and extradition requests.
- Ochoa was extradited to the United States and received a lengthy federal sentence.

## Broader Context

- The Medellín Cartel era is remembered for intense conflict between traffickers, the state, and rival organizations, as well as political violence and corruption.
- The period also influenced Colombia’s legal and security policy, including debates over extradition and international cooperation.

## Legacy

- High-profile cartel prosecutions became a symbol of changing pressure on transnational drug networks.
- The Ochoa name remains closely tied to the history of the Medellín Cartel and its impact on Colombia and U.S. drug policy.
- The case is often referenced in discussions of extradition policy as a tool against organized crime.
