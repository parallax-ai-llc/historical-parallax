## Summary

Dorgon (1612-1650) was a Manchu prince and regent of the early Qing Dynasty who played a pivotal role in the Manchu conquest of China. As the 14th son of Nurhaci, the founder of the Qing Dynasty, Dorgon served as regent for his young nephew, the Shunzhi Emperor, from 1643 to 1650. He led the Manchu armies through the Shanhai Pass in 1644, establishing Qing control over China proper and ending the Ming Dynasty. Despite his military brilliance and political acumen, his autocratic rule and alleged ambitions to usurp the throne made him a controversial figure whose legacy oscillated between veneration and condemnation for centuries after his death.

---

## Early Life and Background

Dorgon was born on November 17, 1612, in the Manchu homeland of what is now northeastern China. He was the 14th son of Nurhaci (1559-1626), the chieftain who unified the Jurchen tribes and founded what would become the Qing Dynasty. His mother was Lady Abahai of the Ulanara clan, one of Nurhaci's most favored consorts. Dorgon's name in Manchu means "badger," reflecting the traditional naming practices of the Jurchen people.

Dorgon grew up during a period of intense military expansion and political consolidation. His father Nurhaci had established the Eight Banners system, a military and social organization that would become the backbone of Qing power. From a young age, Dorgon was trained in military tactics, horseback riding, archery, and the arts of war that were essential to Manchu culture. He also received education in Chinese classical texts, as Nurhaci recognized the importance of understanding Chinese civilization for the eventual conquest of China proper.

The young prince showed exceptional talent in both military and political affairs. He was noted for his intelligence, strategic thinking, and charismatic personality. When his father died in 1626, Dorgon was only 14 years old, but he had already participated in several military campaigns. His mother Lady Abahai was forced to commit suicide following Nurhaci's death, according to the Manchu custom of following one's husband in death, though historical sources suggest this may have been orchestrated by political rivals. This traumatic event left young Dorgon and his full brothers in a vulnerable position within the complex politics of the Manchu court.

Dorgon's early experiences shaped his political consciousness and survival instincts. He witnessed firsthand the brutal power struggles among the Manchu nobility and learned to navigate the treacherous waters of court intrigue. Under the reign of his half-brother Hong Taiji (r. 1626-1643), who succeeded Nurhaci, Dorgon distinguished himself as a capable military commander, leading successful campaigns against the Ming Dynasty and Korea.

## Rise to Power

Dorgon's ascent to supreme power began with the sudden death of Hong Taiji in September 1643. The succession crisis that followed would determine the fate of the Manchu state and, ultimately, China itself. Hong Taiji had not clearly designated an heir, leading to intense competition among his brothers and sons. The two main factions supported either Hooge, Hong Taiji's eldest son, or Dorgon himself, who had emerged as the most capable of Hong Taiji's surviving brothers.

The succession struggle was resolved through a political compromise. Dorgon, recognizing that claiming the throne himself might trigger civil war, supported the candidacy of Hong Taiji's ninth son, the six-year-old Fulin, who would become the Shunzhi Emperor. In exchange for this support, Dorgon was appointed as one of two regents, sharing power initially with his brother Jirgalang. However, Dorgon quickly maneuvered to consolidate his authority, marginalizing Jirgalang and assuming the title of "Uncle Prince Regent" (Shufu Shezheng Wang).

The year 1644 proved to be the turning point not only for Dorgon's career but for Chinese history. The Ming Dynasty, weakened by peasant rebellions, economic crisis, and military defeats, was on the verge of collapse. In April 1644, the rebel leader Li Zicheng captured Beijing, and the last Ming emperor, the Chongzhen Emperor, committed suicide. Ming general Wu Sangui, stationed at the strategic Shanhai Pass that guarded the entrance from Manchuria to China proper, faced a critical decision: submit to the rebel Li Zicheng or ally with the Manchus.

Dorgon, demonstrating remarkable political acumen, convinced Wu Sangui to open the pass to Manchu forces by promising to avenge the Ming Dynasty and restore order to China. On May 27, 1644, Dorgon led the Manchu armies through the Shanhai Pass. In the subsequent Battle of Shanhai Pass, the combined forces of the Manchus and Wu Sangui defeated Li Zicheng's army. Within weeks, Dorgon's forces occupied Beijing, and on October 30, 1644, the young Shunzhi Emperor entered the city, establishing the Qing Dynasty's rule over China proper.

Dorgon's strategic brilliance lay in his ability to present the Manchu conquest not as a foreign invasion but as a restoration of order and civilization. He portrayed the Qing as the legitimate successors to the Ming, coming to rescue China from banditry and chaos. This narrative, combined with his willingness to employ Han Chinese officials and generals, facilitated the Manchu conquest of the rest of China over the following decades.

## Major Achievements

Dorgon's seven years as regent (1643-1650) were marked by extraordinary political and military accomplishments that laid the foundation for the Qing Dynasty's nearly three-century rule over China. His achievements can be categorized into military conquests, administrative reforms, and political consolidation.

**Military Conquests and Strategic Expansion**

Under Dorgon's command, the Qing forces rapidly expanded southward, conquering vast territories that had been under Ming control. While the capture of Beijing in 1644 was the symbolic beginning of Qing rule, most of China remained unconquered. Dorgon directed multiple military campaigns to subdue Ming loyalist resistance in southern China. By the time of his death in 1650, Qing forces had conquered most of northern China and were making significant inroads into the south. His military strategies combined overwhelming force with political inducements, offering amnesty and positions to Ming officials who surrendered.

Dorgon personally led several major campaigns, demonstrating his courage and tactical brilliance. His ability to coordinate multiple armies across vast distances and maintain supply lines was remarkable for the era. He also successfully integrated Ming defectors and their troops into the Qing military structure, particularly the former Ming general Wu Sangui, who would become one of the most important Qing commanders in the conquest of southwestern China.

**Administrative and Political Reforms**

Recognizing that military conquest alone could not sustain Qing rule, Dorgon implemented a series of administrative reforms designed to legitimize Manchu authority and create a functional government. He largely preserved the Ming bureaucratic structure, maintaining the six ministries, the examination system, and the provincial administration. This continuity helped reassure the Han Chinese elite and facilitated the transition of power.

Dorgon established the principle of Manchu-Han dyarchy, whereby major government positions were filled by both a Manchu and a Han Chinese official. This system, which would characterize Qing administration throughout the dynasty, served multiple purposes: it gave Han Chinese a stake in the new regime, provided experienced administrators, and ensured Manchu oversight of government functions. He also moved quickly to restore the civil service examination system, which had been disrupted during the late Ming chaos, thereby earning the support of the scholar-official class.

One of Dorgon's most controversial policies was the "Queue Order" issued in 1645, which required all Han Chinese men to adopt the Manchu hairstyle—shaving the front of the head and wearing the remaining hair in a long braid (queue). This policy, enforced with the slogan "Keep your hair and lose your head, or keep your head and cut your hair," provoked widespread resistance and became a symbol of foreign domination. Despite the backlash, Dorgon insisted on this policy as a test of loyalty and a visible marker of submission to Qing authority.

**Economic and Social Policies**

Dorgon recognized the importance of economic stability for consolidating Qing rule. He implemented tax reforms aimed at reducing the burden on peasants, who had suffered greatly during the late Ming period. He ordered land surveys to establish a fair tax base and attempted to curb the corruption that had plagued Ming administration. These measures, though imperfectly implemented, helped restore agricultural production and win popular support in some areas.

He also addressed the land distribution issue by confiscating lands from Ming loyalists and distributing them to Manchu bannermen and loyal Han Chinese. While this created a new landed elite loyal to the Qing, it also dispossessed many and created long-term social tensions. Dorgon attempted to balance the interests of Manchu conquerors, who expected rewards for their service, with the need to maintain social stability among the vastly more numerous Han Chinese population.

**Cultural and Religious Policies**

In religious matters, Dorgon demonstrated pragmatism and relative tolerance. He patronized Buddhism and allowed the continuation of Confucian rituals and ceremonies, recognizing their importance to Han Chinese culture. He permitted Jesuit missionaries to remain in China and even employed some of them in technical positions, particularly in astronomy and calendar-making. This tolerance was partly strategic—he sought to avoid alienating any group that might support the regime—but also reflected the Manchu tradition of religious pluralism.

Dorgon also invested in cultural projects, including the compilation of histories and the preservation of Ming archives. These efforts were designed to demonstrate the Qing Dynasty's commitment to Chinese cultural traditions and to legitimize their rule as a continuation of Chinese civilization rather than its disruption.

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his achievements, Dorgon remains one of the most controversial figures in Chinese history, with criticisms ranging from his autocratic rule to alleged personal misconduct and political ambitions that threatened dynastic stability.

**Autocratic Rule and Power Accumulation**

Dorgon's tenure as regent was marked by an increasingly autocratic exercise of power. He systematically eliminated potential rivals and concentrated authority in his own hands. His co-regent and brother Jirgalang was progressively sidelined until he held the title in name only. Dorgon accumulated numerous honorific titles, including "Imperial Father Regent Prince" (Huangfu Shezheng Wang), a title that suggested authority equal to or even greater than the emperor himself. Critics charged that he had effectively usurped imperial power while maintaining the fiction of serving as regent for the young Shunzhi Emperor.

He dealt ruthlessly with opposition from within the Manchu nobility. Several princes who questioned his authority were stripped of their titles, imprisoned, or forced into retirement. This created resentment among the Manchu elite, who felt that Dorgon was violating the collective leadership traditions that had characterized early Manchu governance. Some historians argue that his concentration of power, while effective in the short term, planted the seeds for the factional conflicts that would trouble the Qing court in later years.

**The Queue Order and Cultural Suppression**

The enforcement of the queue hairstyle remains one of Dorgon's most criticized policies. The requirement that Han Chinese men adopt Manchu hairstyles was seen as a humiliating symbol of subjugation. The policy triggered widespread resistance and several major rebellions, leading to massacres in cities like Jiangyin and Jiading, where tens of thousands were killed for refusing to comply. Critics argue that this policy was unnecessarily provocative and that it prolonged resistance to Qing rule, costing countless lives.

More broadly, Dorgon's policies represented a tension between sinification (adapting to Chinese culture) and maintaining Manchu identity. While he employed Han Chinese officials and preserved many Chinese institutions, he also insisted on visible markers of Manchu dominance. This ambivalence created a system in which Han Chinese were simultaneously incorporated and subordinated, leading to long-term ethnic tensions.

**Alleged Relationship with Empress Dowager Xiaozhuang**

One of the most scandalous allegations against Dorgon concerns his rumored relationship with Empress Dowager Xiaozhuang, the mother of the Shunzhi Emperor and widow of Hong Taiji. Historical sources, including some Korean diplomatic records, suggest that Dorgon may have married or cohabited with the empress dowager, which would have been a serious violation of Confucian propriety and Manchu custom. While the evidence is inconclusive and the matter remains debated among historians, the rumors themselves damaged Dorgon's reputation and provided ammunition for his enemies.

Some scholars argue that this alleged relationship, if it occurred, was a political arrangement designed to legitimize Dorgon's regency and strengthen his position. Others view it as evidence of Dorgon's personal ambition and disregard for traditional morality. The controversy reflects broader anxieties about Dorgon's power and intentions during his regency.

**Excessive Purges and Literary Inquisition**

Dorgon initiated what some historians characterize as a reign of terror against Ming loyalists and political opponents. The literary inquisition, which would become a characteristic feature of Qing rule, began under his regency. Scholars and officials suspected of harboring anti-Qing sentiments were persecuted, and books deemed seditious were banned and destroyed. While defenders argue these measures were necessary for consolidating a fragile new dynasty, critics see them as evidence of tyranny and cultural repression.

The treatment of surrendered Ming officials was often harsh and unpredictable. While some were welcomed into Qing service, others were executed on dubious pretexts, creating an atmosphere of fear and uncertainty. This inconsistency suggested that Dorgon's policies were driven more by personal power considerations than by coherent principles of governance.

**Economic Exploitation and Land Confiscation**

Dorgon's land redistribution policies, while rewarding Qing supporters, involved massive confiscations from landowners suspected of Ming loyalism. Entire communities were sometimes displaced to provide lands for Manchu bannermen. The enclosure of lands for Manchu use around Beijing created particular hardship and resentment. Critics argue that these policies prioritized the interests of the Manchu military elite over the welfare of the general population and created economic instability in critical regions.

## Personal Life

Dorgon's personal life was complex and, in many ways, tragic. He was married multiple times, following the Manchu aristocratic practice of maintaining multiple wives and consorts. Historical records indicate he had at least six principal wives and numerous secondary consorts, though the exact number is uncertain. Despite these marriages, Dorgon died without a surviving male heir, a significant failure for a Manchu prince given the importance of lineage and succession.

His primary consort was Lady Borjigit, a Mongol princess from the Khorchin tribe, reflecting the Qing Dynasty's diplomatic marriage alliances with Mongol nobility. This marriage was politically motivated, designed to secure Mongol support for the Qing conquest of China. Dorgon's other wives came from prominent Manchu families, serving to strengthen alliances within the Manchu elite.

Contemporary accounts describe Dorgon as physically imposing and charismatic, with exceptional skills in horseback riding, archery, and hunting—accomplishments highly valued in Manchu culture. He was known for his intelligence and quick decision-making, traits that served him well in both military campaigns and political maneuvering. However, he was also described as ruthless, cunning, and ambitious, willing to eliminate rivals and suppress opposition without hesitation.

Dorgon's personality reflected the contradictions of his position. He could be generous and magnanimous to those who served him loyally, rewarding followers with titles, lands, and positions. Yet he could also be cold and calculating, sacrificing individuals and principles when political necessity demanded. This pragmatism, while effective in achieving his goals, left him with few genuine friends and many hidden enemies who waited for opportunities to strike against him.

His relationship with his nephew, the Shunzhi Emperor, appears to have been complex and strained. As the emperor matured, he increasingly resented Dorgon's dominance and the limitations placed on imperial authority. Historical sources suggest tension between the two, though Dorgon maintained firm control throughout his regency. The young emperor's inability to exercise real power until Dorgon's death created a resentment that would have significant consequences for Dorgon's posthumous reputation.

Dorgon was reportedly devoted to hunting and military exercises, spending considerable time in these pursuits even during his regency. In December 1650, while on a hunting trip in the Khalkha region of Mongolia, he died suddenly at the age of 38. The exact cause of death remains uncertain—some sources suggest he fell from his horse, others that he died of illness. The suspicious circumstances of his death so soon after reaching the pinnacle of power have led some historians to speculate about possible assassination, though no definitive evidence supports this theory.

## Legacy

Dorgon's legacy is among the most contested in Chinese history, with his reputation undergoing dramatic reversals that reflect changing political circumstances and historiographical approaches. His impact on Chinese history is undeniable—he was the principal architect of the Manchu conquest of China and established many of the institutions and policies that would characterize Qing rule for nearly three centuries. Yet evaluations of his character and achievements have varied wildly across time and perspective.

**Immediate Posthumous Persecution**

Within months of Dorgon's death, his enemies moved swiftly to destroy his reputation and erase his accomplishments. The Shunzhi Emperor, finally free from his uncle's domination, was persuaded by Dorgon's rivals to condemn him posthumously. In 1651, Dorgon was accused of crimes including harboring imperial ambitions, conducting an improper relationship with the empress dowager, and various abuses of power. His titles and honors were stripped, his image was removed from the imperial ancestral temple, and his corpse was reportedly exhumed and mutilated—an extreme form of posthumous punishment in Chinese tradition.

Dorgon's followers and family members were purged from positions of power. His brothers and allies were demoted, imprisoned, or executed. This systematic destruction of his legacy was both a political purge—removing potential threats to the young emperor's authority—and a moral condemnation designed to delegitimize his regency and justify the new power structure. For several decades, Dorgon's name became synonymous with treachery and ambition in official histories.

**Rehabilitation and Recognition**

The pendulum swung back in 1778 during the reign of the Qianlong Emperor, one of the most successful Qing rulers. Recognizing Dorgon's crucial role in establishing the dynasty, the Qianlong Emperor issued an edict rehabilitating Dorgon's reputation. His titles and honors were posthumously restored, and he was officially recognized as a founding hero of the Qing Dynasty. This rehabilitation reflected both the Qianlong Emperor's historical consciousness and the dynasty's confidence after more than a century of successful rule—it could now afford to acknowledge even controversial figures from its founding period.

The rehabilitation established Dorgon as one of the great statesmen and military leaders in Chinese history. Traditional Chinese historiography from the later Qing period generally portrayed him favorably as a brilliant strategist and capable administrator who, despite personal flaws and political mistakes, successfully guided the Manchu conquest of China and established the institutions that enabled Qing prosperity.

**Modern Reassessments**

In the 20th and 21st centuries, Dorgon's legacy has been reassessed through various ideological and methodological lenses. Nationalist Chinese historians, particularly in the early 20th century and in Taiwan, often portrayed him negatively as a foreign conqueror who subjugated China and imposed humiliating policies like the queue order. This perspective emphasized the ethnic dimension of the Manchu conquest and viewed Dorgon as a symbol of foreign domination.

Conversely, historians in the People's Republic of China, especially after the 1980s, have generally adopted a more balanced view. Recognizing China as a multi-ethnic nation, PRC historiography has emphasized Dorgon's role in unifying China, ending the chaos of the late Ming period, and establishing a dynasty that incorporated diverse ethnic groups. This interpretation sees Dorgon as a Chinese historical figure—albeit one of Manchu ethnicity—rather than a foreign invader.

Contemporary scholarship has produced increasingly nuanced analyses of Dorgon's legacy. Historians now recognize the complexity of the Manchu conquest, acknowledging both the violence and cultural suppression it entailed and the political stability and territorial expansion it ultimately achieved. Dorgon is understood as a product of his time—a skilled practitioner of early modern statecraft operating in an era when military conquest and authoritarian rule were normative political strategies.

**Enduring Impact**

Dorgon's most enduring legacy lies in the institutional framework he established for Qing governance. The Manchu-Han dyarchy, the incorporation of Ming administrative structures, the strategic use of Han Chinese collaborators, and the balance between Manchu identity and Chinese cultural adaptation—all these elements, which would characterize Qing rule throughout its history, were largely established during Dorgon's regency. His pragmatic approach to conquest and governance created a template that subsequent Qing rulers would follow and refine.

His military strategies and political tactics have been studied by Chinese military theorists and historians as examples of successful conquest and consolidation. His ability to turn potential enemies into allies, to present conquest as liberation, and to rapidly establish functioning government in conquered territories demonstrated strategic brilliance that transcended his particular historical moment.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and historians who view Dorgon favorably emphasize his extraordinary political and military abilities, his pragmatic governance, and his essential role in establishing one of China's most successful dynasties. These defenders argue that his achievements must be evaluated in the context of his time rather than judged by modern standards of democracy and human rights.

Dorgon's military genius is widely acknowledged even by his critics. His strategic vision in recognizing the opportunity presented by the Ming Dynasty's collapse, his successful negotiation with Wu Sangui to gain entry to China proper, and his subsequent military campaigns demonstrated exceptional strategic and tactical abilities. Military historians point to his coordination of multiple armies across vast distances, his integration of former Ming forces into Qing military structures, and his ability to adapt Manchu cavalry tactics to the different terrains and opponents encountered in China proper.

His political acumen in managing the transition from conquest to governance is seen as equally impressive. Qing historian Frederic Wakeman Jr. argued that Dorgon's policies of compromise and incorporation—preserving Ming institutions while asserting Manchu supremacy, employing Han Chinese officials while maintaining Manchu oversight—created a stable foundation for Qing rule. Rather than imposing wholesale replacement of Chinese institutions with Manchu ones, Dorgon crafted a hybrid system that proved remarkably durable and effective.

Defenders point out that many of Dorgon's controversial policies, including the queue order, while harsh, were consistent with the political logic of establishing a new dynasty's authority. In the context of 17th-century political culture, where displays of submission and loyalty were essential to legitimate rule, such requirements were not exceptional. Some scholars argue that the queue order, despite its initial unpopularity, eventually became a unifying symbol that helped integrate Manchus and Han Chinese under a common political system.

The rehabilitation of Dorgon's reputation by the Qianlong Emperor in 1778 is cited as evidence that even subsequent Qing rulers, with the benefit of historical perspective, recognized his indispensable contributions to the dynasty. The Qianlong Emperor's edict acknowledged that without Dorgon's leadership during the critical years of 1643-1650, the Qing conquest might have failed or taken a very different course, potentially resulting in prolonged civil war and fragmentation.

Modern Chinese historians like Dai Yi have portrayed Dorgon as a skilled statesman who navigated the complex challenges of conquest and governance with remarkable success. They emphasize his efforts to restore order after the chaos of the late Ming period, his policies to reduce the tax burden on peasants, and his patronage of Chinese cultural traditions. This perspective views Dorgon as a unifying figure who, despite his Manchu ethnicity, worked to create a stable political order that benefited people of all ethnic backgrounds within the empire.

Some scholars have also defended Dorgon's personal character against the more scandalous allegations. They argue that many of the charges against him—including the alleged relationship with the empress dowager—were fabrications by his political enemies after his death, when he was no longer able to defend himself. These historians point to the lack of concrete contemporary evidence for many accusations and suggest that posthumous condemnations often reveal more about political struggles following his death than about his actual conduct.

### Negative Views

Critics of Dorgon emphasize his ruthless authoritarianism, his cultural suppression of the Han Chinese, and his alleged personal ambitions to usurp the throne. These negative assessments highlight the violence and suffering that accompanied the Manchu conquest and Dorgon's personal role in orchestrating and perpetuating that violence.

The queue order remains the most symbolically powerful criticism of Dorgon's rule. Historian Jonathan Spence and others have documented the massacres that resulted from resistance to this policy, with tens of thousands killed in cities like Jiangyin, where residents held out for 81 days before being massacred by Qing forces. Critics argue that this policy was unnecessary for establishing Qing authority and that Dorgon's insistence on it revealed a cultural chauvinism and cruelty that belied claims of benevolent rule. The slogan "Keep your hair and lose your head" became a symbol of oppression that poisoned Manchu-Han relations for generations.

Dorgon's autocratic accumulation of power and his treatment of potential rivals within the Manchu nobility is seen as a betrayal of the collective leadership traditions that had characterized early Manchu governance. By concentrating power in his own hands and eliminating checks on his authority, critics argue, he transformed the regency from a temporary arrangement into a personal dictatorship. The title "Imperial Father Regent Prince" suggested pretensions to imperial status that violated both his duty to the young emperor and Manchu political traditions.

The literary inquisition and persecution of intellectuals that began under Dorgon's regency is viewed as the foundation for later Qing cultural repression. Critics argue that Dorgon's policies created an atmosphere of fear that stifled intellectual creativity and political discourse, establishing patterns of thought control that would characterize the Qing Dynasty throughout its history. This cultural repression is seen as particularly damaging because it targeted the scholar-official class that had been the carriers of Chinese civilization.

Nationalist Chinese historians have portrayed Dorgon as a foreign conqueror whose conquest represented a catastrophe for Chinese civilization. This perspective emphasizes the ethnic and cultural dimensions of the Manchu conquest, viewing it as a colonial subjugation rather than a dynastic transition within Chinese history. From this viewpoint, Dorgon's policies—particularly the queue order—were designed to humiliate and subjugate the Han Chinese, and his administrative arrangements were intended to maintain Manchu dominance over a conquered population.

The allegations regarding Dorgon's relationship with the empress dowager, while historically uncertain, remain a significant element in negative assessments of his character. Whether or not the relationship actually occurred, the scandal reflects broader concerns about Dorgon's respect for propriety and his ambitions. Critics argue that his accumulation of titles and power, his treatment of the young Shunzhi Emperor, and his conduct of state affairs all suggested a man who had effectively usurped imperial authority and might have formally claimed the throne had he lived longer.

Some historians have criticized Dorgon's land confiscation policies as economically destructive and socially unjust. The massive redistribution of land to Manchu bannermen displaced Han Chinese landowners and disrupted agricultural production. While defenders argue these policies were necessary to reward Qing supporters, critics see them as ethnic favoritism that created long-term economic inefficiency and social resentment.

Finally, Dorgon's legacy has been criticized for establishing patterns of ethnic inequality and authoritarian governance that would persist throughout the Qing Dynasty. While the dynasty eventually achieved stability and prosperity, critics argue this came despite rather than because of Dorgon's policies, and that his emphasis on Manchu supremacy and cultural suppression created fundamental contradictions within the Qing system that would contribute to its eventual collapse in 1911.

## Famous Quotes

> "The empire is won on horseback, but it cannot be governed from horseback." - Context: Attributed to Dorgon when arguing for the preservation of Ming administrative structures and the civil service examination system, demonstrating his understanding that military conquest required transition to civilian governance.[^1]

> "Keep your hair and lose your head, or keep your head and cut your hair." - Context: The infamous slogan enforcing the Queue Order of 1645, representing Dorgon's uncompromising approach to enforcing Manchu cultural dominance and testing loyalty to the new dynasty.[^2]

> "We come not as conquerors but as liberators, to restore order and end the banditry that has plagued this land." - Context: Dorgon's proclamation upon entering Beijing in 1644, framing the Manchu conquest as a restoration of legitimate rule rather than foreign invasion.[^3]

> "The Ming Dynasty has exhausted the Mandate of Heaven. Heaven has chosen the Qing to bring peace to All Under Heaven." - Context: Dorgon's justification for Qing rule, invoking the traditional Chinese concept of the Mandate of Heaven to legitimize Manchu conquest.[^4]

> "Though we are of different origins, we shall be of one heart in serving the empire." - Context: Dorgon's statement regarding the Manchu-Han dyarchy system, attempting to create unity between Manchu conquerors and Han Chinese officials.[^5]
