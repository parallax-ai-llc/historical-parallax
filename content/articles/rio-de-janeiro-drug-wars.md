## Summary

Decades-long conflict between drug factions and police in Rio's favelas has killed tens of thousands. Police operations often resemble military invasions, killing many civilians.

## Context

The term "Rio de Janeiro drug wars" typically refers to recurring armed conflict involving drug-trafficking organizations, militias, and state security forces across Rio de Janeiro, particularly in and around favelas (informal urban communities).[^1]

## Key actors (high level)

While specific groups and alliances have shifted over time, reporting commonly distinguishes among:

- **Drug factions** controlling retail narcotics markets and territory
- **Militias** (paramilitary-style groups) that extract revenue through protection rackets and control of local services
- **Police and security forces** conducting raids, patrols, and intelligence-driven operations

## Typical dynamics

Patterns frequently described include:

- **Territorial disputes** between rival groups and splinter factions
- **Armed confrontations during police operations**, often in dense residential areas
- **Civilian exposure to violence**, including stray bullets, forced closures of schools/transport, and displacement

## Public policy responses

Authorities have employed a mix of strategies over the decades, including:

- Large-scale tactical raids and targeted arrests
- Community policing and "pacification" initiatives in some periods
- Intelligence efforts aimed at leadership, arms trafficking, and financial networks

Outcomes have varied by neighborhood and political cycle, and the conflict has remained a persistent public-safety and governance challenge.[^1]

## Human-rights and accountability concerns

The conflict has been accompanied by repeated debates about:

- Use of force standards and proportionality in urban operations
- Accountability for killings and misconduct
- The difficulty of distinguishing combatants from civilians in highly populated areas

## Why the topic is studied

Researchers and journalists often use Rio as a case study for how organized crime, inequality, corruption, and urban geography can interact to produce long-running cycles of violence and state responses.[^1]
