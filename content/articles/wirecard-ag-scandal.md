## Summary

German payment company that fabricated €1.9 billion in non-existent cash balances. CEO Markus Braun arrested and COO Jan Marsalek became one of Europe's most wanted fugitives.

## Background

Wirecard AG grew from a late-1990s payments processor into a member of Germany’s DAX index, positioning itself as a fast-growing fintech.
The scandal became a major test of European financial regulation and auditing practices.

## Key Developments

- Persistent allegations centered on inflated profits and fabricated assets tied to third-party acquiring businesses.
- Investigative reporting, including the Financial Times series, intensified scrutiny and triggered regulatory and corporate responses.
- In June 2020, Wirecard disclosed that roughly €1.9 billion that had been reported as held in trustee accounts likely did not exist.

## Investigations and Aftermath

Following the disclosure, Wirecard filed for insolvency and German authorities opened criminal investigations.
Former CEO Markus Braun was arrested, while former COO Jan Marsalek fled and became a high-profile fugitive.
The collapse prompted inquiries into the roles of the company’s auditor, regulators, and market oversight bodies.

## Why It Matters

The Wirecard case is widely cited as a landmark corporate-fraud episode in modern Germany.
It highlighted how complex international payment structures and reliance on counterparties can be used to obscure financial reality.
It also influenced debates over whistleblowing, short-selling restrictions, and the supervision of financial reporting.
