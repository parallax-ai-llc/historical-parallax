## Summary

Indian rebel leader during the 1857 uprising who ordered or permitted the Cawnpore massacre of British women and children. He disappeared after the rebellion's suppression and was never found.

## Background

Nana Sahib (also known as Dhondu Pant) emerged as a prominent figure during the Indian Rebellion of 1857. He was closely linked to the Maratha Peshwa legacy through adoption and patronage networks that remained influential even after the East India Company’s expansion.

## Political Grievances

A commonly cited grievance in accounts of Nana Sahib’s path to rebellion involves disputes over recognition and financial arrangements after the death of Baji Rao II (the exiled Peshwa). Debates over pensions and status were part of a broader pattern of annexation policies and administrative decisions that fueled resentment among various Indian elites.

## Role in the 1857 Rebellion

- **Kanpur (Cawnpore):** Nana Sahib became a leading rebel authority at Kanpur, a strategically important garrison and commercial center.
- **Control and administration:** Rebel leaders in several cities attempted to establish alternative governance and mobilize troops and supplies during the uprising.
- **Conflict with Company forces:** Kanpur saw intense fighting and shifting control as British forces attempted to reassert authority.

## Cawnpore Massacre (Bibighar)

The killings of British captives at Kanpur—especially women and children—became one of the most infamous episodes of the rebellion. Historical narratives differ on the degree of direct command responsibility, but Nana Sahib is widely associated with the events in British sources and subsequent historiography.

## Aftermath and Disappearance

After the rebellion was suppressed, Nana Sahib reportedly fled toward the Nepal border. His later fate is uncertain: various reports and legends placed him in hiding for years, but no definitive evidence settled the question.

## Historical Legacy

Nana Sahib’s reputation remains highly contested:

- In some British-era accounts, he was depicted primarily as a principal villain of the uprising.
- In parts of Indian nationalist memory, he has also been framed within a wider anti-colonial struggle, though the violence at Kanpur complicates interpretation.
