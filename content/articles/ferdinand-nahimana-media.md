## Summary

Rwandan historian who co-founded RTLM, the hate radio station that broadcast calls to kill Tutsis during the genocide. His conviction was a landmark in holding media accountable for incitement.

## Additional Context

### Role in RTLM and the "Media Case"

Nahimana was associated with Radio Télévision Libre des Mille Collines (RTLM), which became infamous for broadcasting propaganda and messages that encouraged violence during the 1994 genocide against the Tutsi in Rwanda.

The International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR) prosecuted several figures in what became known as the "Media Case," treating mass media not only as a channel for information but also as an instrument capable of mobilizing and directing collective violence.

### Why the case matters historically

High-level takeaways often cited by historians and legal scholars include:

- Incitement can be criminally punishable even when delivered through public speech and media.
- Editors, owners, and influential media figures can be held responsible when content is used to encourage atrocities.
- The case shaped later discussions about hate media, propaganda, and accountability in modern conflicts.

### Terminology note

This article title uses "Media" as a descriptor; most references discuss Ferdinand Nahimana as an individual whose media activities were central to the ICTR prosecution.
