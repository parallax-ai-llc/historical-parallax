## Summary

Genocide by the Khmer Rouge from 1975-1979 that killed 1.5-2 million Cambodians through execution, starvation, and forced labor.

## Background

After years of civil war and U.S. bombing during the wider Indochina conflict, the Communist Party of Kampuchea (Khmer Rouge) captured Phnom Penh in April 1975. The new regime pursued a radical agrarian program intended to rapidly transform Cambodian society.

The government sought to eliminate perceived class enemies, dismantle urban life, and impose strict political control through mass relocation and forced labor.

## Patterns of violence

The Khmer Rouge emptied cities, sent millions to rural labor camps, and operated a security apparatus that carried out arrests, torture, and executions. Starvation and disease were widespread due to harsh conditions, disrupted agriculture, and punitive policies.

Victims included targeted groups such as former officials, intellectuals, religious figures, ethnic minorities, and anyone suspected of disloyalty. Many deaths also resulted from indiscriminate brutality and systemic neglect.

## End of the regime and aftermath

In late 1978 and early 1979, Vietnam invaded Cambodia and removed the Khmer Rouge from power, installing a new government. The Khmer Rouge continued insurgency for years, and Cold War geopolitics complicated international recognition and accountability.

Over time, international and domestic efforts pursued justice, including the establishment of the Extraordinary Chambers in the Courts of Cambodia (ECCC) to prosecute senior leaders.

## Historical significance

The genocide is a central case in studies of mass atrocity, revolutionary violence, and state-led social engineering. It also remains a defining trauma in modern Cambodian history, shaping memory, politics, and diaspora communities.
