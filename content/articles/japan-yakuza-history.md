## Summary

The yakuza have been part of Japanese society for centuries, evolving from itinerant gamblers and merchants to modern organized crime groups controlling construction, real estate, and entertainment.

## Historical Roots

Common high-level histories trace yakuza formation to early-modern Japan, especially two occupational subcultures:

- **Bakuto (gamblers)**: groups tied to illegal gambling, debt collection, and protection rackets.
- **Tekiya (peddlers)**: market vendors and stall organizers who developed territorial control and informal enforcement.

These groups helped shape later yakuza identity, including ritual practices and hierarchical bonds.

## Organization and Culture

- **Oyabun–kobun structure**: a patron–client model often described as "parent–child" relationships, used to formalize loyalty and obligations.
- **Territory and revenue**: historically associated with gambling, labor brokerage, protection, entertainment districts, and later white-collar crime.
- **Public visibility**: unlike many criminal groups elsewhere, Japanese syndicates have at times maintained offices and semi-open presence, though this has been constrained by modern regulation.

## Postwar Growth and Modern Crackdowns

Post-1945 social disruption and black markets contributed to growth in organized crime. In later decades, authorities introduced stronger legal and financial pressure:

- **Anti-boryokudan frameworks**: national and local measures aimed at restricting business dealings and social acceptance of designated gangs.
- **2010s ordinances**: many prefectures adopted "exclusion" rules limiting contracts and services to gang-affiliated individuals.

## Contemporary Landscape

Major syndicates (often cited in overviews) include groups such as Yamaguchi-gumi and Sumiyoshi-kai, though membership and influence have fluctuated under enforcement and demographic change.
