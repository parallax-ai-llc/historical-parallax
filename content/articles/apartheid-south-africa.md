## Summary

South Africa's system of institutionalized racial segregation that denied basic rights to the Black majority for 46 years. Included forced removals, massacres, and imprisonment of opponents.

## Overview

Apartheid was a state-enforced system of racial classification and segregation implemented by South Africa’s National Party government after 1948. It structured political rights, movement, residence, education, labor, and personal life around legally defined racial categories.

## Core Features (High-Level)

- **Racial classification**: People were categorized (e.g., Black/African, White, Coloured, Indian/Asian) with different legal rights.
- **Segregation of space**: Residential areas, public amenities, and services were separated, often with drastic inequality.
- **Political exclusion**: The Black majority was denied meaningful national political representation under apartheid rule.

## Legal and Administrative Mechanisms

Many apartheid outcomes were enforced through a web of laws and bureaucratic controls, including:

- Pass laws and restrictions on movement
- Forced removals from designated areas
- Creation of "homelands" (Bantustans) presented as separate political entities

## Resistance and Repression

Apartheid faced sustained opposition from domestic movements (including mass protests, labor organization, and political parties) and was maintained through policing, surveillance, censorship, detention, and violence by state security forces.

## International Response

Over time, apartheid became a major international issue, leading to diplomatic pressure, cultural and sporting boycotts, and various forms of sanctions and divestment campaigns in multiple countries.

## End of Apartheid and Transition

The transition away from apartheid accelerated in the early 1990s with reforms, negotiations, and the unbanning of political organizations, culminating in the 1994 multi-racial democratic elections and the beginning of a new constitutional order.

## Historical Significance

Apartheid is widely studied as an example of modern institutionalized racial domination and as a case where internal resistance and international pressure contributed to a negotiated political transition.
