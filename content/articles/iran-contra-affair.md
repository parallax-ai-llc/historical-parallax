## Summary

Political scandal in which Reagan administration officials secretly sold arms to Iran and used the proceeds to fund Contra rebels in Nicaragua in violation of US law.

## Background

- The episode unfolded during the Cold War, when the United States backed anti-communist forces in Central America.
- In Nicaragua, the Contra rebels fought the Sandinista government; in the U.S., Congress restricted support for the Contras through the Boland Amendments.
- Separately, U.S. hostages were held by groups linked to Iran in Lebanon, shaping back-channel diplomacy and pressure for a deal.

## What Happened (High Level)

- U.S. officials facilitated covert arms sales to Iran, despite an arms embargo and public policy stating the U.S. would not negotiate with terrorists.
- Proceeds from the arms transactions were diverted to support the Contras, bypassing congressional restrictions.
- The arrangement involved intermediaries and secret financial channels intended to reduce oversight and maintain plausible deniability.

## Legal and Constitutional Issues

- The scandal raised questions about separation of powers, congressional control of appropriations, and executive branch accountability.
- Investigations focused on whether officials violated statutes governing arms exports, covert action reporting, and obstruction of oversight.
- A major public issue was recordkeeping: the destruction or concealment of documents complicated reconstruction of events.

## Aftermath and Impact

- Congressional hearings in 1987 publicized the network of actors, decision paths, and failures in supervision.
- Multiple officials were indicted or convicted; some convictions were later overturned on appeal, and others received presidential pardons.
- The affair became a reference point for debates over covert operations, oversight mechanisms, and executive discretion in national security.
