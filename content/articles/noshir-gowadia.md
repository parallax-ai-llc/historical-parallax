## Summary

Indian-born American engineer who helped design the B-2 stealth bomber's propulsion system and later sold classified stealth technology to China. He was convicted of selling national defense secrets to multiple countries.

## Engineering Work

Gowadia worked as an aerospace engineer in the United States and was associated with the development of stealth-related aircraft technology. In reporting about the case, he has been described as having had access to sensitive information related to how aircraft signatures can be reduced and how propulsion and exhaust systems can be designed to be less detectable.

## Espionage and Prosecution

U.S. authorities accused him of providing restricted technical information to foreign contacts, including information presented as relevant to stealth aircraft design. The case proceeded in federal court, where the central questions involved what information was classified, what had been disclosed, and whether the disclosures were authorized.

## Sentence and Significance

After conviction, he received a lengthy federal prison sentence. The case is often cited as an example of how specialized, high-value engineering knowledge can become a counterintelligence target and how export controls and secrecy obligations continue after government or contractor work ends.

## Broader Context

Cases like this are often discussed alongside insider-risk programs, contractor compliance, and the difficulty of drawing lines between general engineering expertise and protected technical data. They also illustrate how international demand for advanced aerospace know-how can create incentives for illicit transfer.

From a policy perspective, such prosecutions are sometimes used to signal deterrence and to reinforce the idea that technical documentation, briefings, and performance data can constitute national defense information even when they resemble ordinary engineering work.

### Common Themes in Reporting

- Foreign approaches through intermediaries
- Long investigations built on travel, communications, and document trails
