## Summary

Robert Johnson was an American blues singer and guitarist whose 29 recordings are the most influential body of work in blues history. His songs including Cross Road Blues and Sweet Home Chicago became standards. The legend that he sold his soul to the devil at the crossroads embodies the mythology of the blues.

## Musical Style and Influence

- **Guitar technique**: Johnson's recordings feature intricate fingerstyle rhythms and single-note lines that influenced later blues and rock guitar vocabulary.
- **Songwriting**: Many of his songs blend personal narrative, folk motifs, and blues archetypes; several became widely covered standards.
- **Recording legacy**: The small number of surviving recordings contributed to an outsized cultural footprint, amplified by reissues and scholarship.

## Mythology and Cultural Memory

- The "crossroads" story is often treated as a metaphor for artistic transformation and the perceived cost of mastery, even when discussed skeptically by historians.
- Johnson's life is a core example of how scarcity of documentation can turn a musician into a legend, shaped by later retellings.

## Legacy

- Johnson is frequently cited as a foundational figure for postwar blues revival and for rock musicians who drew from Delta blues traditions.
- His work is commonly used in teaching the lineage from rural blues to modern popular music.

## Notes on Discography

- Johnson's recorded output is small enough that individual takes and alternate versions receive close attention from historians and musicians.
- Later compilation albums played a major role in introducing his music to audiences beyond the original 1930s record market.
