## Summary

Forced relocation of Native American nations from southeastern US to Oklahoma in the 1830s. Approximately 60,000 people were displaced and over 4,000 Cherokee died during the march.

## Historical Context

- **Policy driver:** The Trail of Tears is closely associated with U.S. federal Indian removal policy, particularly the Indian Removal Act of 1830.
- **Land pressure:** Removal efforts were shaped by settler expansion, agricultural land demand, and state and federal conflicts over Native sovereignty.

## Who Was Affected

- **Multiple nations:** While the Cherokee experience is often central in popular memory, forced removals affected several Native nations of the Southeast, including the Cherokee, Muscogee (Creek), Seminole, Chickasaw, and Choctaw.
- **Displacement:** Communities were uprooted from ancestral homelands and moved westward, with long-term consequences for social structure, governance, and cultural continuity.

## Conditions and Human Cost (High Level)

- **Hardship:** Accounts commonly emphasize exposure, disease, inadequate supplies, and harsh travel conditions as major contributors to mortality.
- **Scale:** Tens of thousands of people were relocated, and thousands died during the process.

## Legacy

- **Enduring trauma:** The event remains a symbol of forced displacement and the breakdown of treaty and legal protections for Native peoples.
- **Modern remembrance:** The Trail of Tears is commemorated through historical scholarship, memorials, and educational programs focused on U.S. Indigenous history.
