## Background

Steven Donziger is an American lawyer and activist best known for his role in environmental litigation related to oil pollution in Ecuador’s Amazon region. The dispute became one of the most prominent and contentious examples of transnational mass-tort litigation, involving parallel proceedings in Ecuador, the United States, and other jurisdictions.

## Chevron–Ecuador Litigation (High-Level Overview)

- **Underlying allegations:** Plaintiffs alleged extensive environmental damage and health impacts from oil operations in Ecuador during the period when Texaco operated in the region; Chevron later acquired Texaco and disputed liability.
- **Ecuador judgment:** The Ecuadorian courts issued a large damages award (later modified), which plaintiffs sought to enforce internationally.
- **Chevron response:** Chevron argued the judgment was illegitimate and the product of fraud and coercion, and it pursued counter-litigation and enforcement defenses in multiple countries.

## U.S. Court Findings and Consequences

In U.S. proceedings, a federal judge found that the Ecuador judgment was obtained through racketeering-related conduct and enjoined Donziger from profiting from the judgment through U.S. enforcement pathways. These findings had major downstream consequences for Donziger’s professional standing and the enforceability strategy for the Ecuador judgment.

## Contempt Case and Public Controversy

Donziger’s refusal to comply with certain discovery and turnover orders led to contempt findings and later criminal contempt prosecution. His supporters characterized the prosecution and sanctions as an example of corporate retaliation and an imbalance of resources in litigation; critics argued the sanctions reflected serious professional misconduct and defiance of court orders. The case remains a widely cited flashpoint in debates over environmental justice, litigation ethics, and corporate accountability.
