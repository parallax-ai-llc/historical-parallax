## Summary

Colombian drug cartel that became the worlds largest cocaine producer after the fall of the Cali Cartel. Internal wars between factions resulted in over 1,500 murders.

## Origins and Evolution

- **Post-Cali landscape**: After major pressure on the Medellín and Cali cartels, trafficking capacity fragmented into successor groups and regional networks.
- **Brand vs. organization**: “Norte del Valle” is often described as a label for interconnected trafficking factions rather than a single centralized cartel.

## Operations (High Level)

- **Cocaine production and export**: The group(s) were associated with large-scale cocaine logistics, coordinating labs, transport, and distribution partners.
- **Routes and intermediaries**: Trafficking commonly relied on coastal routes, clandestine air/sea transport, and partnerships with brokers and international distributors.
- **Violence and enforcement**: Like many trafficking organizations, internal discipline and competition with rivals were enforced through violence.

## State Response

- **Captures and extraditions**: Colombian and U.S. pressure in the 2000s contributed to arrests, extraditions, and cooperation agreements involving senior figures.
- **Asset disruption**: Financial investigations and asset seizures are a core strategy against cartel resilience, alongside traditional policing.

## Historical Significance

- **Fragmentation era**: The Norte del Valle period is often used to illustrate how dismantling large cartels can lead to a more fragmented and competitive criminal ecosystem.
- **Regional spillover**: The organization’s activities intersected with broader conflicts involving armed groups and corruption risks in Colombia.
