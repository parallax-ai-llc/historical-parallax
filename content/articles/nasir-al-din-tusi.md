## Overview

Nasir al-Din al-Tusi was a major Persian scholar of the medieval Islamic world, known for contributions to **mathematics**, **astronomy**, **philosophy**, **theology**, and **ethics**. His work circulated widely and influenced later scientific traditions in the Middle East and, indirectly, in Europe.

## Education and Intellectual Milieu

Tusi’s career unfolded in a period of political upheaval, including the Mongol invasions. Like many scholars of the era, he moved among courts and patronage networks, producing works across disciplines rather than specializing narrowly.

## Astronomy and the Maragheh Observatory

One of the most commonly cited achievements associated with Tusi is his leadership role in the scientific program at **Maragheh**, where an observatory and research center were established under Mongol patronage.

- The Maragheh project brought together instruments, observational programs, and teams of scholars.
- Astronomical tables and models developed in this environment contributed to later Islamic astronomy.

## Mathematics and the “Tusi Couple”

Tusi is credited with important developments in trigonometry and astronomical modeling. A concept often linked to his name is the **Tusi couple**, a geometric device used to generate linear motion from circular motions in planetary models.

- The device appears in later astronomical works.
- It is frequently discussed in histories of pre-modern astronomy as part of the refinement of planetary theory.

## Philosophy, Theology, and Ethics

Beyond the mathematical sciences, Tusi authored influential texts on ethics and philosophy.

- His ethical writings were widely read and commented on.
- He engaged with earlier philosophical traditions and the religious debates of his time.

## Legacy

Tusi’s reputation rests on the breadth of his scholarship and his role in institutionalizing astronomical research at Maragheh. Modern historians of science often cite him when tracing the development of trigonometry and the evolution of planetary modeling prior to early modern European astronomy.
