## Summary

Ottoman governor of Van province known as the 'Horseshoe of Van' for nailing horseshoes to victims' feet during the Armenian Genocide. Orchestrated mass killings.

## Additional Context

- Cevdet Bey is commonly identified in English-language sources as Mehmed Cevdet (or Mehmet Cevdet), an Ottoman official active during World War I.
- He served as governor (vali) of Van Vilayet, a strategically important province in eastern Anatolia near the Russian frontier.
- Contemporary descriptions and later historical accounts associate him with extreme brutality and coercive tactics.

## Role in the Armenian Genocide (High-level)

- In 1915, Van became one of the focal points of mass violence and forced displacement of Armenians in the Ottoman Empire.
- Accounts attribute to Cevdet Bey a coordinating role in arrests, executions, and mass killings in and around Van province.
- The epithet "Horseshoe of Van" appears in multiple secondary summaries as a shorthand for his reputed use of torture.
- The broader campaign in the region combined military pretexts, security accusations, and systematic targeting of civilian communities.

## Disappearance and Aftermath

- Following the Ottoman defeat and political upheaval after World War I, many perpetrators of wartime atrocities avoided accountability through flight or concealment.
- Cevdet Bey is widely described as having disappeared in 1919; details vary by source and remain less documented than his wartime tenure.
- In postwar memory, his name functions as an emblematic example of provincial-level implementation of centrally directed violence.
