## Summary

Persian ruler who invaded India and ordered the massacre of 30,000 people in Delhi after a riot. He looted the Peacock Throne and Koh-i-Noor diamond from the Mughals.

## Historical Context

- Nadir Shah ruled in the 18th century during a period of shifting power across Iran, the Ottoman frontier, Central Asia, and the Mughal Empire.
- He is associated with the Afsharid dynasty and is often characterized as a military leader who restored Iranian power after years of crisis.

## Invasion of India (1739)

- The campaign against the Mughal Empire is remembered for the sack of Delhi and for the seizure of high-profile treasures later embedded in regional political mythology.
- Accounts of the violence in Delhi differ in emphasis and detail across sources, but the episode is widely cited as a major shock to Mughal prestige.

## Rule and Decline

- His state relied on military mobilization and heavy extraction to fund campaigns, which contributed to unrest.
- Late-reign brutality and paranoia are recurring themes in biographies and historical summaries.

## Long-Term Significance

- Nadir Shah's career is often used to illustrate how conquest can create short-lived imperial power without stable institutions.
- The memory of his Delhi campaign continues to appear in discussions of Indo-Persian history and the decline of Mughal authority.

## Historiography

- Popular summaries tend to compress complex campaigns into a few dramatic episodes (especially Delhi), which can overshadow regional governance and diplomacy.
- As with many early-modern figures, accounts may mix administrative records with later moral judgments.
