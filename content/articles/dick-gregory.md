## Summary

Dick Gregory was an American comedian, civil rights activist, social critic, and writer whose career bridged the worlds of entertainment and political protest with uncommon force. Rising to national prominence in the early 1960s, he became one of the first Black comedians to perform for predominantly white audiences in mainstream nightclubs, using sharp, politically charged humor to confront racial injustice at the height of the civil rights movement.

Beyond comedy, Gregory was a tireless activist who participated in major civil rights campaigns, ran for political office, and used hunger strikes as a form of protest. His life embodied the conviction that art and activism are inseparable, and his influence on comedians from Richard Pryor to Dave Chappelle remains profound.

## Early Life & Background

Richard Claxton Gregory was born on October 12, 1932, in St. Louis, Missouri, the second of six children raised in poverty by his mother, Lucille, after his father largely abandoned the family. Growing up in a segregated city, Gregory experienced firsthand the grinding indignities of American racism. He found early refuge in humor and athletics — he became a standout track star at Sumner High School, earning a track scholarship to Southern Illinois University in 1951.

His college career was interrupted by military service from 1953 to 1955, during which he performed in Army talent shows and honed his comedic instincts. He returned briefly to Southern Illinois but left without graduating to pursue comedy full-time in Chicago's club circuit.

## Rise to Prominence

Gregory's breakthrough came on January 13, 1961, when Hugh Hefner booked him to perform at the Chicago Playboy Club as a last-minute replacement. His performance before a predominantly white Southern audience — where he defused racial tension with wit rather than avoidance — was a sensation. A subsequent profile in *Time* magazine and a contract with Playboy Clubs nationally followed almost immediately.

His 1964 autobiography, *Nigger*, dedicated to his mother ("whenever you hear the word, it will be a commercial for my book"), became a bestseller and a landmark in American literature about race. He simultaneously began joining civil rights marches, appearing alongside Martin Luther King Jr. in Selma and Birmingham, and marching in Mississippi voter registration drives.

## Key Achievements

- Performed at the 1961 Chicago Playboy Club, becoming one of the first Black comedians to headline for white mainstream audiences
- Published *Nigger* (1964), which sold over 7 million copies
- Ran for Mayor of Chicago in 1967 and for U.S. President in 1968 as a write-in candidate on the Freedom and Peace Party ticket, receiving an estimated 1.5 million votes
- Conducted numerous prolonged hunger strikes protesting the Vietnam War, apartheid in South Africa, and drug abuse
- Authored over a dozen books on topics ranging from civil rights to nutrition and conspiracy theories

## Controversies & Criticism

Gregory's later career was marked by increasingly fringe political views, including promotion of conspiracy theories surrounding the assassinations of John F. Kennedy and Martin Luther King Jr. He also advanced unproven nutritional claims, marketing diet products that drew regulatory scrutiny. Critics argued these positions undermined his credibility as a serious public voice, though supporters saw them as extensions of his lifelong skepticism toward government institutions.

## Legacy & Influence

Gregory died on August 19, 2017, in Washington, D.C., at age 84. He is credited with fundamentally reshaping what American stand-up comedy could address and who could address it. His willingness to speak directly about race to white audiences opened doors for generations of Black comedians and established the template of the comedian-as-activist. He received the Mark Twain Prize for American Humor posthumously in 2021.
