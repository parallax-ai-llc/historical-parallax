## Summary

American arms dealer who founded Interarms, once the world's largest private arms dealer. He supplied weapons to governments and rebel groups worldwide. His company had warehouses holding 500,000 weapons at its peak.

## Interarms (International Armament Corporation)

Interarms operated as a private intermediary in the global small-arms trade during the Cold War period.
It dealt in surplus weapons and commercial sales, and it maintained international contacts for procurement and distribution.
Accounts of the firm emphasize its scale (warehousing, inventory management, and logistics) relative to typical private dealers.

## Operating environment

Cummings’ career is often discussed in the context of post–World War II surplus markets and the growth of international arms transfers.
In that period, governments, brokers, and private firms participated in complex supply chains that could span multiple jurisdictions.
The line between commercial sales, state-backed transfers, and covert support operations could be difficult for outsiders to disentangle.

## Public scrutiny and controversy

Large private arms dealers have attracted attention from journalists and policymakers because of where weapons can ultimately appear.
Coverage of Cummings and Interarms has therefore often focused on end users, diversion risks, and regulatory oversight.
Such debates typically involve export licensing, embargo compliance, and the challenge of enforcing controls across borders.

## Legacy

Cummings remains a frequently cited figure in histories of private arms dealing because Interarms became a reference point for scale.
His name is also used as an example in discussions about how private actors can influence conflict dynamics through supply.
