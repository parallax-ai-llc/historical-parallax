## Summary

John Stuart Mill (1806-1873) was the most influential English-speaking philosopher of the nineteenth century[^1], renowned for his contributions to liberalism, utilitarianism, and political economy. Born into an intensely intellectual environment under his father James Mill's rigorous tutelage, he became a leading advocate for individual liberty, women's rights, and social reform. His seminal works—including *On Liberty* (1859), *Utilitarianism* (1861), and *The Subjection of Women* (1869)—profoundly shaped modern liberal democratic thought and continue to influence contemporary political philosophy, ethics, and feminist theory.

---

## Early Life and Background

John Stuart Mill was born on May 20, 1806, in Pentonville, then a northern suburb of London, to Harriet Barrow and the Scottish philosopher, historian, and economist James Mill[^2]. His birth into one of Britain's most intellectually ambitious families would determine the extraordinary trajectory of his life. James Mill, a follower of Jeremy Bentham and adherent of associationism, undertook an audacious educational experiment with his eldest son, explicitly aiming to create a genius intellect that would carry forward the utilitarian cause after he and Bentham had died[^3].

Mill's education was extraordinarily rigorous and precocious, conducted entirely by his father with advice from Bentham and Francis Place. At the age of three, young John Stuart began learning Greek; by age eight, he had started Latin, the geometry of Euclid, and algebra, while also teaching his younger siblings[^4]. The most important aspect of this intensive training was the close association it fostered with the strenuous character and vigorous intellect of his father, with Mill spending much time in his father's study and habitually accompanying him on walks[^5]. By age twelve, Mill had absorbed most of the classical canon, along with advanced mathematics. In his early teenage years, he studied political economy, logic, and calculus, utilizing his spare time to digest treatises on experimental science as recreation[^6].

This isolated, hothouse education deliberately shielded Mill from association with children his own age other than his siblings. James Mill, who had been educated at Edinburgh University under Dugald Stewart and moved to London in 1802, became a prominent ally of Jeremy Bentham and the Philosophical Radicals[^7]. The father's ambitions for his son were grandiose: to create an intellectual prodigy who would advance the utilitarian project into the next generation. In 1820, Mill spent a year in France, broadening his intellectual horizons beyond the narrow confines of his father's London study[^8].

---

## Rise to Prominence

Mill's ascent to intellectual prominence began in his teenage years and accelerated through his twenties, despite a severe interruption. At age seventeen in 1823, following his father's path, Mill joined the British East India Company as a clerk[^9]. This position would provide him financial stability for the next thirty-five years, allowing him to pursue his philosophical work. He began rising through the Company's ranks, and in 1836 was promoted to the political department, where he managed correspondence pertaining to the Company's relations with Indian princely states. By 1856, he reached the position of Examiner of Indian Correspondence, roughly equivalent to Undersecretary of State, involving managing dispatches for colonial administration[^10].

However, Mill's path was not smooth. In autumn 1826, at age twenty, he suffered a profound mental crisis that lasted through the melancholy winter of 1826-27[^11]. Mill fell into a deep depression and contemplated suicide, questioning whether achieving all his reformist goals would actually bring him happiness. In his *Autobiography*, he described this as a "mental crisis," during which he experienced a nervous breakdown marked by severe depression[^12]. This crisis was likely precipitated by the emotional sterility of his purely rationalist education, which had developed his intellect at the expense of his feelings.

Mill's recovery began when he read the *Memoirs* of the obscure French writer Marmontel, and more significantly, when he discovered the poetry of William Wordsworth and the Romantics[^13]. These works awakened the emotional and aesthetic dimensions of his personality that his father's utilitarian training had neglected. Over several months, his depression gradually lifted, never to return with such severity. This experience profoundly transformed Mill's philosophy, leading him to emphasize "the culture of the feelings" alongside rational analysis, and to recognize that the stark Enlightenment philosophy of his upbringing contained "only one side of the truth"[^14]. His primary philosophical goal became integrating and reconciling the opposing schools of Enlightenment rationalism and Romantic emotionalism.

In 1830, Mill met Harriet Hardy Taylor at a dinner party, and the two quickly fell in love—an encounter that would prove as intellectually significant as his mental crisis[^15]. Harriet had married John Taylor, a pharmacist, four years earlier. Their intense intellectual and emotional relationship, though maintaining Victorian propriety, caused scandal and led to a rift between Mill and many friends. When John Taylor died in 1849, Mill and Harriet married in 1851[^16]. Mill regarded Harriet as his intellectual equal and collaborator, crediting her with co-authoring many of his ideas, particularly on women's rights.

---

## Major Achievements

Mill's intellectual achievements spanned philosophy, economics, political theory, and social reform, establishing him as one of the nineteenth century's most influential thinkers. His first major work, *A System of Logic, Ratiocinative and Inductive* (1843), reinvented the modern study of logic and laid foundations for his later work in political economy, women's rights, and representative government[^17]. This two-volume treatise formulated the five principles of inductive reasoning known as Mill's Methods, which remain central to scientific methodology[^18]. The work's influence extended beyond philosophy: Mill's demolition of William Hamilton's reputation in his later *Examination of Sir William Hamilton's Philosophy* (1865) led to the removal of Scottish "common sense" philosophy from British and American curricula, fundamentally reshaping Anglo-American philosophy[^19].

In 1848, Mill published *Principles of Political Economy, With Some of Their Applications to Social Philosophy*, which became the leading economics textbook of the English-speaking world during the second half of the nineteenth century[^20]. This comprehensive work synthesized classical economic theory while advocating for social reforms including workers' cooperatives, labor organizations, and progressive taxation. Mill's political economy differed from his predecessors by emphasizing that while production follows natural laws, distribution is subject to human institutional arrangements and thus amenable to reform in pursuit of social justice.

Mill's most enduring contribution came with *On Liberty* (1859), published shortly after Harriet's death in 1858—a work he regarded as jointly authored with her. This essay articulated what became known as the harm principle: "The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others"[^21]. *On Liberty* concerns civil and social liberty, or conversely, "the nature and limits of the power that can legitimately be exercised by society over the individual"[^22]. Mill warned against not merely political tyranny but the "tyranny of the prevailing opinion and feeling," arguing that social pressure could be "more formidable than many kinds of political oppression"[^23]. The ideas presented in this essay have remained the basis of liberal political thought, with copies traditionally passed to presidents of the Liberal Democrats as symbols of office[^24].

In *Utilitarianism* (1861), Mill refined and defended the ethical theory he inherited from Bentham, famously distinguishing between higher and lower pleasures and asserting that "it is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied"[^25]. He defined the utilitarian principle as holding "that actions are right in the proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness," with happiness meaning "pleasure and the absence of pain"[^26]. However, Mill rejected Bentham's view that humans are driven by narrow self-interest, arguing instead that "a desire of perfection" and sympathy for fellow humans belong to human nature.

Mill's advocacy for women's equality represented perhaps his most radical achievement. *The Subjection of Women*, written in 1861 but published in 1869, developed ideas jointly with Harriet Taylor Mill[^27]. This work is widely recognized as one of the key texts of Enlightenment liberal feminism, arguing that women's inequality was a relic from the past when "might was right" but had no place in the modern world[^28]. Mill challenged the notion that women were naturally inferior, arguing that society had never truly allowed women to demonstrate their full capabilities. He called for dismantling social and legal relationships that subjugate women and establishing "perfect equality and partnership between the sexes, in both the public and private spheres"[^29].

Beyond his writings, Mill achieved political influence. In 1865, he was elected Member of Parliament for Westminster representing the Liberal Party[^30]. Mill was one of the few political philosophers ever to serve in government as an elected official. During his three years in Parliament, he became the second MP (after Henry Hunt in 1832) to call for women's suffrage[^31]. He advocated for proportional representation, labor organizations, farm cooperatives, and Irish land reform, though he proved more willing to compromise than his "radical" writings might suggest.

## Controversies and Criticisms

Mill's career and ideas generated substantial controversy during his lifetime and have faced significant criticism from subsequent scholars. His work at the East India Company remains perhaps his most troubling legacy. For thirty-five years, Mill worked for an organization governing India, rising to Examiner of Indian Correspondence with significant influence over colonial policy[^32]. When the Crown proposed direct control over the Company's territories following the 1857 Indian Rebellion, Mill defended Company rule in his *Memorandum on the Improvements in the Administration of India during the Last Thirty Years*[^33]. Though offered a seat on the new Council of India, he declined, disapproving of the new system.

Mill's defense of British imperialism has attracted severe scholarly criticism. While he was "a much more uncertain and anxious imperialist than he is often presented to be," he nonetheless justified British rule in India on civilizational grounds[^34]. Mill argued that the principle of liberty did not apply to "backward" peoples, who required benevolent despotism to prepare them for self-government. Recent scholarship characterizes him as a "tolerant imperialist" who defended interference in India to promote legal rights and commercial society, though not to forcibly reshape India in Britain's monistic image[^35]. Toward the end of his life, Mill became more pessimistic about colonialism's progressive possibilities, particularly after evidence of settler violence and genocide mounted from mid-century[^36]. His 1860s correspondence suggests he feared settler violence compromised imperialism's supposed progressive promise, but he never fully repudiated his earlier justifications.

*The Subjection of Women*, though groundbreaking, generated "more negative comment than anything else Mill wrote" upon publication in 1869[^37]. The essay's argument for equality between the sexes affronted European conventional norms regarding men's and women's status. Even progressive contemporaries found Mill's proposals shocking, including his advocacy for married women's property rights and equal access to education and employment.

Modern feminist critics have identified significant limitations in Mill's liberal feminism. Though he criticized women's status as wives and mothers and condemned "marital slavery," his views on marriage showed certain limits[^38]. Critics note inconsistencies in *Subjection* due to Mill's desire to consider all sides of the question, reducing his arguments to the point of inconsistency. A 1970s feminist critique observed that Mill expected and clearly hoped that most women would continue choosing traditional domestic roles[^39]. His minimal legal prescriptions have been deemed insufficient for achieving justice and equality in any meaningful sense. Different feminist ideologies—liberal, intersectional, and Marxist—offer various critiques of his work's adequacy[^40]. Defenders argue that many perceived shortcomings reflect the work's rootedness in Victorian social structures rather than fundamental theoretical flaws.

Philosophical critics have questioned the coherence of Mill's thought, particularly the relationship between his utilitarian ethics and his defense of liberty. Some scholars argue it is impossible for a political philosophy to have two ultimate ends, claiming Mill's liberalism is inherently flawed due to tensions between individual freedom in *On Liberty* and social welfare in *Utilitarianism*[^41]. Mill attempted to reconcile these by grounding liberty in utilitarian considerations: "I regard utility as the ultimate appeal on all ethical questions; but it must be utility in the largest sense, grounded on the permanent interests of man as a progressive being"[^42]. Whether this reconciliation succeeds remains contested.

Mill's parliamentary career ended in defeat in the 1868 election, with his uncompromising advocacy for unpopular causes contributing to his loss. His support for women's suffrage, birth control advocate Charles Bradlaugh, and the prosecution of Governor Edward Eyre for atrocities in Jamaica made him politically vulnerable. Critics accused him of being too radical and theoretical, insufficiently practical for effective governance.

## Personal Life

Mill's personal life was shaped by two dominant relationships: with his father James Mill and with Harriet Taylor Mill. The two most important facts about Mill's life, as scholars note, were "that he was the son of James Mill and that he fell in love with Harriet Hardy Taylor"[^43]. His father's influence pervaded his early years through the intensive, isolating education that simultaneously developed his extraordinary intellect and contributed to his mental crisis. The most important aspect of his training was the close association with his father's "strenuous character and vigorous intellect," though this came at the cost of normal childhood socialization[^44].

Mill's relationship with Harriet Taylor, beginning in 1830 when both were in their twenties, became the central emotional and intellectual partnership of his life. Their relationship, while Harriet remained married to John Taylor, maintained Victorian propriety but generated significant scandal that caused rifts with Mill's friends. After John Taylor's death in 1849, they married in 1851[^45]. Mill credited Harriet as his intellectual equal and collaborator, particularly on women's rights. In his *Autobiography*, he lavished extraordinary praise on her abilities, describing her as possessing a genius surpassing his own. Modern scholars debate the extent of Harriet's actual contributions versus Mill's possibly idealized perception, but her influence on his thought—especially regarding gender equality—is undeniable.

Harriet's death in 1858 during their travels through France devastated Mill. She died in Avignon and was buried in the cemetery at Cimetière Saint-Véran. Mill purchased a house nearby and lived there for the remainder of his life, returning to England only briefly each year, until his own death[^46]. This self-imposed exile reflected the depth of his devotion and grief.

Mill's personality combined rigorous intellectual discipline with emotional depth developed after his mental crisis. He was described as maintaining "laborious habits" and "ardent outlook on human affairs" throughout his life[^47]. The crisis of 1826 had taught him to value poetry and art both for their own sake and as means of developing feelings and character. He believed happiness involved "the rich and varied growth of one's personality"[^48]. Mill was a lifelong botanist and frequent contributor to the journal *Phytologist*, finding in nature study a respite from philosophical labors[^49].

His acquaintance with Romantic thinkers gave him lasting openness to Romantic thought and acute awareness that Enlightenment philosophy contained "one side of the truth"[^50]. This integration of rationalist and Romantic sensibilities defined his mature character. Despite his fame, Mill lived modestly, dedicating his East India Company salary to supporting his large family (his father had nine children) and his philosophical work. Unlike many Victorian intellectuals, he declined university appointments, preferring the independence his Company position provided.

Mill never had children with Harriet, though she had three children from her first marriage. His relationship with his siblings was complex, particularly after his father's death in 1836, as he assumed some financial responsibility for the family while pursuing his own intellectual projects. His stepdaughter Helen Taylor became his companion and collaborator after Harriet's death, assisting with his final works and political activities.

## Legacy

John Stuart Mill's legacy as "the most influential English-speaking philosopher of the nineteenth century" extends across multiple domains of modern thought[^51]. His influence on liberalism proved foundational: he is considered one of the most influential thinkers in the history of liberalism and social liberalism, contributing widely to social theory, political theory, and political economy[^52]. No other thinker has had such broad influence on modern constitutional rights traditions, particularly conceptions of personal freedom, equality, and freedom of expression[^53].

The ideas presented in *On Liberty* have remained the basis of much political thought for over 160 years. Mill's harm principle—that individuals should be free to act as they wish unless their actions harm others—has become a cornerstone of liberal political philosophy and jurisprudence. His warnings against the "tyranny of the majority" and social conformity resonate in contemporary debates about free speech, privacy rights, and individual autonomy. The Liberal Democrats traditionally pass a copy of *On Liberty* to their party president as a symbol of office, testifying to the work's enduring significance[^54].

In democratic theory, Mill's contributions proved equally substantial. Nadia Urbinati's scholarship highlights Mill's neo-Athenian contributions to "the making of the modern democratic vision," characterizing him as a proponent of "liberal plebeianism"—an activist-theorist who confronted oligarchy's threat by promoting working-class mobilization within a liberal, parliamentary framework[^55]. His advocacy for proportional representation, protection of minority viewpoints, and educated citizenship shaped modern deliberative democratic theory. Through education, Mill believed, individuals could become full participants in representative democracy, avoiding tyranny of the majority by ensuring all voters were fully developed individuals[^56].

Mill's ethical legacy remains controversial but influential. His refinement of utilitarianism, particularly the distinction between higher and lower pleasures, shaped subsequent utilitarian thought and ongoing debates in normative ethics. Contemporary utilitarians continue engaging with Mill's formulations, though many modify or reject aspects of his theory. His attempt to ground individual liberty in utilitarian principles inspired ongoing philosophical debates about the relationship between consequentialist ethics and deontological rights.

His feminist legacy proves perhaps most complex. Mill was among the earliest defenders of complete political and social equality between the sexes[^57]. *The Subjection of Women* became a foundational text for the women's suffrage movement and liberal feminism. His arguments against natural differences justifying inequality influenced subsequent feminist theory, even as modern feminists critique his limitations. His parliamentary advocacy for women's suffrage represented the first major political campaign for this cause in Britain. Contemporary feminist philosophy engages critically with Mill's work, acknowledging both his pioneering contributions and his failure to challenge certain Victorian assumptions about gender and domesticity.

Mill's influence on political economy was substantial but more contested. *Principles of Political Economy* dominated economic education for decades, but his openness to socialism and workers' cooperatives differentiated him from classical liberals and later marginalist economists. His vision of economic democracy through cooperatives influenced later movements, though mainstream economics largely abandoned his approach. Recent scholarship explores "market socialism" elements in Mill's political economy[^58].

His educational philosophy emphasized character development, emotional cultivation, and critical thinking alongside knowledge acquisition. Mill's own experience of educational extremes—the prodigious learning that enabled extraordinary intellectual achievement but nearly destroyed him emotionally—informed his advocacy for balanced education developing both reason and feeling. This vision influenced progressive education movements emphasizing holistic human development.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholarly defenders of Mill emphasize his systematic integration of liberal political theory with utilitarian ethics, arguing he successfully demonstrated how maximizing human happiness requires protecting individual liberty and promoting human flourishing in its highest forms. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy's characterization of Mill as "the most influential English-speaking philosopher of the nineteenth century" reflects widespread recognition of his philosophical achievement[^59]. Scholars particularly praise his ability to synthesize diverse intellectual traditions—Enlightenment rationalism, Romantic emotionalism, utilitarianism, and liberalism—into a coherent philosophical vision addressing fundamental questions about ethics, politics, and human nature.

Mill's contributions to free speech theory earn particular scholarly praise. His arguments in *On Liberty* that truth emerges through free discussion, that even false opinions contain "portions of truth," and that unchallenged beliefs become "dead dogma" rather than living truth remain central to free speech jurisprudence and democratic theory[^60]. The Foundation for Individual Rights and Expression cites Mill's "enduring arguments for free speech" as foundational to contemporary free expression advocacy[^61]. His recognition that social pressure and conformity threaten liberty as much as legal restrictions anticipated twentieth-century concerns about cultural authoritarianism.

Feminist scholars, despite criticizing limitations, acknowledge Mill's pioneering role in gender equality advocacy. His willingness to challenge fundamental Victorian assumptions about women's nature and proper social role, his parliamentary advocacy for women's suffrage, and his insistence that women's apparent differences from men resulted from socialization rather than nature proved revolutionary[^62]. His collaboration with Harriet Taylor Mill demonstrated genuine intellectual partnership between the sexes, modeling equality in practice. Contemporary feminist philosophy continues engaging substantively with Mill's arguments, indicating their ongoing theoretical relevance.

Scholars of democratic theory praise Mill's sophisticated understanding of democracy's challenges and his proposals for protecting minority rights, promoting educated citizenship, and preventing majority tyranny. His advocacy for proportional representation influenced electoral reform movements. His emphasis on civic education and public deliberation anticipated contemporary deliberative democracy theories. Urbinati's interpretation of Mill as a "liberal plebeian" activist highlights his recognition that formal political equality requires active citizen participation and social movements to resist oligarchic tendencies[^63].

Mill's personal courage in advocating unpopular causes—women's suffrage, birth control, colonial subjects' rights, and working-class political participation—during an era of rigid social hierarchies earns admiration. His parliamentary career demonstrated willingness to sacrifice political success for principled advocacy. His support for the prosecution of Governor Eyre for atrocities in Jamaica showed moral courage in challenging imperial impunity. These actions exemplified his philosophy that intellectuals must engage politically, not merely theorize abstractly.

---

### Negative Views

Critics from diverse perspectives challenge fundamental aspects of Mill's thought and legacy. His defense of British imperialism represents perhaps the most damaging criticism. Postcolonial scholars emphasize that Mill spent his career administering colonial rule in India while simultaneously developing liberal theory supposedly grounded in human equality and liberty. This apparent contradiction reveals, critics argue, that Mill's liberalism was fundamentally exclusionary, reserving freedom for "civilized" Europeans while justifying despotism over colonized peoples deemed "backward"[^64]. His argument that the principle of liberty does not apply to those in their "nonage" provided philosophical justification for imperial domination.

Recent scholarship on "John Stuart Mill and the practice of colonial rule in India" demonstrates how Mill's extensive writings on Indian administration reveal committed imperialism, not merely theoretical musings[^65]. His defense of Company rule against Crown takeover, his *Memorandum on the Improvements in the Administration of India*, and his policy work show active participation in colonial governance. Critics argue this fundamentally compromises his liberal credentials, revealing liberalism's historical complicity with imperialism and racial hierarchy. The claim that Mill became "much more pessimistic about the progressive possibilities of British colonialism" late in life appears inadequate given decades of active colonial administration[^66].

Feminist critics, while acknowledging Mill's pioneering role, identify significant limitations revealing his inability to fully transcend Victorian gender assumptions. Despite advocating legal equality, Mill expected most women would continue choosing domestic roles, suggesting his vision of equality remained constrained by conventional gender ideology[^67]. His focus on formal legal equality, critics argue, neglected structural economic and social inequalities that legal reforms alone cannot address. Marxist feminists critique his liberal individualism's failure to address women's reproductive labor and economic dependence. Intersectional feminists note his exclusive focus on middle-class white women's concerns, ignoring how race and class intersect with gender oppression.

Philosophical critics question the coherence of Mill's system. The relationship between his utilitarian ethics and defense of liberty remains contested, with critics arguing they represent incompatible commitments. If maximizing happiness is the ultimate moral principle, critics ask, why shouldn't we sacrifice individual liberty when doing so increases overall utility? Mill's attempt to derive rights from utility appears to many philosophers unsuccessful, leaving his liberalism without secure foundations. His distinction between higher and lower pleasures strikes critics as introducing perfectionist judgments incompatible with utilitarian hedonism, or as circular reasoning that defines higher pleasures as those preferred by competent judges who are defined as those preferring higher pleasures.

Economic critics from both left and right challenge Mill's political economy. Free market advocates argue his openness to socialism and workers' cooperatives represents incoherent deviation from classical liberal economics. Socialists counter that his failure to challenge private property in productive capital renders his reforms inadequate for addressing economic exploitation. His optimism that education and voluntary cooperation could transform capitalism appears naive to critics emphasizing structural economic imperatives resistant to moral persuasion.

Contemporary critics question the applicability of Mill's framework to modern challenges. His harm principle, seemingly clear, generates intractable difficulties determining what constitutes harm in complex modern societies where individual actions have diffuse social consequences. His faith in rational deliberation and educated public opinion appears quaint in an era of mass media manipulation, filter bubbles, and weaponized disinformation. His optimistic progressive vision that education and free discussion naturally lead toward truth seems contradicted by persistent irrationality and ideological polarization.

Critics note that Mill's elitism pervades his ostensibly democratic theory. His proposal for plural voting giving educated voters more votes than the uneducated reveals distrust of mass democracy. His conception of competent judges authorized to pronounce on quality of pleasures introduces hierarchical authority into supposedly egalitarian utilitarianism. His expectation that workers would defer to employers in cooperative enterprises reveals class assumptions limiting his egalitarianism. These elitist elements, critics argue, compromise his democratic credentials and reveal liberalism's historical alignment with elite interests despite egalitarian rhetoric.

## Famous Quotes

> "The only freedom which deserves the name is that of pursuing our own good in our own way, so long as we do not attempt to deprive others of theirs, or impede their efforts to obtain it." - *On Liberty* (1859)[^68]

> "If all mankind minus one were of one opinion, and only one person were of the contrary opinion, mankind would be no more justified in silencing that one person than he, if he had the power, would be justified in silencing mankind." - *On Liberty*, arguing for absolute freedom of opinion[^69]

> "The peculiar evil of silencing the expression of an opinion is, that it is robbing the human race; posterity as well as the existing generation; those who dissent from the opinion, still more than those who hold it." - *On Liberty*[^70]

> "Over himself, over his own body and mind, the individual is sovereign." - *On Liberty*, defining the limits of legitimate state power[^71]

> "Bad men need nothing more to compass their ends, than that good men should look on and do nothing." - Inaugural Address at University of St. Andrews (1867), often paraphrased as "the only thing necessary for evil to triumph is for good men to do nothing"[^72]

> "It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied." - *Utilitarianism* (1861), defending qualitative distinctions between pleasures[^73]

> "The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others." - *On Liberty*, stating the harm principle[^74]

> "Society can and does execute its own mandates: and if it issues wrong mandates instead of right, or any mandates at all in things with which it ought not to meddle, it practises a social tyranny more formidable than many kinds of political oppression." - *On Liberty*, warning against tyranny of public opinion[^75]

> "Precisely because the tyranny of opinion is such as to make eccentricity a reproach, it is desirable, in order to break through that tyranny, that people should be eccentric." - *On Liberty*, defending nonconformity[^76]

> "Actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness. By happiness is intended pleasure, and the absence of pain; by unhappiness, pain, and the privation of pleasure." - *Utilitarianism*, defining the utilitarian principle[^77]

> "I regard utility as the ultimate appeal on all ethical questions; but it must be utility in the largest sense, grounded on the permanent interests of man as a progressive being." - *On Liberty*, reconciling liberty and utilitarianism[^78]

> "The worth of a State, in the long run, is the worth of the individuals composing it." - *On Liberty*, emphasizing individual development[^79]
