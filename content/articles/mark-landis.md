## Summary

American art forger who donated forged paintings to over 50 museums across the US over 30 years. Since he never sold them, he committed no crime. He used cheap materials from Walmart. His story was told in the documentary 'Art and Craft.'

## Background and context

Landis became known for a long-running pattern of presenting forged works as donations to museums, often targeting smaller institutions with limited resources for authentication. The case is frequently discussed as an example of how provenance checks and curatorial workflows can fail under time pressure.

## Modus operandi

He typically copied existing artworks and styles, producing pieces that would pass quick visual inspection rather than detailed technical analysis. Reports about the case emphasize that inexpensive, readily available materials were part of what made the practice scalable over many years.

Landis often approached museums in a persona intended to establish credibility, such as representing himself as a collector or heir. This social-engineering element is one reason the episode is cited in museum studies and fraud-prevention discussions.

## Legal and ethical significance

Because the forged works were donated rather than sold, the legal response differed from cases of commercial art fraud. Even when criminal charges are not feasible, institutions can still face real costs: staff time, conservation and storage expenses, exhibition planning disruptions, and reputational harm.

## Cultural impact

The documentary *Art and Craft* helped popularize the story, framing it as a blend of deception, institutional vulnerability, and questions about motive. The case is also used to illustrate the importance of documentation standards, independent verification, and cross-institution communication when suspicious patterns emerge.

## Why the case is studied

In practice, the Landis episode is often treated as a training case about internal controls: separation of duties for acquisitions, documentation requirements, and the value of independent review when an offer seems unusually generous.
