## Summary

Belgian terrorist who detonated a suicide bomb at Maalbeek metro station in Brussels killing 16 people. He rented safe houses used by the Paris attacks cell.

## Background

El Bakraoui was a Belgian national whose criminal record was reported in connection with later counterterrorism investigations. European security reporting on the 201516 period often highlighted the intersection of petty crime networks, prison radicalization, and extremist operational planning.

## Role in the Brussels Attacks (2016)

On 22 March 2016, coordinated bombings struck Brussels Airport and the Maalbeek (Malbeek) metro station. El Bakraoui carried out a suicide bombing at the metro station, causing mass casualties.

Investigations linked the Brussels cell to the broader network associated with the November 2015 Paris attacks. Multiple safe houses and logistical arrangements (including rentals and forged documents) were cited as evidence of a transnational support structure.

## Connections and Logistics

Open-source summaries note that El Bakraoui was connected to his brother, Ibrahim El Bakraoui, who was also involved in the Brussels attacks. The use of rented apartments and short-term lodging was a recurring operational method, helping militants store materials and avoid detection.

## Aftermath

The Brussels attacks led to intensified security measures across Belgium and Europe, including reassessments of information sharing, watchlist practices, and the monitoring of returnees from conflict zones.

## Security Lessons Cited by Analysts

Post-attack reviews frequently emphasized gaps in cross-border intelligence coordination and the difficulty of tracking small cells using everyday infrastructure such as rental housing and prepaid communications. The Brussels case is often paired with the Paris attacks as a connected chapter in Europe's 201516 security crisis.

## Additional Notes

Public accounts of the Brussels network underscored how quickly operational planning can move once a cell feels exposed, contributing to debates about when to intervene versus continue surveillance.
