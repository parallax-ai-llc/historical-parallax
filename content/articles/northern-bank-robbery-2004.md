## Summary
The Northern Bank robbery occurred on December 20, 2004, when approximately £26.5 million was stolen from the Northern Bank headquarters in Belfast. It was attributed to the Provisional IRA. It was one of the largest bank robberies in British history.

## What is known about the operation
High-level accounts describe the robbery as a coordinated operation that combined coercion of bank personnel with a tightly timed cash removal from the Belfast cash centre. Public reporting has emphasized:

- **Coercion and leverage:** In widely reported versions of events, individuals connected to bank staff were threatened to compel cooperation, reducing the need for violence at the scene.
- **Operational planning:** The scale of the cash taken and the ability to move it quickly suggested prior reconnaissance, logistics, and prepared transport.
- **Attribution controversy:** Police and political leaders attributed the robbery to the Provisional IRA, while the organization denied involvement. The dispute became part of the broader debate about paramilitary criminality and the post-conflict transition. [^5]

## Investigative and financial consequences
The Northern Bank robbery became notable not only for its size but for the downstream challenges it created:

- **Money recovery constraints:** Even when suspects are identified, large cash thefts are difficult to recover because banknotes can be broken up, laundered, or moved across jurisdictions.
- **Banking and compliance attention:** The case was frequently discussed in the context of anti–money laundering practices and the importance of reporting suspicious cash movements.
- **Public trust impacts:** Major robberies can drive scrutiny of a bank's internal controls and physical security, even when staff are coerced rather than complicit.
