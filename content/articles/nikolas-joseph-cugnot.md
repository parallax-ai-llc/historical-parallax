## Summary

Nicolas-Joseph Cugnot was a French inventor who built the first self-propelled mechanical vehicle, a steam-powered tricycle (fardier à vapeur) in 1769. His steam wagon, designed to haul artillery for the French army, is considered the first automobile. He also may have caused the first automobile accident when his vehicle ran into a wall. His original vehicle survives in the Conservatoire national des arts et métiers in Paris.

## Context

Cugnot’s *fardier à vapeur* is often discussed as an early attempt to apply steam power to ground transport. In the 18th century, steam engines were primarily associated with stationary or industrial uses, so adapting them to a vehicle required solving practical problems in weight, steering, stability, and power delivery.

The project is also tied to military logistics: moving heavy equipment such as artillery placed strong demands on traction and reliability. Even when prototypes were limited, they helped demonstrate what would later become a central idea in transportation history—**self-propelled vehicles** replacing animal power.

## Technical and Historical Significance

- **Steam propulsion**: The vehicle is commonly used as an example of how early steam engineering influenced later transportation experiments.
- **Design constraints**: Early steam vehicles faced issues like limited range, slow speed, and handling difficulties—factors that shaped later engineering priorities.
- **“First automobile” debates**: Historians sometimes distinguish between “first self-propelled road vehicle,” “first practical automobile,” and “first commercially adopted automobile,” which depend on the criteria used.

## Key Takeaways

- Many “firsts” in technology are best understood as **prototypes** that proved a concept rather than finished products.
- Military or state-backed needs can accelerate experimentation, even when the resulting machines are not immediately scalable.
