## Summary

William Campbell is an Irish-American parasitologist who shared the 2015 Nobel Prize for developing ivermectin.

## Additional Context

- **Field**: Parasitology and antiparasitic drug discovery, with a focus on diseases caused by helminths (parasitic worms).
- **Global health relevance**: The therapies associated with ivermectin became widely used against **onchocerciasis (river blindness)** and were important in broader efforts to control neglected tropical diseases.

## Key Contributions

- **Ivermectin development**: Campbell’s work (notably in collaboration with industrial research) helped translate an antiparasitic discovery into a treatment with major public-health impact.
- **Partnership model**: The ivermectin story is often cited as an example of academic–industry collaboration and large-scale medicine donation programs aimed at disease control.

## Nobel Prize Context

The 2015 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine recognized discoveries of therapies that transformed treatment for parasitic infections. Campbell shared the award for the ivermectin-related work, which became one of the most widely deployed antiparasitic interventions in modern medicine.

## Legacy

Campbell is frequently discussed in the context of how targeted drug discovery can produce outsized benefits when combined with delivery systems (screening, distribution, and sustained public-health programs) that reach populations most affected by tropical diseases.

## Related Concepts

- **Neglected tropical diseases (NTDs)**: A category of infections that disproportionately affect low-income regions and often require sustained control programs.
- **Mass drug administration (MDA)**: Community-wide distribution strategies used to reduce parasite prevalence over time.
- **Translational research**: Moving discoveries from lab or screening programs into approved, scalable therapies.
