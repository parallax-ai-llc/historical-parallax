## Summary

French pirate considered one of the most brutal buccaneers of the Caribbean. He was known for extreme cruelty including torturing and eating the heart of a Spanish prisoner. He raided Maracaibo and Gibraltar. Reportedly eaten by cannibals.

## Additional Context

### Buccaneering in the 17th-century Caribbean

L'Olonnais operated in the era of Caribbean buccaneers, when European empires competed for control of trade routes and colonies. Many pirates and privateers moved between semi-legal raiding and outright piracy, often using port towns and hidden coves as bases.

### Reputation for brutality

Contemporary and later accounts portray him as exceptionally violent, with stories of torture used to extract information about treasure and shipping. Some of the most sensational anecdotes may be difficult to verify, but his legend is consistently associated with cruelty toward prisoners.

### Major raids

The sack of Maracaibo and nearby Gibraltar (in present-day Venezuela) is commonly cited as his most notable campaign, illustrating how pirate raids could terrorize coastal settlements and disrupt regional commerce.

### Death and myth-making

Narratives about his death (including claims he was killed and eaten by Indigenous people) contribute to a broader pattern in pirate historiography where rumor, propaganda, and moral storytelling blur into a durable legend.

### Sources and historiography

Much of what is popularly repeated about l'Olonnais comes from early-modern pirate narratives that blended eyewitness report, hearsay, and moralized cautionary tales. As a result, modern summaries often distinguish between:

- Actions that appear consistently across multiple accounts (e.g., participation in major raids).
- Anecdotes that may be exaggerated to emphasize a reputation for cruelty.

### Why he is remembered

Even among a cohort of violent buccaneers, l'Olonnais is often singled out as an extreme case, making him a recurring example in discussions of piracy as both organized predation and theatrical myth.
