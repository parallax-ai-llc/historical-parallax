## Daewoo Group and the Chaebol Model

Kim Woo-choong founded **Daewoo Group**, which grew into one of South Korea's major conglomerates (chaebol). Daewoo expanded aggressively across manufacturing, shipbuilding, automobiles, and international trading, reflecting a broader South Korean development strategy that combined export-led growth with large, diversified corporate groups.

## Rapid Expansion and Risk Accumulation

Accounts of Daewoo's growth frequently emphasize heavy use of leverage and ambitious overseas investments. In the late 1990s, South Korea faced severe macroeconomic stress during the Asian financial crisis period, and highly indebted corporate structures became more fragile under tightening credit and shifting market conditions.

## Collapse and Legal Aftermath

Daewoo's collapse in 1999 became one of the defining corporate failures in modern South Korean economic history. Subsequent investigations and court proceedings focused on allegations of:

- large-scale accounting manipulation,
- embezzlement and breach of trust,
- improper financing practices.

Kim Woo-choong left South Korea and later returned to face trial; he was convicted and sentenced, and later reports note subsequent reductions or pardons as part of South Korea's periodically debated approach to white-collar crime and economic policy.

## Historical Significance

The Daewoo case is often discussed in relation to:

- post-crisis corporate restructuring,
- governance reforms and transparency standards,
- the economic and political influence of chaebol in South Korea.
