## Summary

The most violent prison riot in US history where inmates murdered 33 fellow prisoners, many through torture and mutilation. Guards were also taken hostage but none were killed.

## Background

- Location: the New Mexico State Penitentiary (also known as the Penitentiary of New Mexico) in Santa Fe, New Mexico.
- Period: the riot began on February 2, 1980 and unfolded over roughly 36 hours.
- Setting: the facility faced major overcrowding and tensions around living conditions and supervision.

## Causes and Conditions (High-Level)

- Chronic overcrowding and understaffing increased day-to-day volatility.
- Prison governance and classification practices contributed to dangerous concentrations of vulnerable prisoners.
- Contraband access (including improvised weapons) enabled rapid escalation once control was lost.

## What Happened

- Inmates seized parts of the prison and took correctional officers as hostages.
- Violence was primarily directed at other incarcerated people; 33 inmates were killed.
- Many deaths involved extreme brutality, including torture and mutilation, which became a defining feature of the incident’s historical notoriety.
- Despite the hostage-taking, no guards were killed.

## Immediate Response

- The crisis required negotiation and coordinated law-enforcement response outside the prison walls.
- Control was eventually re-established after prolonged standoffs and phased surrender.

## Aftermath

- The riot triggered investigations, legal proceedings, and renewed scrutiny of prison conditions.
- It accelerated reforms and modernization efforts in New Mexico’s corrections system, including changes in facility design and management.

## Key Facts and Figures

- Duration: ~36 hours.
- Fatalities: 33 inmates killed; guard deaths: 0.
- Historical note: frequently cited as the most violent prison riot in U.S. history.

## Legacy

- Became a reference point in discussions of:
  - prison overcrowding and staffing,
  - hostage response and negotiation,
  - institutional design that can prevent mass-casualty inmate-on-inmate violence.
