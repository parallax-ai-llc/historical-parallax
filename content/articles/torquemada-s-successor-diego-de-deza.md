## Summary

Second Grand Inquisitor of Spain who continued and intensified the persecution of conversos and heretics. His Inquisition tribunals burned hundreds at the stake.

## Context

- Diego de Deza was a Spanish Dominican cleric who held high church offices in late-15th and early-16th century Spain.
- He served as an adviser to the Catholic Monarchs during a period when the Spanish Crown consolidated power and pursued religious uniformity.

## Role as Grand Inquisitor

- Deza succeeded Tomás de Torquemada and continued the institutional expansion of inquisitorial tribunals.
- Sources describe an intensified focus on conversos (Christians of Jewish ancestry) and other groups suspected of heresy.
- The Inquisition combined legal procedure with coercive interrogation practices and public punishments, including autos-da-fé.

## Removal from office

- Deza was removed as Grand Inquisitor in 1507.
- Historical accounts attribute his fall to political pressure and controversy around inquisitorial excesses, though details vary by source.

## Historical legacy

- Deza’s tenure is usually discussed as part of the broader evolution of the Spanish Inquisition rather than as a single individual’s program.
- Modern scholarship debates the scale of persecution and the interaction between church authority, royal policy, and local enforcement.
