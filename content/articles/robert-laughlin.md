## Summary

Robert Laughlin is an American physicist who shared the 1998 Nobel Prize in Physics for his theoretical explanation of the fractional quantum Hall effect.

## Scientific Context

- Condensed matter physics studies how large collections of particles (especially electrons in solids) produce emergent phenomena.
- The **quantum Hall effect** arises in two-dimensional electron systems under low temperatures and strong magnetic fields, producing quantized electrical conductance.

## Fractional Quantum Hall Effect

- The **fractional** quantum Hall effect involves quantization at fractional values and is associated with strongly correlated electron behavior.
- Laughlin proposed an influential theoretical description (often associated with the **Laughlin wavefunction**) that accounted for the observed fractional quantization.

## Recognition and Influence

- The 1998 Nobel Prize in Physics recognized work explaining the discovery and theory of the fractional quantum Hall effect.
- Laughlin’s contribution is frequently used as a foundational example of how new “collective” particles and states of matter can emerge from interacting systems.
