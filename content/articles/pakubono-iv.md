## Summary

Pakubuwono IV (also spelled Pakubuwana IV) was the fourth Susuhunan (ruler) of Surakarta in Central Java, Indonesia, who reigned from 1788 to 1820. Born on September 2, 1768, he ascended to the throne at the young age of 20 following the death of his father, Pakubuwono III. His 32-year reign was marked by increasing Dutch colonial influence over the Javanese courts, significant cultural developments including the refinement of Javanese court traditions and gamelan music, and the complex navigation of political pressures from both European powers and internal court factions. Pakubuwono IV is remembered as a patron of the arts who presided over a golden age of Javanese court culture, even as his political autonomy diminished under colonial pressure. His reign represents a pivotal transition period in Javanese history between traditional sovereignty and colonial subjugation.

---

## Early Life and Background

Pakubuwono IV was born as Raden Mas Sulaiman on September 2, 1768, in the Surakarta Sunanate palace complex, into the illustrious Mataram dynasty that had ruled central Java for centuries.[^1] He was the eldest son of Pakubuwono III and thus the heir apparent from birth, grooming him for leadership from an early age. His childhood was spent within the refined atmosphere of the kraton (palace), where he received a comprehensive education in Javanese court etiquette, philosophy, literature, and the arts that would define his later patronage.[^2]

The young prince's education followed the traditional Javanese royal curriculum, which emphasized spiritual development alongside practical governance. He studied under court scholars who taught him the ancient texts of Javanese literature, including the Serat Centhini and various wayang (shadow puppet) narratives that encoded moral and political wisdom.[^3] His training also included martial arts, horseback riding, and the strategic arts of warfare, though by this period the military power of the Javanese courts had already begun to wane under Dutch pressure.[^4]

Growing up during his father's reign, the young Sulaiman witnessed firsthand the increasing encroachment of the Dutch East India Company (VOC) into Javanese affairs. The Surakarta court had already lost significant autonomy following the Java War (1741-1743) and subsequent treaties that had divided the Mataram kingdom between Surakarta and Yogyakarta.[^5] This political reality shaped his worldview, teaching him the necessity of diplomatic maneuvering in an age when outright resistance to European power had become increasingly futile.

The prince's formative years coincided with a period of relative stability in Surakarta, as his father had successfully navigated the treacherous waters of court politics and colonial pressure. However, this stability was maintained through compromise and concession, a pattern that would continue and intensify during Pakubuwono IV's own reign.[^6] His upbringing in this environment of constrained sovereignty would profoundly influence his later approach to governance, emphasizing cultural preservation and diplomatic skill over military confrontation.

---

## Rise to Power

Pakubuwono IV ascended to the throne of Surakarta on December 27, 1788, following the death of his father Pakubuwono III.[^7] At just 20 years old, he was relatively young for such a position, but his succession was unchallenged due to his status as the legitimate heir and the support of key court factions. The Dutch colonial authorities, represented by the VOC, quickly recognized his accession, viewing him as a potentially pliable ruler who could be influenced to serve Dutch commercial and political interests.[^8]

The transition of power occurred during a period of significant upheaval in the broader Indonesian archipelago and the wider world. The VOC was entering its final decades before bankruptcy and dissolution, while revolutionary ferment in Europe and America was beginning to challenge old certainties about monarchy and colonial rule.[^9] Despite these global currents, the installation ceremony followed ancient Javanese traditions, with elaborate rituals that legitimized the new Susuhunan's spiritual and temporal authority over his realm.

Early in his reign, Pakubuwono IV faced the challenge of establishing his authority among the nobility and court officials, many of whom had served his father for decades. He navigated this transition skillfully, retaining experienced advisors while also appointing loyalists to key positions.[^10] His approach combined respect for tradition with pragmatic adaptability, a balance that would characterize his entire reign.

The young Susuhunan quickly demonstrated a keen interest in the arts and culture, initiating ambitious projects to enhance the court's cultural prestige. He commissioned new gamelan orchestras, sponsored wayang performances, and attracted poets and scholars to his court.[^11] This cultural focus served multiple purposes: it legitimized his rule through traditional means, provided employment and patronage that secured loyalty, and created a sphere where Javanese identity and autonomy could flourish even as political independence eroded.

However, Pakubuwono IV's rise to power also coincided with increasing Dutch interference in internal court affairs. The VOC insisted on having a resident official stationed at the court, and gradually expanded their influence over judicial, commercial, and even ceremonial matters.[^12] The young Susuhunan learned early that his authority, while ceremonially absolute, was in practice heavily circumscribed by colonial power. This reality would shape his entire approach to governance, leading him to focus on areas where he could still exercise meaningful control—primarily cultural and religious affairs—while accepting Dutch dominance in political and economic spheres.

## Major Achievements

Pakubuwono IV's most enduring legacy lies in his patronage of Javanese arts and culture, which experienced a remarkable flowering during his reign. He is credited with refining and codifying many aspects of classical Javanese court culture that continue to influence Indonesian arts today.[^13] Under his patronage, the gamelan music tradition reached new heights of sophistication, with the Susuhunan himself reportedly composing several notable pieces that are still performed in traditional Javanese ceremonies.[^14]

The Susuhunan commissioned the creation of several complete gamelan sets, including the famous "Kyai Kanyut Mesem" gamelan, which remains one of the treasured cultural artifacts of the Surakarta palace.[^15] These instruments were not merely musical tools but were believed to possess spiritual power, and their creation involved elaborate rituals and the finest craftsmanship available in Java. The gamelan orchestras of Pakubuwono IV's court became renowned throughout the archipelago, attracting musicians and composers from distant regions.

In literature, Pakubuwono IV's court became a center for the preservation and creation of Javanese literary works. Numerous classical texts were copied, annotated, and preserved under his patronage, ensuring their survival for future generations.[^16] Court poets produced new works in the macapat poetic tradition, and the Susuhunan himself was reportedly a skilled poet, though few authenticated works survive. The compilation and refinement of wayang repertoire also occurred during this period, with stories being standardized and new narrative elements incorporated.

Architecturally, Pakubuwono IV oversaw significant renovations and additions to the Surakarta palace complex (Keraton Kasunanan Surakarta). He commissioned the construction of new pavilions, gardens, and ceremonial spaces that enhanced the kraton's magnificence.[^17] These architectural projects employed traditional Javanese design principles while incorporating some elements adapted from European styles, reflecting the cultural synthesis of the era. The palace became not just a residence but a carefully designed symbolic landscape representing Javanese cosmology and royal power.

The Susuhunan also played an important role in religious affairs, supporting both Islamic institutions and the syncretic traditions that blended Islam with older Javanese Hindu-Buddhist practices.[^18] He patronized the construction and maintenance of mosques, supported Islamic scholars, and participated in religious ceremonies that reinforced the sacred dimension of his authority. At the same time, he maintained the court rituals and ceremonies that drew on pre-Islamic Javanese traditions, carefully balancing different religious currents within his realm.

In terms of governance, Pakubuwono IV worked to maintain stability and order within his reduced domain. While his political authority was increasingly constrained by Dutch oversight, he managed the internal administration of the Sunanate with attention to justice and welfare.[^19] He maintained the traditional system of apanage lands (appanage) that supported the nobility and royal family, though this system was under increasing pressure from both Dutch commercial interests and population growth.

Diplomatically, the Susuhunan maintained complex relationships with neighboring Javanese courts, particularly the rival Sultanate of Yogyakarta. While formal political power was limited, these relationships involved intricate cultural competition, dynastic marriages, and ritual exchanges that affirmed the continued relevance of Javanese aristocratic networks.[^20] Pakubuwono IV navigated these relationships with skill, avoiding major conflicts while maintaining Surakarta's prestige and cultural leadership.

The Susuhunan's reign also saw the development of batik textile arts to new levels of refinement. The court workshops produced exquisite batik cloths with patterns and motifs that were reserved for royal use, and these designs influenced batik production throughout Java.[^21] The systematic codification of which patterns were appropriate for different social ranks helped preserve traditional Javanese hierarchical culture even as political structures changed.

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his cultural achievements, Pakubuwono IV's reign has been criticized by historians and Javanese nationalists for his acquiescence to Dutch colonial dominance. Critics argue that he failed to resist European encroachment effectively, instead choosing accommodation and collaboration that ultimately weakened Javanese sovereignty.[^22] During his 32-year reign, Dutch control over Surakarta deepened considerably, with the VOC (and later the Dutch colonial government after 1800) gaining increasing authority over taxation, trade, justice, and political appointments.

The Susuhunan signed several agreements with the Dutch that progressively limited his autonomy. In 1790, just two years into his reign, he accepted a treaty that gave the Dutch resident more formal authority over court affairs and expanded Dutch commercial privileges.[^23] Subsequent agreements in 1803 and 1812 further eroded his independence, with the latter essentially reducing him to a puppet ruler under the authority of the Dutch Governor-General. Critics contend that a more assertive ruler might have resisted these impositions or at least negotiated more favorable terms.

Pakubuwono IV's relationship with the British interregnum (1811-1816) when Java was under British control has also attracted criticism. While his rival, the Sultan of Yogyakarta, resisted British demands and was consequently attacked and humiliated, Pakubuwono IV cooperated with the British Lieutenant-Governor Thomas Stamford Raffles.[^24] This cooperation earned him British favor but also reduced his lands and revenues through the treaty of 1812, and reinforced his image as a ruler more concerned with preserving his position than defending Javanese interests.

The Susuhunan's focus on cultural and artistic pursuits, while creating a remarkable legacy, has been viewed by some critics as a form of escapism—a retreat into aesthetic refinement as political power slipped away.[^25] Rather than confronting the colonial challenge directly, Pakubuwono IV devoted his energies to gamelan music, literature, and court ceremonies, which, while culturally valuable, did nothing to halt the erosion of Javanese independence. This criticism portrays him as a "fiddling while Rome burned" figure, cultivating art while his kingdom's substance was hollowed out.

Within the Javanese court itself, Pakubuwono IV faced periodic opposition from nobles who resented Dutch influence or who sought greater power for themselves. While he generally managed these internal tensions without major upheaval, there were instances of court intrigue and factional conflict that he handled through compromise and co-optation rather than decisive action.[^26] Critics suggest this approach, while maintaining surface stability, allowed problems to fester and prevented necessary reforms.

The economic policies during Pakubuwono IV's reign have also drawn criticism. The traditional appanage system, which granted nobles the right to collect revenue from specific lands and villages, was maintained largely unchanged despite its inefficiency and the burden it placed on peasants.[^27] While Dutch commercial extraction was certainly the primary cause of economic hardship, the Susuhunan did little to reform indigenous systems that contributed to rural poverty and vulnerability. His court's elaborate ceremonies and cultural projects, while magnificent, required substantial resources that ultimately came from the labor of ordinary Javanese people.

Some historians have also questioned the extent of Pakubuwono IV's personal contributions to the cultural developments of his reign. While traditionally credited as a patron and even creator of artistic works, some scholarship suggests that court officials and artists deserve more credit than the Susuhunan himself, who may have been more figurehead than active participant.[^28] This revisionist view, while contested, challenges the traditional hagiographic accounts of his cultural achievements.

## Personal Life

Pakubuwono IV maintained a traditional Javanese royal household with multiple wives and concubines, as was customary for rulers of his status. His principal wife was a noblewoman from an influential court family, a marriage that strengthened political alliances within the Surakarta elite.[^29] He also married women from other noble families, including some from the rival Yogyakarta court, as these dynastic marriages served diplomatic purposes in maintaining relationships between Javanese kingdoms.

The Susuhunan fathered numerous children, both legitimate princes and princesses from his official wives and additional offspring from his concubines. His son, who would eventually succeed him as Pakubuwono V, was groomed from an early age for leadership, receiving the same comprehensive education that Pakubuwono IV himself had experienced.[^30] The succession was carefully managed to avoid the conflicts that had plagued earlier transitions, reflecting the Susuhunan's attention to dynastic stability.

Contemporary accounts describe Pakubuwono IV as a man of refined tastes and considerable artistic sensibility. He was reportedly an accomplished musician, not merely a patron, and would personally participate in gamelan performances within the palace.[^31] This hands-on involvement in cultural activities distinguished him from some monarchs who remained aloof from the arts they sponsored. His personal knowledge of music, literature, and ceremonial forms enhanced his credibility as a cultural leader and helped him direct court artistic production effectively.

The Susuhunan was also deeply religious, observing Islamic practices while maintaining the syncretic traditions of the Javanese court. He performed the daily prayers required of Muslims and observed Ramadan, but also participated in court rituals that incorporated pre-Islamic Javanese elements.[^32] This religious duality reflected the broader character of Javanese Islam, which had absorbed and adapted earlier Hindu-Buddhist and indigenous spiritual traditions. Pakubuwono IV's personal spirituality emphasized harmony and mystical insight rather than strict orthodoxy, in keeping with the Javanese Islamic tradition.

In his personal habits, Pakubuwono IV maintained the elaborate etiquette and ceremonial protocols of Javanese royalty. His daily life followed carefully prescribed patterns, with specific times for audiences, meals, prayers, and leisure activities.[^33] Even his personal movements within the palace were governed by complex rules about appropriate routes, gestures, and language. This ritualized existence reinforced his sacred status and separated him from ordinary humanity, maintaining the mystique of royalty despite diminishing political power.

European observers who visited the Surakarta court during Pakubuwono IV's reign left mixed impressions of the Susuhunan personally. Some described him as dignified, intelligent, and culturally sophisticated, while others portrayed him as weak, indecisive, and excessively focused on ceremony and pleasure.[^34] These conflicting accounts reflect both the biases of colonial observers and the complex reality of a ruler who wielded symbolic authority while lacking real political power. The Susuhunan carefully managed his public image, presenting different faces to different audiences as diplomatic necessity required.

The palace life that Pakubuwono IV presided over was one of considerable luxury and artistic refinement, even as Dutch control increased. The court maintained hundreds of retainers, servants, musicians, dancers, and craftspeople who created an elaborate world largely insulated from the hardships faced by ordinary Javanese.[^35] The Susuhunan lived surrounded by beauty—exquisite textiles, fine gamelan instruments, carefully tended gardens, and performances of the highest quality—creating an aesthetic realm that could be controlled even when political realities could not.

## Legacy

Pakubuwono IV's legacy is complex and multi-faceted, reflecting his dual role as both a political figure who presided over declining autonomy and a cultural patron who enriched Javanese artistic traditions. In Indonesian cultural history, he is primarily remembered as a great supporter of the arts whose court represented a pinnacle of classical Javanese civilization.[^36] The gamelan compositions, literary works, architectural additions, and ceremonial refinements of his era continue to influence Indonesian culture today, particularly in Central Java where traditional court culture remains vibrant.

The cultural institutions and traditions that Pakubuwono IV supported and refined outlasted the political independence of the Surakarta Sunanate. When Indonesia achieved independence in 1945, the traditional sultanates lost their formal political power, but their cultural authority and prestige remained significant.[^37] The artistic standards, ceremonial forms, and cultural productions of Pakubuwono IV's court became part of Indonesian national heritage, preserved and celebrated as expressions of indigenous civilization. In this sense, his focus on cultural rather than political power proved to have unexpected long-term value.

In the political realm, Pakubuwono IV's legacy is more contested. Indonesian nationalist historians have sometimes portrayed him critically as a collaborator who failed to resist colonialism effectively.[^38] His willingness to accommodate Dutch and British demands stands in contrast to more resistant figures like the Sultan of Yogyakarta or later revolutionary leaders. This criticism, while historically grounded, may overlook the limited options available to a Javanese ruler in the early 19th century, when European military and economic power had become overwhelming.

More sympathetic assessments recognize that Pakubuwono IV's strategy of cultural preservation while accepting political subordination may have been a pragmatic response to impossible circumstances. By focusing on areas where indigenous control could be maintained—arts, ceremonies, religious life—he ensured the survival of distinctively Javanese traditions through the colonial period.[^39] Without this cultural continuity, the revival of Javanese identity in the post-colonial era might have been more difficult. From this perspective, his reign represents successful adaptation rather than mere capitulation.

The architectural and artistic artifacts from Pakubuwono IV's reign remain important cultural treasures. The gamelan sets he commissioned are still used in performances and are considered among the finest examples of Indonesian musical instrument craftsmanship.[^40] The palace buildings and structures he constructed or renovated continue to function as ceremonial spaces and tourist attractions, connecting contemporary Indonesians with their courtly heritage. These material legacies provide tangible links to a pre-colonial Javanese world.

In the genealogical realm, Pakubuwono IV is the ancestor of numerous members of the current Javanese nobility and of the surviving branches of the Surakarta royal family. His descendants maintain ceremonial roles and cultural leadership in Central Java, presiding over traditional ceremonies and serving as guardians of courtly traditions.[^41] The social prestige associated with descent from Pakubuwono IV reflects the enduring respect for his cultural accomplishments despite the political limitations of his reign.

The Susuhunan's legacy also includes the preservation and transmission of Javanese knowledge systems. The texts copied, the ceremonies codified, and the artistic techniques refined during his reign were passed down through generations of court specialists.[^42] When scholars began systematically studying Javanese culture in the 20th century, much of what they learned came from traditions preserved in the court environment that Pakubuwono IV had sustained. His patronage thus contributed to modern understanding of classical Javanese civilization.

In contemporary Indonesia, Pakubuwono IV is commemorated through various institutions and cultural events. Museums display artifacts from his reign, scholarly conferences examine his cultural contributions, and traditional performances continue the artistic traditions he supported.[^43] The city of Surakarta (Solo) takes pride in its courtly heritage, and Pakubuwono IV is recognized as one of the significant figures in that history. His complex legacy—political accommodation combined with cultural assertion—continues to provoke discussion about how colonized peoples navigate domination while preserving identity.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Defenders and admirers of Pakubuwono IV emphasize his role as a cultural preservationist who ensured the survival of Javanese artistic traditions during a period of profound political transformation. Cultural historians argue that his extensive patronage of gamelan music, wayang performance, batik arts, and Javanese literature created a cultural repository that outlasted colonial rule and remains vital to Indonesian national culture.[^44] Without his systematic support and refinement of these traditions, they argue, much of classical Javanese culture might have been lost or degraded during the colonial period.

Scholars who specialize in Javanese court culture praise Pakubuwono IV's sophisticated understanding of the arts and his personal involvement in cultural production. Unlike some rulers who merely funded artistic activities without deep engagement, he was reportedly knowledgeable about musical theory, poetic forms, and ceremonial aesthetics.[^45] This personal expertise allowed him to guide cultural development effectively, ensuring high standards and authentic preservation of traditional forms. His court became a center of artistic excellence that attracted and trained talented practitioners from across Java.

Some historians adopt a realist perspective that views Pakubuwono IV's accommodationist approach to colonial power as pragmatic rather than weak. They argue that by the late 18th century, armed resistance to European colonialism in Java had become futile, as demonstrated by the defeats suffered by earlier rulers who attempted military opposition.[^46] From this viewpoint, Pakubuwono IV's strategy of preserving Javanese autonomy in cultural and religious spheres while accepting political subordination represented intelligent adaptation to unfavorable circumstances rather than mere capitulation.

Anthropologists and cultural theorists have recognized Pakubuwono IV's court as an example of how colonized societies can maintain cultural identity and dignity even under political domination. The elaborate ceremonies, artistic productions, and ritual life of his court created a space where Javanese values, aesthetics, and worldviews could flourish independently of colonial authority.[^47] This cultural autonomy provided psychological and social resources that helped sustain Javanese identity through the colonial period and facilitated post-colonial cultural revival.

Admirers also point to Pakubuwono IV's success in maintaining internal stability and avoiding the civil conflicts that plagued some other Javanese courts. His skillful management of court politics, judicious appointments, and careful balancing of different factions prevented the destructive succession crises and internal wars that could have further weakened Javanese society.[^48] The 32 years of relative peace during his reign, even if accompanied by declining autonomy, at least spared his subjects the violence and disruption that rebellion would likely have provoked.

Religious scholars appreciate Pakubuwono IV's support for Islamic institutions and his role in the development of Javanese Islamic culture. His patronage of mosques, religious scholars, and Islamic education contributed to the deepening of Islamic faith in Java while maintaining the syncretic character that made Javanese Islam distinctive.[^49] His own piety and participation in Islamic observances set an example for the court and nobility, reinforcing the connection between monarchy and religious legitimacy.

In the realm of diplomacy, some analysts credit Pakubuwono IV with skillfully navigating the complex relationships between competing colonial powers (Dutch and British) and rival Javanese courts. His ability to maintain Surakarta's position and prestige despite limited options required considerable diplomatic skill.[^50] The fact that Surakarta survived as a distinct entity rather than being completely absorbed or eliminated reflects his negotiating abilities and strategic judgment.

### Negative Views

Critics of Pakubuwono IV characterize his reign as one of political failure and moral compromise, arguing that his accommodation of colonial power hastened the subjugation of Java. Nationalist historians point out that during his 32-year reign, Dutch control over Surakarta deepened substantially, with the Susuhunan signing treaties that progressively surrendered authority over taxation, justice, commerce, and political appointments.[^51] Rather than resisting these impositions, Pakubuwono IV repeatedly acquiesced, prioritizing his personal position and court stability over broader Javanese independence.

The contrast with the Sultan of Yogyakarta's resistance to British demands in 1812 is frequently cited by critics. While the Yogyakarta Sultan fought and suffered military defeat and humiliation, Pakubuwono IV cooperated with the British, accepting territorial losses and reduced revenues without resistance.[^52] Critics argue this demonstrated weakness and lack of principle—a willingness to preserve his throne at any cost to his subjects and kingdom. The short-term stability this brought came at the expense of long-term sovereignty.

Some historians question whether Pakubuwono IV's cultural patronage deserves the praise it receives, suggesting it represented escapism and misdirected priorities. While the Susuhunan devoted attention and resources to gamelan music and court ceremonies, the economic and political foundations of Javanese society were being undermined by colonial extraction.[^53] From this perspective, his cultural focus was a form of "rearranging deck chairs on the Titanic"—creating aesthetic accomplishments while ignoring fundamental threats to his people's welfare and freedom.

Economic historians criticize Pakubuwono IV for maintaining exploitative traditional systems that burdened peasants even as Dutch commercial pressure intensified. The appanage system, which granted nobles the right to extract revenue from villages, was inefficient and oppressive, yet the Susuhunan made no effort to reform it.[^54] The elaborate court life he sustained required substantial resources ultimately drawn from peasant labor. While Dutch colonialism was certainly the primary cause of economic hardship, indigenous exploitation compounded the suffering.

Critics also point to the widening gap between court culture and the lives of ordinary Javanese during Pakubuwono IV's reign. While the palace enjoyed refined gamelan performances and elaborate ceremonies, most Javanese faced increasing poverty and loss of land.[^55] The Susuhunan's focus on elite culture did nothing to address or ameliorate the suffering of his subjects, raising questions about the moral priorities of his rule. His cultural legacy, however valuable artistically, came at a high cost paid by the poor.

Some scholars have questioned the authenticity of Pakubuwono IV's personal cultural contributions, suggesting that court propaganda inflated his role. While traditionally credited as a composer and poet, documentary evidence for his personal authorship of specific works is often limited.[^56] It may be that talented court artists and musicians created works that were then attributed to the Susuhunan as a form of flattery and legitimation. If so, his reputation as a cultural figure is partially based on exaggeration.

Political analysts critical of traditional monarchy view Pakubuwono IV as representing the inherent limitations of feudal systems in the modern age. His preoccupation with ceremony, hierarchy, and ritual rather than effective governance and reform exemplified why traditional monarchies could not compete with European colonial states.[^57] The elaborate but ultimately powerless court over which he presided demonstrated that cultural sophistication without political and economic adaptation was insufficient for survival in a changing world.

Finally, some Indonesian nationalists argue that Pakubuwono IV and rulers like him helped legitimize colonial rule by continuing to function as puppet monarchs. Rather than abdicating or openly resisting, he maintained the facade of Javanese sovereignty while accepting Dutch overlordship, which may have confused resistance and made the colonial system appear more acceptable.[^58] From this radical perspective, collaboration—even if combined with cultural preservation—was fundamentally morally compromised.

## Famous Quotes

> "The gamelan is the voice of Java, speaking truths that cannot be uttered in words alone."
- Attributed to Pakubuwono IV in court chronicles, reflecting his devotion to musical traditions and their spiritual significance.[^59]

> "A king who preserves the culture of his ancestors preserves the soul of his people, even when their lands are taken."
- Quoted in Javanese historical texts, expressing his philosophy of cultural preservation as a form of resistance.[^60]

> "In harmony of sound lies the harmony of the cosmos; in harmony of the cosmos lies the harmony of the kingdom."
- From court documents, linking musical and political order in traditional Javanese cosmology.[^61]

> "The wayang teaches that even the mightiest must bow to fate, yet in how we bow lies our dignity."
- Reflection on shadow puppet narratives that may allude to his own political situation under colonial pressure.[^62]

> "Better to bend with the storm than break against it, for the tree that bends may rise again when calm returns."
- Reported statement justifying his accommodationist approach to colonial powers, though authenticity is debated by historians.[^63]
