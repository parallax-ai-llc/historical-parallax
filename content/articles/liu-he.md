## Summary

Liu He is a Chinese economist and politician who served as Vice Premier of the People's Republic of China from 2018 to 2023, playing a pivotal role in shaping China's economic policy during a critical period of trade tensions with the United States. A trusted advisor to President Xi Jinping since their youth, Liu rose through the ranks of China's economic planning apparatus to become the country's top economic official and chief negotiator during the US-China trade war. Known for his market-oriented views and pragmatic approach to economic reform, he has been instrumental in formulating China's response to technological competition, financial sector reform, and the COVID-19 pandemic's economic fallout. Despite his influence, Liu has also faced criticism for China's mounting debt levels, regulatory crackdowns on the tech sector, and the challenges of navigating between market reforms and party control.

---

## Early Life and Background

Liu He was born in 1952 in Beijing, China, during the early years of the People's Republic.[^1] His early life coincided with some of the most tumultuous periods in modern Chinese history, including the Great Leap Forward and the Cultural Revolution. Despite the political upheaval of the era, Liu managed to pursue education during a time when traditional schooling was often disrupted by revolutionary fervor.

Liu's formative years were shaped by the intellectual environment of Beijing, where he developed an early interest in economics and policy. During the Cultural Revolution (1966-1976), like many of his generation, Liu's formal education was interrupted. However, he used this period to engage in self-study and develop the analytical skills that would later define his career.[^2]

After the Cultural Revolution ended, Liu was among the first generation to benefit from the restoration of China's university entrance examination system in 1977. He enrolled at Renmin University of China, one of the country's premier institutions for economics and social sciences, where he studied industrial economics.[^3] This education provided him with a strong foundation in economic theory and planning, which was particularly valuable during China's transition from a planned to a market-oriented economy.

Liu's early career began in the 1980s, a period of dramatic economic reform under Deng Xiaoping's leadership. He joined the State Planning Commission (later reorganized as the National Development and Reform Commission), where he gained extensive experience in macroeconomic policy and industrial planning.[^4] During this period, Liu also pursued advanced studies, earning a master's degree in public administration from Harvard University's Kennedy School of Government in 1995, which exposed him to Western economic thought and policy-making approaches.[^5]

---

## Rise to Power

Liu He's rise through China's political and economic hierarchy was marked by a combination of technical expertise, political acumen, and strategic relationships. His early work at the State Planning Commission positioned him at the heart of China's economic reform efforts during the 1980s and 1990s, a period when the country was rapidly transforming from a centrally planned economy to one that incorporated market mechanisms.[^6]

A crucial factor in Liu's ascent was his long-standing friendship with Xi Jinping, which reportedly dates back to their youth when both were sent to the countryside during the Cultural Revolution.[^7] This personal connection would prove instrumental in Liu's later career, as Xi rose through the ranks of the Communist Party. However, Liu's advancement was not solely based on personal relationships; he built a reputation as a thoughtful economist with a deep understanding of both Chinese conditions and international economic trends.

During the 1990s and 2000s, Liu held various positions within China's economic planning apparatus, including stints at the State Council's Development Research Center, where he focused on long-term strategic planning and economic restructuring.[^8] He became known for his work on industrial policy, financial sector reform, and the challenges of managing China's transition to a market economy while maintaining social stability.

Liu's profile rose significantly in the early 2010s when he was appointed to lead the General Office of the Central Leading Group for Financial and Economic Affairs, effectively becoming Xi Jinping's top economic advisor.[^9] In this role, he was instrumental in drafting major economic policy documents and coordinating economic strategy across different government departments. His influence grew as he became the principal architect of several key economic initiatives, including supply-side structural reform and efforts to reduce financial risks in China's economy.[^10]

In March 2018, Liu was appointed Vice Premier of the State Council, with a portfolio covering financial and economic affairs.[^11] This appointment came at a critical juncture, as tensions with the United States over trade were escalating. Liu was quickly designated as China's chief negotiator in trade talks with the US, a role that would define much of his tenure as Vice Premier.[^12]

## Major Achievements

Liu He's tenure as China's top economic official was marked by several significant achievements that shaped the country's economic trajectory during a turbulent period. His work spanned domestic economic reform, international trade negotiations, and crisis management during the COVID-19 pandemic.

**Trade Negotiations and US-China Relations**

Perhaps Liu's most visible achievement was his role as China's lead negotiator during the trade war with the United States. Beginning in 2018, Liu engaged in extensive negotiations with US Trade Representative Robert Lighthizer and Treasury Secretary Steven Mnuchin, ultimately leading to the Phase One trade deal signed in January 2020.[^13] While the agreement had limitations and many issues remained unresolved, Liu's diplomatic skills and economic expertise helped prevent a complete breakdown in US-China economic relations during an extremely tense period.[^14]

Liu's approach to the trade negotiations demonstrated his pragmatic style. He sought to balance the need to make concessions to reach an agreement with protecting China's core economic interests and industrial policy priorities.[^15] His fluent English and familiarity with Western economic thinking, gained during his time at Harvard, made him an effective communicator with American counterparts.[^16]

**Financial Sector Reform**

Liu played a central role in China's ongoing efforts to reform and stabilize its financial sector. He was instrumental in the 2017-2018 deleveraging campaign aimed at reducing excessive debt accumulation in China's economy, particularly in the shadow banking sector.[^17] This initiative, while controversial for its impact on economic growth, helped address systemic risks that had built up in the financial system.

Under Liu's leadership, China also accelerated the opening of its financial sector to foreign investment, removing ownership caps on foreign participation in securities, insurance, and banking.[^18] These reforms, announced in 2018 and implemented over subsequent years, represented a significant liberalization of China's financial markets and were seen as a response to both domestic needs and international pressure.

**Supply-Side Structural Reform**

Liu was a key architect of China's supply-side structural reform initiative, launched in 2015. This comprehensive policy framework aimed to address structural imbalances in the Chinese economy, including overcapacity in heavy industries, excessive leverage in the corporate sector, and inefficiencies in resource allocation.[^19] The initiative involved reducing excess industrial capacity, particularly in steel and coal, cutting corporate leverage, lowering business costs, and strengthening weak economic sectors.[^20]

**Technology and Industrial Policy**

Liu He played a significant role in formulating China's industrial policy, particularly regarding technological development and innovation. He was involved in the development and implementation of the Made in China 2025 initiative, which aimed to upgrade Chinese manufacturing and reduce dependence on foreign technology in strategic sectors.[^21] While this policy attracted international criticism and became a focal point of US-China tensions, it reflected Liu's vision of economic development centered on technological advancement and industrial upgrading.

**COVID-19 Economic Response**

During the COVID-19 pandemic, Liu helped coordinate China's economic response, including fiscal stimulus measures, support for small and medium enterprises, and efforts to stabilize employment.[^22] His experience in crisis management and macroeconomic policy was crucial in navigating the unprecedented economic challenges posed by the pandemic and China's strict zero-COVID policy.

**Institutional Reforms**

Liu contributed to several institutional reforms aimed at improving economic governance in China. He supported the establishment of new regulatory bodies, including the reorganization of financial regulatory agencies and the creation of new mechanisms for coordinating economic policy across different government departments.[^23]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his achievements, Liu He's tenure as China's top economic official was not without significant controversies and criticisms, both from domestic and international observers. These criticisms reflect the enormous challenges of managing China's economy during a period of structural transition and geopolitical tension.

**Debt Accumulation and Financial Risks**

One of the most persistent criticisms of Liu's economic stewardship concerns China's continued accumulation of debt, particularly in the corporate and local government sectors. While Liu championed deleveraging campaigns, critics argue that these efforts were insufficient and inconsistently applied.[^24] China's total debt-to-GDP ratio continued to rise during his tenure, raising concerns about long-term financial stability and the potential for a banking crisis.[^25] Some economists have questioned whether Liu's policies adequately addressed the root causes of debt accumulation or merely postponed a reckoning.

**Regulatory Crackdowns and Market Volatility**

Liu faced criticism for his role in the regulatory crackdowns that began in late 2020 and intensified through 2021, targeting China's technology sector, private education companies, and other industries.[^26] The sudden and sweeping nature of these interventions wiped out hundreds of billions of dollars in market value and created uncertainty among private investors.[^27] While defenders argue these actions addressed legitimate regulatory gaps and social concerns, critics contend they reflected policy incoherence and damaged China's investment climate.

The most dramatic example was the abrupt suspension of Ant Group's IPO in November 2020, which would have been the world's largest stock offering.[^28] Liu's role in this decision, while not fully transparent, contributed to perceptions that political considerations could override market principles in China's economy. This incident, along with subsequent regulatory actions against major tech companies like Alibaba and Tencent, raised questions about the predictability and consistency of economic policy under Liu's leadership.[^29]

**Trade Deal Implementation**

The Phase One trade deal that Liu negotiated with the United States faced criticism on multiple fronts. Chinese critics argued that the agreement required excessive concessions, particularly regarding agricultural purchases and intellectual property protection, without securing meaningful relief from US tariffs.[^30] International observers noted that China fell significantly short of meeting the purchase commitments outlined in the agreement, raising questions about whether the deal represented a genuine resolution of trade tensions or merely a temporary pause.[^31]

**State-Owned Enterprise Reform**

Liu's approach to state-owned enterprise (SOE) reform disappointed some market-oriented economists who hoped for more aggressive privatization and restructuring. Instead of reducing the role of SOEs, Liu's policies often emphasized "strengthening, optimizing, and expanding" state-owned capital.[^32] Critics argue this approach perpetuates inefficiencies, crowds out private sector investment, and reflects prioritization of political control over economic efficiency.

**Handling of Economic Slowdown**

During Liu's tenure as Vice Premier, China's economic growth rate steadily declined, from around 6.9% in 2017 to 3% in 2022 (heavily impacted by COVID-19 policies).[^33] While much of this slowdown reflected structural factors beyond any individual's control, some economists criticized Liu's policy responses as insufficient or misdirected. The zero-COVID policy, in particular, had devastating economic consequences, and Liu faced criticism for not advocating more forcefully for policy adjustments even as the economic costs mounted.[^34]

**Market vs. State Tensions**

A fundamental criticism of Liu's economic philosophy concerns the inherent tension between his stated support for market mechanisms and the reality of increasing state intervention in the economy under Xi Jinping's leadership. Critics argue that Liu, despite his market-oriented education and rhetoric, ultimately accommodated or enabled a shift toward greater state control and party dominance in economic affairs.[^35] This perceived contradiction has led some to question whether Liu's pragmatism crossed into opportunism, sacrificing reform principles for political survival.

**Transparency and Communication**

Liu's policy-making process was frequently criticized for lack of transparency and inadequate communication with markets. Major policy shifts, such as regulatory crackdowns or financial sector reforms, were often announced suddenly without sufficient advance consultation or explanation.[^36] This communication gap created uncertainty and volatility in Chinese markets and frustrated both domestic and international investors seeking to understand the direction of economic policy.

## Personal Life

Liu He has maintained a relatively low public profile compared to some other senior Chinese officials, with limited information publicly available about his personal life. This discretion is consistent with the practices of many high-ranking Chinese Communist Party members, who typically keep their family lives private and maintain a focus on their official responsibilities.[^37]

Liu is married and has at least one daughter, though details about his family members are scarce in public records.[^38] His wife has maintained an especially low profile, with little information available about her background or activities. This privacy extends to other family members, reflecting both personal preference and the political sensitivity surrounding the relatives of senior officials in China.

Colleagues and those who have worked with Liu describe him as scholarly, soft-spoken, and intellectual in temperament. Unlike some of the more assertive personalities in Chinese politics, Liu is known for his analytical approach and careful consideration of complex issues.[^39] His demeanor reportedly reflects his academic background and his years spent studying economic theory and policy.

Liu is known to be an avid reader with broad intellectual interests extending beyond economics. Associates have noted his familiarity with history, philosophy, and literature, suggesting a well-rounded education that extends beyond his professional specialization.[^40] This intellectual breadth has reportedly influenced his approach to economic policy, which often reflects awareness of historical precedents and long-term trends.

His time studying at Harvard in the mid-1990s had a significant impact on his worldview and approach to economics. Liu has spoken positively about his experience in the United States and the insights he gained into market economies and Western economic thinking.[^41] This international exposure distinguishes him from many Chinese officials of his generation and has been reflected in his relatively sophisticated engagement with international economic institutions and foreign counterparts.

Despite his senior position, Liu has maintained a reputation for relative modesty and lack of ostentation. He does not display the trappings of wealth or power as conspicuously as some other officials, though like all senior Chinese leaders, he enjoys access to privileges associated with his rank.[^42]

Liu's long-standing friendship with Xi Jinping has been a defining feature of his political career. The two men reportedly bonded during their youth and maintained contact as both advanced through their respective careers.[^43] This personal relationship has been crucial to Liu's influence, giving him direct access to China's paramount leader and allowing him to shape economic policy at the highest levels. However, it has also created speculation about the extent to which Liu's policy positions reflect his own views versus accommodation to Xi's political priorities.

In terms of health, Liu has generally maintained a rigorous schedule despite his advancing years. However, there have been occasional reports of health concerns, and observers have noted that the intense pressure of his position during the trade war and pandemic years took a visible toll.[^44]

## Legacy

Liu He's legacy as one of China's most influential economic officials of the early 21st century is complex and will likely be debated for years to come. His impact spans multiple dimensions of China's economic development, from domestic reform to international economic diplomacy, and his tenure coincided with a pivotal period in China's transformation.

**Economic Policy Framework**

Liu's most enduring contribution may be his role in shaping the intellectual framework for China's economic policy during the Xi Jinping era. His emphasis on supply-side structural reform, financial risk management, and technological innovation has provided conceptual foundations that will likely influence Chinese economic thinking for years.[^45] Whether one views these contributions positively or negatively depends largely on one's perspective on the broader direction of China's economic model.

His work helped articulate a vision of economic development that attempts to balance market mechanisms with state guidance, technological advancement with social stability, and opening to the world with protection of strategic industries.[^46] This framework, while controversial and still evolving, represents a significant intellectual contribution to debates about China's economic future.

**US-China Economic Relations**

Liu's role in managing US-China economic relations during one of their most turbulent periods will be a significant part of his historical legacy. He served as the principal Chinese interlocutor with American officials during the trade war, helping to prevent a complete breakdown in the world's most important bilateral economic relationship.[^47] While the outcomes of his negotiations remain contested, his efforts to maintain dialogue and find areas of agreement during a period of intense geopolitical competition were notable.

The long-term trajectory of US-China economic relations will inevitably shape assessments of Liu's legacy. If the relationship stabilizes and economic interdependence is maintained, his negotiations may be viewed as having successfully navigated a difficult period. If decoupling accelerates and relations deteriorate further, his approaches may be seen as insufficient to address fundamental conflicts.[^48]

**Financial Sector Transformation**

Liu's work on financial sector reform, including opening to foreign investment and addressing systemic risks, represents another significant legacy. He helped oversee important changes in China's financial architecture during a period when the sector was becoming increasingly complex and interconnected with global markets.[^49] The stability or instability of China's financial system in coming years will significantly influence how his tenure is remembered.

**Technology and Industrial Policy**

Liu's involvement in formulating China's technology and industrial policies, including Made in China 2025, positions him as a key figure in China's push for technological self-sufficiency and industrial upgrading. This agenda has become increasingly central to China's economic strategy and has significant implications for global technology competition.[^50] His legacy in this area will depend partly on whether China achieves its technological ambitions and how the international community responds to its industrial policies.

**Political Economy Evolution**

Perhaps most fundamentally, Liu's tenure coincided with and influenced a significant shift in China's political economy toward greater state involvement and party control. His legacy includes both his contributions to this shift and the contradictions it reveals. As an economist trained in market principles who presided over a period of increasing state intervention, Liu embodies the tensions at the heart of contemporary Chinese economic policy.[^51]

Supporters view Liu as a pragmatic reformer who adapted market-oriented principles to Chinese conditions and political realities, while critics see him as someone who compromised reform principles to accommodate political imperatives.[^52] This fundamental disagreement about his role will likely persist in historical assessments.

**Institutional Development**

Liu contributed to the development of economic policy-making institutions and coordination mechanisms within the Chinese government. His work in strengthening the Central Leading Group for Financial and Economic Affairs and improving policy coordination across agencies has had lasting impact on how economic policy is formulated and implemented in China.[^53]

**Global Economic Governance**

Liu's engagement with international economic institutions and his role in discussions about global economic governance have positioned him as one of China's most internationally engaged economic officials. His contributions to dialogues about reforming international economic institutions and China's role in the global economy will be part of his lasting impact.[^54]

As Liu retired from his position as Vice Premier in March 2023, at the conclusion of the 14th National People's Congress, assessments of his legacy began to take shape. His successor, He Lifeng, represents a different profile—less market-oriented and more closely associated with state planning—suggesting a potential shift in China's economic policy direction that may, in retrospect, define the distinctiveness of Liu's approach.[^55]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Supporters and sympathetic analysts of Liu He emphasize several aspects of his tenure that they view as accomplishments given the challenging circumstances he faced. These perspectives highlight his pragmatism, technical competence, and efforts to maintain economic stability during turbulent times.

**Pragmatic Economic Management**

Many economists credit Liu with skillfully managing China's economy through a particularly difficult period, including the trade war with the United States, increasing domestic economic challenges, and the COVID-19 pandemic.[^56] Barry Naughton, a prominent scholar of Chinese economics at UC San Diego, has noted Liu's sophisticated understanding of economic policy and his ability to navigate complex political and economic constraints.[^57]

Supporters argue that Liu's supply-side structural reforms, while not universally successful, represented a serious attempt to address genuine structural problems in the Chinese economy, including overcapacity, excessive leverage, and inefficient resource allocation.[^58] These were difficult issues that previous leaders had avoided or addressed only superficially, and Liu's willingness to tackle them demonstrated policy courage.

**Financial Stability**

Defenders of Liu's record point to the fact that China avoided a financial crisis during his tenure despite significant risks in the banking sector, shadow banking system, and local government debt.[^59] The deleveraging campaign he championed, while painful in some respects, arguably reduced systemic risks and made the financial system more resilient. His work in opening the financial sector to foreign investment is seen by some as an important step toward modernization and integration with global markets.[^60]

**Trade War Management**

Some international relations experts credit Liu with preventing an even worse outcome in the US-China trade conflict. Given the political constraints he faced domestically and the aggressive negotiating stance of the Trump administration, Liu's ability to reach the Phase One agreement represented a reasonable compromise that maintained some economic engagement between the world's two largest economies.[^61] His diplomatic skills and understanding of American perspectives, enhanced by his Harvard education, facilitated dialogue that might otherwise have broken down completely.[^62]

**Intellectual Contribution**

Scholars who study Chinese economic thought recognize Liu as one of the more intellectually sophisticated economic policy-makers in recent Chinese history. His writings and speeches demonstrate engagement with complex economic theory and awareness of international economic trends.[^63] Nicholas Lardy of the Peterson Institute for International Economics has acknowledged Liu's genuine understanding of market economics, even while questioning some of his policy choices.[^64]

**Institutional Development**

Liu's contributions to strengthening economic policy coordination mechanisms and improving the institutional framework for economic decision-making are viewed positively by some analysts. His work in the Central Leading Group for Financial and Economic Affairs helped create more systematic approaches to economic policy formulation.[^65]

### Negative Views

Critics of Liu He's tenure focus on what they view as missed opportunities for reform, policy mistakes, and accommodation to political pressures at the expense of economic principles. These negative assessments come from both market-oriented economists disappointed by the direction of Chinese economic policy and critics of China's political system more broadly.

**Reform Retreat**

The most fundamental criticism from market-oriented economists is that Liu presided over a retreat from market-oriented reforms and a return to greater state control of the economy. Despite his rhetoric about markets playing a "decisive role" in resource allocation, critics argue that the reality during his tenure was increasing state intervention, growing power of state-owned enterprises, and diminishing space for private sector dynamism.[^66]

Minxin Pei, a prominent critic of Chinese governance, has argued that Liu ultimately served as an enabler of Xi Jinping's statist economic agenda, lending technocratic credibility to policies that undermined market mechanisms.[^67] From this perspective, Liu's market-oriented credentials were used to legitimize policies that actually moved China away from market reforms.

**Regulatory Chaos**

Critics point to the regulatory crackdowns of 2020-2021 as evidence of policy incoherence and poor economic management under Liu's leadership. The sudden, sweeping interventions in the technology sector, private education, and other industries destroyed enormous amounts of wealth and severely damaged investor confidence.[^68] Michael Pettis, a finance professor at Peking University, has criticized these actions as reflecting poor understanding of the relationship between regulatory stability and economic development.[^69]

**Debt Problem Unresolved**

Despite Liu's focus on deleveraging and financial risk management, China's debt problems have continued to worsen by most measures. Critics argue that his policies failed to address the fundamental drivers of debt accumulation—including soft budget constraints for state-owned enterprises, local government financing incentives, and monetary policy that favors credit expansion.[^70] The result has been continued buildup of financial risks that threaten long-term economic stability.

**Trade Deal Failure**

Many international trade experts view the Phase One trade deal that Liu negotiated as a failure on multiple levels. China did not meet its purchase commitments, the agreement did not address fundamental structural issues in US-China trade relations, and it legitimized managed trade approaches that undermine the multilateral trading system.[^71] Chad Bown of the Peterson Institute documented China's systematic failure to meet the agreement's targets and questioned whether Liu ever seriously intended to fulfill the commitments.[^72]

**Missed Reform Opportunities**

Critics argue that Liu had opportunities to advance significant economic reforms but failed to do so, whether due to political constraints, personal limitations, or unwillingness to challenge vested interests. State-owned enterprise reform remained superficial, market access for private firms actually diminished in some sectors, and financial sector liberalization proceeded far more slowly than initially promised.[^73]

**Political Subservience**

Perhaps the harshest criticism is that Liu, despite his technical expertise and market-oriented education, ultimately subordinated economic principles to political imperatives. Rather than using his influence to advocate for policies that would promote economic efficiency and dynamism, critics argue he crafted intellectual justifications for Xi Jinping's political agenda.[^74] From this perspective, Liu's legacy is one of talented technocratic enablement of problematic political choices.

Anne Stevenson-Yang, co-founder of J Capital Research, has been particularly critical of Liu, arguing that his policies favored political stability and party control over economic efficiency and that his reputation as a reformer was largely undeserved.[^75]
