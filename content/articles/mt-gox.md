## Summary

Once the world's largest Bitcoin exchange, collapsed after 850,000 Bitcoin worth $450 million were stolen. CEO Mark Karpelès was convicted of manipulating financial records.

## Background

- Mt. Gox began as a trading site for *Magic: The Gathering* cards (“Magic: The Gathering Online eXchange”) before pivoting to Bitcoin.
- By the early 2010s, it processed a large share of global Bitcoin trading volume, making it a key piece of crypto market infrastructure.

## What Happened

- In February 2014, Mt. Gox halted withdrawals and later suspended trading and closed its website.
- The company reported that a large quantity of bitcoin was missing (commonly cited at about 850,000 BTC), attributing losses to theft.
- The collapse highlighted operational and security failures at centralized exchanges, including weak controls over private keys and accounting.

## Aftermath and Legal Proceedings

- Mt. Gox filed for bankruptcy protection in Japan in 2014.
- In subsequent years, proceedings shifted toward a civil rehabilitation process aimed at compensating creditors.
- Mark Karpelès faced criminal charges in Japan; he was convicted for manipulating electronic data (financial records) while being acquitted on other charges.

## Impact on the Crypto Industry

- Accelerated adoption of basic exchange security practices (segregated cold storage, audits, and stricter operational controls).
- Increased regulatory attention to custodial crypto businesses and the risks of centralized custody.
- Became a reference case in later debates about proof-of-reserves, transparency, and consumer protection in crypto markets.

## Key Facts (Commonly Reported)

- Peak influence: a dominant venue for Bitcoin trading in the early 2010s.
- Failure mode: loss/theft of customer assets combined with inadequate internal controls.
- Significance: one of the earliest, most prominent exchange failures in the Bitcoin era.
