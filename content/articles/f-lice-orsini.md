## Summary

Italian revolutionary who attempted to assassinate Napoleon III on January 14, 1858, by throwing three bombs at his carriage in Paris. Eight bystanders were killed but Napoleon survived. The attack ironically led Napoleon to support Italian unification.

## Additional Context

## Political Background

- Orsini was part of the broader 19th-century Italian revolutionary milieu that sought to end foreign domination and establish a unified Italy.
- His trajectory is often discussed alongside republican networks, clandestine organizing, and repeated crackdowns by European states.

## The 1858 Attack (High-level)

- The attempt on Napoleon III used explosive devices thrown at the imperial carriage as it arrived at the opera in Paris.
- While Napoleon III survived, bystanders were killed and injured, making the event both a political and public-safety shock.

## Consequences and “Orsini Affair”

- The assassination attempt triggered diplomatic tensions and domestic security responses in France.
- Historically, the episode is sometimes portrayed as a paradox: the attack helped push Napoleon III toward policies that eventually favored Italian unification.

## Legacy

- Orsini’s “martyr” framing in some nationalist narratives contrasts with the indiscriminate harm caused by the bombing.
- The event remains a reference point in discussions of political violence, propaganda effects, and unintended strategic outcomes.
