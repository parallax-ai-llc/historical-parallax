## Summary

Clinton Davisson shared the 1937 Nobel Prize for experimentally proving electron diffraction, confirming wave-particle duality.

## Additional Context

- **Key discovery:** Davisson’s work helped demonstrate that electrons can exhibit wave-like behavior through diffraction patterns, supporting de Broglie’s hypothesis.
- **Davisson–Germer experiment:** The classic experiment is often taught as a foundational result in quantum mechanics, showing diffraction of electrons from a nickel crystal.
- **Why it mattered:** The finding provided direct experimental evidence for wave–particle duality, a central concept in the development of modern physics.

## Recognition

- **Nobel Prize (1937):** Davisson shared the prize with George Paget Thomson, who independently demonstrated electron diffraction using a different experimental approach.
