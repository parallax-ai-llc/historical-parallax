## Summary

American hacktivist associated with Anonymous who hacked the private intelligence firm Stratfor in 2011, leaking millions of emails to WikiLeaks. He also hacked law enforcement and corporate systems. Sentenced to 10 years.

## Background and ideology (high level)

Hammond is commonly described as part of the “hacktivist” milieu, where computer intrusions are framed by participants as political protest.
In that framing, targets are chosen for symbolic value (e.g., corporations, government-linked entities, or security firms) rather than for direct financial gain.
Critics counter that the harm to victims and third parties (especially through data exposure) can be substantial regardless of intent.

## Stratfor breach (2011)

The 2011 intrusion into Stratfor became notable because of the scale of material obtained and the subsequent publication through WikiLeaks.
The event reinforced public awareness that private-sector organizations can hold large volumes of sensitive correspondence and client information.
It also illustrated the “secondary impact” problem: even if a firm is the immediate target, many individuals mentioned in communications can be affected.

## Investigation and the role of informants

Public reporting on the case has discussed the use of cooperating individuals and undercover online engagement in identifying participants.
Cybercrime investigations often combine digital forensics with traditional law-enforcement techniques such as informants, plea agreements, and controlled communications.
These methods can accelerate attribution, but they also raise debates about proportionality, entrapment concerns, and the ethics of cooperation.

## Charges and sentencing context

Hammond pleaded guilty to charges related to computer hacking.
The 10-year sentence is frequently referenced in debates around the U.S. Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) and sentencing severity for non-violent offenses.
Supporters of harsher sentencing emphasize deterrence and the systemic risks posed by large-scale breaches.
Critics emphasize that penalties can be uneven and that data disclosure cases can involve complex motivations.

## Legacy and discussion

The Hammond case is often used as a reference point for:

- The blurred boundary between political activism and criminal intrusion
- The downstream harms of mass data leaks
- How law enforcement adapts to decentralized online groups
