## Summary

Ralph Steinman was a Canadian-American immunologist who shared the 2011 Nobel Prize for discovering dendritic cells.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1943 | Born in Montreal |
| 1973 | Discovered dendritic cells |
| 2011 | Awarded Nobel Prize posthumously |

## Scientific Contribution (High-Level)

- Steinman is best known for identifying **dendritic cells**, a class of immune cells central to how the immune system detects and responds to threats.
- His work helped clarify how these cells:
  - capture and process antigens,
  - interact with T cells,
  - influence the initiation and shaping of adaptive immune responses.

## Impact on Immunology and Medicine

- Dendritic-cell biology became foundational for multiple research areas, including:
  - vaccine design and adjuvants,
  - cancer immunotherapy concepts,
  - understanding autoimmunity and immune tolerance.
- The discovery is often cited as a turning point that expanded knowledge of antigen presentation beyond earlier models.

## Nobel Prize Context

- The 2011 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine recognized insights related to immune activation and regulation.
- Steinman’s award drew attention because it was announced shortly after his death, raising discussion around the Nobel committee’s rules on posthumous awards.

## Legacy

- Steinman’s findings continue to be referenced in modern immunology as dendritic cells remain a central concept in both basic and translational research.
- Many later therapies and experimental approaches build on the principle that controlling antigen presentation can modulate immune outcomes.
