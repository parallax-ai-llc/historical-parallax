## Background

- Greensill is an Australian financier known for building a supply-chain finance business that later collapsed.
- He worked in finance roles before founding Greensill Capital, and became a prominent figure in UK political and business circles.[^1]

## Greensill Capital (high level)

- **Business concept**: supply-chain finance (also called reverse factoring), where financiers advance payments to suppliers on behalf of large corporate buyers, then get repaid later.
- **Growth strategy**: Greensill Capital expanded rapidly by packaging receivables into investment products and relying heavily on insurance arrangements intended to reduce perceived risk.[^1]
- **Key relationships**: the firm cultivated large corporate customers and institutional investors; it also developed ties with banks and asset managers distributing related funds.[^1]

## Collapse and aftermath

- The firm’s failure was widely reported as a major shock to the supply-chain finance market and to investors exposed via funds holding Greensill-linked assets.[^1]
- The collapse prompted inquiries into lobbying and conflicts of interest involving former officials and the firm’s attempts to access government-backed support during the COVID-19 period.[^1]

## Key Takeaways

- Rapid growth in credit intermediation can mask concentrated counterparty and insurance dependencies.
- Political access can amplify reputational risk when a financial firm faces distress.
- Complex funding chains (originating receivables → securitization/investment funds → end investors) can transmit losses quickly when confidence breaks.[^1]
