## Summary

The Kashi Vishwanath Temple, dedicated to the Hindu god Shiva, is one of the most famous and sacred Hindu temples in India. Located in Varanasi (also known as Kashi or Benares) on the western bank of the holy Ganges River in the state of Uttar Pradesh, the temple enshrines one of the twelve Jyotirlingas — the most sacred manifestations of Shiva in the Hindu tradition. Varanasi itself is considered the holiest city in Hinduism, a city so sacred that dying there is believed to guarantee liberation (moksha) from the cycle of rebirth.

The current temple structure was built in 1780 CE by the Maratha queen Ahilyabai Holkar on a site adjacent to where earlier temples had stood. In 2021, the Kashi Vishwanath Corridor project, commissioned by Prime Minister Narendra Modi, significantly enlarged and beautified the area around the temple, creating a grand walkway connecting the temple to the Ganges ghats. The temple's distinctive gold-plated spires — covered with approximately 800 kilograms of gold donated by Maharaja Ranjit Singh of Punjab in 1835 — are iconic features of Varanasi's skyline.

The temple receives several thousand pilgrims daily and tens of thousands during major festivals. It is the spiritual epicenter of one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities on Earth, a city that has been a center of learning, philosophy, music, and religious life for at least three thousand years.

## History & Origins

Varanasi is among the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world, with evidence of settlement dating to the 12th century BCE or earlier. Hindu tradition holds that the city is even more ancient, founded by Lord Shiva himself at the dawn of creation. The city's Sanskrit name, Kashi, means "the city of light" and refers to the divine luminosity with which Hindu texts describe the site. The Ganges River, which flows northward at Varanasi in a configuration considered auspicious, is itself a goddess (Ganga Mata) whose waters are believed to purify all sins.

The Kashi Vishwanath Temple has roots extending back thousands of years, though the exact history of the earliest structures on the site is difficult to trace due to the repeated construction, destruction, and rebuilding that characterizes the site. Hindu texts including the Skanda Purana, the Shiva Purana, and the Kashi Khanda describe the sanctity of the Vishwanath linga in terms suggesting an extremely ancient origin.

The temple was reportedly destroyed multiple times during the medieval period by invaders. The most historically documented destruction occurred in 1194 CE when Muhammad of Ghor's forces conquered Varanasi and demolished Hindu temples. A mosque was built on the site. Hindu rulers subsequently rebuilt temples, which were again destroyed — a cycle that reflects the broader pattern of the medieval period.

The most consequential destruction occurred in 1669 CE, when the Mughal Emperor Aurangzeb ordered the demolition of the Kashi Vishwanath Temple and the construction of the Gyanvapi Mosque on its site. The mosque, which stands adjacent to the current temple, was built using the materials of the demolished temple — columns, cornices, and carved elements of the Hindu structure are visible incorporated into the mosque's fabric to this day. The Gyanvapi Mosque has been at the center of intense legal controversy in modern India, with Hindu groups claiming rights to the site.

After Aurangzeb's reign, Hindu rulers began the process of rebuilding. The current Kashi Vishwanath Temple was built in 1780 by the Maratha queen Ahilyabai Holkar of Indore, who was a devout Hindu and one of the most celebrated rulers in Maratha history. She selected a site adjacent to the mosque and constructed the temple that stands today.

In 1835, Maharaja Ranjit Singh of Punjab donated approximately 800 kilograms of gold to be used for gold-plating the temple's twin spires and the dome. This gift gave the temple its popular name of the "Golden Temple" (though this appellation is today more commonly associated with Harmandir Sahib in Amritsar).

## Religious Significance

The Kashi Vishwanath Temple is the most important of the twelve Jyotirlingas — sacred sites where Shiva is believed to be self-manifested as a column (linga) of light. The Shiva Purana records a myth in which Brahma and Vishnu argued over who was supreme, whereupon Shiva manifested as an infinite column of light, and the twelve places where this column of light burst through the earth became the Jyotirlinga shrines. Visiting and worshipping at all twelve Jyotirlingas is considered an especially meritorious pilgrimage.

But Varanasi itself is more than just a Jyotirlinga site — the city is considered the abode of Shiva himself. Hindu texts describe Varanasi as a tirtha — a "crossing place" between the earthly and divine realms — of unparalleled sanctity. The Kashi Khanda of the Skanda Purana teaches that Shiva personally whispers the taraka mantra (a name of God that liberates the soul) into the ear of every being who dies in Varanasi, regardless of caste, creed, or the nature of their life. This teaching has drawn the dying to Varanasi for centuries and is one reason the city's ghats are lined with hospices for the dying.

The linga enshrined in the Kashi Vishwanath Temple is known as Vishwanath ("Lord of the Universe") or Vishveshvara ("Lord of All"). Offerings of milk, flowers, sandalwood paste, and Ganges water are made to the linga throughout the day in a series of ritual offerings (pujas) that follow a precise schedule from before dawn until late at night.

## Architecture & Description

The current Kashi Vishwanath Temple is a relatively compact structure, having been built on a restricted site in the dense urban fabric of old Varanasi. The temple is entered through a series of gates and courtyards. The main spire (shikhara) rises above the inner sanctum (garbhagriha) where the linga is enshrined, and there are two additional spires over flanking shrines.

The three spires are gold-plated — the dominant visual feature of the temple. The main spire rises to approximately 15.5 meters. The gold plating, applied in the 19th century using gold donated by Maharaja Ranjit Singh, gives the temple a gleaming presence that catches the light dramatically against the Varanasi sky.

The interior of the temple is organized around the central sanctum containing the Jyotirlinga, which is a cylindrical stone column about 60 centimeters in height and 90 centimeters in circumference, set in a silver platform. The sanctum is typically crowded with worshippers and priests throughout the day.

The 2021 Kashi Vishwanath Corridor project dramatically transformed the area around the temple. The project involved the demolition of hundreds of buildings to create a wide processional corridor approximately 320 meters long connecting the temple directly to the Manikarnika and Lalita Ghats on the Ganges. The corridor is paved with stone, flanked by colonnaded galleries, and features temples, rest facilities, and viewing areas. The project is controversial — critics argue that thousands of residents were displaced — but it has dramatically increased the temple's accessibility and created a new visual axis connecting the temple to the river.

## Pilgrimage & Rituals

Varanasi is one of the seven sacred cities (Sapta Puri) of Hinduism, and pilgrimage to Varanasi is one of the most important acts of devotion in the Hindu tradition. Pilgrims come to Varanasi for many purposes: to take a purifying bath in the Ganges at the ghats, to worship at the Kashi Vishwanath Temple, to seek liberation for a dying relative, and to perform last rites for the deceased.

The standard pilgrimage program in Varanasi involves bathing in the Ganges at dawn — preferably at the most sacred ghat, the Dasaswamedh Ghat — and then proceeding to the Kashi Vishwanath Temple for morning darshan (ritual viewing of the deity). Devotees bring Ganges water as an offering to the linga, one of the most auspicious acts of Shiva worship.

The Ganga Aarti at the Dasaswamedh Ghat, performed every evening at sunset by teams of priests bearing elaborate multitiered lamps, is one of the most spectacular religious ceremonies in India. Hundreds of worshippers and tourists gather along the ghats to watch as the priests perform choreographed rituals with fire, incense, and flowers, accompanied by music and chanting. The ceremony has been broadcast internationally and is among the most iconic images of Hindu religious life.

Among the annual festivals, Maha Shivaratri (the Great Night of Shiva) is the most important. The entire city erupts in religious celebration, with pilgrims arriving from across India and abroad. The temple is open throughout the night, and the queue for darshan can extend for many hours.

## Modern Significance

Varanasi and the Kashi Vishwanath Temple are among the most politically and culturally significant sites in contemporary India. The city is the parliamentary constituency of Prime Minister Narendra Modi, who has championed the temple corridor project as a statement of Hindu cultural revival. The project's completion in 2021 was accompanied by elaborate inaugurating ceremonies and significant national media attention.

The Gyanvapi Mosque dispute — the question of the mosque built on the site of the earlier Kashi Vishwanath Temple by Aurangzeb — has been a flashpoint of Hindu-Muslim tensions. A 2022 court-ordered survey of the mosque found what Hindu petitioners claimed to be a Shiva linga in the mosque's ablution pool, though Muslim parties disputed this identification. Indian courts have been hearing petitions related to Hindu rights at the site, and the dispute is likely to remain contentious.

The city of Varanasi itself is undergoing rapid transformation, with significant investment in infrastructure, tourism, and heritage preservation under both state and central government programs. The tension between modernization and preservation of the ancient urban fabric that gives Varanasi its unique character is a subject of ongoing debate.

## Timeline

- **c. 1200 BCE or earlier**: Evidence of settlement in Varanasi; the city is among the oldest in the world.
- **6th century BCE**: Varanasi is a major center of learning and commerce; the Buddha delivers his first sermon at nearby Sarnath.
- **1194 CE**: Invading forces under Muhammad of Ghor destroy Hindu temples in Varanasi.
- **1585 CE**: Akbar, the tolerant Mughal emperor, permits the rebuilding of the Kashi Vishwanath Temple.
- **1669 CE**: Emperor Aurangzeb orders the temple demolished; the Gyanvapi Mosque is built on the site.
- **1780 CE**: Maratha queen Ahilyabai Holkar builds the current Kashi Vishwanath Temple on an adjacent site.
- **1835 CE**: Maharaja Ranjit Singh donates gold for the gold-plating of the temple spires.
- **1947 CE**: India gains independence; Varanasi remains a major center of Hindu religious life.
- **2021 CE**: Prime Minister Narendra Modi inaugurates the Kashi Vishwanath Corridor.
- **2022 CE**: Court orders a survey of the Gyanvapi Mosque; controversy over claimed Hindu artifacts intensifies.
