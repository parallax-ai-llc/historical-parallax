## Summary

Mormon Manson who ordered murders of at least 25 people through his Church of the Firstborn.

## Background

- LeBaron emerged from a schismatic strain of Mormon fundamentalism centered on plural marriage after the LDS Church’s 1890–1904 abandonment of polygamy.
- His family operated across the U.S.–Mexico border, particularly in northern Mexico, where some fundamentalist Mormon colonies had existed since the early 20th century.
- He proclaimed himself a prophet and organized followers into what he called the Church of the Firstborn of the Fulness of Times.

## Notability and Criminality

- LeBaron became notorious for directing violence against perceived rivals and defectors, using religious authority to justify killings.
- Multiple murders were attributed to his organization, and he was widely linked to a pattern of intimidation within the movement.
- He was ultimately arrested, convicted, and incarcerated, where he died.

## Legacy

- The case is frequently cited in discussions of coercive religious groups, charismatic authority, and intra-sect violence.
- Media and true-crime accounts often compare his role within his community to other cult leaders because of the combination of apocalyptic claims and organized violence.

## Key Themes

- Schism and succession disputes inside fundamentalist movements can escalate when a leader claims exclusive prophetic authority.
- Cross-border communities and remote settlements can reduce external scrutiny, increasing follower dependence on internal structures.
- The story is often used as a cautionary example of how religious rhetoric can be weaponized to normalize violence.
