## Summary

American investigative reporter killed by a car bomb in Phoenix, Arizona while investigating land fraud and organized crime connections to Arizona politicians. His death inspired the Arizona Project investigation.

## What He Investigated

Bolles worked for *The Arizona Republic* and became known for sustained reporting on corruption and organized crime in Arizona. His work touched topics such as:

- Land deals and real-estate speculation tied to political influence
- Alleged bribery and kickback networks around public contracts
- Organized-crime figures and their local business or political relationships

## The Assassination and Aftermath

The bombing that killed Bolles is widely cited as a turning point for investigative journalism in the United States because it demonstrated that local political corruption stories could carry lethal risk. In response, journalists from multiple outlets collaborated on the **Arizona Project**, an early example of coordinated reporting intended to finish a story when a reporter is attacked.

## Legacy

Bolles is frequently referenced in discussions of press freedom and the idea that threats against journalists can be countered by collaboration rather than silence. His case is also used as an example in journalism ethics and training materials about:

- Verifying sources in high-risk corruption investigations
- Documentation practices that allow colleagues to continue reporting
- The importance of institutional support for investigative teams

## Continuing Relevance

Bolles is remembered not only for the reporting he produced, but for the idea that investigative work can be made more resilient when notes, sources, and leads are structured so that a newsroom can continue the investigation if a reporter is threatened or harmed.
