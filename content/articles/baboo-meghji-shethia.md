## Summary

Indian smuggler known as the 'King of Smuggling' who ran a vast smuggling empire in Mumbai during the 1970s-1980s. He controlled gold and electronics smuggling networks before turning legitimate.

## Background

Baboo Meghji Shethia is primarily documented in secondary sources as an underworld-linked smuggler operating in Bombay (now Mumbai) in the late 20th century.
Accounts describe him as a figure who benefited from high demand for restricted or heavily taxed goods, especially in the period before India’s economic liberalization.

## Smuggling context (high-level)

In 1970s–1980s India, import restrictions and high duties made certain items scarce and expensive through legal channels.
Smuggling networks typically used maritime routes, intermediaries, and layered distribution to move goods into urban markets.
Shethia is commonly associated (in popular and press descriptions) with:

- Gold smuggling into western India.
- Consumer electronics and other high-value goods.
- Financing and coordination roles rather than street-level retail.

## Public profile and law-enforcement attention

Notoriety around Shethia largely comes from the scale attributed to his operations and the label “King of Smuggling.”
Such reputations often reflect a mix of confirmed cases, investigative reporting, and broader underworld lore.
Wikipedia’s summary treatment indicates that his prominence declined as enforcement pressure increased and the business environment changed.

## Later business turn and legacy

Sources commonly state that he later moved toward legitimate business activities.
In historical perspective, his story is often used as an example of:

- How regulatory arbitrage can create incentives for black markets.
- The role of organized logistics and capital in large-scale smuggling.
- The decline of certain smuggling models as policies and trade regimes shift.
