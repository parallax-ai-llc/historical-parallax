## Summary

See Martin Winterkorn. German automaker installed 'defeat devices' in 11 million diesel vehicles to cheat emissions tests, polluting far more than allowed.

## Scope and mechanism (high level)

- The scandal centered on diesel vehicles that used software to recognize regulatory test conditions and to activate emissions controls more aggressively during the test.
- Outside the test cycle, emissions controls could be reduced, producing substantially higher real-world NOx emissions than permitted or advertised.

## Relationship to "Dieselgate"

- "Dieselgate" is a common shorthand for the same core events; "Volkswagen emissions scandal" is often used as the broader umbrella term that covers investigations, global regulatory responses, and the long tail of legal cases.

## Regulatory and legal outcomes (overview)

- **United States**: Major civil settlements (consumer remedies and environmental mitigation) and a criminal resolution.
- **Europe and elsewhere**: A mix of recalls, administrative actions, civil damages litigation, and criminal proceedings in some countries; timelines and outcomes vary by jurisdiction.

## Broader impact

- Prompted expanded use of real-world testing frameworks (and greater scrutiny of laboratory-only certification).
- Became a prominent example in discussions of corporate governance, compliance incentives, and the enforcement capacity of environmental regulation.
- Contributed to strategic shifts in the auto industry toward electrification and away from light-duty diesel in several markets.
