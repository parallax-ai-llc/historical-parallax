## Summary

Jean-Pierre Sauvage is a French chemist who shared the 2016 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for molecular machines.

## Additional Context

## Scientific Contribution (High-Level)

- Sauvage is known for pioneering work in **supramolecular chemistry**, especially the synthesis of molecules with mechanically interlocked architectures.
- A **catenane** is a structure made of two (or more) interlocked molecular rings, analogous to links of a chain.
- Such structures are important because they enable controlled motion at the molecular scale, a key idea behind **molecular machines**.

## Nobel Prize in Chemistry (2016)

- The 2016 Nobel Prize recognized foundational work that made it possible to design and build molecules that can perform mechanical-like functions.
- Nobel citations commonly highlight the progression from interlocked molecules (catenanes/rotaxanes) to systems that can rotate, shuttle, or respond to energy inputs.

## Why It Matters

- Molecular machines are often discussed as a long-term platform for nanoscale devices, responsive materials, and chemical systems that can do work in a directed manner.
- The field also influenced how chemists think about **control** and **function** beyond static molecular structures.

## Selected Concepts (Glossary)

- **Supramolecular chemistry**: the study of chemical systems formed through non-covalent interactions between molecules.
- **Rotaxane**: a molecular architecture in which a ring is threaded onto an axle-like molecule and held in place by bulky end groups.
- **Mechanical bond**: a way of linking parts of a molecule through interlocking rather than traditional covalent connectivity.
