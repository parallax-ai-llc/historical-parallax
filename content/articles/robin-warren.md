## Summary

Robin Warren is an Australian pathologist who shared the 2005 Nobel Prize for discovering H. pylori.

## Discovery of *Helicobacter pylori*

Warren observed spiral-shaped bacteria in gastric biopsy specimens and noted an association with inflammation of the stomach lining.
At the time, peptic ulcers were commonly attributed to stress, diet, and excess acid, and the stomach was thought to be largely sterile due to acidity.
The observation challenged prevailing assumptions in gastroenterology and pathology.

## Collaboration and Evidence

Warren collaborated with Barry Marshall, who pursued culture methods and clinical correlation.
Their work connected *H. pylori* infection with chronic gastritis and many cases of peptic ulcer disease.
The hypothesis gained acceptance as studies showed that eradicating the bacterium could prevent ulcer recurrence.

## Medical Impact

The discovery shifted ulcer management toward antibiotic-based eradication therapy combined with acid suppression.
It also influenced research into gastric cancer risk, as chronic infection and inflammation are associated with increased risk in some populations.
More broadly, it became a landmark example of infectious causes contributing to diseases previously considered non-infectious.

## Recognition and Legacy

Warren and Marshall received the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2005 for their contributions.
Their work is frequently cited as a case study in careful clinical observation, persistence in research, and paradigm change in medicine.
