## Summary

Coordinated bombings across Mumbai on March 12, 1993 that killed 257 people. The attacks were organized by Dawood Ibrahim in retaliation for the Babri Masjid demolition.

## Additional Context

### Background and motives

The March 1993 blasts occurred in a period of heightened communal tension in India following the demolition of the Babri Masjid in December 1992 and subsequent riots in Mumbai (then Bombay). Investigations and court proceedings described the attacks as both retaliatory and intended to destabilize the city’s financial and civic life.

### How the attacks were carried out

- The bombings were coordinated across multiple targets within a short time window on March 12, 1993.
- Devices used military explosives (often reported as RDX) and were placed in vehicles or public areas to maximize casualties and disruption.
- Several key targets were associated with commerce and transportation, including the Bombay Stock Exchange area and major markets.

### Investigation and prosecutions

Indian authorities pursued a large, long-running investigation involving multiple agencies and special anti-terrorism legal frameworks of the era. Trials extended for years, with a mix of convictions for conspiracy, explosives offenses, and direct participation.

Notable elements frequently cited in public reporting include:

- Allegations of involvement by underworld networks linked to Dawood Ibrahim and associates.
- A supply chain for explosives and weapons that used smuggling routes and local facilitators.
- Later appellate rulings that modified some sentences while affirming the overall finding of a coordinated conspiracy.

### Impact and legacy

The 1993 blasts became a reference point for India’s counter-terrorism policy, inter-agency coordination, and the security posture of major cities. They also reinforced the long-standing entanglement between organized crime, illicit finance, and political or communal violence in Mumbai.
