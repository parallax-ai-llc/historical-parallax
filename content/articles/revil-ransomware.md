## Summary

Russian-based ransomware gang responsible for high-profile attacks including JBS Foods ($11 million ransom) and Kaseya (affecting 1,500 businesses). They operated as a ransomware-as-a-service. FSB arrested several members in 2022.

## Organization and Operating Model

- REvil (also written Sodinokibi) is widely described as a ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) operation, where core developers maintain malware and infrastructure while affiliates conduct intrusions.
- In a typical RaaS arrangement, affiliates share a portion of proceeds with operators in exchange for tooling, payment handling, and negotiation support.

## Notable Incidents (High Level)

- The JBS Foods incident highlighted how ransomware can disrupt critical supply chains; reports connected the event to a major meat processor and a large ransom payment.
- The Kaseya attack drew attention because it leveraged a managed service provider (MSP) ecosystem, creating downstream impact across many organizations via a single software supply-chain vector.

## Law-Enforcement Response and Disruption

- Public reporting has described multi-country efforts to identify operators and seize infrastructure, alongside periods where the group appeared to go offline.
- In 2022, Russia’s Federal Security Service (FSB) announced arrests connected to REvil; the long-term operational status and any successor branding have been less clear in open sources.

## Broader Significance

- REvil became a commonly cited case study for the economics of RaaS, including the professionalization of extortion negotiations and the use of data theft to increase leverage.
- The group’s activity is often discussed in policy contexts around cross-border cybercrime, safe-haven dynamics, and the limitations of unilateral enforcement.

## Notes

- Open reporting sometimes uses multiple names for the same cluster (e.g., REvil/Sodinokibi), reflecting attribution uncertainty and shifting branding over time.
