## Summary

Berta Cáceres was a Honduran indigenous rights activist and environmental defender who co-founded the Civic Council of Popular and Indigenous Organizations of Honduras (COPINH) in 1993. A member of the Lenca people, she campaigned tirelessly against illegal logging, dam construction, and resource extraction projects that threatened indigenous lands and waterways. In 2015, she was awarded the Goldman Environmental Prize for her successful campaign to halt construction of the Agua Zarca hydroelectric dam on the Gualcarque River, a site sacred to the Lenca. She was assassinated in her home in La Esperanza in March 2016, becoming a symbol of the deadly risks faced by environmental and indigenous rights defenders worldwide.

## Early Life and Background

Berta Isabel Cáceres Flores was born on March 4, 1971, in La Esperanza, the capital of the Intibucá department in western Honduras. She was a member of the Lenca people, one of the largest indigenous groups in Honduras, with a population of several hundred thousand concentrated in the western highlands. Her mother, Austra Berta Flores López, was a midwife, local politician, and advocate for indigenous rights who had taken in Salvadoran refugees fleeing civil war during Berta's childhood—an experience that shaped her daughter's understanding of displacement, solidarity, and political struggle from an early age.

Growing up in a household where activism was normalized, Cáceres was immersed in discussions of social justice, land rights, and indigenous sovereignty. She witnessed the marginalization of Lenca communities, who had long been dispossessed of traditional territories and excluded from political decision-making. The Lenca's deep spiritual relationship with land and water—particularly rivers, which hold sacred significance in their cosmology—instilled in her a reverence for the natural environment that would define her life's mission.

Cáceres studied at the National Autonomous University of Honduras and was politically active from a young age, joining student movements and grassroots organizing efforts. She helped found COPINH in 1993 in her early twenties, channeling her education and lived experience into an organization that would become one of Central America's most important indigenous advocacy bodies.

## Founding of COPINH and Early Activism

The Civic Council of Popular and Indigenous Organizations of Honduras (COPINH) was established in 1993 to defend the territorial, cultural, and political rights of the Lenca people. Cáceres served as a founding member and later as coordinator of the organization, building a network of indigenous communities across the country. COPINH's early work focused on land titling, resistance to illegal logging operations backed by powerful landowners, and advocacy for the recognition of indigenous rights under Honduran and international law.

Honduras is one of the most dangerous countries in the world for environmental defenders. Large landowners, agribusiness interests, and extractive industries have historically operated with the tacit or explicit support of state security forces, leaving indigenous communities with little legal recourse. COPINH challenged this system through community organizing, legal petitions, and direct action, staging occupations of illegally logged forests and blockades of roads used by timber companies.

Cáceres was arrested multiple times for her activism and received frequent death threats. Rather than retreating, she used each confrontation to publicize the cause and build international support networks. She connected the Lenca struggle with broader movements for indigenous rights across Latin America, attending conferences and building alliances with organizations in Mexico, Guatemala, and beyond. Her ability to articulate local struggles in terms of global frameworks—colonialism, capitalism, and the rights of indigenous peoples under the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples—made her a respected voice in international environmental and human rights circles.

## The Agua Zarca Dam Campaign

The campaign that brought Cáceres international recognition began around 2013, when the Honduran government granted a concession to Desarrollos Energéticos SA (DESA), a private energy company, to build the Agua Zarca hydroelectric dam on the Gualcarque River in western Honduras. The Gualcarque is considered sacred by the Lenca people, who regard it as the dwelling place of spirits and a source of cultural identity, not merely a water resource. Construction of the dam would have flooded Lenca communities, disrupted fishing and agriculture, and desecrated a site of deep spiritual importance.

Critically, the concession was granted without the free, prior, and informed consent (FPIC) of indigenous communities—a right enshrined in the ILO Convention 169 and the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples, both of which Honduras had ratified. COPINH organized community consultations in which Lenca people voted overwhelmingly against the project, but the government and DESA pressed forward regardless.

Cáceres led a sustained campaign of protests, blockades, and international lobbying. She mobilized communities to physically obstruct construction equipment and coordinated with international human rights organizations to pressure financiers. In 2015, the German company Voith Hydro and the Dutch development bank FMO both withdrew from the project following sustained pressure from Cáceres and her allies. The Chinese company Sinohydro, which had been contracted as the main builder, also suspended its involvement. The Agua Zarca project effectively collapsed, representing a landmark victory for indigenous environmental activism in Central America.

## Goldman Environmental Prize and International Recognition

In April 2015, Berta Cáceres was awarded the Goldman Environmental Prize, often called the "Nobel Prize for grassroots environmentalists." The prize, presented annually to environmental defenders from six global regions, recognized her decade-long campaign to protect Lenca lands and her successful effort to stop the Agua Zarca dam. In her acceptance speech, delivered in San Francisco, she invoked the name of the Río Blanco—a river near the dam site—and called on activists worldwide to awaken and defend nature.

The Goldman Prize brought Cáceres unprecedented international visibility. She used the platform to speak at universities, international forums, and before human rights bodies about the violence faced by indigenous land defenders in Honduras and across Latin America. She was explicit about her own vulnerability, describing the death threats she and COPINH members faced regularly. Despite the danger, she refused offers of exile or permanent relocation abroad, insisting that her place was with her community.

The prize also drew attention to the broader situation in Honduras, which had experienced a coup d'état in 2009 that ousted democratically elected President Manuel Zelaya. Human rights organizations documented a sharp increase in repression of social movements and land defenders following the coup, with dozens of activists killed in the years that followed. Cáceres was outspoken about the role of political instability and US support for the post-coup government in enabling violence against indigenous and environmental activists.

## Assassination and Its Aftermath

In the early hours of March 2, 2016, armed intruders broke into Berta Cáceres's home in La Esperanza and shot her to death. A colleague, Mexican activist Nelson García, was killed the following day. The murders provoked international outrage, with human rights organizations, governments, and the United Nations calling for a swift and thorough investigation.

The Honduran government's initial investigation was widely criticized as inadequate. Several men were arrested and convicted of carrying out the killing, but activists and Cáceres's family insisted that the intellectual authors—those who ordered the assassination—had not been identified. Years of legal proceedings and international pressure eventually led to a breakthrough: in 2021, David Castillo, the president of DESA, was convicted by a Honduran court of being a co-conspirator in Cáceres's murder. The conviction was seen as a partial victory, though her family and COPINH continued to call for accountability from the full chain of command, including politicians and other powerful figures alleged to have sanctioned or benefited from her death.

Evidence presented at trial showed that Cáceres had been under surveillance in the period leading up to her killing, with security forces and company operatives monitoring her movements. This implicated not just corporate actors but elements of the Honduran state in her assassination—a finding with troubling implications for the rule of law in Honduras and the safety of environmental defenders more broadly.

## Legacy and Continuing Impact

Berta Cáceres's assassination galvanized environmental and indigenous rights movements worldwide. The international campaign "Justicia para Berta" (Justice for Berta) became a rallying cry that kept pressure on Honduran authorities and their international partners. Her daughter, Bertha Zúñiga Cáceres, took over as coordinator of COPINH and continued her mother's work under similarly dangerous conditions.

In Honduras, the Agua Zarca dam project was formally cancelled in 2017, vindicating the decade of struggle that Cáceres had led. The Lenca communities of the Río Blanco retained their river, though the broader threats to indigenous land and water rights in Honduras continued.

Internationally, her legacy helped shape policy debates about the obligations of corporations and development financiers to respect indigenous rights, and the responsibility of governments to protect environmental defenders. The UN Special Rapporteur on Human Rights Defenders and various treaty bodies cited her case in developing stronger standards for the protection of land and environmental activists.

Cáceres has been commemorated in murals, songs, documentaries, and academic works across Latin America and beyond. She is remembered not only as a martyr but as an organizer of rare skill and courage—someone who built durable institutions, won concrete victories, and refused to be silenced even when facing mortal danger. Her life and death have become a reference point in discussions of the intersections of indigeneity, environmentalism, gender, and political violence in the twenty-first century.

## Historical Significance

Berta Cáceres occupies a unique place in the history of environmental and indigenous rights movements. She demonstrated that grassroots organizing, combined with strategic international advocacy, could halt major infrastructure projects backed by state and corporate power. Her success against the Agua Zarca dam showed that even the most powerful actors could be made accountable to the rights of indigenous communities when those communities were organized, visible, and connected to global solidarity networks.

Her assassination underscored the lethal stakes of environmental activism in Central America and contributed to growing international recognition that land and environmental defenders require specific legal protections. Honduras remained among the most dangerous countries in the world for environmental activists in the years after her death, but her case helped energize legal and diplomatic efforts to address this crisis—including regional human rights instruments such as the Escazú Agreement, which entered into force in 2021 and includes provisions specifically protecting environmental human rights defenders.

Cáceres's life also challenges simplistic narratives about development and progress. Her insistence that rivers, forests, and mountains are not merely resources to be exploited, but living entities with rights and relationships to human communities, anticipated and amplified debates that have since moved to the center of global environmental discourse. In defending the Gualcarque River, she was defending a way of being in the world that offered a profound alternative to extractivist models of development—an alternative that her death made impossible to ignore.
