## Summary

Peruvian Marxist guerrilla group that carried out the 1996-1997 Japanese embassy hostage crisis in Lima, holding hundreds hostage for 126 days. Peruvian commandos stormed the building, killing all 14 hostage-takers.

## Origins and Ideology

The Túpac Amaru Revolutionary Movement (MRTA) emerged in the 1980s as a leftist insurgent organization in Peru. It is commonly described as Marxist and urban-focused compared with the Maoist Shining Path (Sendero Luminoso), though both operated during the same broader period of political violence.

MRTA’s name references Túpac Amaru II, an 18th-century indigenous-led anti-colonial rebel figure, reflecting the group’s attempt to draw legitimacy from nationalist and anti-imperialist themes.

## Methods and Operations (high level)

Open-source summaries attribute to MRTA a mix of guerrilla tactics and urban operations, including:

- armed attacks on security forces
- robberies and extortion to finance operations
- kidnappings and hostage-taking aimed at political leverage

## The Japanese Embassy Hostage Crisis (1996–1997)

In December 1996, MRTA militants seized the residence used for the Japanese ambassador’s events in Lima, taking a large group of guests hostage. Over the following months, many hostages were released in stages while a smaller group remained as negotiations continued.

In April 1997, Peruvian commandos carried out a raid that ended the standoff. The operation killed the MRTA hostage-takers; it also resulted in deaths among the hostages and security forces. The event remains one of the most internationally known episodes associated with MRTA.

## Aftermath and Controversy

The crisis’ end was followed by debate over the conduct of the raid and the treatment of captured militants. Human-rights reporting and later legal proceedings discussed allegations that some militants may have been executed after being subdued; accounts differ across sources and have been politically contested.

## Decline and Historical Significance

MRTA declined sharply after the 1990s due to arrests, loss of capacity, and the changing political environment in Peru. In historical overviews of Peru’s internal conflict, MRTA is often treated as the second major insurgent actor after Shining Path, with a smaller scale but high-profile operations.
