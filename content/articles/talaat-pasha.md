## Summary

Grand Vizier of the Ottoman Empire and chief architect of the Armenian Genocide. He issued the deportation orders that led to the death of 1.5 million Armenians. Assassinated in Berlin by Armenian genocide survivor Soghomon Tehlirian.

## Political Background

Talaat Pasha emerged from the Committee of Union and Progress (CUP), a key political force in the late Ottoman Empire that rose to dominance after the 1908 Young Turk Revolution. During World War I, CUP leaders held decisive influence over internal security, population policy, and wartime governance.

## Role in Wartime Policies

As interior minister and later grand vizier, Talaat was positioned at the center of the state bureaucracy responsible for domestic administration. Historians commonly link his office to the authorization and coordination of deportations and broader policies that led to mass death and dispossession of Ottoman Armenians during 1915–1917, events widely recognized as the Armenian Genocide.

## Flight and Postwar Accountability Efforts

After the Ottoman defeat in World War I, leading CUP figures fled the empire amid political upheaval and international pressure. In the postwar period, there were attempts—both domestic and international—to assign responsibility for wartime atrocities, including proceedings that addressed the conduct of senior officials.

## Assassination and Aftermath

Talaat was killed in Berlin in 1921 by Soghomon Tehlirian, an Armenian survivor whose family was affected by the genocide. The subsequent German trial drew international attention and is often discussed in the history of political violence, memory, and the legal framing of mass atrocity in the early twentieth century.

## Historical Significance

Talaat Pasha remains a central figure in scholarly and public discussions about the causes, mechanisms, and responsibility for mass violence in the late Ottoman Empire. His career is also used as a case study in how wartime authoritarian governance can transform state administrative tools—policing, deportation, and property seizure—into instruments of large-scale persecution.
