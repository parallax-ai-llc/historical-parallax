## Background

Bernard L. Madoff (1938–2021) was an American financier and the operator of one of the largest Ponzi schemes in history. Through the investment-advisory side of his firm, he reported steady, above-market returns to clients over many years, while in reality using incoming client funds to pay redemptions and fabricate account statements.

## How the Fraud Worked (High-Level)

- **Reported strategy vs. reality:** Clients were told returns were generated through consistent trading strategies; the underlying transactions were largely fictitious.
- **Fabricated records:** Account statements and trade confirmations were produced to show trades and balances that did not exist.
- **Liquidity illusion:** Redemptions were funded primarily from new inflows rather than investment profits.

## Consequences and Aftermath

- **Victims:** The losses affected a wide range of investors, including individuals, charities, and institutions.
- **Trustee recovery efforts:** Court-appointed recovery processes sought to return funds by pursuing clawbacks and liquidating assets, often focusing on net investor positions over time.
- **Market and regulatory impact:** The case became a major reference point in discussions of custody, independent verification, auditor oversight, and due diligence.
