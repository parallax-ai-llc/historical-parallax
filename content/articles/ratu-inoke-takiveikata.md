## Summary

Fijian paramount chief convicted of inciting a mutiny at the Queen Elizabeth Barracks in 2000 that resulted in deaths. The mutiny was linked to the George Speight coup attempt.

## Context and background

- In Fiji, **"Ratu"** is commonly used as an honorific for iTaukei (Indigenous Fijian) chiefly figures.
- The 2000 crisis in Fiji included an attempted coup associated with George Speight and subsequent instability within state institutions, including the military.
- The **Queen Elizabeth Barracks** in Suva is the main base of the Republic of Fiji Military Forces and was a focal point during the period.

## Mutiny and prosecution (high-level)

The 2000 mutiny referenced in this article is generally described in sources as a violent incident at the main military barracks, with fatalities. In accounts that discuss Takiveikata, the central allegation is **incitement**—that he encouraged or helped precipitate the mutiny rather than personally carrying out the violence.

Legal proceedings in such cases typically turn on:

- witness credibility and corroboration,
- whether speech/communications amount to incitement under applicable law,
- the chain of events linking political turmoil to actions by armed personnel.

## Why this case is discussed

Takiveikata is often mentioned in summaries of Fiji’s post-coup accountability because it connects:

- chiefly/political influence,
- the 2000 coup-related crisis,
- military discipline and security at a national barracks,
- long-running court processes (trial, sentencing, and appeals).
