## Summary

After abolition, Southern states used the criminal justice system to re-enslave Black men through convict leasing. Prisoners were leased to plantations and mines where thousands died.

## Background

Convict leasing emerged in the post–Civil War South as state governments sought revenue and labor after the abolition of slavery. The practice intersected with Black Codes and later Jim Crow-era policing patterns that criminalized minor or fabricated offenses, disproportionately targeting Black communities.

## How the System Worked

States and local jurisdictions contracted incarcerated people to private businesses and landowners for a fee. Lessees assumed day-to-day control of prisoners, often providing minimal food, shelter, or medical care. Because the state could replace workers by arresting and sentencing more people, lessees had weak incentives to protect health or safety.

## Industries and Conditions

Leased labor was used in plantations, railroads, turpentine camps, and especially coal and iron mining. Historical accounts describe extreme violence, high mortality, and widespread abuse, with prisoners subjected to brutal discipline and hazardous working environments.

## Legal and Political Context

The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery and involuntary servitude except as punishment for a crime, a clause frequently cited in analyses of convict leasing. Reform efforts and investigative reporting periodically exposed abuses, but the system persisted for decades due to political power, economic demand, and institutional racism.

## Decline and Legacy

By the early 20th century, some states shifted from leasing to state-run chain gangs and prison farms, which critics argued continued coerced labor under government control. Convict leasing is widely studied as a mechanism that extended racialized forced labor after emancipation and shaped long-term patterns in Southern criminal justice and labor relations.

## Key Points

- Revenue and labor extraction were central incentives.
- Arrest and sentencing practices determined the labor supply.
- Abolition of leasing did not immediately end coerced penal labor.
