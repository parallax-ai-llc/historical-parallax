## Summary

Dolores Ibárruri Gómez, universally known by her pseudonym "La Pasionaria," was a Spanish politician, writer, and orator who became one of the most iconic figures of the Spanish Civil War. Born into a mining family in Gallarta, Biscay, she joined the Communist Party of Spain (PCE) in 1920 and rose through its ranks to become a deputy in the Spanish Cortes. Her legendary rallying cry "¡No pasarán!" ("They shall not pass!") during the defence of Madrid against Nationalist forces in 1936 became one of the defining phrases of the twentieth century. After nearly four decades in exile in the Soviet Union following the Nationalist victory, she returned to Spain after the death of Francisco Franco and was re-elected to the Cortes in 1977 at the age of 81.

## Historical Significance

Dolores Ibárruri's significance extends far beyond Spanish politics. As one of the most powerful orators of the twentieth century, she gave voice to the Republican cause during the Spanish Civil War at a time when fascism was on the advance across Europe. Her phrase "¡No pasarán!" rallied not only the defenders of Madrid but also the thousands of volunteers from around the world who joined the International Brigades. The slogan became a universal symbol of resistance against fascism and authoritarianism.

Ibárruri's life also embodied the intersection of class struggle, feminism, and anti-fascism. Rising from extreme poverty in a Basque mining community, she defied the social conventions of her time to become one of Spain's most prominent political figures. She championed women's rights and workers' rights within a deeply patriarchal society, and her personal sacrifices—including the loss of her son Rubén, who was killed fighting at the Battle of Stalingrad in 1942—made her a symbol of the human cost of ideological commitment.

During her long exile in the Soviet Union, Ibárruri kept the flame of Spanish Republican memory alive, serving as a rallying point for anti-Franco opposition abroad. Her dramatic return to Spain in 1977, and her re-election to the Cortes at the age of 81, became one of the most emotionally charged moments of the Spanish transition to democracy. She stood in the same parliament from which she had been expelled nearly four decades earlier, a living link between the democratic aspirations of the Republic and the new constitutional order.

Her legacy remains a subject of debate. Admirers celebrate her as a champion of the oppressed and a fearless opponent of fascism, while critics point to her unwavering loyalty to the Soviet Union and her defence of Stalinist policies. Regardless of perspective, Ibárruri's impact on twentieth-century European history—as orator, symbol, and political leader—is undeniable.
