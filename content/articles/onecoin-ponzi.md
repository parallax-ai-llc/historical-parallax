## Summary

OneCoin cryptocurrency was a massive Ponzi scheme that defrauded investors of $4 billion worldwide. Co-founder Karl Sebastian Greenwood was sentenced to 20 years while Ruja Ignatova remains a fugitive.

## How the Scheme Worked

OneCoin was marketed as a cryptocurrency, but investigators and regulators noted that it did not operate like an open, verifiable blockchain network. Instead, participants typically bought “education” packages that came with tokens, and then were encouraged to recruit others in a multi-level marketing (MLM) structure.[^1]

Key elements commonly described in reporting and official warnings include:

- **Centralized control**: balances and “mining” were presented through company-controlled systems rather than a public ledger.[^1]
- **Recruitment incentives**: commissions and bonuses rewarded bringing in new buyers, a pattern consistent with pyramid/Ponzi dynamics.[^1]
- **Limited liquidity**: the ability to cash out depended on internal exchange mechanisms and company rules, rather than market trading on independent exchanges.[^1]

## Regulatory and Law-Enforcement Response

Authorities in multiple countries issued public warnings or took action against OneCoin-related entities as concerns grew about fraud and illegal financial promotion.[^1]

## Legal Proceedings and Aftermath

The case became internationally prominent as prosecutions and civil actions advanced. Ruja Ignatova disappeared and remains a fugitive, while other principals faced charges and sentencing in the United States.[^1]

## Impact

At a high level, the OneCoin affair is often cited as a cautionary example of:

- how “crypto” branding can be used to lend credibility to schemes,
- how cross-border marketing complicates enforcement,
- why transparency and independent verification matter in financial products.
