## Summary

Bill Browder's Hermitage Capital was targeted by Russian officials who stole $230 million in tax refunds. Browder's lawyer Sergei Magnitsky died in prison; Browder became Putin's leading critic.

## Core allegations (high-level)

The case is typically summarized as a complex fraud in which companies connected to Hermitage were allegedly **raided and re-registered**, then used to generate a large **tax refund claim** that was paid out by Russian authorities.

## Sergei Magnitsky and detention

Sergei Magnitsky, a Russian lawyer/accountant working with Hermitage-linked entities, was arrested after providing testimony and materials to investigators. He died in pre-trial detention in 2009.

His death became a focal point for:

- debates over detention conditions and accountability in Russia
- international human-rights advocacy tied to financial crime

## Browder’s advocacy and sanctions policy

Bill Browder subsequently pursued international campaigns pushing for targeted sanctions. The resulting **Magnitsky-style laws** (starting with the U.S. Magnitsky Act) created a model for sanctioning individuals linked to human-rights abuses and, in some jurisdictions, significant corruption.

## Disputed narratives

The Hermitage/Magnitsky story has been contested in Russian official statements and in various legal and media claims. Public-facing summaries often distinguish between:

- the documented sequence of corporate raids, tax claims, and arrests
- contested claims about responsibility and motive

## Broader significance

The case is frequently used as an example of how:

- financial crime and state power can intertwine
- whistleblowing/investigative work can trigger retaliation risks
- sanctions regimes evolved toward naming individual perpetrators rather than only states
