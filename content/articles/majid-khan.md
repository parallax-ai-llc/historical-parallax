## Background

- Majid Khan is a Pakistani national who became associated with al-Qaeda and later entered U.S. custody in the early years of the post‑9/11 counterterrorism campaign.[^1]
- Public reporting about his case is closely tied to the U.S. detention and prosecution systems used for terrorism suspects, including the CIA’s former "black site" program and subsequent transfer to Guantánamo Bay.[^1]

## Detention and Treatment (high-level)

- Khan’s case is frequently cited in discussions of detainee treatment because his sentencing proceedings included a detailed account of abusive interrogation and detention conditions under CIA custody, as summarized in open sources.[^1]
- These accounts have been referenced in broader debates about the legality, oversight, and long-term consequences of coercive interrogation practices.[^1]

## Legal Proceedings and Sentencing

- Khan was prosecuted through the Guantánamo military commission system rather than U.S. federal courts.[^1]
- In 2021, a sentencing hearing drew attention because of testimony describing torture and mistreatment, which was incorporated into the record and widely reported in connection with his plea and cooperation arrangements.[^1]

## Why the Case Matters

- The case is notable for the way it links three themes:
  - The CIA detention program and later transfers to Guantánamo.
  - The military commission process, including plea/cooperation structures.
  - Public, on-the-record descriptions of detainee mistreatment emerging years after the underlying events.[^1]
