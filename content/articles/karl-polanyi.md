## Summary

Karl Polanyi was a Hungarian-Austrian economic historian and social theorist whose 1944 masterwork, *The Great Transformation*, fundamentally challenged the assumptions of classical liberal economics. By arguing that free markets are not natural phenomena but historically constructed institutions requiring sustained political effort to create and maintain, he offered one of the twentieth century's most penetrating critiques of market society.

His concept of the "double movement" — the simultaneous drive to expand self-regulating markets and the counter-movement of social protection that resists them — remains a foundational framework in economic sociology and political economy. At a time when neoliberalism reshaped global policy, Polanyi's work experienced a major revival, making him one of the most cited heterodox economists of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries.

## Early Life & Background

Born on October 25, 1886, in Vienna to a Hungarian-Jewish family, Polanyi grew up in Budapest during the intellectual ferment of the late Austro-Hungarian Empire. His father, Mihály Pollacsek, was a railway entrepreneur; his mother, Cecília Wohl, ran a prominent intellectual salon. The household nurtured vigorous debate across philosophy, literature, and politics.

Polanyi studied law at the University of Budapest, graduating in 1909, and became a founding member of the Galileo Circle, a radical student movement that engaged with progressive and socialist ideas. His early intellectual formation was shaped equally by Marx, Robert Owen, and the guild socialism of G.D.H. Cole.

## Rise to Prominence

After serving in the Austro-Hungarian cavalry during World War I, Polanyi settled in Vienna, where from 1924 to 1933 he worked as a senior editor at *Der Österreichische Volkswirt* (The Austrian Economist), one of Europe's leading financial weeklies. In this role he engaged directly with the Austrian School economists — particularly Ludwig von Mises — producing sharp critiques of market liberalism.

The rise of fascism forced him to flee Vienna in 1933 for London, where he lectured for the Workers' Educational Association and deepened his historical research into the origins of industrial capitalism. He emigrated to the United States in 1940, where he completed *The Great Transformation* (1944) while a fellow at Bennington College, Vermont.

From 1947 to 1953 he held a professorship at Columbia University, though Cold War-era visa restrictions on his wife, Ilona Duczynska, a former communist, prevented him from living full-time in the United States. He settled in Pickering, Ontario, Canada, commuting to Columbia for his teaching duties.

## Key Achievements

- **The Great Transformation** (1944): His defining work, arguing that the nineteenth-century self-regulating market was a historical aberration that destroyed social fabric, ultimately producing fascism and world war as reactions.
- **Primitive, Archaic and Modern Economies** (1968, posthumous): A collection of essays developing his concept of "substantivist" economics — the study of how economies are embedded in social relations — in contrast to "formalist" neoclassical theory.
- **Trade and Market in the Early Empires** (1957, co-edited with Conrad Arensberg and Harry Pearson): A landmark interdisciplinary volume applying his ideas to ancient and non-Western economies.
- Coined the influential concept of **embeddedness**, arguing that economic activity is always embedded in social institutions, norms, and relations rather than operating autonomously.

## Controversies & Criticism

Polanyi's historical account of the nineteenth century has drawn sustained scholarly criticism. Historians have questioned his idealized portrayal of pre-market societies, suggesting he romanticized medieval and early modern arrangements. His reading of the Speenhamland Poor Law system — central to his argument — has been challenged by economic historians who dispute his empirical claims about its effects on wages and labor.

Formalist economists reject his substantivist framework as unscientific, arguing that rational choice models apply universally regardless of cultural context. Some critics on the left have faulted him for insufficient attention to class conflict and for a vagueness about what viable alternatives to market capitalism might look like.

## Legacy & Influence

Polanyi's influence spans economic sociology, anthropology, political science, and development studies. The "Polanyian" tradition informs scholars such as Fred Block, Margaret Somers, and Beverly Silver. His work surged in academic and policy prominence after the 2008 global financial crisis, when the contradictions of deregulated markets made his arguments newly resonant.

The Karl Polanyi Institute of Political Economy, founded at Concordia University in Montreal in 1988, continues to promote research in his tradition. He died on April 23, 1964, in Pickering, Ontario, leaving a body of work that continues to shape debates about the relationship between markets, states, and society.
