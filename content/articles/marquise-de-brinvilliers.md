## Summary

French noblewoman who poisoned her father and brothers for inheritance, sparking the Affair of the Poisons.

## Background

- Marie-Madeleine-Marguerite d’Aubray, Marquise de Brinvilliers, was born into a well-connected Parisian family and married into the nobility.
- Her case became notorious in 17th‑century France because it combined aristocratic status, allegations of repeated poisonings, and public confession.

## Crimes (High-Level)

- The best-known accusations involve the poisoning deaths of her father and two brothers, often described as motivated by inheritance and family conflict.
- Contemporary accounts and later summaries emphasize poison (rather than overt violence) as the core mechanism of the crimes.

## Investigation and Trial

- The Brinvilliers affair is frequently cited as a precursor to the wider “Affair of the Poisons,” a period in which authorities pursued suspected poisoners and occult practitioners.
- After flight and pursuit, she was captured, tried in Paris, and condemned.
- Historical summaries commonly note that she provided a confession (with details varying by source and retelling).

## Execution

- She was executed in 1676; the case received significant public attention and was used by authorities as a warning about concealed crimes within elite circles.

## Historical Significance

- The notoriety of the case helped heighten official and popular anxiety about poisoning in late 17th‑century France.
- In many narratives, Brinvilliers becomes a symbolic figure: an aristocrat whose alleged crimes challenged assumptions about class, morality, and domestic safety.
