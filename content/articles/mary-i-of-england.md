## Summary

English queen known as 'Bloody Mary' who burned 280 Protestants at the stake during her five-year reign. Her persecutions were memorialized in Foxe's Book of Martyrs.

## Historical context

Mary Tudor was the daughter of Henry VIII and Catherine of Aragon.
Her early life was shaped by the English Reformation and the dispute over Henry's marriage.
During the reign of her half-brother Edward VI, Protestant reforms accelerated.
Mary's accession in 1553 occurred after a contested succession crisis involving Lady Jane Grey.

## Governance and major decisions

As queen, Mary sought to restore Roman Catholic worship and papal authority in England.
Her government reversed or repealed elements of earlier religious legislation.
A major political issue of her reign was her marriage to Philip of Spain (later Philip II).
The marriage was controversial domestically and connected English policy to wider Habsburg power politics.

## Persecution and legal mechanism

The executions associated with the Marian persecutions were carried out through the revived heresy laws.
Victims included clergy and laypeople identified with Protestant doctrine.
Many of the most famous accounts in English memory come from later martyrologies.
The phrase "Bloody Mary" reflects a polemical tradition and is not a neutral descriptor.

## Legacy and historiography

Mary is often contrasted with her successor Elizabeth I, whose religious settlement proved more durable.
Her reign is frequently judged through the lens of confessional conflict rather than administrative policy.
Modern scholarship tends to emphasize the broader European context of religious violence in the 16th century.
Mary's reputation remains contested, balancing her pursuit of religious uniformity against the human cost of persecution.
