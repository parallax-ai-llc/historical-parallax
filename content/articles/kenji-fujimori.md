## Summary

Peruvian politician and son of Alberto Fujimori who was sentenced for selling his Congressional vote. He broke with his sister Keiko politically and was convicted of influence peddling.

## Political Background

Kenji Fujimori entered Peruvian politics as part of the Fujimori family network that remained influential after the presidency of his father, Alberto Fujimori. He served as a member of Congress and was associated with Fujimorismo, a political current that has been both electorally significant and highly polarizing in Peru.

## Relationship with Fujimori-aligned Parties

Within the broader Fujimori political camp, he became notable for an internal split with his sister, Keiko Fujimori, who led a major Fujimori-aligned party and ran for the presidency multiple times. The family and factional tensions were closely watched because they affected parliamentary voting blocs and coalition dynamics.

## Vote-trading Scandal and Legal Case

Kenji Fujimori was implicated in a scandal involving videos and allegations that lawmakers were offered inducements in exchange for votes (often referenced in Peruvian media coverage as "Kenjivideos"). The controversy contributed to his loss of congressional standing and subsequent prosecution.

A Peruvian court later convicted him in a vote-buying / influence-peddling case and imposed a prison sentence (reported as 4.5 years in many summaries). The case is frequently cited in discussions of corruption enforcement and political bargaining in Peru's fragmented party system.

## Broader Significance

His trajectory illustrates how personal networks and party factionalism can shape legislative politics, and how anti-corruption investigations in Peru have repeatedly touched high-profile figures across the political spectrum.

## Legacy and Public Perception

Because he carried the Fujimori name, his political fortunes were closely tied to public debates about the earlier Fujimori administration and Peru's recurring cycles of scandal and institutional crisis. His conviction is often referenced as an example of how intra-elite conflicts can surface through anti-corruption prosecutions.

## Additional Notes

In Peru's highly volatile post-2000 political landscape, high-profile convictions have often been interpreted through both legal and factional lenses, making his case a frequent reference point in commentary on institutional trust.
