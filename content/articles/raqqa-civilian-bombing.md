## Summary

US-led coalition assault on Raqqa to defeat ISIS killed an estimated 1,600 civilians in airstrikes and artillery fire. The campaign reduced much of the city to rubble.

## Context

Raqqa served as the de facto Syrian stronghold and administrative hub for the Islamic State (ISIS) for a period during the Syrian Civil War. The U.S.-led coalition and local partner forces targeted the city as part of a broader campaign to dismantle ISIS’s territorial control.

## The 2017 battle and sources of civilian harm

The assault involved intensive airstrikes and artillery support alongside ground operations. Urban combat conditions—dense neighborhoods, limited civilian exit routes, and ISIS defensive tactics—contributed to a high risk of civilian casualties and large-scale destruction of housing and infrastructure.

## Casualty estimates and documentation

Different organizations have produced estimates of civilian deaths in and around Raqqa during the 2017 campaign. Investigative groups such as Airwars compiled incident reports from local sources, media, and open-source imagery to estimate casualties and attribute them to coalition actions where evidence supported such conclusions. Coalition statements and post-strike assessments have at times disputed specific incidents or casualty figures, reflecting the inherent uncertainty and contested attribution common in wartime casualty accounting.

## Investigations and controversy

Reporting on Raqqa has often focused on questions of proportionality, rules of engagement, target verification, and the adequacy of post-strike investigations. The episode is frequently cited in policy debates about urban warfare, the use of heavy explosive weapons in cities, and the transparency of civilian harm reporting.

## Aftermath

Beyond immediate loss of life, the campaign left extensive physical damage that complicated the return of displaced residents and slowed reconstruction. Unexploded ordnance and damaged critical services became major post-battle hazards, shaping humanitarian priorities long after ISIS forces were driven out.
