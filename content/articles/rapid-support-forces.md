## Summary

Sudanese paramilitary force evolved from the Janjaweed militia that launched a civil war against the Sudanese army in April 2023. They have committed mass atrocities in Darfur and Khartoum.

## Origins and Structure

- The RSF traces its roots to militias associated with the Darfur conflict, often discussed in connection with the Janjaweed.
- It later became a formal paramilitary organization with its own command structure and economic interests.
- Analysts commonly describe it as distinct from Sudan’s regular armed forces while still operating as a major state-linked security actor.

## Role in Sudan’s Recent Politics

- The RSF has been a central force in the post-2019 political transition period and power struggles between military factions.
- In April 2023, conflict between the RSF and the Sudanese Armed Forces escalated into a large-scale war, including heavy fighting in Khartoum.
- Human-rights reporting has documented allegations of widespread abuses, particularly in Darfur, making the group a focal point in international accountability debates.

## International Context

- The RSF’s rise has been linked to regional security dynamics, cross-border commerce, and competition over resources.
- During the 2023–present war, diplomatic efforts have repeatedly sought ceasefires and humanitarian access while violence continued.
- The group’s actions and command responsibility have been central to international discussions about sanctions, mediation, and war-crimes documentation.

## Notes

- Reporting on the RSF often highlights the difficulty of distinguishing formal command decisions from decentralized violence in fragmented conflict zones.
- Humanitarian access and civilian protection have remained recurring issues in coverage of areas under contested control.
