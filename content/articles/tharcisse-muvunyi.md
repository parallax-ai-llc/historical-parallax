## Summary

Rwandan military officer convicted by the ICTR for direct and public incitement to commit genocide. He was a lieutenant colonel who encouraged soldiers and civilians to kill Tutsis.

## Context: ICTR and the Rwandan Genocide

Muvunyi’s case was prosecuted at the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR), which focused on individuals alleged to bear responsibility for genocide and other serious violations of international humanitarian law committed in 1994.

## Charges and Legal Concept of Incitement

A central element in summaries of the case is “direct and public incitement to commit genocide,” a crime that treats certain kinds of public encouragement of mass violence as independently punishable. ICTR jurisprudence often discussed the required elements for incitement, including public reach and the intent behind the statements.

## Trial, Appeals, and Sentencing

ICTR proceedings in this period frequently involved appeals and, in some instances, retrials or re-sentencing based on how evidence was evaluated at first instance. In Muvunyi’s case, public accounts note a conviction connected to incitement and subsequent legal proceedings that resulted in a prison term.

## Why the Case Is Cited

The Muvunyi case is sometimes referenced in legal writing to illustrate how international courts analyze speech acts, authority, and the relationship between leadership messaging and violence on the ground.
