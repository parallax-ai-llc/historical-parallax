## Summary

South African president who enforced apartheid and authorized cross-border military raids against neighboring states. He refused to cooperate with the Truth and Reconciliation Commission and was convicted of contempt.

## Governance and Apartheid Era Policies

Botha rose through South Africa’s National Party and became a central figure in the late-apartheid state. His administration is associated with a strong security orientation and an effort to maintain white minority rule while responding to growing internal resistance and international pressure.

Key features of the period included:

- **Security-state approach**: Expanded powers for security agencies and supported extensive policing and intelligence operations against anti-apartheid organizations.
- **States of emergency**: South Africa declared states of emergency in the 1980s as unrest and protest intensified.
- **Limited constitutional reform**: The 1983 constitution created a **tricameral parliament** that extended representation to some non-Black groups while **excluding the Black majority**, reinforcing apartheid’s core structure.

## Regional Conflict and Military Actions

Botha’s government pursued an aggressive regional policy during the Cold War, including military operations and support for allied forces in neighboring countries. Cross-border raids and covert actions were justified domestically as counterinsurgency and anti-communist measures, and were condemned internationally.

## End of Tenure and Accountability Debates

Botha resigned in 1989 amid health issues and political shifts within the ruling party. After the democratic transition, he refused to cooperate with the **Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC)**, leading to a contempt conviction.

Botha’s legacy is widely viewed through the lens of apartheid’s repression and the violence of the late-apartheid security state, alongside debates about whether his limited reforms delayed or accelerated systemic change.

## Related Terms

- **Apartheid**: South Africa’s system of institutionalized racial segregation and discrimination (1948–1994).
