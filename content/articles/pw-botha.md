## Summary

Apartheid South African president who refused to negotiate with the ANC and authorized cross-border raids and assassinations. Known as 'Die Groot Krokodil' (The Big Crocodile).

## Background

P. W. Botha rose through South Africa’s National Party during the apartheid era, serving in senior government roles before becoming prime minister (and later state president after constitutional changes). His tenure is often described as combining limited institutional reforms with intensified security-state measures.

## Governance and security policy

Botha’s government expanded the use of emergency powers and security operations against anti-apartheid organizations and activists. Cross-border actions targeting perceived opponents in neighboring states are a recurring theme in accounts of his administration.

## Reform attempts and limits

He is also associated with proposals framed as “reform,” including limited political restructuring, while refusing core demands such as majority-rule elections and full political inclusion. Critics argue these measures aimed to preserve apartheid’s fundamentals under mounting internal and external pressure.

## Transition period

By the late 1980s, South Africa faced escalating unrest, international sanctions, and economic strain. Botha’s departure preceded the subsequent negotiation process that eventually ended apartheid under his successors.

## Legacy

Botha remains a polarizing figure: supporters highlight his emphasis on state authority and stability, while opponents stress the repression and human-rights abuses carried out under his leadership.
