## Summary

Klaus von Klitzing is a German physicist who won the 1985 Nobel Prize in Physics for the discovery of the integer quantum Hall effect.

## Scientific Contribution

Von Klitzing is best known for discovering the integer quantum Hall effect, observed in two-dimensional electron systems under low temperatures and strong magnetic fields.
A key outcome of this discovery is the precise quantization of the Hall resistance, which appears in exact integer steps.
Because the quantization is extremely stable and reproducible, it became foundational for precision electrical metrology.

## Impact on Measurement Standards

The effect led to the definition of the von Klitzing constant, used in the context of resistance standards.
National metrology institutes adopted quantum Hall devices as practical realizations of a resistance standard due to their accuracy.
This work also helped demonstrate how fundamental constants can underpin measurement systems.

## Later Work and Legacy

Beyond the original discovery, von Klitzing continued to work in condensed matter physics and related areas of semiconductor research.
His Nobel-recognized discovery influenced subsequent research on quantum transport, including fractional quantum Hall physics and topological phases.
He is often cited in discussions about the bridge between basic physics and real-world standards used in engineering and industry.

## Why It Matters

The integer quantum Hall effect is a frequently taught example of a macroscopic quantum phenomenon.
It shows that material systems can produce exact numerical values tied to universal physics, not to the details of a particular sample.
