## Summary

Roh Moo-hyun (1946-2009) served as the 16th President of South Korea from 2003 to 2008, representing the progressive Millennium Democratic Party and later the Uri Party. A self-educated human rights lawyer from humble origins, Roh championed democratic reforms, transparency in government, and improved relations with North Korea. His presidency was marked by efforts to reduce regional inequalities, promote participatory democracy, and challenge traditional power structures, but also faced criticism for economic management and partisan conflicts. His tragic death by suicide in 2009, following corruption allegations involving his family, shocked the nation and led to a significant reassessment of his legacy.

---

## Early Life and Background

Roh Moo-hyun was born on September 1, 1946, in Bongha Village, Gimhae, in the South Gyeongsang Province of South Korea, during the final year of Japanese colonial rule.[^1] He came from a poor farming family and experienced significant hardship during his childhood. His family's poverty meant that educational opportunities were limited, and young Roh had to balance his studies with helping his family survive economically.[^2]

Despite these challenges, Roh was an ambitious student. He graduated from Busan Commercial High School in 1966, but his family's financial situation prevented him from attending university.[^3] Instead of pursuing higher education through traditional means, Roh worked various jobs, including as a fisherman and in a factory, while studying law independently. This period of self-education would become a defining characteristic of his political identity, as he often emphasized his status as a self-made man who succeeded without elite educational credentials.[^4]

In 1975, after years of self-study, Roh passed the bar examination, an extraordinary achievement for someone without formal legal education.[^5] He began practicing law in 1977, initially handling commercial cases. However, he soon became involved in human rights and labor rights cases, defending pro-democracy activists and workers during South Korea's authoritarian period under President Park Chung-hee and later Chun Doo-hwan.[^6] This work brought him into conflict with the military dictatorship and established his credentials as a defender of democratic values.

Roh married Kwon Yang-sook in 1972, and the couple had one son and one daughter. His wife came from a similarly modest background, and their partnership was characterized by mutual support through the difficult years of his legal practice and political career.[^7]

## Rise to Power

Roh Moo-hyun's entrance into politics came through his human rights work. In 1987, during South Korea's democratic transition, he gained national attention as a lawyer defending students arrested during pro-democracy demonstrations.[^8] His passionate courtroom arguments and willingness to challenge the authoritarian regime made him a popular figure among democracy activists.

In 1988, Roh was elected to the National Assembly representing Dong District in Busan as a member of the Party for Peace and Democracy.[^9] His parliamentary career began inauspiciously—he was a backbencher from a minority party in a region dominated by conservative politicians. However, Roh gained sudden nationwide fame in 1988 during televised National Assembly hearings investigating political corruption and the Gwangju Democratization Movement massacre.[^10]

During these hearings, Roh conducted a forceful interrogation of Chun Doo-hwan's associates, demonstrating courage and rhetorical skill that captivated the public. His questioning of Park Jun-byeong, a former military official, became particularly famous and established Roh as a fearless investigator willing to confront powerful interests.[^11] This moment transformed him from an obscure provincial politician into a national political figure.

However, Roh's political career faced significant obstacles. He lost his parliamentary seat in 1992, reflecting the continued conservative dominance of his home region.[^12] He returned to legal practice but remained politically active. In 1995, he was elected to the National Assembly again, this time representing Jongno District in Seoul, a symbolically important constituency.[^13]

Roh aligned himself with Kim Dae-jung, a veteran opposition leader who became president in 1998. Under Kim's administration, Roh served as Minister of Maritime Affairs and Fisheries from 2000 to 2001.[^14] This position, while not central to government policy, gave Roh executive experience and kept him in the public eye.

In 2002, Roh emerged as an unlikely presidential candidate. Initially far behind in polls, his campaign gained momentum through innovative use of the internet and strong support from younger voters frustrated with traditional politics.[^15] His plain-spoken style, emphasis on transparency, and promise to challenge the established order resonated with citizens seeking change. In a dramatic upset, Roh won the presidential election in December 2002 with 48.9% of the vote, defeating the conservative candidate Lee Hoi-chang.[^16]

## Major Achievements

As president from 2003 to 2008, Roh Moo-hyun pursued an ambitious agenda focused on democratic reform, decentralization, and engagement with North Korea. One of his signature initiatives was the promotion of participatory democracy. Roh frequently communicated directly with citizens through online platforms, held regular town hall meetings, and encouraged public input on policy decisions.[^17] He believed that democracy should involve active citizen engagement rather than passive delegation to elected officials.

Roh's administration implemented significant administrative reforms aimed at reducing corruption and increasing transparency. He established stronger ethics requirements for government officials, promoted merit-based appointments, and attempted to reduce the influence of money in politics.[^18] These efforts, while not entirely successful, represented a genuine attempt to change South Korea's political culture.

In foreign policy, Roh pursued a "policy of peace and prosperity" toward North Korea, continuing and expanding the "Sunshine Policy" initiated by his predecessor Kim Dae-jung.[^19] In 2007, Roh became only the second South Korean president to meet with a North Korean leader, traveling to Pyongyang for a summit with Kim Jong-il. The resulting October 4 Declaration outlined agreements on economic cooperation, family reunifications, and reducing military tensions.[^20]

Roh's administration also pursued balanced diplomacy between the United States and China, seeking to position South Korea as an independent regional actor rather than simply following American leadership.[^21] While this approach created tensions with the Bush administration, particularly regarding North Korea policy, it reflected Roh's vision of South Korea as a mature democracy capable of pursuing its own interests.

Domestically, Roh championed efforts to reduce regional inequalities and the dominance of the Seoul metropolitan area. He relocated several government ministries and agencies to the planned administrative city of Sejong, despite fierce opposition from conservative politicians and bureaucrats.[^22] He also promoted development projects in traditionally underdeveloped regions, particularly in the Honam region (southwestern Korea).

In the area of transitional justice, Roh's government established the Truth and Reconciliation Commission to investigate human rights abuses during South Korea's authoritarian period, including the Gwangju Democratization Movement and other incidents of state violence.[^23] This effort to confront historical injustices was controversial but represented an important step in South Korea's democratic consolidation.

Roh also promoted labor rights and attempted to strengthen social welfare programs. His administration increased the minimum wage, expanded health insurance coverage, and introduced various programs to support low-income families.[^24] However, these initiatives were limited by budgetary constraints and opposition from conservative political forces and business interests.

In terms of gender equality, Roh's administration increased female representation in government and promoted policies to address gender discrimination in the workplace.[^25] While progress was incremental, his government showed greater commitment to gender issues than previous administrations.

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his reformist agenda, Roh Moo-hyun's presidency faced significant controversies and criticisms. His economic management was frequently attacked by conservatives and business leaders who argued that his policies were anti-business and contributed to slower economic growth compared to previous administrations.[^26] Critics pointed to increasing household debt, rising unemployment among certain sectors, and concerns about South Korea's economic competitiveness.

Roh's relationship with the United States deteriorated during his presidency, reaching its lowest point in decades. His progressive stance on North Korea, reluctance to fully support American foreign policy initiatives, and negotiation of the Korea-U.S. Free Trade Agreement (which faced opposition from his own political base) created tensions with both Seoul and Washington.[^27] Conservative critics accused him of undermining the South Korea-U.S. alliance, while progressives felt he ultimately conceded too much in trade negotiations.

The decision to deploy South Korean troops to Iraq in 2004, despite significant public opposition and his own progressive credentials, damaged Roh's credibility with anti-war activists and raised questions about his independence from American pressure.[^28] Approximately 3,600 South Korean troops were sent to Iraq, making it the third-largest foreign contingent after the U.S. and U.K.

Roh's political style often exacerbated conflicts rather than building consensus. His blunt, confrontational rhetoric alienated potential allies and contributed to intense political polarization.[^29] He frequently criticized the conservative media, the judiciary, and establishment politicians in language that his opponents considered undignified for a president. This combative approach energized his base but made governance more difficult.

The relocation of government functions from Seoul to Sejong City, while conceptually aimed at reducing regional inequality, was criticized as poorly planned and economically wasteful.[^30] Many government officials resisted the move, and the project's costs were substantial. Critics argued that the policy was driven more by political considerations than sound planning.

Roh's attempts to reform the constitution and political system, including proposals for a parliamentary system and changing the presidential term limits, were criticized as attempts to extend his political influence.[^31] These initiatives failed to gain sufficient support and contributed to perceptions that Roh was more interested in political maneuvering than governance.

His appointment policies sometimes prioritized political loyalty over competence, leading to criticism that he surrounded himself with inexperienced advisers from his regional base rather than drawing on broader expertise.[^32] Several of his appointees became embroiled in minor scandals or demonstrated administrative incompetence.

Most devastatingly, in 2009, after leaving office, Roh became the target of a corruption investigation involving allegations that his family and associates had received millions of dollars in illegal payments from a businessman named Park Yeon-cha.[^33] Although Roh maintained that he was unaware of most of these transactions, which primarily involved his wife and other family members, the investigation caused him profound distress. On May 23, 2009, Roh died by suicide, jumping from a cliff near his home village.[^34] He left a brief note expressing remorse and stating that he had become a burden to too many people.

## Personal Life

Roh Moo-hyun's personal life reflected his humble origins and remained relatively modest even after he became president. His marriage to Kwon Yang-sook, which lasted until his death, was characterized by mutual support through difficult periods, including poverty, political setbacks, and ultimately the corruption scandal that preceded his suicide.[^35] Kwon was known for her reserved nature and generally avoided the public spotlight, unlike some other presidential spouses.

The couple had two children: a son, Roh Geon-ho, and a daughter, Roh Jeong-yeon. Both children largely stayed out of politics and public life, though they became unwilling subjects of public attention during the corruption investigation that targeted their father.[^36]

Roh's personality was marked by directness and emotional expressiveness that was unusual for a Korean politician. He was known for speaking his mind without the careful diplomatic language typical of political leaders, which endeared him to supporters who saw him as authentic but troubled critics who considered him unpresidential.[^37] He could be self-deprecating about his lack of elite credentials, often joking about his commercial high school education in contrast to the university degrees of other politicians.

After leaving the presidency in 2008, Roh returned to his home village of Bongha, where he lived a simple life and maintained an active blog communicating with supporters.[^38] He engaged in farming and appeared to enjoy the return to rural life, though he remained politically engaged and concerned about his legacy.

Roh was an avid reader despite his lack of formal higher education, and he often quoted philosophers and political theorists in his speeches.[^39] His self-education extended beyond law to include history, political theory, and economics. Friends and colleagues described him as deeply thoughtful but also sensitive to criticism and prone to taking political attacks personally.

The corruption investigation that led to his suicide deeply affected Roh. Those close to him reported that he was devastated by the allegations and felt profound guilt that his family members and associates had become involved in activities that violated his principles.[^40] His suicide note was brief and expressed remorse, asking to be cremated and stating, "I am in debt to too many people. Too many people have suffered because of me. And I cannot imagine the suffering they will go through in the future."[^41]

## Legacy

Roh Moo-hyun's legacy remains deeply contested in South Korean politics, with dramatically different assessments from progressives and conservatives. His tragic death in 2009 led to an immediate outpouring of grief and a significant reassessment of his presidency, transforming him into a martyr figure for the progressive movement.[^42]

For progressives and many younger South Koreans, Roh represents an authentic leader who genuinely tried to reform Korean politics and society. His emphasis on transparency, direct communication with citizens, and challenges to traditional power structures are seen as ahead of their time.[^43] The massive crowds that gathered to mourn his death—with over five million people visiting memorial sites and his funeral procession drawing hundreds of thousands—demonstrated his continued significance to many South Koreans.[^44]

The Roh Moo-hyun Foundation, established after his death, has worked to preserve and promote his political philosophy, emphasizing democracy, human rights, and peace.[^45] His home village of Bongha has become a pilgrimage site for supporters, with a memorial park and museum dedicated to his life and presidency.

Roh's influence on South Korean politics extended beyond his death. Moon Jae-in, Roh's chief of staff and close associate, was elected president in 2017, explicitly running on a platform of continuing Roh's legacy.[^46] Moon's presidency saw renewed emphasis on engagement with North Korea, democratic reform, and addressing economic inequality—all themes central to Roh's political vision.

In terms of policy legacy, Roh's initiatives on decentralization and balanced regional development, particularly the Sejong City project, ultimately proceeded despite initial resistance.[^47] His efforts to expand social welfare and labor protections, while limited during his presidency, helped establish frameworks that subsequent progressive governments built upon.

Roh's approach to North Korea, while controversial, contributed to periods of reduced tension and increased inter-Korean cooperation. The industrial complex at Kaesong and various cultural exchanges initiated during his and Kim Dae-jung's administrations created people-to-people connections that persisted despite subsequent political changes.[^48]

However, conservatives continue to criticize Roh's presidency as economically misguided, diplomatically naive, and excessively partisan. They argue that his North Korea policy failed to prevent nuclear development, his economic policies weakened growth, and his confrontational political style deepened national divisions.[^49]

The corruption scandal that led to his death complicated his legacy. While many supporters believe the investigation was politically motivated and that the actual amounts involved were modest by Korean political standards, the episode contradicted Roh's emphasis on clean government.[^50] His family's acceptance of payments, even if Roh himself was largely unaware, represented a failure to maintain the ethical standards he proclaimed.

Roh's legacy is also evident in the political polarization that characterizes contemporary South Korea. His presidency accelerated the division between progressive and conservative camps, with each side viewing political conflict as a fundamental struggle over national values rather than normal democratic competition.[^51] Whether this polarization was an inevitable result of democratization or could have been mitigated by different leadership remains debated.

In historical terms, Roh is increasingly seen as a transitional figure who attempted to consolidate South Korea's democracy and redefine its international role, with mixed success.[^52] His commitment to democratic values, human rights, and social justice is widely acknowledged, even by some critics, while his political and policy effectiveness remains contested.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholarly and political assessments from progressive perspectives emphasize Roh Moo-hyun's contributions to democratic consolidation and his genuine commitment to reform. Political scientists have noted that Roh's emphasis on participatory democracy and direct communication with citizens represented an important evolution in South Korean political culture, challenging the top-down traditions inherited from authoritarian rule.[^53]

Roh is credited with advancing transitional justice in South Korea through the establishment of truth commissions investigating past human rights abuses. Scholars of democratization point to these efforts as crucial for addressing historical wounds and establishing accountability for authoritarian-era crimes.[^54] His willingness to confront powerful conservative interests, including the military and intelligence services, demonstrated political courage that strengthened democratic institutions.

Supporters argue that Roh's economic policies have been unfairly criticized, noting that South Korea maintained solid growth during his presidency despite global economic challenges.[^55] Progressive economists contend that his emphasis on reducing inequality and strengthening social welfare represented necessary corrections to the growth-first policies of previous administrations, even if implementation was incomplete.

In foreign policy, defenders of Roh's approach argue that his efforts to maintain balanced relations with the United States while pursuing engagement with North Korea reflected mature diplomacy appropriate for a middle power.[^56] The 2007 inter-Korean summit and resulting agreements, while ultimately not fully implemented, demonstrated that dialogue was possible and created frameworks for future engagement.

Human rights advocates particularly praise Roh's record on political freedom, noting that his administration did not restrict press freedom despite his frequent criticism of conservative media.[^57] His government maintained democratic norms even when facing intense political opposition, in contrast to the authoritarian reflexes that characterized earlier periods of South Korean history.

Moon Jae-in and other close associates have written extensively about Roh's personal integrity, intellectual honesty, and deep commitment to his principles.[^58] They argue that his tragic death resulted from an impossible standard of perfection applied to him but not to other politicians, and that the corruption investigation was politically motivated.

Academic analyses of Roh's presidency increasingly acknowledge his role in transforming South Korean politics, even when disagreeing with specific policies. His emergence from humble origins to the presidency inspired many South Koreans who felt excluded from traditional elite politics.[^59]

### Negative Views

Conservative critics and some centrist scholars offer substantially different assessments of Roh's presidency, emphasizing policy failures, divisive rhetoric, and problematic governance. Economic analysts critical of Roh's presidency point to slower GDP growth, increasing household debt, and deteriorating business confidence as evidence of misguided economic policies.[^60]

Critics argue that Roh's approach to North Korea was naive and failed to prevent nuclear weapons development despite significant South Korean concessions. Conservative foreign policy experts contend that his administration's eagerness for engagement allowed North Korea to play South Korea against the United States while continuing its weapons programs.[^61] The fact that North Korea conducted its first nuclear test in 2006, during Roh's presidency, is cited as evidence of this policy's failure.

Roh's political style is criticized as unnecessarily confrontational and divisive. Rather than building consensus or seeking compromise, critics argue, Roh regularly attacked opponents in harsh terms, portrayed disagreements as moral struggles, and contributed to a political culture of extreme polarization.[^62] His frequent complaints about media bias and conservative opposition are characterized as attempts to deflect from policy failures rather than legitimate criticisms.

Some analysts argue that Roh's emphasis on participatory democracy and direct communication, while appealing, actually weakened governmental effectiveness. By constantly appealing to public opinion and emphasizing his connection with ordinary citizens, Roh allegedly undermined the deliberative processes necessary for sound policymaking.[^63]

The corruption scandal involving his family is seen by critics as revealing hypocrisy at the heart of Roh's political project. Having built his career on promises of clean government and transparency, the discovery that his family had accepted substantial payments fundamentally contradicted his claims to represent a new kind of politics.[^64] Even sympathetic observers acknowledge this as a serious failure.

Conservative historians place Roh's presidency in the context of what they see as a broader progressive movement that romanticized North Korea, weakened national security, and pursued ideological agendas at the expense of practical governance.[^65] They argue that his administration's policies strengthened leftist political forces that threatened South Korean prosperity and security.

Centrist critics suggest that while Roh's intentions were often admirable, his political skills and policy competence were insufficient for the presidency. His background as a human rights lawyer, while ethically impressive, did not prepare him for the complex challenges of economic management and international diplomacy.[^66] His appointments of political loyalists rather than experienced administrators compounded these limitations.

Some scholars argue that Roh's suicide, while tragic, should not obscure legitimate questions about his administration's failings. They contend that the outpouring of grief after his death and subsequent mythologization by progressives has prevented honest assessment of his presidency.[^67]

> "I am not a person of great ability, but I am a person who has tried to be honest." - From a speech during his presidency, reflecting his emphasis on integrity over elite credentials[^68]

> "Let's live as equals, helping each other." - A frequent saying that encapsulated his political philosophy of equality and mutual support[^69]

> "Power must not be privatized." - From his anti-corruption speeches, emphasizing that governmental authority must serve public rather than private interests[^70]
