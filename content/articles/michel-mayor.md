## Summary

Michel Mayor shared the 2019 Nobel Prize for discovering the first exoplanet orbiting a sun-like star.

## Scientific Contribution

Mayor is best known for the discovery, with Didier Queloz, of the exoplanet 51 Pegasi b in 1995. The detection used the radial-velocity method, measuring small periodic shifts in a star's spectral lines caused by the gravitational pull of an orbiting planet.

## Why 51 Pegasi b Was a Breakthrough

51 Pegasi b was the first confirmed planet found orbiting a Sun-like star. Its existence demonstrated that planetary systems could be very different from the Solar System and helped launch exoplanet science as a major research field.

## Methods and Legacy

The radial-velocity approach became one of the foundational techniques for exoplanet detection and characterization, particularly for measuring planetary mass (or minimum mass) and orbital parameters. Mayor's work contributed to the development of precision spectroscopy and to the broader shift toward systematic searches for planets around other stars.

## Nobel Prize

In 2019, Mayor and Queloz were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics (shared with James Peebles, who was recognized for work in physical cosmology). The award highlighted the scientific and cultural significance of discovering planets beyond the Solar System.

## Broader Impact

The discovery of 51 Pegasi b helped motivate new observational programs and space missions aimed at finding and studying exoplanets. It also reshaped theories of planet formation and migration, since the planet is a "hot Jupiter" in a close-in orbit.

## Related Concepts

Common exoplanet detection methods include radial velocity, transit photometry, direct imaging, and gravitational microlensing. Mayor's work is most closely associated with pushing radial-velocity precision to the level needed for reliable planet detection.
