## Summary

Rwandan military officer considered the mastermind of the 1994 genocide that killed 800,000 Tutsis and moderate Hutus. He organized weapon distribution to Interahamwe militia.

## Role in 1994 violence (high level)

Bagosora was a senior figure in Rwanda’s defense establishment at the time of the 1994 genocide against the Tutsi. He has been described by many commentators as a key organizer within the structures that enabled mass killing, including the coordination of security forces and the mobilization of allied militias.

Accounts of his influence often focus on the period immediately after the assassination of President Juvénal Habyarimana, when authority in parts of the state apparatus shifted rapidly and extremist networks moved to implement plans for widespread attacks.

## Arrest and international prosecution

After leaving Rwanda, Bagosora was arrested in Cameroon and transferred to the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR), established by the United Nations to prosecute those responsible for genocide and other serious violations of international humanitarian law.

## ICTR judgment and sentencing

The ICTR convicted Bagosora in 2008 on multiple counts related to genocide and crimes against humanity. Appeals proceedings later modified parts of the verdict and sentence, but the case remained one of the tribunal’s most prominent prosecutions of senior officials.

## Historical significance

Bagosora’s case is frequently cited in discussions of command responsibility, the organization of mass violence through state and militia structures, and the role of international tribunals in documenting events and establishing judicial records.

## Notes on terminology

Many summaries describe “moderate Hutu” and “Tutsi” victims together to emphasize that political opponents were also targeted. In most scholarly and legal accounts, the core characterization is the **genocide against the Tutsi**, with additional categories of killings and persecution addressed under crimes against humanity.
