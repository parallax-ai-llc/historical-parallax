## Summary

Sir Francis Drake was the first Englishman to circumnavigate the globe (1577-1580). He played a key role in defeating the Spanish Armada in 1588.

## Early Life and Entry into Seafaring

Drake was born in Devon in the mid-16th century and came of age during a period of intense maritime rivalry between England and Spain. He entered seafaring through coastal and Atlantic voyages and built a reputation in ventures that blended trade, raiding, and naval warfare.

## Privateering and Anglo-Spanish Rivalry

Drake’s career is frequently described through the lens of **privateering**—state-sanctioned raiding against enemy shipping. In English accounts, these actions were framed as strategic blows against Spain; in Spanish accounts, Drake was viewed as a pirate. His activities also intersected with early English involvement in the Atlantic slave trade, a dimension that is central to modern reassessments of his legacy.

## Circumnavigation (1577–1580)

Drake’s circumnavigation is remembered as a major English maritime achievement.

- **Flagship**: the _Golden Hind_.
- **Goals**: combine exploration with attacks on Spanish interests and acquisition of treasure.
- **Outcome**: return to England with substantial captured wealth; Drake was knighted by Elizabeth I.

The voyage expanded English geographic knowledge and demonstrated the feasibility of long-range oceanic operations for an English expedition.

## Role in the Spanish Armada (1588)

During the Armada campaign, Drake served as a senior naval commander. English strategy emphasized maneuverability, harassment of supply and formation cohesion, and forcing the Spanish fleet into disadvantageous positions. Drake’s leadership contributed to England’s ability to contest Spanish sea power in a highly symbolic conflict.

## Later Expeditions and Death

Drake continued to lead expeditions in the Atlantic and Caribbean, but later campaigns did not match the success of earlier ventures. He died in 1596 during an operation off Panama, reportedly of disease.

## Legacy and Reassessment

Drake’s reputation varies strongly by perspective:

- **In England**: often celebrated as an emblem of Elizabethan maritime expansion.
- **In Spain and parts of the Americas**: remembered for raids, violence, and disruption.
- **Modern evaluation**: includes scrutiny of privateering’s human cost and early English participation in slave-trading ventures.

He remains a prominent figure for studying the links between state power, commerce, naval warfare, and empire-building in the early modern Atlantic.
