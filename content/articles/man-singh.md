## Summary

Legendary Indian dacoit known as 'Robin Hood of the Chambal' who evaded capture for 20+ years. Surrendered after Gandhi follower negotiations.

## Background

- The Chambal ravines spanning parts of Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, and Uttar Pradesh became associated with organized dacoity in the early-to-mid 20th century.
- “Dacoit” (daku) bands often formed around kinship ties and local patronage networks, sometimes engaging in both violent crime and informal redistribution.
- Man Singh is frequently portrayed in popular accounts as a folk figure, reflecting the gap between local myth-making and the record of criminal violence.

## Notability and Narrative

- The “Robin Hood” label is commonly used in Indian journalism and folklore to describe some dacoits who allegedly aided poor villagers, though such claims vary by source.
- Long periods “on the run” were facilitated by difficult terrain, sympathetic intermediaries, and limited state presence in remote areas.
- Stories about Man Singh’s elusiveness are part of a broader cultural memory of the Chambal era of banditry.

## Surrender, Custody, and Aftermath

- Some Chambal dacoits surrendered through negotiated processes that involved political and social mediators, including religious or Gandhian figures.
- Such surrenders were often framed publicly as moral reform and reintegration, while also serving practical state goals of reducing violence.
- Man Singh’s later years are typically described in terms of surrender, imprisonment, and declining public profile.

## Additional Context

- Chambal dacoity later became a recurring theme in Indian cinema and journalism, shaping how figures like Man Singh were remembered.
- Accounts often highlight the role of local intermediaries (village leaders, landholders, and social reformers) in negotiating surrenders.
- In historical writing, separating folklore from documented events is difficult because many narratives spread through oral retellings.
