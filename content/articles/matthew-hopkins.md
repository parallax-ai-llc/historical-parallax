## Summary

Self-appointed Witchfinder General who was responsible for the execution of over 300 accused witches in England.

## Historical Context

Hopkins operated during the English Civil War, when disruption of local governance, population displacement, and religious/political polarization made communities more prone to suspicion and moral panic.

## Methods and Practices

Accounts describe Hopkins and his associates using techniques that were presented as "tests" of witchcraft, including:

- **Watching (sleep deprivation):** keeping suspects under constant observation for extended periods.
- **Searching for "witch marks":** examining the body for alleged marks or insensitive spots.
- **Dunking / swimming tests:** associated with early modern witch trials (though not always used directly by Hopkins).

These practices are widely regarded by modern historians as coercive and unreliable.

## Campaign and Geography

Hopkins' activity is most associated with East Anglia. Towns and counties frequently linked to the 1645–1646 surge include Essex, Suffolk, Norfolk, and Cambridgeshire.

### Common Features of the Cases

- Reliance on local testimony, rumor, and communal disputes.
- Use of paid services (Hopkins was known to charge fees), which raised concerns even among contemporaries.
- A cluster of accusations during a short, intense period rather than a sustained long-term campaign.

## Controversy and Backlash

Hopkins attracted criticism in his own lifetime. A notable contemporary critique was published by clergyman John Gaule, who challenged the legitimacy and morality of aggressive witch-hunting.

Hopkins later published **_The Discovery of Witches_ (1647)**, a pamphlet that defended his approach and helped shape later perceptions of his role.

## Legacy

In later centuries Hopkins became a symbol of judicial abuse and moral panic around witchcraft. His story is frequently cited in discussions of:

- Due process and evidentiary standards
- The social dynamics of mass accusations
- The relationship between war, crisis, and scapegoating
