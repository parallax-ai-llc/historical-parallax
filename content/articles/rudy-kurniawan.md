## Summary

Indonesian wine counterfeiter who sold over $35 million in fake vintage wines. Created convincing forgeries of rare Burgundy and Bordeaux wines that fooled experts and collectors.

## Additional Context

### How the fraud worked (high-level)

- The scheme is commonly described as combining **bottle sourcing**, **label fabrication**, and **blending/recorking** to imitate rare wines that trade on provenance.
- The case is frequently cited as an example of how status markets can be vulnerable when authentication relies heavily on reputation and informal expert consensus.

### Legal and market impact

- U.S. federal prosecution helped push the fine-wine industry toward more rigorous provenance documentation and skepticism about extraordinary "cellar finds".
- The story is often referenced in discussions of auction-house responsibility, due diligence, and the economics of counterfeiting luxury goods.

### Aftermath

- Public reporting describes a prison sentence followed by release and subsequent immigration/deportation proceedings, keeping the case visible well beyond the trial.
