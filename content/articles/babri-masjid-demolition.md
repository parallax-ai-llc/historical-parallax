## Summary

Demolition of the 16th-century Babri Masjid mosque in Ayodhya by Hindu nationalists on December 6, 1992. The demolition triggered communal riots across India killing over 2,000 people.

## Background

The demolition occurred against the backdrop of a long-running dispute in Ayodhya over claims about the site’s religious significance. The event became a defining flashpoint in modern Indian politics, with sustained debate over identity, secularism, and the role of mass mobilization.

## Immediate Consequences

- **Communal violence:** The destruction of the mosque was followed by major outbreaks of communal riots in multiple parts of India.
- **Political escalation:** The incident intensified polarization and became a recurring reference point in electoral and ideological conflicts.

## Legal and Institutional Responses

In the years after 1992, the dispute produced extensive litigation and public inquiry. The Supreme Court’s later judgment on the land title is frequently cited as a major milestone in the legal resolution of the site, while criminal cases related to the demolition continued to be a significant part of the public record.

## Long-Term Significance

The Babri Masjid demolition is often discussed as:

- a case study in the relationship between religion and state institutions
- an example of how symbolic sites can drive mass politics
- a catalyst for changes in the security and governance approach to communal conflict

## Notes on Terminology

Many write-ups distinguish between the demolition event (1992), the subsequent cycles of violence, and the later legal judgments about the site. Keeping those layers separate helps avoid conflating historical description with later legal outcomes.
