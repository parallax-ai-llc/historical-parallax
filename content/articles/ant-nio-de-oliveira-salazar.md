## Summary

Portuguese dictator who established the Estado Novo authoritarian regime and ruled for 36 years. His regime used secret police (PIDE), censorship, and colonial wars in Africa. He became incapacitated in 1968 and was never told he lost power.

## Political System and Institutions

Salazar's Estado Novo ("New State") was a corporatist, authoritarian system that rejected competitive party politics and emphasized social order, Catholic moral influence, and state control over organized labor. The regime maintained censorship and political policing through institutions such as the PVDE/PIDE (secret police), while presenting itself as a stabilizing alternative to the instability of the First Portuguese Republic.

## Foreign Policy and Colonial Wars

Portugal remained officially neutral during World War II while navigating relations with both Allied and Axis powers, including strategic negotiations over Atlantic bases. In the postwar period, the regime's most consequential commitment was to maintaining overseas colonies. Anti-colonial insurgencies in Angola, Guinea-Bissau, and Mozambique escalated into prolonged wars beginning in the early 1960s, shaping Portuguese politics and international isolation.

## End of Rule and Legacy

After his 1968 incapacity, Marcelo Caetano replaced him, but the Estado Novo continued until the 1974 Carnation Revolution. Salazar remains a polarizing figure: credited by some with financial stabilization and criticized for repression, censorship, and the human and economic costs of colonial conflict.
