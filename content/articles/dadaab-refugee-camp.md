## Summary

World's largest refugee camp complex in Kenya holding 400,000 Somali refugees. Al-Shabaab has used it for recruitment, while residents face extreme deprivation and violence.

## Background

The Dadaab refugee complex is located in Garissa County in northeastern Kenya, near the Somali border. It was established in the early 1990s as people fled Somalia's civil war, and over time expanded into multiple camps administered in coordination with the Kenyan government and UNHCR.

## Living conditions and services

Large, long-running refugee settlements typically face chronic constraints:

- Overcrowding and periodic shortages of water, food, and healthcare
- Limited formal employment opportunities and restrictions on movement
- Security risks affecting both residents and host communities

Despite these constraints, Dadaab has also functioned as a long-term community where education, health clinics, and local markets developed over decades.

## Security and politics

- Kenyan authorities have periodically cited security concerns, including attacks by the Islamist militant group al‑Shabaab, in debates over the camp's future.
- Plans to close or reduce the camp have been announced at different times, often prompting legal and humanitarian objections as well as negotiations with international partners.

## Humanitarian significance

Dadaab is frequently referenced in discussions about protracted displacement: situations where people remain refugees for many years and emergency camps evolve into semi-permanent cities without a durable political solution.
