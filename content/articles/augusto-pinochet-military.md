## Summary

Chilean general who overthrew Salvador Allende in a 1973 coup and ruled for 17 years while over 3,000 people were killed or disappeared. He pioneered Operation Condor's cross-border state terrorism.

## Expanded overview

Augusto Pinochet (1915–2006) was a Chilean military officer who led the country’s military government after the 1973 coup d’état that overthrew President Salvador Allende. He ruled as head of a junta and later as the country’s de facto leader for many years.

## Military rule

- The Pinochet era involved major political repression, including widespread human rights violations documented by Chilean truth commissions and international organizations.
- His government also pursued significant economic reforms, often associated with free-market policies and technocratic advisers.

## Transition and later years

Chile transitioned back toward civilian rule after a 1988 plebiscite and subsequent elections. Pinochet later remained influential as commander-in-chief and then a senator-for-life under arrangements of the transition period.

## International legal actions

Pinochet became a landmark figure in debates about universal jurisdiction after legal proceedings abroad, including his detention in the United Kingdom in 1998 following a Spanish request. The episode influenced international discourse on accountability for former heads of state.

## Legacy

In Chile and internationally, his legacy remains deeply contested, often framed between narratives of economic change and severe repression, with extensive documentation and ongoing debate about responsibility and remembrance.
