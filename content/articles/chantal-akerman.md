## Summary

Belgian filmmaker whose Jeanne Dielman is considered the greatest film ever made by a woman. Her experimental, durational approach to cinema influenced generations of filmmakers.

## Additional Context

### Style and themes

- Akerman is frequently associated with feminist film theory and avant-garde cinema, but her work also ranges across documentary, fiction, installation, and essay film.
- She is known for long takes, fixed camera positions, and attention to everyday gestures—using duration and repetition to make domestic labor, routine, and space feel newly perceptible.
- Recurring subjects include women’s lives, family and memory, migration and borders, Jewish identity, and the relationship between private interior spaces and public life.

### Key works (selected)

- *Jeanne Dielman, 23 quai du Commerce, 1080 Bruxelles* (1975): a landmark of slow cinema centered on domestic routine and its unraveling.
- *News from Home* (1977): an essay film pairing images of New York City with letters from her mother, often discussed for its use of voiceover and urban observation.
- *From the Other Side* (De l'autre côté, 2002): documentary work engaging with the U.S.–Mexico border and the human costs of migration policy.
- *No Home Movie* (2015): a late, intimate film focused on her mother, grief, and the fragility of home.

### Legacy

- Akerman’s influence is evident in later “slow cinema” and in filmmakers who use minimalism, observational framing, and attention to labor and time.
- Critical polls and retrospectives have increasingly placed *Jeanne Dielman* among the major works in film history, contributing to broader re-evaluations of canon formation.
