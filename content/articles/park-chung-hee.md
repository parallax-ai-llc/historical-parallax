## Summary

South Korean military dictator who ruled for 18 years through authoritarianism and political repression while driving rapid economic development. He was assassinated by his own intelligence chief.

## Rise to Power

Park Chung-hee emerged as a central figure in South Korean politics after the **May 16 coup** in 1961. His rule evolved from military-led government into a long-running presidency that concentrated power in the executive branch.

## Economic Policy and Industrialization

His administrations are strongly associated with state-led development strategies, including multi-year economic plans, export-oriented manufacturing, and support for large industrial groups. South Korea's rapid growth during this period became a reference point in later debates about "developmental state" models.

## Authoritarian Governance

The **Yushin (Revitalizing Reforms) Constitution** of 1972 expanded presidential authority and reduced checks and balances. Political opposition, labor activism, and press freedom were constrained, and security institutions played an outsized role in public life.

## Assassination and Legacy

Park was assassinated in 1979 by KCIA director **Kim Jae-gyu**, triggering another period of political upheaval. Public assessments of his legacy remain divided, often weighing economic transformation against the costs of repression and curtailed democratic rights.

## Historical Interpretation

In scholarship and public debate, Park's era is often analyzed through two overlapping lenses: (1) institutional and policy choices that accelerated industrial growth and exports, and (2) the consolidation of an authoritarian political order. The balance between these frames continues to shape how the period is taught, memorialized, and contested.
