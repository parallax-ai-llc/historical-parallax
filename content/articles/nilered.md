## Summary

NileRed, the online alias of Nigel Braun, is a Canadian chemistry YouTuber and science communicator who has built one of the most respected chemistry channels on the platform. Born on April 20, 1996, he is known for conducting ambitious, multi-step chemical synthesis projects that blend rigorous scientific methodology with compelling storytelling and cinematography.

His channel distinguishes itself from mainstream science entertainment by tackling genuinely complex organic chemistry — synthesizing compounds from unusual starting materials, often spending weeks or months on a single video. Projects such as turning plastic gloves into hot sauce capsaicin, extracting pure nicotine, and making transparent wood have earned him a reputation for both ambition and accuracy.

With over 5 million subscribers across his main channel (NileRed) and a more accessible companion channel (NileBlue), Braun has demonstrated that demanding, technically dense science content can attract a mass audience. His work has helped normalize advanced chemistry concepts for a generation of students and enthusiasts worldwide.

## Early Life & Background

Nigel Braun grew up in Canada with an early interest in science and experimentation. He pursued post-secondary education in chemistry, which formed the technical backbone of his later content. Unlike many science communicators who simplify for accessibility, Braun's academic background allowed him to engage with advanced organic chemistry techniques — including multi-step synthesis, distillation, and chromatography — and present them authentically on camera.

His early home-lab experiments, conducted with improvised equipment, became a hallmark aesthetic of his channel, demonstrating that serious chemistry need not require a fully equipped research institution.

## Rise to Prominence

Braun launched the NileRed YouTube channel around 2015, initially posting relatively straightforward chemistry demonstrations. His growth accelerated as he leaned into longer, more ambitious projects. A pivotal shift came with videos tackling end-to-end synthesis challenges — starting with common or unusual materials and working through complex chemical pathways to reach a final product.

The 2021 video *Turning old plastic gloves into hot sauce* attracted tens of millions of views and brought widespread mainstream attention, showcasing how compelling storytelling could make advanced organic chemistry broadly appealing. He simultaneously launched NileBlue as a secondary channel for shorter experiments and behind-the-scenes content, broadening his reach.

## Key Achievements

- Grew NileRed to over 5 million YouTube subscribers, with individual videos regularly exceeding 10–20 million views.
- Produced widely circulated synthesis projects including transparent wood, homemade sugars from unusual sources, and extraction of rare compounds from everyday materials.
- Launched NileBlue, accumulating over 2 million additional subscribers for supplementary chemistry content.
- Recognized as one of the foremost examples of long-form science communication on YouTube, cited in discussions about STEM education and digital media.
- Maintained scientific rigor and accuracy in an online landscape often criticized for sensationalism, earning respect from working chemists and educators.

## Controversies & Criticism

NileRed has largely avoided significant controversy. Occasional criticism has centered on the handling of hazardous chemicals in home or semi-professional lab settings, with some chemists raising safety concerns about procedures conducted outside regulated environments. Braun has generally addressed these concerns transparently, explaining precautions taken and acknowledging risks in video descriptions and commentary.

## Legacy & Influence

NileRed is widely credited with demonstrating that nuanced, technically demanding science content can achieve mainstream reach on digital platforms. His approach has influenced a wave of chemistry and science creators who prioritize depth over simplification. Educators have used his videos as supplementary materials, and his channel is frequently cited in conversations about the future of science communication. By treating his audience as intellectually capable, Braun helped shift expectations for what educational YouTube could achieve.
