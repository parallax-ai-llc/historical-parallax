## Summary

Collapse of Italys largest private bank in 1982 with $1.3 billion missing, linked to the Vatican Bank, Mafia, and secret Masonic lodge P2. Chairman Roberto Calvi was found dead in London.

## Background

- Banco Ambrosiano was a major Italian private bank that expanded aggressively in the 1970s, including through overseas affiliates.
- The scandal is often discussed in the context of weak oversight, opaque offshore structures, and political entanglements in Italy during the period.

## Key actors and institutions

- **Roberto Calvi** served as chairman; his downfall became emblematic of the bank’s collapse.
- The **Institute for the Works of Religion (IOR)**, commonly referred to as the Vatican Bank, was connected to Ambrosiano through financial relationships and guarantees cited in later disputes.
- The clandestine Masonic lodge **Propaganda Due (P2)** appeared in investigative narratives about influence networks intersecting with finance, politics, and security services.

## Mechanisms described in reporting

- Investigations and journalism have described the use of foreign subsidiaries, shell entities, and complex inter-company loans.
- Such structures can conceal losses and liabilities, inflate asset values, and move funds outside normal supervisory visibility.

## Collapse and aftermath

- When irregularities surfaced, the bank’s balance sheet could not sustain confidence, contributing to the 1982 failure.
- Calvi fled Italy and was later found dead in London, a death that produced years of legal proceedings and competing theories.
- The Vatican Bank’s later payment is generally described as a settlement related to disputed letters of patronage/guarantees rather than an admission of criminal liability.

## Why it mattered

- The case became one of Europe’s most notorious banking scandals, illustrating how financial institutions can be destabilized by governance failures and hidden leverage.
- It also influenced public debate about transparency, political corruption, and the regulation of cross-border banking operations.

## Related topics

- Italian financial regulation reforms in the 1980s.
- P2 investigations and their broader political implications.
- Modern compliance concepts: beneficial ownership, offshore supervision, and anti–money laundering controls.
