## Summary

First directly elected Brazilian president after military rule, impeached for corruption involving a $350 million influence-peddling scheme run by his campaign treasurer.

## Background and Political Rise

- Collor built his national profile as governor of Alagoas and as a television-forward political figure during Brazil's transition away from military rule.
- His 1989 campaign emphasized anti-corruption rhetoric and modernization, tapping into public frustration with hyperinflation and patronage politics.

## Presidency (1990–1992)

- Early in office, his administration launched a dramatic stabilization plan that included freezing bank deposits and imposing strict liquidity controls (often described as a shock program).
- The approach aimed to rapidly curb inflation but carried severe short-term social and economic disruption.

## Corruption Allegations and Impeachment

- The impeachment centered on allegations that a scheme linked to his campaign treasurer, Paulo César Farias, solicited illicit payments from businesses in exchange for influence and access.
- Brazil's Congress opened impeachment proceedings in 1992; Collor resigned while the process was underway, but the Senate continued and removed him from office.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- The case became a defining moment for Brazil's post-dictatorship accountability mechanisms, demonstrating the political viability of impeachment in the new democratic era.
- Collor later returned to elective politics, illustrating the persistence of political networks even after high-profile scandals.
