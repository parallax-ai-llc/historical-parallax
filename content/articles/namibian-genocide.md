## Summary

German colonial forces exterminated 80% of the Herero and 50% of the Nama peoples in the first genocide of the 20th century. Survivors were placed in concentration camps.

## Context

The genocide took place in German South West Africa (present-day Namibia) during the German Empire’s colonial rule. Armed uprisings by Herero communities in 1904 were met with escalating military repression, including mass killings, forced displacement into desert regions, and starvation tactics.

## Key Policies and Methods

- **Extermination order (Vernichtungsbefehl):** German commander Lothar von Trotha issued an order that Herero people were to be driven out and killed if found within German-controlled areas.
- **Forced removal and pursuit:** Large groups were pushed toward the Omaheke region of the Kalahari Desert, where access to water sources was restricted.
- **Concentration camps and forced labor:** Survivors, including many Nama, were confined in camps (including at coastal sites) and subjected to forced labor, disease, and high mortality.

## Major Affected Groups

- **Herero:** Suffered catastrophic losses after 1904, with death resulting from combat, dehydration, starvation, and camp conditions.
- **Nama:** Faced similar repression in the later phases of the conflict, including imprisonment and forced labor.

## Aftermath

By 1908, the organized campaign had effectively ended, but the demographic, social, and economic effects persisted for generations. Land dispossession and coerced labor regimes reshaped Namibian society under colonial administration.

## Recognition and Historical Significance

Many historians describe the events as the first genocide of the 20th century and a key case in studies of colonial violence. In the 21st century, the genocide has been the subject of renewed public attention, memorialization, and negotiations over acknowledgement and reparations.
