## Summary

Tyco International CEO Dennis Kozlowski looted $400 million from the company including a $6,000 shower curtain and $2 million birthday party for his wife in Sardinia.

## What Happened (High Level)

The “Tyco scandal” refers to a set of corporate governance and executive-compensation abuses that emerged around Tyco International in the early 2000s.

At the center was CEO Dennis Kozlowski, who became a symbol of the era’s executive excess: lavish personal spending, loans, and other benefits that were alleged to be improperly authorized or inadequately disclosed.

While the most widely repeated details are the extravagant items (e.g., luxury parties and decor), the underlying issue was governance: whether company funds and incentive programs were used in ways that breached fiduciary duties, internal controls, and shareholder expectations.

## Key Themes

- **Executive compensation and perks**: the dispute was not only about salary, but about how bonuses, loans, and benefits were approved and reported.
- **Internal controls**: the scandal highlighted weaknesses in approval workflows, board oversight, and documentation.
- **Disclosure and accountability**: the case became part of a broader early-2000s push for stricter corporate reporting and compliance.

## Legal and Civil Outcomes

In criminal proceedings, Kozlowski was convicted in 2005 of crimes related to his receipt of **$81 million in unauthorized bonuses**, the **purchase of art for $14.725 million**, and Tyco’s payment of a **$20 million investment banking fee** to a former Tyco director, among other issues.

In addition to criminal penalties, Tyco pursued civil claims. A court found that compensation and benefits received during the period of disloyalty (between **1997 and 2002**) were forfeited back to the company under New York’s **“faithless servant” doctrine**.

## Why It Became a Reference Point

The Tyco case is often cited alongside other early-2000s corporate scandals because it made the costs of weak governance concrete and easy to understand.

It also contributed to a lasting skepticism toward opaque executive pay practices, and to the expectation that boards and auditors should be able to clearly justify how top executives are compensated.
