## Summary

Palestinian terrorist attack at the 1972 Munich Olympics where Black September militants took 11 Israeli athletes hostage. All 11 hostages, 5 terrorists, and 1 German police officer were killed.

## Additional Context

### What happened

On September 5, 1972, members of the Palestinian militant organization Black September infiltrated the Olympic Village in Munich and attacked the Israeli team’s quarters. Hostages were taken, and negotiations unfolded over many hours under intense media scrutiny.

A rescue attempt was later staged at Fürstenfeldbruck airbase after the hostage-takers and hostages were transported there. The operation failed, and the hostages were killed along with several attackers and a German police officer.

### Key factors in the failed rescue

Post-incident reviews and historical accounts have highlighted multiple systemic problems:

- Limited specialized counter-terrorism capacity and equipment at the time.
- Poor coordination between negotiators, police units, and planners.
- Inadequate intelligence about the number and positioning of attackers.
- Live media coverage that inadvertently revealed aspects of police movement.

### Consequences

The massacre had far-reaching political and security effects:

- West Germany and other countries accelerated development of specialized counter-terror units (Germany later created GSG 9).
- Israel launched covert operations aimed at identifying and killing individuals believed to be responsible (often referred to as “Operation Wrath of God”).
- The Olympics and other mass events adopted more intensive security practices, changing the character of international sporting events.

### Memory and accountability

The event remained a major point of contention for decades, including debates about responsibility for security failures, long-term support for victims’ families, and how the Olympics should commemorate the dead.
