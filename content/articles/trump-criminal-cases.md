## Summary

Donald Trump became the first US president criminally indicted, facing 91 felony charges across four cases. Convicted on 34 counts in the hush money case; other cases ongoing.

## Overview of the four prosecutions

- The set of cases commonly summarized as “four criminal cases” involved separate prosecutors, courts, and legal theories.
- The matters included both state and federal proceedings, and they advanced on different schedules.
- Public reporting typically grouped them into:
  - A New York state case related to business records and payments connected to the 2016 campaign period.
  - A federal case related to retention of classified documents and obstruction allegations.
  - A federal case related to efforts to overturn the 2020 election and events surrounding January 6.
  - A Georgia state case related to election-interference allegations connected to 2020.

## 2024 New York conviction (hush-money / business records)

- In 2024, a New York jury convicted Trump on 34 felony counts in the case commonly described as the hush-money or business-records matter.
- The case is often cited as the first criminal conviction of a former U.S. president.
- Post-trial motions, sentencing issues, and appeals became a major procedural focus after the verdict.

## Significance and legal implications

- The prosecutions raised recurring questions about presidential immunity, executive power, and the treatment of former officials in criminal court.
- They also influenced election-year legal strategy, scheduling decisions, and the interaction between criminal proceedings and political campaigns.
- Courts considered issues including venue, evidentiary disputes, and the timing of trials relative to elections and other proceedings.

## Terminology and reporting notes

- “Indictment,” “arraignment,” “trial,” “conviction,” and “sentencing” are distinct procedural steps and occurred at different times across jurisdictions.
- The number of charges and their classification (felony vs. misdemeanor) varied by case and could change due to superseding indictments and court rulings.
