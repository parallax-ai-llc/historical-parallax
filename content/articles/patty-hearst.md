## Summary

American heiress kidnapped by the Symbionese Liberation Army in 1974 who later participated in their bank robberies. She claimed she was brainwashed but was convicted of bank robbery before being pardoned.

## The Symbionese Liberation Army (SLA)

The Symbionese Liberation Army was a small, radical left-wing group active in the United States in the early 1970s. Hearst's kidnapping became one of the most widely covered incidents connected to the group and helped define public perceptions of domestic political violence in that era.

## Coercion, "Brainwashing," and Legal Arguments

A central question in the case was how to interpret Hearst's actions after the kidnapping. The defense argued that she acted under coercion and psychological pressure, while prosecutors portrayed her as a willing participant. The case is frequently discussed in relation to concepts such as coercive control, duress, and the limits of consent under captivity.

## Conviction and Clemency

After conviction, her sentence was later commuted, and she ultimately received a presidential pardon. The sequence of conviction, commutation, and pardon made the case a lasting reference point in debates about executive clemency and the extent to which it should account for contested narratives about coercion.

## Cultural Afterlife

Hearst's story has been retold in books, documentaries, and films, and it continues to be cited as an example of how intense media coverage can shape legal and political understanding of high-profile crimes.
