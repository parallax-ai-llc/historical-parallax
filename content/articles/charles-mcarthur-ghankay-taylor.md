## Summary

Liberian president convicted of war crimes for supporting the RUF in Sierra Leone. His election campaign slogan was 'He killed my ma, he killed my pa, I'll vote for him.'

## Rise to Power

- Taylor emerged as a major figure during Liberia’s civil conflict, leading an armed faction that fought for control of the state.
- The Liberian civil wars involved mass atrocities, the collapse of institutions, and extensive regional spillover effects.

## Presidency (1997–2003)

- Taylor won the 1997 election in a post-war environment marked by fear, displacement, and armed groups that were not fully demobilized.
- His presidency was associated with repression, renewed conflict, and international isolation, as well as allegations of supporting armed actors beyond Liberia.

## Sierra Leone and International Justice

- Taylor was prosecuted by the Special Court for Sierra Leone for aiding and abetting war crimes and crimes against humanity committed during Sierra Leone’s civil war.
- The conviction is often cited as a significant precedent: a former head of state held criminally responsible by an internationalized tribunal for conduct linked to a neighboring conflict.

## Imprisonment and Legacy

- After conviction, Taylor was transferred to serve his sentence in the United Kingdom.
- His case is central to discussions about accountability for leaders who enable violence through financing, arms transfers, or political support rather than direct command on the battlefield.

## Why It Matters

- Taylor’s trajectory is frequently used in studies of warlord politics, post-conflict elections, and the limits of peace settlements that leave armed power intact.
- The Special Court record also provides extensive documentation about regional conflict networks in West Africa during the 1990s and early 2000s.
