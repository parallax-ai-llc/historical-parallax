## Background

Kim Dae-jung was a prominent South Korean opposition politician and a leading critic of authoritarian rule in the 1960s–1970s. His growing domestic popularity and international profile made him a high-value target for political intimidation.

## The 1973 Tokyo Kidnapping (High-Level)

- **Date and place:** August 1973, Tokyo, Japan.
- **Perpetrators:** Agents associated with South Korea's intelligence services (commonly described as the KCIA in contemporary accounts).
- **Method:** Kim was seized from a Tokyo hotel and taken to a boat, in an apparent attempt to remove him from Japan and return him to South Korea.
- **Near-fatal outcome:** Reports describe an intended killing at sea, which was not carried out.

## International Pressure and Aftermath

Japan protested the violation of its sovereignty, and the incident drew attention from allied governments. Kim ultimately survived and later returned to political life, remaining a central figure in South Korea's democratization movement.

## Historical Significance

The kidnapping is often cited as an emblematic case of Cold War–era authoritarian repression in South Korea and of the risks faced by opposition leaders. In later decades, Kim's career trajectory—from dissident to president and Nobel Peace Prize laureate—shaped how the episode is remembered.
