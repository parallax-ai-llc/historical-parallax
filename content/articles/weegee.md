## Summary

Weegee, born Usher Fellig (later Arthur Fellig), was an Austrian-born American press photographer whose raw, unflinching images of New York City crime scenes, fires, and street life defined a generation of photojournalism. Operating largely between 1935 and 1946, he became famous for arriving at disaster scenes — often before the police — armed with a 4x5 Speed Graphic camera and a fierce instinct for the dramatic moment. His work transformed the aesthetics of documentary photography and embedded the gritty reality of urban life into the American visual consciousness.

Weegee's photographs did more than document events; they humanized tragedy and exposed the stark inequalities of mid-century New York. His images ran in tabloids such as *PM* and *Acme Newspictures*, reaching mass audiences and cementing his reputation as the city's most relentless chronicler. His 1945 book *Naked City* became one of the most influential photography collections of the 20th century, inspiring a film, a television series, and countless photographers who followed.

## Early Life & Background

Usher Fellig was born on June 12, 1899, in Złoczów, Austria-Hungary (present-day Ukraine). His family emigrated to the United States in 1909, settling on Manhattan's Lower East Side. Growing up in poverty amid the tenements of the immigrant neighborhood, Fellig left school at fourteen to help support his family. He worked a series of odd jobs before finding work as a darkroom assistant at a commercial photography studio around 1918. By the early 1920s he had joined the *Acme Newspictures* agency, where he developed his technical skills processing and printing wire photographs.

## Rise to Prominence

Fellig went freelance around 1935, a pivotal decision that freed him to pursue crime scenes on his own terms. He installed a police-band radio and darkroom equipment in his car — one of the first photographers to do so — enabling him to arrive at scenes with extraordinary speed. The nickname "Weegee" derived from "Ouija board," a nod to his seemingly supernatural ability to anticipate where news would break. He rented a room near police headquarters on Centre Street in Manhattan and made it his base of operations, monitoring calls through the night. By the late 1930s he was a fixture in the New York tabloid world, selling images to *PM*, the *New York Daily News*, and wire services.

## Key Achievements

His 1945 book *Naked City* collected his most powerful work, presenting stark flash-lit portraits of murder victims, grieving families, arrested criminals, and Coney Island beachgoers. The book sold widely and was adapted into the 1948 noir film of the same name. A second collection, *Weegee's People* (1946), broadened his scope to include the city's social fabric more broadly. He was given a solo exhibition at the Photo League in 1941 and was the first photographer granted an official police press pass allowing him unrestricted access to crime scenes. His total output is estimated at over 5,000 published photographs during his peak years.

## Controversies & Criticism

Weegee's methods raised persistent ethical questions. Critics charged that he staged or manipulated scenes for dramatic effect and that his intrusion on grieving families was exploitative. His use of harsh flash photography was seen by some contemporaries as aesthetically crude. In later years, his distortion photography — created using special lenses to warp celebrity portraits — was viewed by some as a commercial decline from his earlier serious work.

## Legacy & Influence

Weegee's influence on documentary and street photography is immeasurable. His work anticipated the aesthetic of photographers such as Diane Arbus and influenced the visual language of film noir. The *Naked City* remains in print and is taught in photography programs worldwide. His archive is held by the International Center of Photography in New York, which has organized multiple retrospective exhibitions of his work.
