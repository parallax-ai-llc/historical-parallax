## Summary

Cryptocurrency exchange Binance pleaded guilty to money laundering and sanctions violations, paying $4.3 billion in penalties. CEO Changpeng Zhao resigned and was sentenced to prison.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2023 | Binance pleaded guilty to money laundering |

## Additional Context

- The case is commonly discussed as part of a broader push to bring major crypto exchanges under **bank-like compliance expectations** (KYC/AML, sanctions screening, and transaction monitoring).
- Regulatory actions against crypto platforms have often focused on whether firms operated as **unregistered** intermediaries and whether they maintained adequate compliance controls.

## What the Enforcement Emphasized (High Level)

- **Anti–money laundering (AML):** policies, staffing, and tooling to detect suspicious flows.
- **Sanctions compliance:** preventing access by sanctioned persons or jurisdictions.
- **Governance:** internal controls and leadership accountability when compliance is treated as secondary to growth.

## Why It Matters

- The penalties and leadership change were interpreted by many as a **watershed moment** for the industry: large exchanges can no longer assume regulatory arbitrage will last indefinitely.
- The episode is used in policy debates about how to regulate crypto while preserving legitimate innovation.

## Practical Takeaways

- Compliance programs are treated as core infrastructure once an exchange reaches global scale.
- Enforcement actions can reshape market structure by pushing volume toward platforms that can meet licensing and reporting requirements.
- Cases like this are frequently cited in discussions of stablecoin on/off-ramps, mixers, and cross-border sanctions evasion.

- It is also a reference point for how compliance expectations scale from startups to systemically important intermediaries.
