## Summary

Rwandan singer whose songs were used to incite hatred and violence against Tutsis during the genocide. Convicted of incitement to genocide through his music and public speeches.

## Music, propaganda, and the 1994 genocide

- Bikindi was associated with popular music that carried political messages in the years leading up to the genocide against the Tutsi.
- Prosecutors argued that particular lyrics and public statements contributed to an atmosphere of dehumanization and mobilization for violence.
- The case is often discussed as an example of how cultural production can intersect with political incitement in mass-violence contexts.

## Trial and conviction (high-level)

- Bikindi was tried at the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR).
- He was convicted on counts related to direct and public incitement to commit genocide, with findings linked to speeches and the dissemination of messages during the genocide period.
- Legal commentary on the case frequently focuses on evidentiary standards for incitement and the boundary between expression and criminal responsibility.

## Legacy

- The Bikindi case is cited in discussions of media and hate speech in Rwanda, alongside radio incitement cases.
- It remains a reference point for international criminal law debates about propaganda, intent, and causation.

## Related context

- The ICTR also tried prominent media actors connected to genocide-era propaganda, reinforcing the legal focus on direct and public incitement.
- In Rwanda and in international justice scholarship, these cases are often discussed together as part of a broader record on mass-atrocity communication.
