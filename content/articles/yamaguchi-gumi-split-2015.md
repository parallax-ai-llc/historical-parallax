## Summary

Japan's largest yakuza group split when 13 senior members broke away to form the Kobe Yamaguchi-gumi. The split raised fears of a gang war similar to the 1985 Yama-Ichi War.

## Context

The Yamaguchi-gumi is widely described as Japan’s largest yakuza syndicate, with a long history of internal factions and periodic violence between rival groups.[^1]

Major splits and succession disputes have historically been significant because they can reshape alliances, territory, and revenue streams across multiple prefectures.[^1]

## The 2015 Split

In August 2015, a bloc of senior members and affiliated groups left the Yamaguchi-gumi and formed a rival organization commonly referred to as the Kobe Yamaguchi-gumi.[^1]

The split was reported to involve multiple constituent gangs (affiliated groups) rather than a small personal dispute, which increased concern about a broader conflict.[^1]

## Causes (as reported)

Public reporting summarized on Wikipedia describes the breakup as stemming from internal dissatisfaction and power struggles within the Yamaguchi-gumi leadership structure.[^1]

Because yakuza organizations are federated networks of semi-autonomous groups, disputes over leadership, money, and autonomy can escalate into organizational fracture.[^1]

## Immediate Aftermath

Authorities and media raised concerns about potential retaliatory violence, often drawing comparisons to earlier yakuza conflicts such as the Yama-Ichi War in the 1980s.[^1]

In response to the heightened risk, Japanese police increased monitoring and implemented measures intended to deter open conflict between the factions.[^1]

## Law Enforcement and Public Impact

Japan’s anti-boryokudan (anti-organized-crime) framework has historically aimed to restrict yakuza activities through regulation, enforcement pressure, and limits on business relationships.[^1]

High-profile organizational splits can amplify public scrutiny and lead to additional policing around known offices and areas with a history of gang activity.[^1]

## Significance

The 2015 Yamaguchi-gumi split is notable as a modern example of how internal disputes can produce durable rival organizations rather than temporary breakaway groups.[^1]

It is also frequently cited in discussions of contemporary yakuza structure, including how federated syndicates manage alliances and internal discipline under sustained legal pressure.[^1]
