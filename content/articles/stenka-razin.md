## Summary

Don Cossack leader who led a massive peasant rebellion and pirate raids on the Caspian Sea.

Stepan (Stenka) Razin was a 17th-century Don Cossack ataman whose activities combined **raiding, river-and-sea piracy, and political rebellion** in the Tsardom of Russia. He became a major symbol of popular unrest during a period marked by social stratification, heavy obligations on peasants, and frontier militarization.

His uprising (often dated 1670–1671) spread rapidly along major river routes, drew support from various discontented groups, and culminated in large-scale clashes with state forces before his capture and execution.

## Context and Causes (High-Level)

- **Frontier society**: Don Cossack communities operated with semi-autonomous traditions, military organization, and a raiding economy.
- **Social tensions**: broad dissatisfaction among peasants and lower strata, aggravated by tightening controls and obligations.
- **River corridors**: the Volga and connected routes enabled fast movement of forces, supplies, and propaganda, helping the revolt spread.

## The Uprising (1670–1671)

Razin’s revolt blended promises of liberation and retribution against officials with practical incentives (loot, debt relief, and local autonomy). The rebellion’s momentum depended on controlling strategic towns and waterways; once the central government concentrated forces and retook key positions, the movement fragmented.

## Capture and Execution

After setbacks and loss of control over major strongholds, Razin was betrayed or surrendered by factions under pressure, then taken to Moscow. His execution served as a public demonstration of state authority and a warning against future mass uprisings.

## Legacy and Cultural Memory

- Razin became a lasting figure in **Russian folklore, songs, and popular legend**, often portrayed as a rebel hero or outlaw leader.
- Later writers and artists used his story to explore themes of social justice, violence, and the limits of imperial power.
- In historical interpretation, he is frequently discussed alongside other large popular movements in early modern Russia as an example of frontier unrest turning into nationwide crisis.
