Lindsey Vonn won 82 World Cup races, the most by any female skier, and 2010 Olympic downhill gold.

## Sport Context (Alpine Skiing)

- Alpine skiing at the World Cup level spans multiple disciplines, including **downhill**, **super-G**, **giant slalom**, **slalom**, and combined formats.
- Downhill and super-G emphasize speed and line choice, while technical events emphasize turning precision and rhythm.

## Career Profile (High Level)

- Vonn is best known for elite performance in the speed disciplines, combining aggressive technique with strong tactical decision-making on course.
- She became one of the most recognizable athletes in the sport due to sustained success over many seasons and major-event wins.

## Major Achievements

- Her 2010 Olympic downhill gold is often treated as the defining highlight of her Olympic career.
- Beyond the Olympics, she is widely associated with repeated World Cup victories and overall season dominance during her peak years.

## Injuries and Comebacks

- Vonn’s career is also notable for serious injuries that interrupted seasons and required extended rehabilitation.
- Despite setbacks, she repeatedly returned to competition at a top level, reinforcing a public reputation for resilience.

## Retirement and Aftermath

- She retired from World Cup racing in 2019, closing a career that is frequently cited in discussions of modern women’s alpine skiing.
- After retirement, she has remained in the public eye through sports commentary, advocacy, and broader media appearances.

## Legacy

- Vonn is commonly referenced as a benchmark for excellence in women’s speed events and a model of longevity in an injury-prone sport.
- Her record-setting results helped expand mainstream visibility for women’s alpine skiing in the United States.

## Key Takeaways

- Vonn’s story is often told through a mix of peak dominance, high-risk speed events, and repeated comebacks.
- The combination of World Cup consistency and Olympic success made her one of her era’s defining alpine skiers.
