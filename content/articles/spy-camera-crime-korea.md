## Summary

South Korea faces an epidemic of illegal spy camera (molka) recordings in bathrooms, changing rooms, and hotels. Over 6,000 cases reported annually; mass protests demanding action occurred in 2018.

## Scope and Modus Operandi

The Korean term "molka" is commonly used to describe covert filming, often involving small concealed cameras placed in private or semi-private spaces such as public restrooms, changing rooms, accommodation, and other areas where victims expect privacy.[^1]

Recordings may be sold, traded, or uploaded to online communities, sometimes alongside other forms of non-consensual intimate imagery. While cases vary, the harm often includes long-term digital persistence, doxxing risks, and repeated re-victimization when content is redistributed.[^1]

## Social and Political Response

Public concern intensified as reported incidents and media coverage highlighted both the prevalence of hidden-camera devices and the perceived difficulty of removing content once it spreads online. Critics have also argued that investigation and prosecution outcomes did not match the scale of offending, fueling distrust and calls for stronger enforcement.[^1]

In 2018, large demonstrations—frequently associated with the slogan "My Life Is Not Your Porn"—demanded tougher penalties, improved investigative capacity, and greater accountability for digital sex crimes.[^1]

## Law Enforcement and Policy Measures

Authorities have responded with a mix of policing initiatives, device-detection efforts, and legal reforms aimed at increasing penalties and improving victim support. Government agencies and local jurisdictions have also conducted inspections of public facilities and expanded counseling and reporting channels for victims.[^1]

Because technology and distribution platforms change rapidly, enforcement challenges often include identifying perpetrators, tracing uploads across multiple sites, and balancing evidentiary requirements with urgent victim needs.[^1]

## Relation to Broader Digital Sex Crime

The problem is frequently discussed alongside wider categories of digital sex crime in South Korea, including illicit distribution networks and blackmail schemes. High-profile cases have periodically renewed public pressure for stronger regulation of online platforms and faster takedown mechanisms for abusive content.[^1]
