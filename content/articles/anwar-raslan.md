## Summary

Former Syrian intelligence officer convicted in Germany of crimes against humanity for overseeing the torture of at least 4,000 people at Branch 251 detention center in Damascus.

## Case Context

Raslan’s conviction is widely discussed in connection with Germany’s use of **universal jurisdiction**, a legal framework that can allow domestic courts to prosecute certain international crimes even when they were committed outside the country. The proceedings are often described as among the first major trials worldwide addressing state-linked torture in Syria’s conflict.

## Branch 251 (Al-Khatib) Detention Center

Branch 251, also known as Al-Khatib, has been referenced in human-rights reporting as an intelligence detention facility in Damascus. Court findings in Germany addressed allegations of systematic detention and torture at the site during the period covered by the charges.

## Trial and Evidentiary Themes (High Level)

Public reporting on the trial has emphasized:

- testimony from survivors and witnesses,
- documentation and corroboration efforts by investigators and NGOs, and
- the challenge of prosecuting international crimes when many direct perpetrators and records remain in the country where the crimes occurred.

## Significance

The case is frequently cited as a precedent for accountability pathways when international tribunals are unavailable or politically blocked. It also illustrates how diaspora communities and transnational investigations can contribute to domestic prosecutions.
