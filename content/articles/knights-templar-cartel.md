## Summary

Mexican drug cartel that succeeded La Familia Michoacana. They combined extreme violence with quasi-religious ideology and controlled the Michoacan avocado trade.

## Origins and Ideology

The Knights Templar Cartel emerged in the Mexican state of Michoacán after splintering from La Familia Michoacana.
Open sources often describe the group as using propaganda, rules of conduct, and pseudo-religious language to legitimize its power.
This messaging blended claims of moral order with extortion and organized violence.

## Criminal Activities

The cartel has been linked in reporting to drug trafficking, kidnapping, extortion, and control over local businesses.
In Michoacán, it was frequently associated with imposing "taxes" on industries and communities, including agriculture and transport.
References to the avocado trade appear in public discussions as an example of how criminal groups can extract rents from legitimate supply chains.

## Conflict with Communities and Self-Defense Forces

A notable development during the cartel's peak was the rise of armed self-defense groups (autodefensas) in some communities.
These militias formed in response to violence and extortion, creating a complex landscape involving civilians, cartels, and state forces.
The situation drew national attention and raised questions about security policy, rule of law, and the state's capacity to protect residents.

## State Response and Fragmentation

High-profile arrests and operations weakened the organization over time.
As with many criminal groups, pressure from law enforcement can lead to fragmentation, leadership turnover, and splinter groups.
The Knights Templar case is often cited in analyses of cartel evolution and the instability created by leadership decapitation strategies.
