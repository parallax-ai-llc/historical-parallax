## Summary

Global organ trafficking generates $840 million to $1.7 billion annually. Organs are harvested from trafficked victims, prisoners, and the desperately poor, primarily for wealthy recipients.

## Additional Context

Organ trafficking refers to the illicit removal, sale, or brokerage of human organs and tissues for transplantation outside regulated medical systems. It overlaps with (but is distinct from) **human trafficking for the purpose of organ removal**, where coercion, deception, or abuse of vulnerability is central.

### Common patterns

- **Brokered transplants:** intermediaries match buyers with sellers (or victims), often across borders.
- **Coercion and fraud:** victims may be deceived about medical risks, paid far less than promised, or threatened.
- **Weak oversight:** corruption, poor record-keeping, and under-resourced enforcement can enable illicit procurement.

### Why it persists

Demand for transplantable organs can far exceed legal supply, creating incentives for black-market activity. Economic inequality and lack of access to healthcare can increase the risk that vulnerable people are exploited.

### International responses

- **Professional standards:** bodies such as the World Health Organization have issued guidance emphasizing voluntary consent, traceability, and bans on organ commercialization.
- **The Declaration of Istanbul:** a widely cited framework opposing organ trafficking and transplant tourism.
- **Criminal enforcement:** prosecutions tend to focus on brokers and corrupt facilitators, though cases are difficult due to cross-border evidence and victim intimidation.

### Terminology note

In policy discussions, “organ trafficking” is often grouped with **transplant tourism** and **trafficking in persons for organ removal**, but these categories are not identical.

## How organ trafficking is investigated and prosecuted

Because procurement, travel, and transplantation can occur in different jurisdictions, investigations often rely on financial records, hospital auditing, and cross-border cooperation. Common enforcement targets include brokers, corrupt medical staff, and document forgers, while victim testimony can be hard to obtain due to fear, stigma, or migration status.

## Data and measurement challenges

Reliable prevalence estimates are difficult: illicit transplants are designed to evade reporting systems, and legitimate transplant statistics do not always capture coercion or fraud in the supply chain. As a result, many figures are presented as ranges and are sensitive to the definitions used (organ trafficking vs. transplant tourism vs. trafficking in persons).

## Public health and ethics considerations

Organ trafficking raises major ethical issues around consent, exploitation, and medical harm. It also creates public health risks when surgeries are performed without adequate screening, follow-up care, or infection control, potentially harming both donors and recipients.
