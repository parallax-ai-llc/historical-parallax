## Summary

Duplicate consolidated.

R. Allen Stanford is best known for leading a large-scale investment fraud centered on Stanford International Bank (SIB) and the sale of high-yield certificates of deposit, a case that became one of the major U.S. financial fraud prosecutions of the 2000s.

## Career Timeline

## Key Facts (High Level)

- **Central entity:** Stanford International Bank (SIB), headquartered in Antigua.
- **Core product:** Certificates of deposit marketed as safe, high-yield investments.
- **Allegation:** Customer funds were not invested as represented; funds were instead misused and recycled to sustain the operation.
- **Impact:** Large investor losses and extensive litigation/receivership activity.

## What the Case Is Known For

- **Offshore marketing + U.S. distribution:** A structure that mixed offshore banking with U.S.-based sales and advisory networks.
- **Consistency narrative:** Promotional materials emphasized steady returns and low volatility, which is a common hallmark in later fraud retrospectives.
- **Regulatory action:** The case is frequently cited alongside other major post‑2000 U.S. enforcement actions involving complex financial sales.

## Aftermath

- **Criminal outcome:** Stanford was convicted in U.S. federal court (2012).
- **Asset recovery:** The matter involved receivership and attempts to marshal assets for restitution, though recoveries in fraud cases can be limited relative to losses.
