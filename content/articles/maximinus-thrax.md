## Summary

First soldier-emperor of Rome who never visited the city and ruled through extreme taxation and violence. He was murdered by his own troops during the siege of Aquileia.

## Historical context: the Crisis of the Third Century

- Maximinus’ reign is commonly placed at the beginning of the “Crisis of the Third Century,” a period marked by rapid imperial turnover, civil conflict, and external pressures.
- The Roman army’s role in selecting emperors increased, and legitimacy increasingly depended on military support rather than senatorial acceptance.
- Fiscal strain was a recurring feature of the era, as frontier warfare and internal instability demanded revenue.

## Rule and governance (high level)

- Ancient sources portray Maximinus as governing primarily through the army and provincial command structures rather than through the Roman political elite.
- Heavy taxation and requisitions are frequently cited as drivers of resentment among elites and communities expected to fund military campaigns.
- The Senate’s hostility toward soldier-emperors is a key theme in narratives about this reign.

## 238 CE and collapse of authority

- Opposition to Maximinus intersected with revolts and competing claimants during 238 CE (the “Year of the Six Emperors” in many modern summaries).
- The siege of Aquileia and logistical difficulties are commonly mentioned as undermining morale.
- His killing by troops reflects a broader pattern of military-driven successions during the period.

## Legacy

- Maximinus is often used as an example of how emperors could rise from military ranks and fall when support eroded.
- His reign is cited in discussions of how political instability can accelerate administrative and fiscal breakdown.
