## Summary

The Reconquista — literally "the Reconquest" — refers to the centuries-long process by which the
Christian kingdoms of the Iberian Peninsula gradually recovered territory from the Muslim polities
that had ruled much of Iberia since the Umayyad conquest of 711 CE.
Spanning nearly eight hundred years, from the early resistance movements in the Asturian mountains
to the fall of the Emirate of Granada on January 2, 1492, it is one of the longest military and
political campaigns in Western history, reshaping the religious, cultural, and ethnic composition
of what would become Spain and Portugal.

The historical reality of the Reconquista was far more complex than later Christian nationalist
narratives suggested.
For much of its duration, the Iberian Peninsula was characterized by fluid alliances that cut
across religious lines: Christian kings sometimes allied with Muslim rulers against other Christians,
and Muslim taifa lords regularly hired Christian mercenaries.
The interfaith cultural synthesis known as *convivencia* — the coexistence of Christians, Muslims,
and Jews — produced remarkable intellectual and artistic achievements, particularly in centers like
Toledo, Córdoba, and Seville, even as warfare raged at the political frontiers.

The final decade of the Reconquista coincided with a series of events that permanently shaped world
history.
The fall of Granada in January 1492 was followed within months by the expulsion of Spain's Jewish
community under the Alhambra Decree, the forced conversion or expulsion of Muslims, and Christopher
Columbus's first voyage to the Americas — all under the patronage of the same monarchs, Ferdinand
and Isabella, who completed the Reconquista.
The dynamics of crusading ideology, religious uniformity, and expansionist ambition forged in the
Iberian Peninsula were thus directly exported to the New World.

## Historical Context

The Reconquista was born out of the speed and totality of the initial Muslim conquest of Iberia.
In 711 CE, a mixed Berber and Arab force under Tariq ibn Ziyad crossed the Strait of Gibraltar,
defeated the Visigothic King Roderic at the Battle of Guadalete, and within three years had
overrun virtually the entire peninsula.
The Umayyad Caliphate's control extended north to the Pyrenees; only small Asturian and Basque
territories in the far north remained outside Muslim governance.
The new polity, called Al-Andalus, was administered first as a province of the Umayyad Caliphate
and later as an independent emirate and caliphate.
It would endure in various forms for nearly eight centuries.

The Christian resistance that coalesced in the northern mountains from the 720s onward was at first
militarily insignificant.
The traditional starting point of the Reconquista — the Battle of Covadonga, around 718–722,
in which the Asturian nobleman Pelayo defeated a Muslim raiding force — was a minor skirmish
inflated by later chronicles into a foundational myth.
The Kingdom of Asturias, which emerged from this resistance, spent its first century consolidating
control over northwestern Iberia rather than mounting any serious offensive against Al-Andalus.
It was the internal fragmentation of Muslim Iberia — particularly the collapse of the unified
Umayyad Caliphate of Córdoba in 1031 into dozens of competing petty kingdoms called *taifas* —
that created the structural opportunity for Christian expansion.

The eleventh through thirteenth centuries were the period of the Reconquista's most dramatic
territorial advances.
The capture of Toledo in 1085 by Alfonso VI of León and Castile brought the symbolic Visigothic
capital back under Christian rule and opened the Meseta to further southward expansion.
The great victory at Las Navas de Tolosa in 1212, where a joint crusading force from Castile,
Aragon, Navarre, and Portugal shattered the Almohad army, broke the last powerful North African
dynasty's hold on Al-Andalus and opened Andalusia to conquest.
Within fifty years, Castile had taken Córdoba (1236), Seville (1248), and Cádiz.
Aragon had taken Valencia (1238) and the Balearic Islands.
By 1270, only the Nasrid Emirate of Granada remained as an independent Muslim polity, paying tribute
to Castile and surviving through two centuries of diplomatic skill and mountain geography.

## The Event

### The War of Granada (1482–1492)

The final conquest of Granada was less a single event than a decade-long military campaign prosecuted
with systematic determination by Ferdinand of Aragon and Isabella of Castile — the "Catholic
Monarchs" whose marriage in 1469 had unified the two largest Christian kingdoms of Iberia.
Granada's last Nasrid emirate was weakened by internal dynastic feuding between the emir Abu
al-Hasan Ali, his son Muhammad XII (known to the Spanish as Boabdil), and his brother Muhammad
XIII (El Zagal), offering Ferdinand and Isabella the opportunity they had been seeking.

The campaign began in 1482 when Castilian forces captured Alhama de Granada, a fortress town
commanding key routes into the emirate's heartland.
The fall of Alhama — lamented in a famous Arabic poem, the "Romance of Alhama" — shocked the Muslim
world and demonstrated the vulnerability of Granada's defenses.
Over the following decade, Ferdinand prosecuted a patient strategy of systematic conquest, bypassing
heavily fortified positions where possible and reducing them by siege and starvation where necessary.
The use of artillery — still relatively new in Iberian warfare — proved decisive in reducing walled
towns.

### The Surrender of Granada (January 2, 1492)

By 1490, the emirate had been reduced to the city of Granada itself and a handful of surrounding
towns.
Muhammad XII (Boabdil), who had been captured, ransomed, and effectively turned into a Castilian
client king, negotiated the terms of Granada's surrender in the capitulation treaties of November
1491.
The terms were, on paper, relatively generous:
- Muslims were guaranteed the right to practice their religion.
- They could maintain their mosques and customs.
- They would be governed by their own laws under Castilian sovereignty.
- Property rights were to be respected.

On January 2, 1492, Boabdil handed over the keys of the Alhambra palace to Ferdinand and Isabella.
According to legend, he wept as he rode away and looked back at the city from a mountain pass later
called the "Pass of the Moor's Sigh" (*El Suspiro del Moro*).

### The Aftermath: Expulsions and Forced Conversions

The generous terms of the capitulation proved short-lived.
Within a decade, the policy of religious tolerance gave way under pressure from Cardinal Cisneros
and the institutional machinery of the Spanish Inquisition.

**1492 — Expulsion of Jews:**
The Alhambra Decree of March 31, 1492 ordered all Jews who refused baptism to leave Spain within
four months.
An estimated 100,000 to 200,000 people were affected.
Tens of thousands converted to become *conversos*; tens of thousands more emigrated to Portugal,
North Africa, the Ottoman Empire, and the Netherlands.

**1502 — Forced Conversion of Muslims:**
Muslims in Castile were ordered to convert to Christianity or leave the kingdom.
Those who converted — the *moriscos* — remained under perpetual Inquisitorial suspicion of
secretly practicing Islam.

**1609–1614 — Final Expulsion of Moriscos:**
Under Philip III, approximately 300,000 *moriscos* were expelled from Spain in the final act of
ethnic-religious cleansing, ending nearly a millennium of Islamic presence on the Iberian Peninsula.

### 1492 and the Opening of the Atlantic World

The year 1492 concentrated in a few months events of world-historical consequence:
- January 2: Granada falls — the Reconquista ends.
- March 31: The Alhambra Decree expels Spain's Jews.
- August 3: Christopher Columbus sails westward from Palos de la Frontera.
- October 12: Columbus reaches the Americas.

The ideological infrastructure of the Reconquista — crusading holy war, religious uniformity
enforced by state power, the division of the world between Christian civilization and infidel
territory — traveled directly with the conquistadors to the New World.

## Multiple Perspectives

### Christian Kingdoms of Iberia View

For the Christian kingdoms of Asturias, León, Castile, Aragon, Portugal, and Navarre, the
Reconquista was at its ideological core a war of recovery and restoration.
The Visigothic kingdom destroyed by Muslim forces in 711 was understood — especially from the ninth
century onward, as Asturian court historians developed an elaborate theory of royal continuity —
to be the legitimate inheritance of the Christian kings, whose duty was to reclaim it.
The language of *restauratio* — restoration — pervades medieval Iberian chronicles: the Christian
kings were not conquerors but restorers of what was rightfully theirs.

This ideology intensified as Iberian warfare became integrated into the broader crusading movement
of the eleventh and twelfth centuries.
The papacy granted crusade indulgences to fighters in Iberia from 1063 onward.
The military orders — the Order of Santiago, the Order of Calatrava, the Order of Alcántara —
gave the Reconquista a permanent institutional warrior class dedicated to holy war.
The cult of Saint James (Santiago Matamoros, "Moor-slayer") at Santiago de Compostela became the
spiritual center of this ideology, with the apostle depicted iconographically as a mounted knight
trampling Muslim warriors.

The Catholic Monarchs Ferdinand and Isabella brought this centuries-long project to its completion,
understanding it explicitly in providential terms.
The fall of Granada was celebrated throughout Christendom as a divine triumph.
The Spanish Inquisition, established in 1478, and the subsequent expulsions and forced conversions
were understood within the framework of creating a religiously unified kingdom — a "New Israel"
fully consecrated to the Christian mission of world evangelization.

### Moorish Islamic Perspective

For the Muslim inhabitants of Al-Andalus, the Reconquista was experienced as an eight-century
process of progressive dispossession, displacement, and ultimately cultural annihilation.
At its height in the tenth century, under Abd ar-Rahman III and his son al-Hakam II, the Umayyad
Caliphate of Córdoba was among the wealthiest and most sophisticated polities in Europe.
Contemporary observers estimated its capital at half a million inhabitants, its library held
hundreds of thousands of volumes, and its court patronized astronomy, medicine, philosophy, and
poetry.
The contrast between that flourishing and the ultimate fate of Iberian Islam — expulsion, forced
conversion, the destruction of mosques and manuscripts — was experienced by Andalusi writers as a
profound tragedy.

Medieval Arabic sources from Al-Andalus express complex attitudes toward the Christian advance.
Court poets lamented the loss of cities with an elegiac intensity — the laments for Seville,
Córdoba, and finally Granada are among the great poems of the Arabic tradition.
Andalusi intellectuals were acutely aware of the fragility of their civilization, particularly after
the Caliphate's collapse in 1031 and the subsequent invasions by the North African Almoravid and
Almohad dynasties — which, while militarily necessary against Christian pressure, brought a
culturally austere and alien fundamentalism that disrupted the sophisticated urban culture many
Andalusis had built.

The memory of Al-Andalus in the Islamic world has been, since the fifteenth century, a powerful
symbol of loss and longing.
This memory has been mobilized across centuries: as a warning about the vulnerability of Muslim
civilization, as an argument for protecting Muslim minorities, and more recently, in some political
rhetoric, as a historical grievance.
Modern Arab and Muslim historians have emphasized both the genuine achievements of Andalusi
civilization and the traumatic rupture of 1492, while also engaging critically with the
mythologized nature of *convivencia* — pointing out that tolerance in Al-Andalus was always
hierarchical, with Christians and Jews as protected but subordinate *dhimmis*, not equals.

### Jewish Community Perspective

The Jewish communities of Iberia — the Sephardim — occupied a uniquely painful position across
the Reconquista's long history.
Under the Umayyad Caliphate, Jews had experienced a period of relative flourishing.
Figures like Hasdai ibn Shaprut and Samuel ibn Naghrela reached positions of extraordinary influence
as courtiers, physicians, and military commanders.
The intellectual culture of Andalusi Jewry — the poetry of Solomon ibn Gabirol, the philosophy of
Maimonides, the biblical commentary of Abraham ibn Ezra — represented a golden age in Jewish
cultural history.

As Christian rule expanded, Jewish communities navigated an increasingly precarious existence.
They were useful to Christian kings as merchants, physicians, financiers, and administrators,
and they received royal protection.
But they were also subject to growing popular hostility, Church pressure for conversion, and
expanding legal disabilities.
The pogroms of 1391 — in which mobs attacked Jewish communities across Castile and Aragon,
killing thousands and forcing mass conversions — created a large population of *conversos* whose
sincerity was perpetually suspect and whose existence helped provide the rationale for the
Spanish Inquisition.

The Alhambra Decree of March 31, 1492 severed a Jewish community from a homeland it had occupied
for more than a millennium.
The Sephardic diaspora scattered across the Ottoman Empire, North Africa, Italy, and the Netherlands,
carrying their Judeo-Spanish language (Ladino), religious traditions, and cultural memory of Spain.
Sephardic communities of Thessaloniki, Istanbul, Livorno, and Amsterdam became major centers of
Jewish intellectual life.
The expulsion of 1492 is commemorated as one of the foundational traumas of modern Jewish history.

### Modern Historians' View

Modern historiography has transformed understanding of the Reconquista in several important ways.
The concept itself — the idea of a unified, continuous, self-consciously religious eight-century
"reconquest" — is now understood as largely a retrospective construction, built up by medieval
chronicles in the service of specific political programs and later reinforced by Romantic
nationalism in the nineteenth century.
The actual participants across most of the period did not think of themselves as engaged in a single
continuous project: they fought for dynastic advantage, land, tribute, and prestige, and they made
alliances and truces with Muslim rulers as political interest dictated.

The concept of *convivencia* — interfaith coexistence — has been similarly revisited.
Scholars like Brian Catlos and Simon Barton argue for a more nuanced picture that neither idealizes
Iberian interfaith relations as a model of tolerance nor reduces them to simple conflict.
The coexistence of Christians, Muslims, and Jews in medieval Iberia was real, produced genuine
cultural exchange, and generated extraordinary intellectual achievements.
It was also hierarchical, punctuated by violence, and undergirded by legal structures that placed
non-Christians in subordinate positions.

Contemporary historians also situate the Reconquista within broader global frameworks.
The connections between Iberian crusading and the broader crusading movement, the role of North
African dynasties in sustaining Al-Andalus, and the direct links between the Reconquista's
conclusion and the opening of the Atlantic colonial era are all emphasized in current scholarship.
The expulsions of 1492 are understood not as incidental postscripts to the military Reconquista
but as integral expressions of the same drive toward religious uniformity — a drive that traveled
with devastating consequences to the Americas.

## Legacy & Consequences

The Reconquista's most immediate consequence was the demographic and religious transformation of
the Iberian Peninsula.
The Muslim and Jewish populations who had lived in Iberia for centuries were expelled, forcibly
converted, or subject to ongoing persecution.
The *converso* and *morisco* communities remained under the scrutiny of the Spanish Inquisition
for generations, their "purity of blood" (*limpieza de sangre*) challenged by the emerging
Spanish state's institutional racism.
This obsession with racial and religious purity, born in the fires of the Reconquista, shaped
Spanish society and its imperial ventures for centuries.

The intellectual legacy was more ambivalent.
The destruction or displacement of Iberian Jewish and Muslim scholarly communities represented an
incalculable cultural loss — libraries burned, manuscript traditions broken, intellectual networks
severed.
Yet much of the learning of Al-Andalus had already been transmitted to Christian Europe through
the translation movement centered in Toledo, where Arabic scientific, philosophical, and medical
texts were rendered into Latin during the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, directly feeding the
European intellectual renaissance.
The works of Aristotle, Avicenna, Averroes, and al-Khwarizmi reached Western Europe largely
through Iberian intermediaries.

The political legacy of the Reconquista extended far beyond the peninsula.
The crusading ideology, the military orders, the Inquisitorial institutions, and the habits of
conquest and forced conversion that were honed across eight centuries of Iberian warfare traveled
directly to the Americas with the conquistadors.
Hernán Cortés and Francisco Pizarro came from the same Castilian military culture that had taken
Granada.
The legal frameworks, the theological justifications for conquest, and even the specific language
of *reconquista* were applied to the subjugation of indigenous American peoples.
In this sense, the Reconquista's true end came not in 1492 but across the following century,
as its dynamics were replicated on a global scale.

## Key Facts at a Glance

- **Duration:** ~774 years (718–1492)
- **Starting event:** Battle of Covadonga, c. 718–722
- **Ending event:** Surrender of Granada, January 2, 1492
- **Key battle:** Las Navas de Tolosa (1212) — broke Almohad power
- **Catholic Monarchs:** Ferdinand II of Aragon and Isabella I of Castile
- **Last Muslim ruler:** Muhammad XII (Boabdil) of Granada
- **1492 expulsion:** ~100,000–200,000 Jews expelled (Alhambra Decree)
- **Morisco expulsion:** ~300,000 expelled 1609–1614
- **Cultural legacy:** Translation movement transmitted Greek and Arabic learning to Europe
- **Global legacy:** Crusading and conquest culture exported directly to the Americas
