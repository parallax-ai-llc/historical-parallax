## Background and Origins

CJNG emerged in the early 2010s in the western Mexican state of Jalisco amid fragmentation of older trafficking groups and intensified federal pressure on cartel leadership. The group is widely associated with Nemesio Rubn "El Mencho" Oseguera Cervantes, who has been described as a key leader in its rise.

## Organization and Methods

Open reporting describes CJNG as combining territorial control with rapid, violent demonstrations intended to deter rivals and influence local authorities. Its operations have been linked to attacks on security forces and high-visibility incidents that signal capability and willingness to escalate.

## Areas of Activity

CJNG has been reported to operate across multiple Mexican states and to compete with rival organizations for trafficking routes, ports, and urban distribution hubs. Analysts and law-enforcement reporting have also associated the group with synthetic-drug production, including fentanyl, alongside other illicit markets.

## Government and International Response

Mexican authorities and international partners have pursued CJNG through arrest operations, targeted sanctions, and coordination focused on trafficking networks and precursor supply chains. The group remains a major subject of security policy discussions because of its violence levels and cross-border trafficking impact.

## Legacy and Significance

CJNG is frequently cited in contemporary accounts as an example of a cartel that scaled quickly through both organizational adaptability and extreme violence. Its evolution is often discussed in the broader context of Mexico's post-2006 security environment and the shift toward synthetic-drug economies.
