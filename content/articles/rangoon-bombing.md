## Summary

North Korean agents bombed a memorial ceremony in Rangoon targeting South Korean President Chun Doo-hwan. Chun survived but 21 people were killed including 4 Korean cabinet ministers.

## Key Facts

- **Date:** 9 October 1983
- **Location:** Martyrs' Mausoleum, Rangoon (now Yangon), Burma (now Myanmar)
- **Intended target:** South Korean President Chun Doo-hwan (arrived late and was not hit)
- **Method:** Bomb placed at/near the mausoleum during an official wreath-laying ceremony
- **Perpetrators (attributed):** North Korean agents (per Burmese investigation and later reporting) [^1]
- **Fatalities:** 21 killed (including senior South Korean officials) [^1]

## Background

In 1983, South Korea's president Chun Doo-hwan made an official visit to Burma. The stop included a state ceremony at Martyrs' Mausoleum, a site used for official commemorations. The visit occurred amid Cold War–era tensions on the Korean peninsula, with North Korea and South Korea competing for diplomatic recognition and influence abroad. [^1]

## The Attack (Overview)

- A bomb detonated at the mausoleum shortly before or as the South Korean delegation was assembling.
- Chun Doo-hwan survived because he was delayed and had not yet reached the blast area.
- The explosion killed members of the visiting party and Burmese bystanders/officials present at the ceremony. [^1]

## Casualties and Victims

The attack is remembered for killing senior South Korean officials, including cabinet-level figures, alongside others present at the ceremony. Contemporary summaries commonly cite **21 fatalities**. [^1]

## Investigation and Attribution

Burmese authorities investigated the incident and attributed the bombing to North Korean operatives. Accounts of the case describe arrests, identification of suspects, and subsequent legal proceedings within Burma. [^1]

## Diplomatic Aftermath

The bombing contributed to diplomatic fallout involving Burma (Myanmar) and North Korea, and it reinforced South Korea's security concerns regarding overseas travel and protective details for senior officials. The incident is frequently discussed as a major example of North Korean covert action targeting South Korean leadership abroad. [^1]

## Legacy

- Often referred to as the **1983 Rangoon bombing**.
- Used in historical narratives to illustrate the risks of political violence and state-linked covert operations during the Cold War in Asia.
- Remains a notable event in modern South Korean political history and in Myanmar's diplomatic history of the period. [^1]
