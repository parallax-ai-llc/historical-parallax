## Summary

Rwandan PM during the 1994 genocide. First head of government convicted of genocide by an international tribunal.

## Additional Context

## Background

- Jean Kambanda was trained as an economist and worked in the Rwandan banking sector before entering national politics.
- He became prime minister during the interim government formed after the assassination of President Juvénal Habyarimana.

## Role and Responsibility

- As head of government, Kambanda led cabinet meetings and signed official documents during the period of mass violence.
- International prosecutors argued that senior political leaders helped enable genocidal policies through authority, coordination, and public messaging.

## ICTR Proceedings

- Kambanda was prosecuted by the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR), established by the United Nations to address crimes committed in 1994.
- He pleaded guilty to genocide and crimes against humanity, a rare outcome for such cases at the tribunal.
- The ICTR emphasized that a guilty plea does not eliminate responsibility for leadership-level crimes, and can still result in severe sentencing.

## Significance

- The conviction is often cited as a precedent: heads of government can be held personally accountable under international criminal law.
- The case is also referenced in discussions about command responsibility, incitement, and the duties of public officials during mass atrocities.
