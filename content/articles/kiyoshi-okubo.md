## Summary

Japanese serial killer who abducted and murdered 8 young women in Gunma Prefecture using his car. Known as 'The Wolf' for prowling streets in search of victims.

## Background

Okubo was a Japanese offender whose crimes occurred in the early 1970s. Accounts typically describe him targeting young women and using a private vehicle to approach, abduct, and transport victims, reflecting a pattern that complicated immediate identification.

## Crimes and Modus Operandi

Reports of the case emphasize repeated attacks over multiple years in and around Gunma Prefecture. As with many serial crime investigations, the pattern was reconstructed retrospectively from victim reports, crime-scene analysis, and later confessions or admissions described in secondary sources.

## Investigation and Arrest

The investigation involved linking separate incidents that initially appeared unconnected. Public summaries of the case note that the eventual arrest depended on conventional police work, including tracing movements, examining vehicle-related leads, and compiling witness information.

## Trial and Sentencing

Okubo was tried and convicted for multiple murders. Japan’s criminal justice system allows for capital punishment in cases involving severe multiple homicide, and he was sentenced to death and later executed.

## Social Impact and Legacy

High-profile serial cases in Japan have periodically influenced public discussions about policing, urban safety, and the vulnerability of young people traveling alone. Okubo’s case is often cited in overviews of postwar Japanese criminal history as an example of a multi-victim predatory offender.

## Notes on Sources

Many widely circulated details come from press coverage and secondary summaries; specific counts, timelines, and victim circumstances can vary by retelling. For careful historical work, primary court documents and contemporary reporting are typically treated as the most reliable.
