## Summary

Leonardo Wilhelm DiCaprio is an American actor and film producer, widely regarded as one of the greatest actors of his generation. His name appeared in Epstein-related court documents, but in a context that suggests "name-dropping" rather than any substantive association. There are **no allegations** of wrongdoing, **no evidence** of meetings with Epstein, **no flight logs**, and **no witness testimony** placing them together. DiCaprio's inclusion exemplifies how famous names can appear in documents through casual mention, creating false impressions of connection where none exists.

## Media Coverage

### Responsible vs. Irresponsible

**Bad Headlines**:
- "Leonardo DiCaprio Named in Epstein Documents"
- Implies connection where none exists
- Clickbait over accuracy

**Good Headlines**:
- "DiCaprio Among Names Mentioned, No Evidence of Connection"
- "Documents Include Celebrity Names, Most Without Allegations"
- Provides context immediately

**Viral Lists**:
- "Epstein List" includes DiCaprio
- No context about type of mention
- Conspiracy theories flourish
- Impossible to fully correct

**Correction Problem**:
- Initial false impression spreads widely
- Corrections reach far fewer people
- Damage persists

## Lessons from DiCaprio's Case

### For Media Literacy

**Question Headlines**: Read beyond "Named in documents"
**Demand Evidence**: Mention ≠ connection ≠ guilt
**Understand Context**: Why was name mentioned?
**Resist Lists**: Don't trust compilations without verification

### For Journalism Ethics

**Protect Innocent**: Don't publish names without substance
**Provide Context**: Explain type of mention immediately
**Resist Clickbait**: Long-term credibility over short-term traffic
**Follow Evidence**: Names without evidence shouldn't be emphasized

### For Public Discourse

**Proportionality**: Treat similar cases similarly
**Fairness**: Don't assume worst without evidence
**Complexity**: Accept that not all mentions are equal
**Grace**: Allow for correction of false impressions
