## Summary

Mohamed Bouazizi's self-immolation sparked the Tunisian revolution that overthrew dictator Ben Ali. Security forces killed over 300 protesters during the uprising.

## Background

In the years leading up to 2010, Tunisia experienced high youth unemployment, regional economic disparities, corruption allegations, and limits on political expression under President Zine El Abidine Ben Ali.

## Key events (2010–2011)

- **Trigger:** Street vendor Mohamed Bouazizi set himself on fire in Sidi Bouzid after a confrontation with local authorities, becoming a symbol of economic and political grievance.
- **Protests:** Demonstrations spread nationally, with labor unions, lawyers, students, and ordinary citizens participating.
- **State response:** Authorities used policing and emergency measures; human-rights groups documented deaths and injuries among protesters.
- **Outcome:** Ben Ali left the country on 14 January 2011, ending a presidency that began in 1987.

## Aftermath and political transition

Following Ben Ali’s departure, Tunisia formed interim governments and held elections for a constituent assembly.
The process produced a new constitution (2014) and competitive elections, often cited as a comparatively successful political transition among the early Arab Spring uprisings.

## Regional significance

The Tunisian Revolution is widely regarded as the opening episode of the broader **Arab Spring**, influencing protest movements across North Africa and the Middle East.

## Ongoing challenges

Even after the initial transition, Tunisia faced continuing debates over economic reform, security, institutional trust, and the balance between executive power and democratic checks.

## Commonly discussed factors

- Rising costs of living and perceptions of unequal opportunity
- Corruption and patronage networks associated with the pre-2011 political order
- The role of digital media and satellite television in spreading information
- The importance of organized civil society, including labor and professional associations
