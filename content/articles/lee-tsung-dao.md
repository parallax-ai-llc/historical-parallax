## Summary

See earlier entry.

Lee Tsung-Dao (often cited as T. D. Lee) was a Chinese-American physicist best known for foundational work in particle physics, including the theoretical proposal—together with Chen-Ning Yang—that **parity symmetry is not conserved** in certain weak interactions.

## Scientific contributions (high level)

Lee’s most widely referenced contribution concerns **symmetry principles** in fundamental physics. Prior to the mid-20th century, parity (a left-right “mirror” symmetry) was often assumed to be a universal feature of physical laws. Lee and Yang argued that available experimental evidence did not actually establish parity conservation in the **weak force**.

Experiments soon confirmed parity violation, changing how physicists understood fundamental interactions and helping shape the modern view of the Standard Model.

## Legacy and why parity matters

At a high level, parity violation means that nature can distinguish between left and right in certain processes. This idea is significant because:

- It challenged an assumption that had guided theory-building for decades.
- It provided a clear, testable prediction that experiments could verify.
- It influenced later work on symmetries, conservation laws, and the structure of weak interactions.
