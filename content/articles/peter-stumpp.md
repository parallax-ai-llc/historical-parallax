## Summary

German farmer known as the 'Werewolf of Bedburg' convicted of serial murder, cannibalism, and incest.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1589 | Executed by breaking wheel for serial murder |

## Background and Historical Context

- Stumpp (also spelled **Stumpf** or **Stübbe**) was associated with the town of **Bedburg** in the Electorate of Cologne (in the Holy Roman Empire).
- The case unfolded during a period of **religious conflict** and war in the Rhineland, when fear of witchcraft, demonic pacts, and “monstrous crime” was widespread in print culture.
- Many details about Stumpp’s life come from **pamphlet literature** and later retellings rather than modern-style court records.

## Allegations and Trial (As Reported)

Contemporary accounts portray Stumpp as being accused of a long series of crimes, including:

- Multiple murders over a number of years (often framed as attacks on travelers and local residents).
- **Cannibalism**, including descriptions of consuming victims’ flesh.
- **Incest**, notably allegations involving a daughter.
- A supposed **pact with the Devil** and the use of a “magic belt” enabling transformation into a werewolf.

These claims should be read as **reported allegations** from the era’s sources; the evidentiary basis is difficult to reconstruct.

## Execution and Punishment

Descriptions of the execution emphasize exemplary, public punishment:

- Breaking on the wheel (a severe method intended as deterrence).
- Additional mutilation and post-execution display are commonly mentioned in summaries of the case.

## Historiography and Reliability

- The Stumpp story is frequently cited in discussions of **werewolf trials** alongside early-modern witch persecutions.
- Scholars and historians often treat the pamphlet narrative cautiously, noting that:
  - Confession-based cases could be shaped by **torture** and leading interrogation.
  - Sensational elements (demonology, transformation) fit common **moral-panic templates** of the period.
  - The surviving accounts may have been influenced by **political and confessional** tensions.

## Cultural Legacy

- Stumpp is remembered as the “**Werewolf of Bedburg**,” becoming a recurring example in:
  - Popular histories of the supernatural
  - True-crime anthologies
  - Discussions of how early-modern Europe narrated deviance and violence
