## Summary

Galina Sergeyevna Ulanova (1910–1998) was the foremost Soviet ballerina of the twentieth century, widely regarded as one of the greatest classical dancers in the history of ballet. A product of the Leningrad school, she defined the dramatic and lyrical style of Soviet ballet for three decades, becoming the standard against which all subsequent interpreters of Giselle and Juliet were measured.

Her importance extended beyond technical mastery. Ulanova embodied the ideal of the dancer as actor, bringing psychological depth and emotional authenticity to roles at a time when Soviet cultural policy demanded art that spoke to human feeling. She was awarded the Stalin Prize four times and was twice named Hero of Socialist Labour, making her one of the most decorated artists in Soviet history.

After retiring from the stage in 1962, Ulanova spent the following three decades as a rehearsal coach at the Bolshoi Ballet, shaping generations of dancers including Ekaterina Maximova and Vladimir Vasiliev. Her influence on Russian ballet pedagogy remains active long after her death.

---

## Early Life & Background

Ulanova was born on 8 January 1910 in St. Petersburg into a theatrical family. Both parents worked at the Mariinsky Theatre — her father Sergei as a dancer and later stage director, her mother Maria Romanova as a dancer and teacher. She entered the Petrograd Ballet School in 1919, studying first under her mother and then under Agrippina Vaganova, whose rigorous analytical method would shape her technique permanently.

She graduated in 1928 and joined the corps de ballet of the State Academic Theatre of Opera and Ballet (GATOB, later the Kirov). The transition from student to professional was swift: within her first season she was dancing principal roles.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Ulanova's ascent was rapid. By the early 1930s she was the leading ballerina of the Kirov, acclaimed for her performances in *Giselle*, *Swan Lake*, and *The Sleeping Beauty*. In 1940 she created the role of Juliet in Sergei Prokofiev's *Romeo and Juliet* — a production that became inseparable from her interpretation and remains her most celebrated achievement.

In 1944, during wartime, she was transferred to the Bolshoi Ballet in Moscow, a move reflecting her status as a national cultural asset. Her Bolshoi debut consolidated her reputation across the Soviet Union. In 1956 she toured London with the Bolshoi — the company's first visit to the West — and her performances produced an extraordinary critical response, with observers describing her Juliet as a revelation of what ballet could express dramatically.

---

## Key Achievements

- Created the role of Juliet in Prokofiev's *Romeo and Juliet* (1940), a performance that became definitive.
- Won the Stalin Prize in 1941, 1946, 1947, and 1950.
- Named People's Artist of the USSR in 1951.
- Twice awarded Hero of Socialist Labour (1974, 1980).
- Her 1956 London performances are credited with transforming Western perception of Soviet ballet.

---

## Controversies & Criticism

Ulanova's career unfolded entirely within the Soviet system, and she received state honours that were inseparable from political patronage. Some Western observers noted that her transfer from Leningrad to Moscow in 1944 was effectively a state directive rather than a personal choice, raising questions about artistic autonomy under Soviet cultural policy. Within the Soviet ballet world, her prolonged dominance at the Kirov and Bolshoi meant that younger ballerinas had limited access to major roles for years.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Ulanova's legacy operates on two levels. As a performer, she established a template for lyrical, dramatically grounded ballet that shaped Soviet and international practice for half a century. As a teacher and coach at the Bolshoi from 1963 to her death in 1998, she transmitted her approach directly to dancers who carried it into the twenty-first century. A statue by sculptor Igor Mitoraj stands outside the Bolshoi Theatre. She died in Moscow on 21 March 1998 at the age of 88.
