## Summary

German Nazi foreign minister who negotiated the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact with the Soviet Union. He was the first person executed at Nuremberg. His prosecution helped establish the principle that aggressive war is a crime.

## Early Life and Entry into Politics

Ribbentrop worked in business before becoming involved in right-wing politics in interwar Germany. He joined the Nazi Party and leveraged social connections and personal access to Adolf Hitler to build influence in foreign-policy circles.[^1]

## Diplomatic Role in Nazi Germany

Before becoming foreign minister, Ribbentrop was used by Hitler as a diplomatic emissary and was involved in efforts to reshape Germany’s international position. As foreign minister (from 1938), he was part of the leadership that pursued territorial expansion and helped dismantle constraints imposed after World War I.[^1]

A central episode of his tenure was the 1939 non-aggression pact with the Soviet Union (the Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact), which included secret protocols dividing spheres of influence in Eastern Europe. The agreement facilitated the German invasion of Poland and the opening phase of World War II in Europe.[^1]

## Role During World War II

As the war expanded, the German Foreign Office participated in the administration of occupied territories and in diplomatic coordination with allied and client states. Histories of the period also associate the Nazi state’s diplomatic apparatus with policies that enabled persecution and deportation, even when the primary operational control lay with other Nazi organizations.[^1]

## Nuremberg Trial

After Germany’s defeat, Ribbentrop was tried by the International Military Tribunal at Nuremberg. He was convicted on major counts, including crimes against peace (planning and waging aggressive war), reflecting the tribunal’s view that leadership-level decision-making could constitute a punishable international crime.[^1]

He was sentenced to death and executed by hanging on 16 October 1946; he was the first of those executed that night.[^1]

## Historical Significance

Ribbentrop’s case is frequently cited in discussions of how international criminal law developed after World War II, especially the concept that initiating aggressive war is itself a crime, not merely an outcome judged by victors.[^1]
