## Summary

David Bohm was a theoretical physicist who proposed the Bohm interpretation of quantum mechanics (pilot wave theory), an alternative to the Copenhagen interpretation. He made contributions to plasma physics and the holonomic model of brain function.

## Key Scientific Contributions (High Level)

- **Bohmian mechanics / pilot-wave theory (1952):** a deterministic hidden-variable framework where particles have definite positions guided by a wave function.
- **Plasma physics:** early influential work on collective behavior in plasmas and instabilities (Bohm diffusion is often mentioned in this context).
- **Foundations of quantum theory:** persistent critique of orthodox interpretations and emphasis on conceptual clarity.

## Philosophical Ideas

- **Implicate and explicate order:** Bohm’s later writings argued that observed phenomena (explicate order) could be understood as unfoldings from a deeper, enfolded structure (implicate order).
- **Holomovement:** a term he used for an underlying, dynamic “whole” that gives rise to patterns and objects.

## Life and Career Notes

- Bohm’s career was shaped by Cold War politics; after legal and professional pressures in the United States, he worked abroad for extended periods.
- He held positions and collaborations in several countries, including periods associated with Brazil and the United Kingdom.

## Public Influence

- Beyond physics, Bohm became known for work on **dialogue** and communication, and for conversations with thinkers such as J. Krishnamurti.
- His books and lectures influenced interdisciplinary discussions spanning physics, philosophy, and cognitive science.
