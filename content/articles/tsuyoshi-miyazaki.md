## Summary

Murdered four young girls in Tokyo and Saitama prefectures, sending body parts and postcards to victims' families. His massive collection of horror manga led media to demonize otaku culture.

## Background

- Miyazaki was a Japanese criminal whose case became one of the most notorious in late-1980s Japan.
- Reporting often linked the investigation and trial to wider debates about youth culture, media consumption, and social isolation.

## Crimes and investigation (high-level)

- He was convicted of the kidnapping and murder of multiple child victims in the Tokyo/Saitama area in 1988.
- The case attracted sustained national attention because of the brutality of the crimes and the fear it generated among families.
- Police investigation and evidence collection led to his arrest and prosecution.

## Trial and sentence

- The case proceeded through Japan’s criminal-justice system and resulted in a death sentence.
- He was executed in 2008.

## Cultural and media impact

- The discovery of his large collection of manga and videotapes was heavily emphasized by some media coverage.
- Critics later argued that this framing unfairly stigmatized “otaku” culture by implying a causal link between fandom and violent crime.
- The case is therefore frequently cited in discussions of moral panic, sensational reporting, and how societies explain rare but extreme violence.
- It also prompted discussions about child safety, policing, and victim support in Japan.
