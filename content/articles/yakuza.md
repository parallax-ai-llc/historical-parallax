## Summary

Japanese organized crime syndicates that have existed since the 17th century. At their peak they had 184,000 members across multiple syndicates controlling gambling, drugs, and construction.

## Origins and Terminology

“Yakuza” is a broad label for Japanese organized crime groups. Historical accounts commonly trace roots to early-modern social formations such as **tekiya** (peddlers) and **bakuto** (gamblers), with later consolidation into large syndicates.

## Organization

Yakuza groups are often described as hierarchical organizations with a strong emphasis on loyalty and patron-client relationships (frequently compared to a family structure). Many groups have identifiable names, leadership structures, and regional spheres of influence.

## Activities Commonly Associated

While specific operations vary by era and by group, discussions of yakuza activity often include:

- Gambling and gambling-related protection rackets
- Extortion and coercive “business mediation”
- Fraud and financial crime
- Involvement in illicit drug distribution (not uniform across time and group)
- Control of certain gray-market industries and subcontracting networks

## Cultural Markers and Public Image

Popular portrayals often emphasize features such as elaborate **irezumi** (traditional tattoos) and ritualized practices like **yubitsume** (finger shortening). These elements exist in historical reporting and memoir literature, but they are also amplified by film, television, and games.

## Law Enforcement and Regulation

Japan has adopted legal and administrative measures aimed at limiting yakuza influence (often referred to as anti-yakuza or anti-boryokudan frameworks). A key theme in modern coverage is the long-term decline in membership and visibility, paired with concerns that tighter restrictions can push remaining activity into less observable forms.
