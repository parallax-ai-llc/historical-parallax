## Summary

North Korean state-sponsored hacking group Lazarus stole over $1.7 billion in cryptocurrency in 2022 alone. The stolen funds finance North Korea's nuclear weapons program.

## Context

“Lazarus Group” is a label commonly used by governments and private cybersecurity firms for a set of North Korea–linked hacking operations. In the 2010s and 2020s, the name has been applied to multiple campaigns, including financially motivated intrusions and high-profile cryptocurrency thefts.

Attribution in cyber incidents is probabilistic: public claims typically rely on technical indicators, infrastructure overlaps, malware similarities, and investigative reporting. Different organizations may attribute the same incident differently or with varying confidence.

## Why cryptocurrency became a target

Cryptocurrency ecosystems can present attractive targets for state-linked and criminal actors because:

- Large values may be held in hot wallets or smart-contract bridges
- Transactions can be fast and cross-border by design
- Recovery and chargeback mechanisms are limited compared with traditional banking
- Laundering tools (e.g., swaps, mixers, chain-hopping) can complicate tracing

## Commonly reported techniques (high level)

Public analyses of Lazarus-attributed heists frequently mention combinations of:

- Social engineering (including recruiting lures and malicious attachments)
- Supply-chain compromise or credential theft
- Exploitation of smart-contract or bridge vulnerabilities
- Post-theft laundering through multiple wallets, services, and asset conversions

## Policy and enforcement responses

Responses described in public sources include:

- Sanctions and designations targeting entities and infrastructure linked to North Korea
- Seizures or freezing efforts when funds transit compliant exchanges
- Industry-led incident response, audits, and enhanced bridge security practices

## Disputed or hard-to-verify claims

Many summaries of Lazarus crypto thefts connect proceeds to broader North Korean state financing priorities. While such links are widely discussed by policymakers and researchers, the details can be difficult to prove publicly because they often combine intelligence assessments with open-source investigation.
