## Summary

American financier who stole $208 million from insurance companies and fled to Germany with diamonds sewn into his clothes. Created a fake Vatican charity to acquire insurance firms.

## Background

Martin Frankel was a U.S. financier associated with a major insurance-industry fraud case in the 1990s. The scandal is often cited as an example of how complex ownership structures and uneven oversight can be exploited in regulated financial sectors.

## Scheme (High-Level)

Public accounts describe a pattern in which Frankel:

- used front entities to acquire or control insurance companies,
- obtained access to investment portfolios and cash-like assets held for policyholders,
- diverted or removed funds through transactions that were difficult for regulators to track quickly.

A notable aspect of the case was the reported use of a **fake charity presented as having Vatican connections**, which helped create legitimacy when acquiring insurance firms.

## Why Insurance Companies Were Targeted

Insurance companies can hold substantial investable assets tied to long-duration liabilities. This can create an opportunity for misconduct if governance and custody controls are weak, especially when oversight is fragmented across jurisdictions.

## Flight and Capture

Frankel fled the United States as the scheme unraveled. Media summaries often highlight the unusual detail that he traveled with valuables (including diamonds) concealed on his person.

He was later detained abroad and extradited back to the United States.

## Legal Outcome

Frankel ultimately faced federal prosecution. In broad reporting, the case is associated with the theft of **hundreds of millions of dollars** from insurers and with a long prison sentence.

## Legacy

The case is referenced in discussions about:

- strengthening insurance holding-company oversight,
- improving cross-state regulatory coordination,
- the importance of custody controls and independent audits.
