Leaked Credit Suisse data revealed the bank held accounts for human rights abusers, sanctions evaders, and corrupt politicians. Over $100 billion linked to problematic clients.

## Background and Scope

Suisse Secrets refers to a large leak of information about Credit Suisse accounts, reported in February 2022. Reporting described the leak as covering more than CHF 100 billion held in accounts connected to tens of thousands of clients, with account history spanning decades (often summarized as roughly the 1940s through 2010).

The investigation was coordinated across dozens of media outlets internationally, after data was received anonymously and analyzed collaboratively.

## Key Themes Reported

- **Client risk and due diligence**: reporting highlighted accounts linked (allegedly or by official status) to politically exposed persons and individuals accused or convicted of serious crimes.
- **Swiss banking secrecy tensions**: coverage noted that strict Swiss bank-secrecy rules constrained Swiss domestic media participation/publication, prompting debate about press freedom versus secrecy law.
- **Legacy compliance questions**: many of the accounts discussed were historical, raising questions about when banks are expected to detect and remediate legacy risk.

## Impact and Aftermath (High Level)

Public reaction focused on whether financial institutions had adequately applied anti-money-laundering controls and enhanced due diligence for high-risk clients. The reporting also contributed to broader scrutiny of Switzerland’s role in cross-border wealth management and the effectiveness of secrecy and reporting regimes.

## Notable Reporting Partners (as described in coverage)

International reporting referenced participation by outlets and networks such as Süddeutsche Zeitung and the Organized Crime and Corruption Reporting Project (OCCRP), alongside major newspapers including The Guardian, The New York Times, and Le Monde.

## What the Leak Was (and Was Not)

The reporting centered on account-holder and account metadata rather than a complete, audited ledger of all Credit Suisse clients. As with many leaks, downstream interpretation depends on journalistic verification, public records, and on-the-record responses.
