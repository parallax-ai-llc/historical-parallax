## Summary

Delhi Sultanate ruler who murdered his uncle to seize power and launched devastating invasions of southern India. He ordered the massacre of 30,000 Mongol prisoners and implemented draconian economic controls.

## Governance, Coercion, and “Criminal” Framing

Alauddin Khalji is often presented in popular retellings as a ruthless usurper and a highly coercive ruler. The “criminal” label in many modern write-ups reflects **acts of political murder, mass violence, and forced extraction** associated with state power, rather than a modern criminal-justice context.[^1]

Key themes that appear across standard summaries:

- **Usurpation and consolidation:** seizure of power through assassination and rapid neutralization of rivals.
- **Military expansion:** campaigns in North India and incursions into the Deccan aimed at tribute and control.
- **Internal security:** emphasis on surveillance, harsh punishments, and deterrence.

## Economic Controls and State Capacity

One of the most-cited aspects of Alauddin’s reign is the attempt to impose strict market regulation and price controls (especially in Delhi) to support the army and stabilize provisioning.[^1] High-level elements commonly mentioned include:

- regulation of grain and essential goods markets
- controls on prices, hoarding, and profiteering
- administrative enforcement through inspectors and penalties

## Legacy and Controversies

Assessments of Alauddin Khalji tend to split between:

- **state-builder arguments** (expansion, administrative reach, fiscal control)
- **atrocity-centered arguments** (massacres, repression, violent seizure of power)

Because the evidentiary base is medieval chronicle tradition and later historiography, details are often debated; careful treatment distinguishes what is consistently attested from what is interpretive or dramatized in modern media.
