## Summary

Francisco "Pancho" Villa (born José Doroteo Arango Arámbula) was one of the most prominent and controversial figures of the Mexican Revolution. Born on June 5, 1878, and assassinated on July 20, 1923, Villa transformed from a bandit and outlaw into one of Mexico's most powerful revolutionary generals, commanding the legendary División del Norte. He was instrumental in toppling two regimes—Porfirio Díaz in 1911 and Victoriano Huerta in 1914—and briefly served as governor of Chihuahua, where he implemented significant land reforms and social programs. His legacy remains deeply contested: to some, he was a champion of the poor and a Mexican Robin Hood; to others, a brutal warlord whose forces committed numerous atrocities, including the infamous raid on Columbus, New Mexico, in 1916.[^1][^2][^3]

---

## Early Life and Background

José Doroteo Arango Arámbula was born on June 5, 1878, at Hacienda de Río Grande, San Juan del Río, Durango, Mexico, into a family of poor sharecroppers. His father, Agustín Arango, worked the land as a tenant farmer, while his mother, Micaela Arámbula, managed the household. As the oldest of five children, young Doroteo shouldered significant family responsibilities from an early age.[^4][^5]

The family lived at the Rancho de la Coyotada, one of the largest haciendas in Durango state, where the stark inequalities of the Porfirian system were evident daily. Young Doroteo received limited education from a local church-run school, achieving only basic literacy—a fact that would later shape his passionate commitment to educational reform.[^6] His childhood ended abruptly when his father died, leaving the twelve-year-old boy as head of the family. He immediately quit school to help support his mother and siblings, working as a sharecropper, muleskinner (arriero), butcher, bricklayer, and foreman.[^7]

According to his own dictated remembrances, the pivotal moment that transformed Doroteo Arango into an outlaw occurred when he was sixteen years old. After moving briefly to Chihuahua, he returned to Durango to track down and kill a hacienda owner named Agustín López Negrete, who had allegedly raped his sister. Following this act of vengeance, he stole a horse and fled to the Sierra Madre Occidental region of Durango, where he joined a bandit band and began using the name "Arango."[^8][^9]

For years, Villa roamed the hills as a thief and cattle rustler, developing the survival skills, knowledge of terrain, and understanding of guerrilla tactics that would later make him a formidable military leader. In 1903, after killing an army officer and stealing his horse, he adopted a new identity entirely, taking the name Francisco "Pancho" Villa after his paternal grandfather, Jesús Villa. This name change marked his complete break from his past life and the beginning of his transformation into the legendary figure known throughout Mexico.[^10][^11]

## Rise to Power

Villa's transition from bandit to revolutionary began in 1910 when he joined Francisco I. Madero's movement to overthrow the long-standing dictatorship of Porfirio Díaz. Madero's call for "effective suffrage and no re-election" resonated with Villa, who saw an opportunity to fight against the oppressive hacienda system that had defined his youth. Villa's intimate knowledge of northern Mexico's terrain, his natural leadership abilities, and his skill in guerrilla warfare made him an invaluable asset to the revolutionary cause.[^12][^13]

At the end of 1910, Villa led his troops in capturing the small town of San Andrés in Chihuahua. This victory, the first of many, established Villa as one of the most daring and competent military leaders of the revolution. His reputation grew rapidly as he demonstrated an uncanny ability to recruit men, inspire loyalty, and achieve seemingly impossible victories.[^14] In 1911, Villa and Pascual Orozco attacked Ciudad Juárez, capturing the city after two days of fierce fighting in what became known as the first Battle of Ciudad Juárez. This victory was crucial in forcing Díaz to resign and flee Mexico, bringing Madero to power.[^15]

However, Villa's relationship with the new government quickly soured. When Pascual Orozco rebelled against Madero in 1912, Villa remained loyal to the president, but his success on the battlefield provoked jealousy from General Victoriano Huerta, who ordered Villa's execution without trial. Only Madero's personal intervention saved Villa's life, commuting his sentence to imprisonment. While in prison, Villa learned to read and write more proficiently, an experience that deepened his commitment to education as a tool for social advancement.[^16]

When Madero was overthrown and murdered in a coup led by General Victoriano Huerta in February 1913, Villa escaped from prison and fled to the United States. He soon returned to Mexico to organize resistance against Huerta's regime, declaring, "My sole ambition is to rid Mexico of the class that has oppressed her and given the people a chance to know what real liberty means."[^17] Villa joined the Constitutionalist Army led by Venustiano Carranza, quickly building a formidable military force that would become legendary.

Between 1913 and 1914, Villa created and commanded the División del Norte (Northern Division), which initially formed with 3,000 men but grew to 30,000 combatants, becoming the most powerful and feared military unit in all of Mexico. During this period, Villa won a rapid succession of victories at Ciudad Juárez, Tierra Blanca, Chihuahua, and Ojinaga, establishing himself as the most successful revolutionary general in Mexico. The Battle of Torreón in spring 1914 and the Battle of Zacatecas—known as the largest and bloodiest battle of the revolution against Huerta—cemented Villa's reputation as an unstoppable military force.[^18][^19][^20]

## Major Achievements

Pancho Villa's achievements as a revolutionary leader and military strategist were remarkable, fundamentally reshaping Mexico's political landscape and demonstrating innovative approaches to warfare that would influence later revolutionary movements worldwide.

### Military Innovations and Victories

Villa was the first revolutionary leader to defeat regular government soldiers, breaking the myth of the federal army's invincibility. His military genius lay in combining traditional cavalry tactics with modern innovations. Villa's generalship was noted for the unprecedented speed of troop movements by railroad, using the infrastructure built during the Díaz administration to move quickly from one engagement to another.[^21] This mobility allowed him to strike where enemies least expected and to concentrate forces rapidly for decisive engagements.

The División del Norte was not merely a collection of guerrilla fighters but a total army rather than a regular division, with troops assigned military ranks, equipped with hospital trains and horse ambulances, and well supplied with machine guns and artillery units captured from the Mexican Federal Army.[^22] Villa created an elite cavalry unit called Los Dorados ("the golden ones"), and he attempted to supply a horse to each infantryman to increase the speed of movement of his entire army, not just his cavalry detachments.[^23]

Villa employed sophisticated military tactics including hit-and-run strategies, multi-front coordination, and continuous assault methods. He would surround cities, cut off supply routes, and prevent reinforcements from reaching federal troops. At Zacatecas, he sent 16,000 revolutionary soldiers who attacked night and day, with federal troops becoming weary and fearing the night attacks, leading to victory after ten days of engagement.[^24] Villa relied on his cavalry's boldness and fury to put enemies to flight, and his División del Norte was considered invincible—men joined because Villa always attacked and always won.[^25]

### Governance and Social Reform

Villa's achievements extended beyond the battlefield. At the end of 1913, his military successes earned him the position of provisional governor of Chihuahua, serving from December 1913 to January 1914.[^26] Despite his brief tenure, Villa implemented transformative policies that demonstrated his commitment to social justice and revealed sophisticated understanding of economic and social reform.

In control of Chihuahua from late 1913 through 1915, Villa expropriated the estates of the landed oligarchy and used the revenues they produced to finance his army and government. His rule represented an ingenious compromise between satisfying revolutionary demands for land reform and obtaining immediate funds to win the war. He promised to distribute confiscated properties after the revolution's triumph, and in the meantime, these estates—some managed by his generals, others by a state agency—supported widows and orphans of veterans and the starving unemployed of the mining and timber regions of Chihuahua.[^27][^28]

Villa's social programs were revolutionary for their time. He ordered the construction of approximately 100 schools for poor children, believing education would empower them to break the cycle of poverty he had experienced. He lowered food prices, especially for meat, bread, and milk, putting large quantities of beef on the local market at drastically reduced prices. Orphanages and children's charities received generous amounts of food.[^29][^30] In 1913, while acting as provisional governor, he created the Bank of Chihuahua that financed agrarian reform and urban cooperatives, demonstrating sophisticated understanding of economic development.[^31]

As Villa's coffers overflowed from the confiscated hacienda revenues, he redistributed funds to causes he deemed worthy, allocating money to family members of citizens who had fallen in revolutionary fighting. This Robin Hood-like redistribution of wealth, combined with his humble origins and personal charisma, made him a hero to Mexico's rural dispossessed and impoverished working class, as well as to Mexican nationalists.[^32][^33]

Villa's military achievements were instrumental in toppling two regimes: Díaz in 1911 and Huerta in 1914. The period from 1913 to 1914 marked the time of Villa's greatest international fame and military and political success, with his exploits broadening into legends that remain an integral part of Mexican history and folklore.[^34][^35]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his status as a folk hero to many Mexicans, Pancho Villa's career was marked by brutal violence, atrocities, and actions that have led many historians to view him as a war criminal whose methods undermined the very revolutionary ideals he claimed to champion.

### Documented Atrocities and War Crimes

Based on accounts collected during the revolution, historians have documented darker events in Villa's career, including the mass execution of men in villages in northern Sonora, as well as numerous accounts of rapes, murders, and the burning of villages.[^36] In Namiquipa, Villa sought to punish civilians who had formed a home guard, but when the village men learned of Villa's approach and fled to the hills, Villa rounded up their wives and allowed his soldiers to rape them.[^37] This pattern of sexual violence was not isolated; when Villa would attack small towns with his forces, any civilians who opposed his attack were slaughtered, and the systematic rape of women was sanctioned by Villa himself. Word of these atrocities spread throughout Mexico and the United States, souring the name of "Pancho Villa" especially among the American population.[^38]

Villa's hatred for certain ethnic groups manifested in targeted violence. During the fight against Huerta, Villa first manifested his intense hatred for Spaniards, and he would later commit additional atrocities against them, viewing all Spaniards in Mexico as representatives of the colonial oppression that had impoverished his people.[^39] Critics argue that his actions undermined revolutionary ideals of social justice and democracy, with civilian casualties resulting from his raids and retaliatory strikes against perceived enemies painting a picture of a leader who sometimes prioritized military success over moral considerations.[^40]

### The Columbus Raid and International Infamy

Villa's most infamous act occurred on March 9, 1916, when he led approximately 485 troops across the U.S.-Mexican border in a raid on Columbus, New Mexico. The attack began at approximately 4:20 a.m., catching the entire town and the army camp by surprise. Villa's men looted and burned several houses and commercial buildings, fighting civilians who defended their homes. American casualties included 10 civilians and 8 soldiers killed, with 2 civilians and 6 military personnel wounded. Between 70 and 75 of Villa's raiders were killed in the fighting.[^41][^42][^43]

Prior to the Columbus raid, Villa's forces committed another atrocity at San Ysabel. On January 10, 1916, Villistas stopped a train at San Ysabel, removed eighteen American miners and engineers who had been assured safe passage by the Carranza government, lined them up, stripped off their clothes, and executed them.[^44] Additionally, Villistas had captured three Americans from the Palomas Land & Cattle Company; two of the captives were hanged and the third man was shot when efforts to trample him with horses failed.[^45]

The reasons for the Columbus raid have never been established with certainty, though several theories exist. One suggests personal revenge: Villa may have been involved in a shady business deal where Sam Ravel sold ammunition to both Villa's buyers and Carranza's forces, but the cartridges delivered to Villa were deliberately underloaded.[^46] Another theory focuses on anti-American resentment, as Mexican troop movements by American rail stirred Villa's anger and were believed to have been a major cause of the attack.[^47]

The Columbus raid prompted President Woodrow Wilson to order the Punitive Expedition led by Brigadier General John J. Pershing. On March 15, 1916, the expedition crossed the border into Mexico with 6,600 men to search for Villa. The Army scoured portions of northern Mexico for six months using Curtiss Jenny airplanes for reconnaissance and trucks to carry supplies, but Villa was never captured. In January 1917, with the United States likely to enter World War I soon and under intense diplomatic pressure from the Mexican government, these troops were withdrawn from Mexico.[^48][^49][^50]

### Historical Criticism and Reassessment

Villa's legacy has been contested by historians and political factions. To some, he was a violent opportunist who exploited the revolution for his own advancement.[^51] Because he ultimately lost to Carranza in the revolutionary power struggle, Villa's importance has been downgraded by some American historians and PRIista apologists, who prefer to consign "the Centaur of the North" to a secondary role in the revolution's pantheon of heroes, portraying him as a destroyer, a common vaquero and bandit who capriciously took advantage of chaotic times by ruthlessly catapulting himself into a position of power.[^52]

Critics point to Villa's brutality and atrocities as evidence that he inspired fear and hatred rather than genuine social progress. The systematic violence against civilians, the sanctioned sexual violence, and the execution of prisoners of war all suggest a leader whose moral compass was severely compromised. Many historians now argue that while Villa may have articulated revolutionary ideals, his actions frequently betrayed those principles, making him a complex and troubling figure whose legacy must be examined critically rather than romanticized.[^53]

## Personal Life

Pancho Villa's personal life was as unconventional and controversial as his military and political career, characterized by polygamy, charismatic personality, and behavior that ranged from generous kindness to shocking brutality.

### Marriages and Family

During his lifetime, Villa never bothered with conventional arrangements in his family life, contracting several marriages without seeking annulment or divorce.[^54] On May 29, 1911, Villa married María Luz Corral, who has been described as "the most articulate of his many wives."[^55] She was recognized by Mexican courts as Villa's legal wife and therefore heir to Villa's estate, providing some legal clarity to an otherwise complicated domestic situation.

The exact number of Villa's wives remains hotly disputed among historians. Mainstream historians usually agree to a conservative estimate of 23 unions, most informal, while sensationalized accounts inflate the number to around 75.[^56] Villa had at least 20 children by as many as seven wives, creating a complex network of families across northern Mexico.[^57]

Among his documented wives, Austreberta Rentería was considered his "official wife" at his Canutillo ranch, and they had two sons together, Francisco and Hipólito.[^58] He also had relationships with Soledad Seañez and Manuela Casas, with whom he had a son, and Juana Torres, whom he married in 1913 and had a daughter with.[^59] Remarkably, Villa shared his final home, the hacienda in Canutillo, Durango, with three of his wives and their respective children, a domestic arrangement that defied social conventions but which Villa maintained without apology.[^60]

Villa did nothing to hide his polygamy, viewing it as his right and prerogative as a powerful man. This openness about his unconventional family arrangements was characteristic of his personality—he refused to be bound by social conventions that he viewed as imposed by the upper classes he fought against.[^61]

### Personality and Character

Villa's charismatic personality captivated men and women alike, drawing people to his cause through force of will and personal magnetism.[^62] Despite his reputation as a fierce warrior, Villa had surprising personal habits. He never drank liquor, which set him apart from many of his contemporaries in the revolutionary movement. Instead, a man with an insatiable sweet tooth, Villa gulped strawberry soda like a child, revealing a softer, almost innocent side to his personality.[^63]

Those who knew Villa described him as capable of extreme kindness and extreme cruelty, sometimes within the same day. He could order brutal executions in the morning and then personally ensure that orphans received food in the afternoon. This duality made him unpredictable and complex—a man whose emotions and decisions seemed to operate on impulse rather than consistent principle.

Villa's lack of formal education remained a source of both pride and insecurity throughout his life. He famously said, "I am not an educated man. I never had an opportunity to learn anything except how to fight."[^64] This self-awareness drove his passionate commitment to building schools and providing educational opportunities for poor children, as he sought to ensure that future generations would not suffer the same limitations he had endured.

His relationship with his soldiers was personal and paternal. He knew many of his men by name, inquired about their families, and ensured that the widows and orphans of fallen soldiers received support. This personal touch, combined with his willingness to share hardships with his troops and his reputation for military success, generated fierce loyalty among his followers. Men followed Villa not just because of revolutionary ideology, but because of personal devotion to a leader who they believed genuinely cared about their welfare.[^65]

## Legacy

Pancho Villa's legacy is one of the most contested and complex in Mexican history, with his image continuously reinterpreted by successive generations for different political, cultural, and social purposes.

### Cultural Impact and Folklore

Villa's exploits on and off the battlefield have broadened into legends that remain an integral part of Mexican history and folklore, especially as a champion of the poor and landless masses.[^66] The image of Villa as a Robin Hood-like figure has been deeply embedded in popular culture, symbolizing resistance against oppression and the fight for justice, characterized by tales of his generosity toward the poor and his fierce opposition to corrupt leaders.[^67]

The corridos—traditional Mexican ballads—often recount his deeds and adventures, blending historical facts with artistic embellishments that enhance the narrative of his heroism. These songs have kept Villa's memory alive in Mexican popular consciousness, transmitting his legend from generation to generation through oral tradition and musical performance.[^68] Villa has dominated Mexican folklore as a champion of the poor and landless masses, with numerous myths and legends, popularized in songs and romanticized tales, recounting Villa's feats.[^69]

Cultural mediums such as folk songs, comic books, cinema, and ephemeral objects—keychains, t-shirts, belt buckles, action figures, and board games—have visualized Villa, transforming him from the historical man into a figure now shaped by popular culture.[^70] This transformation demonstrates how Villa has become a malleable symbol that can be adapted to different narratives and purposes, from revolutionary inspiration to commercial commodity.

### Official Recognition and Controversy

The Mexican government's relationship with Villa's legacy has evolved significantly over time. In 2023, the government declared the year to be the "Year of Francisco Villa" to honor Villa's legacy in the Mexican Revolution, demonstrating official recognition of his importance to Mexican history.[^71] For many Mexicans, Villa heroically redistributed wealth from rich to poor with no desire to enrich himself, embodying revolutionary ideals of social justice and economic equality.[^72]

However, this official commemoration sparked debate, as some historians pointed to darker events in Villa's career, including the mass execution of men in villages in northern Sonora, as well as accounts of rapes, murders, and burning villages. The controversy surrounding the commemoration reflects the broader tension in Mexican society about how to remember revolutionary figures whose actions were morally complex and often contradictory.[^73]

### Historical Reassessment

Historical opinion of Villa grew more positive in Mexico by the late 20th century, with scholars like Friedrich Katz producing comprehensive biographies that separated the man from the myth, acknowledging his flaws while also highlighting his achievements and the social forces he represented.[^74] Katz's work, described as "a masterpiece of contemporary historiography" and "the definitive work on Mexican revolutionary Pancho Villa," provided exhaustively researched and scrupulously documented analysis that moved beyond simple hero-worship or condemnation.[^75]

The enduring nature of Villa's legacy reflects the complex relationship between history and cultural identity in Mexico, where he continues to be remembered as both a revolutionary hero and a controversial figure.[^76] Villa influenced the course of Mexican history and has endured as a hero to his countrymen, despite conflicting interpretations of his motives and actions.[^77]

Villa appeared as himself in several films including Life of Villa (1912), Barbarous Mexico (1913), With General Pancho Villa in Mexico (1913), The Life of General Villa (1914), and Following the Flag in Mexico (1916).[^78] In January 1914, Villa made a deal with Mutual Film Corporation to receive $25,000 and 20% of box office receipts, demonstrating his understanding of modern media and publicity.[^79] The 1934 biopic Viva Villa! was nominated for an Academy Award for Best Picture, while in 2003, HBO broadcast And Starring Pancho Villa as Himself, with Antonio Banderas as Villa.[^80][^81]

Pancho Villa remains a controversial figure in the United States, with USA Today reporting in 2011, "A terrorist in 1916, a tourist attraction in 2011," capturing the dramatic shift in how Villa is perceived north of the border.[^82] This transformation from villain to curiosity reflects broader changes in U.S.-Mexico relations and American understanding of the Mexican Revolution's complexity.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Many historians and scholars have recognized Pancho Villa as a transformative figure whose contributions to the Mexican Revolution and social reform deserve serious consideration beyond simplified narratives of banditry and violence.

Friedrich Katz's monumental work "The Life and Times of Pancho Villa" stands as "a masterpiece of contemporary historiography" and "the definitive work on Mexican revolutionary Pancho Villa" that is "exhaustively researched and scrupulously documented."[^83] According to Katz, while Villa was perceived as a destroyer, there were positive aspects to that assessment—Villa played a decisive role not just in the destruction of Huerta's regime but also the entire old regime. During his brief time as governor of Chihuahua, he carried out significant land reform that began addressing centuries of inequality.[^84]

During his revolutionary career, Villa emerged as a hero to Mexico's rural dispossessed and impoverished working class, as well as to Mexican nationalists who saw in him a genuine representative of Mexican independence and resistance to foreign domination.[^85] Many historians describe Pancho Villa as the Latin American Robin Hood because of Villa's lifestyle of taking from the wealthy and redistributing to the poor, a pattern that was not merely propaganda but documented in his governance of Chihuahua.[^86]

Despite conflicting interpretations of his motives and actions, Villa influenced the course of Mexican history and has endured as a hero to his countrymen, with his commitment to social justice and land reform inspiring later revolutionary movements throughout Latin America.[^87] Villa was regarded as a strong underdog leader and visionary who considered himself an advocate for the poor and social justice, articulating a revolutionary ideology that challenged the fundamental inequalities of Mexican society.[^88]

Scholars have noted that many of Villa's military tactics and strategies were adopted by later twentieth-century revolutionaries, demonstrating his innovative contributions to revolutionary warfare.[^89] His use of rapid mobility, combined arms tactics, and attention to troop welfare set new standards for revolutionary armies and influenced military thinking far beyond Mexico.

Progressive historians argue that Villa must be understood within the context of a brutal civil war in which all factions committed atrocities, and that judging him by peacetime standards of morality obscures the revolutionary significance of his efforts to redistribute land and empower the dispossessed. They contend that Villa's genuine commitment to education and social reform, demonstrated through concrete actions like building schools and creating financial institutions to support cooperatives, reveals a leader with sophisticated understanding of social transformation.[^90]

### Negative Views

Critics and revisionist historians have increasingly challenged romanticized portrayals of Villa, arguing that his brutality and violations of basic human rights disqualify him from hero status and reveal him as an opportunistic warlord who exploited revolutionary rhetoric for personal power.

Villa's legacy is contested—to some he was a violent opportunist who exploited the revolution for his own advancement rather than from genuine ideological commitment.[^91] Because he ultimately lost to Carranza in the revolutionary power struggle, some American historians and PRIista apologists have downgraded Villa's importance, consigning "the Centaur of the North" to a decidedly secondary role in the revolution's pantheon of heroes, portraying him as a destroyer, a common vaquero and bandit who capriciously took advantage of chaotic times by ruthlessly catapulting himself into a position of power.[^92]

Based on accounts collected by his enemies during the revolution, some historians have pointed to darker events in Villa's career, including the mass execution of men in villages in northern Sonora, as well as accounts of rapes, murders, and burning villages.[^93] In Namiquipa, Villa rounded up wives of men who had fled and allowed his soldiers to rape them, while the systematic rape of women during his attacks on small towns was sanctioned by Villa himself. Word of these atrocities spread throughout Mexico and the United States, souring the name of "Pancho Villa" especially among the American population.[^94]

Critics argue that his actions undermined the revolutionary ideals of social justice and democracy he claimed to represent. The civilian casualties resulting from his raids and retaliatory strikes against perceived enemies, including rival revolutionary factions, paint a picture of a leader who sometimes prioritized military success and personal vengeance over moral considerations.[^95] To many contemporary observers and later historians, Villa's brutality and atrocities inspired fear and hatred, while others responded to his leadership and acts of kindness with respect and adulation—creating a deeply divided assessment that persists to this day.[^96]

Scholars critical of Villa argue that the systematic nature of violence against civilians, particularly sexual violence against women and execution of prisoners, cannot be excused as the unfortunate byproduct of revolutionary warfare but instead reveals fundamental character flaws and absence of moral constraint. They contend that Villa's lack of education and sophisticated political ideology left him vulnerable to impulses of revenge and cruelty, making him an unreliable instrument for genuine social transformation.

Some historians argue that Villa's land reform was more pragmatic than idealistic—designed primarily to finance his army rather than to achieve genuine redistribution of wealth. They note that he promised to distribute land after victory but during the war used confiscated estates to enrich his military machine, suggesting that social justice rhetoric served primarily as recruitment propaganda rather than sincere policy commitment.

The Columbus raid and the San Ysabel massacre are cited by critics as evidence of Villa's transformation from revolutionary to terrorist, with actions against American civilians serving no legitimate military purpose but instead reflecting personal grievances and desire for revenge. These actions, critics argue, damaged Mexico's international standing and provoked American intervention that prolonged conflict and suffering.[^97]

## Famous Quotes

> "My sole ambition is to rid Mexico of the class that has oppressed her and given the people a chance to know what real liberty means. And if I could bring that about today by giving up my life, I would do it gladly."
> — Villa's declaration of revolutionary purpose[^98]

> "Don't let it end like this. Tell them I said something."
> — Reportedly Villa's last words before death[^99]

> "I am not an educated man. I never had an opportunity to learn anything except how to fight."
> — Villa on his lack of formal education[^100]

> "I, Pancho Villa, was a loyal man that destiny brought into the world to fight for the good of the poor and that I will never betray nor forget my duty."
> — Villa on his mission and loyalty[^101]

> "The country must be governed by someone who really loves his people and his land, who shares wealth and progress. I have all that, only that I am ignorant."
> — Villa on leadership and governance[^102]

> "Companions of arms and lords. Do not believe that the one who is going to speak to them is a philosopher, I am a man of people, but you will understand that these men when they speak, speak with the heart."
> — Villa addressing his followers[^103]

> "Armies are the greatest supports of tyranny. There can be no dictator without his army."
> — Villa on military power and dictatorship[^104]
