## Summary

Igor Stravinsky (1882-1971) was one of the most influential composers of the 20th century, whose revolutionary works transformed classical music and shaped modernism across multiple artistic disciplines. Born in Oranienbaum, Russia, he rose to international fame with his three groundbreaking ballets for Sergei Diaghilev's Ballets Russes: *The Firebird* (1910), *Petrushka* (1911), and *The Rite of Spring* (1913), the latter causing one of the most famous riots in music history. Throughout his seven-decade career, Stravinsky continually reinvented his musical language, moving through distinct stylistic periods including primitivism, neoclassicism, and serialism, demonstrating an unprecedented capacity for evolution that both fascinated and frustrated critics. His influence extended far beyond classical music, affecting jazz, popular music, and the broader cultural landscape of the modern era.

---

## Early Life and Background

Igor Fyodorovich Stravinsky was born on June 17, 1882 (June 5 in the Old Style calendar), in Oranienbaum, a resort town near St. Petersburg, Russia.[^1] He was the third of four sons born to Fyodor Ignatievich Stravinsky, a renowned bass singer at the Mariinsky Theatre, and Anna Kirillovna Kholodovskaya, a talented pianist.[^2] Growing up in an artistic household, young Igor was exposed to music and opera from an early age, regularly attending rehearsals and performances at the Mariinsky Theatre where his father was a celebrated performer.[^3]

Despite this musical environment, Stravinsky's parents did not initially encourage him to pursue music professionally. Instead, he was directed toward a more conventional career path, enrolling at St. Petersburg University in 1901 to study law.[^4] However, Stravinsky's passion for music persisted, and he continued to study piano and composition privately. During his university years, he became acquainted with Vladimir Rimsky-Korsakov, the youngest son of the famous composer Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov, which proved to be a pivotal connection.[^5]

In 1902, Stravinsky showed some of his early compositions to Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov, who discouraged him from entering the St. Petersburg Conservatory, believing that academic training would stifle his natural talent.[^6] Instead, Rimsky-Korsakov agreed to take Stravinsky on as a private student, a mentorship that would last until the elder composer's death in 1908. Under Rimsky-Korsakov's guidance, Stravinsky developed his orchestration skills and absorbed the rich traditions of Russian music, including the use of folk melodies and modal harmonies that would later inform his early masterpieces.[^7]

Stravinsky's early works, including the *Symphony in E-flat* (1907) and *Feu d'artifice* (Fireworks, 1908), showed promise but remained largely derivative of his teacher's style. However, these compositions caught the attention of Sergei Diaghilev, the impresario of the Ballets Russes, who was searching for new Russian talent to showcase in Paris.[^8]

## Rise to Prominence

Stravinsky's meteoric rise to international fame began in 1909 when Sergei Diaghilev commissioned him to orchestrate music for the Ballets Russes production in Paris.[^9] Impressed by Stravinsky's work, Diaghilev commissioned an original ballet score, which became *The Firebird* (*L'Oiseau de feu*), premiered on June 25, 1910, at the Paris Opera.[^10] The ballet was an immediate triumph, establishing the 28-year-old composer as a significant new voice in European music. The work brilliantly synthesized Russian folk traditions with vivid orchestral colors, creating an exotic yet accessible sound that captivated Parisian audiences.[^11]

Building on this success, Stravinsky composed *Petrushka*, which premiered in June 1911. This ballet depicted the tragic story of a puppet who comes to life, featuring innovative use of bitonality (two keys simultaneously), complex rhythms, and a revolutionary approach to orchestration.[^12] The work further cemented Stravinsky's reputation as a composer of extraordinary originality and technical mastery.

However, it was *The Rite of Spring* (*Le Sacre du printemps*) that would transform Stravinsky from a celebrated composer into a revolutionary figure who changed the course of music history. Premiered on May 29, 1913, at the Théâtre des Champs-Élysées in Paris, the ballet depicted pagan Russian rituals culminating in a sacrificial dance.[^13] The work's savage rhythms, dissonant harmonies, and unconventional orchestration shocked the audience, provoking what is often described as one of the most famous riots in the history of the performing arts.[^14]

Eyewitness accounts describe audience members shouting, fighting, and throwing objects, with the uproar so intense that the dancers could barely hear the orchestra.[^15] While the exact nature and extent of the "riot" has been debated by historians, the premiere undeniably created a sensation that transcended music, making Stravinsky a symbol of modernist rebellion against tradition.[^16] Within a year, however, the work was recognized as a masterpiece, with the concert version (without the controversial choreography) receiving enthusiastic acclaim.[^17]

The outbreak of World War I in 1914 forced Stravinsky to leave Russia, beginning a period of exile that would last for the rest of his life. He settled first in Switzerland, then in France, never returning to live in his homeland after the Bolshevik Revolution of 1917.[^18]

## Major Achievements

Stravinsky's career spanned seven decades and encompassed an extraordinary range of musical styles, making his major achievements impossible to summarize in a single narrative. His work is typically divided into three major periods: the Russian period (1907-1919), the neoclassical period (1920-1951), and the serial period (1952-1971).[^19]

Beyond the groundbreaking early ballets, Stravinsky's Russian period produced other significant works including *The Nightingale* (1914), an opera based on Hans Christian Andersen's fairy tale, and *Les Noces* (*The Wedding*, 1923), a cantata depicting a Russian peasant wedding with innovative scoring for voices, four pianos, and percussion.[^20] *L'Histoire du soldat* (*The Soldier's Tale*, 1918), composed during World War I for a small ensemble, demonstrated Stravinsky's ability to create powerful music with limited resources, incorporating elements of tango, ragtime, and other popular styles.[^21]

The neoclassical period marked a dramatic stylistic shift as Stravinsky turned away from Russian folk materials toward the forms and aesthetics of earlier Western classical music, particularly the 18th century. This transformation bewildered and sometimes angered critics who expected him to continue in the revolutionary vein of *The Rite of Spring*.[^22] Major works from this period include *Pulcinella* (1920), based on music attributed to Pergolesi; the opera-oratorio *Oedipus Rex* (1927), with a Latin libretto by Jean Cocteau; *Symphony of Psalms* (1930), commissioned for the Boston Symphony Orchestra's 50th anniversary; and the ballet *Apollon musagète* (1928), which initiated a long collaboration with choreographer George Balanchine.[^23]

The opera *The Rake's Progress* (1951), with a libretto by W.H. Auden and Chester Kallman inspired by William Hogarth's engravings, represented the culmination of Stravinsky's neoclassicism, employing 18th-century opera conventions with sophisticated modern harmonies.[^24] This three-act opera is considered one of the finest English-language operas of the 20th century.

In his seventies, Stravinsky shocked the music world once again by adopting the twelve-tone serial technique pioneered by Arnold Schoenberg, a composer with whom he had previously had an ambivalent relationship.[^25] This late-period transformation was influenced by his collaboration with conductor Robert Craft, who introduced him to the Second Viennese School's techniques.[^26] Major serial works include *Canticum Sacrum* (1955), *Threni* (1958), *Movements for Piano and Orchestra* (1959), and *Requiem Canticles* (1966), his final major composition.[^27]

Stravinsky was also an influential conductor of his own works, making numerous recordings that remain valuable documents of his interpretive intentions.[^28] He was a prolific writer, producing several books about music and aesthetics, including *Poetics of Music* (1942), based on lectures given at Harvard University.[^29] His influence extended beyond classical music into jazz, with musicians like Duke Ellington and Woody Herman drawing inspiration from his rhythmic innovations.[^30]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his towering achievements, Stravinsky's career was marked by significant controversies and persistent criticisms. His stylistic changes, particularly the turn to neoclassicism in the 1920s, were seen by many as a betrayal of modernism and a reactionary retreat from his revolutionary early works.[^31] Theodor Adorno, the influential German philosopher and music theorist, became one of Stravinsky's most severe critics, arguing that his neoclassical works represented a "regression" and a capitulation to bourgeois taste.[^32]

Adorno's *Philosophy of Modern Music* (1949) presented a dichotomy between the "progressive" Arnold Schoenberg and the "reactionary" Stravinsky, accusing the latter of creating music that was "lifeless," "mechanical," and devoid of authentic expression.[^33] This critique influenced a generation of critics and composers, particularly in Germany, where Stravinsky's reputation suffered in comparison to the Second Viennese School.[^34]

Stravinsky's political views and associations also attracted criticism. During the 1930s and early 1940s, some of his statements appeared to align with authoritarian and nationalistic ideologies. He expressed admiration for Mussolini and made statements that were interpreted as anti-Semitic, though the extent and sincerity of these views remain debated by scholars.[^35] In 1935, he published an autobiography that some critics found self-serving and dismissive of his collaborators.[^36]

His relationship with collaborators was often contentious. While Stravinsky benefited enormously from his partnerships with Diaghilev, choreographer Vaslav Nijinsky, designer Léon Bakst, and others, he frequently downplayed their contributions in later accounts, insisting on his sole authorship of the works' revolutionary impact.[^37] This led to disputes about credit, particularly regarding *The Rite of Spring*, where Nijinsky's choreography was arguably as shocking as Stravinsky's music.[^38]

Financial disputes and legal battles over rights and royalties marked much of Stravinsky's career. He was known for his meticulous attention to contracts and his aggressive protection of his commercial interests, which some viewed as unseemly for an artist of his stature.[^39] He frequently revised and rearranged his earlier works, creating new versions that generated additional income but sometimes compromised the original conceptions.[^40]

Critics also questioned the depth and authenticity of Stravinsky's later serial works, suggesting that his adoption of twelve-tone technique in his seventies was more a calculated response to changing fashions than a genuine artistic evolution.[^41] Some felt that these late works lacked the vitality and conviction of his earlier music, representing the efforts of an aging composer trying to remain relevant.[^42]

His relationship with Robert Craft, who became his assistant, conductor, and spokesman in the 1950s and 1960s, raised questions about authorship and influence. The numerous books published under Stravinsky's name during this period were actually collaborative efforts with Craft, leading to debates about whose voice and ideas they truly represented.[^43]

## Personal Life

Igor Stravinsky married his cousin Catherine (Yekaterina) Nossenko in 1906, a union that required special dispensation from the church due to their blood relationship.[^44] The couple had four children: Fyodor (Theodore, 1907-1989), Ludmila (1908-1938), Sviatoslav (Soulima, 1910-1994), and Maria Milena (1913-1938).[^45] The marriage was marked by difficulties, including Catherine's long battle with tuberculosis, which required frequent stays in sanatoriums.[^46]

During the 1920s, while Catherine was ill, Stravinsky began a long-term affair with Vera de Bosset (née Sudeikina), a Russian-born artist and actress married to the painter and stage designer Serge Sudeikin.[^47] The relationship continued for years while both were still married to others, causing considerable scandal in émigré Russian society.[^48] Catherine died in 1939, and Stravinsky married Vera in 1940, a union that would last until his death in 1971.[^49]

Stravinsky's personality was complex and often contradictory. He was known for his sharp wit, his fastidious attention to detail, and his remarkable capacity for work.[^50] He maintained a disciplined daily routine, composing every morning with the precision of a craftsman rather than waiting for romantic inspiration.[^51] This professional approach to composition was reflected in his famous statement: "I haven't understood a bar of music in my life, but I have felt it."[^52]

He was fluent in multiple languages (Russian, French, English, and German) and moved easily in international artistic circles, counting among his friends and collaborators Jean Cocteau, Pablo Picasso, Dylan Thomas, and Aldous Huxley.[^53] Despite his cosmopolitan life, he retained certain Russian characteristics throughout his life, including a fondness for vodka and Russian cuisine.[^54]

Stravinsky was notoriously sensitive to criticism and engaged in public feuds with critics and fellow composers. He had a complicated relationship with Arnold Schoenberg, whom he both admired and resented, and the two maintained a cautious distance despite their mutual influence.[^55] His correspondence reveals a man capable of great loyalty to friends but also of petty jealousies and calculated self-promotion.[^56]

In 1939, Stravinsky moved to the United States, initially to deliver lectures at Harvard University, and he became an American citizen in 1945.[^57] He settled in West Hollywood, California, where he lived for the remainder of his life, becoming part of the émigré community that included fellow artists, writers, and intellectuals who had fled Europe.[^58] His home became a gathering place for the cultural elite of Los Angeles, from film industry figures to visiting European artists.[^59]

## Legacy

Igor Stravinsky's impact on 20th-century music is immeasurable, with his influence extending across virtually every genre and style of Western art music. He fundamentally changed how composers thought about rhythm, liberating it from its subordinate role to melody and harmony and demonstrating that rhythmic innovation alone could drive musical structure.[^60] The complex, irregular, and propulsive rhythms of *The Rite of Spring* influenced not only classical composers but also jazz musicians, progressive rock bands, and film composers.[^61]

His approach to neoclassicism—not merely copying earlier styles but reinterpreting them through a modern sensibility—provided a model for countless composers seeking alternatives to both late-romantic excess and twelve-tone serialism.[^62] Composers as diverse as Aaron Copland, Benjamin Britten, and Leonard Bernstein acknowledged their debt to Stravinsky's example.[^63]

Stravinsky's willingness to continually reinvent himself challenged the Romantic notion of artistic consistency and authentic self-expression. His career demonstrated that a composer could adopt multiple styles without sacrificing artistic integrity, a concept that became central to postmodern aesthetics.[^64] Pierre Boulez, despite initially criticizing Stravinsky's neoclassicism, later acknowledged him as a liberating force who showed that composers need not be bound by stylistic orthodoxy.[^65]

His collaboration with George Balanchine produced some of the most important works in 20th-century ballet, including *Apollon musagète*, *Orpheus* (1948), and *Agon* (1957).[^66] These works established new possibilities for the relationship between music and dance, with Balanchine's abstract, musically-driven choreography perfectly matched to Stravinsky's formal precision.[^67]

Stravinsky's influence on film music was equally significant. Composers like Bernard Herrmann, John Williams, and Howard Shore have acknowledged learning from Stravinsky's orchestration techniques and dramatic pacing.[^68] The use of ostinato patterns, sudden dynamic contrasts, and colorful orchestration in film scores owes much to Stravinsky's innovations.[^69]

His recordings of his own works, though sometimes controversial in their interpretations, provide invaluable documentation of his musical thinking and have influenced performance practice for generations.[^70] The Stravinsky estate and the numerous scholarly editions of his works continue to shape how his music is understood and performed.[^71]

Major music institutions have honored his legacy through festivals, symposia, and research centers. The Paul Sacher Foundation in Basel, Switzerland, holds extensive Stravinsky archives, making his sketches and manuscripts available to scholars.[^72] Annual Stravinsky festivals occur worldwide, and his works remain among the most frequently performed of any 20th-century composer.[^73]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and musicians have consistently ranked Stravinsky among the greatest composers in Western music history. Leonard Bernstein called him "the most protean figure in modern music," praising his ability to work successfully in multiple styles while maintaining a distinctive voice.[^74] Aaron Copland wrote that Stravinsky "seemed to have provided the musical conscience of an entire generation," noting that even composers who rejected his aesthetic had to define themselves in relation to him.[^75]

Musicologist Richard Taruskin, author of the most comprehensive scholarly study of Stravinsky's works, argues that the composer's Russian period music represents the culmination of 19th-century nationalist traditions while simultaneously breaking new ground.[^76] Taruskin defends even Stravinsky's controversial neoclassical period, arguing that these works demonstrate sophisticated engagement with tradition rather than simple regression.[^77]

Pierre Boulez, representing the post-war avant-garde generation that initially rejected neoclassicism, later revised his assessment of Stravinsky, stating: "I came to understand that Stravinsky's importance lay not in any particular style but in his rigorous musical thinking and his absolute integrity as an artist."[^78] Boulez particularly admired Stravinsky's late serial works, seeing in them a unique synthesis of the composer's lifelong concerns with form and structure.[^79]

Conductor Kent Nagano has argued that Stravinsky's greatest achievement was demonstrating that modernism need not be expressionist or emotionally autobiographical, offering instead a more objective, craftsmanlike approach to composition.[^80] This "anti-romantic" stance, while controversial, opened new creative possibilities for composers uncomfortable with the expressionist aesthetic.[^81]

Music psychologists and theorists have praised Stravinsky's rhythmic innovations as fundamentally expanding the language of Western music. Studies have shown that his complex metrical structures engage the brain differently than traditional rhythmic patterns, creating new possibilities for temporal perception in music.[^82]

The continued vitality of Stravinsky's music in performance is itself a testament to its enduring quality. Works like *The Firebird*, *Petrushka*, and *The Rite of Spring* have never left the standard orchestral repertoire, while pieces like *Symphony of Psalms* and *The Rake's Progress* are regularly performed worldwide.[^83]

### Negative Views

Critics of Stravinsky have argued that his chameleon-like stylistic changes reveal a lack of authentic artistic conviction, suggesting that he was more concerned with fashion and commercial success than with genuine self-expression.[^84] Theodor Adorno's critique remains influential, with some scholars continuing to argue that Stravinsky's music represents a problematic objectification and mechanization of art.[^85]

Some critics have argued that Stravinsky's neoclassical works, while technically accomplished, lack the emotional depth and human warmth of the great Romantic composers or even of his contemporary, Béla Bartók.[^86] The emotional distance and ironic detachment that Stravinsky cultivated can seem cold or superficial to listeners expecting more obvious emotional communication.[^87]

Questions persist about the extent to which Stravinsky's early success was due to his collaborators. Ballet historian Lynn Garafola has argued that the revolutionary impact of *The Rite of Spring* cannot be separated from Nijinsky's choreography and the overall production values of the Ballets Russes.[^88] Some scholars suggest that Stravinsky exaggerated his own role and minimized his collaborators' contributions in later accounts.[^89]

His political statements and associations, particularly in the 1930s, continue to trouble scholars. While some defenders argue these were isolated incidents or have been taken out of context, others see them as revealing problematic aspects of his character and worldview.[^90] His apparent political opportunism—adapting to whatever regime he lived under, from Tsarist Russia to Soviet overtures in his final years—suggests a troubling lack of moral principle to some critics.[^91]

The authenticity of the late serial works remains contested. Some critics argue that these pieces demonstrate Stravinsky's remarkable capacity for continued growth into his eighties, while others see them as the work of a composer past his prime, mechanically applying techniques developed by others without fully integrating them into his personal style.[^92]

His relationship with Robert Craft has been questioned, with some scholars suggesting that Craft exerted undue influence on the aging composer and that the ideas presented in the "conversation books" may reflect Craft's views more than Stravinsky's.[^93] This raises uncomfortable questions about the reliability of Stravinsky's late writings and pronouncements.[^94]

## Famous Quotes

> "I haven't understood a bar of music in my life, but I have felt it." - On his intuitive approach to composition[^95]

> "The more constraints one imposes, the more one frees oneself of the chains that shackle the spirit." - From *Poetics of Music*, expressing his belief in the creative power of formal restrictions[^96]

> "My music is best understood by children and animals." - Often quoted to illustrate his view that music should be experienced directly rather than intellectually analyzed[^97]

> "Lesser artists borrow, great artists steal." - On his practice of incorporating material from other composers, often attributed to Stravinsky though the exact origin is disputed[^98]

> "I have learned throughout my life as a composer chiefly through my mistakes and pursuits of false assumptions, not by my exposure to founts of wisdom and knowledge." - Reflecting on his creative process and willingness to experiment[^99]

> "Harpists spend 90 percent of their lives tuning their harps and 10 percent playing out of tune." - Displaying his famous wit and sometimes acerbic humor about performers[^100]
