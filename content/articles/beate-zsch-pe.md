## Summary

German far-right terrorist and member of the National Socialist Underground (NSU) that murdered 10 people across Germany between 2000 and 2007. The NSU targeted Turkish and Greek immigrants in a series of racially motivated killings.

## NSU Context (High Level)

The National Socialist Underground (NSU) was a clandestine neo-Nazi cell responsible for a series of murders, bombings, and robberies. The murders disproportionately targeted immigrants, and the case later became a major national scandal due to investigative failures and misdirected suspicion toward victims’ communities.

## Legal Outcome

Zschäpe was tried in a high-profile Munich trial that examined both the crimes and the broader support networks around the NSU. In 2018 she was convicted and sentenced to life imprisonment, with the judgment treating her as a central participant even though other members carried out specific attacks.

## Public Impact and Controversy

The NSU case prompted extensive debate in Germany about:

- Far-right extremism and underground networks
- Institutional bias and investigative errors
- Protection and accountability within security services

## Legacy

Zschäpe’s name is closely tied to one of modern Germany’s most consequential far-right terrorism cases, often cited in discussions of racism, political violence, and state capacity to detect domestic extremist threats.

The case continues to influence public policy and civil-society monitoring of extremist violence in Germany.
