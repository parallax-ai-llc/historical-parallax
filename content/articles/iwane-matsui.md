## Summary

Japanese general who commanded forces during the Nanjing Massacre. He was convicted of war crimes by the Tokyo Tribunal for failing to prevent the atrocities.

## Military Career (High-Level)

- Matsui served as a career officer in the Imperial Japanese Army and held senior commands during Japan's expansion in East Asia.
- He is most closely associated in historical memory with the 1937 capture of Nanjing and the atrocities committed during and after the occupation.

## Command Responsibility

- Discussions of Matsui's conviction often focus on the doctrine of command responsibility: the legal and moral responsibility of commanders to prevent or punish crimes committed by forces under their control.
- The Tokyo Tribunal treated failures of supervision and prevention as grounds for culpability, even when direct, personally ordered acts are difficult to document.

## Postwar Trial and Historical Debate

- Matsui was tried by the International Military Tribunal for the Far East (IMTFE), commonly referred to as the Tokyo Trials.
- Scholarship and public debate around the Nanjing Massacre includes disputes over scope, documentation, and political memory; however, the occurrence of mass atrocities is extensively documented in mainstream historiography.

## Legacy

- The case is frequently referenced in studies of wartime atrocities, transitional justice, and how postwar tribunals shaped international criminal law norms.
