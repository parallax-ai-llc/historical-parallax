## Summary

Natalia Makarova (born November 21, 1940, Leningrad, USSR) is one of the most celebrated ballet dancers of the twentieth century, widely regarded as the defining dramatic ballerina of her generation. A principal artist with the Kirov Ballet before her defection to the West in 1970, she brought a rare synthesis of technical brilliance and expressive depth to roles that had previously been approached as technical showcases alone.

Her defection at the age of 29 transformed not only her own career but the broader landscape of Western ballet. Over the following two decades she performed with virtually every major company in the world, collaborated with the era's foremost choreographers, and staged landmark reconstructions of nineteenth-century classics that remain in repertoire today.

Beyond performing, Makarova became an important bridge between the Russian imperial tradition and contemporary Western practice, transmitting pedagogical and stylistic knowledge that had been largely inaccessible outside the Soviet Union.

## Early Life & Background

Makarova was born in Leningrad on November 21, 1940, during the early months of World War II. She studied at the Vaganova Academy of Russian Ballet, the same institution that had trained Anna Pavlova and Rudolf Nureyev, graduating in 1959. That year she joined the Kirov Ballet (now Mariinsky), where she rose quickly to principal status under the coaching of Natalia Dudinskaya.

## Rise to Prominence

Within the Kirov, Makarova became the company's foremost dramatic ballerina through the 1960s, acclaimed for her portrayals of Giselle and the dual role in Swan Lake. During a Kirov tour to London in September 1970, she requested political asylum in the United Kingdom — the second major Soviet ballet defection after Rudolf Nureyev in 1961 and preceding Mikhail Baryshnikov's in 1974.

She joined American Ballet Theatre (ABT) almost immediately, becoming a principal dancer and later a regular guest with the Royal Ballet, London Festival Ballet, and numerous other companies. Her partnership with Baryshnikov at ABT from 1974 onward was considered one of the great pairings in ballet history.

## Key Achievements

Makarova's 1974 staging of La Bayadère (Kingdom of the Shades act) for ABT introduced the work to Western audiences for the first time and is widely credited with sparking international interest in the full-length ballet. Her 1980 full-length production of La Bayadère for ABT was the first complete Western staging of the work. She also mounted productions of Swan Lake and Sleeping Beauty that drew directly on Petipa-era staging details preserved in Soviet practice.

In 1983 she made her Broadway debut in the musical On Your Toes, winning a Tony Award for Best Actress in a Play — an exceptional crossover achievement for a classical dancer. She was named a Commander of the Order of the British Empire (CBE) in 2003.

## Controversies & Criticism

Her defection was a significant Cold War event and was condemned by Soviet authorities, who effectively erased her from official records for years. Some Russian critics argued that her work with Western commercial productions compromised the purity of classical training, though such views were minority positions. Occasional critical debate surrounded staging choices in her reconstructions, particularly regarding how faithfully they reflected Petipa's original intentions versus Kirov house practice of the 1960s.

## Legacy & Influence

Makarova's productions of La Bayadère and other nineteenth-century works remain in active repertoire at ABT and have been licensed internationally, ensuring the transmission of the imperial Russian style to successive generations. Her memoirs, A Dance Autobiography (1979), remain a primary source on Kirov practice and life in Soviet-era Leningrad. She is consistently cited by dancers such as Alessandra Ferri and Julie Kent as a formative influence on how dramatic characterization is understood within classical ballet.
