## Summary

Saddam Hussein's government exploited the UN Oil-for-Food Programme to earn $1.8 billion through oil smuggling and kickbacks. Several UN officials and politicians were implicated.

## Background (UN Oil-for-Food Programme)

- The UN Oil-for-Food Programme (OFFP) was created in the 1990s to allow Iraq to sell limited quantities of oil under UN supervision.
- The stated purpose was to fund humanitarian imports (food, medicine, and other civilian goods) while broader sanctions remained in place.
- Oil sales revenue was intended to be held and disbursed through controlled UN mechanisms rather than directly through the Iraqi government.

## How the Scheme Worked (High Level)

- Iraq leveraged its position as an oil supplier to influence contracting and pricing.
- Alleged abuses included:
  - **Oil smuggling** outside the program’s controls.
  - **Kickbacks/surcharges** embedded in contracts (payments extracted from suppliers or intermediaries).
  - **Manipulation of allocations** (granting oil liftings to favored entities who could resell for profit).

## Investigations and Findings

- The scandal triggered multiple national and international investigations in the early-to-mid 2000s.
- A widely cited inquiry was the Independent Inquiry Committee chaired by Paul Volcker, which reviewed program administration and oversight.
- Investigations focused on failures of controls, procurement integrity, and oversight across a complex international supply chain.

## Consequences and Legacy

- The scandal became a major case study in:
  - sanctions enforcement challenges,
  - corruption risks in large-scale humanitarian procurement,
  - and institutional accountability for multinational programs.
- It contributed to reforms and stricter compliance practices around sanctions, contracting, and third-party due diligence.

## Commonly Noted Lessons

- Oversight must match the complexity and scale of the program.
- Incentives created by sanctions and controlled markets can drive sophisticated evasion.
- Transparency in contracting and auditing is essential when large commodity revenues are involved.
