## Summary

Eric Clapton is a British guitarist, singer, and songwriter widely regarded as one of the greatest and most influential guitarists in the history of rock and blues music. Over a career spanning more than six decades, he has earned the nickname "Slowhand" and is the only three-time inductee into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame — as a member of the Yardbirds, as a member of Cream, and as a solo artist. His mastery of blues guitar technique and his ability to bridge British rock and American blues made him a defining figure of the 1960s and beyond.

Clapton's career has encompassed landmark work with the Yardbirds, John Mayall & the Bluesbreakers, Cream, Blind Faith, Derek and the Dominos, and a prolific solo career. His recordings introduced generations of listeners to the Delta blues tradition of Robert Johnson and inspired countless guitarists worldwide. Despite significant personal struggles with addiction and tragedy, he rebuilt his career and his life, becoming an elder statesman of rock.

## Early Life & Background

Eric Patrick Clapton was born on 30 March 1945 in Ripley, Surrey, England. He was raised by his maternal grandparents, Rose and Jack Clapp, believing them to be his parents — a family secret he discovered at age nine. His biological mother, Patricia Molly Clapton, had him at sixteen while his father, a Canadian soldier named Edward Fryer, had returned to Canada before his birth. This discovery left a lasting emotional mark.

Clapton showed an early passion for music, particularly the American blues he heard on the radio. At thirteen he received his first guitar and taught himself to play by listening to records by Muddy Waters, B.B. King, and Robert Johnson. He briefly attended Kingston College of Art before being asked to leave, after which he devoted himself entirely to music.

## Rise to Prominence

Clapton joined the Yardbirds in 1963 as lead guitarist, departing in 1965 when the band moved toward pop — a decision that reflected his unwavering commitment to blues purity. He joined John Mayall & the Bluesbreakers, and the 1966 album *Blues Breakers with Eric Clapton* became a landmark of British blues. His tone and technique inspired graffiti across London reading "Clapton is God."

In 1966 he co-founded Cream with Jack Bruce and Ginger Baker, one of rock's first supergroups. Albums including *Disraeli Gears* (1967) and *Wheels of Fire* (1968) fused blues, psychedelia, and hard rock. After Cream dissolved in 1968, Clapton formed Blind Faith and then collaborated with Delaney & Bonnie before forming Derek and the Dominos, whose 1970 double album *Layla and Other Assorted Love Songs* produced the iconic track "Layla."

## Key Achievements

- Three Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inductions (1992, 1993, 2000)
- 18 Grammy Awards across his career
- *Layla* (1970) and *Tears in Heaven* (1992) are among the most recognized songs in rock history
- Sold over 280 million records worldwide
- *Slowhand* (1977) reached number 2 on the UK Albums Chart and spawned "Wonderful Tonight" and "Cocaine"
- Co-founded the Crossroads Centre in Antigua (1998), a rehabilitation facility for addiction

## Controversies & Criticism

Clapton's reputation has been significantly damaged by remarks he made during a 1976 concert in Birmingham, where he delivered an alcohol-fuelled racist rant in support of Enoch Powell, shocking fans and the music community. The incident directly inspired the formation of the Rock Against Racism movement. Clapton later expressed regret but has faced renewed scrutiny over the years when the remarks resurfaced. During the COVID-19 pandemic he publicly opposed vaccine mandates and lockdown measures, recording the song "This Has Gotta Stop" (2021), which drew widespread criticism and alienated portions of his fanbase.

His personal life has also attracted controversy, including his decade-long heroin and alcohol addiction, and a prolonged infatuation with Pattie Boyd — then the wife of his close friend George Harrison — whom he later married in 1979 and divorced in 1989.

## Legacy & Influence

Clapton's influence on guitar playing is immeasurable. His tone, phrasing, and emotional expressiveness set a template for rock guitar that shaped players from Stevie Ray Vaughan to John Mayer. He is credited with bringing the work of Robert Johnson to mainstream audiences and sustaining the vitality of the blues tradition within rock. The Crossroads Guitar Festival, which he has organized since 2004, raises funds for his rehabilitation centre and showcases guitar talent across generations. Despite the controversies, his musical legacy remains foundational to the development of rock, blues-rock, and beyond.
