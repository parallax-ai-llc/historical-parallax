## Early work and experiments

Niepce pursued a broad range of technical experiments, including work on engines and early lithographic processes. His photographic breakthrough came from years of attempts to fix images created by light so they could be preserved without hand drawing.

## Heliography and the first permanent images

He developed a process he called heliography, using light-sensitive materials (notably bitumen of Judea) on metal or stone plates. After long exposures, the hardened material could resist solvents, allowing the image to be revealed and retained.

The most famous surviving example is often titled *View from the Window at Le Gras* (c. 1826–1827), widely cited as the earliest known surviving photograph made in a camera.

## Collaboration and legacy

Niepce entered a partnership with Louis Daguerre, sharing methods and continuing research toward more practical photographic techniques. After Niepce’s death, Daguerre’s later work contributed to the daguerreotype, one of the first widely adopted photographic processes.

## Historical significance

Niepce’s experiments are foundational in the history of photography because they demonstrated that:

- A camera image could be chemically fixed and preserved
- Photographic processes could be systematized and repeated
- Later commercial methods built on earlier, less sensitive (long-exposure) approaches
