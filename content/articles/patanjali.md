## Summary

Patanjali was an ancient Indian sage who compiled the Yoga Sutras, the foundational text of Raja Yoga and one of the most influential philosophical works in Hindu tradition. His systematic presentation of yoga as an eight-limbed path (ashtanga yoga) — from ethical disciplines to meditation and samadhi — defined the practice for centuries. He is also traditionally credited with writing a major commentary on Panini's Sanskrit grammar. The Yoga Sutras remain the most authoritative text on yoga philosophy.

## Why He Matters

Patanjali is influential less as a biographical figure (the historical details are debated) and more as a name attached to a highly organized presentation of yoga philosophy. The Yoga Sutras became a compact reference text that later commentators could interpret, expand, and transmit across centuries.

## The Yoga Sutras (High-Level)

- **Aphoristic style**: The sutra format is intentionally concise, making commentaries essential for interpretation.
- **Eight-limbed path (ashtanga)**: A structured progression from ethical commitments and personal discipline to posture, breath, sense withdrawal, concentration, meditation, and absorption.
- **Goal orientation**: The text is often framed around reducing mental fluctuation and cultivating stable, clear awareness.

## Attribution and Dating Notes

- Scholarly views vary on when the Yoga Sutras were compiled and whether "Patanjali" refers to a single historical person or a traditional attribution.
- Patanjali is also associated in tradition with Sanskrit grammar (via commentary literature), showing how later cultural memory can merge multiple intellectual lineages.
- Modern global yoga movements frequently draw on Patanjali as a symbol of philosophical legitimacy, even when practice emphasizes physical disciplines more than classical metaphysics.
