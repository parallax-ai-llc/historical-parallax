## Summary

German SS officer who was a major organizer of the Holocaust, managing the logistics of mass deportation to extermination camps. Captured by Mossad in Argentina, he was tried and executed in Israel.

## Historical Context

Eichmann worked within the SS and the Nazi state bureaucracy during the period in which Nazi Germany implemented policies of persecution and mass murder against European Jews and other targeted groups. In common historical descriptions, his influence is associated less with frontline command than with administrative coordination and interagency logistics.

## Role in the Holocaust (High-Level)

- Worked in offices responsible for Jewish affairs and deportations within the Reich Security Main Office (RSHA).
- Helped organize deportation transports from multiple European countries to ghettos and extermination sites.
- Became closely linked to the bureaucratic language and processes used to implement the “Final Solution.”

## Postwar Flight and Capture

After Germany’s defeat, Eichmann avoided immediate postwar prosecution, lived under assumed identities, and eventually settled in Argentina. In 1960, Israeli intelligence agents captured him in Buenos Aires and transported him to Israel to stand trial.

## Trial, Judgment, and Legacy

Eichmann’s 1961 trial in Jerusalem is widely cited for its extensive survivor testimony and documentation of Nazi crimes. He was convicted on multiple counts related to crimes against the Jewish people, crimes against humanity, and war crimes, and was executed in 1962.

The case also became influential in later public and academic discussions about individual responsibility within bureaucratic systems, including debates about obedience, careerism, and moral agency.
