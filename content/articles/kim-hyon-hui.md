## Summary

North Korean spy who bombed Korean Air Flight 858 in 1987, killing all 115 on board. She posed as a Japanese tourist and planted a bomb. Captured and sentenced to death, she was later pardoned by the South Korean president and became a defector.

## Additional Context

- **Korean Air Flight 858 (1987):** The bombing occurred during heightened Cold War tensions on the Korean Peninsula and was widely treated as a state-directed operation.
- **Cover identity:** Public accounts describe Kim traveling with an accomplice while using a Japanese alias and carrying materials used to construct and conceal an explosive device.
- **Capture and interrogation:** After the attack, she was detained and interrogated by South Korean authorities; her case became a major international news story in the late 1980s.

## Aftermath and Significance

- **Political impact:** The bombing is frequently cited in discussions of North Korean covert operations and their effects on inter-Korean relations.
- **Legal outcome:** Although initially sentenced to death, she was later pardoned; her pardon has remained controversial, with debates focusing on culpability, coercion, and propaganda value.
- **Defector profile:** Following her defection, she has been referenced in South Korean public discourse about intelligence, terrorism, and the human consequences of authoritarian systems.

## Key Takeaways

1. The Flight 858 bombing is one of the most infamous mass-casualty attacks linked to the Korean Peninsula in the late 20th century.
2. Kim’s later pardon and public status made the case persist beyond the initial criminal proceedings.
3. The episode continues to be used as a reference point in security policy and historical analysis.
