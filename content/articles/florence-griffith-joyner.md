## Summary

Flo-Jo set the 100m (10.49s) and 200m (21.34s) world records that still stand. She won three golds at the 1988 Seoul Olympics. She died at age 38.

## Additional Context

### Athletic peak and records

Griffith-Joyner's 1988 season is widely viewed as one of the most dominant single-year peaks in sprinting history. Her 100 m and 200 m world records remain the benchmark in women's sprinting and are frequently cited in discussions about the limits of human speed.

### Public image

Beyond performance, she became a pop-culture figure, known for distinctive race outfits and long, stylized fingernails. This visibility helped expand mainstream attention for women's track and field.

### Post-competition work

After stepping back from elite competition, she remained involved in sport and public life, including roles connected to athletics promotion and youth engagement.

### Ongoing debate

Because her record-setting times were extraordinary, they have been the subject of persistent debate and scrutiny within the broader history of sprinting. Her records nevertheless remain official in the sport's record books.

### Broader influence

In many retrospectives, Griffith-Joyner is credited with helping make track and field athletes more marketable as individual public figures. Her style-forward presentation is often compared to later eras in which branding and personal storytelling became central to elite sport.

### Record context

Discussion of her 1988 100 m record also often mentions that the wind reading for the semifinal race has been disputed, even while the time remains recognized as the world record.
