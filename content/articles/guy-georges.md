## Summary

French serial killer known as 'The Beast of the Bastille' who raped and murdered 7 women in Paris between 1991 and 1997. He was caught through DNA evidence. Sentenced to life with no parole for 22 years.

## Additional Context

## Background

Guy Georges grew up in France and later lived in the Paris area. Reporting around the case often describes a difficult childhood and a pattern of earlier criminal behavior prior to the murders, which became relevant during later court proceedings.

## Investigation and Identification

The case became widely associated with the growing use of forensic genetics in France. Investigators linked multiple crime scenes using DNA profiles and ultimately connected Georges to the series through a DNA match after his arrest, strengthening the evidentiary basis for prosecution.

## Trial and Sentencing

Georges was tried in the early 2000s and convicted of multiple counts, receiving a life sentence. In French legal terms, “life” can include a minimum period before eligibility for parole review; summaries of the case often cite a long minimum term reflecting the gravity of the convictions.

## Broader Significance

In public discussion, the case is frequently referenced in relation to:

- the development and acceptance of DNA databases and forensic practice
- policing challenges in linking serial offenses across time and locations
- media coverage and public fear during periods of unsolved violent crime
