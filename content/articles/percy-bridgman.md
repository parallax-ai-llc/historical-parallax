## Summary

Percy Williams Bridgman was an American physicist who won the 1946 Nobel Prize in Physics for pioneering experimental techniques that enabled the study of materials under very high pressures.

## Scientific Contributions

- Bridgman developed high-pressure apparatus and measurement methods that made it possible to systematically study compressibility, phase transitions, and transport properties of materials.
- His work helped establish “high-pressure physics” as a practical experimental domain, influencing later research in condensed matter physics, geophysics, and materials science.
- Beyond specific results, his enduring impact is often described as methodological: building reliable instruments and protocols for regimes that were previously inaccessible.

## Philosophy of Science (Operationalism)

- Bridgman is associated with “operational definitions,” arguing that scientific concepts should be defined by the operations used to measure them.
- This view influenced 20th-century discussions in philosophy of science and in how physicists communicate measurement-dependent concepts.

## Personal Notes

- Late in life he faced serious illness; accounts commonly note that he ended his life while suffering from cancer.
