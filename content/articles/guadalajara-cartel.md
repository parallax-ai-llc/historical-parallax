## Summary

First major Mexican drug trafficking organization founded in the 1980s by Felix Gallardo, Caro Quintero, and Fonseca Carrillo. It controlled most cocaine and marijuana entering the US.

## Origins and Structure

- The Guadalajara Cartel is widely described as a foundational modern Mexican trafficking organization that consolidated routes and corruption networks in the 1980s.
- It was associated with a leadership group that coordinated operations across multiple regions before later fragmentation.

## Trafficking Networks

- The cartel’s rise coincided with expanding cocaine flows from South America into U.S. markets, using Mexico as a key transit corridor.
- Marijuana and heroin trafficking also featured in its portfolio, with logistics depending on smuggling routes, bribery, and violence.

## Law-Enforcement Pressure

- The 1985 kidnapping and murder of U.S. DEA agent Enrique "Kiki" Camarena became a major turning point, intensifying bilateral pressure and investigations.
- Subsequent arrests and internal power struggles contributed to the decline of centralized control.

## Fragmentation and Legacy

- Later accounts describe the organization splitting into territorially based successor groups, which helped shape the next era of Mexican cartel competition.
- In historical narratives, the Guadalajara Cartel is often treated as a bridge between older smuggling groups and the large, diversified cartels of the 1990s and 2000s.
