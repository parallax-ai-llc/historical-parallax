## Summary

Anwar Raslan, a former Syrian intelligence officer convicted by a German court of crimes against humanity for overseeing torture of detainees at the Al-Khatib detention center in Damascus. It was the first conviction worldwide for Syrian state torture.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2011 | Directed torture operations at Branch 251 in Damascus |
| 2014 | Fled Syria and sought asylum in Germany |
| 2019 | Arrested in Germany under universal jurisdiction |
| 2022 | Convicted and sentenced to life imprisonment |

## Additional Context

- The name **"Omar al-Marwan"** is often referenced in reporting on Syrian detention abuses, but the linked Wikipedia page may not exist (or may be a redirect/missing page) depending on when it is accessed.
- This profile's summary and timeline describe the **Koblenz trial** and the conviction of **Anwar Raslan**, a former Syrian intelligence officer associated with **Branch 251 (Al-Khatib)** in Damascus.

### Universal Jurisdiction (Why Germany Could Try the Case)

Germany applied the principle of **universal jurisdiction**, which allows national courts to prosecute certain grave crimes (such as crimes against humanity) regardless of where they were committed.

### Allegations Described in Public Reporting

High-level allegations connected to Branch 251 in public sources include:

- Systematic detention and torture of perceived opponents of the Syrian government
- Interrogation practices involving physical and psychological abuse
- Large numbers of detainees held without due process

### Significance

- The case is widely discussed as an early, landmark example of domestic courts addressing atrocities linked to the Syrian conflict.
- It is frequently cited in discussions about accountability when international tribunals are unavailable or blocked.
