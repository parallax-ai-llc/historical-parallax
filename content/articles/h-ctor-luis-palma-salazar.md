## Summary

Co-founder of the Sinaloa Cartel whose wife was murdered and children thrown off a bridge by a rival. One of Mexico's most feared drug lords of the 1990s.

## Additional Context

### Background and Role

Héctor Luis Palma Salazar, often associated with the Sinaloa Cartel’s early leadership, rose from regional trafficking networks in northwestern Mexico to become a senior figure during a period of rapid cartel consolidation. Sources commonly describe him as a co-founder or key lieutenant within the Sinaloa organization, operating alongside other well-known leaders.

### Operations and Influence

At a high level, his career is discussed in relation to:

- **Transnational drug trafficking routes** linking Mexico to U.S. markets during the late 1980s and 1990s.
- **Organizational growth** as groups shifted from loosely connected smuggling cells into more hierarchical cartel structures.
- **Violent competition** among rival factions, which shaped both law-enforcement priorities and cartel security practices.

### Arrests, Detention, and Legal Status

Public reporting emphasizes that Palma Salazar spent extended periods in custody across multiple jurisdictions. His case is often cited as an example of how cartel prosecutions can involve:

- Extradition processes and multi-agency investigations
- Complex sentencing timelines and procedural delays
- Ongoing disputes over charges, releases, and re-arrest decisions

### Significance

In historical overviews of Mexico’s drug war era, Palma Salazar is frequently referenced for what his trajectory illustrates about the time: the rise of large-scale trafficking organizations, their internal rivalries, and the long arc of legal proceedings that can follow major cartel leadership cases.
