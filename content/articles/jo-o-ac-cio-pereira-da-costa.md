## Summary

Brazilian serial killer known as 'Bandido da Luz Vermelha' (Red Light Bandit) who murdered 4 people and committed dozens of robberies in São Paulo in the 1960s. He became a folk criminal figure. Sentenced to 351 years.

## Background

João Acácio Pereira da Costa became notorious in São Paulo during the late 1960s amid intense media coverage of violent urban crime. The nickname “Red Light Bandit” referred to his reported use of a red light (or flashlight) during home invasions, which became a defining element in press accounts.[^1]

## Crimes and Modus Operandi

Sources describe a pattern of residential robberies and assaults in which victims were threatened with weapons and sometimes killed. While he is widely labeled a serial killer, much of the public notoriety came from the volume of robberies and the sensational way the crimes were reported in newspapers at the time.[^1]

The case also reflects how a single suspect can become a symbol for broader anxieties about modernization, inequality, and public safety in a major city.[^1]

## Arrest and Prosecution

Pereira da Costa was arrested in 1967 and later tried for multiple crimes, including killings connected to the robbery series. He received an aggregate sentence reported as 351 years in prison, a figure that is often cited in summaries of the case.[^1]

Brazil’s legal system does not typically result in imprisonment for a literal “351 years,” but large cumulative sentences are sometimes used to represent the totality of convictions across many charges.[^1]

## Cultural Impact

The “Bandido da Luz Vermelha” label entered Brazilian popular culture and has been referenced in journalism and creative works. The blend of real violence, tabloid storytelling, and urban myth-making contributed to his status as a folk criminal figure, even as the underlying case involved severe harm to victims.[^1]

## Later Life

Public reporting has periodically revisited his imprisonment and subsequent status, though details can be inconsistent across retellings. When summarizing the case, it is common to treat his long sentence and the legend-like nickname as the central points rather than a complete biographical chronology.[^1]
