## Summary

Francois Jacob shared the 1965 Nobel Prize for discovering genetic regulation through the operon model.

## Additional Context

- Jacob was trained as a physician and served in the Free French forces during World War II before moving into laboratory research.
- At the Pasteur Institute, he collaborated closely with Jacques Monod; their work connected genetics to molecular mechanisms of gene expression.
- The operon model provided a general framework for how cells switch genes on and off in response to environmental signals.

## Legacy

Jacob’s ideas helped establish molecular biology as a unified discipline linking DNA, RNA, proteins, and regulation. The operon concept became a standard teaching model and influenced later research on regulatory networks in more complex organisms.

## Key Ideas (High Level)

- Gene expression can be regulated by proteins that respond to signals and bind DNA.
- Cells can coordinate multiple genes as a unit to efficiently manage metabolic pathways.
- Regulation is not just “on/off” but can be tuned by conditions and feedback.

These principles generalized beyond bacteria and became part of the conceptual toolkit for modern genetics and systems biology.
