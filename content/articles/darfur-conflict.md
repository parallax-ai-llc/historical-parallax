## Summary

Ongoing conflict in Darfur since 2003 between Sudanese government-backed Janjaweed militia and rebel groups. Over 300,000 killed and 2.5 million displaced in what the US called genocide.

## Background

The Darfur conflict is commonly described as involving a combination of political marginalization, competition over land and resources, and the militarization of local disputes.

It unfolded within Sudan’s broader pattern of center–periphery tensions and overlapping civil conflicts.

## Humanitarian Dimension

The conflict generated large-scale displacement and humanitarian need, including:

- large numbers of internally displaced people (IDPs),
- cross-border refugee flows,
- and repeated challenges to aid delivery due to insecurity and restrictions.

Independent estimates of deaths and displacement vary by source and methodology, and casualty counts have been politically contested.

## International Response

International responses included diplomatic initiatives, peace processes, and the deployment of peacekeeping missions.

The ICC proceedings relating to Sudan’s leadership made Darfur a prominent case in debates over international criminal justice, state sovereignty, and enforcement.

## Continuing Relevance

Darfur remains a reference point for discussions about atrocity prevention, the limits of external intervention, and the long-term social and economic impacts of protracted conflict.
