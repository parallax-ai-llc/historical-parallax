## Case Overview ("Thomas Quick")

Bergwall became internationally known under the name **Thomas Quick**, a persona used during a period when he was treated in secure psychiatric care in Sweden. While hospitalized, he gave extensive confessions to a series of unsolved murders. Swedish courts convicted him of multiple killings based largely on these confessions, and the case later became one of the most discussed examples of how investigatory and therapeutic dynamics can interact with criminal proceedings.

Key features often highlighted in accounts of the scandal include:

- **Confessions given in a treatment setting**, with therapists and investigators conducting repeated interviews.
- **Limited physical/forensic corroboration** for several of the alleged crimes.
- A later reassessment that emphasized the risks of **confirmation bias** and of relying on confession evidence without strong independent support.

## Legal Proceedings and Exonerations

Between the 1990s and early 2000s, Bergwall was convicted in Sweden for several murders attributed to “Thomas Quick.” Years later he **retracted the confessions**, and the underlying investigations and trials were re-examined. One by one, the convictions were overturned, culminating in all convictions being set aside by 2013.

Because the public story changed dramatically over time, many summaries frame Bergwall less as a conventional “serial killer” profile and more as the central figure in a **wrongful-conviction** narrative that reshaped Swedish debates about evidence, interrogation practices, and expert influence.

## Notes for Readers

- Different sources emphasize different causes (therapeutic context, investigatory methods, evidentiary standards). For a neutral starting point, rely on court decisions and high-level summaries that distinguish **what was alleged** from **what was proven**.
