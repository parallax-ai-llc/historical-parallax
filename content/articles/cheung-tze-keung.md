## Summary

Hong Kong gangster known as Big Spender who carried out two of the largest kidnapping-for-ransom operations in history.

## Background

Cheung Tze-keung (often nicknamed “Big Spender” in English-language reporting) became known in the 1990s for high-value criminal operations that involved cross-border planning and the targeting of prominent individuals. Accounts of his early life and criminal associations differ by source, but he is generally described as operating in the Hong Kong–mainland China area.

## Kidnappings and related crimes

Cheung’s notoriety largely stems from major kidnapping-for-ransom cases in which relatives of wealthy business figures were abducted and ransoms were demanded and, according to some reports, paid. These cases attracted intense media attention and became part of the broader narrative of organized crime and security concerns during the period around Hong Kong’s 1997 handover.

## Arrest, trial, and execution

Cheung was arrested and prosecuted in mainland China. His case is often discussed as an example of how cross-border crimes can raise questions of jurisdiction and legal process when offenses involve Hong Kong residents and mainland authorities.

He was convicted and executed in 1998.

## Legacy

The case is frequently cited in discussions of personal security among high-net-worth individuals in the region, the role of organized crime networks, and the interaction between Hong Kong and mainland law enforcement in the late 1990s.
