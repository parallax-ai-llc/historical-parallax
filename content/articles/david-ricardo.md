## Summary

David Ricardo (1772–1823) was a British political economist who became one of the most influential figures in the history of classical economics. Rising from a career as a stockbroker to become a towering intellectual force, Ricardo developed rigorous theoretical frameworks for understanding wages, profits, rent, and international trade that shaped economic thought for generations.

His 1817 masterwork, *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*, systematized economic theory in ways that built upon and critiqued Adam Smith, establishing a tradition of deductive reasoning in economics. Ricardo's ideas were so formative that the dominant school of 19th-century economic thought is often called "Ricardian economics."

His theory of comparative advantage remains one of the most cited and enduring concepts in economics, providing the intellectual foundation for arguments in favor of free trade that persist to the present day.

## Early Life & Background

David Ricardo was born on April 18, 1772, in London, England, the third of seventeen children in a Sephardic Jewish family that had emigrated from the Netherlands. His father, Abraham Ricardo, was a successful stockbroker, and David entered the family business at age fourteen, receiving a practical financial education rather than a formal academic one.

At age 21, Ricardo converted to Quakerism and married Priscilla Anne Wilkinson, a decision that estranged him from his family. He struck out independently in the financial markets and proved enormously talented. By his early thirties he had accumulated a substantial fortune, largely through government bond speculation during the Napoleonic Wars.

His intellectual turn came around 1799 when he read Adam Smith's *The Wealth of Nations* while convalescing at Bath. The encounter ignited a lifelong passion for economic theory.

## Rise to Prominence

Ricardo began publishing economic pamphlets around 1809, starting with *The High Price of Bullion, a Proof of the Depreciation of Bank Notes* (1810), which argued that inflation was caused by the Bank of England's over-issuance of currency. The pamphlet brought him to the attention of leading thinkers, including James Mill, who became a close friend and encouraged Ricardo to write his major theoretical work.

*Principles of Political Economy and Taxation* (1817) established Ricardo's reputation definitively. In 1819 he entered Parliament as a member for Portarlington, an Irish pocket borough, where he advocated for free trade, the abolition of the Corn Laws, and fiscal restraint.

## Key Achievements

Ricardo's most celebrated contribution is the **theory of comparative advantage**, which demonstrated that two countries could mutually benefit from trade even if one was absolutely more efficient at producing every good. His model using England and Portugal trading cloth and wine remains a staple of introductory economics curricula worldwide.

He also developed the **labor theory of value**, which held that the relative value of goods is determined primarily by the labor required to produce them. This framework was later adopted and transformed by Karl Marx. His **theory of differential rent** explained how landowners extract surplus value as population and cultivation expand to less fertile land, a concept that influenced both socialist and land-reform movements.

## Controversies & Criticism

Ricardo's method of "strong cases" — constructing abstract models with simplifying assumptions — drew criticism both in his time and later. Thomas Malthus, his close friend and intellectual sparring partner, persistently challenged Ricardo's deductive approach, arguing it was too detached from empirical reality.

His iron law of wages, which predicted wages would naturally tend toward subsistence level, proved overly pessimistic and was largely abandoned as living standards rose during industrialization. Later economists, including those of the marginalist revolution in the 1870s, argued that Ricardo's cost-of-production framework neglected the role of demand and utility in determining value.

## Legacy & Influence

Ricardo died on September 11, 1823, at age 51 from an ear infection that spread to his brain. Despite his short career, his influence was profound. John Stuart Mill, Karl Marx, and Alfred Marshall all engaged deeply with his work. The Ricardian school dominated British economic policy debates through the mid-19th century.

His advocacy helped shift opinion against the Corn Laws, which were eventually repealed in 1846, more than two decades after his death. The theory of comparative advantage continues to underpin international trade policy and is consistently ranked among the most important ideas in the history of economics.
