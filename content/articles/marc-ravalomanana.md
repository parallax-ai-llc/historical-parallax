## Summary

Former President of Madagascar who used his presidency to expand his private dairy empire Tiko. Overthrown in 2009 coup and convicted in absentia of abuse of power.

## Additional Context

### Business background

Ravalomanana is frequently described as a prominent businessman-turned-politician. Before national office, he built a major presence in Madagascar’s food and retail sectors, which later became a central element in public debate about conflicts of interest during his presidency.

### Rise in politics

He entered politics as mayor of Antananarivo and then ran for president, taking office after a contested election process and political standoff. His political organization (often associated with the acronym **TIM**) became a major governing force during the 2000s.

### Governance record and criticism

Supporters point to development initiatives and efforts to strengthen anti-corruption infrastructure. Critics argue that economic gains were uneven, that the state favored allied business interests, and that political power became increasingly centralized.

### 2009 crisis and aftermath

The end of his presidency is closely tied to the 2009 political crisis and transfer of power, often described as a coup. Subsequent years included legal disputes, periods outside the country, and continued participation—directly or through allies—in Madagascar’s contested electoral politics.

### Why it matters

Ravalomanana’s career is often used as a case study in how business elites transition into political leadership, how state-building and growth narratives compete with governance concerns, and how political legitimacy can fracture during mass protest and security crises.

In modern Malagasy politics, his era remains a key reference point for both supporters and opponents.
