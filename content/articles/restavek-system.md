## Summary

Child domestic servitude system in Haiti where an estimated 300,000 children are sent to live with wealthier families as unpaid servants. Many face abuse, malnutrition, and denial of education.

## Terminology

The term **restavek** is commonly explained as deriving from Haitian Creole / French usage meaning "to stay with" (often glossed from *rester avec*). In practice, it refers to a child sent away from their family to live with another household, where the child is expected to perform domestic labor.

## Social and economic drivers (high-level)

Reporting on the restavek phenomenon typically points to:

- **extreme poverty** and lack of reliable income,
- limited access to schooling and basic services,
- family strategies meant to secure food, shelter, or education for a child.

While some placements may be framed as informal fostering, human-rights discussions emphasize that the arrangement can become **exploitative** when the child is unpaid, controlled, and prevented from attending school.

## Harms and rights concerns

Commonly cited risks include:

- physical and sexual abuse,
- malnutrition and overwork,
- social stigma and isolation,
- denial of education and healthcare.

Because the victims are children, the issue is frequently analyzed through the lens of **child labor**, **trafficking**, and **modern slavery** frameworks.

## Responses

Responses discussed in overviews include:

- public-awareness campaigns and community education,
- efforts to strengthen school access and social protection,
- NGO and international programs supporting vulnerable families,
- legal and policing initiatives aimed at child exploitation.
