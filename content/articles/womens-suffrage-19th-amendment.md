## Additional Context

## What the 19th Amendment Did

The 19th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution, ratified in 1920, prohibits federal and state governments from denying the right to vote on the basis of sex. It created a constitutional guarantee that women could not be excluded from voting solely because they were women.

## How Ratification Worked (High Level)

Constitutional amendments require approval by a two‑thirds vote in both houses of Congress and ratification by three‑fourths of the states. The final phase of the suffrage campaign combined long‑term organizing with targeted efforts in state legislatures.

## Limits and Ongoing Barriers

Although the amendment removed sex as a legal basis for denying the vote, many women—especially Black women and other marginalized groups—continued to face obstacles due to poll taxes, literacy tests, intimidation, and other forms of voter suppression until later civil rights reforms.

## Historical Significance

The amendment is widely treated as a pivotal expansion of democratic participation in the United States and a reference point for later debates about civil rights, equal protection, and political representation.
