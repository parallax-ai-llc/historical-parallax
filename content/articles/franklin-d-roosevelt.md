## Summary

Franklin Delano Roosevelt (1882–1945), commonly known as FDR, served as the 32nd President of the United States from 1933 until his death in 1945 — the only U.S. president elected to four terms. He assumed office during the depths of the Great Depression and led the nation through the majority of World War II, making him one of the most consequential leaders in American history.

Roosevelt's presidency fundamentally reshaped the relationship between the federal government and American citizens. His New Deal programs expanded government's role in economic management, established social safety nets, and created lasting institutions such as the Social Security Administration and the Securities and Exchange Commission. His wartime leadership forged the Allied coalition that defeated Nazi Germany and Imperial Japan.

Often ranked among the top three U.S. presidents alongside Washington and Lincoln, FDR's legacy is defined by his capacity to project optimism and confidence during successive national crises, and by the durable governmental frameworks he constructed that shaped American life for generations.

## Early Life & Background

Roosevelt was born on January 30, 1882, in Hyde Park, New York, into a wealthy, patrician family of Dutch descent. He was educated by private tutors and at Groton School before attending Harvard College, graduating in 1903, and Columbia Law School. In 1905 he married his distant cousin Eleanor Roosevelt, with whom he had six children (five surviving to adulthood).

In 1921, at age 39, Roosevelt contracted poliomyelitis, which paralyzed his legs permanently. Rather than withdrawing from public life, he spent years attempting rehabilitation at Warm Springs, Georgia, and learned to manage public appearances carefully — a physical struggle that many historians credit with deepening his empathy for the disadvantaged.

## Rise to Prominence

Roosevelt entered politics as a New York state senator in 1910. He served as Assistant Secretary of the Navy under President Woodrow Wilson from 1913 to 1920, gaining federal executive experience. He was the Democratic vice-presidential nominee in 1920, though the ticket lost. After his polio diagnosis, he staged a political comeback, winning the New York governorship in 1928 and re-election in 1930 by a large margin. His success in administering relief programs during the Depression positioned him for the 1932 presidential race, which he won over incumbent Herbert Hoover by 57% to 40% of the popular vote.

## Key Achievements

- **The New Deal (1933–1939):** A sweeping series of programs and reforms including the Civilian Conservation Corps (employed 3 million), the Works Progress Administration (8.5 million workers), the Social Security Act of 1935, the Banking Act, and the creation of the FDIC.
- **World War II Leadership:** Roosevelt steered Lend-Lease aid to Britain before U.S. entry, then led the Allied war effort after the December 7, 1941 attack on Pearl Harbor. He co-authored the Atlantic Charter with Churchill and coordinated strategy at the Tehran and Yalta Conferences.
- **Four Presidential Terms:** Elected in 1932, 1936, 1940, and 1944 — the only person to serve more than two terms, which prompted the 22nd Amendment (1951) limiting future presidents to two terms.

## Controversies & Criticism

Roosevelt's record carries significant moral blemishes. Executive Order 9066 (1942) authorized the forced internment of approximately 120,000 Japanese Americans — an action later acknowledged by the U.S. government as a grave injustice. His administration also largely failed to prioritize the rescue of European Jews during the Holocaust, declining to bomb rail lines to Auschwitz despite available intelligence. Domestically, his 1937 "court-packing" plan — proposing to add up to six justices to the Supreme Court — was widely condemned as an assault on judicial independence and defeated in Congress.

## Legacy & Influence

Roosevelt died on April 12, 1945, in Warm Springs, Georgia, just weeks before Germany's surrender. He left a government transformed: the federal bureaucracy, the regulatory state, and the social insurance system he built became the architecture of modern American governance. His rhetorical style — the Fireside Chats, the phrase "the only thing we have to fear is fear itself" — established a template for presidential communication. Scholars consistently rank him among the greatest American presidents, and his image appears on the U.S. dime.
