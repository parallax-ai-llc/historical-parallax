## Summary

Stanley Prusiner is an American neurologist who won the 1997 Nobel Prize for discovering prions.

## What is a prion?

A prion is an infectious agent proposed to be composed primarily of misfolded protein rather than DNA or RNA. In prion diseases, abnormal protein conformations can induce normal versions of the protein to misfold, leading to progressive neurodegeneration.

## Scientific significance

Prusiner’s work challenged prevailing assumptions about the nature of infection and heredity in biology. The idea that a protein could propagate a disease state was controversial for years, but it reshaped research into several fatal brain disorders.

## Related diseases

Prion research is closely linked with human conditions such as Creutzfeldt–Jakob disease and kuru, as well as animal diseases including scrapie and bovine spongiform encephalopathy (commonly known as “mad cow disease”). Understanding prion mechanisms has also informed broader research on protein misfolding in neuroscience.

## Legacy

The prion concept influenced diagnostic approaches, laboratory safety practices, and public-health responses to outbreaks involving transmissible spongiform encephalopathies. It also remains an important reference point in debates about how scientific paradigms shift under strong empirical pressure.
