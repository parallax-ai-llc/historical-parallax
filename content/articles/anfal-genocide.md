## Summary

Iraqi governments systematic campaign against Kurds in 1986-1989 that killed 50,000-182,000 people. It included the use of chemical weapons at Halabja which killed 5,000.

## Overview

The Anfal campaign was conducted by the Iraqi government during the late stages of the Iran–Iraq War and is commonly associated with the Ba'athist state's efforts to destroy Kurdish rural life and armed resistance. Operations combined conventional military assaults with mass detentions and the destruction of villages.

## Methods and Targets

Reporting on the campaign describes a range of coercive and violent tactics, including:

- **forced displacement** and the depopulation of rural areas,
- **mass arrests** and disappearances,
- **execution sites** and mass graves,
- systematic **village destruction** and property confiscation,
- the use of **chemical weapons**, most infamously at Halabja.

## Aftermath and Accountability

In the post-2003 period, Iraqi courts prosecuted senior officials for crimes connected to the campaign. The Anfal has been recognized by multiple observers and institutions as a genocide against the Kurdish population, and it remains a central reference point in discussions of transitional justice in Iraq.

## Memory and Documentation

Documentation about Anfal draws on survivor testimony, human-rights investigations, and forensic work at mass grave sites. The event continues to shape Kurdish political identity and debates over state violence and minority protection in the region.
