## Summary

Indian counterfeiter who ran the biggest stamp paper counterfeiting scam in Indian history worth an estimated $6 billion. He forged government stamp papers and distributed them through corrupt officials.

## Overview

Abdul Karim Telgi (1955–2017) was an Indian figure associated with one of the country’s most widely reported financial frauds: the counterfeit stamp paper scam. He became known for orchestrating a large-scale operation involving forged stamp papers used in legal and commercial transactions, a scheme that exposed vulnerabilities in procurement and verification systems.

## The stamp paper scam (high-level)

- **What stamp paper is:** In India, stamp papers are used as proof of payment of certain taxes and fees for legal documents and agreements.
- **How the fraud worked:** Forged or illegally produced stamp papers were distributed to intermediaries and end users, allowing documents to appear properly stamped while evading lawful payments.
- **Institutional impact:** The scandal prompted investigations, prosecutions, and reforms in how stamp duties and document authentication are handled, including increased digitization in some jurisdictions.

## Aftermath

The case is frequently cited in discussions of corruption, regulatory enforcement, and the modernization of state revenue systems tied to legal documentation.
