## Summary

Coordinated suicide bombings in Sri Lanka on Easter Sunday 2019 that killed 269 people at churches and hotels. The attacks were carried out by local ISIS-affiliated militants.

## Context

- The 2019 Easter attacks occurred against a backdrop of post–civil war Sri Lanka, with ongoing concerns about extremist networks and communal tensions.
- Investigations described the perpetrators as members of a local Islamist militant group (commonly reported as National Thowheeth Jama’ath), with inspiration and/or links claimed by the Islamic State (ISIS).

## Targets and casualties

- The bombings struck Christian churches during Easter services and international hotels in Colombo and nearby areas.
- Victims included Sri Lankan nationals as well as foreign visitors; casualty counts were widely reported in the hundreds killed and many more injured.

## Immediate aftermath

- The government imposed emergency measures, including curfews and temporary restrictions on social-media platforms, citing public-order and misinformation risks.
- Security operations and raids followed, with authorities seeking remaining suspects and explosives.

## Investigations and accountability

- A major public issue was whether security services had acted on prior intelligence warnings.
- Subsequent inquiries and political debate focused on intelligence coordination, leadership responsibility, and reforms to prevent similar attacks.

## Legacy

- The attacks significantly affected Sri Lanka’s sense of security and had economic impacts, including on tourism.
- They remain a key reference point in discussions of counterterrorism policy, interfaith relations, and crisis governance in Sri Lanka.
