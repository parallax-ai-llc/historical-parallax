## Summary

Doug McMillon is an American business executive who has served as President and Chief Executive Officer of Walmart Inc. since 2014, making him one of the most influential figures in global retail. Under his leadership, Walmart has navigated a dramatic transformation from a traditional brick-and-mortar retailer into a technology-driven omnichannel enterprise, competing directly with Amazon and other e-commerce giants.

McMillon's tenure has been defined by bold strategic investments in digital infrastructure, supply chain innovation, and workforce development. He has consistently championed the role of large corporations in addressing social issues, including minimum wage increases and sustainability commitments, positioning Walmart as a socially engaged Fortune 1 company. His influence extends beyond retail, as he has served in prominent roles with the Business Roundtable and other influential policy organizations.

## Early Life & Background

Douglas McMillon was born on January 17, 1966, in Memphis, Tennessee, and grew up in Bentonville, Arkansas — the hometown of Walmart's global headquarters. His proximity to Walmart's founding culture shaped his career from an early age. He attended the University of Arkansas, earning a Bachelor of Science in Business Administration in 1989, and later completed an MBA from the University of Tulsa in 1991.

McMillon's connection to Walmart began as a teenager: at age 17, he worked as a summer associate unloading trucks at a Walmart distribution center. This ground-level experience instilled an operational perspective that would define his management philosophy throughout his career.

## Rise to Prominence

After completing his MBA, McMillon rejoined Walmart in 1990 as a buyer in the company's merchandising division. He rose steadily through the ranks, holding leadership roles across multiple business segments. In 2005, he was appointed CEO of Sam's Club, Walmart's membership warehouse division, where he modernized the membership model and improved profitability.

In 2009, McMillon took over as CEO of Walmart International, overseeing the company's operations in more than 25 countries. His success in managing complex, diverse global markets made him the clear successor to outgoing CEO Mike Duke. On February 1, 2014, McMillon was named President and CEO of Walmart Inc., becoming only the fifth CEO in the company's history.

## Key Achievements

McMillon's most significant achievement has been Walmart's e-commerce transformation. He spearheaded the $3.3 billion acquisition of Jet.com in 2016, which accelerated Walmart's digital capabilities and brought key technology talent into the organization. By fiscal year 2024, Walmart's global e-commerce sales exceeded $100 billion annually.

He also raised Walmart's minimum starting wage to $15 per hour by 2021 and announced further increases to $14–$19 depending on store location by 2024 — affecting over 1.4 million U.S. associates. Under his leadership, Walmart launched Walmart+, a subscription membership service, in 2020 to compete with Amazon Prime. McMillon served as chair of the Business Roundtable from 2020 to 2021, where he signed the landmark 2019 Statement on the Purpose of a Corporation, moving away from pure shareholder primacy.

## Controversies & Criticism

McMillon and Walmart have faced criticism over labor practices, including allegations that wage increases were insufficient relative to Walmart's profit levels and executive compensation. The company has also drawn scrutiny over its role in the opioid crisis: in 2022, Walmart reached a $3.1 billion settlement with U.S. federal and state governments related to its pharmacy operations dispensing opioid prescriptions. Critics have questioned whether McMillon's public stances on social issues translate into substantive internal change at scale.

## Legacy & Influence

McMillon has repositioned Walmart as a technology and logistics company that competes on speed, data, and supply chain efficiency — not merely on price. His leadership during the COVID-19 pandemic, when Walmart served as a critical supplier of essential goods and a major vaccine distribution site, burnished the company's reputation as a national institution. His emphasis on associate investment and corporate purpose has influenced the broader conversation about the responsibilities of Fortune 500 CEOs in the 21st century.
