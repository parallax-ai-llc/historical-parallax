## Summary

Roman governor of Judea who ordered the crucifixion of Jesus Christ and other mass executions.

## Background and Office

- **Title/role:** Pilate served as the Roman prefect (often loosely called “governor”) of Judaea, a smaller imperial province under the broader authority of the governor of Syria.
- **Approximate tenure:** Commonly dated to **26–36 CE**, during the reign of Emperor Tiberius.
- **Primary responsibility:** Maintaining order, administering justice, collecting revenues, and representing Roman authority in Jerusalem and the surrounding region.

## Historical Sources

Pilate is attested in multiple source types:

- **Roman/Jewish historians:** Josephus and Philo describe incidents involving Pilate’s administration and conflicts with local customs.
- **Christian texts:** The canonical Gospels and Acts include Pilate as the Roman official involved in Jesus’s trial and execution.
- **Archaeology:** The *Pilate Stone* (an inscription from Caesarea Maritima) provides material evidence for his office and name.

## Actions as Prefect

Accounts portray Pilate as a hardline administrator who sometimes provoked local unrest:

- Disputes over **imperial symbols** and **Jerusalem sensitivities** are described in ancient narratives as triggers for confrontation.
- Josephus records episodes involving **public works** and funding decisions that were perceived as violations of religious norms.
- Later tradition suggests Pilate was ultimately **recalled** or removed from office after a violent suppression of a Samaritan movement, though details vary by source.

## Role in the Trial of Jesus (High-Level)

- In the New Testament narrative, Pilate presides over proceedings that result in Jesus’s condemnation.
- Roman crucifixion functioned as a public, deterrent punishment; Pilate’s order is therefore central to the execution as an act of state power.
- The Gospel accounts differ in emphasis and detail, and historians treat them as theological texts that also contain historical claims.

## Legacy and Cultural Impact

- Pilate became a prominent figure in Christian memory through creeds and liturgy (“suffered under Pontius Pilate”).
- Later literature and art variously depict him as a cynical bureaucrat, a conflicted judge, or a symbol of imperial injustice.
- His name remains associated with debates about **responsibility**, **law**, and **political violence** in antiquity.

## Notes on Dates and Labels

- Exact birth/death years are **not securely known**; many modern summaries use approximate ranges.
- “Prefect” and “procurator” are sometimes both used in secondary writing; the terminology reflects evolving Roman administrative titles and later usage.
