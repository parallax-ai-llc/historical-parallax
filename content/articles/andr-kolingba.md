## Background

André Kolingba was a career officer in the Central African armed forces. He is commonly associated with the Yakoma ethnic group, a fact that later shaped perceptions of patronage and exclusion under his rule.

## Rule in the Central African Republic

Kolingba came to power through a military coup in 1981 and led the country for much of the 1980s. His government operated as a military-backed regime and is often described in sources as authoritarian, with strong control over political life and security institutions.

A recurring theme in accounts of his presidency is the role of elite networks within the army and state administration, including allegations of preferential treatment for allies and co-ethnics. Opposition activity was constrained, and political competition was limited for extended periods.

## Transition and Later Political Activity

In the early 1990s, the Central African Republic moved toward multiparty politics. Kolingba ultimately left office after elections, but he remained an important political actor and later sought to regain power.

After losing influence, he was linked to a failed coup attempt in 2001. He spent periods outside the country and continued to be referenced in domestic politics primarily through the lens of past military rule and ethnic factionalism.

## Legacy

Kolingba’s legacy is generally discussed in relation to the militarization of politics in the Central African Republic, the use of security forces to sustain governing coalitions, and the long-term consequences of patronage and ethnic polarization for fragile state institutions.
