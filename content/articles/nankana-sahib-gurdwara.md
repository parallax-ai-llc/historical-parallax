## Summary

Nankana Sahib is the birthplace of Guru Nanak Dev Ji (1469–1539 CE), the founder of
Sikhism and one of the most spiritually influential figures in the history of South Asia.
Located approximately 75 kilometers west of Lahore in the Punjab province of Pakistan, the
city of Nankana Sahib (historically known as Rai Bhoi di Talwandi) has been a major
pilgrimage destination for Sikhs since Guru Nanak's lifetime and remains the most
significant Sikh sacred site in Pakistan.

The gurdwara complex at Nankana Sahib contains several individual gurdwaras — Sikh places
of worship — each associated with specific events in Guru Nanak's early life. The central
and most important is Gurdwara Janam Asthan ("the place of birth"), built on the site
where Guru Nanak was born. Together, the gurdwaras of Nankana Sahib form a sacred complex
that draws hundreds of thousands of Sikh pilgrims annually from India, Pakistan, and the
global Sikh diaspora.

Sikhism, with approximately 25 to 30 million adherents worldwide, is the fifth-largest
religion in the world by number of followers. Although the majority of Sikhs today live in
the Punjab state of India, the historical heartland of Sikh tradition — including most of
the founding Gurus' birthplaces and many of the holiest Sikh shrines — lies in what is now
Pakistan following the partition of British India in 1947. Nankana Sahib is thus not
merely a sacred site but a symbol of a profound historical rupture and of the Sikh
community's ongoing relationship with a homeland that was divided by partition.

## History & Origins

Guru Nanak Dev Ji was born on April 15, 1469 CE (some sources cite October 20, 1469,
following a later 18th-century tradition) in the village of Rai Bhoi di Talwandi, in the
Punjab region of the Indian subcontinent. His father was Mehta Kalu, a Hindu accountant
employed by a Muslim landlord; his mother was Mata Tripta. Even in childhood, according to
the hagiographic accounts collected in the Janam Sakhis (life stories), Nanak displayed
unusual spiritual insight and a nature that transcended ordinary religious boundaries.

At age thirty, Guru Nanak had the foundational spiritual experience that launched his
mission. While bathing in the Vein river, he was drawn into the divine presence —
described as being taken to "God's court" — and received the calling to spread the divine
message. Upon returning from this experience after three days, he uttered the declaration
that has become fundamental to Sikh theology: "There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim" —
meaning that true spirituality transcends religious labels and identities.

For the next twenty-four years, Guru Nanak undertook four great journeys (Udasis) across
South Asia and beyond — traveling to Haridwar, Varanasi, Ayodhya, Jagannath Puri, Sri
Lanka, Mecca, Medina, Baghdad, and many other sacred centers of multiple religious
traditions — engaging in spiritual dialogues and challenging religious formalism wherever
he went. He eventually settled at Kartarpur (also in modern Pakistan) and founded the
first Sikh community (sangat), where he lived until his death in 1539 CE.

The village of Rai Bhoi di Talwandi began to be called "Nankana Sahib" — "the honored
place of Nanak" — in recognition of its association with the Guru's birth. A gurdwara was
established at his birth site during his own lifetime or shortly thereafter. The history
of the site through the Mughal period is intertwined with the broader history of the Sikh
community's complex relationship with Mughal authority — periods of patronage alternating
with severe persecution.

During the Sikh Confederacy period (18th century) and then under the rule of Maharaja
Ranjit Singh (r. 1801–1839 CE), who founded the Sikh Empire, Nankana Sahib received
substantial royal patronage. Ranjit Singh ordered the rebuilding and expansion of the main
Gurdwara Janam Asthan, and the gilded dome that now defines the complex's skyline dates
essentially to this period of Sikh imperial patronage.

In 1921 CE, a major tragedy occurred at Nankana Sahib: the Sikh caretaker of the gurdwara,
Narain Das (associated with the hereditary Mahant system), ordered a massacre of Sikh
pilgrims who had gathered to protest mismanagement of the shrine. The massacre, known as
the Nankana Sahib massacre, claimed approximately 100 to 130 Sikh lives and became a
catalyst for the Gurdwara Reform Movement, which led to the establishment of the Shiromani
Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee (SGPC) — the elected body that has administered Sikh shrines
in India since the Sikh Gurdwaras Act of 1925.

The Partition of British India in 1947, which created the nations of India and Pakistan,
had a devastating effect on Nankana Sahib and on the broader Sikh community. Nankana
Sahib, located in what became Pakistan, was abandoned by virtually the entire Sikh
population as millions of Sikhs and Hindus fled west into India while millions of Muslims
fled east into Pakistan. The gurdwara complex was placed under the administration of the
government of Pakistan (later specifically under the Evacuee Trust Property Board, and
subsequently managed jointly with the SGPC in India through a bilateral arrangement).

## Religious Significance

For Sikhs, Nankana Sahib holds a significance analogous to Bethlehem for Christians — it
is the birthplace of the faith's founder and the most intimate geographical connection to
Guru Nanak's divine mission. The Sikh faith teaches that Waheguru (God) — the one formless
divine reality — revealed the path of righteousness (Dharam) most fully through the Ten
Gurus, beginning with Guru Nanak. To be at the place where that first Guru was born is to
be in contact, through space, with the origin of the living divine Word (Shabad Guru) that
is the heart of the Sikh tradition.

The ten gurdwaras at Nankana Sahib commemorate different episodes in Guru Nanak's early
life, making the complex a devotional biography in sacred geography:

**Gurdwara Janam Asthan** — The central gurdwara, marking the Guru's birthplace. The main hall houses the Guru Granth Sahib Ji (the Sikh sacred scripture, itself revered as the eternal living Guru) and is the focus of daily prayer (nitnem) and congregational worship (diwan).

**Gurdwara Patti Sahib** — Marks the place where Guru Nanak's first teacher, Gopal Pandha, recognized his extraordinary gifts. According to tradition, Nanak wrote a verse on his slate (patti) that expressed the highest spiritual wisdom — astonishing his teacher.

**Gurdwara Kiara Sahib** — Associated with an episode in which Guru Nanak, put to work tending cattle, let them wander into a neighbor's field; when the neighbor complained, the crops were found to be miraculously undamaged.

**Gurdwara Tambu Sahib** — Marks the spot where Guru Nanak slept in the shade of a tree; according to tradition, the shadow of the tree miraculously did not move throughout the day.

**Gurdwara Bal Leela** — Commemorates the Guru's sacred childhood games in this area.

**Gurdwara Maal Ji Sahib** and **Gurdwara Kiari Sahib** — Associated with further episodes from Guru Nanak's youth.

The daily liturgical life of the gurdwara complex centers on the Guru Granth Sahib Ji,
which is installed in each gurdwara and read from continuously in the akhand path
(uninterrupted reading). Congregational worship involves the singing of kirtan (sacred
music), listening to the path (scriptural reading), and receiving langar — the community
meal that is one of Sikhism's most distinctive practices, expressing the equality of all
people before God regardless of caste, gender, or religion.

## Architecture & Description

The Gurdwara Janam Asthan complex is built on a large compound enclosed by white-painted
walls. The main building is a multi-storied structure in the Sikh gurdwara architectural
style developed during the Ranjit Singh period: a combination of Mughal and Rajput
architectural elements, with arched iwans, ornamental kiosks (chattris), and a prominent
gilded dome. The dome, sheathed in gold, is the visual anchor of the complex and is
visible from a considerable distance.

The main hall (darbar sahib) is a spacious room with marble flooring and the Guru Granth
Sahib Ji installed under a canopy (manji sahib) on a raised dais. The interior walls are
decorated with paintings depicting scenes from Guru Nanak's life.

The complex contains a sarovar (sacred tank) — a body of water used for ritual bathing, a
standard feature of major Sikh gurdwaras, reflecting the tradition that bathing in the
sacred tank carries spiritual merit. Pilgrims perform ishnaan (bathing) in the sarovar as
part of their pilgrimage.

The complex also contains extensive langar facilities — large community kitchens and
dining halls where free meals are served to all visitors regardless of faith, caste, or
background. The principle of langar, instituted by Guru Nanak himself, remains one of the
most powerful expressions of Sikh egalitarianism and service (seva).

## Pilgrimage & Visitors

The most important pilgrimage event at Nankana Sahib is the celebration of Guru Nanak's
birth anniversary — Gurpurab — which falls on the full moon of the Kartik month
(October-November in the Gregorian calendar). Hundreds of thousands of Sikh pilgrims from
India, many from other countries, and Pakistan's own small Sikh community gather at
Nankana Sahib for the celebrations. The festival involves three days of continuous kirtan
(sacred music), processions with the Guru Granth Sahib Ji, and the distribution of langar
on an enormous scale.

The Pakistan-India border, which stands between the majority of Sikh pilgrims (who live in
India) and Nankana Sahib (which lies in Pakistan), has been one of the most politically
sensitive borders in the world since 1947. Special visa arrangements for Sikh pilgrims
visiting Pakistan's holy sites have been negotiated bilaterally, and the Pakistani
government generally facilitates large-scale pilgrimage visits. However, access has
periodically been disrupted by political tensions between India and Pakistan.

A significant development for Sikh pilgrimage was the opening of the Kartarpur Corridor in
November 2019: a visa-free crossing between India and Pakistan specifically for Sikh
pilgrims visiting Gurdwara Darbar Sahib Kartarpur (where Guru Nanak spent his final years)
in Pakistan. This crossing, widely celebrated in the Sikh community, represents one of the
few instances of cross-border religious cooperation between the two countries.

## Modern Significance

Nankana Sahib exists at the intersection of religion, partition history, diaspora
identity, and India-Pakistan relations. For the global Sikh diaspora — which numbers
several million in the United Kingdom, Canada, the United States, and elsewhere — the
inability to freely visit the birthplace of their faith's founder represents a persistent
wound of partition.

The site is managed by the Evacuee Trust Property Board (ETPB) of Pakistan in coordination
with the SGPC in India. Diplomatic relations between India and Pakistan directly affect
the pilgrimage experience: during periods of tension, pilgrim visas are restricted; during
periods of warmer relations, access is facilitated.

There has been ongoing scholarly and community discussion about the long-term preservation
and administration of the Nankana Sahib complex and other Sikh shrines in Pakistan.
Questions of conservation, of the rights of religious minorities to administer their own
sacred sites, and of the meaning of sacred heritage in a divided land all converge at
Nankana Sahib.

For Pakistan's small Sikh minority — approximately 20,000 to 30,000 people, concentrated
in urban areas of Punjab — Nankana Sahib is a center of community life, worship, and
identity.

## Timeline

- **April 15, 1469 CE** — Guru Nanak Dev Ji born at Rai Bhoi di Talwandi (Nankana Sahib)
- **c. 1499 CE** — Guru Nanak's foundational spiritual experience at the Vein river; mission begins
- **1499–1521 CE** — Guru Nanak's four Udasis (journeys); visits to sacred sites across South Asia and beyond
- **1521–1539 CE** — Guru Nanak settles at Kartarpur; establishes the first Sikh community (sangat)
- **1539 CE** — Guru Nanak dies at Kartarpur
- **16th–18th century CE** — Gurdwaras established and maintained at Nankana Sahib under various patrons
- **1801–1839 CE** — Maharaja Ranjit Singh's Sikh Empire; major patronage and rebuilding at Nankana Sahib
- **1921 CE** — Nankana Sahib massacre; Sikh Reform Movement accelerates
- **1925 CE** — Sikh Gurdwaras Act; SGPC established to administer major Sikh shrines
- **1947 CE** — Partition of India; Nankana Sahib becomes part of Pakistan; Sikh population evacuates
- **1947–present** — Pakistan's ETPB administers the site; special pilgrimage arrangements negotiated with India
- **2019 CE** — Kartarpur Corridor opened; simplified access for Sikh pilgrims between India and Pakistan
