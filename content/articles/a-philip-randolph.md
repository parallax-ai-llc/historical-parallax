## Summary

A. Philip Randolph was an African American civil rights activist and labor leader who played a pivotal role in both the American labor movement and the civil rights movement of the twentieth century. He founded the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters in 1925, the first predominantly Black labor union to receive a charter from the American Federation of Labor. Randolph was a key organizer of the 1963 March on Washington, where Martin Luther King Jr. delivered his "I Have a Dream" speech. His decades of activism helped dismantle racial discrimination in the military, the federal workforce, and organized labor.

## Historical Significance

Randolph demonstrated that economic justice and racial equality were inseparable causes. By building the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters into a powerful union, he gave Black workers an organized voice at a time when they were systematically excluded from most labor organizations. His strategy of threatening mass protest to force presidential action—used successfully in 1941 and again in 1948—became a model for civil rights campaigning. His role in the 1963 March on Washington linked the labor movement directly to the struggle for civil rights, shaping the political coalition that helped pass the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965.
