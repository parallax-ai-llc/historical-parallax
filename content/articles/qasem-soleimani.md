## Summary

Iranian major general who commanded the Quds Force and was considered the second most powerful person in Iran. He directed proxy wars across the Middle East. Killed in a US drone strike in Baghdad. Iran designated as terrorism sponsor.

## Background and Rise

Soleimani served in the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) and gained prominence after the Iran–Iraq War, which shaped a generation of Iranian military leaders. Over time he became a central figure in Iran's external security strategy, especially through the Quds Force, the IRGC unit focused on overseas operations and liaison with allied militias.

## Quds Force and Regional Strategy

As commander, Soleimani was widely described as an architect of Iran's network of partners and proxy forces in places such as Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, and Yemen. Analyses of his role often emphasize that his influence combined military planning, intelligence coordination, and political relationships with non-state armed groups and state actors.

## International Designations and Controversy

Soleimani and the IRGC/Quds Force were subject to various international sanctions, and the U.S. government designated the IRGC as a foreign terrorist organization in 2019. Public narratives about Soleimani vary sharply: within Iran he was frequently portrayed as a defender of national security, while many governments and critics associated him with destabilizing regional interventions and abuses tied to allied forces.

## 2020 Killing and Aftermath

He was killed in January 2020 in a U.S. strike near Baghdad International Airport, an action that triggered a major escalation in U.S.–Iran tensions. The event led to retaliatory Iranian missile strikes on bases in Iraq housing U.S. personnel and intensified debate over the legality, strategic consequences, and norms surrounding targeted killings.

## Legacy

Soleimani remains a significant figure in contemporary Middle Eastern history because his career illustrates how states project power through a mix of conventional forces, advisory missions, and relationships with non-state actors. Discussions of his impact often focus on the long-term effects of proxy warfare, sectarian polarization, and regional security competition.

He is often discussed alongside broader IRGC institutional influence within Iran.
