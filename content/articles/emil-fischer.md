## Summary

Emil Fischer won the 1902 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for his work on sugar and purine syntheses. He determined the structures of many biological molecules.

## Additional Context

- **Core contributions**: Fischer’s work clarified the chemistry of carbohydrates (sugars) and purines, helping establish reliable synthetic pathways and structural understanding.
- **Fischer projection**: He introduced a widely used two-dimensional notation for representing stereochemistry in sugars and amino acids.

## Enzymes and Biochemistry

- The **lock-and-key model** (often associated with Fischer) provided an influential early explanation of enzyme specificity: why certain enzymes act on particular substrates.
- His work helped bridge organic chemistry and emerging biochemistry by connecting molecular structure to biological function.

## Legacy

- Fischer’s methods and conceptual tools became standard in organic and biological chemistry education.
- His research influenced later developments in understanding proteins, metabolism, and medicinal chemistry.

## Named Concepts and Methods

- **Fischer projection**: A standard representation for stereochemistry in organic chemistry.
- **Fischer indole synthesis**: A classic synthetic route to indoles.
- **Fischer–Speier esterification**: A commonly taught method for forming esters from carboxylic acids and alcohols under acidic conditions.

## Notes

- Fischer’s influence is felt both in laboratory technique (synthesis and representation) and in conceptual models linking structure to biological activity.
