## Summary

Jian Zihao, known professionally as Uzi, is a Chinese esports player widely regarded as one of the greatest AD Carry players in the history of League of Legends. Competing primarily for Royal Never Give Up (RNG), Uzi became synonymous with mechanical excellence, aggressive laning, and an unrelenting drive to win that captivated fans across the world.

Over a career spanning nearly a decade, Uzi reached the World Championship finals twice, won the Mid-Season Invitational in 2018, and claimed a gold medal at the 2018 Asian Games — a landmark moment for esports recognition. His career was marked by both extraordinary highs and persistent health struggles, making him one of the most compelling figures in competitive gaming history.

## Early Life & Background

Jian Zihao was born on April 5, 1997, in Yichang, Hubei, China. He developed an early passion for gaming and began playing League of Legends competitively at a remarkably young age. By 16, he had already entered the professional scene, forgoing a traditional education path to pursue esports full-time — a decision that was uncommon in China at the time but would prove transformative for his career.

## Rise to Prominence

Uzi burst onto the international stage at the 2013 World Championship, where he led Royal Club to the grand finals as a 16-year-old rookie. Though they fell to SK Telecom T1, Uzi's aggressive Vayne play and mechanical prowess immediately established him as a world-class talent. He returned to the World Championship finals in 2014 with Star Horn Royal Club, again finishing as runner-up against Samsung White.

After several roster changes and a brief stint with OMG in 2015, Uzi joined Royal Never Give Up in 2016, where he would spend the most productive years of his career. Despite consistent domestic success, the World Championship title remained elusive — a narrative that defined much of his career and endeared him to fans who admired his perseverance.

## Key Achievements

Uzi's crowning achievements came in 2018. At the Mid-Season Invitational, he led RNG to victory over Kingzone DragonX, securing the first MSI title for a Chinese team. Weeks later, he represented China at the 2018 Asian Games in Jakarta, winning the gold medal in League of Legends — an exhibition event that carried significant national pride. He won multiple LPL titles throughout his career and was a perennial All-Pro selection. His Vayne, Ezreal, and Kai'Sa play set the standard for the AD Carry position globally.

## Controversies & Criticism

Uzi's career was shadowed by recurring health issues, most notably Type 2 diabetes diagnosed during his playing years, as well as chronic hand and wrist injuries attributed to the physical demands of professional gaming. He announced his initial retirement in June 2020 at the age of 23, citing these health concerns. His retirement sparked broader conversations about player welfare and the physical toll of esports careers. Uzi attempted a comeback in 2022 with Bilibili Gaming, but the return was short-lived and met with mixed results, leading some to question whether the comeback was premature given his health.

## Legacy & Influence

Uzi's legacy extends beyond his individual achievements. He helped popularize League of Legends esports in China and inspired a generation of AD Carry players across the LPL and beyond. His mechanical skill and teamfight positioning remain benchmarks for the role. The narrative of his career — extraordinary talent paired with heartbreaking near-misses at Worlds — resonates as one of esports' most compelling stories. His 2018 Asian Games gold medal also played a role in legitimizing esports as a competitive discipline in the eyes of traditional sports organizations across Asia.
