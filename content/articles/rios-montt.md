## Summary

José Efraín Ríos Montt (1926–2018) was a Guatemalan army general and head of state who ruled Guatemala after a military coup in 1982. His short de facto presidency occurred during the most intense phase of the Guatemalan Civil War and is widely associated with counterinsurgency campaigns, mass atrocities against Indigenous communities (notably the Maya Ixil), and later landmark legal proceedings over genocide and crimes against humanity.

Key points (high-level):
- Came to power via a coup in March 1982 and led a military junta before becoming the dominant figure of the regime.
- Promoted an anti-communist, “national security” counterinsurgency approach, including scorched-earth tactics in conflict zones.
- After leaving power in 1983, remained influential in Guatemalan politics and later served as President of Congress.
- Faced major domestic and international scrutiny; was tried in Guatemala decades later in a case that became a precedent-setting human-rights prosecution.

## Background

- **Military career:** Ríos Montt rose through the Guatemalan armed forces and became identified with hardline counterinsurgency thinking during the Cold War era.
- **Political role before 1982:** He entered national politics and remained connected to power networks that shaped Guatemala’s response to guerrilla movements.

## De Facto Presidency (1982–1983)

During his rule, Guatemala was in an acute phase of civil conflict. Reporting and later judicial findings connected state forces and aligned patrols to widespread violence in rural highland regions.

Policies and characteristics often noted in summaries:
- **Counterinsurgency emphasis:** The state prioritized defeating guerrilla groups, particularly in areas suspected of supporting insurgents.
- **Scorched-earth operations:** Military campaigns included village destruction, displacement, and targeted violence against civilians in some regions.
- **Civil patrol structures:** Expansion of local militia/patrol systems is commonly cited as part of the state security strategy.

## Human Rights Allegations and Legal Proceedings

Ríos Montt became one of the most prominent former heads of state in Latin America to face domestic prosecution for atrocities committed during civil conflict.

- **Charges:** The most cited case concerned genocide and crimes against humanity against the Maya Ixil population.
- **2013 verdict and aftermath:** A Guatemalan court convicted him in 2013; the Constitutional Court later annulled the conviction and ordered legal proceedings to roll back to an earlier procedural stage.
- **Broader significance:** The case is frequently referenced as a landmark test of transitional justice and accountability in Guatemala.

## Legacy

Assessments of Ríos Montt remain sharply divided:
- Supporters have portrayed him as a decisive anti-insurgency leader.
- Critics and many human-rights organizations emphasize the scale of civilian harm and the long-term trauma and displacement tied to counterinsurgency campaigns.
- In historical discussions of the Guatemalan Civil War, his rule is often used as a focal point for debates about state responsibility, impunity, and reconciliation.
