## Summary

Haitian dictator known as 'Papa Doc' who ruled from 1957 to 1971. He maintained power through the Tonton Macoutes secret police who terrorized the population. An estimated 30,000-60,000 Haitians were killed during his rule.

## Additional Context

### Consolidation of power

Duvalier combined electoral legitimacy with authoritarian consolidation, leveraging patronage networks, political repression, and a pervasive security apparatus. The Tonton Macoutes (Milice de Volontaires de la Sécurité Nationale) operated as a paramilitary force that intimidated opponents and enforced regime loyalty.

### Ideology and symbolism

He drew on elements of Black nationalism and Haitian cultural symbolism, and was known for cultivating a personality cult. This mix of political messaging and fear-based control helped sustain his rule despite institutional weakness and economic hardship.

### Human rights impact

Accounts of his period in power frequently emphasize:

- Arbitrary detention and torture.
- Extrajudicial killings and disappearances.
- Exile of political opponents and suppression of independent institutions.

### Succession and legacy

Duvalier prepared for dynastic succession by declaring himself president for life, and he was followed by his son Jean-Claude Duvalier ("Baby Doc"). The Duvalier era remains a defining reference point in modern Haitian politics and debates over accountability.
