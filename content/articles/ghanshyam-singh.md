## Summary

Dacoit leader who operated in the ravines of Madhya Pradesh and Uttar Pradesh terrorizing villages for decades. He was known for targeting wealthy landowners.

## Context

In parts of north and central India, “dacoity” historically referred to organized banditry involving armed gangs operating in difficult terrain, including ravine networks that can provide cover and mobility. Such groups were often associated with kidnapping for ransom, raids, and intimidation campaigns.

## Activities and Reputation

Accounts describe Singh as a prominent figure among ravine-based gangs active along the Madhya Pradesh–Uttar Pradesh border region. He was portrayed as a long-running security challenge for local authorities and communities, with repeated police efforts to disrupt gang networks and recover abducted victims.

## Law Enforcement Response

Indian policing of dacoit groups has commonly involved specialized units, intelligence-led operations, and efforts to cut off local support networks. Public attention can intensify during high-profile incidents, leading to sustained operations and political pressure to restore order.

## End of Life

Singh was reported killed in a police encounter in 2005. In Indian media and policing terminology, an “encounter” typically refers to an armed confrontation leading to deaths or arrests.

## Broader Significance

The phenomenon of dacoity is often discussed in relation to regional inequality, local power structures, and the challenges of policing remote geographies. Cases like Singh’s are used as examples of how criminal networks can persist for years when terrain, fear, and weak institutions intersect.
