## Summary

Former Serbian intelligence officer convicted alongside Jovica Stanišić for aiding and abetting crimes in Bosnia. He helped establish and direct Serbian paramilitary units.

## Role and Case Context

- Simatović was linked to Serbia’s state security apparatus during the Yugoslav wars, a period marked by multiple conflicts and widespread atrocities.
- The case against him and Stanišić centered on support for paramilitary forces and the question of responsibility for crimes committed by units operating across borders.
- ICTY proceedings in such cases often involve complex chains of command and disputes over whether assistance constituted direct participation, aiding and abetting, or broader joint criminal enterprise theories.

## Significance

Convictions of senior security officials were viewed as important precedents for accountability beyond front-line perpetrators, highlighting how intelligence and security structures can enable violence indirectly through organization, training, and logistics.

## Key Takeaways

- International tribunals often devote years to establishing responsibility for indirect support roles.
- The Simatović case sits within a broader attempt to document and adjudicate crimes committed during the breakup of Yugoslavia.
- Such cases help clarify how “state resources” can be used to empower non-state armed groups.
