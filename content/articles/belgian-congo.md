## Summary

Belgian colonial rule over the Congo from 1908 to 1960 succeeded the Congo Free State and extracted vast resources through coercive labor systems. Economic development primarily served European interests while Congolese political rights were severely restricted.

## Governance and Administration

Belgian Congo was governed through a centralized colonial administration that combined state authority, missionary activity, and private economic enterprises.

Key structural features often highlighted include:

- limited political representation for Congolese people
- legal and social hierarchies organized around race and colonial status
- extensive use of "indirect" control through local chiefs under colonial oversight

## Economy and Labor

The colony's economy was shaped by extraction of minerals and agricultural commodities, supported by infrastructure such as railways and ports built to move goods to global markets.

Historical accounts frequently emphasize:

- coercive labor practices and harsh discipline in many sectors
- uneven investment that prioritized export capacity over local welfare
- profits accruing largely to Belgian firms and the metropolitan state

## Social Impact

Colonial rule profoundly transformed Congolese society through urbanization, mission education, and changes in land use.

At the same time, restrictions on association and speech limited organized political activity until late in the colonial period.

## Decolonization and Legacy

Independence in 1960 followed a rapid and contested transition, with limited preparation for broad-based self-government.

Debates about Belgian Congo remain central to discussions of:

- colonial violence and accountability
- long-run economic patterns tied to resource extraction
- the political instability that followed independence
