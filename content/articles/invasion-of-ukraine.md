## Summary

On February 24, 2022, Russia launched a full-scale military invasion of Ukraine, marking the largest conventional military conflict in Europe since World War II. Russian forces attacked from multiple directions — from Belarus in the north toward Kyiv, from Russia in the east and northeast, and from occupied Crimea in the south. The assault followed eight years of low-intensity conflict in eastern Ukraine that began with Russia's annexation of Crimea in 2014.

The invasion triggered a massive humanitarian catastrophe, displacing over 14 million Ukrainians and killing tens of thousands of civilians and combatants. It reshaped European security architecture, prompted NATO expansion, and set off a global energy and food supply crisis. As of early 2026, active combat continues across a largely static front line in eastern and southern Ukraine.

## Background & Causes

Russia's relationship with Ukraine deteriorated sharply after Ukraine's 2014 Euromaidan revolution, which ousted pro-Russian President Viktor Yanukovych. Russia responded by annexing Crimea in March 2014 and backing separatist forces in the Donbas region, sparking a conflict that claimed over 14,000 lives before 2022. NATO's eastward expansion since the 1990s was cited by Moscow as a core security grievance. Russian President Vladimir Putin published a July 2021 essay denying Ukrainian nationhood, framing Ukrainians and Russians as "one people." In late 2021, Russia massed over 100,000 troops near Ukraine's borders. Despite Western warnings and diplomatic efforts, no agreement was reached, and Putin recognized the Donetsk and Luhansk "People's Republics" as independent states on February 21, 2022, three days before the invasion.

## Key Figures

- **Vladimir Putin** — Russian President who ordered the invasion, framing it as a "special military operation" to "denazify" and demilitarize Ukraine.
- **Volodymyr Zelensky** — Ukrainian President who remained in Kyiv, becoming a global symbol of resistance and rallying international military and financial support.
- **Valery Zaluzhny** — Commander-in-Chief of Ukraine's Armed Forces through 2024, credited with key defensive and counteroffensive strategies.
- **Oleksiy Reznikov / Rustem Umerov** — Successive Ukrainian Defense Ministers overseeing weapons procurement.
- **Jens Stoltenberg** — NATO Secretary-General who coordinated alliance-wide support for Ukraine.

## Consequences & Impact

The invasion killed an estimated 50,000–100,000 soldiers on each side and over 10,000 Ukrainian civilians by mid-2024, per UN figures. More than 8 million Ukrainians fled abroad and 6 million were internally displaced. Russia faced sweeping Western sanctions targeting its energy, banking, and technology sectors. Finland and Sweden joined NATO in 2023–2024, a direct consequence of the invasion. Global wheat and energy prices spiked in 2022, causing food insecurity in Africa and the Middle East. Ukraine received over $200 billion in combined military, economic, and humanitarian assistance from Western nations through 2025.

## Perspectives

**Western/Ukrainian view:** The invasion is an unprovoked war of aggression violating international law and Ukrainian sovereignty, demanding collective defense and accountability for war crimes documented in Bucha, Mariupol, and elsewhere.

**Russian government view:** The operation was framed as defensive, aimed at protecting Russian-speaking populations in Donbas and preventing NATO encirclement, with Ukraine portrayed as a destabilizing Western proxy state.

**Global South perspective:** Many nations in Africa, Asia, and Latin America declined to condemn Russia at the UN, citing concerns about Western double standards on sovereignty and the economic impact of sanctions on developing nations.

**Historical analysts:** Many scholars contextualize the conflict within the post-Cold War security order's failure to integrate Russia while expanding NATO, though most conclude that Russia's resort to full-scale invasion was neither legally justified nor inevitable.
