## Summary

Russian-operated cryptocurrency exchange used to launder $4 billion including proceeds from the Mt. Gox hack. Operator Alexander Vinnik was arrested in Greece.

## Additional Context

- **Type:** Cryptocurrency exchange.
- **Reputation:** BTC-e became a recurring reference point in reporting about illicit finance in early crypto markets.

## How an Exchange Can Enable Illicit Finance (High-Level)

- **On/off ramps:** Exchanges connect crypto to traditional money systems, making them attractive for cashing out.
- **Account controls:** Weak customer verification and monitoring can allow repeated use by bad actors.
- **Market services:** Conversions between assets can be used to complicate tracing.

## Shutdown and Consequences

- **Law-enforcement action:** The platform’s closure is widely cited as a major enforcement milestone.
- **Operator allegations:** Public reporting connected BTC-e to laundering flows, including theft proceeds from major hacks.

## Regulatory Legacy

- **KYC/AML focus:** The case is frequently mentioned as part of the rationale for tightening compliance expectations for crypto businesses.
- **Jurisdiction challenges:** Exchanges operating across borders can exploit regulatory gaps, complicating enforcement.
- **Industry effect:** Cases like BTC-e contributed to a shift toward more formal compliance programs at major exchanges.
- **Risk lesson:** Poor controls at a single venue can amplify ecosystem-wide trust and enforcement risks.
