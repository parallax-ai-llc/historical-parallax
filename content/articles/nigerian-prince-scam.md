## Summary

Advance fee fraud scheme originating from Nigeria where victims are promised large sums in exchange for small upfront payments. Also known as 419 scams, they cost victims billions worldwide.

## How the Scam Works

Advance-fee fraud typically begins with an unsolicited message promising access to a large payment (inheritance, “frozen” funds, government contract proceeds) if the target provides help. The scammer then requests escalating “fees” for items such as taxes, legal paperwork, bank charges, or bribes—each framed as the final step before release of the money.

## “419” Name and Origins

The term “419 scam” refers to Section 419 of Nigeria’s criminal code, which addresses fraud. While the stereotype is tied to Nigeria, advance-fee techniques have appeared in many countries and evolved over time.

## Common Variants

Well-known variants include:

- Fake inheritance or lottery winnings
- Romance and relationship-driven advance-fee requests
- Business email compromise that uses corporate impersonation rather than “prince” narratives
- Overpayment schemes and fake escrow arrangements

## Why It Works

These schemes rely on social engineering: urgency, secrecy, authority cues, and the target’s desire to recover sunk costs. The back-and-forth communication is often designed to filter for victims willing to continue despite warning signs.

## Enforcement and Countermeasures

Law-enforcement agencies worldwide investigate advance-fee fraud, sometimes through coordinated operations and large-scale arrests. Practical defenses include skepticism toward unsolicited financial proposals, verifying identities through independent channels, and refusing to send money or sensitive documents to unknown parties.
