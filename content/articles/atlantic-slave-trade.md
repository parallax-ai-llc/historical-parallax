## Summary

The transatlantic slave trade forcibly transported an estimated 12.5 million Africans to the Americas over four centuries. An estimated 1.8 million died during the Middle Passage.

## Scope and Mechanics

The Atlantic slave trade was a system of forced migration and commodification that linked African regions, European empires, and plantation economies in the Americas. It is often described as part of a broader “triangular trade” in which ships carried manufactured goods to Africa, enslaved people to the Americas, and plantation commodities (such as sugar, tobacco, coffee, and cotton) to Europe.

## Major Routes and Actors (High-Level)

- **Departure regions:** West and West-Central Africa were among the major areas targeted by slavers, though patterns shifted over time.
- **Destination regions:** Enslaved people were transported to multiple colonies and states across the Caribbean, Brazil, and North America, with large flows to Portuguese and Spanish America and to plantation colonies in the Caribbean.
- **Imperial involvement:** Multiple European powers participated at different times, including Portuguese, Spanish, British, French, Dutch, and others, along with networks of merchants, shipowners, and colonial authorities.

## Human Impact

- **Middle Passage:** The ocean crossing involved extreme violence, coercion, and lethal conditions, contributing to significant mortality.
- **Family and social disruption:** Raids, warfare, and capture destabilized societies and tore apart families and communities.
- **Creation of new societies:** The trade forcibly reshaped demographics and contributed to the formation of African diaspora cultures across the Americas.

## Resistance and Abolition

- **Resistance:** Enslaved people resisted through flight, sabotage, the creation of maroon communities, and uprisings; resistance was a persistent feature of the system.
- **Legal abolition vs. practice:** Abolition often occurred in stages—first the trade, later slavery itself—and illegal trafficking continued in various forms after formal bans.
- **19th-century suppression:** Naval patrols and international treaties gradually increased enforcement against slave ships, though outcomes varied by region.

## Legacy

The Atlantic slave trade left enduring political, economic, and cultural consequences, including racialized systems of coercion in the Americas, long-term inequalities, and continuing debates over historical responsibility, memory, and reparative justice.
