## Summary

Leak of 715,000 documents exposed how Isabel dos Santos systematically plundered Angola's wealth. Western consultants including McKinsey, BCG, and PwC were implicated in enabling the corruption.

## Background

Luanda Leaks refers to a collaborative investigative journalism project that centered on the business empire of **Isabel dos Santos** (daughter of Angola’s long‑time president José Eduardo dos Santos) and the way politically connected wealth was reportedly built and protected through cross‑border corporate structures.[^2][^3]

## What the leak contained (high level)

Reporting on the project describes a document cache numbering in the **hundreds of thousands** that was analyzed by journalists and partners, including materials such as corporate records, emails, and financial/contractual documentation used to reconstruct ownership and deal flows.[^2]

Key themes described in coverage include:
- Use of **shell companies** and multi‑jurisdiction holding structures.
- Reliance on **professional service firms** (e.g., consultants, auditors, lawyers) as “enablers” in setting up and operating entities.[^2]
- Concentration of economic opportunities around **state-linked assets** (notably in telecom and energy), where governance decisions can create outsized private gains.

## Allegations and patterns described in reporting

The reporting frames the scandal as an example of **kleptocratic extraction**: public resources and privileged access being converted into private control, often via related‑party transactions, favorable financing, and opaque ownership chains.[^2]

Common mechanisms discussed in summaries and follow‑up coverage include:
- Preferential contracts and insider access to state assets.
- Related‑party lending and guarantees routed through intermediaries.
- Complex ownership structures that complicate oversight and recovery efforts.

## Reported responses and fallout

A few widely reported downstream developments (not exhaustive):
- **Dec 2019:** Angolan courts ordered freezes and seizures connected to dos Santos’ local assets and company stakes (as reported by major outlets and summarized on Wikipedia).[^3]
- **2021:** The U.S. State Department announced entry bans on dos Santos and immediate family members, citing significant corruption allegations.[^4]
- **2022:** International reporting described steps such as an Interpol arrest warrant and continued cross‑border legal action tied to asset recovery efforts.[^2][^5]
