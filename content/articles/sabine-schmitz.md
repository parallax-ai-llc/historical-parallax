## Summary

Sabine Schmitz (May 14, 1969 – March 16, 2021) was a German racing driver and television presenter widely regarded as one of the most accomplished drivers ever to tackle the Nürburgring Nordschleife. Known internationally as the "Queen of the Nürburgring," she was the only woman to have won the 24 Hours Nürburgring outright, achieving the feat twice — in 1996 and 1997. Her combination of fearless driving talent and infectious on-screen charisma made her one of motorsport's most beloved figures.

Beyond the racetrack, Schmitz became a cultural ambassador for the Nürburgring itself, working for years as a professional taxi driver on the circuit and appearing on BBC's *Top Gear*, where her exuberant personality reached a global audience of millions. She championed accessibility in motorsport, embodying the idea that the Nordschleife belonged to anyone willing to learn its 20.8 kilometers of unforgiving tarmac.

Her death from cancer in March 2021 prompted an outpouring of grief from the motorsport community worldwide, underscoring the unique and irreplaceable space she had occupied in the sport for more than two decades.

## Early Life & Background

Sabine Schmitz was born on May 14, 1969, in Adenau, a small town in the Eifel region of West Germany — just minutes from the Nürburgring. Growing up in a family that operated the Pistenklause restaurant adjacent to the circuit, she was immersed in racing culture from childhood. The track was effectively her backyard, and she began driving on the Nordschleife as a teenager, developing an intimate knowledge of its 154 corners that would later set her apart from virtually every other driver on earth.

## Rise to Prominence

Schmitz began competing seriously in the early 1990s and quickly distinguished herself in endurance racing. Her breakthrough came in 1996 when she co-drove a BMW M3 to overall victory at the 24 Hours Nürburgring — a grueling race widely considered among the most demanding in motorsport. She repeated the win in 1997, cementing her reputation as an elite endurance driver. Alongside her racing career, she worked as a professional driver for the Nürburgring's "Ring Taxi" service, piloting passengers around the track at speed in a modified Porsche 911.

Her international profile expanded dramatically after appearing on *Top Gear* in 2004, where she challenged presenter Jeremy Clarkson to a lap duel in a Ford Transit van. Her van lap of 10 minutes 8 seconds, driven with casual precision, captivated viewers globally and introduced her to an audience far beyond traditional motorsport. She became a recurring presence on the show and later joined the main presenting team during its troubled 2016–2017 series.

## Key Achievements

- Two-time overall winner of the 24 Hours Nürburgring (1996, 1997), the only woman to win the race outright
- Completed thousands of laps of the Nordschleife as a professional Ring Taxi driver over roughly two decades
- Appeared as a presenter and contributor on BBC *Top Gear* across multiple series
- Competed in the Nürburgring Langstrecken-Serie (VLN) and various touring car championships throughout her career
- Served as a prominent ambassador for the Nürburgring circuit and Eifel region motorsport tourism

## Controversies & Criticism

Schmitz's tenure as a co-presenter on the relaunched *Top Gear* in 2016–2017 drew mixed reviews; critics felt the show itself had lost direction, though Schmitz personally received sympathetic coverage. During this period she was already privately battling cancer, a fact that became public only later. No significant professional controversies attached to her racing career, which was largely celebrated for its consistency and sportsmanship.

## Legacy & Influence

Schmitz's legacy is threefold: as a racing pioneer who proved women could compete at the highest levels of endurance motorsport, as the definitive human face of the Nürburgring, and as a communicator who brought the visceral appeal of the Nordschleife to a mass global audience. The Nürburgring honored her memory with a dedicated tribute section, and tributes poured in from Formula 1 champions, manufacturers, and fans alike following her death at age 51 on March 16, 2021. She remains the standard by which Nürburgring mastery is measured.
