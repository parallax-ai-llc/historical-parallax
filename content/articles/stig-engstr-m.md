## Background

Engström was a Swedish graphic designer and communications worker. He was employed at the insurance company Skandia, whose offices were close to the location where Palme was killed.

## The Palme Assassination Context

On the night of 28 February 1986, Olof Palme was shot on a central Stockholm street after leaving a cinema. The assassination became one of Sweden’s most consequential unsolved crimes, generating decades of investigation, political controversy, and extensive media coverage.

Because Engström was in the vicinity and interacted with the aftermath, he entered the case both as a witness figure and, later, as a potential suspect. Accounts about his movements and claims about what he did at the scene were disputed over time.

## Investigation and 2020 Identification

In June 2020, the Swedish Prosecution Authority announced it would close the Palme investigation and named Engström as the most likely perpetrator. The announcement relied on a reconstruction of opportunity, proximity, and inconsistencies in earlier accounts, rather than on a trial-tested evidentiary record.

The decision was contentious: some observers argued the identification provided a plausible resolution to a long-running national trauma, while others criticized the closure as insufficiently supported given the absence of a court process and the fact that the suspect could not be questioned further.

## Significance

Engström’s posthumous identification illustrates the challenges of cold-case investigations where key witnesses and suspects die, physical evidence degrades, and earlier investigative decisions shape what can be proven later. The Palme case remains a reference point in Sweden for public trust in institutions, investigative accountability, and the long-term societal impact of political violence.

## Key Points

- Known publicly as “Skandiamannen” due to his proximity to the crime scene and workplace
- Identified as the main suspect only when prosecutors closed the case in 2020
- No criminal trial occurred; conclusions remain debated in Swedish public discourse
