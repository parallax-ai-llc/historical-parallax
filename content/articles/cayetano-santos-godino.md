## Summary

Argentine serial killer known as 'Petiso Orejudo' who murdered four children and attempted to kill several others in Buenos Aires between 1906 and 1912. He began his crimes at age 10 and was imprisoned for life.

## Background

Godino’s case occurred in early 20th-century Buenos Aires, a rapidly growing city with sharp social inequality and expanding police and judicial institutions. His youth at the time of the crimes has made the case a reference point in discussions about juvenile offending and criminal responsibility.

The nickname “Petiso Orejudo” (“short one with big ears”) reflects how popular press labels can become attached to notorious cases and persist in collective memory.

## Investigation and prosecution

Because several of the attacks involved children and occurred over multiple years, the case drew public attention and anxiety. His eventual arrest and interrogation were widely reported, and the trial became part of Argentina’s early criminological history.

Contemporary accounts and later summaries often note that psychiatric and behavioral interpretations were invoked, though the scientific frameworks of the period differ from modern standards.

## Imprisonment

Godino was sentenced to life imprisonment and transferred to the penitentiary in Ushuaia, sometimes described as one of Argentina’s harshest institutions at the time. His time in custody has been recounted as marked by persistent disciplinary problems and poor health.

## Legacy

The case is frequently cited in Argentine true-crime history and in broader debates about how societies respond to violent crimes committed by minors. It also illustrates how sensational reporting and public fear can influence the way a criminal episode is remembered.

Modern retellings often emphasize the limits of the era’s policing and forensic methods, which shaped both the investigation and the public narrative.
