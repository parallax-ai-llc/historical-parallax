## Summary

Korean Air executive known for the 'nut rage incident' when she forced a taxiing plane to return to the gate because macadamia nuts were served in a bag instead of on a plate. She was convicted of violating aviation safety law.

## Incident Details (High Level)

- The episode occurred on a Korean Air flight preparing for departure from New York in December 2014.
- It drew attention because the aircraft was already taxiing when it returned to the gate, and because the dispute centered on service protocol.

## Legal and Corporate Consequences

- The incident led to criminal proceedings and convictions under South Korean aviation-related laws.
- It also triggered internal and public scrutiny of corporate culture, workplace hierarchy, and employee treatment in large conglomerates.
- Public reporting at the time described resignations, disciplinary actions, and reputational damage for the airline.

## Significance

- “Nut rage” became a widely used shorthand in South Korea for perceived abuse of power by executives.
- The case is frequently discussed in the context of corporate governance reforms and consumer backlash.

## Legacy

- The incident helped popularize broader discussion of “gapjil” (abuse of power in hierarchical relationships) in workplaces.
- It remains a recurring example in aviation-industry compliance conversations about cockpit authority and operational decision-making.
- The phrase “nut rage” entered international news coverage as a symbol of executive misconduct.
