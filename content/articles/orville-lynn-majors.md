## Summary

American nurse who murdered at least 6 patients at Vermillion County Hospital in 1993-1995. Death rate increased 900% during his shifts.

## Background

Majors worked as a licensed practical nurse in rural Indiana. Prosecutors and investigators later focused on a sharp, sustained spike in patient deaths during the shifts when he was on duty, compared with normal hospital baselines.

## Modus Operandi (as described in court reporting)

- Victims were typically vulnerable patients under his care.
- Deaths were linked to injections of potassium chloride or other medications administered without a medical need.
- The pattern was alleged to be opportunistic: the acts were committed in the course of routine hospital work rather than through a separate, overt attack.

## Investigation and Prosecution

The case developed from internal concerns and law-enforcement review of death certificates, medication access, and shift records. At trial, the prosecution relied heavily on circumstantial evidence: timing correlations, access to drugs, and testimony about unusual events around the deaths.

## Sentence and Later Life

Majors was convicted and received a lengthy prison sentence. He later died while incarcerated.

## Why the Case Matters

The Majors case is often cited in discussions of:

- Patient-safety controls and medication handling in hospitals
- The difficulty of detecting caregiver-perpetrated harm
- The role of statistical signals (mortality spikes) in triggering clinical investigations
