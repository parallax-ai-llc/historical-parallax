## Summary

Demand for rhino horn in Asia has driven poaching to crisis levels. South Africa lost over 1,000 rhinos per year at peak; organized criminal networks control the trade.

## Drivers of Demand

Demand for rhino horn has been associated with status consumption and use in some traditional medicine markets, despite the lack of scientific evidence for many claimed medical benefits. Demand shocks can quickly translate into higher black-market prices and more poaching pressure.

## Trafficking and Criminal Networks

The modern trade is often described as transnational and highly organized:

- poaching operations typically rely on local facilitators and access to protected areas
- horns are moved through layered smuggling routes and laundered via falsified permits or intermediaries
- trafficking has been linked to broader illicit networks that also trade other wildlife products

## Policy and Enforcement

- **CITES restrictions:** international commercial trade in rhino horn has been restricted for decades under the CITES framework.
- **Domestic policy debates:** some range states have repeatedly debated regulated domestic trade versus total bans, arguing about incentives, enforcement capacity, and unintended consequences.
- **Anti-poaching measures:** strategies include ranger patrols, intelligence-led interdiction, surveillance technology, and (controversially) dehorning in some contexts.

## Conservation Impact

Sustained poaching pressure threatens small or recovering rhino populations, can destabilize conservation financing, and increases security costs for parks and private reserves.
