## Overview

The phrase “Deutsche Bank scandals” is used as a catch-all for a series of legal, regulatory, and compliance problems affecting Deutsche Bank over multiple years. These have included market-manipulation cases, weaknesses in anti–money laundering controls, and issues tied to mortgage and structured-finance products.

## Major Categories of Cases (High Level)

- **Benchmark and market misconduct:** Investigations and settlements related to benchmark-rate manipulation (including LIBOR-era cases) and other market conduct allegations.
- **Mortgage and securitization matters:** U.S. settlements connected to the sale and underwriting of mortgage-backed securities around the global financial crisis.
- **Anti–money laundering (AML) and controls:** Enforcement actions that cited deficiencies in transaction monitoring, “know your customer” processes, and escalation of suspicious activity.
- **Cross-border trades and mirror-trading allegations:** Public reporting and enforcement activity around certain trading patterns used to move funds across jurisdictions.

## Regulatory and Governance Response

In response to recurring enforcement actions, banks typically face requirements such as independent monitorships, strengthened compliance staffing, upgrades to surveillance systems, and governance reforms. Deutsche Bank publicly announced multi-year restructuring efforts and changes to business lines in the period when many of these matters were being resolved.

## Reputational and Systemic Discussion

The bank’s repeated involvement in high-profile investigations has made it a frequent example in debates about “too-big-to-fail” institutions, cross-border regulatory enforcement, and the challenge of managing risk and compliance in global investment banking.
