## Summary

Thai national convicted of being the kingpin of a major rhino horn trafficking ring. He organized pseudo-hunts in South Africa where Thai sex workers posed as hunters to obtain rhino horns for Asian markets. Sentenced to 40 years in South Africa.

## Additional Context

### How the "pseudo-hunt" model worked

The scheme is commonly described as using so-called *pseudo-hunts*: legal hunting permits were obtained in a way that appeared compliant on paper, while the true intent was to acquire rhino horns for trafficking. In reporting on the case, Thai women were brought to South Africa to act as the nominal "hunters" on permit applications, while organizers coordinated logistics and onward export.

### Conservation and trade backdrop

- Rhinoceros horn is one of the highest-value wildlife products in illegal trade, driven by demand in parts of Asia.
- International trade is regulated through frameworks such as **CITES**, but criminal networks exploit jurisdictional gaps, corrupt facilitation, and documentation fraud.

### Prosecution and sentencing (high-level)

Court reporting on the South African proceedings emphasized the organized nature of the operation: recruitment, travel arrangements, paperwork, and attempted movement of horns into international markets. The sentence is often cited as **40 years**, reflecting multiple counts aggregated by the court.

### Why the case is cited

The case is frequently referenced in discussions of how wildlife trafficking networks adapt to enforcement pressure, shifting from direct poaching to more complex "legal cover" strategies that require coordinated paperwork and intermediaries.
