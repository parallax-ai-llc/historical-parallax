## Summary

Violent bank robbery and shootout in North Hollywood, Los Angeles on February 28, 1997. Two heavily armed robbers in body armor battled police for 44 minutes, injuring 12 officers.

## Background

- **Target**: The incident began as an attempted bank robbery in Los Angeles’ North Hollywood neighborhood.
- **Preparation**: Contemporary reporting emphasizes that the perpetrators prepared extensively, including heavy weaponry and protective gear, which shaped the tactical imbalance early on.

## Police Response (High Level)

- **Initial equipment gap**: Many responding patrol officers were equipped primarily with handguns and shotguns, which were less effective against armor and long-range fire.
- **Adaptation in real time**: Officers improvised tactics, took cover, and in some cases obtained additional firearms from nearby sources while coordinating perimeter and medical response.
- **Duration and aftermath**: The prolonged nature of the event made it a major case study in incident command, communications, and casualty management.

## Operational and Policy Impact

- **Patrol rifles**: The shootout is frequently cited as a catalyst for wider adoption of patrol rifles and upgraded protective equipment in U.S. policing.
- **Training and doctrine**: Agencies revisited training on active shooter response, barricade situations, and coordination between patrol and specialized units.
- **Public debate**: The incident became part of broader discussions about police militarization, use-of-force, and the balance between preparedness and escalation risk.
