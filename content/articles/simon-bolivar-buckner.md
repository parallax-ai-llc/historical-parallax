## Historical Context

The **Congo Free State** (1885–1908) was the personal colony of King Leopold II of Belgium. Its administration relied on coercive extraction of ivory and, later, wild rubber. Violence was used as a routine tool of governance, enforced by a colonial army known as the **Force Publique**.

Rubber quotas were often imposed on villages with collective punishment for non-compliance. Contemporary accounts describe hostage-taking, beatings, arson, and executions as mechanisms to compel labor.

## Rubber Terror and Mutilation as Control

Within the broader Congo Free State system, mutilation—especially **cutting off hands**—became a notorious symbol of the rubber regime. In many documented cases, severed hands were treated as proof that ammunition had been used “properly,” and they functioned as a form of terror intended to deter resistance and enforce production.

Even when specific attribution is difficult at the individual level, the pattern reflects an institutional environment where extreme brutality was incentivized and normalized.

## Why This Matters

The Congo Free State abuses became a major international human-rights scandal in the early 20th century. Missionary reports, investigative journalism, and reform campaigns helped expose the scale of atrocities and contributed to Belgium annexing the territory in 1908.
