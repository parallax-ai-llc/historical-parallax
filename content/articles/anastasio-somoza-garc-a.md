## Summary

Nicaraguan dictator who founded the Somoza dynasty that ruled Nicaragua for over 40 years. FDR reportedly said of him, 'He may be a son of a bitch, but he's our son of a bitch.'

## Early life and rise

Anastasio Somoza García was a Nicaraguan political and military figure who rose to power during a period of U.S. intervention and state-building in Nicaragua. He became closely associated with the U.S.-trained National Guard, which was established as a centralized armed force and became a decisive instrument of political control.

## Consolidation of power

Somoza’s ascent is often summarized as a combination of:

- Building influence within the National Guard and leveraging it in national politics.
- Defeating or marginalizing rivals, including figures tied to earlier anti-intervention movements.
- Using elections and constitutional mechanisms alongside coercion to maintain authority.

Although he served multiple presidential terms and sometimes ruled through allies, he is widely described as the founder of a family-based authoritarian system.

## Governance and political style

Histories of the Somoza era typically emphasize patronage networks, repression of opposition, and the blending of state institutions with private family interests. The Somoza family accumulated significant economic holdings while maintaining control over key security and administrative posts.

## Relationship with the United States

Somoza García is frequently discussed in the context of U.S. Cold War and pre–Cold War hemispheric policy, where anti-communism and perceived stability often shaped diplomatic relationships with authoritarian leaders. The quote attributed to Franklin D. Roosevelt is commonly repeated in popular accounts; even when its precise provenance is debated, it reflects a widely noted pattern of pragmatic U.S. support for allied strongmen.

## Assassination and legacy

He was assassinated in 1956 by Rigoberto López Pérez, a poet and political opponent. After Somoza García’s death, the Somoza dynasty continued under his sons, remaining a dominant force in Nicaragua’s politics for decades and becoming a central reference point in later revolutionary and counterrevolutionary conflicts.
