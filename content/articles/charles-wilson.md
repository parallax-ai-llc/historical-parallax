## Background

Charles Thomson Rees Wilson was a Scottish physicist whose work connected atmospheric phenomena with experimental physics. His early interests were influenced by observations of natural clouds and optical effects, which later informed his laboratory approach.

## The Cloud Chamber (Principle)

Wilson’s cloud chamber makes otherwise invisible charged particles observable. By creating a supersaturated vapor inside a sealed chamber, ionizing radiation leaves behind tracks of condensation, forming thin lines of droplets that can be seen and photographed.

## Scientific Impact

The cloud chamber became a foundational instrument in early particle physics. It provided direct visual evidence of particle trajectories and interactions and helped researchers study phenomena such as cosmic rays and the behavior of electrons and other charged particles.

## Nobel Prize and Recognition

Wilson shared the 1927 Nobel Prize in Physics for the invention of the cloud chamber. The award recognized not only the device itself but also how instrumental methods can open new fields by making new kinds of measurements possible.

## Legacy

While later detectors (such as bubble chambers and electronic tracking devices) expanded on similar ideas, Wilson’s chamber remains a classic example of elegant experimental design. It is frequently cited in histories of modern physics as an enabling technology that bridged theory and observation.
