## Summary

South Korean intelligence chief who assassinated President Park Chung-hee during a private dinner in 1979. He claimed he killed the dictator to restore democracy but was executed.

## Additional Context

- **Role in the Park era:** As KCIA director, Kim operated at the center of South Korea’s authoritarian security state during the 1970s.
- **The 1979 assassination:** Park Chung-hee was killed during a private gathering; the event immediately triggered a political crisis and accelerated elite conflict within the regime.
- **Stated motives:** Kim claimed his act was intended to end dictatorship and open a path toward democratization, while critics have argued it also reflected factional rivalry and personal grievances.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- **Trial and execution:** Kim was tried, convicted, and executed in 1980. The case remains one of the most consequential political killings in modern Korean history.
- **Power transition:** The vacuum after Park’s death contributed to instability and eventually to a new military-dominated order before later democratic reforms.
- **Historical debate:** Interpretations vary: some view Kim as a would-be democrat, others as a senior regime insider whose violence deepened turmoil.

## Why It Matters

1. The assassination ended an 18-year presidency and reshaped South Korea’s political trajectory.
2. It illustrates the internal fragility of authoritarian systems, where security institutions can become political actors.
3. The controversy over Kim’s motives shows how democratic transitions often produce contested narratives.
