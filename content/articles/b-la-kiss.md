## Summary

Hungarian serial killer who murdered at least 24 people before World War I. He pickled his victims in large metal drums filled with alcohol. He disappeared during WWI and was never captured.

## Modus Operandi (Reported)

Accounts describe Kiss as using personal advertisements to contact potential victims, often women seeking marriage. After killings, he reportedly concealed bodies in large containers filled with alcohol, which both hid decomposition and reduced odor, delaying detection.

## Discovery and Investigation

The case became known after authorities searched his property and found multiple bodies. The outbreak of World War I created conditions for confusion and mobility that complicated efforts to locate him, contributing to the mystery around his fate.

## Disappearance and Uncertainty

Kiss was never definitively captured or confirmed dead. Later alleged sightings have been reported, but the historical record remains inconclusive and is often presented with caveats due to the mix of rumor and limited documentation.

## Why the Case Persists

The combination of sensational concealment methods, the large number of suspected victims, and the unresolved disappearance has made Kiss a recurring figure in true-crime history as an example of early 20th-century serial killing and investigative limitations.

## Caveats

Much of the narrative is reconstructed from early reports and later retellings. Precise victim counts, timelines, and details of alleged sightings can vary by source, so summaries often emphasize what is reported rather than what is conclusively proven.

This uncertainty is part of why Kiss is sometimes treated as a “legendary” case, even though it originated in real criminal investigations.
