## Summary

Saddam Hussein (1937–2006) was an Iraqi Ba'athist political leader who became president of Iraq in 1979 and ruled until he was overthrown in 2003. His government built a highly centralized, security-state regime and pursued ambitious modernization projects funded by oil revenue, while also carrying out systematic repression, political violence, and mass human-rights abuses. Saddam initiated major regional conflicts—most notably the Iran–Iraq War (1980–1988) and the 1990 invasion of Kuwait—that shaped Iraq’s domestic trajectory and the Middle East’s security order for decades.

## Governance and Domestic Policy (High Level)

- **Authoritarian consolidation:** Saddam's rule relied on the Ba'ath Party, elite patronage networks, and extensive intelligence and security services.
- **State-building and modernization:** The government invested heavily in infrastructure, education, and public services during periods of high oil income, while politicizing institutions.
- **Cult of personality:** State media and political rituals elevated Saddam as the embodiment of the nation and regime legitimacy.
- **Sectarian and ethnic dynamics:** Though the regime was formally secular, political power was concentrated in narrow networks, and communities perceived as disloyal—especially Kurds and many Shi'a groups—faced severe repression.

## Major Conflicts

### Iran–Iraq War (1980–1988)

- Began with Iraq’s invasion of Iran amid border disputes and revolutionary upheaval in Iran.
- Became a prolonged attritional conflict with massive casualties, economic damage, and widespread use of artillery, missiles, and chemical weapons.
- Ended in a UN-brokered ceasefire without a decisive territorial outcome.

### Invasion of Kuwait and the Gulf War (1990–1991)

- Iraq invaded Kuwait in August 1990, citing economic grievances and territorial claims.
- A US-led coalition expelled Iraqi forces in early 1991.
- The aftermath included strict international sanctions, no-fly zones, and recurrent crises over weapons inspections.

### 2003 Iraq War and Regime Collapse

- A US-led invasion in March 2003 quickly dismantled the Iraqi state’s top leadership.
- Saddam evaded capture for months before being found and detained.

## Human Rights and Repression

- Saddam's government is widely documented as responsible for torture, arbitrary detention, extrajudicial killings, and forced disappearances.
- The **Anfal campaign** (late 1980s) targeted Kurdish communities; the **Halabja** chemical attack became an emblematic atrocity associated with the period.
- After 1991, the regime violently suppressed uprisings and maintained power through fear, surveillance, and collective punishment.

## Trial and Execution

- Saddam was tried by an Iraqi court established after 2003; proceedings focused on regime violence, including the Dujail killings.
- He was sentenced to death and executed on 2006-12-30.

## Legacy

- Saddam remains a polarizing figure: some recall state capacity and stability under his rule, while others emphasize war-making, repression, and long-term institutional damage.
- The collapse of the Ba'athist state and subsequent insurgency and sectarian conflict reshaped Iraq and influenced regional geopolitics.
