## Summary

English murderer who killed Maria Marten in the infamous Red Barn Murder, one of the most sensational Victorian crimes.

## Background

William Corder became known through the "Red Barn Murder" case, a crime that attracted intense public attention in early-19th-century England.

High-level context often noted in accounts of the case:

- **Victorian-era crime reporting**: newspapers and popular broadsides amplified sensational cases.
- **Rural community dynamics**: rumors, reputation, and social standing played an outsized role in public interpretation.

## The Red Barn Murder (overview)

- **Victim**: Maria Marten.
- **Location associated with the case**: the "Red Barn" near Polstead, Suffolk.
- The case became famous not only for the killing, but also for the **cultural afterlife** that followed (pamphlets, ballads, stage adaptations, and later retellings).

## Trial and Execution

Corder was tried and convicted for Marten's murder and was executed in 1828.

## Legacy

The Red Barn Murder remained a reference point in British true-crime history because it combined:

- a small-town setting,
- a widely discussed court case,
- and a long-running popular fascination with motives, evidence, and narrative.
