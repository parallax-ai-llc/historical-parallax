## Summary

French CEO of Mt. Gox Bitcoin exchange who was convicted of manipulating financial records after the exchange lost 850,000 Bitcoin to hackers. He received a suspended sentence.

## Background

Karpelès is a French software engineer who became prominent in the early Bitcoin economy after taking control of **Mt. Gox**, a Tokyo-based exchange that at its peak handled a large share of global Bitcoin trading.

## Mt. Gox and the Early Exchange Era

In the early 2010s, Bitcoin exchanges were still experimental financial infrastructure: security practices, auditing, and regulatory expectations were immature compared with traditional finance.

Under Karpelès’s leadership, Mt. Gox grew rapidly, but it also faced recurring operational problems, including security incidents and withdrawal disruptions.

## Collapse (2014)

Mt. Gox halted withdrawals and ultimately filed for bankruptcy in 2014 after reporting that a very large number of bitcoins had been lost or stolen (commonly cited as **~850,000 BTC** in public reporting at the time).

The collapse became one of the defining failures of the first “crypto exchange” generation and shaped later expectations around custody, proof-of-reserves concepts, and exchange governance.

## Legal Proceedings and Conviction

Japanese authorities pursued criminal charges related to Mt. Gox’s operations. In broad summary:

- The most enduring legal outcome discussed in mainstream accounts is his **conviction for manipulating/altering financial records**.
- He received a **suspended sentence**, distinguishing the record-manipulation finding from more severe accusations that were contested in court.

## Impact on Crypto Industry

Mt. Gox’s failure influenced:

- Greater emphasis on exchange security and segregation of customer assets.
- The push for clearer regulation and licensing of digital-asset service providers.
- The cultural expectation that centralized exchanges can be systemic points of failure.

## Legacy

Karpelès is remembered less for technological contributions and more as a central figure in a formative crypto-era scandal—useful for understanding how quickly financial platforms can grow beyond their governance and risk controls.
