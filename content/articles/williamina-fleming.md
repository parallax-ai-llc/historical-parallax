## Summary

Williamina Fleming was a Scottish-American astronomer who classified thousands of stellar spectra and discovered the Horsehead Nebula. She was part of the 'Harvard Computers' group and developed a system for classifying stars by their spectral characteristics. She discovered 10 novae, 59 nebulae, and over 300 variable stars.

## Harvard Computers and Spectral Classification

Fleming worked at Harvard College Observatory during a period when large photographic plate archives enabled systematic study of the sky. She was part of the group later known as the "Harvard Computers"—women employed to measure, catalog, and interpret astronomical data.

She developed an early classification approach for stellar spectra that organized stars by their spectral features, contributing to the broader Harvard classification work that later evolved into the OBAFGKM sequence through subsequent refinements by other astronomers.

## Discoveries and Catalog Work

Fleming is credited with identifying numerous variable stars, novae, and nebulae from photographic plates. This style of work—careful, repeatable measurement at scale—was essential to building the catalogs that later astrophysics relied upon.

Her career is also cited as an example of scientific contribution under gendered labor structures: producing foundational data and systems while operating within constrained professional recognition pathways.

## Legacy

Fleming’s name is frequently invoked in histories of astronomy as both a productive observer/classifier and as a representative figure in the transition to data-intensive astrophysical research. Her appointments and honors were notable for her time and helped open limited institutional pathways for women in the field.

## Further Notes

Fleming’s contributions are commonly discussed alongside other Harvard Computers as part of a broader story about how cataloging and classification enabled modern astrophysics.
