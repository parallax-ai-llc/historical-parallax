## Summary

Exploitation and death of migrant workers building World Cup infrastructure in Qatar. The Guardian reported 6,500 worker deaths from 2010-2020, with workers facing wage theft, confiscated passports, and dangerous conditions.

## Additional Context

- The controversy centers on labor conditions for migrant workers involved in large-scale construction tied to the tournament (stadiums, roads, and related infrastructure).
- Reporting and advocacy groups highlighted risks commonly associated with fast, high-volume construction: heat exposure, long shifts, unsafe worksites, and limited labor protections.
- The issue is frequently discussed alongside the "kafala" sponsorship system, which historically shaped worker mobility and bargaining power in parts of the Gulf.

## Why the Topic Became Global

- Hosting a World Cup concentrates global media attention, which amplified scrutiny of supply chains and labor practices.
- The combination of high-cost projects and a large migrant workforce made the conditions a sustained international story over multiple years.

## Themes in Public Debate

- Accountability: how responsibility is allocated across the state, contractors, subcontractors, and international governing bodies.
- Measurement: disagreements about how deaths are counted and categorized (work-related vs. broader mortality among a worker population).
- Reform: assessment of labor-policy changes and whether implementation matched public commitments.

## Lasting Impact

- Became a reference point for discussions about human rights due diligence in mega-events.
- Influenced expectations that future host bids and preparations include clearer labor standards and external monitoring.
