## Summary

Basque separatist organization that carried out bombings, assassinations, and kidnappings for over 50 years, killing over 850 people. Their campaign for Basque independence from Spain was one of Europe's longest-running insurgencies.

## Additional Context

ETA (Euskadi Ta Askatasuna, “Basque Country and Freedom”) emerged from the political and cultural repression that followed the Spanish Civil War and intensified under Francisco Franco’s dictatorship. Although it began with a nationalist and anti-authoritarian posture, the group became widely defined by an armed campaign that targeted state officials, security forces, politicians, and civilians.

### Tactics and Organization

- **Armed actions** included bombings, shootings, and kidnappings, often framed as retaliation against the Spanish state.
- **Financing** historically drew on robbery, extortion (“revolutionary tax”), and support networks among sympathizers.
- Over time, ETA’s structure evolved into smaller operational units to reduce infiltration risk.

### Political Dimension

- ETA’s violence existed alongside (and sometimes in tension with) legal Basque nationalist parties and civil society groups.
- Spanish and French authorities pursued coordinated counterterrorism efforts, including arrests, intelligence operations, and judicial actions.

### End of the Campaign

The 2011 announcement of a “definitive cessation of armed activity” marked the effective end of ETA’s operational campaign. The 2018 dissolution formalized that end, though issues such as prisoner policy, victims’ rights, and historical memory continued to shape public debate in Spain and the Basque Country.

### Common Historical Markers

Coverage of ETA frequently references the 1973 assassination of Carrero Blanco, later ceasefire declarations, and the long-running security and judicial response by Spain (and, in later phases, France). These markers are often used to periodize the conflict and its aftermath.
