## Additional Background

“El Chapo” is the widely used alias of Joaquín Archivaldo Guzmán Loera, a Mexican drug trafficker described in open sources as a longtime leader within the Sinaloa Cartel. He is one of the most internationally known figures associated with Mexico’s modern drug-trafficking era due to repeated arrests, escapes from custody, and eventual prosecution in the United States.[^1]

## Sinaloa Cartel Leadership (High-Level)

Public sources generally describe Guzmán as:

- A central figure in Sinaloa’s trafficking operations and alliances.
- A high-profile example of how cartel leadership can combine logistics, corruption, and violence to maintain control over routes and markets.

Because cartel structures are often decentralized and factional, “leader” framing can oversimplify the networked nature of operations; however, Guzmán’s name is consistently cited as a top-tier node in that ecosystem.[^1]

## Arrests, Escapes, and Extradition (Context)

Guzmán’s biography in open sources frequently highlights:

- **Repeated capture** by Mexican authorities.
- **High-profile prison escapes**, which became emblematic of institutional vulnerability and corruption risk.
- **Extradition to the United States**, where major conspiracy charges were pursued through federal courts.[^1]

## U.S. Prosecution and Sentencing

Open sources summarize the U.S. case as involving large-scale drug trafficking conspiracy allegations and related criminal enterprise conduct. The case culminated in conviction and a lengthy sentence, commonly framed as a landmark prosecution in the U.S. approach to transnational organized crime.[^1]

## Why This Figure Matters (Analytic Context)

Guzmán’s case is often used to discuss:

- **Cross-border enforcement**: Intelligence sharing, extradition politics, and multi-jurisdiction coordination.
- **Financial flows**: Money laundering mechanisms that sustain large trafficking networks.
- **Narrative warfare**: The role of media, myth-making, and public fear in cartel influence.
