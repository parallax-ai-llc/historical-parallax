## Summary

Blackwater security contractors killed 17 Iraqi civilians and injured 20 in Nisour Square, Baghdad. Four contractors were convicted but pardoned by President Trump in 2020.

## Background

The Nisour Square killings occurred during the U.S.-led Iraq War, in an environment where private security contractors were widely used to protect diplomats, convoys, and facilities. Blackwater (later renamed and reorganized) was one of the most prominent U.S. private security firms operating in Iraq.

## The Incident

Accounts summarized in public reporting describe contractors opening fire in a crowded traffic circle in Baghdad. The event became a flashpoint for Iraqi public anger and raised questions about rules of engagement, contractor oversight, and the use of force in civilian areas.

## Legal Proceedings

U.S. authorities pursued criminal cases against several contractors. Convictions were ultimately obtained for multiple defendants in U.S. federal court, though aspects of sentencing and appeals continued for years.

In 2020, President Donald Trump issued pardons to the convicted contractors, a decision that drew criticism from human-rights organizations and some Iraqi officials.

## Policy and Diplomatic Impact

The massacre intensified scrutiny of contractor accountability and influenced debates over:

- jurisdiction over contractors in war zones,
- transparency and reporting requirements for use-of-force incidents,
- the relationship between military, diplomatic security, and private firms.

It also became a symbol of the perceived imbalance between foreign security operations and Iraqi civilian protection.
