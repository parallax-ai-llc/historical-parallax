## Summary

Hutu paramilitary militia that was the primary perpetrator of the 1994 Rwandan genocide. They systematically murdered Tutsi civilians using machetes and clubs.

## Origins and Political Context

- The Interahamwe emerged in a context of escalating ethnic polarization and competition for state power in Rwanda in the early 1990s.
- The group is commonly described as a militia associated with extremist political currents and mobilized alongside elements of the state security apparatus.

## Role in the 1994 Genocide

- During the genocide, Interahamwe members participated in mass killings, roadblocks, and the targeting of civilians.
- Research and testimony emphasize that violence was enabled by local coordination, coercion, and propaganda, not only by spontaneous mob action.

## Organization and Mobilization

- The militia’s activities are often discussed in relation to political party structures, local administrators, and media incitement.
- Weaponry frequently included both firearms and improvised or agricultural tools, reflecting the scale and locality of the violence.

## Aftermath and Regional Spillover

- After the Rwandan Patriotic Front (RPF) ended the genocide, many perpetrators and militia members fled into what was then Zaire (now the Democratic Republic of the Congo).
- The presence of armed groups in refugee camps and border regions contributed to broader regional instability and subsequent conflict.

## Accountability and Justice

- Prosecutions related to the genocide took place through multiple mechanisms, including Rwanda’s domestic courts, community-based gacaca courts, and the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR).
- The term “Interahamwe” is sometimes used broadly in popular discourse; attribution of individual responsibility requires case-specific evidence.
