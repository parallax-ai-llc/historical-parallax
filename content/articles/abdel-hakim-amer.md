## Summary

Egyptian military commander whose incompetent leadership led to Egypt's catastrophic defeat in the 1967 Six-Day War. He allegedly committed suicide after being implicated in a coup plot against Nasser.

## Background and Rise

- **Free Officers era**: Amer was part of the military cohort associated with the 1952 Egyptian revolution and became a close associate of **Gamal Abdel Nasser**.
- **Senior command**: He rose rapidly in the armed forces and held top command roles during Egypt's consolidation as a post-monarchy republic.

## Military and Political Role

### Commander-in-Chief and the armed forces

- Amer served for years as a central figure in Egypt's military leadership, often described as exercising broad influence over promotions, internal networks, and operational decision-making.
- His relationship with Nasser has been portrayed as both collaborative and tension-filled, particularly as military setbacks accumulated.

### Regional interventions and posture

- **Yemen conflict (1960s)**: Egypt's costly intervention in North Yemen occurred during the period when Amer was a leading military decision-maker; historians often cite it as a major strain on readiness and resources before 1967.

## 1967 War and Aftermath

- **Six-Day War**: Egypt's defeat in June 1967 produced a political crisis and a search for responsibility within the senior command.
- **Removal and investigation**: After the war, Amer was removed from command; accounts describe disputes over whether he and allied officers planned a challenge to Nasser's authority.

## Death and Disputed Accounts

Amer died in September 1967 while in custody.

- **Official account**: The most commonly cited version is suicide.
- **Disputed claims**: Other accounts have alleged coercion or foul play; definitive public documentation is limited and narratives vary by source.

## Legacy (High-Level)

- Seen by critics as a symbol of politicized command structures and poor institutional accountability in the Egyptian military of the era.
- His career is frequently discussed alongside broader debates about civil–military relations under Nasserist governance.
