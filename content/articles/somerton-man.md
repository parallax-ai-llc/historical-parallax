## Summary

Unidentified man found dead on Somerton Beach, Adelaide in 1948. A scrap of paper in his pocket read 'Tamám Shud' (finished). DNA identified him as Carl Webb in 2022.

## Context and Investigation

- **Discovery**: The body was found on Somerton Beach in Adelaide, South Australia, in December 1948. The case quickly drew attention because the man’s identity and cause of death were unclear.
- **The “Tamám Shud” clue**: “Tamám Shud” is a Persian phrase commonly translated as “ended” or “finished,” and it is notably associated with *The Rubaiyat of Omar Khayyam*. In this case it became a shorthand name for the mystery itself.
- **Why the case persisted**: For decades, the Somerton Man case remained unresolved because traditional identification methods (public appeals, missing-person comparisons, and forensic examination) did not produce a definitive match.

## Identification (2022)

- In 2022, researchers reported an identification of the Somerton Man as **Carl Webb**, using genetic genealogy techniques.
- This kind of identification typically combines DNA analysis with family-tree research to narrow down potential identities, and then checks those results against historical records.

## Significance

The Somerton Man case is frequently cited as a major Australian “cold case” because it combines:

- unexplained circumstances surrounding a death,
- an unusual textual clue (“Tamám Shud”), and
- long-running public interest that continued until modern DNA methods offered a plausible identification.

## Open Questions

Even with a reported identification, historical cases like this often leave open questions about circumstances, timelines, and how specific clues (such as the paper scrap) connect to the person’s life.
