## Summary

Palestinian intelligence chief known as the Red Prince who commanded Force 17 and was a key planner of the Munich Olympics massacre. He was simultaneously a CIA asset before being assassinated by Mossad.

## Additional Context

- Salameh rose within Fatah/PLO security circles during the 1960s and 1970s and became associated with elite protection and intelligence functions.
- He is often described as a senior figure in Force 17, a unit tied to the protection of Palestinian leadership and sensitive operations.
- Reporting on his role in the 1972 Munich attack varies by source; many accounts attribute planning or coordination responsibilities to him, while details remain contested and are frequently reconstructed from intelligence and investigative reporting.

## Relationship With Foreign Intelligence

- Multiple historical accounts describe a clandestine relationship between Salameh and the United States, in which he was treated as a valuable contact by CIA officers while also remaining a prominent PLO security official.
- This type of relationship is sometimes characterized as an intelligence “asset” arrangement: a channel for information and influence rather than an endorsement of broader organizational goals.

## Assassination and Aftermath

- Salameh was killed in Beirut in January 1979 by a car-bomb attack widely attributed to Israel’s Mossad, following years of pursuit connected to the Munich killings.
- His death became part of the longer arc of covert retaliation, counter-retaliation, and internal security escalation that defined the era.

## Why He Matters

Salameh remains a notable figure because his story sits at the intersection of militant operations, state intelligence tradecraft, and the ambiguities of back-channel relationships during Cold War–era Middle East politics.
