## Summary

Leak of methyl isocyanate gas from Union Carbide's pesticide plant in Bhopal killed at least 3,787 people and affected 500,000. It remains the world's worst industrial disaster.

## Background

Union Carbide India Limited (UCIL) operated a pesticide plant in Bhopal, Madhya Pradesh, producing carbaryl (marketed as Sevin) using methyl isocyanate (MIC) as a key intermediate. MIC is highly toxic and reactive, and the plant stored it in large quantities.

## What Happened (High Level)

- In the early hours of 2 December 1984, water entered a MIC storage tank (widely reported as Tank 610), triggering an exothermic reaction.
- Pressure and temperature rose rapidly, and safety systems were unable to prevent a large release.
- A dense toxic cloud moved over nearby densely populated neighborhoods.

## Contributing Factors (Often Cited)

Accounts and investigations commonly point to a combination of issues rather than a single cause:

- Storage of MIC in large volumes and at conditions that increased risk.
- Maintenance and staffing problems reported around the period of the incident.
- Safety systems (refrigeration, gas scrubber, flare tower) reported as compromised, unavailable, or ineffective.
- Inadequate warning/communication and limited time for residents to take protective action.

## Human, Environmental, and Public Health Impact

- Immediate fatalities numbered in the thousands; longer-term mortality and morbidity estimates vary across sources and methodologies.
- Survivors reported lasting respiratory, ocular, and neurological impacts, among other chronic conditions.
- Soil and groundwater contamination at and around the plant site remained a long-running concern, with periodic studies and cleanup debates continuing for decades.

## Legal and Corporate Aftermath

- The disaster led to prolonged litigation and compensation processes in India and abroad.
- Union Carbide Corporation reached a settlement with the Government of India in 1989 (widely reported in secondary sources).
- Criminal proceedings and appeals continued over many years; in 2010 an Indian court convicted several former employees in connection with the incident.

## Legacy

The Bhopal disaster is frequently cited in industrial safety, chemical plant regulation, and corporate accountability discussions worldwide. It also influenced emergency planning practices and community-right-to-know movements related to hazardous industrial facilities.
