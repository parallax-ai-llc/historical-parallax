## Summary

Japanese revolutionary who directed international terrorist operations from Lebanon as leader of the Japanese Red Army. She was one of the most wanted women in the world for decades.

## Additional Context

### Background

Shigenobu was born in postwar Japan and became politically active during the late-1960s era of student and leftist movements. She later left Japan and based herself for long periods in the Middle East, where the Japanese Red Army (JRA) operated in cooperation with other militant organizations.

### Role in the Japanese Red Army

As a leading figure of the JRA, she is widely described as having played a strategic and organizational role rather than being known primarily as an on-the-ground attacker. The group sought to internationalize revolutionary violence, using high-profile incidents to attract attention to its political goals.

### Operations and International Profile

The JRA is associated with a series of international attacks and hostage incidents in the 1970s and 1980s, including events linked to airports, embassies, and aircraft. Shigenobu’s name became closely tied to the group’s global notoriety, and she was pursued by Japanese authorities for decades.

### Arrest, Prosecution, and Release

After returning to Japan, she was arrested in 2000 and later convicted in connection with specific JRA-related activities. She served a lengthy prison sentence and was released in 2022.

### Legacy

Shigenobu remains a polarizing figure: viewed by supporters as a revolutionary symbol and by critics as a leader responsible for enabling terrorism. Her case is often discussed in studies of transnational militancy, Cold War-era revolutionary networks, and the long tail of political violence.
