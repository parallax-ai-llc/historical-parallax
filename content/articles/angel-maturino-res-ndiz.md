## Summary

Mexican serial killer known as the 'Railroad Killer' who murdered at least 15 people along railroad routes in the US and Mexico between 1986 and 1999. He used trains to travel between crime scenes. Executed by lethal injection in Texas.

## Pattern of Crimes

Reséndiz was nicknamed the "Railroad Killer" because he used freight trains to move across long distances, allowing him to appear in different jurisdictions. Victims were attacked in or near their homes, and the attacks were often described as opportunistic rather than tied to a single local area.

## Investigation and Capture

Because the crimes crossed state lines and involved multiple police departments, coordination and information-sharing were important to the investigation. Reséndiz became a fugitive after being identified as a suspect and later surrendered, an outcome that relied in part on pressure and cooperation involving family members.

## Trial and Execution

He was prosecuted in Texas for murder, received a death sentence, and was executed by lethal injection in 2006. The case is frequently cited in discussions about how mobile offenders exploit transportation networks and how law enforcement tracks serial crimes across jurisdictions.

## Public and Media Attention

The interstate nature of the murders and the distinctive method of travel drew extensive media coverage. The label itself became part of the public narrative, though summaries of the case often note that the exact number of victims attributed to him varies by source.

## Notes

Accounts of the case describe a pattern of improvised weapons and theft from victims' homes, with assaults occurring in multiple states. The investigation is sometimes used as a case study in how offenders can leverage public infrastructure (rail corridors and freight networks) to evade predictable geographic profiling.
