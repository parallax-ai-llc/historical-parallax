## Summary

Chinese-Thai serial killer who murdered and cannibalized at least five children in Thailand during the 1950s. His mummified body was displayed in a museum until 2020.

## Additional Context

### Historical and Social Context

Si Ouey Sae Urng’s case became widely known in Thailand in the mid-20th century and has been retold repeatedly in popular media. Accounts often emphasize extreme allegations, which contributed to his notoriety and to the use of his story as a cautionary tale.

### Legal Case and Public Narrative

He was arrested in the late 1950s and executed in 1959. Over time, some commentators and researchers have raised questions about aspects of the case record and the way the story was reported, including whether certain claims were exaggerated in later retellings.

### Posthumous Display and Ethical Debate

A distinctive aspect of the case is the posthumous handling of his remains: his preserved body was displayed publicly for decades in a museum setting. This became controversial, with critics arguing that such display was degrading and ethically problematic regardless of guilt.

### Reburial

In 2020, his remains were removed from public exhibition and later buried, an outcome often described as part of a broader reassessment of the case and of the ethics of displaying human bodies.

### Legacy

The case sits at the intersection of criminal history, folklore, and public memory in Thailand, illustrating how sensational crimes can evolve into enduring cultural narratives and disputes about historical accuracy.
