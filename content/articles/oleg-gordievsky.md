## Summary

KGB colonel who secretly worked for MI6 and became the highest-ranking KGB officer to defect to the West. His intelligence helped Western leaders understand Soviet thinking during the Cold War's final decade.

## KGB Career and Western Contact

Gordievsky rose within the KGB during the Cold War and served in foreign postings that provided access to sensitive intelligence and political assessments. Public biographies emphasize that he became disillusioned with aspects of the Soviet system and, while still an active KGB officer, began providing information to Britain’s Secret Intelligence Service (MI6).

## Work as a Double Agent (High-Level)

As a double agent, Gordievsky supplied the West with insight into Soviet intelligence operations and leadership perceptions. Accounts often focus on his value as a source on Moscow’s strategic anxieties and interpretations of Western actions—information considered particularly significant during periods of heightened tension.

## 1985 Exfiltration

In 1985, Gordievsky was recalled to Moscow and came under suspicion. He was ultimately extracted from the Soviet Union in a covert operation widely reported in intelligence history as a notable exfiltration success. After reaching the West, he lived under British protection.

## Strategic Impact and Historical Claims

Gordievsky has been credited in some narratives with contributing to improved Western understanding of Soviet decision-making at a time when misperception risk was high. His reporting is often mentioned in discussions of Cold War crisis management and the transition toward the late-1980s thaw.

## Later Years and Controversies

Like many high-profile intelligence sources, assessments of Gordievsky’s impact vary, and some details remain contested or dependent on memoir accounts. He has been the subject of books, interviews, and debates about intelligence reliability, source handling, and the ethics of espionage.

## Recognition and Public Profile

After his defection, Gordievsky became one of the most cited KGB defectors in Western media. His accounts have been used by historians as one input among many when reconstructing Soviet intelligence priorities, especially for the late Cold War period.
