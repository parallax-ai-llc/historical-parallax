## Summary

Samarco mining company's dam collapsed in Mariana, Brazil, releasing 60 million cubic meters of iron ore waste. Killed 19 and contaminated 668 km of rivers in Brazil's worst environmental disaster.

## Background

- The collapse involved a tailings dam (waste storage) associated with the Samarco mining operation near Mariana, in the state of Minas Gerais.
- Samarco is a mining company associated with major global mining firms through joint ownership (often noted in public reporting as Vale and BHP).

## What Happened (High-Level)

- The dam failure released a large volume of iron-ore tailings into downstream communities and river systems.
- The tailings flow affected settlements immediately below the dam and then traveled through river networks toward the Atlantic coast.

## Human Impact

- Reported fatalities: 19.
- Large-scale displacement and loss of housing occurred in affected communities, with long-running resettlement and compensation disputes reported afterward.

## Environmental Impact

- Tailings contamination and sediment deposition affected waterways for hundreds of kilometers.
- Impacts frequently cited include degraded water quality, harm to aquatic life, and disruption to local agriculture and fisheries.

## Response, Accountability, and Aftermath

- The disaster triggered investigations, civil claims, and ongoing debate about liability, monitoring, and enforcement for tailings dams.
- Long-term remediation efforts have been reported, alongside criticism over pace, adequacy of compensation, and transparency.

## Why It Matters

- It is widely referenced as a case study in tailings-dam risk management, corporate accountability, and environmental governance.
- The event influenced public and regulatory attention on mining waste storage safety in Brazil and internationally.
