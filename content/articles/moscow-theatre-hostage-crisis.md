## Summary

Chechen militants took 850 hostages at the Dubrovka Theatre in Moscow in October 2002. Russian forces used an unknown chemical gas killing all militants but also 130 hostages.

## Context

The hostage-taking occurred during the period of conflict associated with the Second Chechen War and a wave of militant attacks linked to the Chechen separatist struggle. The assailants demanded an end to Russian military operations in Chechnya.

## The Siege

Armed militants seized the Dubrovka Theatre during a performance and held hundreds of people for several days. Negotiations and humanitarian discussions took place during the standoff, including the release of some hostages, while the crisis remained a major national and international news event.

## Rescue Operation and Use of Gas

Russian special forces ended the siege by pumping a chemical incapacitating agent into the building before storming it. The exact composition of the agent was not publicly disclosed at the time, and many hostage deaths were attributed to the effects of the gas combined with the challenges of emergency medical response.

## Casualties and Aftermath

All of the hostage-takers were killed. The death toll among hostages is commonly reported in the low hundreds, with the precise number varying by source. The operation sparked long-running controversy and legal disputes, including criticism about secrecy surrounding the chemical agent and the adequacy of medical preparations.

## Longer-Term Impact

The event influenced Russian domestic security policy and became a reference point in debates over counterterrorism tactics, transparency, and the balance between forceful rescue methods and risks to civilians.
