## Summary

An estimated 6,500 migrant workers from South Asia died during construction of Qatar's World Cup infrastructure. Workers faced 50°C heat, confiscated passports, and unpaid wages.

## Background

International scrutiny of labor conditions in Qatar intensified after the country won the right to host the 2022 FIFA World Cup. Qatar’s construction boom relied heavily on migrant labor, particularly from South Asia and other regions, and drew attention to recruitment practices, workplace safety, and legal protections.

## Reported deaths and disputed counts

Media investigations and human-rights reporting compiled figures for migrant-worker deaths over the broader period of World Cup-related development. These totals are frequently debated: some counts refer to all-cause deaths among migrant workers in Qatar, while other figures attempt to isolate deaths directly attributable to specific World Cup projects.

## Working conditions and alleged abuses

Commonly reported problems included excessive heat exposure, long hours, inadequate safety oversight, debt-bondage risks through recruitment fees, wage disputes, and restrictions linked to the kafala sponsorship system. Conditions varied by employer and time period, and reforms were unevenly implemented.

## Reforms and oversight

In response to criticism, Qatar announced and implemented labor-law changes in the years leading up to the tournament, including adjustments to sponsorship rules and wage protections. Observers disagreed on the degree of improvement and on enforcement in practice.

## Significance

The controversy became a major global case study in how mega-sporting events intersect with labor rights, supply-chain accountability, and the responsibilities of host governments, international sporting bodies, and contractors.
