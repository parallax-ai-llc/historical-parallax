Polykarp Kusch shared the 1955 Nobel Prize for precisely measuring the electron magnetic moment.

## Background

Kusch was trained as an experimental physicist in an era when quantum electrodynamics (QED) was being tested by increasingly precise measurements. He later worked in the United States and became associated with laboratory work that connected spectroscopy, atomic beams, and magnetic measurements.

## Nobel-Prize Work (Electron Magnetic Moment)

The "electron magnetic moment" is often summarized through the electron's *g-factor*, a dimensionless quantity that is close to 2 but not exactly 2. Kusch's experiments helped establish that the electron's magnetic moment deviates slightly from the value predicted by the simplest Dirac theory, providing key empirical support for QED.

These measurements were part of a broader set of postwar precision tests of quantum theory (alongside phenomena such as the Lamb shift). The ability to detect and quantify tiny deviations was important because it turned subtle theoretical corrections into experimentally measurable numbers.

## Academic Career and Influence

Kusch held academic positions and trained students in experimental methods, contributing to the mid-20th-century growth of precision measurement as a core tool in physics. His work is frequently cited in historical accounts of how QED moved from a theoretical framework to a quantitatively confirmed theory.

## Legacy

Beyond the Nobel Prize, Kusch is remembered as an example of how careful experimental technique can reveal small effects with large theoretical significance. Modern measurements of the electron g-factor have become even more precise, but they build on the experimental tradition that his work helped establish.
