## Summary

Mauritanian held in Guantanamo for 14 years without charge, author of Guantanamo Diary.

## Additional Context

### Background

- Born in Rosso, Mauritania (December 21, 1970).[^1]
- Traveled to Afghanistan in 1990 and later stated he severed ties with al-Qaeda after early 1990s involvement; U.S. authorities have disputed aspects of his account and alleged later support activities.[^1]
- Lived for periods in Germany and Canada (including Montreal), and was questioned by Canadian authorities in connection with the 2000 millennium attack plot investigations before returning to Mauritania.[^1]

### Detention and Legal Proceedings

- Detained after being questioned in Mauritania in late 2001, then transferred through the CIA extraordinary rendition program, including detention in Jordan and Afghanistan, before arrival at Guantánamo Bay on August 4, 2002.[^1]
- Subjected to harsh interrogation methods at Guantánamo, including sleep deprivation, isolation, and threats; elements of the treatment were later documented and discussed in public reporting and legal records.[^1]
- A U.S. military prosecutor (Lt. Col. Stuart Couch) declined to pursue a commission case in 2003, citing concerns that core statements were obtained through torture and would be inadmissible.[^1]
- In 2010, a federal district judge granted habeas relief and ordered release; the decision was appealed, vacated, and remanded by the D.C. Circuit, and the district court did not ultimately hold a second hearing.[^1]
- In 2016, a Periodic Review Board approved him for transfer, leading to release and repatriation to Mauritania (October 2016).[^1]

### Writings and Public Impact

- Wrote his memoir while imprisoned (2005). The U.S. government declassified the manuscript in 2012 with redactions; it was published in 2015 as *Guantánamo Diary* and became an international bestseller.[^1]
- The memoir has been widely cited in discussions of indefinite detention, due process, and interrogation policy in the post-9/11 era.[^1]

### Key Takeaways (High-Level)

- His case is frequently referenced as an example of long-term detention without criminal charges in the U.S. Guantánamo system.
- The publication history of *Guantánamo Diary* is notable for the role of government review/redaction in shaping the public record of detainee narratives.
