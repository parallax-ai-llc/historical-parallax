## Summary

Tarana Burke is an American civil rights activist and community organizer best known for founding the Me Too movement in 2006, more than a decade before it became a global phenomenon. Her work centers on empowering survivors of sexual violence, particularly Black women and girls in underserved communities. Burke's decades-long advocacy has reshaped public discourse around sexual harassment and assault, earning her recognition as one of the most influential activists of her generation.

Born and raised in the Bronx, New York, Burke built her career through grassroots organizing and youth development work. Her approach to social justice emphasizes healing, community, and the dignity of survivors rather than punitive outcomes alone. In 2017, when the hashtag #MeToo went viral following revelations about Harvey Weinstein, Burke's original vision was amplified to an international scale, forcing a long-overdue reckoning with sexual misconduct across industries.

## Early Life & Background

Tarana Burke was born on September 12, 1973, in the Bronx, New York. She grew up in a working-class family and experienced sexual violence herself as a child, an experience that would shape her life's work. She attended Alabama State University, a historically Black institution, where she deepened her engagement with civil rights activism and community organizing.

During her time in Selma, Alabama in the 1990s, Burke worked closely with young Black women and girls, witnessing firsthand the prevalence of sexual abuse and the lack of resources available to survivors. It was during this period that she first began articulating the framework of empathy and solidarity that would become the foundation of Me Too.

## Rise to Prominence

In 2006, Burke founded Just Be Inc., a nonprofit organization focused on the health and well-being of young women of color. That same year, she coined the phrase "Me Too" as a way to convey empathy to survivors of sexual violence — a tool for healing rooted in shared experience rather than shame.

Her national profile surged in October 2017 when actress Alyssa Milano used the hashtag #MeToo on Twitter in response to the Weinstein scandal. Burke quickly clarified the movement's origins and refocused attention on its original purpose: centering survivors. Time magazine named Burke, alongside other prominent #MeToo figures, as Person of the Year in 2017.

## Key Achievements

- Founded the Me Too movement in 2006, over a decade before its viral moment
- Named one of Time magazine's Persons of the Year in 2017
- Published her memoir *Unbound: My Story of Liberation and the Birth of the Me Too Movement* in 2021
- Received numerous honorary degrees and awards, including the Sydney Peace Prize in 2019
- Serves as Senior Director at Girls for Gender Equity, continuing direct community work

## Controversies & Criticism

Some critics within activist circles have raised concerns about the #MeToo movement's evolution toward celebrity-driven narratives, which they argue has overshadowed the communities of color Burke originally sought to serve. Burke herself has addressed this tension publicly, consistently redirecting focus toward marginalized survivors. She has also faced scrutiny over organizational governance at Just Be Inc., though no major legal or ethical violations have been established.

## Legacy & Influence

Burke's most enduring contribution is demonstrating that survivor-centered activism, built over years of grassroots work, can catalyze global change. The Me Too movement has influenced workplace harassment policies, legal reforms, and cultural norms across dozens of countries. Her emphasis on healing and community-building offers a model of social justice that extends beyond the viral moment into sustained structural change.
