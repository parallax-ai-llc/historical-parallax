## Summary

Japanese yakuza boss who led the Goto-gumi within the Yamaguchi-gumi. He secretly cooperated with the FBI in exchange for a liver transplant in the US.

## Yakuza Context

Japan’s yakuza groups are organized crime syndicates with hierarchical structures and semi-public identities in some periods, often maintaining offices and formalized leadership. The Yamaguchi-gumi has long been described as the largest of these organizations, with multiple subordinate factions.

## Leadership of the Goto-gumi

Goto is described in open sources as the head (kumicho) of the Goto-gumi, a faction within the Yamaguchi-gumi network. Such faction leadership typically involves control over membership, revenue streams, and relationships with other groups.

## Allegations and Criminal Activity (reported)

Public reporting commonly associates senior yakuza figures with crimes such as extortion, fraud, trafficking, and violence. For Goto specifically, summaries often focus less on a single signature case and more on his role as an underworld leader within a broader syndicate.

## Cooperation with U.S. Authorities

Goto’s international notoriety largely stems from reports that he cooperated with the FBI, providing information in exchange for assistance related to travel and medical treatment, including a liver transplant in the United States. The story has been cited in discussions about:

- how law-enforcement agencies sometimes use informants in organized-crime investigations
- ethical and legal questions around cooperation agreements
- the intersection of transnational crime, visas, and medical access

## Expulsion and Later Period

According to widely available accounts, Goto was later expelled from the Yamaguchi-gumi. Expulsions and internal discipline can reflect shifts in alliances, reputational risk, or leadership disputes within large syndicates.

## Notes on Sources

Much of what is publicly known about yakuza leadership figures comes from journalistic investigations and secondary summaries rather than court records that are easily accessible. Where possible, primary legal documents and contemporaneous reporting provide the most reliable detail.
