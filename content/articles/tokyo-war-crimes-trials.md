## Summary

International military tribunal that tried 28 Japanese leaders for war crimes from 1946-1948. Seven were sentenced to death including PM Tojo and General Matsui.

## Additional Context

- **Official name:** International Military Tribunal for the Far East (IMTFE).
- **Location and period:** Proceedings were held in Tokyo after World War II, with trials running from 1946 to 1948.
- **Purpose:** To establish individual criminal responsibility for major wartime decisions and actions, distinct from broader political or military defeat.

## Tribunal and Charges

- **Judges and prosecution:** The tribunal included judges from multiple Allied nations, and prosecutors presented evidence drawn from wartime records, witness testimony, and governmental archives.
- **Charge categories (high-level):**
  - *Class A:* Crimes against peace (planning and waging aggressive war).
  - *Class B:* Conventional war crimes.
  - *Class C:* Crimes against humanity.

## Outcomes and Legacy

- **Verdicts:** The tribunal convicted a number of defendants, issuing sentences that ranged from imprisonment to execution.
- **Historical impact:** The Tokyo trials are often discussed alongside the Nuremberg trials as part of the development of modern international criminal law.
- **Debate:** Scholarship commonly notes ongoing debate over issues such as victors’ justice, the scope of jurisdiction, and how responsibility was allocated among wartime leaders.
