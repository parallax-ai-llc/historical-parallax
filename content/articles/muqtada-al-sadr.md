## Summary

Iraqi Shia cleric who leads the Mahdi Army militia. His forces fought US troops and committed sectarian violence. He wields enormous political power in Iraq while leading a populist anti-corruption movement despite his militia's own abuses.

## Additional Context

- al-Sadr is part of the influential al-Sadr clerical family in Iraq, and his movement draws legitimacy from that lineage.
- His base of support has historically included poorer urban Shia communities, including areas in Baghdad often associated with the Sadrist movement.
- The militia he organized in the post-2003 period is commonly referred to as the Mahdi Army; later iterations and rebrandings of armed groups have also been associated with his network.
- His political influence has been exercised both through street mobilization and participation in elections via allied blocs.
- Analysts often describe his public posture as nationalist and anti-foreign-interference, while critics point to armed factionalism and coercive tactics by aligned groups.

## Why He Matters

- The Sadrist current has been a recurring power broker in Iraq’s post-2003 political system, affecting government formation and parliamentary dynamics.
- Periodic calls for reform and anti-corruption campaigns have intersected with Iraq’s broader protest movements and debates over militia integration.
- His actions are frequently cited in discussions about the relationship between clerical authority, militia power, and democratic institutions in contemporary Iraq.
