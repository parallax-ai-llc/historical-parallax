## Summary

Last major dacoit of the Chambal Valley who operated until the 2000s when most banditry had ended. He was killed in a police operation marking the effective end of Chambal dacoitry.

## Background and Context

The Chambal ravines spanning parts of Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, and Uttar Pradesh were historically associated with "dacoit" (bandit) gangs. Geography, weak state presence in some areas, and long-running local disputes contributed to cycles of organized outlawry and police crackdowns.

## Notability

Beer Singh Gujjar is often discussed as a late-era figure in Chambal banditry because many prominent gangs had already surrendered or been eliminated by the 1990s.

High-level themes commonly cited in reporting and retrospective accounts include:

- the decline of large, politically connected outlaw networks
- improved policing and intelligence operations compared to earlier decades
- shifting rural economies that reduced the space for sustained bandit control

## Death and Aftermath

His death in a police operation in 2005 is frequently framed as a symbolic endpoint for the "Chambal dacoit" era.

After such operations, the region's public narrative often shifts from outlaw folklore to questions of:

- rehabilitation of former gang members
- long-term governance and development of ravine communities
- the role of media depictions in mythologizing bandit figures
