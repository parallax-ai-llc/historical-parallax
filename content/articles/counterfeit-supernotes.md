## Summary

North Koreas decades-long operation producing near-perfect counterfeit US $100 bills known as supernotes. The program has produced hundreds of millions in fake currency.

## Key Details

### What “supernotes” refers to

"Supernote" is a term used for counterfeit U.S. $100 bills that were described by investigators as unusually high quality, including accurate intaglio-style printing, convincing security features for the era, and paper/ink characteristics that made detection difficult. The bills were distinct from common counterfeits and were treated as a strategic counterfeiting problem rather than routine criminal forgery.

### Alleged production and attribution

U.S. officials and some reporting attributed the production to entities connected to North Korea, framing the effort as state-supported. Attribution has also been debated in public sources, with periodic disputes about whether all specimens came from a single origin, whether multiple producers existed, and how confidently any one government could assign responsibility.

### Distribution and law-enforcement response

Investigations associated supernotes with international trafficking networks and money-laundering conduits, including cases involving diplomatic channels, organized crime groups, and intermediaries. U.S. and allied authorities responded through seizures, prosecutions, financial sanctions, and pressure on banks and jurisdictions believed to be facilitating circulation.

### Economic and security impact

Even when the absolute volume was limited compared with total U.S. currency in circulation, supernotes posed outsized risk because they were intended to undermine trust in banknotes and complicate cash-based commerce. Concerns about high-grade counterfeits were also part of the broader rationale for periodic redesigns of U.S. banknotes and enhanced anti-counterfeiting measures.

### Banco Delta Asia episode

The 2005–2006 U.S. actions involving Banco Delta Asia (BDA) are often discussed as a turning point, linking alleged illicit finance—including counterfeiting and laundering—to targeted financial sanctions and compliance pressure on regional banks.
