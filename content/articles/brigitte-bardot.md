## Summary

Brigitte Bardot (born September 28, 1934) is a French actress, model, and singer who became one of the most iconic sex symbols of the 20th century. Rising to international fame in the 1950s and 1960s, she embodied a new image of liberated femininity that challenged postwar social conventions and helped define the visual language of French cinema's golden era.

Her 1956 film *And God Created Woman*, directed by Roger Vadim, transformed her into a global phenomenon and single-handedly put the fishing village of Saint-Tropez on the international map. At the height of her fame, she was among the most photographed women in the world and a subject of fascination for intellectuals including Simone de Beauvoir, who wrote a celebrated 1959 essay analyzing Bardot as a new kind of woman.

After retiring from entertainment in 1973, Bardot reinvented herself as a prominent animal rights activist, founding the Brigitte Bardot Foundation for the Welfare of Animals in 1992.

## Early Life & Background

Bardot was born in Paris into an upper-middle-class family. Her father, Louis "Pilou" Bardot, was an industrialist; her mother, Anne-Marie "Toty" Mucel, came from a bourgeois Parisian family. Recognizing her daughter's aptitude for dance, her mother enrolled her at the Cours Bourdin at age seven. By thirteen, Bardot was accepted into the National Conservatory of Music and Dance of Paris, studying under the renowned Russian ballerina Madame Bourgat. She appeared on the cover of *Elle* magazine in May 1949 at age fifteen, catching the attention of aspiring director Roger Vadim Plemiannikov, whom she married in December 1952 at age eighteen.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Bardot made her film debut in *Crazy for Love* (1952) and worked steadily through the early 1950s in minor French productions. The breakthrough came with *And God Created Woman* (1956), filmed entirely in Saint-Tropez. The film's frank depiction of female sexuality caused controversy across Europe and North America while generating enormous box-office returns. It grossed approximately $4 million in the United States alone, a remarkable figure for a foreign-language film at the time.

Through the late 1950s and 1960s, she starred in over 40 films, including *En cas de malheur* (1958) with Jean Gabin, *Babette Goes to War* (1959), and *Contempt* (1963), directed by Jean-Luc Godard alongside Jack Palance and Michel Piccoli. She also pursued a parallel music career, releasing recordings with Serge Gainsbourg, including the provocative duet "Je t'aime... moi non plus" (recorded in 1967, though her version was not commercially released at the time due to her then-partner Gunter Sachs's objections).

## Key Achievements

- Appeared in 47 films between 1952 and 1973
- Named a cultural ambassador of France by the French government
- Her likeness was used as the model for Marianne, the allegorical symbol of the French Republic, beginning in 1970
- Received a César d'honneur (honorary César Award) in 1976
- Her Foundation for the Welfare of Animals has campaigned across multiple continents since 1992

---

## Controversies & Criticism

Bardot's later decades brought significant controversy distinct from her earlier reputation. From the 1990s onward, she made repeated public statements about Islam and immigration in France that resulted in five convictions for inciting racial hatred between 1997 and 2008, with fines totaling over €30,000. These statements drew condemnation from civil rights organizations and tarnished her legacy for many observers. She has also expressed views supporting the French far-right National Front party, positions widely criticized as inconsistent with her earlier image as a symbol of liberation.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Bardot reshaped the archetype of the female film star, displacing the polished Hollywood glamour model with a more natural, overtly sensual aesthetic. Her influence is visible across fashion, film, and popular culture — the "Bardot neckline" (an off-shoulder style she popularized) remains a standard garment silhouette decades later. Directors from Godard to Louis Malle cited her ability to project authentic spontaneity on camera as transformative. As an animal rights campaigner, she helped shift public discourse in France around seal hunting, foie gras production, and the treatment of stray animals, contributing to several legislative changes.
