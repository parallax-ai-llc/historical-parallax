## Summary

Haitian paramilitary leader who founded FRAPH death squad responsible for thousands of killings after the 1991 coup. He fled to the United States and was eventually convicted of mortgage fraud.

## Additional Context

### Political context in Haiti

- Constant rose to prominence in the early 1990s amid Haiti’s post-Duvalier political instability and the military-backed overthrow of President Jean-Bertrand Aristide in 1991.[^2]
- During the 1991–1994 period, armed groups and elements tied to the de facto authorities were accused of targeting Aristide supporters through intimidation, killings, and forced displacement.[^2]

### FRAPH and allegations of abuses

- Constant is best known as the founder and leader of the Revolutionary Front for the Advancement and Progress of Haiti (FRAPH), a paramilitary organization accused of involvement in political violence and human rights abuses.[^1][^3]
- Reporting and later investigations linked FRAPH to attacks on civilians and to a broader campaign of repression during the coup period, though specific incidents and legal findings vary by source.[^1][^3]

### Flight to the United States and legal outcomes

- After leaving Haiti, Constant lived in the United States for many years and became a subject of dispute between U.S. and Haitian authorities over deportation or extradition.[^1]
- He was ultimately convicted in New York state on mortgage fraud-related charges, a case separate from the alleged political crimes attributed to FRAPH leadership.[^1]

### Legacy

- Constant’s case is often cited in discussions about accountability after political violence in Haiti, including questions about how alleged perpetrators of abuses can evade prosecution by relocating abroad.[^2][^3]
