## Summary

British man who drove a van into Muslim worshippers near the Finsbury Park Mosque in London on June 19, 2017, killing one and injuring 12. He was radicalized by far-right content online. Convicted of murder and attempted murder.

## Incident Overview

The attack took place in the early hours after Ramadan prayers, when a vehicle was driven into a crowd near a mosque. It was treated as a terrorist incident due to the deliberate targeting of Muslims and the attacker’s stated motivations.

## Radicalization and Online Ecosystem

Court reporting described a pathway shaped by exposure to extremist and conspiratorial content. The case became part of a wider public debate in the UK about how:

- algorithmic feeds can accelerate exposure to inflammatory material;
- high-profile political events and polarizing narratives can be exploited by extremists;
- offline violence can be catalyzed by online networks without formal organizational ties.

## Legal Outcome

Osborne was convicted of murder and attempted murder, with the court emphasizing the ideological nature of the attack and the vulnerability of the victims. Sentencing reflected both punishment and public protection considerations in terrorism cases.

## Wider Significance

The Finsbury Park attack was widely cited as a reminder that terrorism threats can emerge from multiple ideological directions, including far-right extremism. It also contributed to policy discussions about community protection, counter-extremism programs, and the responsibilities of online platforms.
