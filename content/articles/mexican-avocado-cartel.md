## Summary

Mexican cartels extort avocado farmers in Michoacán, taxing production and controlling distribution. The $2.4 billion avocado industry is heavily infiltrated by organized crime.

## Context

The phrase “Mexican avocado cartel” is commonly used to describe organized-crime involvement in the avocado supply chain—especially in the state of Michoacán, Mexico’s dominant avocado-producing region and, for years, the only Mexican state authorized to export fresh avocados to the United States.

Rather than referring to a single unified organization, reporting typically describes a pattern of criminal groups (including factions of larger cartels and local armed groups) extracting revenue from growers, packers, and transporters through extortion, forced “protection” payments, and control of logistics.

## How the Extortion Works (high level)

Commonly reported mechanisms include:

- **“Tax” or cuota payments** demanded from growers or packing houses, sometimes assessed per hectare, per truckload, or per kilogram.
- **Control of transportation routes** and checkpoints, enabling theft of product or mandatory payments for safe passage.
- **Coercion of local markets** (inputs, land, labor) through threats, kidnapping, and violence.
- **Money laundering and asset capture**, where legitimate agribusiness can be used to disguise illicit proceeds.

These pressures can raise costs, distort competition, and push compliant farmers and businesses into informal arrangements simply to keep operating.

## Industry and Geography

- **Michoacán** is central to most accounts, with key producing and packing corridors around cities such as **Uruapan** and **Tancítaro**.
- The crop’s high export value and relatively predictable demand (including seasonal spikes around major U.S. events) can make the sector attractive for criminal rent-seeking.
- The supply chain involves multiple choke points—orchards, packing plants, certification/inspection steps, and trucking—each of which can be exploited.

## International Trade and Inspection Risk

Because the U.S. market relies on phytosanitary and security protocols, threats against inspectors or supply-chain personnel have sometimes triggered **temporary import suspensions** or heightened scrutiny. These episodes illustrate how local security issues can quickly become an international trade disruption.

## Notes

- The “avocado cartel” label is often used in media as shorthand; on the ground, involvement may vary by municipality, season, and the balance of power among armed groups.
- Some communities have reported forms of self-organization or local defense initiatives, while federal and state authorities have periodically announced security operations aimed at extortion and illegal checkpoints.
