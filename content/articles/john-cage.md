## Summary

John Milton Cage Jr. (1912-1992) was an American composer, music theorist, artist, and philosopher who fundamentally transformed 20th-century music through his pioneering work in indeterminacy, electroacoustic music, and non-standard use of musical instruments.[^1] Best known for his controversial 1952 composition *4'33"*—four minutes and thirty-three seconds of intentional silence—Cage challenged the very definition of music and became one of the most influential figures of the post-war avant-garde.[^2] His integration of Zen Buddhist philosophy with chance operations derived from the ancient Chinese *I Ching* revolutionized compositional practice and expanded the boundaries between music, philosophy, and life itself.[^3] Through innovations like the prepared piano and his lifelong creative partnership with choreographer Merce Cunningham, Cage's radical experimentation continues to influence contemporary music, visual arts, and performance to this day.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

John Cage was born on September 5, 1912, in Los Angeles, California, to John Milton Cage Sr., an inventor, and Lucretia Harvey Cage, a journalist.[^5] Of English and Scottish descent, Cage grew up in an Episcopalian household that fostered intellectual curiosity and creative thinking.[^6] His father, an eccentric inventor who held patents for various devices including an early submarine, instilled in young Cage a philosophy of innovation, telling him, "if someone says 'can't,' that shows you what to do."[^7] His mother, whom Cage described as a woman with "a sense of society" who was "never happy," provided a contrasting influence that shaped his understanding of social conventions and their limitations.[^8]

As a child, Cage was introduced to music through relatives and various private piano teachers in the Los Angeles area.[^9] One particularly significant early influence was Fannie Charles Dillon, his high school music teacher and a pioneering American pianist who exposed him to contemporary compositional techniques.[^10] Cage excelled academically, graduating as valedictorian from Los Angeles High School, demonstrating the intellectual prowess that would later characterize his theoretical writings.[^11]

In 1928, Cage enrolled at Pomona College to study theology, where he encountered the works of modernist innovators who would profoundly shape his artistic vision.[^12] He found inspiration in the revolutionary visual art of Marcel Duchamp, the stream-of-consciousness prose of James Joyce, and the transcendentalist philosophy of Henry David Thoreau.[^13] However, after two years, Cage grew disillusioned with conventional academic education and made the bold decision to drop out and travel to Europe.[^14] During his European sojourn, he experimented with various art forms—writing poetry, painting, and studying architecture—before settling in Majorca, Spain, where he composed his first musical works.[^15] This period of experimentation and self-directed learning established a pattern that would define Cage's entire career: a willingness to abandon established paths in pursuit of new forms of creative expression.[^16]

---

## Rise to Prominence

Returning to the United States in 1931, Cage began his formal musical education in earnest, studying with several influential teachers who shaped his early development.[^17] He worked with pianist Richard Buhlig, who introduced him to contemporary piano techniques, and composers Adolph Weiss and Henry Cowell, both of whom exposed him to modernist compositional approaches.[^18] Most significantly, from 1934 to 1936, Cage studied composition, harmony, and counterpoint with the renowned Austrian composer Arnold Schoenberg, who had revolutionized Western music through his twelve-tone technique.[^19]

Schoenberg agreed to take Cage as a pupil free of charge on the condition that Cage would dedicate his life to music—a vow the young composer honored throughout his career.[^20] However, Cage's studies with Schoenberg also revealed a fundamental incompatibility: Cage recognized that he had "no feeling for harmony," which Schoenberg considered essential to musical composition.[^21] When Schoenberg told Cage he would encounter a wall in his compositional development due to this limitation, Cage responded that he would "devote [his] life to beating [his] head against that wall."[^22] This exchange foreshadowed Cage's career-long rejection of traditional harmonic structures in favor of alternative organizational principles based on rhythm, timbre, and chance.

Cage's breakthrough innovation came in 1940 when he invented the prepared piano technique for his dance composition *Bacchanale*.[^23] Serving as an accompanist for dancer Syvilla Fort in a Seattle venue that lacked space for a percussion ensemble, Cage needed to create percussive sounds using only a grand piano.[^24] Drawing inspiration from Henry Cowell's "string piano" techniques, Cage began inserting household objects—bolts, screws, rubber erasers, weather stripping, and mutes—between the piano strings, creating an instrument that could be played from the standard keyboard but produced exotic, gamelan-like timbres.[^25] This invention established Cage's reputation as an innovative composer and demonstrated his characteristic approach to problem-solving: transforming limitations into opportunities for creative discovery.[^26]

In 1942, Cage moved to New York and reconnected with Merce Cunningham, whom he had first met in 1938 when Cage was working as an accompanist at the Cornish School for the Arts in Seattle.[^27] At that time, Cage was 26 and married to Xenia Kashevaroff, while Cunningham was a 19-year-old student.[^28] Their relationship evolved from teacher-student to artistic collaborators and eventually romantic partners, though they remained discreet about their personal relationship, particularly during Cage's marriage.[^29] Cage invited Cunningham, then a member of the Martha Graham Dance Company, to collaborate on a project, initiating a creative partnership that would last fifty years and profoundly influence both modern music and contemporary dance.[^30]

---

## Major Achievements

John Cage's career produced approximately 300 compositions that fundamentally altered the landscape of contemporary music and expanded the possibilities of sonic art.[^31] His innovations spanned multiple domains—from instrumental preparation and electronic music to chance operations and conceptual art—each contributing to his reputation as one of the 20th century's most influential composers.[^32]

The prepared piano works, beginning with *Bacchanale* (1940) and culminating in *Sonatas and Interludes* (1946-48), represent Cage's first major achievement.[^33] *Sonatas and Interludes*, a seventy-minute work consisting of sixteen sonatas and four interludes for prepared piano, explores the "permanent emotions" of Indian aesthetic theory: the heroic, erotic, wondrous, mirthful, sorrowful, fearful, angry, and odious, along with their common tendency toward tranquility.[^34] This masterwork demonstrated that the prepared piano was not merely a novelty but a legitimate medium for serious artistic expression, earning Cage a Guggenheim Fellowship and a grant from the National Academy of Arts and Letters.[^35]

Cage's encounter with Zen Buddhism in the late 1940s through D.T. Suzuki's lectures at Columbia University precipitated the most revolutionary phase of his career.[^36] Zen philosophy's emphasis on non-intention, acceptance, and the dissolution of ego profoundly resonated with Cage's growing dissatisfaction with personal expression in art.[^37] When composer Christian Wolff presented Cage with a copy of the *I Ching* (Book of Changes) in early 1951, Cage discovered the perfect tool for implementing chance operations in his compositions.[^38] The *I Ching*, an ancient Chinese divination text, uses a system of 64 hexagrams generated through the random throwing of coins or yarrow stalks.[^39] Cage adapted this method to make compositional decisions, removing "the likes and dislikes of the ego" from the creative process.[^40]

His first major chance-based work, *Music of Changes* (1951), used the *I Ching* to determine nearly every aspect of the composition—pitches, durations, dynamics, and tempos.[^41] This marked a decisive break from traditional compositional practice, where composers exercised intentional control over musical parameters. The *I Ching* remained Cage's primary compositional tool for the remainder of his life, employed in works ranging from *Music for Piano* (where paper imperfections determined note placement) to the later *Number Pieces* of the 1980s and early 1990s.[^42]

Cage's most famous and controversial work, *4'33"* (1952), represents the logical culmination of his exploration of silence and environmental sounds.[^43] The composition, which can be performed by any instrument or combination of instruments, instructs performers to remain silent for the duration specified by the title (four minutes, thirty-three seconds), divided into three movements.[^44] Inspired by Robert Rauschenberg's "White Paintings" (1951)—seemingly blank canvases that changed according to lighting conditions and shadows—*4'33"* directs attention to the ambient sounds that constitute any acoustic environment.[^45] The premiere performance by pianist David Tudor on August 29, 1952, caused immediate controversy, with some audience members viewing it as a provocative joke and others recognizing it as a profound philosophical statement about the nature of music and listening.[^46]

Cage's insight into silence had been crystallized by his experience in an anechoic chamber at Harvard University, where he expected to hear absolute silence but instead heard two sounds—the high-pitched sound of his nervous system and the low rumble of his blood circulation.[^47] This led to his famous conclusion: "There is no such thing as silence. There is always something to see, something to hear."[^48] *4'33"* thus reframes music-making as an act of framing and directing attention rather than producing intentional sounds, transforming the audience's role from passive recipients to active participants in the creation of the sonic experience.[^49]

In the realm of electronic music, Cage produced pioneering works that exploited new technologies. *Imaginary Landscape No. 4* (1951) scored for twelve radio receivers operated by two performers each, creating a composition whose content varied with each performance based on whatever radio broadcasts were available.[^50] *Williams Mix* (1952), composed for eight-track magnetic tape, used chance operations to splice together more than 600 tape fragments containing six categories of sounds, requiring a 192-page score to notate the splicing instructions.[^51] *Fontana Mix* (1958) employed a graphical score that could be used to generate tape compositions or realize live performances, demonstrating Cage's interest in creating flexible systems rather than fixed works.[^52]

Cage's collaborations with Merce Cunningham produced more than fifty groundbreaking dance works that established new principles for the relationship between music and choreography.[^53] Rejecting the conventional approach where music provided rhythmic support or emotional coloring for dance, Cage and Cunningham created works where music and dance shared only a common time structure but were otherwise independent.[^54] This radical separation allowed each art form to maintain its integrity while coexisting in shared performance space—a principle that influenced generations of experimental choreographers and composers.[^55]

Throughout his career, Cage received numerous honors recognizing his contributions to contemporary art. He was elected to the National Institute of Arts and Letters in 1968 and the American Academy of Arts and Sciences in 1978.[^56] Major institutions including the Boston Symphony Orchestra and the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation commissioned works from him.[^57] In 1989, Cage received the Kyoto Prize, Japan's highest artistic honor, in recognition of his lifetime achievements.[^58] His influence extended far beyond the concert hall, affecting visual artists, poets, filmmakers, and performance artists who recognized in Cage's work a model for breaking down boundaries between art and life.[^59]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Throughout his career, John Cage remained one of the most controversial figures in contemporary music, with his experimental works provoking responses ranging from hostile rejection to reverential admiration.[^60] His radical reconceptualization of music challenged fundamental assumptions about composition, performance, and the aesthetic experience, ensuring that his work would remain divisive throughout his lifetime and beyond.[^61]

The premiere and subsequent performances of *4'33"* generated immediate and intense controversy.[^62] Critics and audience members struggled with whether the piece constituted music at all or was instead a conceptual prank, a philosophical statement, or an act of artistic charlatanism.[^63] Traditional musicians found the work particularly threatening because it appeared to undermine the very foundation of musical practice—the intentional production of sounds through skilled performance.[^64] One audience member reportedly told Cage, "John you are important to me but I cannot listen to the noise you inflict on us," while others called his work "the worst kind of squalor of sounds that can be imagined."[^65]

Even some of Cage's modernist colleagues rejected his chance-based compositions. Pierre Boulez, the French composer and conductor who had been Cage's friend, eventually broke with him over fundamental aesthetic disagreements about indeterminacy.[^66] Boulez felt that chance-determined works like *Atlas Eclipticalis* (1961-62), where star charts were used to determine pitch placement, were "no longer music" because they abdicated the composer's responsibility for making meaningful artistic choices.[^67] This composition caused particular outrage when performed by the New York Philharmonic, with orchestra members reportedly expressing open hostility toward the work and its composer.[^68]

The critical assessment that Cage's music was "more interesting to think about than to hear" became a common refrain among skeptics.[^69] This criticism contained a grain of truth that Cage himself would likely have accepted: his work operated as much in the realm of ideas and philosophy as in the domain of conventional musical pleasure.[^70] His compositions challenged listeners to reconceptualize their relationship to sound and listening rather than providing the emotional catharsis or formal satisfaction associated with traditional music.[^71]

Commercially, Cage's work never achieved popular success or widespread public acceptance.[^72] While his influence on the avant-garde was profound, his music remained largely inaccessible to mainstream audiences who found it difficult, frustrating, or incomprehensible.[^73] Traditional conservative musicians and audiences reacted "often with indifference at best, and hostility at worst," viewing Cage's experiments as attacks on musical tradition rather than extensions of it.[^74]

Some critics argued that Cage's adoption of chance operations represented an abdication of artistic responsibility rather than a liberation from ego-driven composition.[^75] By removing intentional control from the compositional process, these critics contended, Cage eliminated the possibility of meaningful expression and reduced music-making to arbitrary sound production.[^76] The charge that chance music was essentially random and therefore devoid of artistic value persisted throughout Cage's career, despite his careful articulation of how chance operations functioned within structured compositional systems.[^77]

The question of whether Cage's prepared piano technique was truly his innovation also sparked scholarly debate, with some musicologists noting that Henry Cowell and others had experimented with placing objects on piano strings before Cage's systematic development of the technique.[^78] While Cage himself credited Cowell as an inspiration, some viewed Cage's claim to invention as overstated.[^79]

## Personal Life

John Cage's personal life reflected the same unconventionality and willingness to challenge social norms that characterized his artistic practice.[^80] In 1935, Cage married Xenia Andreyevna Kashevaroff, an Alaska-born artist and bookbinder of Russian descent.[^81] The marriage lasted until 1945, when Cage left Xenia for Merce Cunningham, initiating a profound personal and professional transformation.[^82]

Cage's relationship with Merce Cunningham became the defining partnership of his life—a fifty-year collaboration that encompassed romantic, creative, and intellectual dimensions.[^83] They first met in 1938 at the Cornish School for the Arts in Seattle, where Cage worked as a class accompanist and the nineteen-year-old Cunningham was a student.[^84] Their romantic relationship began in earnest in New York in 1942, initially conducted discreetly due to Cage's marriage and the social climate of the era.[^85] The pair remained intensely private about their personal relationship throughout their lives, rarely discussing it publicly even after societal attitudes toward same-sex relationships began to evolve.[^86]

A collection of Cage's love letters to Cunningham, published posthumously as *Love, Icebox: Letters from John Cage to Merce Cunningham*, reveals the depth of their emotional bond and provides intimate glimpses into what has been called "one of the great Modernist love affairs."[^87] The letters document the evolution of their relationship from initial attraction to a mature partnership that sustained both men's creative work for five decades.[^88] They staged more than fifty dance works together before Cage's death, with their artistic collaboration remaining consistent and generative throughout.[^89]

Beyond his relationship with Cunningham, Cage cultivated deep friendships with numerous artists, composers, and intellectuals who shared his experimental sensibilities. These included composers Earle Brown, Morton Feldman, and Christian Wolff (together forming what became known as the New York School of composers); visual artists Robert Rauschenberg and Jasper Johns; and writer Norman O. Brown.[^90] These relationships provided intellectual stimulation and mutual support within a community of like-minded experimentalists.[^91]

Cage was an avid mycologist—an expert in mushrooms and fungi—throughout his adult life, pursuing this scientific interest with the same intensity he brought to music.[^92] In 1962, he co-founded the New York Mycological Society, serving as its president and leading mushroom-foraging expeditions.[^93] This seemingly idiosyncratic hobby reflected Cage's broader philosophy of attention and discovery: mushroom hunting required careful observation, patience, and openness to finding unexpected varieties in unlikely places—qualities that directly paralleled his approach to listening and composition.[^94] He once appeared on the Italian television quiz show *Lascia o raddoppia?* (*Double or Nothing?*) as an expert on mushrooms, winning significant prize money.[^95]

In his later years, Cage pursued visual art with increasing dedication, producing prints, watercolors, and drawings that employed chance operations similar to those in his musical compositions.[^96] These visual works extended his aesthetic investigations into new media while demonstrating the consistency of his philosophical approach across different art forms.[^97]

Despite achieving significant recognition within the avant-garde art world, Cage maintained a relatively modest lifestyle and remained committed to his work with characteristic discipline.[^98] In the final five years of his life, though nearly paralyzed by arthritis, recovering from a stroke, and suffering from various ailments, he continued composing at an extraordinary pace, creating the *Number Pieces*—over forty works that represent some of his most refined explorations of time and sound.[^99] On August 11, 1992, while preparing evening tea for himself and Cunningham, Cage suffered another stroke and was taken to St. Vincent's Hospital in Manhattan, where he died the following morning at age 79, just weeks before a major celebration of his 80th birthday planned in Frankfurt.[^100]

## Legacy

John Cage's influence on contemporary music, performance art, and aesthetic philosophy extends far beyond his immediate circle of collaborators, establishing him as one of the defining figures of 20th-century experimental art.[^101] Critics and musicologists consistently rank him among the most influential composers of the modern era, with his ideas continuing to generate new artistic possibilities decades after his death.[^102]

Within the realm of classical and experimental music, Cage's impact proved both immediate and enduring. His rhythmic structure experiments and interest in sound directly influenced close associates Earle Brown, Morton Feldman, and Christian Wolff, who together with Cage formed the New York School of composers.[^103] This influence then spread to minimalist composers including La Monte Young, Terry Riley, Steve Reich, and Philip Glass, all of whom acknowledged how Cage's work opened pathways for their own explorations of repetition, stasis, and non-developmental musical processes.[^104] The English experimental school—comprising composers Michael Parsons, Christopher Hobbs, John White, Gavin Bryars, and Howard Skempton—similarly recognized Cage's pioneering work as foundational to their own practices.[^105]

The development of electronic and electroacoustic music bears Cage's imprint, with his tape compositions and use of electronic instruments providing models for subsequent generations of experimentalists.[^106] Brian Eno, the influential ambient music pioneer and producer, acknowledged Cage's impact and released several of Cage's works on his Obscure Records label in the mid-1970s.[^107] Musicologist Paul Hegarty traced the origins of noise music directly to *4'33"*, arguing that Cage's redefinition of music opened space for artists to explore extreme, non-musical sounds as legitimate aesthetic material.[^108]

Cage's influence extended remarkably into popular music, where rock acts such as Sonic Youth (who performed some of the *Number Pieces*), Stereolab (who named a song after Cage), and Radiohead incorporated his aesthetic principles into their work.[^109] Electronic music producer Aphex Twin featured the prepared piano on the album *Drukqs* (2001), bringing Cage's innovation to a new generation of listeners.[^110] This cross-pollination between experimental classical music and popular forms would likely have pleased Cage, who sought to break down hierarchical distinctions between different musical cultures.[^111]

Beyond music, Cage's use of chance operations and his reconceptualization of the artist's role profoundly influenced visual arts movements including Neo-Dada, Fluxus, and Conceptual art.[^112] Artists associated with these movements—including Allan Kaprow (who studied with Cage), Yoko Ono, George Brecht, and numerous others—adopted and adapted Cage's methods, creating happenings, event scores, and conceptual works that blurred boundaries between art and everyday life.[^113] His principle that art should imitate nature "in her manner of operation" rather than in appearance influenced artists working across media to develop process-based and systems-oriented approaches.[^114]

Cage's extensive writings, particularly his book *Silence: Lectures and Writings* (1961), ensured that his ideas reached audiences beyond those who encountered his music in performance.[^115] These texts, combining philosophical reflection, practical instruction, humor, and typographical experimentation, influenced poets, playwrights, and writers who found in Cage's work permission to experiment with form and meaning.[^116] The "mesostic" poems he created in later life—texts where a vertical string of letters down the center of the page spelled out a name or word—demonstrated how chance operations could generate new literary forms.[^117]

In the field of dance, Cage's collaboration with Cunningham established new paradigms for the relationship between music and movement that continue to influence contemporary choreography.[^118] Their principle of independence—where music and dance share time but not expressive content—freed choreographers from the need to illustrate or interpret musical meaning, allowing movement to develop according to its own logic.[^119]

Contemporary musicians and performance artists continue to engage with Cage's work through performances, reinterpretations, and creative extensions of his methods.[^120] The centenary of his birth in 2012 occasioned worldwide celebrations and performances, demonstrating the vitality of his artistic legacy.[^121] Young composers continue to discover in Cage's work provocations and possibilities for their own practice, ensuring that his influence remains active rather than merely historical.[^122]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and advocates of experimental music consistently celebrate John Cage as a visionary who fundamentally expanded music's possibilities and liberated compositional practice from outdated conventions.[^123] Musicologist Richard Taruskin, despite his complex relationship with Cage's work, acknowledged that "no composer of the twentieth century has been more influential" within the domain of experimental music.[^124] Supporters emphasize how Cage's philosophical approach—grounded in Zen Buddhism's principles of non-attachment and acceptance—offered an alternative to the European tradition's emphasis on personal expression and formal development.[^125]

Advocates particularly praise Cage's democratization of sound, his insistence that any auditory phenomenon could constitute music if properly framed and attended to.[^126] This openness, they argue, broke down elitist hierarchies that privileged certain sounds (produced by traditional instruments, organized according to Western harmonic principles) over others.[^127] By directing attention to environmental sounds, traffic noise, coughs from the audience, and the ambient acoustics of performance spaces, Cage invited listeners to engage with their sonic environment with the same concentrated awareness typically reserved for masterworks of the classical tradition.[^128]

Defenders of Cage's chance operations argue that they represent not an abdication of artistic responsibility but a more subtle and profound form of compositional engagement.[^129] Rather than imposing personal preferences and habitual patterns onto musical material, Cage created sophisticated systems that generated unexpected results, discoveries that would have been impossible through conventional composition.[^130] This approach, supporters contend, aligned with scientific methods of experimentation and with creative practices in non-Western cultures where improvisation and spontaneity play central roles.[^131]

Cultural critics and philosophers have celebrated Cage's challenge to authorship and originality, seeing in his work a prefiguration of postmodern skepticism toward the autonomous artistic genius.[^132] His questioning of fundamental categories—What is music? Who is the composer? What constitutes a performance?—opened intellectual territory that artists continue to explore.[^133] The interdisciplinary nature of his work, crossing boundaries between music, visual art, poetry, performance, and philosophy, provided a model for subsequent generations of artists who resist specialization in favor of expansive, multimedia practices.[^134]

### Negative Views

Critics of Cage's work offer numerous objections to both his compositional methods and his broader aesthetic philosophy. Conservative musicians and scholars argue that his chance-based compositions represent a rejection of music's essential qualities—intentional expression, formal coherence, and emotional communication.[^135] By removing the composer's will from the creative process, these critics contend, Cage eliminated the possibility of meaningful artistic statement, reducing composition to arbitrary sound production indistinguishable from random environmental noise.[^136]

The assessment that Cage's music is "more interesting to think about than to hear" constitutes a persistent criticism that questions whether his work succeeds as sonic art regardless of its conceptual interest.[^137] Skeptics argue that Cage's compositions often fail to provide the perceptual richness, formal satisfaction, or emotional depth that listeners legitimately seek from musical experience.[^138] His emphasis on philosophy over sensory pleasure, they suggest, appeals primarily to intellectuals interested in ideas rather than to audiences seeking profound aesthetic experiences.[^139]

Some critics view Cage's embrace of Zen Buddhism and Eastern philosophy as superficial appropriation rather than deep understanding, arguing that he selectively adapted concepts to justify predetermined aesthetic positions rather than genuinely transforming his consciousness through spiritual practice.[^140] These critics note that Cage's application of chance operations to maintain systematic control over compositional parameters contradicts Zen's emphasis on spontaneity and direct experience.[^141]

Musicologists have questioned whether Cage's influence has been entirely beneficial for contemporary music, suggesting that his extreme positions may have encouraged lesser talents to produce indulgent, undisciplined works lacking either conceptual rigor or sensory appeal.[^142] The proliferation of chance-based compositions and conceptual pieces in the decades following Cage's innovations led to works that imitated his methods without achieving comparable philosophical depth or sonic interest.[^143]

Political critics, particularly from Marxist perspectives, have argued that Cage's aesthetic of acceptance and non-intention serves quietist ideological functions, encouraging passive reception of existing conditions rather than critical engagement or transformative action.[^144] His famous statement "I have nothing to say and I am saying it" strikes these critics as an abdication of the artist's responsibility to address social and political concerns.[^145] Where other avant-garde composers used experimental techniques to express opposition to oppression and injustice, Cage's work appears to these critics as politically neutered, accepting all sounds equally without discrimination or judgment.[^146]

## Famous Quotes

> "There is no such thing as an empty space or an empty time. There is always something to see, something to hear. In fact, try as we may to make a silence, we cannot." — From his experience in the anechoic chamber, articulating the philosophy behind *4'33"*[^147]

> "The highest purpose is to have no purpose at all. This puts one in accordance with nature, in her manner of operation." — Expressing his Zen-influenced aesthetic philosophy[^148]

> "I have nothing to say, and I am saying it, and that is poetry." — On the relationship between absence of intention and artistic creation[^149]

> "If something is boring after two minutes, try it for four. If still boring, then eight. Then sixteen. Then thirty-two. Eventually one discovers that it is not boring at all." — On attention, patience, and the transformation of perception[^150]

> "I can't understand why people are frightened of new ideas. I'm frightened of the old ones." — On innovation versus tradition[^151]

> "It is not irritating to be where one is. It is only irritating to think one would like to be somewhere else." — On acceptance and presence[^152]

> "When you make music you are acting as a philosopher. You can either do that consciously or you can do it unconsciously, but you're doing it." — On the philosophical dimension of musical practice[^153]
