## Summary

Fire at the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory in New York on March 25, 1911 that killed 146 garment workers, mostly young immigrant women. Locked exit doors prevented escape and led to major labor reforms.

## Working conditions and context

- The Triangle Shirtwaist Factory operated in Manhattan and employed a largely immigrant workforce, including many young women.
- Early 20th-century garment work often involved long hours, crowded floors, and limited workplace protections.
- Labor organizing and demands for safer conditions were already prominent issues in New York’s garment industry before 1911.

## The fire and immediate causes

- The fire broke out on March 25, 1911 and spread rapidly through the factory floors.
- Many workers were unable to escape due to blocked or locked exits and inadequate fire-safety infrastructure.
- The death toll reached 146, making it one of the deadliest industrial disasters in U.S. history.

## Public response and reforms

- The catastrophe triggered public outrage, mass funerals, and calls for investigations into labor practices.
- New York State created investigatory bodies that examined factory conditions and recommended changes.
- The fire is frequently cited as a catalyst for improved building codes, fire-safety rules, and workplace regulations.

## Legacy

- The Triangle fire became a lasting symbol in labor history and is often referenced in debates about worker safety and employer responsibility.
- It helped shape the reform agenda for progressive-era labor legislation and the broader push for industrial safety standards.
