## Summary

New Orleans socialite who kept enslaved people chained in her attic and subjected them to horrific torture. A fire in 1834 revealed her torture chamber, and she fled to France.

## Background and Setting

LaLaurie lived in antebellum New Orleans, a slave society where wealthy households often held enslaved people as domestic and field labor. Her case is discussed within the broader history of slavery in Louisiana and the vulnerability of enslaved people to abuse with limited legal protection or recourse.

## 1834 House Fire and Discovery

Accounts of the 1834 incident generally describe a fire at the LaLaurie residence that led to authorities and neighbors entering the home. Reports of abused and confined enslaved individuals prompted public outrage, and the house later became infamous in local memory.

## Flight and Later Life

After the scandal, LaLaurie is widely reported to have fled the United States, ultimately living in France. Details about her later years, including the exact circumstances and location of her death, have been described differently across sources and retellings.

## Historical Debate and Retellings

The LaLaurie story has been repeatedly retold in newspapers, local histories, and popular culture. While the core event (the 1834 fire and allegations of severe abuse) is a consistent theme, specific claims and sensational details sometimes vary, reflecting the way the case moved from contemporary reporting into legend.
