## Summary

British colonial forces detained 1.5 million Kenyans in concentration camps, subjecting them to systematic torture, forced labor, and starvation. An estimated 20,000-100,000 died.

## Additional Context

- The detention camp system emerged during the **Kenya Emergency**, when colonial authorities sought to isolate suspected insurgents and control rural populations.
- Camps, “works” sites, and screening centers were part of a wider security architecture that also included forced villagization, curfews, and collective punishment.

## Conditions and Practices (High Level)

- Survivors and later investigations describe coercive interrogation, forced labor, overcrowding, disease, and malnutrition.
- Documentation of abuse has been pieced together from survivor testimony, archival records, and later legal proceedings.

## Aftermath and Accountability

- Decades later, civil litigation and archival disclosures renewed public attention on colonial violence and official responsibility.
- Compensation and apologies do not resolve historical debates, but they have been treated as a significant acknowledgment of harm by parts of the UK government.

## Historical Significance

- The case is frequently cited in discussions of empire, counterinsurgency tactics, and the long-term social effects of mass detention.
- It also illustrates how state record-keeping, secrecy, and document preservation shape what can be proven in court and in scholarship.
