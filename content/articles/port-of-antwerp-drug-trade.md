## Summary

The Port of Antwerp has become Europe's main cocaine gateway with 110 tonnes seized in 2022 alone. Corrupt port workers and sophisticated logistics enable the trade.

## Context

- Antwerp is one of Europe’s largest container ports, linking global shipping lanes with dense road, rail, and inland waterway networks into the EU single market.
- Large container volumes, complex logistics chains, and time pressure to move cargo can create opportunities for concealment and diversion.

## How the trafficking typically works (high-level)

- Cocaine is commonly moved in maritime containers, concealed among legitimate cargo or hidden within the container structure.
- “Rip-on/rip-off” methods are widely documented in European ports: illicit packages are added to a container before departure or removed after arrival without the legitimate shipper’s involvement.
- Criminal networks may rely on insider access (e.g., coerced or bribed workers) to locate specific containers, bypass controls, or arrange fast removal.
- Smuggling operations may use short dwell times and rapid onward transport to reduce the chance of detection after discharge.

## Why major ports are attractive targets

- High throughput makes comprehensive physical inspection of all containers impractical.
- The supply chain involves many actors (shipping lines, terminals, trucking, warehousing), increasing attack surface for corruption or infiltration.
- The same infrastructure that makes ports efficient for lawful trade (automation, standardized containers, just-in-time scheduling) can also be exploited by organized crime.

## Law enforcement and policy response

- Authorities have expanded risk-based screening, intelligence-led targeting, and coordination across customs, police, and port operators.
- Common countermeasures include:
  - Increased scanning capacity and container screening prioritization.
  - Anti-corruption investigations and worker vetting in sensitive port roles.
  - Joint investigations with partner countries to disrupt upstream supply chains and downstream distribution.
  - Asset seizures and financial investigations aimed at dismantling networks rather than only intercepting shipments.

## Impact and risks

- Violence and intimidation can increase when criminal groups compete for access, attempt to recover seized loads, or pressure insiders.
- Corruption risk extends beyond the port perimeter into transport and logistics services tied to container movement.
- Large-scale smuggling can strain judicial and detention systems due to the volume and complexity of cases.

## Notes on measurement

- Seizure totals (e.g., tonnes per year) are often used as a proxy for trafficking pressure, but they can reflect both underlying volume and changes in detection capacity.
- Comparisons across years should account for operational changes (new scanners, targeted operations) and shifts in trafficking routes.
