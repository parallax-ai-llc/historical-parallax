## Summary

After Siad Barre's overthrow, clan-based warlords divided Somalia, causing famine that killed 300,000. Mohamed Farrah Aidid became infamous during the Battle of Mogadishu.

## Additional Context

### Collapse of central authority

Following the fall of President Siad Barre in 1991, Somalia experienced a prolonged breakdown of central governance. Armed factions organized along clan and sub-clan lines competed for territory, resources, ports, and aid distribution routes.

### Warlord politics

The term “warlord” in the Somali context commonly refers to militia leaders who combined military power with political and economic control, often funded through checkpoints, port revenues, protection rackets, and external support. Alliances shifted frequently, making ceasefires fragile.

### Humanitarian crisis

Armed conflict and drought contributed to famine conditions in the early 1990s, with mass civilian suffering and displacement. The manipulation or obstruction of aid routes became a major concern for humanitarian agencies operating under insecurity.

### International intervention and its limits

UN and US-led interventions (often referenced through operations such as UNOSOM and UNITAF) aimed to secure humanitarian access and stabilize key areas. The 1993 Battle of Mogadishu, involving forces associated with Mohamed Farrah Aidid, became a turning point that shaped international perceptions of intervention risks.

### Long-run consequences

Somalia’s warlord era is often discussed as a precursor to later political fragmentation and the emergence of other armed actors. The period remains a case study in how state collapse can produce self-reinforcing cycles of violence, informal taxation, and localized authority.

A recurring lesson in analyses is that rebuilding legitimacy and basic services can be as important as security operations when attempting to exit a warlord-dominated equilibrium.
