## Summary

Canadian con artist who defrauded banks of millions by claiming to be Andrew Carnegies illegitimate daughter. She used forged promissory notes.

## Background and aliases

Chadwick was born **Elizabeth Bigley** in Canada and later lived in the United States, using multiple names and identities in different cities.[^1] Her notoriety stems from an early example of modern financial fraud: constructing a credible story, forging documents to support it, and leveraging social pressure to obtain credit.

## How the Carnegie story worked

Her most famous scheme relied on claiming to be the **illegitimate daughter of industrialist Andrew Carnegie**. The fraud depended on a few reinforcing elements:

- **Forged promissory notes and documents** that appeared to show Carnegie owing her large sums.[^1]
- **Cultivated secrecy**: lenders were encouraged to believe the matter was private and politically sensitive.
- **Reputation by association**: once one bank extended credit, the fact that she had obtained loans from others became “proof” of legitimacy.

## Financial and social impact

The sums involved were large for the era, and multiple institutions extended loans based primarily on perceived access to Carnegie’s wealth rather than verified collateral.[^1] The case is often discussed as a cautionary example of:

- weak due diligence and overreliance on social status,
- the risk of lending against expected inheritance or reputational claims,
- how counterfeit documentation can exploit institutional incentives.

## Arrest, conviction, and death

Chadwick was arrested after the scheme began to unravel and eventually pleaded guilty and received a lengthy prison sentence.[^1] She died while incarcerated in 1907.

## Legacy

Cassie Chadwick is remembered less for technical sophistication than for demonstrating how **narrative credibility** can function as leverage in financial markets. Her story remains a frequently cited historical fraud case in discussions of banking risk and verification practices.
