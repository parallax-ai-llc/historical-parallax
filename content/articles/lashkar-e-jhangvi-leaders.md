## Summary

Riaz Basra and Akram Lahori led Lashkar-e-Jhangvi, Pakistan's most violent sectarian terrorist group responsible for hundreds of Shia Muslim killings. Basra was killed in a police encounter.

## Overview

Lashkar-e-Jhangvi (LeJ) is widely described as a Sunni extremist, anti-Shia militant organization that emerged in Pakistan in the 1990s. It is commonly associated with a campaign of sectarian violence, including targeted attacks and killings of Shia civilians.

Because militant groups frequently splinter, rebrand, and operate through loose networks, leadership attribution can be contested. Public accounts about LeJ leadership are typically based on reporting, security briefings, and secondary sources rather than transparent internal records.

## Leadership and roles

High-level descriptions of leadership commonly distinguish between:

- **Founders and early organizers** who established the group’s ideological framing and recruitment networks.
- **Operational commanders** who coordinated attacks, logistics, and local cells.
- **Spokespersons and propagandists** who communicated threats or claimed responsibility.

In many cases, alleged leaders have also been described as having prior links to other sectarian organizations and to broader jihadist ecosystems in the region.

## Modus operandi (general)

Analysts often describe LeJ-linked violence as involving:

- Targeted shootings or ambushes
- Attacks on religious gatherings and processions
- Intimidation campaigns against minority communities

Attribution of specific incidents can vary across sources, and responsibility claims—when present—may not be independently verifiable.

## Impact and responses

LeJ is frequently cited in discussions of sectarian conflict in Pakistan. State responses have included arrests, prosecutions, and security operations; however, observers also note that sectarian militancy can persist through decentralized cells and successor groupings.
