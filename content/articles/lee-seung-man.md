## Summary

Syngman Rhee (Lee Seung-man, 이승만) was the first President of the Republic of Korea, serving from 1948 to 1960.[^1] A 16th-generation descendant of Korean royalty, he transformed from an independence activist and scholar into a founding father of modern South Korea.[^2] His presidency was marked by fierce anti-communism, authoritarian rule, and the tumultuous Korean War.[^3] Forced to resign following the April Revolution of 1960, Rhee's legacy remains deeply controversial—simultaneously celebrated as the architect of South Korean statehood and condemned for political repression and human rights abuses.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Born on March 26, 1875, in Daegyeong, a village in Pyeongsan County, Hwanghae Province, Syngman Rhee came from aristocratic lineage as a 16th-generation descendant of King Taejong of Joseon through Grand Prince Yangnyeong.[^5] He was the third son but the only surviving child among three brothers and two sisters, as his two older brothers died in infancy.[^6] Despite his noble heritage, Rhee's family lived in modest rural circumstances, struggling financially during the declining years of the Joseon Dynasty.

In 1877, at age two, Rhee's family relocated to Seoul, where he received traditional Confucian education in various seodang (traditional Korean schools) in the Nakdong and Dodong neighborhoods.[^7] This classical education grounded him in the scholarly traditions that had dominated Korean intellectual life for centuries. However, the trajectory of his life changed dramatically in 1894 when, at age 19, he enrolled in Pai Chai Academy, an American Methodist missionary school.[^8] There, Rhee encountered Western education, learning English, science, and modern political thought—a transformative experience that would shape his worldview.

At Pai Chai, Rhee converted to Christianity, embracing a faith that would remain central to his identity throughout his life.[^9] The conversion was not merely spiritual but intensely political; Rhee believed Christianity represented modernization and reform for Korea, and that helping others was the divine path to saving his nation.[^10] His Methodist education also introduced him to democratic ideals and nationalist thinking, fueling his growing awareness of Korea's vulnerability amid imperial competition between China, Russia, and Japan.

Rhee's nationalist fervor intensified when he joined the Independence Club in 1896, a reform organization composed of young Korean intellectuals protesting against Japanese and Russian imperial encroachment.[^11] The Independence Club advocated for Korean sovereignty, constitutional monarchy, and modernization—radical ideas in the conservative Joseon court. Rhee threw himself into political activism, writing articles for independent newspapers and organizing public demonstrations, activities that would soon bring him into dangerous conflict with the authorities.

---

## Rise to Power

Rhee's political activism led to his arrest in 1897 when he was implicated in a plot to remove King Kojong from power.[^12] In 1899, he was formally imprisoned for his participation in what authorities deemed an anti-government conspiracy.[^13] Rhee spent nearly six years in Seoul's Hanseong Prison under harsh conditions, sharing cells with common criminals and political dissidents. Rather than breaking his spirit, imprisonment became a crucible for Rhee's intellectual and spiritual development. He deepened his Christian faith, convinced that God had chosen him for a special mission to liberate Korea.[^14] He also continued his education in prison, reading extensively and even teaching fellow inmates English and Christian theology.

Released from prison in 1904 following the outbreak of the Russo-Japanese War, Rhee immediately resumed his reform activities.[^15] However, recognizing the increasing danger of Japanese colonization, he immigrated to the United States in November 1905 with assistance from American missionaries who had been impressed by his academic abilities and strong character during his imprisonment.[^16] In America, Rhee pursued an extraordinary academic career. He graduated from George Washington University in 1907 with a Bachelor of Arts degree, earned a Master's degree from Harvard University in 1908, and obtained his doctorate in political science from Princeton University in 1910—becoming the first Korean to receive a Ph.D. from an American university.[^17] His Princeton dissertation, "Neutrality as Influenced by the United States," examined international diplomacy and reflected his ongoing concern for Korea's geopolitical position.

Tragically, 1910 was also the year Japan formally annexed Korea, extinguishing Korean sovereignty and beginning 35 years of colonial rule.[^18] Rhee, now in America with advanced degrees but a homeland under occupation, dedicated himself entirely to the Korean independence movement. He spent the next three decades traveling globally as what he called "a mouthpiece for Korean independence," lobbying American politicians, addressing international conferences, and writing pamphlets advocating for Korean liberation.[^19]

In 1919, following the March 1st Movement—a massive peaceful uprising against Japanese rule brutally suppressed by colonial authorities—Korean independence activists established the Korean Provisional Government in Shanghai.[^20] Rhee was elected president in absentia, serving from 1919 to 1925 when he was impeached by younger, more radical nationalists based in China who criticized his diplomatic approach as too accommodating to Western powers.[^21] Despite this setback, Rhee was reinstated as president of the Provisional Government from 1947 to 1948 as Korean independence finally appeared achievable following Japan's defeat in World War II.[^22]

When Korea was liberated in August 1945, American military forces occupied the southern half of the peninsula while Soviet forces controlled the north. The U.S. military government facilitated Rhee's return to Korea in October 1945 after 33 years of exile.[^23] He received a hero's welcome from Koreans who saw him as a legendary independence fighter and from Americans who viewed him as a reliable anti-communist partner. Rhee skillfully built a mass political organization supported by youth groups that sometimes employed intimidation and violence against rivals.[^24] With major moderate leaders assassinated—including Kim Ku, who was mysteriously killed in 1949—Rhee's Liberal Party dominated the political landscape.

In May 1948, under United Nations supervision, South Korea held its first constitutional assembly elections.[^25] On July 17, 1948, the National Assembly adopted the Constitution of the Republic of Korea, and on July 20, 1948, Rhee was elected President by the Assembly with an overwhelming 92.3% of the vote.[^26] On August 15, 1948, the Republic of Korea was formally proclaimed, and 73-year-old Syngman Rhee became South Korea's first president.

## Major Achievements

### Establishment of the Republic of Korea

Rhee's most significant accomplishment was establishing South Korea as an independent, sovereign state separate from the communist North.[^27] In 1948, amid Cold War tensions and Soviet-American disagreements over Korea's future, Rhee insisted on creating a separate southern government rather than waiting indefinitely for unified elections that seemed increasingly impossible.[^28] While this decision permanently divided the peninsula, it also created the political foundation for what would eventually become one of Asia's most prosperous democracies. Rhee's unwavering anti-communist stance ensured South Korea's alignment with the United States and the Western bloc during the critical early Cold War period.

### Land Reform

One of Rhee's most progressive and impactful policies was comprehensive land reform, implemented through the Land Reform Act passed on April 28, 1950.[^29] The reform set maximum land ownership limits at 3 chongbo (approximately 3 hectares) per household and required government purchase of excess land from landlords for redistribution to tenant farmers.[^30] Before land reform, in 1945, two-thirds of arable land was owned by just 3 percent of the population, and 80 percent of rural Koreans owned no land at all.[^31] By 1957, these statistics had dramatically reversed: 88 percent of rural Koreans owned land, and tenancy rates fell from over 60% to less than 20%.[^32]

This land reform fundamentally transformed Korean rural society, eliminating the feudal landlord class and creating a nation of independent small farmers. Scholars credit this reform as essential groundwork for South Korea's later economic development, as it created social stability and a more equitable distribution of resources that facilitated capitalist development.[^33] The reform was implemented largely without violent resistance, unlike land reforms in many other developing nations.

### The ROK-US Mutual Defense Treaty

Despite opposing the Korean War armistice—believing the conflict should continue until Korea was reunified by force—Rhee successfully leveraged his resistance to secure the ROK-US Mutual Defense Treaty.[^34] In June 1953, Rhee ordered the unilateral release of approximately 25,000 anti-communist North Korean prisoners of war, deliberately sabotaging armistice negotiations and infuriating both American allies and communist adversaries.[^35] This bold, reckless action prolonged the war by several months and cost an estimated 35,000 additional UN casualties.[^36]

However, it also demonstrated to Washington that Rhee could not be ignored or coerced. To secure his cooperation with the armistice, U.S. Secretary of State John Foster Dulles traveled to Seoul in July 1953 to negotiate a mutual defense pact.[^37] The treaty, signed on October 1, 1953, and effective from November 1954, guaranteed American military protection for South Korea and established the framework for the enduring ROK-US alliance that continues today.[^38] Rhee envisioned this treaty not merely as bilateral security but as the foundation for a broader Pacific alliance including the Philippines, Taiwan, and Thailand, unified by anti-communism and American leadership.[^39]

### Educational Development

Rhee strongly promoted education as essential for national development. He emphasized both traditional Korean values and modern Western learning, believing educated citizens were crucial for Korea's future.[^40] In a 1957 speech, Rhee advocated for child-centered education: "When possible, children should be comfortable and free when they are playing or studying, and it is the way in a civilized country to make them more comfortable by not being too strict and giving grace and love at the same time."[^41] Under his administration, educational institutions expanded significantly, laying groundwork for South Korea's later emphasis on education as a driver of economic development.

### Ilminism and Social Reform

Rhee's political ideology, known as Ilminism (One People Principle), advocated progressive social reforms including elimination of formal discrimination between nobility and commoners, economic equalization of rich and poor through land reform, social and political equality between sexes, and elimination of discrimination between North and South or urban and rural areas.[^42] While implementation was incomplete and often contradicted by his authoritarian governance, these principles represented forward-thinking ideals for post-colonial Korea.

## Controversies and Criticisms

### Authoritarian Rule and Political Repression

Rhee's presidency was fundamentally authoritarian, characterized by systematic suppression of political opposition and civil liberties.[^43] Backed by the United States for his staunch anti-communism, Rhee's rule was nevertheless marred by corruption, repression, and profoundly anti-democratic conduct.[^44] He employed street gangs, police forces, and intelligence agencies to intimidate, arrest, torture, and assassinate political rivals regardless of their ideology.[^45] His internal security forces, led by Kim Chang-ryong, detained and brutally tortured suspected communists, North Korean agents, and anyone deemed a political threat.[^46]

In December 1958, Rhee forced through the National Assembly an amendment to the National Security Law granting sweeping powers to curtail press freedom and prevent opposition members from voting.[^47] The National Security Law, modeled on the notorious Public Order Maintenance Act used by Japanese colonial authorities, became the primary instrument for suppressing dissent and criminalizing political opposition under the guise of anti-communism.[^48]

### The Bodo League Massacre

Perhaps the darkest chapter of Rhee's presidency was the Bodo League massacre of 1950, one of the worst atrocities in modern Korean history.[^49] The National Bodo League was ostensibly a "re-education" program for suspected communist sympathizers, enrolling approximately 300,000 people on the pretext of protecting them from execution.[^50] However, many enrolled were not communists at all but political opponents, or ordinary citizens forced to join to meet enrollment quotas.[^51]

When North Korea invaded on June 25, 1950, President Rhee ordered the execution of Bodo League members and suspected leftists to prevent them from potentially aiding the invaders.[^52] According to military police officer Kim Mansik, Rhee personally ordered these executions on June 27, 1950.[^53] Over the summer of 1950, South Korean security forces systematically massacred between 60,000 and 200,000 people—the exact number remains disputed—without trials or legal proceedings.[^54] United States military officers witnessed and photographed the killings but did not intervene.[^55]

The South Korean government falsely blamed the massacres on North Korean communists and concealed the truth for four decades.[^56] Survivors were forbidden from revealing what happened under threat of being labeled communist sympathizers, facing torture and death if they spoke publicly.[^57] Only in the 1990s, after South Korea's democratization, did the truth begin to emerge, and the government finally acknowledged responsibility in the 2000s.

### Execution of Cho Bong-am and Suppression of the Progressive Party

The case of Cho Bong-am exemplifies Rhee's intolerance of legitimate political opposition. Cho was a respected independence activist and politician who founded the Progressive Party in 1956.[^58] In the May 1956 presidential election, Cho secured approximately 2.2 million votes—about 30% of the total—far exceeding expectations and demonstrating significant public support for his moderate platform.[^59]

The Progressive Party advocated peaceful reunification with North Korea through democratic means, anti-authoritarian politics, and social welfare policies for peasants and the urban poor.[^60] While anti-communist, Cho opposed Rhee's extreme methods and called for dialogue rather than perpetual confrontation. Rhee perceived this as a fundamental threat to his power. On January 13, 1958, Cho and other party officials were arrested on espionage charges and accused of violating the National Security Law.[^61]

The Seoul District Court initially sentenced Cho to five years imprisonment but found that the Progressive Party's platform did not violate the National Security Law.[^62] However, the appellate court, under political pressure, reversed this decision and sentenced Cho to death, ruling that his peaceful unification proposal constituted a plan to overthrow the ROK government.[^63] Cho was executed by hanging on July 31, 1959, just one day after the Supreme Court rejected his final appeal.[^64] The Progressive Party was outlawed, and Rhee had successfully eliminated his most formidable democratic opposition.[^65]

The trial clearly demonstrated that the primary purpose was suppressing political opposition and controlling discourse about national unification, not genuine national security concerns.[^66] In 2011, over 50 years after his execution, South Korea's Supreme Court posthumously acquitted Cho Bong-am, acknowledging the trial had been politically motivated.

### Electoral Fraud and Constitutional Manipulation

Rhee repeatedly manipulated democratic processes to maintain power. When constitutional term limits threatened his continued presidency, Rhee forced constitutional amendments through the National Assembly to allow his re-election in 1952 and 1956.[^67] The 1960 presidential election, in which Rhee's ally Lee Ki-poong won the vice-presidency by an impossibly wide margin, was so blatantly rigged that it triggered nationwide protests.[^68] Electoral fraud included ballot stuffing, voter intimidation, and systematic manipulation of vote counting—practices that ultimately led to Rhee's downfall.

### Limited Economic Development

While land reform was progressive, Rhee's broader economic policies produced limited results. For much of his presidency, large portions of the Korean economy remained under government control, leading some U.S. Congress members to accuse Rhee of socialism.[^69] His administration was characterized by corruption, with government resources often distributed through patronage networks rather than market mechanisms. South Korea remained desperately poor throughout the 1950s, heavily dependent on American aid for survival. The rapid economic growth that would transform South Korea into an industrial powerhouse only began after Rhee's fall, under Park Chung-hee's regime in the 1960s-1970s.

## Personal Life

Syngman Rhee's personal life was marked by tragedy, resilience, and an uncommon marriage that bridged East and West. His first marriage was to Park Seungseon in 1890, an arranged union in the traditional Korean manner when Rhee was just 15 years old.[^70] The couple had one son, Rhee Bong-su, but the marriage was strained by Rhee's political activism and long imprisonment. Tragically, their son died in 1908 while still young, and Park divorced Rhee shortly afterward.[^71]

For over two decades, Rhee lived as a bachelor, entirely devoted to the independence movement and his academic pursuits. His life changed in February 1933 when he met Franziska Donner in Geneva while attending a League of Nations meeting.[^72] Donner, born on June 15, 1900, in Inzersdorf near Vienna, Austria, was the daughter of a soda water industrialist.[^73] Highly educated with a Ph.D. in languages from the University of Vienna, she was working as an interpreter at the League of Nations when she encountered the Korean independence activist 25 years her senior.[^74]

Despite the significant age difference and vastly different cultural backgrounds, Rhee and Donner developed a deep connection. She became his secretary and close collaborator in the independence movement.[^75] They married in October 1934 in New York City; for both, it was a second marriage.[^76] Francesca, as she became known, would serve as Rhee's partner, translator, secretary, and political advisor for over three decades. She accompanied him back to Korea in 1945 and became South Korea's inaugural First Lady when Rhee assumed the presidency in 1948.[^77]

The couple had no biological children, but over the years, Rhee adopted three sons, continuing the Korean tradition of adoption to ensure family continuity.[^78] Their adopted son Rhee In-soo (Yi In-su) would remain particularly close to Francesca throughout her life.

Rhee's personality was complex and often contradictory. Described by contemporaries as "acerbic, prickly, uncompromising," he was regarded by the U.S. State Department as "a dangerous mischief-maker."[^79] His leadership style was intensely paternalistic and personalistic, viewing himself as a father figure to the Korean nation with authority that should not be questioned.[^80] His Christian faith remained central to his identity; he genuinely believed God had chosen him specifically to save Korea from communism and foreign domination.[^81]

Yet this religious conviction coexisted with ruthless pragmatism in politics. Rhee could be charming and eloquent when lobbying Western diplomats, but authoritarian and unforgiving toward Korean opponents. He maintained unwavering certainty in his own judgment, a trait that made him an effective independence fighter but a problematic democratic leader.

## Legacy

### Exile and Death

When police shot demonstrators during protests in Masan in April 1960, student-led uprisings spread throughout South Korea in what became known as the April Revolution.[^82] On April 26, 1960, facing massive public pressure and losing American support, the 85-year-old Rhee formally resigned the presidency.[^83] He spent a month at the Ihwajang House before departing for exile in Honolulu, Hawaii on May 29, 1960, accompanied by Francesca and their adopted son.[^84]

Rhee spent his final five years in Honolulu, far from the nation he had founded. He suffered a stroke and lived under Francesca's devoted care until his death on July 19, 1965, at age 90.[^85] A week later, his body was returned to Seoul and buried with state honors at the Seoul National Cemetery.[^86] Despite his ignominious fall from power, the South Korean government recognized his role in founding the republic.

Francesca Donner Rhee initially returned to Austria after her husband's death but returned to South Korea in 1970, where she lived at Ihwajang with her adopted son's family until her own death on March 19, 1992.[^87]

### Enduring Controversies

Syngman Rhee's legacy remains among the most contested in modern Korean history. His evaluation has become not merely an academic question but an ideological battleground that reveals deep divisions in contemporary South Korean society.[^88] A 2023 Gallup Korea survey found South Koreans almost evenly divided: 30% believed Rhee "did many good things," while 40% thought he "did many wrong things," and 30% had no opinion.[^89]

Research on Rhee has expanded from academic scholarship into intense political confrontation, with competing narratives that glorify, condemn, or attempt to balance his complex record.[^90] Extreme evaluations on both sides have made neutral assessment extraordinarily difficult. Conservative groups, particularly the New Right movement, argue that Rhee's achievements have been wrongly undervalued and that he should be honored as the founding father who established South Korea as a free-market democracy allied with the West.[^91]

Progressive historians counter that this rehabilitation ignores or minimizes Rhee's authoritarianism, political violence, and betrayal of democratic principles. They argue that celebrating Rhee as a founding father legitimizes political repression and dishonors the victims of massacres and unjust executions during his rule.

### Historical Significance

Despite intense controversy, several aspects of Rhee's historical significance are undeniable. He was instrumental in establishing South Korea as an independent state separate from communist North Korea, a decision that determined the fate of 50 million people.[^92] His fierce anti-communism and alliance with the United States provided security during the Korean War and established the geopolitical framework that continues to define the peninsula.

The land reform implemented under his administration fundamentally transformed Korean society and created preconditions for later economic development.[^93] The ROK-US Mutual Defense Treaty he secured remains the cornerstone of South Korean security policy seven decades later.[^94]

However, Rhee's authoritarian governance and political violence also contributed to a political culture of intolerance and suppression that South Korea struggled for decades to overcome.[^95] The democratic institutions he established were systematically undermined by his own actions, setting precedents for future military dictatorships under Park Chung-hee and Chun Doo-hwan. Only through the pro-democracy movements of the 1980s did South Korea achieve the genuine democracy that Rhee had proclaimed but failed to practice.

A U.S. official's description captures the complexity: Rhee was "a statesman with a complicated legacy."[^96] He was simultaneously a genuine nationalist who dedicated his life to Korean independence and an authoritarian ruler who betrayed democratic principles; a visionary who imagined an independent, modern Korea and a dictator who massacred his own people; a founding father worthy of recognition and a tyrant whose crimes cannot be forgotten.

Rhee's life and legacy compel reflection on the tensions between nationalism and democracy, security and freedom, founding vision and governing reality. His story demonstrates that historical figures cannot be reduced to simple heroes or villains but must be understood in all their complexity and contradiction.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Supporters and revisionist scholars emphasize Rhee's indispensable role in establishing South Korean statehood and preventing communist takeover of the entire peninsula.[^97] They argue that without Rhee's insistence on creating a separate southern government in 1948, South Korea might not exist today, and the entire peninsula could have fallen under communist rule similar to North Korea's totalitarian system.

Conservative historians highlight that Rhee's fierce anti-communism, while excessive at times, was not irrational given the genuine threat posed by North Korean aggression and internal communist insurgency in the late 1940s and early 1950s.[^98] The Korean War, initiated by North Korea's invasion, vindicated Rhee's warnings about communist intentions. His uncompromising stance ensured South Korea remained firmly in the Western camp during the critical early Cold War period.

The New Right movement in South Korea has championed Rhee's rehabilitation, organizing conferences and publishing scholarship that emphasizes his positive contributions.[^99] They credit him with laying the foundations for the Republic of Korea and argue that subsequent economic prosperity and democratic development were built on institutions and alliances Rhee established. A 2008 conference at George Washington University, held to honor the Republic of Korea's founding, featured scholars presenting research highlighting Rhee's vision and diplomatic achievements.[^100]

Recent scholarship has also challenged simplistic left-right categorizations of Rhee. Some researchers argue that Rhee's progressive economic policies, including land reform and state intervention in the economy, demonstrate he was neither as reactionary nor as right-wing as often portrayed.[^101] His ideology of Ilminism combined nationalist, Christian, and progressive elements that don't fit neatly into conventional political categories.[^102]

Defenders acknowledge Rhee's authoritarian tendencies but contextualize them within the extreme circumstances of national division, war, and reconstruction. They argue he faced genuine security threats and political instability that made democratic governance extraordinarily difficult. By this view, Rhee's authoritarian measures, while regrettable, were responses to real dangers rather than mere power-hunger.

### Negative Views

Critics argue that Rhee's authoritarianism fundamentally betrayed the democratic principles he claimed to represent and that his political repression was excessive even by the standards of Cold War anti-communism.[^103] Progressive historians emphasize that Rhee assassinated rivals, crushed legitimate political opposition, and employed torture and violence against anyone who questioned his rule, regardless of whether they were communists or democratic reformers.[^104]

The Bodo League massacre and execution of Cho Bong-am represent inexcusable atrocities that cannot be justified by security concerns.[^105] Critics note that many victims were not communists but ordinary citizens, political moderates, or even anti-communist reformers who simply opposed Rhee's authoritarian methods. The systematic cover-up of these crimes for decades demonstrates consciousness of guilt and contradicts claims that such violence was necessary or justifiable.

Scholars critical of Rhee emphasize that his authoritarian governance established dangerous precedents that enabled subsequent military dictatorships.[^106] By undermining democratic institutions, manipulating elections, and normalizing political violence, Rhee created a political culture that made Park Chung-hee's 1961 military coup and decades of authoritarian rule more likely. South Korea's difficult path to democracy in the 1980s-1990s involved overcoming patterns of authoritarian governance that Rhee had established.

Economic critics note that Rhee's administration was characterized by corruption, cronyism, and limited development.[^107] While land reform was significant, overall economic growth during the 1950s was minimal, and South Korea remained desperately poor and dependent on American aid. The economic miracle that transformed South Korea occurred after Rhee's removal, suggesting his governance actually hindered rather than facilitated development.

International relations scholars note that Rhee's recklessness prolonged the Korean War and damaged relations with allies.[^108] His June 1953 release of POWs sabotaged armistice negotiations, causing thousands of additional casualties—including American soldiers—for no strategic gain. His constant demands for military action to reunify Korea by force ignored geopolitical realities and risked triggering World War III.

Critics also challenge hagiographic narratives about Rhee's independence activism. While he worked for Korean independence, his leadership of the Provisional Government was controversial even among fellow independence fighters, leading to his impeachment in 1925.[^109] His diplomatic approach, focused on lobbying Western powers rather than direct action, was less effective than claimed and contributed to divisions within the independence movement.

The most severe critics view Rhee as fundamentally hypocritical: a supposed democrat who established dictatorship, a Christian who ordered massacres, an independence fighter who relied entirely on American support and followed U.S. directives, and a nationalist whose primary legacy was dividing the nation permanently. By this interpretation, Rhee's negative contributions far outweigh any positive achievements, and his veneration represents historical revisionism that dishonors his victims.

## Famous Quotes

> "Communism is cholera and you cannot compromise with cholera." - Defining statement of Rhee's uncompromising anti-communist ideology[^110]

> "The Government and the people of the Republic of Korea consider this is the time to unify Korea, and for anything less than unification to come out of these great sacrifices of Koreans and their powerful allies would be unthinkable." - Statement to U.S. officials during Korean War armistice negotiations, 1950[^111]

> "You have already championed the cause of the oppressed and held out your helping hand to the weak of the earth's races. Your nation is the Hope of Mankind, so we come to you." - Appeal to President Woodrow Wilson regarding Korean independence, 1919[^112]

> "When possible, children should be comfortable and free when they are playing or studying, and it is the way in a civilized country to make them more comfortable by not being too strict and giving grace and love at the same time." - Speech on education at Seoul Board of Education first anniversary, October 2, 1957[^113]

> "Since we are developing a lot of small and medium-sized enterprises, we are trying to make all these industries free by the private sector to increase productivity nationwide to make things for domestic use and export, and at the same time create jobs so that there is no unemployment." - Speech on economic development, 1957[^114]
