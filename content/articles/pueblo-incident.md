## Summary

North Korea seized the USS Pueblo intelligence ship in international waters, killing one sailor and holding 82 crew hostage for 11 months. The ship remains in Pyongyang.

## Background

- **USS Pueblo (AGER-2)** was a U.S. Navy technical research ship conducting signals intelligence collection.
- The incident occurred during a tense Cold War period on the Korean Peninsula following the 1953 armistice.

## Seizure (January 1968)

- North Korean naval units intercepted and **seized the Pueblo on 23 January 1968**.
- The U.S. government maintained the ship was in **international waters**; North Korea asserted it was in **territorial waters**.
- One U.S. sailor, **Duane D. Hodges**, was killed during the capture.

## Captivity and Negotiations

- **82 crew members** were held for roughly **11 months**.
- The United States and North Korea negotiated at **Panmunjom**, with the U.S. seeking release of the crew.
- North Korea obtained a signed document including an apology/acknowledgment; U.S. officials later **repudiated** the confession as coerced.

## Release and Aftermath

- The crew was released on **23 December 1968** via the **Bridge of No Return**.
- The ship itself was **not returned** and has remained in North Korea, later displayed in **Pyongyang** as a museum exhibit.
- The Pueblo remains one of the rare cases of an active-duty U.S. Navy commissioned vessel being captured and retained by a foreign state.

## Why It Matters

- **Escalation risk:** The capture occurred two days after the start of the Tet Offensive, heightening broader geopolitical tension.
- **Intelligence loss:** U.S. officials were concerned about compromise of signals intelligence equipment and materials.
- **Precedent:** The incident is frequently cited in discussions of crisis management, rules of engagement, and maritime claims.

## Key Facts

- **Date of seizure:** 1968-01-23
- **Fatalities:** 1 U.S. sailor killed
- **Hostages:** 82 crew held
- **Outcome:** Crew released; ship retained by North Korea
