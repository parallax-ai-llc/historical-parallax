## Summary

Venezuelan drug lord who controlled drug trafficking through Venezuelan ports with protection from government officials. His arrest revealed deep drug corruption in Venezuela's military.

## Background and methods

Makled was associated with the movement of cocaine through Venezuela, leveraging access to logistics infrastructure and key ports. Reporting around the case highlighted allegations that bribery and protection networks inside state institutions enabled large-scale trafficking operations.

A recurring theme in coverage was the role of transportation and warehousing as a force multiplier: control of port access, security, and customs processes can reduce interdiction risk and make illicit shipments resemble routine commerce.

## Arrest, extradition, and legal status

Makled was arrested in Colombia in 2010 and extradited to Venezuela rather than the United States, which also sought him. The extradition decision became part of the wider political and diplomatic context surrounding narcotics enforcement in the region.

He was widely cited as a source of allegations about official corruption connected to trafficking routes. Such claims were significant because they suggested that drug flows were not only criminal-market phenomena but also governance problems involving compromised enforcement.

## Significance

The Makled case is often referenced as an example of how transnational drug trafficking can intersect with state capacity: when officials who control checkpoints, ports, or investigative powers are corrupted, the practical barrier to large shipments drops sharply.

It also illustrates the strategic importance of chokepoints (ports, airports, customs) in both trafficking and counter-trafficking efforts, and why institutions responsible for those chokepoints become targets for bribery and coercion.

## Common analytical takeaways

High-level analyses of the case tend to emphasize three takeaways:

- Corruption risk concentrates where officials can grant access (ports, customs, licensing).
- Logistical control can be as valuable as armed control for organized crime.
- Extradition and jurisdiction choices can reshape how cases unfold publicly.
