## Summary

Genocide in Darfur from 2003 to present carried out by Sudanese government-backed Janjaweed militia. Over 300,000 killed and 2.5 million displaced.

## Background and Drivers

The conflict in Darfur emerged from long-running disputes over land, political representation, and resource access, intensified by environmental stress and cycles of localized violence. In the early 2000s, rebel groups accused the Sudanese state of marginalization, and the government’s counterinsurgency campaign escalated into mass atrocities against civilian communities.

## Key Actors

- **Sudanese state forces**: elements of the military and security services involved in counterinsurgency operations.
- **Janjaweed / allied militias**: irregular forces accused of widespread attacks on villages, killings, and forced displacement.
- **Rebel movements**: multiple armed groups with shifting alliances and splinter factions over time.

## International Response

The crisis prompted major humanitarian operations and extensive documentation by journalists, NGOs, and UN bodies. The African Union deployed a monitoring and peacekeeping mission that later transitioned into a joint UN–AU operation (UNAMID), reflecting the scale and complexity of protecting civilians in the region.

## Legal and Political Significance

International debates centered on whether the violence met the legal threshold for genocide, alongside broader findings of war crimes and crimes against humanity. The situation in Darfur became a landmark case for international criminal justice, including the ICC’s actions targeting senior Sudanese leadership.

## Impact and Legacy

Beyond the death toll and displacement, Darfur’s violence reshaped Sudan’s domestic politics and affected regional stability, including cross-border dynamics with Chad and the Central African Republic. Long-term consequences include protracted insecurity, contested returns for displaced communities, and ongoing accountability debates.
