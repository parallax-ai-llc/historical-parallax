## Summary

Congolese militia leader from the Lendu community who directed attacks on Hema civilians during the Ituri conflict. He commanded the FNI and was convicted by the ICC of war crimes.

## Ituri Conflict Context

The Ituri conflict in northeastern Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) intensified in the early 2000s amid state collapse after the Congo wars, competition over land and resources, and the militarization of ethnic and political identities. The violence frequently involved Lendu and Hema communities, with armed groups forming around local networks as well as external patrons.

## Role in the FNI (reported)

Ntama is described in open sources as a commander within the Front des nationalistes et intégrationnistes (FNI), a Lendu-associated militia. The FNI operated in Ituri during a period marked by attacks on civilians, looting, and retaliatory violence.

## International Criminal Court Case

The International Criminal Court (ICC) pursued cases connected to atrocities in Ituri as part of its early docket. In summaries, Ntama is associated with the ICC’s focus on attacks directed at civilian populations, including murders and pillage.

Key elements that are commonly cited in overviews of the ICC proceedings include:

- The alleged direction or ordering of attacks against Hema civilians
- Coordination with other militia leaders and local commanders
- Responsibility for crimes committed by forces under effective command

## Conviction and Sentence (high level)

Public summaries describe Ntama as having been convicted at the ICC for war crimes related to directing attacks on civilians, including murder and pillage. Sentencing in ICC cases typically considers the gravity of the crimes, the individual’s role, and aggravating or mitigating factors.
