## Summary

Japanese forces massacred an estimated 100,000 Filipino civilians in Manila during the Battle of Manila. Soldiers committed mass murder, rape, and destruction as American forces liberated the city.

## Historical Context

- The Manila Massacre occurred during the final months of World War II in the Philippines, as Allied forces moved to retake Manila from Japanese occupation.
- Fighting in the city took place amid a collapse of Japanese command cohesion and extreme urban destruction.
- Civilian populations were trapped between artillery bombardment, street-to-street combat, and deliberate atrocities.

## Nature of Atrocities

- Reported crimes included mass shootings, bayonet killings, arson, rape, and the execution of hostages.
- Atrocities were carried out across multiple districts, often in sites such as schools, churches, and hospitals where civilians sought refuge.
- Survivors’ accounts and postwar investigations describe both organized and opportunistic violence by Japanese troops.

## Scale and Damage

- Estimates for civilian deaths commonly cite roughly 100,000 killed, though exact numbers vary with sources and methodologies.
- The battle also left much of Manila in ruins, contributing to Manila being remembered as one of the most devastated Allied capitals of the war.
- Destruction of archives, neighborhoods, and cultural landmarks compounded the human toll.

## Aftermath and Accountability

- Postwar prosecutions included proceedings that addressed command responsibility for war crimes committed by forces under a commander’s control.
- The Yamashita case became internationally significant in debates about the legal standard for “failure to control” troops.
- Public memory in the Philippines has emphasized commemoration of civilian victims and the broader trauma of the occupation.
