## Summary

Israeli con artist who used Tinder to meet women across Europe, posing as the son of a diamond mogul, and defrauded them of an estimated $10 million. His story was told in a Netflix documentary.

## Modus Operandi

Leviev was associated with a pattern of **romance fraud** in which a perpetrator builds trust and intimacy, then requests money under urgent pretexts. Reporting on the case described several recurring elements:

- **Impersonation and prestige cues**: presenting himself as the son of a wealthy diamond figure and surrounding himself with luxury signals (travel, hotels, private transport).
- **Urgency narratives**: claims of imminent danger or business emergencies requiring immediate cash.
- **Borrowing and leverage**: persuading victims to take out loans or open lines of credit, sometimes using documentation provided by the scammer.
- **Rotation across countries**: contacting new targets in different locations as older fraud claims collapsed.

## Public Allegations and Victims’ Accounts

Public accounts from victims emphasized the financial and emotional harms typical of romance scams:

- large personal debt incurred through loans,
- prolonged deception across multiple cities,
- reputational stress and difficulty obtaining restitution.

Because the most widely known details come from journalism and the Netflix documentary, some timelines and figures in popular retellings can differ by source.

## Legal and Criminal Proceedings

According to widely cited reporting, Leviev was arrested in Greece in 2019 and returned to Israel, where he was convicted on charges not limited to the Tinder-related allegations. He served a short prison term relative to the scale described by victims, which became a central point of criticism in media coverage.

## Media Impact

The 2022 documentary **_The Tinder Swindler_** helped mainstream the term “romance scam” and renewed attention to:

- the role of dating apps in facilitating rapid trust-building,
- cross-border enforcement difficulties for fraud,
- financial institutions’ and platforms’ responsibilities for prevention.

## Aftermath and Ongoing Debate

Discussion following the documentary often focused on prevention rather than a single case outcome:

- **platform safety**: verification, reporting mechanisms, and moderation.
- **consumer education**: warning signs, financial boundaries, and identity verification.
- **jurisdiction**: the challenge of investigating a fraud pattern spanning multiple countries.
