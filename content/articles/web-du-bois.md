## Summary

William Edward Burghardt Du Bois was an American sociologist, historian, civil rights activist, and writer whose intellectual output and political organizing shaped the course of African American life and scholarship throughout the twentieth century. Born in 1868 in Great Barrington, Massachusetts, he became the first African American to earn a doctorate from Harvard University in 1895 and went on to produce foundational works in sociology, history, and political thought.

Du Bois co-founded the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP) in 1909 and edited its influential journal *The Crisis* for twenty-four years, reaching hundreds of thousands of readers. His concept of "double consciousness"—the internal tension of being both Black and American—introduced a framework that remains central to discussions of race and identity to this day. His life's work challenged both the scientific racism of his era and the accommodationist politics of Booker T. Washington.

## Early Life & Background

Du Bois was born on February 23, 1868, in Great Barrington, Massachusetts, to Alfred Du Bois and Mary Silvina Burghardt. He grew up in a predominantly white town and demonstrated exceptional academic ability from an early age. With the support of local scholarships and his community, he attended Fisk University in Nashville, Tennessee, where he first encountered the stark realities of Jim Crow segregation. He earned his B.A. from Fisk in 1888, then enrolled at Harvard, receiving a second B.A. in 1890 and a Ph.D. in History in 1895. His dissertation, *The Suppression of the African Slave-Trade to the United States of America, 1638–1870*, was published as the first volume in the Harvard Historical Studies series. He also studied at the University of Berlin from 1892 to 1894, where he was shaped by the social science methods of Max Weber and others.

## Rise to Prominence

After teaching at Wilberforce University, Du Bois joined the University of Pennsylvania in 1896 to conduct research that produced *The Philadelphia Negro* (1899), one of the first systematic sociological studies of an African American community. He then joined Atlanta University, where he directed sociological research on Black life for thirteen years. His 1903 collection of essays, *The Souls of Black Folk*, brought him national attention and placed him in direct ideological conflict with Booker T. Washington, who advocated industrial education and political accommodation rather than civil rights agitation.

## Key Achievements

- **The Souls of Black Folk** (1903): a landmark work introducing "double consciousness" and the "veil" as metaphors for Black experience in America.
- **Co-founder of the NAACP** (1909): helped establish the organization that would lead major civil rights litigation and advocacy for decades.
- **Editor of *The Crisis*** (1910–1934): built the journal into the most widely read African American periodical of its time, with circulation exceeding 100,000 by 1918.
- **Black Reconstruction in America** (1935): a revisionist historical account challenging the prevailing Dunning School narrative of Reconstruction.
- **Pan-Africanism**: organized and participated in Pan-African Congresses beginning in 1919, advocating solidarity among peoples of African descent worldwide.

## Controversies & Criticism

Du Bois's political evolution generated significant controversy. His early criticism of Booker T. Washington alienated portions of the Black establishment. His socialist sympathies and praise for the Soviet Union drew scrutiny during the Cold War; he was indicted in 1951 under the Foreign Agents Registration Act (charges were later dismissed). His increasingly positive view of Stalinism troubled many civil rights allies. In 1961, at age 93, he joined the Communist Party of the United States and emigrated to Ghana at the invitation of President Kwame Nkrumah, renouncing his American citizenship before his death on August 27, 1963.

## Legacy & Influence

Du Bois died in Accra, Ghana, on the eve of the March on Washington. His frameworks of double consciousness, the color line, and structural racism remain foundational in sociology, African American studies, and critical race theory. Institutions including Harvard, Fisk, and the University of Massachusetts Amherst maintain research centers in his name. His archive at UMass Amherst preserves tens of thousands of documents. He is widely regarded as one of the most consequential American intellectuals of the twentieth century.
