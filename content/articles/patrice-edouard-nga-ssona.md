## Context

Ngaïssona is associated with the Anti-balaka movement that emerged during the Central African Republic's civil conflict. The Anti-balaka label has been used for a range of predominantly Christian and animist armed groups and local militias that carried out attacks as the conflict escalated.

## ICC case framing

The International Criminal Court process is often cited as an example of how international tribunals attempt to attribute responsibility to organizers, coordinators, and political figures who are alleged to have enabled or directed violence beyond direct battlefield participation.

## Patterns of harm referenced

Accounts commonly emphasize allegations of:

- Attacks on civilian communities and reprisals framed along religious identity lines.
- Killings, forced displacement, and destruction of property.
- Command-and-control structures that blur between political coordination and armed action.

## Why it matters

The case is frequently referenced in discussions of transitional justice and the limits of state institutions in civil-war settings, where domestic courts may be unable or unwilling to prosecute high-profile figures.

## Aftermath

Proceedings at the ICC can take years, involving pre-trial detention, confirmation of charges, and extensive witness testimony. The process itself becomes part of the historical record of the conflict, regardless of eventual verdicts.
