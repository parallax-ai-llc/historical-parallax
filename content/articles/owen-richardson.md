## Why He Matters

Richardson is best known for establishing a quantitative relationship between temperature and the emission of electrons from heated materials (thermionic emission). This work helped make electron tubes and early electronics more scientifically predictable, and it became part of the wider foundation of modern solid-state and surface physics.

## Key Ideas (High-Level)

- **Thermionic emission**: When a metal is heated, some electrons gain enough energy to escape the surface.
- **Richardson's law / Richardson equation**: A formula describing how emission current depends strongly on temperature and the material's work function.
- **Work function (concept)**: The energy barrier electrons must overcome to leave a material; central to vacuum electronics and later semiconductor device physics.

## Context and Legacy

- His results were influential during the period when vacuum tubes were crucial for radio, telephony, and early computation.
- The Nobel Prize in Physics (1928) recognized the significance of placing thermionic emission on a firm experimental and theoretical basis.
- Richardson’s work is frequently cited in treatments of electron emission, surface science, and the historical development of electronic instrumentation.
