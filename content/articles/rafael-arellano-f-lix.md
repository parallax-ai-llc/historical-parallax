## Summary

Mexican drug lord and member of the Arellano Félix Organization (Tijuana Cartel). He served prison time in the US and was shot dead at a family party in Los Cabos after release.

## Organization and Context

The Arellano Félix Organization (often called the Tijuana Cartel) was a major trafficking group involved in moving narcotics through the U.S.–Mexico border region, particularly via Baja California. The organization was associated with intense competition with rival networks, and the broader period is often described as one of escalating violence among trafficking groups and state security forces.

## Legal Proceedings

Public reporting and court records describe arrests and prosecutions of multiple members of the organization across Mexico and the United States. Extraditions and U.S. federal cases became a prominent feature of cross-border counternarcotics cooperation, reflecting the transnational structure of trafficking and money flows.

## Death

Arellano Félix was killed in 2013 at a gathering in Los Cabos, an event widely reported as reflecting ongoing internal conflicts and the persistent risks faced by figures tied to organized crime even after serving prison sentences.

## Broader Impact

Analysts often treat the Tijuana Cartel era as an example of how enforcement pressure, leadership decapitation, and rivalry can fragment criminal organizations while also triggering short-term spikes in violence.

## Media and Narrative

High-profile cartel figures are often portrayed in journalism and popular media as singular “kingpins,” but the operational reality typically involves networks of brokers, transport coordinators, financiers, and corrupt enablers. Coverage of the Arellano Félix Organization frequently emphasized its family-based leadership structure and the strategic importance of Tijuana as a trafficking corridor.

The group’s decline is commonly linked to sustained law-enforcement pressure and the rise of competing organizations.
