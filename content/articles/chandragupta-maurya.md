## Summary

Chandragupta Maurya (c. 340 BCE – c. 297 BCE) was the founder and first emperor of the Maurya Empire, which became one of the largest empires in ancient India.[^1] Rising from humble origins, he overthrew the Nanda dynasty with the strategic guidance of his mentor Chanakya, establishing a vast centralized empire that stretched from the Hindu Kush to the Bay of Bengal.[^2] His reign marked a transformative period in Indian history, unifying most of the Indian subcontinent under a single administration for the first time.[^3] In his later years, he renounced his throne, converted to Jainism, and died practicing the austere ritual of sallekhana (fasting unto death).[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

The early life of Chandragupta Maurya remains shrouded in legend and uncertainty, with ancient texts providing few concrete historical facts about his origins.[^5] According to traditional accounts, Chandragupta was born around 340 BCE in Pataliputra, the capital of the powerful Magadha kingdom, though some sources place his birth around 346 BCE.[^6] His father was reportedly the chief of the migrant Mauryas who died in a border skirmish, leaving his family destitute.[^7] Following his father's death, Chandragupta's maternal uncles abandoned him, and he was left with a cowherd who raised him as his own son.[^8]

The young Chandragupta's fortunes changed dramatically when he was purchased by a Brahmin politician and scholar named Kautilya, also known as Chanakya.[^9] Recognizing the boy's potential, Chanakya took him to Taxila (in present-day Pakistan), one of the ancient world's premier centers of learning.[^10] At Taxila, Chandragupta received a comprehensive education that included military tactics, statecraft, the aesthetic arts, and political philosophy.[^11] This rigorous training would prove instrumental in his later conquest of the Nanda Empire and establishment of the Mauryan dynasty.

The relationship between Chandragupta and Chanakya transcended that of student and teacher, evolving into a partnership based on mutual reverence and trust.[^12] Chanakya served not merely as Chandragupta's instructor but also as his prime minister, strategic advisor, friend, and well-wisher throughout the conquest and consolidation of the empire.[^13] According to legendary narratives preserved in various traditions dating from the 4th to 11th century CE, Chanakya was instrumental in assisting Chandragupta Maurya in his rise to power and the establishment of the Maurya Empire.[^14]

Historical sources indicate that despite being from the Vaishya (merchant) caste, Chandragupta possessed the ambition, intelligence, and military acumen necessary to challenge the established Nanda dynasty.[^15] His humble origins—being born poor, orphaned, and abandoned—made his eventual rise to become the founder of one of history's greatest empires all the more remarkable.[^16] The combination of Chanakya's political wisdom and Chandragupta's military prowess would prove to be a formidable force that would reshape the political landscape of ancient India.

---

## Rise to Power

Chandragupta Maurya's ascent to power began during a period of significant political upheaval in the Indian subcontinent following Alexander the Great's Indian campaign and subsequent death in 323 BCE.[^17] Around 327 BCE, Chandragupta initiated a rebellion against the ruling Nanda dynasty, specifically targeting King Dhanananda.[^18] When this initial uprising proved unsuccessful, he sought refuge with the local raja Porus, using this time to regroup and strengthen his forces.[^19]

According to legend, Chanakya's motivation to overthrow the Nanda dynasty stemmed from a personal insult he suffered at the hands of King Dhana Nanda.[^20] Chanakya convinced his disciple Chandragupta that conquering the kingdom of Magadha was both necessary and achievable.[^21] Together, they developed a sophisticated military strategy that would gradually chip away at the Nanda Empire's territorial control rather than attempting a direct assault on the heavily fortified capital.

Beginning in 324 BCE, Chandragupta commenced his conquest by securing substantial territory in western India, systematically driving out the remaining Macedonian forces left behind after Alexander's departure.[^22] This western campaign served a dual purpose: it eliminated potential rivals and provided Chandragupta with valuable military experience, resources, and momentum for his ultimate goal—the conquest of Magadha. By 321 BCE, feeling sufficiently strong, Chandragupta turned his attention to his homeland of Magadha and the Nanda dynasty that ruled it.[^23]

The campaign against the Nandas required careful planning and execution. Chandragupta's army initially attempted an unsuccessful direct attack on the Nanda capital of Pataliputra.[^24] Learning from this failure, Chandragupta and Chanakya adopted a different approach, beginning their campaign at the frontier regions of the Nanda empire.[^25] They gradually conquered various territories on their way to the capital, establishing military garrisons in each conquered region to secure their supply lines and prevent rebellion.[^26] This methodical strategy demonstrated the sophisticated military thinking that would characterize Chandragupta's reign.

The final siege of Pataliputra in 322 BCE proved to be a bitterly fought campaign.[^27] Hindu and Jain texts emphasize that the Nanda dynasty possessed a powerful and well-trained army, making the conquest particularly challenging.[^28] However, Chandragupta's superior tactics, combined with reported popular discontent against the Nanda ruler's oppressive policies, eventually led to victory. The Nandas were forced to surrender, and Dhana Nanda, the last Nanda emperor, was killed during the siege.[^29] With this decisive victory, Chandragupta Maurya established himself as the ruler of Magadha, founding what would become the Maurya Empire.

## Major Achievements

Chandragupta Maurya's reign was marked by extraordinary accomplishments in military conquest, administrative innovation, and diplomatic strategy that established the foundation for one of ancient India's most powerful empires.[^30] His most significant achievement was the unification of the Indian subcontinent under a centralized administration—a feat unprecedented in Indian history.[^31] Historians credit Chandragupta with creating the first (nearly) pan-Indian empire, bringing together diverse populations, cultures, and regions under a single political authority.[^32]

The territorial extent of Chandragupta's empire was truly remarkable for its time. His domain stretched from the Himalayas and the Kabul River valley in present-day Afghanistan in the north and west, to the Vindhya Range in the south, and from the Bay of Bengal in the east to regions that possibly included parts of present-day Gujarat and Karnataka.[^33] This vast empire represented one of history's most extensive territorial holdings, rivaling contemporary powers in other parts of the ancient world.[^34] Chandragupta is perhaps best remembered today as the king who unified India, establishing political coherence across a previously fragmented subcontinent.[^35]

Chandragupta's military prowess was demonstrated not only in his conquest of the Nanda dynasty but also in his successful campaigns against the Greek successors of Alexander the Great. In 305 BCE, he defeated an invasion led by Seleucus I Nicator, a prominent Greek general who was contending for control of Alexander's Asian territories.[^36] This victory was particularly significant as it established Indian sovereignty over regions that had briefly fallen under Macedonian influence. Following this military success, Chandragupta negotiated a diplomatic settlement with Seleucus, receiving additional territories in exchange for 500 war elephants.[^37] To cement this peace, Chandragupta married Helena, daughter of Seleucus I Nicator, in a strategic alliance that fostered peace and cultural exchange between the two empires.[^38]

The military forces at Chandragupta's command were formidable by any ancient standard. Greek accounts, particularly those of Megasthenes who served as Seleucus's ambassador to the Mauryan court, describe an army consisting of more than 500,000 foot soldiers, 30,000 cavalry, and 9,000 war elephants.[^39] This massive military establishment enabled Chandragupta to maintain control over his vast empire and deter potential rivals from challenging his authority.

Beyond military conquest, Chandragupta's administrative achievements were equally impressive. Aided by his mentor and later minister Chanakya (also known as Kautilya), he established a vast centralized bureaucratic system whose functioning, social organization, military structure, and economic policies are preserved in the Arthashastra.[^40] The Arthashastra, conventionally attributed to Kautilya, served as the manual in which Chandragupta was tutored and which guided the empire's governance.[^41] This text outlines sophisticated principles of statecraft, economic management, and military strategy that formed the basis of Mauryan administration.

The capital city of Pataliputra under Chandragupta's rule became a marvel of urban planning and administration. Historical accounts from Megasthenes describe it as "a thoroughly well-organized city, administered according to the best principles of social science," with organizational capabilities that were "astonishing" for India in 300 BCE.[^42] Megasthenes ranked Chandragupta's timber palace as exceeding the royal residences of Susa and Ecbatana, the ceremonial capitals of the Persian Empire.[^43]

Chandragupta's economic policies fostered prosperity throughout his empire. Under his rule and that of his successors, the empire witnessed extensive trade networks, economic growth, and cultural development.[^44] The empire's governance and legal system would influence later dynasties and have a lasting impact on the political traditions of the Indian subcontinent.[^45] His sound principles for governance and tireless work for the empire's growth established Chandragupta as one of ancient India's foremost rulers and a near-mythical figure in folklore.[^46]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his achievements, Chandragupta Maurya's rule was not without significant criticisms and controversial aspects that reveal the darker dimensions of his governance.[^47] Classical sources, particularly Greek accounts, provide evidence of authoritarian practices and harsh policies that characterized his administration. The ancient historian Justin recorded that after Chandragupta liberated the eastern satrapies from Greek control, he "oppressed the people whom he had delivered from a foreign power, with a cruel tyranny."[^48] This suggests that liberation from foreign rule did not necessarily translate into freedom for the populace under Mauryan authority.

The principle of dandaniti (the science of punishment) formed a core element of Chandragupta's governing philosophy, and historical accounts indicate he "acted on it without hesitation."[^49] His administration demonstrated "ruthless severity in the enforcement of fiscal regulations for the benefit of the treasury, and of stern repression of crime."[^50] This harsh approach to governance prioritized state revenue collection and crime control over individual liberties and compassionate justice. The rigorous enforcement of fiscal regulations likely imposed heavy burdens on the population, while the stern repression of crime may have involved severe punishments that would be considered excessive by modern standards.

The Magadhan state under Chandragupta has been characterized by historians as "both opulent and totalitarian."[^51] Having come to power through intrigue and military conquest, Chandragupta harbored constant fears of plots against his rule.[^52] Consequently, he "employed an army of secret agents, and no method was considered unscrupulous to destroy his enemies."[^53] This pervasive surveillance system and willingness to use any means necessary to eliminate opposition created an atmosphere of fear and suspicion throughout the empire. The extensive network of spies represented a significant infringement on personal privacy and freedom, while the unscrupulous methods employed against perceived enemies raised serious ethical concerns.

Modern scholarly analysis has identified additional criticisms of Mauryan governance under Chandragupta. One significant critique concerns the lack of separation between civil and personal rights of citizens, which "might restrict the personal development of each individual which is essential for emotional well-being."[^54] This absence of protected personal rights meant that "the personal fundamental rights stand violated," leaving individuals vulnerable to arbitrary state action without legal recourse.[^55] The totalitarian nature of the state apparatus, while effective in maintaining order and collecting revenue, came at the cost of individual autonomy and freedom.

The political structure of Chandragupta's empire was fundamentally monarchical and non-democratic in nature. Chanakya, the chief architect of the Mauryan state, was explicitly against the formation and working of a republican state.[^56] The government was "monarchial in nature and structure" with "the crown hereditary in nature," concentrating power in the hands of a single ruler and his designated successors.[^57] This centralization of authority, while providing stability and unified decision-making, eliminated any meaningful popular participation in governance and perpetuated dynastic rule regardless of the competence or character of individual rulers.

These criticisms reveal that while Chandragupta Maurya successfully unified India and established an efficient administrative system, his methods involved significant authoritarianism, surveillance, harsh punishments, and limited personal freedoms. The effectiveness of his rule in creating a stable, prosperous empire must be weighed against the costs in human liberty and dignity that his subjects endured.

## Personal Life

Chandragupta Maurya's personal life reflected both the strategic necessities of empire-building and deeply held spiritual convictions that would ultimately lead him to renounce worldly power.[^58] Historical sources indicate that Chandragupta had two wives: Durdhara and Helena, each representing different aspects of his political and personal life.[^59]

Durdhara, according to sources like the Mahavamsa-tika, was Chandragupta's first cousin and his first wife.[^60] Their marriage produced a son named Bindusara, who would eventually succeed Chandragupta as emperor.[^61] However, Durdhara's life ended in tragedy under circumstances that highlight the dangerous nature of royal life in ancient India. When she was nine months pregnant, Durdhara consumed poisonous food that had been intended for Chandragupta.[^62] Upon realizing what had happened, Chanakya understood that Durdhara could not be saved but decided to preserve the unborn child by performing an emergency cesarean section, cutting open her womb.[^63] The child survived and was named Bindusara, though some accounts suggest the name derived from "bindu" (drop) because a drop of poison had touched him, resulting in a birthmark.[^64]

Chandragupta's second wife, Helena, was a Seleucid princess and daughter of Seleucus I Nicator, founder of the Seleucid Empire.[^65] This marriage was fundamentally a strategic alliance concluded as part of the peace settlement following Chandragupta's military victory over Seleucus in 305 BCE.[^66] The union between Chandragupta and Helena served to solidify peace between the two powerful empires and fostered cultural exchange between Indian and Hellenistic civilizations.[^67] While less is known about Helena's life in the Mauryan court compared to Durdhara, her marriage to Chandragupta represents an early example of diplomatic marriage as a tool of international relations.

Chandragupta's personality and character can be inferred from his actions and the historical accounts of his reign. Having experienced poverty, abandonment, and hardship in his youth, he possessed the ambition and determination necessary to rise from obscurity to imperial power.[^68] His willingness to employ secret agents and use unscrupulous methods against enemies suggests a pragmatic, even ruthless, approach to maintaining power.[^69] However, his ultimate renunciation of the throne reveals a capacity for spiritual reflection and detachment from material wealth that seems at odds with the typical profile of a conquering emperor.

The most dramatic transformation in Chandragupta's personal life came in his later years when he developed an interest in Jainism, a religious and philosophical tradition emphasizing non-violence, asceticism, and spiritual liberation.[^70] According to Jain tradition, Chandragupta converted to Jainism under the influence of the Jain sage Bhadrabahu, who became his spiritual guide.[^71] When Chandragupta reached his fifties, he made the extraordinary decision to renounce all worldly pleasures and become a monk following Jain tradition.[^72]

After making Bindusara the new emperor, Chandragupta abdicated his throne and traveled south with Bhadrabahu to Shravanabelagola (also spelled Sravana Belgola) in present-day Karnataka.[^73] There, he practiced the austere Jain ritual of sallekhana—fasting to death—meditating in a cave until he died approximately five weeks later around 297 BCE.[^74] This dramatic ending to his life demonstrates a profound spiritual commitment and willingness to embrace the Jain ideal of complete detachment from worldly existence.

However, modern scholarship has raised questions about the traditional account of Chandragupta's conversion to Jainism. Historian Sushma Jansari notes that "a closer look at the evidence for Chandragupta's conversion to Jainism and his and Bhadrabāhu's association with Śravaṇa Beḷgoḷa reveals that it is both late and problematic."[^75] This scholarly skepticism suggests that while the story of Chandragupta's Jain renunciation is deeply embedded in Indian religious tradition, the historical evidence may be less definitive than traditionally assumed.

## Legacy

Chandragupta Maurya's legacy represents one of the most transformative periods in Indian history, establishing patterns of governance, territorial consolidation, and political unity that would influence the subcontinent for centuries.[^76] His establishment of the Maurya Empire created a template for centralized administration that subsequent Indian empires would emulate and adapt.[^77] The Mauryan Empire served as a model for later dynasties and left a lasting impact on the political, administrative, and artistic traditions of the Indian subcontinent.[^78]

The foundational significance of Chandragupta's achievements cannot be overstated. By unifying a large portion of the diverse Indian population under a single rule by 321 BCE, he created the first significant empire on the Indian subcontinent and is credited with establishing the first (nearly) pan-Indian empire.[^79] This political unification brought together regions that had previously existed as independent kingdoms, tribal territories, and fragmented political entities, demonstrating that large-scale governance of the Indian subcontinent was feasible.[^80] His military conquests and administrative reforms laid the foundation for one of the most powerful empires in ancient India.[^81]

The administrative legacy of Chandragupta's reign proved particularly enduring. The principles of governance documented in the Arthashastra—whether actually authored by Chanakya or compiled later—provided a sophisticated framework for statecraft that influenced Indian political thought for generations.[^82] Chandragupta's establishment of a vast centralized bureaucracy, efficient revenue collection systems, and organized military forces demonstrated administrative capabilities that were remarkable for their time and place.[^83] These systems enabled effective governance over vast territories with diverse populations, languages, and cultural traditions.

Under Chandragupta and his successors, the Mauryan Empire witnessed economic prosperity, extensive trade networks both domestic and international, and significant cultural development.[^84] The empire's governance and legal system had a lasting impact on subsequent dynasties, establishing precedents for taxation, justice administration, and military organization.[^85] The economic infrastructure developed during this period—including road networks, irrigation systems, and trading relationships—facilitated commerce and communication across the empire and beyond its borders.

Chandragupta's legacy in Indian collective memory has elevated him to near-mythical status. He left behind a legacy preserved not only in historical texts like the Arthashastra but also in folklore and popular imagination.[^86] Building an empire through his own efforts, overcoming seemingly insurmountable obstacles, and establishing sound principles for governance, Chandragupta's achievements made him "one of ancient India's foremost rulers and a near-mythical figure of folklore."[^87] The narrative of his rise from poverty and abandonment to imperial power has inspired countless generations as a story of ambition, determination, and achievement.

The cultural and religious dimensions of Chandragupta's legacy are also significant. His reported conversion to Jainism and dramatic renunciation of power through sallekhana has made him an important figure in Jain tradition and religious memory.[^88] This aspect of his legacy presents a compelling portrait of spiritual transformation and detachment from material power, offering a counterpoint to the image of the conquering emperor.

The long-term impact of Chandragupta's reign extended well beyond his lifetime. His establishment of the Mauryan Empire laid the groundwork for his grandson Ashoka's even more extensive empire and Buddhist missionary activities that would spread Indian culture and religion across Asia.[^89] Chandragupta Maurya's establishment of the empire laid the groundwork for centralized administration, while Ashoka's reign left an indelible mark on the cultural, social, and religious fabric of India.[^90] The administrative structures, political concepts, and territorial scope established by Chandragupta created possibilities for his successors that would not have existed otherwise.

In the broader context of world history, Chandragupta's achievement of unifying most of India under one administration represents a remarkable feat of political and military organization comparable to the achievements of other great empire-builders of the ancient world.[^91] His successful resistance to Seleucid expansion demonstrated that Indian powers could effectively defend their independence against Hellenistic military forces, altering the balance of power in South and Central Asia.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholarly assessment of Chandragupta Maurya has generally recognized his extraordinary accomplishments in empire-building, administrative innovation, and political unification.[^92] Modern historians acknowledge that his reign marked a watershed moment in Indian history, establishing precedents and possibilities that shaped the subcontinent's political development for centuries. The scholarly consensus indicates that Chandragupta Maurya's legacy represents a transformative period in Indian history, with his establishment of patterns of governance and territorial consolidation influencing the region for generations.[^93]

Historians particularly credit Chandragupta with achieving what no previous Indian ruler had accomplished: the unification of the Indian subcontinent under a centralized administration.[^94] This political unification brought stability, facilitated trade and cultural exchange, and demonstrated the viability of large-scale governance across diverse regions and populations. His empire stretched from the Hindu Kush to the Bay of Bengal, creating an unprecedented political entity that rivaled contemporary empires elsewhere in the ancient world.[^95]

The administrative sophistication of the Mauryan state under Chandragupta has earned scholarly praise. Aided by Chanakya's political wisdom as documented in the Arthashastra, Chandragupta established a bureaucratic system characterized by efficiency, systematic organization, and effective resource management.[^96] The detailed administrative structures described in ancient sources reveal a level of governmental sophistication that was remarkable for its time, with specialized departments for various functions, systematic revenue collection, and organized military forces.[^97]

Chandragupta's military achievements have also received positive scholarly assessment. His systematic conquest of the Nanda Empire demonstrated strategic thinking and tactical flexibility, particularly in his shift from direct assault to gradual territorial acquisition with secured garrisons.[^98] His defeat of Seleucus I Nicator established Indian sovereignty over disputed territories and demonstrated that Indian military forces could successfully resist Hellenistic armies.[^99] The subsequent peace treaty and diplomatic marriage showed sophistication in combining military success with diplomatic resolution.

Economic historians note that under Chandragupta's rule, the empire experienced prosperity and extensive trade networks that benefited various regions and social classes.[^100] The infrastructure development, including road networks and irrigation systems, facilitated commerce and agricultural productivity. The organized tax collection system, while harsh in enforcement, provided resources for public works, military maintenance, and administrative functions that benefited the empire as a whole.

Some scholars and traditionalists view Chandragupta's spiritual journey and ultimate renunciation of power as evidence of profound wisdom and spiritual development.[^101] His willingness to abandon the vast empire he had built to pursue religious goals through Jain asceticism represents a dramatic rejection of worldly power in favor of spiritual liberation. This aspect of his life has made him a revered figure in Jain tradition and presents a compelling example of spiritual transformation.

Defenders of Chandragupta's legacy argue that his authoritarian methods must be understood in the context of the turbulent period in which he ruled.[^102] Following Alexander's invasion and the power vacuum created by his death, the Indian subcontinent faced the threat of further Greek expansion and internal fragmentation. Chandragupta's strong centralized state and harsh policies can be viewed as necessary responses to genuine security threats and the challenges of governing a vast, diverse empire with limited communication and transportation infrastructure.

### Negative Views

Critical scholarly assessment of Chandragupta Maurya has identified significant problems with his methods of governance and the nature of the state he created.[^103] Modern historians applying contemporary values and human rights perspectives find substantial grounds for criticism in the authoritarian, surveillance-based, and repressive character of his rule. While acknowledging his achievements in unification and administration, critical scholars emphasize the human costs and ethical problems inherent in his governing approach.

The characterization of Chandragupta's state as "totalitarian" by some historians highlights fundamental concerns about the nature of his rule.[^104] The employment of "an army of secret agents" and the willingness to use "no method considered unscrupulous to destroy his enemies" created a climate of fear and surveillance incompatible with individual liberty and dignity.[^105] Critics argue that such extensive state surveillance and ruthless elimination of opposition represented a form of tyranny, even if accompanied by administrative efficiency and economic prosperity.

The testimony of classical sources, particularly Justin's account that Chandragupta "oppressed the people whom he had delivered from a foreign power, with a cruel tyranny," provides contemporary evidence of his harsh rule.[^106] This suggests that even observers of the period recognized problematic aspects of his governance. The "ruthless severity in the enforcement of fiscal regulations" and "stern repression of crime" indicate a governing philosophy that prioritized state power and revenue over justice, compassion, or individual rights.[^107]

Modern critical analysis has identified structural problems in the Mauryan state under Chandragupta. The absence of separation between civil and personal rights meant that citizens lacked protected spheres of privacy and autonomy.[^108] Critics argue that this "might restrict the personal development of each individual which is essential for emotional well-being," and that "personal fundamental rights stand violated."[^109] From this perspective, Chandragupta's administrative efficiency came at an unacceptable cost in human freedom and dignity.

The explicitly monarchical and non-democratic nature of Chandragupta's government has drawn criticism from scholars who value participatory governance and popular sovereignty.[^110] Chanakya's opposition to republican forms of government and the establishment of hereditary monarchy eliminated any meaningful popular participation in political decision-making.[^111] Critics argue that this concentration of power in a single individual and his dynasty created a system vulnerable to tyranny and incompetent leadership, as the quality of governance depended entirely on the character and abilities of whoever inherited the throne.

Some historians question whether the traditional accounts of Chandragupta's spiritual transformation and Jain renunciation are historically accurate or later hagiographical constructions.[^112] Historian Sushma Jansari's observation that the evidence for Chandragupta's conversion to Jainism and association with Shravanabelagola is "both late and problematic" suggests that this inspiring narrative may be more legend than history.[^113] If the renunciation story is indeed a later fabrication, it would remove one of the most redeeming aspects of Chandragupta's legacy, leaving a portrait of a successful but tyrannical ruler who never experienced spiritual transformation.

Critical scholars also note that while Chandragupta unified India, this unification was achieved through military conquest and coercion rather than voluntary federation or democratic consensus.[^114] The violence inherent in his rise to power—including the siege of Pataliputra and the killing of Dhana Nanda—and his subsequent expansion through warfare involved considerable bloodshed and suffering. From this perspective, the Mauryan unification represents imposed political unity achieved through force rather than an organic development arising from the will of the people.

## Famous Quotes

> "Once one has made a decision, one should not turn back and revisit it. Those that keep turning to look back at their decisions do not make history." - Attributed to Chandragupta Maurya, reflecting his decisive leadership and forward-looking approach to empire-building[^132]

> "A thoroughly well-organized city, administered according to the best principles of social science." - Megasthenes describing Chandragupta's capital Pataliputra, noting organizational capabilities that were "astonishing" for India in 300 BCE[^133]

> "His timber palace exceeded the royal residences of Susa and Ecbatana." - Megasthenes comparing Chandragupta's palace favorably to the ceremonial capitals of the Persian Empire[^134]

> "He oppressed the people whom he had delivered from a foreign power, with a cruel tyranny." - Justin's critical assessment of Chandragupta's rule after liberating territories from Greek control[^135]

Note: Direct quotes from Chandragupta Maurya himself are extremely limited in historical records, as ancient Indian texts rarely preserved verbatim speeches or sayings of rulers.[^136] Most wisdom literature from this period is attributed to Chanakya (Kautilya) in texts like the Arthashastra and Chanakya Neeti, rather than to Chandragupta himself.[^137]
