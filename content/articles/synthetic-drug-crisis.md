## Additional Context

### What “synthetic drugs” refers to

In public-health and law-enforcement contexts, “synthetic drugs” generally refers to substances that are fully synthetic or primarily synthesized in laboratories rather than derived directly from plant sources. The category includes synthetic opioids and stimulants, as well as synthetic cannabinoids and other novel psychoactive substances.

### Key substances often discussed

- **Fentanyl and analogues:** extremely potent opioids associated with high overdose risk, especially when mixed into other drugs or counterfeit pills.
- **Methamphetamine:** a synthetic stimulant linked to addiction, psychosis risk, and significant social harms.
- **Captagon (fenethylline and related illicit pills):** a stimulant-associated illicit market, often discussed in connection with regional trafficking.

### Supply chains and market dynamics

Synthetic drug markets can scale quickly because production depends on chemical precursors, equipment, and distribution networks rather than crop cycles. Shifts in regulation, precursor controls, and enforcement can change trafficking routes and “product mixes,” sometimes leading to replacement substances or new analogues.

### Public-health impact

The crisis framing typically emphasizes rising overdose deaths (particularly where fentanyl is prevalent), increased strain on emergency services, and the need for prevention, treatment, and harm-reduction capacity. Public messaging often focuses on the dangers of unknown potency and contamination.

### Policy and response themes

Common responses include precursor-chemical regulation, interdiction efforts, expanded addiction treatment, naloxone distribution, and public awareness campaigns. International coordination is frequently cited as necessary because production, shipping, and consumption often span multiple jurisdictions.
