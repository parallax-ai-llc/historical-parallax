## Summary

Russian feminist punk rock group whose members were imprisoned for performing a protest song in a Moscow cathedral. Became symbols of resistance against Putin's authoritarianism.

## Background

- Formed in Moscow in 2011 as an anonymous feminist protest collective associated with the broader Russian art-activism scene.
- Known for short, disruptive performances (“actions”) staged in public spaces and recorded for online release.
- The group’s name and imagery draw on punk aesthetics and third-wave feminist critique, while its messaging targets state power, church-state entanglement, and gender repression.

## Notable Actions (High Level)

- **2012 — Cathedral performance (Moscow):** A brief performance in the Cathedral of Christ the Saviour led to criminal charges and global attention.
- **Courtroom and detention visibility:** The trial proceedings and subsequent imprisonment became part of the group’s public narrative, amplifying criticism of Russia’s justice system.
- **Ongoing protest statements:** Over time, Pussy Riot members and collaborators have continued to publish songs, videos, and statements addressing political prisoners, police violence, and censorship.

## Impact and Legacy

- Became an international symbol of artistic dissent under authoritarian governance.
- Sparked wide debate about freedom of expression, blasphemy laws, and the role of the Russian Orthodox Church in public life.
- Frequently cited in discussions of “art as protest,” especially where performance, media distribution, and political messaging are tightly coupled.

## Themes and Methods

- **Performance-first activism:** Actions are designed to be documented; the resulting video/audio distribution is often the primary channel of impact.
- **Anonymity and collective identity:** Masks and uniform styling emphasize the message over individual celebrity, though some members became publicly identified through legal proceedings.
- **Feminist and anti-authoritarian framing:** Lyrics and slogans commonly address gender equality, state violence, and the suppression of civil society.

## Notes

- Because Pussy Riot functions as a collective with rotating participants and collaborators, membership can be context-dependent across different actions and releases.
