## Summary

Charles Elwood "Chuck" Yeager was an American Air Force officer and test pilot who became the first person confirmed to have broken the sound barrier in level flight, on October 14, 1947. Flying the experimental Bell X-1 rocket plane over the Mojave Desert at Mach 1.06 (approximately 700 mph), he shattered one of aviation's most formidable psychological and technical barriers. His achievement marked a turning point in aerospace history and ushered in the supersonic age.

Beyond that singular moment, Yeager distinguished himself as one of the most gifted combat and test pilots of the 20th century. A World War II ace who downed 13 German aircraft — including one of the Luftwaffe's advanced Me 262 jets — he later logged thousands of hours in experimental aircraft, helping develop the technology that would carry Americans into space. He retired as a brigadier general in 1975 and remained a public figure and aviation advocate until his death at age 97 in December 2020.

## Early Life & Background

Yeager was born on February 13, 1923, in Myra, West Virginia, and grew up in Hamlin, a small coal-country town. The son of a natural gas driller, he showed early mechanical aptitude working with tools and engines. He enlisted in the U.S. Army Air Forces in 1941 at age 18, initially as an aircraft mechanic before being selected for flight training under the Flying Sergeants program. He earned his pilot's wings in 1943.

## Rise to Prominence

Deployed to England with the 357th Fighter Group, Yeager flew P-51 Mustangs over occupied Europe. On his eighth mission in March 1944, he was shot down over France but evaded capture with the help of the French Resistance and returned to duty — a rare exception to the standard policy of grounding evaders. He subsequently became a leading ace, including a single mission on October 12, 1944, in which he downed five enemy aircraft.

After the war, Yeager was selected for test pilot duty at Muroc Army Air Field (later Edwards Air Force Base) in California. Chosen to fly the Bell X-1, he nearly withdrew from the October 14, 1947, flight after breaking two ribs in a riding accident two nights prior. He reported for duty anyway, using a sawed-off broom handle to seal the cockpit hatch. The flight, lasting approximately 14 minutes after release from a B-29 mothership, was classified by the military and not publicly confirmed until June 1948.

## Key Achievements

- **First supersonic flight** (Mach 1.06), October 14, 1947, Bell X-1
- **World speed record**, December 12, 1953, flying the Bell X-1A at Mach 2.44 (~1,650 mph)
- **13 aerial victories** in WWII, including an Me 262 jet
- Flew over 360 different aircraft types and logged more than 10,000 flight hours
- Commanded the USAF Aerospace Research Pilot School, which trained many of NASA's astronaut candidates

## Controversies & Criticism

Yeager was known for blunt, sometimes abrasive opinions. He publicly disputed accounts in Tom Wolfe's 1979 book *The Right Stuff*, which portrayed him as the spiritual ideal of the test pilot. He also faced criticism for his role as a consultant and spokesperson for Delco car products, which some felt commercialized his legacy. In later years, a contentious public dispute with his adult children over his second wife attracted tabloid attention and cast a shadow over his final years.

## Legacy & Influence

Yeager's 1947 flight redefined the limits of human aviation and directly enabled the development of supersonic military jets, commercial flight research, and the space program. Edwards Air Force Base named its flight-test facility the "Chuck Yeager Flight Test Center" in his honor. His 1985 autobiography *Yeager* became a bestseller and reinforced his status as an American folk hero. He was awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1985 and a special Congressional Silver Medal in 2002. His name remains synonymous with cool-headed courage under extreme conditions.
