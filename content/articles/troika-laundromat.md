## What It Refers To

- The term "Troika Laundromat" is used in journalism for an alleged large-scale money-laundering and capital-flight mechanism associated with networks of shell companies and cross-border bank transfers.
- It is commonly described as moving money from Russia through accounts in the Baltics and onward to other jurisdictions.
- Public descriptions typically frame it as a "laundromat" pattern: layering funds through many transactions to obscure origin, ownership, and purpose.

## How Laundromat Schemes Commonly Work (High-Level)

- **Shell entities:** Companies with opaque or nominee ownership are used to open accounts and sign contracts.
- **Fictitious invoicing / contracts:** Paperwork (e.g., consulting, loans, or trade) can be used to justify transfers.
- **Layering:** Funds are split and routed through multiple banks and jurisdictions to make tracing harder.
- **Integration:** Money re-enters the financial system as apparently legitimate payments, investments, or purchases.

## Why It Matters

- **Regulatory focus:** Laundromat cases highlight gaps in beneficial-ownership transparency and cross-border supervision.
- **Banking risk:** They are frequently used as examples in discussions of correspondent-banking due diligence and AML (anti-money laundering) controls.
- **Political and economic implications:** Large-scale capital flight can affect domestic investment and can be linked (in public debate) to corruption and sanctions-evasion risks.

## Commonly Cited Outputs of Investigations

- Identification of recurring shell-company structures and payment corridors.
- Mapping of transaction networks and intermediaries.
- Calls for stronger reporting standards and more consistent enforcement across jurisdictions.
