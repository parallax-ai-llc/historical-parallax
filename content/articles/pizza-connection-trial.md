## Summary

Major 1985-1987 federal trial that exposed a $1.65 billion heroin trafficking operation run through pizza parlors by the Sicilian Mafia in the United States.

## Additional Context

### Investigative Background

The "Pizza Connection" label refers to allegations that heroin trafficking and money movement were facilitated through a network of ostensibly legitimate businesses, including pizza parlors, connected to organized-crime figures. The case is often discussed alongside a broader era of U.S. federal efforts to target transnational narcotics supply chains and Mafia infrastructure.

### Trial Scale and Duration

The proceeding became notable for its exceptional length and complexity. Accounts emphasize the volume of evidence, the number of defendants, and the logistical burden placed on the court, jurors, and security arrangements.

### Charges and Legal Tools

Summaries typically describe the prosecution as relying on conspiracy frameworks and, in some coverage, the broader set of anti-organized-crime statutes used in the 1980s. The case is frequently cited as part of the period when federal prosecutors increasingly pursued large, multi-defendant cases aimed at dismantling networks rather than isolated actors.

### Outcomes and Aftermath

Secondary sources note multiple convictions and lengthy sentences, followed by appeals and continued litigation in subsequent years. The trial has been referenced in historical treatments of U.S.–Italy cooperation against organized crime and the development of investigative approaches to complex financial and narcotics conspiracies.

### Historical Significance

In public memory, the trial functions as a symbol of both the reach of Sicilian Mafia-linked networks in the United States and the capacity of the U.S. federal system to mount long-running prosecutions. It is also cited as an example of the trade-offs involved in mega-trials: comprehensive narrative scope versus cost, duration, and procedural risk.
