## Summary

Russian noblewoman who tortured and murdered at least 38 of her serfs, most of whom were young women, between 1756 and 1762. Known as Saltychikha, she was sentenced to life imprisonment by Catherine the Great.

## Historical Context

- Saltykova’s crimes occurred under the **Russian serfdom** system, where landowners held extensive power over serfs’ lives and labor.
- Her case became notable partly because elite perpetrators were rarely punished severely for violence against serfs.

## Investigation and Trial (High Level)

- Petitions and complaints from victims’ relatives ultimately reached authorities and prompted a formal inquiry.
- The proceedings were closely associated with the reign of **Catherine II (Catherine the Great)**, who used the case to signal limits on noble impunity.

## Sentence and Confinement

- Saltykova was publicly denounced and then confined for life, spending decades imprisoned rather than being executed.
- Accounts emphasize the **symbolic, exemplary** nature of the punishment in addition to its severity.

## Legacy

- Saltykova remains a frequently cited example in discussions of gender, aristocratic power, and violence within the serfdom era.
- In popular history, she is often referenced as one of the most infamous Russian serial killers.
- The case is also used as a lens for understanding the legal and moral boundaries of noble authority in 18th-century Russia.
