## Summary

REvil ransomware group attacked JBS, the world's largest meat processor, forcing shutdown of plants in the US, Australia, and Canada. JBS paid $11 million ransom.

## Additional Context

### What was targeted

- JBS S.A. operates large-scale meat processing and distribution; the incident primarily disrupted operations in North America and Australia.
- Public reporting described the disruption as affecting IT systems used for scheduling, logistics, and plant operations, leading to temporary closures.

### Operational and market impact

- Multiple plants paused operations, creating near-term uncertainty around meat supply and processing throughput.
- The incident was widely cited as an example of how cyberattacks can cause real-world disruption to food supply chains.

### Response and recovery

- JBS stated it worked with incident response firms and law enforcement while restoring systems.
- Plants gradually resumed operations after shutdowns; the event became a high-profile case study for ransomware response in critical industries.

### Ransom payment

- JBS reported paying an $11 million ransom, which drew public and policy attention to the incentives and risks of ransomware payments.
- Reporting around the payment emphasized the tension between rapid restoration of operations and discouraging criminal activity.

### Attributed threat actor

- The attack was commonly attributed in public reporting to the REvil (Sodinokibi) ransomware group.
- Attribution discussions often referenced patterns of prior REvil campaigns and related infrastructure, without necessarily implying a final legal determination.

### Broader significance

- The episode is frequently referenced in discussions about cybersecurity for industrial operators and large suppliers.
- It contributed to broader debates on minimum cybersecurity practices, incident disclosure, and resilience planning for essential services.
