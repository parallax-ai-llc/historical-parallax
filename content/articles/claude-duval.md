## Summary

French highwayman operating in England known for his gallantry toward female victims, reportedly dancing with one on the roadside. Hanged at Tyburn after being caught drunk in a tavern.

## Historical Setting

Duval’s story sits in the Restoration period in England, when highway robbery was both feared and sensationalized. The roads around London were busy with coaches, and travelers could be vulnerable to organized bands of thieves.

## Reputation and Folklore

Many details about Duval—especially the "gallant" anecdotes—come from popular pamphlets, ballads, and later retellings. Such sources often mixed fact and entertainment, creating a recognizable archetype of the charming highwayman.

## Capture and Execution

Tyburn was London’s principal execution site for centuries and a public spectacle. Convicted highwaymen could become minor celebrities, and their deaths were reported and commercialized in print.

## Legacy

Claude Duval endures less as a documented criminal mastermind than as a cultural figure: a case study in how crime, class fascination, and early modern media combined to turn offenders into legends.
