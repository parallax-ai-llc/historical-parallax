## Summary

French con artist who psychologically manipulated the aristocratic de Védrines family for over a decade, convincing them they were targeted by Freemasons and needed his protection. He swindled them out of €4.5 million.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2001 | Began manipulating the de Védrines family |
| 2009 | Arrested in Oxford, England |
| 2012 | Convicted and sentenced to 10 years in prison |

## Background and Context

- Tilly became known for targeting a prominent French family with long-standing social status and substantial assets.
- His influence relied on isolating victims from friends, professional advisers, and outside sources of reality-checking.
- The case is often discussed as an example of coercive control that can occur outside formal religious or political movements.

## Methods of Control (High-Level)

- **Conspiracy framing:** persuading victims they faced a hidden, powerful enemy and that he was uniquely able to protect them.
- **Isolation:** encouraging reduced contact with outsiders and increased dependence on his guidance.
- **Financial extraction:** directing transfers of money and assets under the pretext of security, secrecy, or urgent threats.
- **Identity and role reshaping:** pushing victims into new narratives about who they were and what they “must” do to survive.

## Victims and Impact

- Multiple members of the de Védrines family were drawn into the scheme over many years.
- The reported losses included large financial transfers and the liquidation of property and savings.
- Beyond money, victims described profound personal and psychological harm, including damaged relationships and social disconnection.

## Legal Outcome and Aftermath

- Tilly’s arrest in the United Kingdom and subsequent prosecution in France highlighted cross-border elements common in modern fraud cases.
- His conviction and prison sentence reflected both financial wrongdoing and the severe nature of the manipulation involved.
- The case remains a frequently cited reference point in media discussions about “guru” scams, family coercion, and long-term fraud.
