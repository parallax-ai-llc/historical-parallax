## Summary

Senior Aum member who coordinated the 1995 Tokyo sarin attack as the cults intelligence chief.

| Year | Event |

## Additional Context

- Aum Shinrikyo developed a hierarchical structure, and Inoue was described in court records and reporting as part of its senior leadership.
- The 1995 Tokyo subway sarin attack is widely treated as a turning point in Japan’s modern counterterrorism posture and public awareness of domestic extremism.

## Role and Aftermath

- In accounts of the case, his role is typically framed as coordination and planning rather than direct execution on the trains.
- After the attack, Japanese authorities pursued a long investigation and prosecutions against multiple Aum members across different roles.
- Inoue was ultimately convicted and later executed as part of the broader set of Aum-related death sentences carried out in 2018.

## Why It Matters

- The case is frequently cited in discussions of how charismatic groups can evolve into violent organizations.
- It also appears in policy debates on chemical weapons, emergency response, and the challenges of detecting small-group plots.

## Key Takeaways

- The Tokyo subway sarin attack is one of the most studied modern incidents of chemical terrorism.
- Leadership and coordination roles within extremist groups can be as operationally important as frontline participation.
- The Aum prosecutions are often cited as a landmark episode in Japan’s criminal-justice response to terrorism.
