## Summary

Yugoslav Navy admiral who pleaded guilty at the ICTY for the bombardment of Dubrovnik's Old Town. He was the first naval officer convicted by an international tribunal since Nuremberg.

## Military position and Dubrovnik (1991)

- Jokić was a senior officer in the **Yugoslav Navy** during the breakup of Yugoslavia.
- He was linked to command responsibility for naval forces involved in the 1991 shelling of **Dubrovnik**, including damage to the Old Town, a UNESCO World Heritage site.

## International humanitarian law context

- Cultural heritage sites (such as UNESCO-designated areas) are afforded special protection under the laws of armed conflict.
- The Dubrovnik bombardment became a highly visible case internationally because the Old Town’s cultural significance made the destruction especially legible to global audiences.

## ICTY proceedings

- The International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (**ICTY**) charged Jokić in connection with unlawful attacks and destruction.
- He **pleaded guilty**, which the tribunal treated as significant for establishing an evidentiary record and individual accountability.
- The case is often referenced in discussions of: command responsibility, unlawful attacks on civilian areas, and the prosecution of damage to cultural property.

## Sentence and aftermath

- He received a **7-year** prison sentence.
- The Dubrovnik case contributed to the ICTY’s broader jurisprudence on targeting and proportionality and helped document the course of the 1991 campaign on the Adriatic coast.

## Historical significance

- The event remains one of the best-known episodes of the Croatian War of Independence.
- The legal record helped anchor historical narratives about responsibility for the Dubrovnik attacks.
