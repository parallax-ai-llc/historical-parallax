## Summary

Michelangelo Merisi da Caravaggio (1571-1610) was an Italian Baroque painter who revolutionized European art through his dramatic use of light and shadow, known as tenebrism. Famous for masterpieces like *The Calling of Saint Matthew* and *Judith Beheading Holofernes*, he combined intense realism with theatrical lighting to create emotionally powerful religious works. His violent personal life, including arrests, brawls, and a murder conviction in 1606, forced him into exile across Naples, Malta, and Sicily. Despite dying at age 38 under mysterious circumstances, his influence profoundly shaped Baroque painting and continues to resonate in art, photography, and film today.[^1][^2][^3]

---

## Early Life and Background

Michelangelo Merisi da Caravaggio was born on September 29, 1571, though the exact location remains debated among scholars. Some sources indicate he was born in Milan, where his father Fermo Merixio worked as a household administrator and architect-decorator to the Marquess of Caravaggio, while other historical records suggest he was born in the small town of Caravaggio itself, located approximately 22-35 kilometers east of Milan in Lombardy.[^4][^5][^6] His Christian name of Michelangelo suggests his birth occurred on September 29, the feast day of the Archangel Michael.[^7]

His mother, Lucia Aratori, came from a propertied family of the same district, and his maternal grandfather, Giovan Giacomo Aratori, was a land surveyor who acted as an agent for Francesco Sforza I.[^8] Significantly, Caravaggio's maternal aunt Margarita served as wet nurse to the children of Francesco Sforza I and his wife, Costanza Colonna, marchesa of Caravaggio—connections to these powerful Sforza and Colonna families that would prove crucial throughout his turbulent life.[^9]

Tragedy struck early and devastatingly. In 1576, the family moved to the town of Caravaggio to escape a plague ravaging Milan. The following year, in 1577, both his father and grandfather died on the same day from the plague.[^10][^11] By age six, Caravaggio had lost almost every male member of his family to this devastating disease.[^12] His mother died in 1584, the same year he began his apprenticeship, leaving the young artist essentially orphaned.[^13]

At age twelve in 1584, Caravaggio signed a contract of apprenticeship with Simone Peterzano, a minor Milanese painter described in the contract as a pupil of Titian.[^14] During this four-year apprenticeship, he would have become familiar with Milan's artistic treasures, including Leonardo da Vinci's *Last Supper*, and absorbed the regional Lombard art style that valued simplicity and naturalistic detail—a style closer to German naturalism than to the stylized formality and grandeur of Roman Mannerism.[^15][^16] This early training in careful observation and attention to realistic detail would become fundamental to his revolutionary approach to painting.

Following his apprenticeship, in 1592, the young Caravaggio left Milan for Rome "after certain quarrels" and the wounding of a police officer—the first documented sign of the violent temperament that would shadow him throughout his life.[^17]

---

## Rise to Prominence

Caravaggio arrived in Rome in his early twenties with very little money and few connections, entering a city that was the artistic center of Italy and Europe.[^18] Initially, he struggled to establish himself, working for various picture dealers in the mid-1590s, including Costantino Spata, with whom he formed a working relationship.[^19] During this difficult period, he painted smaller works and genre scenes to survive.

His breakthrough came when one of his paintings, *The Cardsharps*, attracted the attention of Cardinal Francesco Maria del Monte, one of the leading connoisseurs in Rome.[^20] This patronage proved transformative. Del Monte took Caravaggio into his household around 1595, providing him with room and board and introducing him to Rome's wealthy art-loving circle.[^21] For del Monte and his associates, Caravaggio executed intimate chamber pieces including *The Musicians*, *The Lute Player*, a sensuous *Bacchus*, and an allegorical *Boy Bitten by a Lizard*.[^22]

During these years, Caravaggio forged important friendships that would shape his career and personal life. The painter Prospero Orsi introduced him to influential collectors, while the architect Onorio Longhi introduced him to the dangerous world of Roman street brawls.[^23] Most significantly, he befriended Mario Minniti, a sixteen-year-old Sicilian artist who served as his model, friend, and possibly apprentice, appearing in many paintings between 1592 and 1600.[^24][^25]

Another crucial patron was Marchese Vincenzo Giustiniani, del Monte's friend and fellow connoisseur, who became an avid collector of Caravaggio's works, with thirteen paintings listed in his possession in a 1637 inventory.[^26][^27] Giustiniani would prove instrumental in supporting Caravaggio, even purchasing works that other patrons rejected.

The true turning point came on July 23, 1599, when Caravaggio signed a contract to paint two large canvases for the side walls of the Contarelli Chapel of San Luigi dei Francesi, the church of the French in Rome.[^28] The commission was secured through Cardinal del Monte's influence. The two works—*The Martyrdom of Saint Matthew* and *The Calling of Saint Matthew*—delivered in 1600, were an immediate sensation.[^29] These paintings established Caravaggio as the most dynamic and original painter working in Rome, and thereafter he never lacked for commissions or patrons.[^30][^31]

In autumn 1600, more prestigious commissions followed, notably for the funerary chapel of Tiberio Cerasi in Santa Maria del Popolo, where he painted *The Conversion of St. Paul* and *The Crucifixion of St. Peter*.[^32] By 1605, other Roman artists such as Bartolomeo Manfredi were beginning to imitate his signature style, marking the beginning of the Caravaggisti movement.[^33]

## Major Achievements

Caravaggio's artistic achievements fundamentally transformed European painting through his revolutionary techniques and emotionally powerful works. His innovation lay in combining radical naturalism—close physical observation from life—with dramatic, theatrical use of chiaroscuro that came to be known as tenebrism.[^34] He worked rapidly with live models, preferring to forgo preliminary drawings and instead scoring basic guides directly onto the canvas with the end of his brush handle.[^35]

### Revolutionary Technique

Caravaggio was the first painter to incorporate chiaroscuro as a dominant stylistic element, making his shadows darker and painting clearly defined rays of light to spotlight the drama of his narratives.[^36] His employment of tenebrism—extreme contrasts of light and dark—brought high drama to his subjects, creating a theatrical effect that has been likened to a spotlit stage.[^37] In tenebrist paintings, figures are portrayed against backgrounds of intense darkness, illuminated by a bright, searching light that sets off their three-dimensional forms through harsh but exquisitely controlled contrasts.[^38]

### Major Masterpieces

*The Calling of Saint Matthew* (1599-1600) remains one of his most celebrated works, capturing the moment when Jesus calls the tax collector Matthew to discipleship. A beam of light enters from the right, highlighting Matthew and symbolizing his spiritual awakening, while Jesus, bathed in shadow, extends his hand in a gesture echoing Michelangelo's *Creation of Adam*.[^39] The painting remains in its original location in the Contarelli Chapel over 420 years after Caravaggio painted it.[^40]

*Judith Beheading Holofernes* (c. 1598-1599) depicts the biblical widow at the critical moment, resolutely delivering the final blow with a sword, gripping Holofernes's hair as blood streams onto white linen. The look of horror on Holofernes's face and Judith's determined expression exemplify Caravaggio's mastery of psychological realism.[^41][^42]

*The Entombment of Christ* (1603-1604), created for the Chiesa Nuova and now in the Vatican Pinacoteca, demonstrates his ability to convey profound religious emotion through ordinary human bodies and gestures.[^43] The work forms part of what art historian Denis Mahon called Caravaggio's "middle group" representing his mature period.[^44]

*The Conversion of Saint Paul* (1601) for the Cerasi Chapel captures Saul falling from his horse on the Damascus road, lying supine with eyes shut, legs spread, and arms raised upward as if embracing his divine vision—a moment of intense religious ecstasy rendered through dramatic lighting and foreshortening.[^45][^46]

Other significant masterpieces include *Bacchus* (1595), showing the wine god in languid youth; *Narcissus* (c. 1598-1600), depicting the youth mesmerized by his own reflection; *Basket of Fruit*, widely regarded as the first modern still life painting; *The Supper at Emmaus* (1601), where the resurrected Christ reveals himself to his disciples; and *Medusa* (1597), painted on a tournament shield using his own face for the victim.[^47][^48][^49][^50][^51]

### Self-Portrait as Art

Caravaggio incorporated self-portraits into several works, most famously as the severed heads in *Medusa* (1597) and *David with the Head of Goliath* (1610).[^52] The latter painting, sent to the papal court as a painted petition for pardon, shows the young David holding the giant's head—Caravaggio's own face in death. The sword carries the inscription "H-AS OS," interpreted as *humilitas occidit superbiam* ("humility kills pride").[^53][^54] Some scholars believe this may be a double self-portrait, with David representing the painter rejuvenated.[^55]

### Influence on Baroque Art

His paintings for the Contarelli Chapel instantly established him as Rome's leading painter. He went on to secure prestigious commissions for religious works featuring violent struggles, grotesque decapitations, torture, and death—subjects rendered with unprecedented emotional intensity through his combination of theatrical lighting and gritty naturalism.[^56] His technique not only influenced individual artists but became a defining characteristic of Baroque painting as a whole.[^57]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Caravaggio's personal life was as dramatic and violent as the subjects he painted, marked by constant legal troubles, brawls, and ultimately murder. He became notorious for drinking, gambling, sword-carrying, and brawling, accumulating an extensive criminal record between 1598 and 1606.[^58]

### Criminal Record

Between 1598 and 1601 alone, he was arrested for carrying a sword without a permit, sued for beating a man with a stick, and accused of attacking another man with a sword.[^59] He went to trial at least eleven times in the early seventeenth century for offenses including writing libelous poems, throwing a plate of artichokes at a waiter's face, and assaulting people with swords.[^60][^61] Additional arrests included being charged for writing scurrilous verses about rival Giovanni Baglione, arrested for affray and assault (once claiming "I wounded myself with my own sword when I fell down these stairs"), throwing stones at constables, abusing officers, and even smearing excrement on a house.[^62]

Evidence from the 1603 libel trial brought by Giovanni Baglione revealed that the pamphlets attacking Baglione had been distributed by "a certain Giovanni Battista, a bardassa, or boy prostitute, shared by Caravaggio and his friend Onorio Longhi."[^63]

### The Murder

On May 29, 1606, Caravaggio killed a man named Ranuccio Tomassoni during a brawl in Rome.[^64] Historians long theorized the fight occurred over a tennis match, but a 2002 documentary suggested they were actually fighting over a female prostitute and that Caravaggio killed Tomassoni while attempting to castrate him.[^65] Since dueling was illegal, Caravaggio fled Rome and was convicted of murder in absentia, with a death warrant issued meaning anyone could legally kill him for a reward.[^66]

### Exile and Further Violence

In 1608, while in Malta and still wanted for murder in Rome, Caravaggio attacked a senior knight, was imprisoned for the assault, but escaped and fled to Naples.[^67] He was formally expelled from the Knights of Malta and branded *"putridum et foetidum membrum"*—a rotten and stinking limb.[^68] In Naples in 1609, his attacker from Malta tracked him down and disfigured his face in a violent assault outside a tavern.[^69]

### Artistic Scandals

His artistic choices also sparked controversies. One famous scandal involved using a sex worker as a model for *Death of the Virgin*, depicting the Virgin Mary for a church—a choice that outraged the patron when discovered.[^70] His naturalistic treatment of sacred figures, depicting holy beings in contemporary seventeenth-century interiors with ordinary people as models, enraged several critics, and many of his artworks were rejected by contracting institutions for profane or immoral depictions.[^71] Between 1600 and 1605, three out of five public commissions were rejected, charged with lacking decorum and having a stagelike quality.[^72]

### Death Under Mysterious Circumstances

In 1610, while attempting to secure a papal pardon and return to Rome, Caravaggio died at age 38 in Porto Ercole. The official cause was recorded as fever, with possible explanations including syphilis, malaria, an infected sword wound, and lead poisoning from paint.[^73][^74] However, theories persist that he was assassinated by the Knights of Malta with Vatican connivance as an act of vengeance.[^75] Research published in 2018 concluded he likely died from sepsis caused by Staphylococcus aureus resulting from a wound sustained in the Naples brawl.[^76]

## Personal Life

Caravaggio's personal relationships were complex and often tumultuous, reflecting the chaos that characterized most aspects of his existence. The balance of probability suggests that Caravaggio had sexual relations with both men and women, though he settled with no one and was as uneasy in his relationships as in other areas of life.[^77][^78]

### Relationships with Women

He was rumored to be madly in love with Fillide Melandroni, a well-known Roman prostitute who modeled for several important paintings.[^79] A connection with a woman named Lena is mentioned in a 1605 court deposition by Pasqualone, where she is described as "Michelangelo's girl." According to scholar Catherine Puglisi, Lena may have been the same person as courtesan Maddalena di Paolo Antognetti, who named Caravaggio as an "intimate friend" in her own 1604 testimony.[^80]

### Relationships with Men

Historical speculation about Caravaggio's sexuality dates to claims by Honoré Gabriel Riqueti, comte de Mirabeau, who in 1783 contrasted the artist's personal life with the writings of St. Paul.[^81] Evidence also comes from the 1603 libel trial, which revealed his association with a boy prostitute shared with his friend Onorio Longhi.[^82]

His relationship with young Mario Minniti began when Caravaggio was twenty-one and Minniti sixteen. Minniti served as friend, fellow artist, possible apprentice, and model for many paintings between 1592 and 1600.[^83] Another significant relationship was with Cecco del Caravaggio (Francesco Boneri), who was Caravaggio's assistant, model for numerous works, and supposed lover.[^84]

There is no absolute proof of homosexual relationships, only strong circumstantial evidence and much rumor, but scholarly consensus suggests Caravaggio likely had intimate relationships with men as well as women.[^85]

### Friends and Associates

Beyond romantic or sexual relationships, Caravaggio forged some extremely important friendships. The painter Prospero Orsi, established in the profession, introduced him to influential collectors.[^86] The architect Onorio Longhi, more balefully, introduced him to the world of Roman street brawls.[^87] Mario Minniti, years after modeling for Caravaggio in Rome, would be instrumental in helping him obtain important commissions in Sicily during his exile.[^88]

### Personality

Contemporary accounts and his criminal record paint a picture of a man who was violent, touchy, provocative, prone to sudden rages, and incapable of backing down from confrontations.[^89] His art suggests someone capable of intense observation, profound empathy, and psychological insight, yet his life demonstrated an inability to control his impulses or live peacefully within society's constraints.

## Legacy

Despite living only 38 years, Caravaggio exerted a profound and lasting influence on art history that continues to resonate more than four centuries after his death. His revolutionary approach to painting transformed the course of European art and established techniques that remain influential across multiple media today.

### Immediate Artistic Impact

By 1605, just five years after his breakthrough with the Contarelli Chapel paintings, other Roman artists were already imitating his signature style.[^90] Artists heavily influenced by Caravaggio became known as the "Caravaggisti" (or "Caravagesques"), also called tenebrists or tenebrosi ("shadowists").[^91] These painters emulated aspects of his style, technique, and subject choices, becoming responsible for disseminating Caravaggism across the European continent.[^92]

Shortly after his death, artists outside Italy such as Rembrandt and Diego Velázquez were incorporating Caravaggio's dramatic lighting effects into their own landmark works.[^93] The influences of Caravaggio were particularly strong on Peter Paul Rubens, who exploited tenebrism for dramatic effect and even painted a copy of *The Entombment of Christ*.[^94][^95]

### Geographic Spread of Influence

**The Netherlands:** A number of Catholic artists from Utrecht—including Hendrick ter Brugghen, Gerrit van Honthorst, and Dirck van Baburen—traveled to Rome in the first decades of the seventeenth century, where they became profoundly influenced by Caravaggio's work.[^96] Known as the Utrecht Caravaggisti, they introduced Caravaggism into Dutch painting around 1620.[^97] The Utrecht School in turn influenced Frans Hals, Rembrandt, and Vermeer, demonstrating Caravaggio's far-reaching impact on Dutch art.[^98]

**Flanders:** Flemish artists coming onto the art scene in the 1620s were heavily influenced by Caravaggio, creating a Caravaggist craze in Flanders from about 1620 to 1640.[^99] These artists are referred to as the Ghent Caravaggisti and the Antwerp Caravaggisti, including Jan Janssens, Melchior de la Mars, Theodoor Rombouts, Gerard Seghers, Jan Cossiers, and Adam de Coster.[^100]

**Spain:** The Spanish connection—Naples was a Spanish possession—proved instrumental in forming the important Spanish branch of Caravaggio's influence.[^101] Francisco Ribalta became among the first Spanish followers of tenebrism, though it remains unclear if he directly visited Rome or Naples.[^102] Caravaggio's influence can be seen directly or indirectly in the work of Jusepe de Ribera and Velázquez.[^103]

### Long-Term Historical Impact

Even though he lived only to age 38, Caravaggio had a profound influence on later art movements, most notably Baroque art and nineteenth-century Realism.[^104] According to influential art historian Roberto Longhi, whose 1951 Milanese exhibition and 1952 monograph returned Caravaggio to public prominence: "Ribera, Vermeer, La Tour and Rembrandt could not have existed without him. And the art of Delacroix, Courbet, and Manet would have been utterly different."[^105]

The enormously influential American art historian Bernard Berenson stated: "With the exception of Michelangelo, no other Italian painter exercised so great an influence."[^106] Modern art critic Robert Hughes observed: "There was art before him and after him, and they were not the same."[^107]

### Contemporary Relevance

Beyond Baroque painting, Caravaggio's chiaroscuro technique has left a lasting legacy across different genres and mediums.[^108] His dramatic use of light and shadow as an expressive technique continues to inspire artists and has transformed multiple art forms.[^109] The influence of his tenebrism is observed in photography, film noir, and even contemporary digital art, where dramatic lighting and strong contrasts remain powerful storytelling tools.[^110]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Modern and contemporary fascination with Caravaggio is largely due to Italian art historian Roberto Longhi, whose mid-twentieth-century work returned the artist to public attention and cemented his current status as one of history's most important painters.[^111] Longhi's assessment emphasized Caravaggio's revolutionary impact on subsequent masters, arguing that major figures of European art could not have developed as they did without his innovations.[^112]

Bernard Berenson's declaration that no Italian painter except Michelangelo exercised greater influence represents the scholarly consensus on Caravaggio's historical importance.[^113] Contemporary accounts from the seventeenth century noted that "painters in Rome were taken with this novelty" and praised him as a unique imitator of nature.[^114] Importantly, despite negative responses to his naturalism, Caravaggio's contemporaries lavished praise on his innovative use of color—a fact often overlooked. His *colore* was credited with verisimilitude and imaginativeness, with art critic Giovanni Pietro Bellori writing that "Caravaggio's colors are prized wherever art is valued."[^115][^116]

Modern scholars appreciate his radical approach to religious painting, his psychological realism, his technical mastery of light, and his courage in depicting sacred subjects through the lens of ordinary human experience. His ability to convey spiritual truths through gritty earthly realism—making biblical scenes immediate and emotionally accessible—is now recognized as profoundly innovative rather than sacrilegious.

The story of "Late Caravaggio"—his fugitive years from 1606 to 1610—is recognized as the period when he produced some of the most powerful and emotionally intense pictures of the entire seventeenth century.[^117] Far from diminishing his artistry, his exile seemed to deepen his psychological insight and emotional power.

### Negative Views

During his lifetime and in the decades following his death, Caravaggio faced severe criticism from influential figures. His work was accused of "allegedly displaying poor invenzione, being overly dependent on the model, and lacking in disegno and decorum."[^118]

Giovanni Baglione, his direct competitor and arch-enemy, virulently attacked both Caravaggio's personal life and artworks at every opportunity, gleefully authoring a posthumous biography that criticized the artist's work.[^119] Nicolas Poussin, a key French Baroque artist, despised Caravaggio's dark, gritty realism, preferring idealizing classicism. His harsh assessment included the statement: "That man was born to destroy the art of painting."[^120]

John Ruskin, an opponent of the Baroque style, described Caravaggio's paintings as filled with "horror and ugliness and filthiness of sin."[^121] Seventeenth-century critics were particularly wary of how Caravaggio's naturalism treated sacred figures, resulting in a lack of idealization that many found offensive.[^122] The naturalism of his religious works and the contrast of holy beings with contemporary interiors enraged several critics, leading to numerous rejections by commissioning institutions for profane or immoral depictions.[^123]

A cardinal's secretary famously criticized one painting, writing it contained "vulgarity, sacrilege, impiousness and disgust."[^124]

After the damage inflicted on his reputation in the seventeenth century, Caravaggio was largely forgotten, his works misattributed and basically ignored until the twentieth-century reassessment.[^125] Contemporary Caravaggio scholarship has its own challenges: some scholars note "a blatant disproportion between quantity and quality" in recent research, with Caravaggio studies seemingly stuck in a "tautological loop," perpetually reiterating sometimes contradictory notions inherited from nineteenth and twentieth-century scholars.[^126]
