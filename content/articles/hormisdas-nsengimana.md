## Summary

Rwandan Catholic priest acquitted by the ICTR of genocide charges related to massacres at his parish during the genocide. His trial highlighted the controversial role of some clergy during the genocide.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1994 | Accused of participating in genocide at his parish |
| 2003 | Arrested |
| 2009 | Acquitted of all charges by ICTR |

## Additional Context

### ICTR proceedings

- The International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR) was established by the UN to prosecute those most responsible for genocide and other serious violations of international humanitarian law committed in 1994.
- Cases involving clergy attracted attention because churches and parishes were sometimes sites of refuge, but also—according to some allegations—sites of violence.

### Allegations and defense

- In ICTR trials, the prosecution typically sought to prove individual criminal responsibility (for example, ordering, aiding, or otherwise participating in attacks).
- Acquittals can result when the chamber finds the prosecution did not prove the charges beyond reasonable doubt, including issues such as witness credibility, identification, or linkage to specific crimes.

### Aftermath and broader significance

- The case is often discussed in the broader debate about institutional responsibility versus individual guilt during mass violence.
- It also illustrates the evidentiary challenges of post-conflict justice processes that rely heavily on witness testimony years after events.

### Related institutions

- The ICTR operated alongside domestic Rwandan courts and community-based gacaca jurisdictions, each with different mandates and procedures.
- ICTR judgments, including acquittals, form part of the tribunal’s jurisprudence on genocide, crimes against humanity, and modes of liability.
