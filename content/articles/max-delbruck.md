## Summary

Max Delbruck shared the 1969 Nobel Prize for discoveries on virus replication. He founded the phage group that launched molecular biology.

## Scientific Background

Delbrück was trained as a **physicist** in Germany and is often described as part of the cohort of early-20th-century scientists who moved between physics and biology. In the interwar and early postwar periods, ideas from physics—quantification, modeling, and attention to mechanisms—helped shape emerging approaches to genetics and cellular processes.

## The Phage Group and Molecular Biology

Delbrück became a central figure in the informal “**phage group**,” a network of researchers who used **bacteriophages** (viruses that infect bacteria) as model systems to study heredity and replication.

Key features of the phage approach included:

- Simple, fast-replicating organisms that made experiments tractable
- Clear genetic outcomes (e.g., mutation, resistance) that could be measured
- A culture of open exchange of methods and results, including summer courses that trained new researchers

## Major Contributions

- **Virus replication and genetics**: Delbrück’s work helped clarify how viruses reproduce and how genetic variation can be studied experimentally.
- **Luria–Delbrück experiment**: In collaboration with Salvador Luria, he co-developed a classic fluctuation test showing that bacterial resistance to phages arises from **random mutations** rather than directed adaptation—an important result for modern genetics.

## Institutions and Influence

Delbrück spent much of his career in the United States, including association with **Caltech**, where he influenced generations of scientists and helped legitimize “molecular” approaches to biology.

## Nobel Prize (1969)

Delbrück shared the **1969 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine** for discoveries concerning the replication mechanism and the genetic structure of viruses, highlighting the role of phage research in the foundations of molecular biology.

## Legacy

Delbrück is remembered less for a single invention than for catalyzing a research community and methodological style that made molecular biology a coherent field.
