## Background

- Monique Olivier is a French woman known primarily for her role as an accomplice to the serial crimes of Michel Fourniret.
- In public sources, she is often described as Fourniret’s wife and a participant in elements of his abductions and killings.

## Role in the Crimes (High-Level)

- Prosecutors and courts described her as helping lure victims, facilitate kidnappings, and support Fourniret’s movements and concealment.
- Her involvement is commonly framed as *accomplice* behavior rather than being the principal perpetrator.
- Accounts differ on the extent of coercion versus active participation; summaries should be grounded in court findings and documented reporting.

## Legal Proceedings

- She was convicted in France as an accomplice in a major Fourniret case (commonly reported as a 2008 conviction).
- Additional investigative work and proceedings continued for years as older missing-person cases were re-examined and linked to Fourniret.
- Case details (victims, dates, charges, and sentence terms) have evolved across proceedings; consult the reference source for the latest verified status.

## Analytical framing (added high-level context)

- **Accomplice liability:** Many legal systems treat assistance (luring, logistics, concealment) as criminally significant even when a person is not the direct killer; how this is charged and sentenced varies.
- **Coercion vs. agency:** In partner-associated crimes, courts and researchers often consider factors like dependency, intimidation, and prior victimization, while still evaluating concrete acts that enabled the offenses.
- **Cold-case resolution:** Serial investigations sometimes progress through confession, new forensic methods, or renewed witness attention, which can change historical understandings of a perpetrator network.
