## Summary

Choe Yun-ui was a Korean government official who invented the world's first metal movable type printing around 1234, approximately 200 years before Gutenberg's printing press. His innovation was used to print the "Sangjeong Gogeum Yemun" (Prescribed Ritual Texts of the Past and Present). Korean metal movable type is one of Korea's most important technological achievements.

## Historical Context

- The work is associated with the **Goryeo** period, when Korea already had a long tradition of woodblock printing.
- Movable type offered a way to print official and scholarly works more efficiently when texts required frequent revision or reprinting.

## Technology (High Level)

- Metal movable type involves casting individual characters and reusing them to compose pages.
- Compared to carving an entire page on a woodblock, movable type can reduce labor for repeated prints—though it requires precise casting and organization.

## Works and Attribution

- Choe Yun-ui is commonly credited in modern summaries with initiating or overseeing early metal-type printing for state texts such as *Sangjeong Gogeum Yemun*.
- As with many medieval innovations, surviving documentation is limited, and historians discuss the roles of institutions, artisans, and officials alongside named individuals.

## Significance

- Korean metal movable type is widely cited as a major technological milestone in the global history of printing.
- It illustrates how administrative needs, state support, and craft knowledge can converge to produce durable innovations.
- The topic is often contrasted with later European developments to show that printing technologies emerged in multiple regions.
- Modern scholarship also distinguishes between invention, adoption, and scale of use over time.
