## Summary

Athenian metalworker who invented the Brazen Bull torture device, then became its first victim when Phalaris tested it on him.

## Context

Perillos (also rendered *Perilaus* in some translations) is a figure from archaic-era Sicily/Greece whose story is primarily preserved through later literary retellings. He is remembered less for independent inventions and more as a cautionary example about cruelty, tyranny, and poetic justice.

## The Brazen Bull (high-level)

The **Brazen Bull** is described as a hollow metal sculpture of a bull designed to function as an execution/torture device:

- A victim would be placed inside the metal body.
- A fire would be lit beneath it, heating the metal.
- The device was said to be engineered so that screams were acoustically transformed into sounds resembling a bull’s bellowing.

Accounts emphasize the theatrical and intimidatory purpose of the device as much as the physical suffering it inflicted.

## Relationship with Phalaris

Most versions of the story connect Perillos with **Phalaris**, the tyrant of Akragas (Agrigentum) in Sicily:

- Perillos presents the device to Phalaris as a demonstration of craftsmanship.
- Phalaris is portrayed as either testing it immediately or using it to illustrate the moral danger of such an invention.
- The narrative typically culminates in Perillos being forced into the device first, making him the earliest victim in the tradition.

## Legacy and interpretation

Perillos’ story persists because it illustrates recurring themes in ancient moral literature:

- **Hubris of the artisan**: using skill in the service of cruelty.
- **Tyrannical power**: rulers employing spectacle and fear.
- **Retributive irony**: the inventor suffers the fate he designed for others.

Modern readers generally treat the account as a moralized anecdote rather than a fully verifiable biographical record.
