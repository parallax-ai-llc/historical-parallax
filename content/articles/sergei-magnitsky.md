## Summary

Russian tax accountant who uncovered a $230 million tax fraud scheme involving Russian officials and was then arrested and died in custody after being denied medical treatment. His death led to the Magnitsky Act sanctions law.

## Career Timeline

## Case Context (High-Level)

- Magnitsky is widely discussed in connection with allegations of large-scale tax fraud and the subsequent treatment of a key figure who reported the scheme.
- International attention focused not only on the alleged fraud but also on detention conditions, due process concerns, and accountability for officials.

## Detention and Death

- Major summaries describe prolonged pretrial detention, deterioration of health, and disputes over access to medical care.
- His death in custody became a focal point for human-rights reporting and advocacy regarding Russia’s penal and judicial systems.

## International Response and “Magnitsky” Sanctions

- The U.S. Magnitsky Act (2012) is commonly described as a targeted-sanctions framework tied initially to officials linked to the case.
- Over time, “Magnitsky-style” laws became a template for other jurisdictions to impose visa bans and asset freezes on individuals alleged to be involved in serious corruption or human-rights abuses.

## Legacy

- The Magnitsky case is frequently referenced as an example of how a single custody death can catalyze durable foreign-policy tools.
- In contemporary policy discussions, the term “Magnitsky sanctions” often functions as shorthand for individualized accountability mechanisms rather than broad country-wide embargoes.
- The Magnitsky framework is frequently discussed as part of a wider shift toward financial and travel restrictions as tools of diplomatic pressure.
