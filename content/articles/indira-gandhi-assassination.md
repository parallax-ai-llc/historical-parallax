## Summary

Assassination of Indian PM Indira Gandhi by her Sikh bodyguards on October 31, 1984 in retaliation for Operation Blue Star. Her death triggered the 1984 anti-Sikh riots killing 3,000.

## Political and Security Context

- The assassination is commonly understood against the backdrop of rising tensions between the Indian state and Sikh separatist militancy in Punjab in the early 1980s.
- Operation Blue Star (a 1984 Indian military operation at the Golden Temple complex) became a central flashpoint, intensifying political polarization and security concerns.

## The Assassination

- Indira Gandhi was shot by members of her security detail; the attackers were part of her protective unit.
- The event rapidly became a defining moment in modern Indian political history, influencing domestic politics and security policy for decades.

## Aftermath and Communal Violence

- Gandhi’s death was followed by widespread violence against Sikhs in multiple locations, especially in Delhi.
- Estimates for the death toll vary across sources; the episode is widely described as one of the most severe instances of communal violence in independent India.

## Legal Proceedings and Accountability

- The prosecution of the assassination perpetrators and subsequent investigations became a long-running and politically sensitive process.
- Public debate has persisted over the adequacy of investigations and the extent of accountability for the anti-Sikh violence.

## Legacy

- The assassination and its aftermath shaped India’s approach to internal security, intelligence coordination, and communal-relations policy.
- It is frequently referenced in discussions of state power, minority rights, and crisis governance.
