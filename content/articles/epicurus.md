## Summary

Epicurus (341–270 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher who founded Epicureanism, one of the most influential philosophical schools of the Hellenistic period. Born on the island of Samos to Athenian parents, he established his philosophical community known as "The Garden" in Athens around 307/306 BCE, where he taught that the purpose of philosophy was to attain a happy, tranquil life characterized by ataraxia (peace and freedom from fear) and aponia (the absence of pain). His materialist philosophy, based on atomic theory, rejected superstition and divine intervention in human affairs, arguing instead that understanding the natural world could liberate humans from irrational fears. Though only fragments of his extensive writings survive, his ideas profoundly influenced Western thought, from Roman Epicureans like Lucretius to Enlightenment thinkers and modern secular philosophy.

---

## Early Life and Background

Epicurus was born in February 341 BCE on the Aegean island of Samos, where his father Neocles had settled as an Athenian colonist and worked as a schoolteacher.[^1] His mother Chaerestrate was reportedly a fortune-teller, a detail later critics would use to mock his philosophical claims, though the historical accuracy of this characterization remains disputed.[^2] Growing up in a household of modest means, Epicurus received his early education from his father and later studied under various philosophers, including the Platonist Pamphilus when he was around fourteen years old.[^3]

At age eighteen, Epicurus traveled to Athens to complete his two-year military service as an ephebe, a rite of passage for Athenian citizens, arriving in 323 BCE—the year of Alexander the Great's death.[^4] This period coincided with significant political upheaval as Athens lost its independence following the Lamian War. After completing his service, Epicurus joined his family in Colophon, a city in Ionia where many Athenian colonists from Samos had been exiled in 322 BCE.[^5]

During his time in Colophon and later in Mytilene and Lampsacus, Epicurus studied philosophy intensively, particularly the atomistic theories of Democritus and the teachings of various philosophers including Nausiphanes, a follower of Democritus who would significantly influence his thinking about atomism and epistemology.[^6] However, Epicurus later distanced himself from Nausiphanes and claimed to be largely self-taught, a claim that reflected both his independent thinking and perhaps a desire to establish his own philosophical authority.[^7] By his early thirties, Epicurus had begun teaching philosophy, gathering his first students in Mytilene around 311 BCE and then in Lampsacus, where he established a small philosophical community that would form the core of his later school in Athens.[^8]

---

## Rise to Prominence

Epicurus's rise to philosophical prominence began in earnest when he returned to Athens around 307 or 306 BCE, at approximately age thirty-five, and purchased a house with a garden that would become the legendary home of his philosophical school.[^9] Unlike other philosophical schools of the time, such as Plato's Academy or Aristotle's Lyceum, which were located in public gymnasia, Epicurus's school—known simply as "The Garden" (ho Kepos)—was a private space that emphasized withdrawal from public life and the cultivation of friendship among a close-knit community of philosophers.[^10]

The establishment of The Garden represented a radical departure from traditional Greek philosophical education. Epicurus welcomed students regardless of their social status, wealth, or gender—a revolutionary practice in ancient Athens.[^11] Historical sources indicate that women and slaves were admitted to his school, though their exact numbers and roles remain debated among scholars.[^12] This inclusivity, while philosophically consistent with Epicurus's egalitarian ethics, also attracted criticism and scandal from contemporary Athenians who viewed such mixing of social classes and genders as improper.[^13]

Epicurus's teaching method emphasized friendship (philia) as essential to the philosophical life, and members of his community lived together, shared meals, and engaged in philosophical discussion in an atmosphere that reportedly combined serious intellectual inquiry with genuine affection and camaraderie.[^14] The school's motto, inscribed at its entrance, welcomed visitors with the promise: "Stranger, here you will do well to tarry; here our highest good is pleasure."[^15] This public declaration of hedonism—albeit a refined, intellectual hedonism focused on mental tranquility rather than bodily indulgence—immediately set Epicurus apart from and in opposition to the dominant Platonic and Aristotelian schools.

By the time of his death in 270 BCE, Epicurus had established Epicureanism as one of the major philosophical schools of the Hellenistic world, with communities of followers not only in Athens but throughout the Greek-speaking Mediterranean.[^16] His voluminous writings—ancient sources credit him with over three hundred treatises—systematically articulated his philosophy, though tragically only a tiny fraction survives today.[^17] What does survive, primarily through later sources like Diogenes Laertius and Lucretius, reveals a comprehensive philosophical system addressing physics, epistemology, and ethics with remarkable coherence and originality.[^18]

---

## Major Achievements

Epicurus's most significant achievement was the creation of a complete philosophical system that integrated physics, epistemology, and ethics into a coherent whole aimed at achieving human happiness and tranquility.[^19] At the foundation of his philosophy was his atomic theory, adapted from Democritus but modified in crucial ways. Epicurus proposed that the universe consists entirely of atoms—indivisible, eternal particles—and void (empty space), and that all phenomena, including the human soul, could be explained through the motion and combination of these atoms.[^20] Crucially, he introduced the concept of the "swerve" (clinamen in Latin), a slight, unpredictable deviation in the path of falling atoms, which he argued was necessary to explain free will and to avoid strict determinism.[^21]

This materialist physics served an ethical purpose: by demonstrating that the universe operates according to natural laws rather than divine caprice, Epicurus sought to free humans from the fear of gods and death.[^22] He argued that the gods, if they exist, are too blessed and perfect to concern themselves with human affairs, thus eliminating any rational basis for religious fear.[^23] Regarding death, Epicurus offered his famous argument: "Death is nothing to us. When we exist, death is not yet present, and when death is present, then we do not exist."[^24] This reasoning aimed to show that death, being the end of sensation, could neither be experienced nor rationally feared.

In ethics, Epicurus developed a sophisticated form of hedonism—the doctrine that pleasure is the highest good—but defined pleasure primarily as the absence of pain (aponia) in the body and the absence of disturbance (ataraxia) in the soul.[^25] He distinguished between kinetic pleasures (active enjoyments) and katastematic pleasures (the stable state of being free from pain and disturbance), arguing that the latter were superior and constituted true happiness.[^26] This led to surprisingly ascetic practical recommendations: simple food, water instead of wine when water sufficed, and the cultivation of self-sufficiency to minimize dependence on external, unreliable sources of pleasure.[^27]

Epicurus also made significant contributions to epistemology through his theory of knowledge based on sensations, preconceptions, and feelings.[^28] He argued that all sensations are true in the sense that they accurately report the physical interactions between atoms, though our judgments about sensations can be false.[^29] This empiricist approach positioned sense experience as the foundation of all knowledge, anticipating later empiricist philosophers by nearly two millennia.

His ethical teachings emphasized friendship as one of the greatest sources of secure pleasure, arguing that "friendship dances around the world bidding us all to awaken to the recognition of happiness."[^30] The Epicurean community embodied this principle, creating lasting bonds that sustained the school for centuries after its founder's death.[^31] Epicurus also developed a nuanced social philosophy recommending withdrawal from politics and public life—"live unknown" (lathe biosas) was a key maxim—while still acknowledging the necessity of justice as a social contract for mutual advantage.[^32]

His Principal Doctrines and Vatican Sayings, collections of his key teachings preserved by later followers, distilled his philosophy into memorable aphorisms that made his complex system accessible to a wide audience.[^33] The fourfold remedy (tetrapharmakos)—"Don't fear god, don't worry about death; what is good is easy to get, and what is terrible is easy to endure"—became a cornerstone summary of Epicurean practical philosophy.[^34] Through these teachings and the community he established, Epicurus created not merely an abstract philosophical system but a comprehensive way of life that attracted devoted followers for over seven centuries.[^35]

## Controversies and Criticisms

From his own lifetime through antiquity and beyond, Epicurus and his philosophy attracted intense criticism and hostility from rival philosophical schools, religious authorities, and moralists.[^36] The most persistent criticism concerned his hedonism, which opponents caricatured as an endorsement of sensual indulgence, gluttony, and sexual promiscuity.[^37] Stoic philosophers particularly attacked Epicureanism as promoting a shameful, pleasure-seeking life incompatible with virtue, despite Epicurus's own emphasis on moderation and mental pleasures over bodily ones.[^38]

The presence of women in The Garden, including Epicurus's close associate Leontion, generated scandalous rumors about sexual impropriety and debauchery.[^39] Ancient sources hostile to Epicurus claimed that his school engaged in excessive feasting and licentious behavior, allegations that modern scholarship generally regards as unfounded slander from philosophical opponents.[^40] Timocrates, the brother of Epicurus's disciple Metrodorus, defected from the school and wrote exposés claiming that Epicurus vomited twice daily from overeating and lived luxuriously, charges that surviving evidence contradicts.[^41]

Critics also attacked Epicurus's materialism and denial of divine providence as atheism, though technically Epicurus acknowledged the existence of gods while denying their involvement in human affairs.[^42] This position was seen as practically atheistic and dangerous to social order, since it undermined traditional religion and the fear of divine punishment that many believed necessary for moral behavior.[^43] Early Christian writers like Lactantius and Augustine vehemently condemned Epicureanism as incompatible with Christianity, contributing to its eventual decline in late antiquity.[^44]

Philosophers criticized specific doctrines: the "swerve" was mocked as an ad hoc invention lacking evidence; his epistemology was attacked for relying too heavily on sensation; and his ethics were condemned as reducing humans to the level of animals by making pleasure the ultimate good.[^45] Plutarch wrote multiple treatises against Epicureanism, arguing that it made a pleasant life impossible by denying divine providence and reducing the soul to atoms.[^46] Cicero, while personally sympathetic to some Epicurean views, criticized the school's withdrawal from public life as irresponsible and un-Roman.[^47]

The school's exclusivity and intense loyalty to Epicurus's teachings also drew criticism. Members were expected to memorize Epicurus's doctrines and avoid innovations, leading opponents to characterize Epicureans as dogmatic and cultish.[^48] The practice of celebrating Epicurus's birthday monthly and treating his words with near-religious reverence struck critics as inconsistent with the philosophy's rational pretensions.[^49] Modern scholars have noted that this conservatism, while preserving Epicurus's teachings faithfully, may have prevented the school from developing and adapting to new challenges, contributing to its eventual eclipse by Stoicism and later Christianity.[^50]

## Personal Life

Historical sources provide limited but intriguing details about Epicurus's personal life and character. He suffered from poor health, particularly in his later years, experiencing chronic kidney stones and other ailments that caused him considerable pain.[^51] His final letter to Idomeneus, written on the day of his death, poignantly describes his suffering while affirming that the memory of their philosophical conversations provided mental pleasure that counterbalanced his physical pain—a testament to his philosophical principles.[^52]

Epicurus never married and apparently had no children, instead dedicating himself entirely to philosophy and his school community.[^53] His closest relationships were with his philosophical companions, particularly Metrodorus of Lampsacus, whose early death deeply affected him, and Hermarchus of Mytilene, whom he designated as his successor.[^54] The intensity of these friendships, central to his philosophy, has led to scholarly debate about whether they were purely intellectual or included romantic dimensions, though ancient sources provide no clear evidence of sexual relationships.[^55]

Despite teaching that pleasure was the highest good, Epicurus lived simply and frugally. Ancient accounts describe him as content with bread, water, and occasional cheese, finding luxury unnecessary for happiness and even potentially harmful to tranquility.[^56] This ascetic lifestyle was philosophical rather than imposed by poverty; Epicurus deliberately cultivated simple tastes to make himself less vulnerable to fortune and more self-sufficient.[^57] He reportedly said, "Send me some preserved cheese, so that when I like I may have a feast," indicating that modest treats were sufficient for his pleasures.[^58]

Contemporary descriptions portray Epicurus as gentle, kind, and devoted to his students, inspiring strong loyalty and affection.[^59] His will, preserved by Diogenes Laertius, reveals his concern for his school's continuity and his household slaves, whom he manumitted and provided for financially.[^60] He bequeathed his house and garden to Hermarchus and his successors in perpetuity, ensuring the school's physical continuation, and established funds for annual commemorations and celebrations of his memory and that of Metrodorus.[^61]

Epicurus's personality combined philosophical seriousness with warmth and accessibility. Unlike the austere Stoics or the otherworldly Platonists, he emphasized that philosophy should be practical and therapeutic, addressing real human anxieties and suffering.[^62] His numerous letters to followers, fragments of which survive, show a teacher concerned with individual students' progress and wellbeing, offering customized advice and encouragement.[^63] This personal attention and genuine care for his community help explain the extraordinary devotion his followers showed him, both during his life and for centuries after his death.[^64]

## Legacy

Epicurus's influence on Western philosophy and culture has been profound, complex, and often paradoxical. During the Hellenistic and Roman periods, Epicureanism became one of the dominant philosophical schools, rivaling Stoicism in popularity and influence.[^65] Epicurean communities spread throughout the Mediterranean world, from Greece and Asia Minor to Italy, with archaeological evidence of Epicurean inscriptions found as far as modern-day Turkey and Afghanistan.[^66] The school maintained remarkable continuity and orthodoxy, preserving Epicurus's teachings faithfully for over seven hundred years.[^67]

The most significant ancient promulgation of Epicureanism came through the Roman poet Lucretius, whose De Rerum Natura (On the Nature of Things), written in the first century BCE, presented Epicurean physics and ethics in sublime Latin hexameter verse.[^68] This masterpiece ensured the survival of Epicurean thought through the Middle Ages, when direct knowledge of Epicurus's own works was lost, and its rediscovery in 1417 by the humanist Poggio Bracciolini helped spark the Renaissance and the Scientific Revolution.[^69]

However, with the rise of Christianity, Epicureanism faced systematic suppression. Early Church Fathers condemned it as atheistic and hedonistic, incompatible with Christian theology and morality.[^70] The school gradually disappeared during late antiquity, its gardens abandoned, its libraries destroyed or lost, leaving only fragments quoted by later authors and a few papyrus scrolls buried in the volcanic ash of Herculaneum.[^71] For over a millennium, "Epicurean" became a term of abuse, synonymous with godless materialism and sensual excess.[^72]

The Renaissance and Enlightenment witnessed a gradual rehabilitation of Epicurus's reputation. Humanist scholars recognized the distortions in the traditional portrayal, and philosophers like Pierre Gassendi attempted to reconcile Epicurean atomism with Christian theology.[^73] Enlightenment thinkers including Thomas Jefferson, who called himself an Epicurean, admired Epicurus's rational, naturalistic worldview and his emphasis on happiness as the goal of life.[^74] Jefferson copied out Epicurus's Principal Doctrines and recommended his philosophy as superior to competing ancient schools.[^75]

Modern philosophy has drawn extensively on Epicurean ideas, often without full acknowledgment. His atomism anticipated modern atomic theory, though without the scientific evidence later discoveries provided.[^76] His ethical hedonism influenced utilitarian philosophers like Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill, who developed Epicurus's distinction between higher and lower pleasures into a systematic moral philosophy.[^77] His naturalistic approach to religion and death presaged secular humanism and existentialist responses to mortality.[^78]

Contemporary Epicurean philosophy has experienced a revival, with scholars and popular writers rediscovering Epicurus as a proto-scientific thinker whose therapeutic philosophy offers resources for addressing modern anxieties.[^79] His emphasis on friendship, simplicity, and mental tranquility resonates with movements ranging from voluntary simplicity to positive psychology.[^80] The discovery and ongoing decipherment of the Herculaneum papyri, many containing works by Epicurus and his followers, continues to reveal new dimensions of his thought and promises further insights into ancient Epicureanism.[^81]

Epicurus's legacy thus encompasses contradictions: condemned as a hedonist while actually teaching moderation; dismissed as atheistic while acknowledging divine existence; attacked as anti-rational while pioneering empiricist epistemology; and marginalized for centuries while profoundly influencing the development of Western thought.[^82] His fundamental insight—that philosophy should serve human happiness by addressing fears and promoting tranquility—remains influential across contemporary philosophical, psychological, and cultural discussions about the good life.[^83]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and defenders of Epicurus have emphasized his profound humanism, scientific outlook, and therapeutic approach to philosophy. Classical philologist Diskin Clay argues that Epicurus created "the most humane of ancient philosophical schools," centered on friendship and genuine concern for human flourishing rather than abstract metaphysical speculation.[^84] His materialism and rejection of supernatural causation are seen as anticipating the scientific worldview, earning him praise as a proto-scientist who sought natural explanations for phenomena that others attributed to divine intervention.[^85]

Philosophers have praised Epicurus's ethical sophistication, noting that his hedonism was far more nuanced than critics acknowledged. Martha Nussbaum argues that Epicurean therapy offered practical tools for addressing anxiety, grief, and fear that remain psychologically insightful.[^86] His emphasis on mental over bodily pleasures, on lasting tranquility over momentary excitement, and on self-sufficiency over dependence on external goods demonstrates ethical depth that transcends simplistic pleasure-seeking.[^87]

Epicurus's social philosophy has attracted admiration for its egalitarianism and inclusivity. By welcoming women, slaves, and people of all social classes into his school, he embodied an egalitarian ideal rare in ancient Greece.[^88] His conception of justice as a social contract for mutual advantage anticipated modern contractarian theories by over two millennia.[^89] Contemporary philosopher Phillip Mitsis praises Epicurus's political philosophy as offering a coherent alternative to both utopian idealism and cynical realism.[^90]

Historians of science recognize Epicurus's atomism as a remarkable theoretical achievement that, despite lacking experimental confirmation, correctly identified fundamental features of material reality.[^91] His addition of the swerve to Democritean atomism, while empirically unverifiable, showed sophisticated thinking about determinism, causation, and free will.[^92] The empiricist epistemology he developed influenced later philosophers from the ancient Skeptics through early modern empiricists like Locke and Hume.[^93]

Literary scholars celebrate Epicurus's influence on Western literature through Lucretius, Horace, and countless later writers who drew on Epicurean themes.[^94] His philosophical poetry, though lost, apparently combined literary artistry with philosophical argument, creating a model that influenced didactic poetry for centuries.[^95] Modern admirers include writers like Michel de Montaigne, who praised Epicurus's wisdom, and contemporary philosophers like Michel Onfray, who advocates neo-Epicureanism as a life philosophy for the modern world.[^96]

### Negative Views

Critics of Epicurus, both ancient and modern, have raised substantial objections to his philosophy and its implications. The most persistent criticism concerns his hedonism, which many philosophers argue reduces human beings to mere pleasure-seeking animals, ignoring higher goods like virtue, knowledge for its own sake, and self-sacrifice for noble causes.[^97] Stoic critics from Epictetus to modern virtue ethicists contend that making pleasure the criterion of value undermines genuine morality and human dignity.[^98]

Philosophers have attacked the logical coherence of key Epicurean doctrines. The atomic swerve has been criticized as an ad hoc hypothesis introduced to solve the free will problem without empirical evidence or theoretical justification.[^99] Karl Marx, despite writing his doctoral dissertation on Epicurus, noted this weakness in Epicurean physics.[^100] Critics argue that if the swerve is random, it cannot ground free will, but if it responds to mental causation, atomism is violated.[^101]

Epicurus's epistemology has faced substantial critique. Rationalist philosophers argue that relying exclusively on sensation cannot justify mathematical, logical, or metaphysical knowledge, and that Epicurus provided no adequate account of abstraction, reasoning, or scientific theory formation.[^102] His claim that all sensations are true seems to conflate sensory states with the propositions we form about them, leading to confusion about the nature of truth and error.[^103]

The ethical implications of Epicureanism have drawn fire from various quarters. The recommendation to "live unknown" and avoid political involvement has been condemned as socially irresponsible, particularly by Roman moralists who valued public service.[^104] Modern critics argue that Epicurean withdrawal cannot address systemic injustice and that a philosophy focused on personal tranquility may be selfish or complicit with oppression.[^105] Feminist scholars have questioned whether the supposed egalitarianism of The Garden translated into genuine equality or merely tokenism.[^106]

Religious critics, from ancient Christian polemicists to modern theologians, condemn Epicurean materialism as eliminating the transcendent dimension of human existence and reducing the soul to perishable atoms.[^107] The denial of afterlife and divine providence strikes many as nihilistic, removing ultimate meaning and moral accountability.[^108] Pope Benedict XVI, among others, has argued that Epicurean philosophy, despite its appeal, ultimately cannot satisfy the deepest human longings for eternal significance.[^109]

Some scholars criticize the Epicurean school's conservatism and dogmatism, noting that unlike Plato's Academy or Aristotle's Lyceum, which evolved over centuries, Epicurean communities rigidly adhered to the founder's teachings, stifling innovation and adaptation.[^110] This doctrinal rigidity may explain why Epicureanism eventually died out while other schools evolved into new forms.[^111] The near-religious veneration of Epicurus himself, including birthday celebrations and memorization of his sayings, strikes critics as cultish and inconsistent with the philosophy's rational pretensions.[^112]

## Famous Quotes

> "Don't fear god, don't worry about death; what is good is easy to get, and what is terrible is easy to endure."

The Tetrapharmakos (fourfold remedy), a summary of Epicurean philosophy offering liberation from the four main sources of human anxiety.[^113]

> "Death is nothing to us. When we exist, death is not yet present, and when death is present, then we do not exist. Therefore, it does not concern either the living or the dead, since for the former it is not, and the latter are no more."

From his Letter to Menoeceus, presenting his famous argument for why death should not be feared.[^114]

> "It is impossible to live a pleasant life without living wisely and well and justly, and it is impossible to live wisely and well and justly without living a pleasant life."

From Principal Doctrines, expressing the unity of virtue and happiness in Epicurean ethics.[^115]

> "Of all the means to ensure happiness throughout the whole life, by far the most important is the acquisition of friends."

From Principal Doctrines, emphasizing friendship as central to the Epicurean good life.[^116]

> "The magnitude of pleasure reaches its limit in the removal of all pain. When such pleasure is present, so long as it is uninterrupted, there is no pain either of body or of mind or of both together."

From Principal Doctrines, defining katastematic pleasure as the complete absence of pain.[^117]

> "It is not the young man who should be considered fortunate but the old man who has lived well, because the young man in his prime wanders much by chance, vacillating in his beliefs, while the old man has docked in the harbor, having safeguarded his true happiness."

From Letter to Menoeceus, on the value of philosophy throughout life.[^118]
