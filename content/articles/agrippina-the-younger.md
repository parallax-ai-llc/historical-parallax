## Summary

Roman empress who allegedly poisoned her husband Emperor Claudius with mushrooms to make her son Nero emperor. She was later murdered on Nero's orders.

## Background

- Agrippina the Younger (Julia Agrippina) was a prominent member of the Julio-Claudian dynasty.
- She was the sister of Emperor Caligula, the niece and later wife of Emperor Claudius, and the mother of Emperor Nero.
- Roman elite politics of the 1st century CE often involved adoption, strategic marriages, patronage networks, and court intrigue.

## Political Influence and Court Role

- Ancient sources describe Agrippina as unusually influential for a Roman woman of her era, especially in matters of succession.
- She cultivated alliances within the imperial household and among the Senate and Praetorian Guard.
- Her position depended heavily on the stability of her relationship with Claudius and the standing of her son Nero.

## Succession and Allegations

- Several ancient historians report allegations that Claudius was poisoned in 54 CE, with suspicion falling on Agrippina.
- The precise circumstances remain disputed in modern scholarship because primary accounts are partial and politically colored.
- Nero’s rise to power followed Claudius’s death, and Agrippina initially retained visibility and status at court.

## Downfall and Death

- Sources report a widening rift between Nero and Agrippina as Nero consolidated authority.
- Agrippina’s death in 59 CE is commonly described as ordered by Nero; accounts differ on the method and details.
- Her story is frequently used as an example of the volatility of imperial succession politics in early Rome.

## Notes on Sources

- Much of what is “known” about Agrippina comes from later Roman historians writing with their own agendas.
- Interpretations vary, but the outline of her familial ties and political prominence is broadly accepted.
