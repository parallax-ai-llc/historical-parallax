## Summary

Maurizio Pollini (January 5, 1942 – March 23, 2024) was an Italian pianist widely regarded as one of the greatest classical pianists of the twentieth century. His command of the instrument was exceptional across an unusually broad repertoire, ranging from Bach and Beethoven through Chopin and Schumann to Schoenberg, Boulez, and Nono. His playing was distinguished by extraordinary technical precision, intellectual rigor, and interpretive depth.

Pollini occupied a singular position in classical music as both a supreme technician and a committed artistic thinker. He approached the piano not as a vehicle for personal display but as a medium for illuminating the internal logic of musical works. His recordings for Deutsche Grammophon over five decades became benchmarks in the field, and his concert appearances at major halls worldwide were treated as events of cultural significance.

His influence extended beyond performance. By championing twentieth-century composers alongside the canonical repertoire, Pollini helped normalize contemporary music for mainstream concert audiences and demonstrated that technical mastery and intellectual seriousness were not in opposition.

## Early Life & Background

Pollini was born in Milan on January 5, 1942, into a cultured family. His father, Gino Pollini, was a prominent rationalist architect; his mother, Renata Melotti, was the sister of sculptor Fausto Melotti. He began piano studies at the Milan Conservatory, where he showed prodigious ability from an early age. He also studied with Carlo Lonati and later with the Polish pianist Arturo Benedetti Michelangeli, whose influence on his approach to tone and structure proved lasting.

## Rise to Prominence

At the age of eighteen, Pollini entered the 1960 International Chopin Piano Competition in Warsaw and won first prize unanimously, with jury member Arthur Rubinstein reportedly remarking that Pollini already played better than any of the jurors. Despite this triumph, Pollini largely withdrew from public performance for several years, continuing his studies rather than capitalizing immediately on the prize. He returned to the concert stage in the late 1960s with a deliberate, carefully chosen program, signing with Deutsche Grammophon and beginning a recording career that would span decades.

## Key Achievements

Pollini's discography for Deutsche Grammophon is among the most acclaimed in recorded piano history. His recordings of Chopin's études (1972), Beethoven's late sonatas, and the complete Beethoven piano sonatas cycle are considered definitive references. He recorded the complete Schoenberg piano works and the piano music of Webern, Boulez, and Luigi Nono, bringing rigorous attention to twentieth-century repertoire that most pianists of his stature avoided. He performed regularly at the Salzburg Festival, La Scala, Carnegie Hall, and the Berlin Philharmonie, and received the Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award in 2014.

## Controversies & Criticism

Some critics and audiences found Pollini's playing at times emotionally cool or overly controlled, arguing that technical perfection occasionally came at the expense of spontaneity and warmth. His tempos in certain Romantic works were considered metronomic by detractors. In later decades, as age affected his physical facility, some performances drew mixed reviews, though admirers noted that his interpretive intelligence remained undiminished.

## Legacy & Influence

Pollini died in Milan on March 23, 2024, at the age of eighty-two. His legacy rests on his demonstration that the highest technical standards and deep musical thought are inseparable. He influenced generations of pianists in how they approach both the Romantic canon and modern repertoire, and his recordings remain essential listening in concert halls, conservatories, and private collections worldwide.
