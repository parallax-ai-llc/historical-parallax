## Summary

Sponsorship system in Saudi Arabia that ties migrant workers to their employers, enabling abuse, wage theft, and trafficking. The system affects over 10 million workers.

## What the System Is

“Kafala” refers to a sponsorship framework used in parts of the Gulf in which a migrant worker’s legal residency and work authorization are linked to a sponsor (typically an employer). In Saudi Arabia, this linkage historically gave employers significant control over workers’ mobility and legal status.

## Commonly Reported Risks

Human-rights and labor organizations have frequently associated kafala-style arrangements with:

- Restrictions on changing employers without permission.
- Barriers to leaving the country or obtaining exit documentation.
- Contract substitution, non-payment or delayed payment of wages, and excessive recruitment debt.
- Elevated vulnerability for domestic workers due to isolation and limited labor protections.

## Reform Efforts

Saudi authorities have periodically announced reforms intended to increase labor mobility and reduce employer control. Coverage of these reforms often distinguishes between formal policy changes and on-the-ground enforcement, where implementation can vary by sector, worker category, and employer.

## Why It Matters

Because Saudi Arabia hosts a very large migrant workforce across construction, services, and domestic labor, small changes in sponsorship rules can affect millions of people’s day-to-day autonomy, income security, and ability to seek redress for abuse.

International scrutiny has also focused on how recruitment and sponsorship practices interact across sending and receiving countries.
