## Summary

Silicon Valley fraud where Elizabeth Holmes claimed to have revolutionized blood testing at Theranos. The technology never worked, defrauding investors of hundreds of millions.

## What was claimed vs. what was delivered

Theranos promoted a vision of running many laboratory tests from a tiny blood sample collected via fingerstick. In practice, reporting and later testimony indicated that the company relied heavily on conventional analyzers for many tests and that its proprietary devices failed to perform as advertised.

## Key actors

- **Elizabeth Holmes:** Founder and CEO; became the public face of the company’s claims.
- **Sunny Balwani:** Former president/COO; charged and later convicted in a related case.
- **Investigative journalism and whistleblowers:** Reporting, most prominently by *The Wall Street Journal*, helped trigger regulatory scrutiny and investor reassessment.

## Regulatory and legal outcomes

The scandal involved oversight by U.S. regulators responsible for clinical laboratories and medical devices, as well as civil and criminal proceedings.

Holmes was convicted on federal fraud charges related to misleading investors; subsequent sentencing and restitution orders reflected the scale of investor losses and the court’s findings.

## Broader impact

The Theranos case became a widely cited example of:

- the risks of “fake it till you make it” culture when applied to health technology,
- governance failures in high-growth startups,
- the value of independent validation, quality systems, and transparent clinical evidence.
