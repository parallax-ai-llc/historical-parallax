## Summary

Kurdistan Workers' Party founded by Abdullah Öcalan that has waged an armed insurgency against Turkey since 1984, killing over 40,000 people. Designated as a terrorist organization by Turkey, the US, and the EU.

## Origins and Ideology (high-level)

The PKK (Kurdistan Workers’ Party) was founded in the late 1970s and began an armed campaign in 1984. It originally articulated a Marxist-influenced program and Kurdish nationalist aims. Over time, its stated ideology evolved, with later emphasis in its discourse on concepts such as autonomy and political rights, though the organization has remained associated with armed activity.

## Conflict Dynamics

The conflict between the PKK and the Turkish state has varied across decades, including periods of intensified violence and periods of reduced hostilities. Key themes commonly discussed in analyses include:

- **Insurgency and counterinsurgency** tactics, including attacks by the PKK and large-scale security operations by the state.
- **Civilian impact**, including displacement, human rights allegations, and long-term social and economic costs in affected regions.
- **Cross-border dimensions**, with activity and safe havens at times linked to terrain and political conditions in neighboring countries.

## Political Processes and Ceasefires

There have been multiple attempts at ceasefire and political negotiation. The 2013–2015 period is often described as a significant peace initiative that later collapsed, followed by renewed violence.

## Terrorist Designations

Turkey, the United States, and the European Union (among others) designate the PKK as a terrorist organization. These designations shape legal treatment, financial restrictions, and international cooperation related to counterterrorism.

## Related Organizations and Regional Complexity

Reporting on Kurdish armed and political movements in the region often notes overlapping personnel histories, shifting alliances, and distinct organizations operating in Turkey, Syria, Iraq, and Iran. Attribution and organizational boundaries can be contested in public debate, and details may vary by source.
