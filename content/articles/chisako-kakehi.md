## Summary

Japanese 'Black Widow' serial killer convicted of poisoning three partners and one boyfriend with cyanide to collect insurance money. Amassed over $8 million from her victims.

## Pattern of offending (as described in court coverage)

- Japanese media nicknamed Kakehi a “black widow” because the deaths were tied to men she dated or married, often older and financially secure.
- Investigators and prosecutors alleged she used poisoning—frequently reported as cyanide compounds—to cause sudden illness and death.
- The cases drew attention to the use of matchmaking services and social introductions among older adults, and to how financial arrangements (inheritance and insurance) can create incentives for exploitation.

## Investigation and prosecution

- The case expanded as authorities linked multiple suspicious deaths and attempted poisonings across several years.
- Forensic toxicology, financial records, and relationship timelines were key elements reported in summaries of the prosecution’s case.
- Kakehi’s trial became high-profile nationally because of the alleged number of victims, the method (poisoning), and the financial motive.

## Trial outcome and sentencing

- In 2017, a Japanese court convicted her in connection with multiple deaths and an attempted murder (as commonly summarized in secondary sources).
- She received a death sentence, reflecting the severity of the crimes and Japan’s sentencing practices in multi-victim homicide cases.
- Post-conviction proceedings in such cases commonly involve appeals and extended time on death row; public summaries typically focus on the initial verdict and sentence.

## Context: poisoning cases and financial crime

- Poisoning homicide investigations often rely on circumstantial timelines and laboratory detection because symptoms can resemble natural medical emergencies.
- Financial exploitation frequently leaves a paper trail (bank transfers, beneficiary changes, wills, and insurance documentation), which can become central evidence.
