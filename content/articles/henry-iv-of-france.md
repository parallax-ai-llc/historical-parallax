## Summary

Henry IV of France (1553-1610), known as "Good King Henry" or "Henry the Great," was the first monarch of the House of Bourbon and one of France's most beloved rulers. Born as Henry of Navarre into a Protestant family during the tumultuous Wars of Religion, he navigated decades of religious conflict before ascending to the French throne in 1589. His pragmatic conversion to Catholicism and the groundbreaking Edict of Nantes in 1598 ended forty years of civil war and established unprecedented religious tolerance in Europe. Though his reign was cut short by assassination in 1610, Henry IV's legacy as a unifying monarch who prioritized his subjects' welfare has endured for over four centuries.[^1][^2]

---

## Early Life and Background

Henry IV was born on December 13, 1553, in Pau, Béarn, in the Kingdom of Navarre, a small realm straddling the Pyrenees between France and Spain.[^3] His parents were Antoine de Bourbon, Duke of Vendôme, and Jeanne d'Albret, who would become Queen of Navarre in 1555.[^4] Through his father, Henry descended from the Capetian kings of France, though his claim to the French throne seemed impossibly distant at his birth.[^5] His grandfather, Henri d'Albret, King of Navarre, supervised the boy's early childhood and instilled in him a love of the outdoors and connection to common people.[^6]

The young Henry spent part of his early childhood at the Château de Coarraze in the Béarn countryside, where he developed his legendary rapport with peasants during hunting expeditions, earning the nickname "miller of Barbaste."[^7] This upbringing would profoundly shape his later reputation as a king who genuinely cared for his subjects' welfare, famously expressing the wish that every peasant could afford "a chicken in the pot on Sundays."[^8]

Henry's religious upbringing became a point of contention between his parents. Although baptized as a Roman Catholic to maintain political connections, his mother Jeanne d'Albret, a devoted Calvinist, raised him strictly in the Protestant faith after his grandfather's death in 1555.[^9] His mother's fierce commitment to Calvinism meant Henry was educated according to the austere precepts of the Reformation, receiving instruction from tutors Pierre Victor Palma-Cayet and François de La Gaucherie.[^10] This Protestant education combined typical Renaissance learning—classical languages, literature, and philosophy—with rigorous training in horsemanship and military skills.[^11]

From 1561 to 1567, Henry lived at the French royal court with his second cousins, including his future wife Margaret of Valois, daughter of King Henry II and Catherine de' Medici.[^12] During the First French War of Religion (1562-1563), the young prince was moved to Montargis for safety and placed under the protection of Renée of France.[^13] In 1567, his mother brought him back to Béarn, where she entrusted his military education to Gaspard de Coligny, the leader of the Protestant Huguenot forces.[^14] Henry saw his first combat at the Battle of Arnay-le-Duc in 1570, beginning a military career that would define much of his life.[^15]

On June 9, 1572, following his mother Queen Jeanne's death, the nineteen-year-old Henry became King Henry III of Navarre.[^16] Just two months later, on August 18, 1572, he married Margaret of Valois in a politically arranged union intended to reconcile Catholics and Protestants.[^17] This marriage, celebrated on the parvis of Notre Dame Cathedral in Paris, would become the backdrop for one of the darkest chapters in French history.

## Rise to Power

Henry's marriage to Margaret of Valois was orchestrated by Catherine de' Medici as a gesture of peace between the warring Catholic and Protestant factions.[^18] However, the wedding festivities became the occasion for the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre, which began on August 24, 1572.[^19] Between 5,000 and 25,000 Protestants were murdered across France in a wave of violence that one historian noted was "not a day but a season," with death estimates ranging from 7,000 to 21,000.[^20][^21] Henry's mentor Gaspard de Coligny was among the victims brutally killed in Paris.[^22]

Henry of Navarre himself narrowly escaped death during the massacre.[^23] He and the Prince of Condé were brought before King Charles IX, who demanded they renounce their Protestant faith.[^24] Under threat of death, Henry was forced to convert to Catholicism, though this conversion was clearly made under duress and not of his own free will.[^25] He was held essentially as a prisoner at the French court for nearly four years before escaping in 1576 and immediately returning to the Protestant faith.[^26]

The unexpected path to the French throne opened when Henry III's youngest brother, Francis, Duke of Anjou, died in 1584, making Henry of Navarre the heir presumptive under Salic law.[^27] However, the Catholic League, supported by Spain and led by the powerful Guise family, adamantly opposed a Protestant king of France.[^28] This opposition triggered the War of the Three Henrys (1587-1589), involving King Henry III, Henry of Navarre, and Henry, Duke of Guise.[^29]

Henry proved himself a capable military commander during these conflicts, winning significant victories despite often being outnumbered.[^30] His most notable military success came at the Battle of Coutras in 1587, where his forces defeated a Catholic League army.[^31] When Henry III was assassinated by a Catholic fanatic named Jacques Clément on August 1, 1589, the dying king recognized Henry of Navarre as his legitimate successor.[^32] However, Catholic France refused to accept a Protestant monarch, and Henry IV had to fight for his throne for another four years.[^33]

The turning point came in 1593 when Henry made his most famous and pragmatic decision: he converted to Catholicism for the second time, reportedly saying "Paris is worth a mass" (Paris vaut bien une messe), though this quote was likely apocryphal and attributed to him years later.[^34][^35] This conversion was viewed cynically by both sides—Protestants saw it as betrayal, while Catholics doubted its sincerity.[^36] Nevertheless, the conversion allowed Henry to enter Paris on March 22, 1594, and be crowned King of France at Chartres Cathedral on February 27, 1594.[^37] He had finally secured the throne, bringing the Valois dynasty to an end and establishing the House of Bourbon, which would rule France until the French Revolution.[^38]

## Major Achievements

Henry IV's reign is remembered for restoring peace, prosperity, and stability to a France devastated by decades of religious warfare. His achievements in both domestic and foreign policy laid the foundation for France's emergence as a major European power in the seventeenth century.

### The Edict of Nantes

Henry's crowning achievement was the Edict of Nantes, promulgated on April 13, 1598, in Brittany.[^39] This landmark decree granted unprecedented religious liberty to French Protestants (Huguenots), making it one of the first successful attempts in modern European history to allow two religions to coexist within a single kingdom.[^40] The Edict established Catholicism as the official state religion but granted Protestants substantial rights: freedom of conscience throughout France, the right to public worship in many areas (though not in Paris), full civil rights including access to education and public office, and the establishment of special courts composed of both Protestant and Catholic judges to ensure fair treatment.[^41][^42]

The Edict also allowed Huguenots to maintain garrisoned towns as places of security, effectively creating a state within a state.[^43] While controversial among Catholic zealots and viewed with suspicion by the Pope, the Edict successfully ended the Wars of Religion that had killed between two and four million people and torn France apart for nearly forty years.[^44][^45] It would remain in force until Louis XIV revoked it in 1685, triggering the emigration of over 400,000 Protestants and causing immense economic damage to France.[^46]

### Economic Recovery and Reforms

Henry collaborated closely with his minister Maximilien de Béthune, Duke of Sully, to restore France's shattered economy.[^47] The king lowered taxes on common people, particularly peasants who had borne the brunt of wartime exactions.[^48] He encouraged agriculture, promoted the silk industry, and improved infrastructure by building roads, bridges, and canals.[^49] Henry's government also reformed the tax collection system to reduce corruption and ensure more revenue actually reached the royal treasury.[^50]

The king's genuine concern for his subjects' welfare became legendary. His oft-quoted wish that every peasant could afford "a chicken in the pot on Sundays" exemplified his vision of broadly shared prosperity.[^51] Under his and Sully's management, France's finances stabilized remarkably quickly, allowing the kingdom to pay down war debts and even accumulate a treasury surplus.[^52]

### Urban Development and Architecture

Henry IV transformed Paris from a medieval city into a Renaissance capital worthy of a great European power.[^53] He commissioned the completion of the Pont Neuf (New Bridge), which had been begun under Henry III but remained unfinished.[^54] He created the Place Royale (now Place des Vosges), one of the first planned urban squares in Europe, and the Place Dauphine.[^55] The king also extended the Louvre palace and connected it to the Tuileries, beginning the transformation of this royal residence into the magnificent palace it would become under his successors.[^56]

These urban projects were not merely aesthetic; they served practical purposes of improving traffic flow, creating public spaces, and demonstrating royal power and magnificence.[^57] Henry's building program provided employment during the economic recovery and established Paris as a center of art and architecture.[^58]

### Foreign Policy

In foreign affairs, Henry successfully navigated the complex landscape of early modern European power politics.[^59] He made peace with Spain through the Treaty of Vervins in 1598, ending decades of Franco-Spanish conflict.[^60] He established diplomatic and commercial relations with the Ottoman Empire and opened trade routes to East Asia, expanding French commercial interests.[^61] Henry also intervened cautiously in the affairs of the Holy Roman Empire and Italian states, gradually increasing French influence without triggering major new wars.[^62]

His alliance building and diplomatic maneuvering positioned France as a counterweight to Habsburg power in both Spain and the Holy Roman Empire, a policy that his successors would pursue with even greater vigor.[^63] By the end of his reign, France had recovered its status as a major European power, setting the stage for the dominance it would achieve under Louis XIV.[^64]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his historical reputation as "Good King Henry," Henry IV's reign was marked by significant controversies, moral failings, and political decisions that attracted criticism both during his lifetime and from later historians.

### Religious Opportunism and Insincerity

Henry's multiple conversions between Catholicism and Protestantism raised serious questions about his religious sincerity.[^65] Baptized Catholic, raised Protestant, forced to convert to Catholicism during the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre, returning to Protestantism in 1576, and converting again to Catholicism in 1593, Henry's religious identity seemed entirely determined by political expediency.[^66] His Catholic opponents believed he had "recanted his Protestantism merely for political reasons to secure the French throne," while many Protestants viewed his final conversion as betrayal.[^67][^68]

Modern historians largely agree that Henry was a convinced Calvinist who changed his formal religious confession only to achieve his political goals.[^69] While this pragmatism ultimately brought peace to France, it also established a precedent of cynical manipulation of religious identity for political gain. The famous quote attributed to him—"Paris is worth a mass"—whether he actually said it or not, perfectly encapsulates this opportunistic approach to faith.[^70]

### Military Leadership Questioned

While Henry achieved notable battlefield victories, his strategic abilities have been questioned by military historians.[^71] Several scholars have characterized him as a "poor strategist" who was better at winning individual battles than conducting comprehensive military campaigns.[^72] Historians have debated why someone so successful in combat "has been so uniformly condemned" in military assessments, suggesting that later historians wrongly conflated "Henry as a military commander with Henry as the Vert Galant" (the Green Gallant, referring to his amorous adventures).[^73]

Some military historians argue that assessments of Henry's strategic deficiencies are based on anachronistic post-Westphalian assumptions about decisive battles and military objectives that did not apply to sixteenth-century warfare.[^74] Nevertheless, the criticism that Henry sometimes prioritized personal glory in battle over sound strategic planning has persisted in the historiography.[^75]

### Notorious Womanizing

Henry's sexual behavior was scandalous even by the permissive standards of early modern European royalty.[^76] His legendary womanizing earned him the nickname "Le Vert Galant" (The Green Gallant, implying a vigorous, lustful nature), and his sexual appetite was said to have been insatiable.[^77] He maintained multiple mistresses simultaneously throughout both his marriages, producing numerous illegitimate children.[^78]

His treatment of his wives was particularly problematic. Both Henry and his first wife, Margaret of Valois, were "repeatedly unfaithful to each other," leading to the collapse of their marriage and their living apart before their eventual annulment in 1599.[^79] With his second wife, Marie de' Medici, whom he married in 1600 primarily for her substantial dowry, Henry continued his infidelities and even "insisted that she raise his illegitimate children along with her own."[^80][^81] This humiliating treatment created significant tension at court and contributed to Marie's eventual role in political intrigues during the regency after Henry's death.[^82]

### Incomplete Reconciliation

While the Edict of Nantes was groundbreaking, it did not achieve complete religious harmony.[^83] Catholics resented the privileges granted to Huguenots, particularly their fortified towns and independent military forces.[^84] Protestants, meanwhile, remained suspicious of a convert king and felt the restrictions on their worship (especially exclusion from Paris and other major cities) were unjust.[^85] The Edict represented not genuine tolerance but rather a pragmatic truce that left fundamental tensions unresolved.[^86]

Henry's assassination by a Catholic fanatic in 1610 demonstrated that religious hatreds had not been extinguished.[^87] The king's military plans to intervene in Germany on the Protestant side in the War of the Jülich Succession had inflamed Catholic opposition and contributed to his assassin's motivations.[^88]

## Personal Life

Henry IV's personal life was as colorful and complex as his political career, marked by two marriages, numerous affairs, a vibrant personality, and health challenges that would eventually contribute to the circumstances of his death.

### Marriages and Family

Henry's first marriage to Margaret of Valois in 1572 was a political disaster disguised as a diplomatic triumph.[^89] The union, intended to bridge the Catholic-Protestant divide, produced no children and quickly deteriorated into mutual infidelity and estrangement.[^90] Both parties conducted open affairs—Margaret with various courtiers, and Henry with a succession of mistresses.[^91] After years of living apart, the marriage was annulled by Pope Clement VIII in December 1599 on the grounds that it had been coerced, which was technically true given the circumstances of the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre.[^92] Remarkably, after the annulment, Margaret remained on good terms with Henry and was particularly fond of his second wife, Marie de' Medici.[^93]

Henry married Marie de' Medici on December 17, 1600, attracted primarily by her family's enormous wealth and the substantial dowry she brought to the French crown.[^94] Marie bore Henry six children, including the future Louis XIII (born 1601), Elisabeth (1602), Christine Marie (1606), Nicolas Henri (1607, died young), Gaston (1608), and Henrietta Maria (1609), who would become Queen of England.[^95] Despite Marie's fertility and her important role as mother to his legitimate heirs, Henry continued his numerous extramarital affairs, causing considerable humiliation to the queen.[^96]

### Mistresses and Illegitimate Children

Henry's most famous mistresses included Gabrielle d'Estrées, whom he deeply loved and might have married had she not died suddenly in 1599; Henriette d'Entragues, who bore him two children; and Charlotte Marguerite de Montmorency, his final passion, which indirectly contributed to his death.[^97] His infatuation with Charlotte, a young beauty married to the Prince of Condé, became so intense that the prince fled with his wife to Brussels to escape the king's attentions, creating a diplomatic crisis.[^98]

Henry fathered at least eleven illegitimate children by various mistresses, whom he openly acknowledged and provided for.[^99] He insisted that Marie de' Medici help raise these children alongside their legitimate half-siblings, an arrangement that created considerable tension in the royal household.[^100]

### Personality and Character

Contemporary accounts describe Henry as charismatic, witty, and possessing an earthy sense of humor that endeared him to common soldiers and peasants alike.[^101] Unlike the aloof majesty cultivated by many monarchs, Henry was known for his accessibility and genuine interest in his subjects' welfare.[^102] His famous statement about wanting every peasant to afford a chicken for Sunday dinner reflected a real concern for popular prosperity, not merely royal propaganda.[^103]

However, Henry also displayed traits less flattering: impulsiveness in his romantic pursuits, a tendency toward crude humor, and what some courtiers considered an undignified informality.[^104] His religious conversions, while politically astute, suggested a man who prioritized practical results over ideological consistency.[^105] Yet even critics acknowledged his bravery in battle, his relatively merciful treatment of defeated enemies, and his sincere desire to heal France's wounds.[^106]

### Health and Habits

Henry maintained physical vigor through much of his life, regularly hunting and participating in military campaigns well into his fifties.[^107] However, contemporaries noted that he suffered from gout in his later years, a condition exacerbated by his lifestyle.[^108] On the day of his assassination, May 14, 1610, the fifty-six-year-old king was riding in his carriage through Paris when it became stuck in traffic on the Rue de la Ferronnerie, creating the opportunity for François Ravaillac to strike.[^109]

## Legacy

Henry IV's assassination on May 14, 1610, transformed him from a controversial figure into a near-mythical embodiment of French national unity and good kingship. His legacy has profoundly influenced French identity, political culture, and historical memory for over four centuries.

### Immediate Aftermath and Successor

Henry's sudden death plunged France into shock and mourning.[^110] The nine-year-old Louis XIII succeeded his father under the regency of Marie de' Medici, who reversed many of Henry's policies and allied France with Spain, the traditional enemy her late husband had worked to contain.[^111] The political stability and administrative reforms Henry had established were tested during the turbulent regency years and the troubled early reign of Louis XIII.[^112]

Nevertheless, the institutional foundations Henry had built proved resilient. The Edict of Nantes remained in force for another seventy-five years, and the Bourbon dynasty he established would rule France (with brief interruptions) until 1830.[^113] His minister Sully's financial reforms had created a treasury surplus that, although quickly dissipated, demonstrated the potential for sound fiscal management.[^114]

### The Myth of Good King Henry

Over time, historical memory transformed Henry IV into "le bon roi Henri" (Good King Henry), one of France's most beloved monarchs.[^115] This mythologization emphasized his common touch, his supposed statement about chickens in pots, his role in ending the Wars of Religion, and his assassination by a religious fanatic.[^116] The St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre came to be remembered as demonstrating "the bloodthirsty cruelty of the Valois" in contrast to Henry's tolerance.[^117]

French popular culture embraced Henry as a folk hero who genuinely cared for his people, in contrast to the distant splendor of Louis XIV or the incompetence of later Bourbon kings.[^118] This romanticized image, while based on real achievements, simplified and sanitized the complex, flawed historical figure.[^119] His religious opportunism became pragmatic wisdom; his numerous affairs became proof of vitality; his sometimes questionable strategic decisions became bold leadership.[^120]

### Architectural and Urban Legacy

Henry's urban development projects transformed Paris into a European capital worthy of a great power.[^121] The Pont Neuf, Place des Vosges, and Place Dauphine remain iconic Parisian landmarks four centuries later.[^122] His expansion of the Louvre established the trajectory for its development into one of the world's great palaces and, eventually, museums.[^123] Later French monarchs, particularly Louis XIV, built upon Henry's vision of using architecture and urban planning to express royal power and national grandeur.[^124]

### Religious and Political Legacy

The Edict of Nantes established a precedent for religious coexistence that, despite its eventual revocation, influenced Enlightenment thinking about tolerance and religious liberty.[^125] Philosophers like Voltaire pointed to Henry's edict as proof that religious pluralism could work, using it as ammunition against religious persecution.[^126] The concept that state interests could supersede religious uniformity, embodied in Henry's pragmatic conversion and subsequent tolerance of Huguenots, contributed to the gradual secularization of European politics.[^127]

The Bourbon dynasty Henry founded ruled France for over two centuries, producing Louis XIII, Louis XIV (the Sun King), Louis XV, and Louis XVI.[^128] Through his daughter Henrietta Maria, who married King Charles I of England, Henry became an ancestor of the British royal family and numerous other European royal houses.[^129] His descendants have occupied thrones across Europe, making him a pivotal figure in European dynastic history.[^130]

### Historical Reassessment

Modern historians have moved beyond the "Good King Henry" mythology to appreciate both Henry's genuine achievements and his significant flaws.[^131] Scholars recognize that his success stemmed from pragmatism rather than idealism, political calculation rather than religious conviction, and fortunate timing as much as personal ability.[^132] Nevertheless, even skeptical historians acknowledge that Henry's reign marked a crucial turning point in French history, ending decades of civil war and establishing the foundations for France's seventeenth-century power.[^133]

Henry IV's assassination ensured he would be remembered at the height of his success, before the challenges of implementing his planned intervention in Germany or managing the inevitable conflicts between Catholics and Protestants could tarnish his reputation.[^134] His early death transformed him into what historian Roland Mousnier called "a king of legend more than history," whose mythical image has proven almost as influential as his actual achievements.[^135]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Historians sympathetic to Henry IV emphasize his remarkable achievement in bringing peace and stability to a kingdom torn apart by four decades of religious civil war. Scholar Desmond Seward argues that "much of the success of the reign was directly the result of Henry's personality and political and military ability," noting that "the rapidity with which he restored order surprised his contemporaries."[^136] This view highlights Henry's personal leadership as the crucial factor that allowed France to recover from the Wars of Religion far more quickly than anyone had anticipated.[^137]

Supporters particularly praise the Edict of Nantes as a watershed moment in European history. Historian Mack P. Holt describes it as "Henry IV's crowning achievement" and "the first successful attempt in modern European history to reconcile the presence of two religions within a single kingdom."[^138] This assessment positions Henry as a pioneer of religious tolerance and political pragmatism at a time when religious uniformity was considered essential to political stability throughout Europe.[^139]

Henry's economic policies have also received considerable scholarly approval. Historians credit him and his minister Sully with implementing fiscal reforms that stabilized royal finances, reduced the tax burden on peasants, and promoted economic recovery through infrastructure development and support for agriculture and commerce.[^140] His genuine concern for popular welfare, whether motivated by compassion or political calculation, distinguished him from monarchs who viewed their subjects merely as resources to be exploited.[^141]

Military historians who defend Henry's reputation argue that criticisms of his strategic abilities are based on anachronistic assumptions derived from later models of warfare between nation-states.[^142] They contend that Henry's military leadership should be evaluated by sixteenth-century standards, by which his battlefield victories and successful campaigns to secure his throne demonstrate considerable tactical skill and strategic flexibility.[^143]

Admirers also point to Henry's role as a unifying figure who transcended religious divisions. Despite his Protestant origins and multiple conversions, he earned the loyalty of both Catholics and Protestants through his even-handed governance and commitment to peace.[^144] His famous (if possibly apocryphal) statement that "Paris is worth a mass" is interpreted by sympathetic scholars as evidence of his pragmatic wisdom in prioritizing national unity over personal religious conviction.[^145]

### Negative Views

Critical historians challenge the romanticized image of "Good King Henry," pointing to serious flaws in both his character and his policies. The central criticism concerns his religious insincerity. Scholars emphasize that Henry "was a convinced Calvinist, and only changed his formal religious confession to achieve his political goals," suggesting that his conversions represented pure opportunism rather than genuine conviction.[^146] Catholic contemporaries certainly viewed him this way, believing "it seemed clear to Henry's Catholic opponents that he had recanted his Protestantism merely for political reasons to secure the French throne."[^147]

Critics also question the depth of his achievements. The peace he established proved temporary—the Wars of Religion may have ended, but religious tensions persisted, ultimately leading to Louis XIV's revocation of the Edict of Nantes in 1685 and renewed persecution of Protestants.[^148] Some historians argue that Henry's policy of religious coexistence was based on political necessity rather than principled tolerance, and that it failed to address the fundamental incompatibility between France's Catholic identity and the Protestant minority's aspirations.[^149]

His military reputation has been persistently questioned. Multiple historians have dismissed him as a "poor strategist" despite his battlefield successes.[^150] These critics argue that Henry often prioritized immediate tactical advantages over long-term strategic planning, and that his victories owed more to the weakness and division of his enemies than to his own military genius.[^151]

Henry's notorious personal behavior attracts significant criticism. His "sexual appetite was said to have been insatiable," and he maintained multiple mistresses simultaneously, producing numerous illegitimate children whom he forced his wife to help raise.[^152][^153] This behavior is seen not merely as personal failing but as evidence of a selfish, undisciplined character that prioritized his own desires over his duties as husband and king.[^154]

Finally, critical scholars note that Henry's assassination by François Ravaillac, a Catholic zealot, demonstrated the failure of his reconciliation project.[^155] The very fact that religious fanaticism remained strong enough to motivate regicide in 1610, after twelve years of Henry's supposedly healing reign, suggests that his policies had created only a superficial peace while leaving deep religious hatreds unresolved.[^156] His planned military intervention in Germany on the Protestant side had inflamed Catholic opposition, showing that even Henry had not fully transcended the religious divisions of his era.[^157]

Some historians also criticize the long-term consequences of Henry's dynasty. The Bourbon absolutism that reached its apex under Louis XIV, with its centralization of power, suppression of Protestantism, and expensive foreign wars, can be traced back to precedents and institutions Henry established.[^158] From this perspective, Henry's reign laid the groundwork not only for France's seventeenth-century greatness but also for the royal despotism that would eventually provoke the French Revolution.[^159]

## Famous Quotes

> "Paris is worth a mass." ("Paris vaut bien une messe.")

Allegedly said in 1593 upon converting to Catholicism to secure the French throne, though historians note there is no contemporary evidence he actually said this, and it was likely attributed to him years later. Whether authentic or apocryphal, the quote perfectly captures Henry's pragmatic approach to religion and politics.[^160][^161]

> "I want there to be no peasant in my kingdom so poor that he cannot have a chicken in his pot every Sunday." ("Je veux qu'il n'y ait si pauvre paysan en mon royaume qu'il n'ait tous les dimanches sa poule au pot.")

This statement, whether exactly as quoted or paraphrased, exemplifies Henry's expressed concern for the welfare of common people and his vision of broadly shared prosperity. It became central to his posthumous reputation as "Good King Henry."[^162]

> "Those who follow their conscience are of my religion, and I am of the religion of those who are brave and good."

This quote reflects Henry's pragmatic approach to religious differences and his emphasis on moral character and loyalty over doctrinal conformity.[^163]

> "Heresy is in the point of the sword."

A cynical observation about religious conversion through military force, reflecting Henry's experience of the Wars of Religion.[^164]

> "I would give my life to make you happy."

Reportedly said to one of his mistresses, exemplifying his romantic nature and the passionate declarations that characterized his numerous affairs.[^165]
