## Wave optics and key contributions

Fresnel is widely regarded as one of the founders of the wave theory of light. Building on earlier work by Thomas Young, he developed mathematical and experimental arguments that explained interference and diffraction phenomena that were difficult to reconcile with purely corpuscular (particle-only) models of light.

Notable contributions associated with Fresnel include:

- **Fresnel diffraction**: analysis of near-field diffraction and the geometry of wavefront propagation.
- **Fresnel integrals**: functions used to compute diffraction patterns and interference effects.
- **Fresnel equations**: relations (derived within wave optics) that describe reflection and transmission of light at an interface as a function of polarization and angle of incidence.
- **Fresnel lens**: a stepped lens design that preserves the optical power of a large lens while reducing thickness and material, enabling brighter and more efficient lighthouse illumination.

## Engineering and public service context

Although remembered primarily for theoretical optics, Fresnel also worked within French engineering institutions of his time. His lighthouse optics work connected theory to practical navigation safety, influencing the standardization of lighthouse lighting systems in the 19th century.

## Legacy

Fresnel’s name remains attached to foundational tools in physical optics used in modern engineering and science, from optical design and photonics to electromagnetic wave propagation and signal analysis.
