## Summary

Tyrant of Akragas who roasted victims alive inside a bronze bull he commissioned as a torture device.

## Historical Context

- **Polis / city**: Phalaris is associated with **Akragas (Agrigentum)**, a Greek colony on the southern coast of Sicily.
- **Period**: Archaic Greek era, when many poleis experienced cycles of oligarchy, tyranny, and reforms.
- **Reputation**: Later authors treat him as a byword for **cruelty**, and the most famous story tied to his name is the bronze bull.

## The Bronze Bull (Brazen Bull)

- **What it was**: A hollow bronze statue shaped like a bull, reportedly designed so that a person could be locked inside.
- **How it functioned (as described in later sources)**:
  - A fire was lit beneath the metal body.
  - The victim’s screams were said to be channeled through pipes so they resembled the sound of a bull.
- **Source caution**: The best-known details come from **later literary traditions** that emphasize moral exempla; historians often treat the most sensational elements with care.

## Governance and Rule (High-level)

- Tyrants in Greek poleis typically relied on **personal guards**, control of civic institutions, and selective alliances among local elites.
- In later Greek and Roman writing, Phalaris is used to illustrate how tyranny can be remembered less for policy and more for **spectacle and fear**.

## Legacy

- **Cultural shorthand**: “Phalaris” and “bull of Phalaris” became a recurring reference point in discussions of **inhuman punishment**.
- **Moral literature**: Ancient writers frequently used notorious tyrants as cautionary figures; Phalaris fits this pattern.
