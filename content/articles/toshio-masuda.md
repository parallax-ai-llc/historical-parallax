## Summary

Head of the Sumiyoshi-kai, Japan's second-largest yakuza organization. Oversaw operations including gambling, extortion, and real estate during Japan's economic transformation.

## Organizational context

- The Sumiyoshi-kai is one of Japan’s largest yakuza syndicates and is often described as a federation of groups rather than a single centralized organization.
- Yakuza groups historically engaged in a mix of illicit enterprises and semi-open “gray zone” activities, while maintaining offices and public-facing structures in some periods.

## Leadership and era

- Masuda is commonly identified as a senior leader/chairman during the late 1990s and 2000s.
- This period overlapped with stronger legal and financial pressure on organized crime in Japan, including anti-yakuza ordinances and increased scrutiny of corporate ties.

## Commonly attributed activities

- Reporting on yakuza organizations frequently mentions income streams such as gambling operations, protection rackets, loan-sharking, and influence in construction and real estate.
- The specifics of any one leader’s direct operational control can be difficult to verify from open sources, because organizational details are opaque by design.

## Modern relevance

- Discussions of the Sumiyoshi-kai often focus on how the group adapted to changing enforcement and social stigma.
- Analysts also track fragmentation, succession, and the changing economics of organized crime.
