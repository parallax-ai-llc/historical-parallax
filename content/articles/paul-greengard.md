## Summary

Paul Greengard was an American neuroscientist who shared the 2000 Nobel Prize for signal transduction in the nervous system.

## Scientific Contributions

Greengard is best known for elucidating how neurons use intracellular chemical signaling to translate external stimuli into changes in cell function. His work emphasized the importance of phosphorylation and related biochemical pathways in synaptic transmission, connecting molecular events to behavior and disease.

## Nobel Prize Context

The 2000 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine recognized discoveries about signal transduction in the nervous system. Greengard's research helped establish a framework for understanding how neurotransmitters can trigger cascades inside neurons rather than acting only through rapid electrical effects.

## Institutions and Mentorship

He spent significant portions of his career in leading research environments, including at Rockefeller University. Beyond publications, he is remembered for training and collaborating with scientists who advanced neurobiology, pharmacology, and psychiatry.

## Relevance to Medicine

By mapping molecular signaling pathways in the brain, Greengard's work contributed to how researchers conceptualize drug targets for neurological and psychiatric disorders. The broader field of neuropharmacology draws on such mechanisms to explain why certain drugs have delayed or long-term effects.

## Legacy

Greengard's contributions are often summarized as bridging biochemistry and neuroscience: showing how cellular signaling machinery shapes neural circuits over time. His career is also cited as an example of how basic research can eventually inform therapeutic strategies.
