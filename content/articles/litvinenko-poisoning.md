## Summary

Former FSB officer Alexander Litvinenko was poisoned with radioactive polonium-210 in London. A UK inquiry concluded the assassination was 'probably approved' by Putin.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|

## Background

- **Alexander Litvinenko** was a former officer of Russia's Federal Security Service (FSB) who became a vocal critic of the Russian state after leaving Russia.
- He lived in the **United Kingdom** and was involved in public allegations about Russian security services and organized crime.
- The incident became notable because it involved **polonium-210**, a rare radioactive isotope that is difficult to obtain and handle.

## Key People and Entities

- **Alexander Litvinenko** — victim; former FSB officer and UK resident at the time of poisoning.
- **Andrey Lugovoy** — identified by UK authorities as a principal suspect; later became a Russian politician.
- **Dmitry Kovtun** — identified by UK authorities as a principal suspect.
- **UK authorities** — led the criminal investigation and later supported a public inquiry.
- **Russian authorities** — rejected UK extradition requests and disputed UK conclusions.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- Litvinenko fell severely ill in **London** after meeting Lugovoy and Kovtun.
- Medical investigation determined he had been poisoned with **polonium-210**.
- Litvinenko died later in November 2006; the case received worldwide attention due to the method and geopolitical implications.

## Investigation and Inquiry (UK)

- UK police conducted a counter-terrorism investigation and examined possible contamination routes.
- A **UK public inquiry** (reported in 2016) concluded that Lugovoy and Kovtun carried out the poisoning.
- The inquiry stated the operation was **"probably approved"** by senior Russian state figures (widely reported as including the head of the FSB and the Russian president).

## Aftermath

- The UK imposed or discussed **diplomatic and policy responses** after inquiry findings.
- Russia refused to extradite suspects; the case remained a persistent point of UK–Russia tension.
- The incident is frequently cited in discussions of **state-linked targeted killings** and the use of unusual toxins.

## Significance

- Demonstrated the potential use of **radiological materials** in an assassination context.
- Highlighted challenges in accountability when suspects and evidence are tied to **cross-border intelligence and diplomacy**.
- Became a reference case for later debates about Russian security services, defections, and overseas operations.
