## Summary

French CEO of Mt. Gox, once the world's largest Bitcoin exchange, which lost 850,000 bitcoins worth $450 million. The hack was the largest cryptocurrency theft at the time and crashed the Bitcoin market.

## Context and Significance

- **What Mt. Gox was:** Mt. Gox began as a Bitcoin exchange and grew into a dominant venue for early Bitcoin trading.
- **Why the collapse mattered:** The failure highlighted how concentrated early crypto infrastructure was, and how operational failures at a single intermediary could affect global market confidence.
- **Custody risk:** Centralized exchanges act as custodians; users typically do not control private keys, which creates systemic risk when internal controls are weak.

## Common Lessons Cited in Retrospectives

- **Security and accounting controls:** Exchanges are expected to maintain strong wallet management, logging, and reconciliation processes.
- **Segregation of duties:** Separating authorization, deployment, and audit responsibilities is a basic internal-control principle often discussed in postmortems.
- **Transparency and disclosure:** Communication practices during incidents can materially affect customer outcomes and market stability.

## Legal and Operational Aftermath (High-Level)

- The collapse led to **bankruptcy and restructuring processes** in Japan, and years of claims administration for creditors.
- Karpelès faced **criminal charges** related to management of the exchange; outcomes included a conviction on some counts and acquittal on others.
- The event became a frequently referenced example in debates about **regulation, consumer protection, and exchange oversight** in cryptocurrency markets.

## Additional Notes

- Articles about Mt. Gox often distinguish between **external theft** and **internal accounting/operational failures**; both can contribute to customer losses.
- Because narratives vary by source, treat specific attribution (who did what, when) as a **sourcing question** and prefer primary filings when available.
