## Summary

Collapse of the Rana Plaza garment factory building in Dhaka on April 24, 2013 that killed 1,134 workers. It was the deadliest garment factory disaster in history and exposed exploitative labor conditions.

## Context and Causes

- Rana Plaza was a commercial building complex in Savar, near Dhaka, that housed multiple garment factories supplying global apparel brands.
- The disaster became a defining case study in building-code enforcement failures, weak workplace safety governance, and intense cost pressure in fast-fashion supply chains.
- Reports described structural cracks appearing before the collapse; despite concerns, factory work resumed the next day.

## What Happened

- On 24 April 2013, the building collapsed during the workday, trapping thousands of workers inside.
- A large-scale rescue effort followed, involving Bangladeshi authorities, the military, firefighters, and volunteers.
- The event drew sustained international attention because of the scale of fatalities and the role of export-oriented garment production.

## Aftermath and Accountability

- Investigations focused on construction quality, permissions, and the decision-making that kept factories operating despite warning signs.
- Compensation and remediation efforts included international agreements and brand-funded initiatives intended to improve factory inspections and worker safety.
- The collapse accelerated global scrutiny of labor conditions, subcontracting practices, and transparency in apparel sourcing.

## Key Takeaways

- The collapse became a global reference point for discussions about workplace safety in outsourced manufacturing.
- It increased attention on auditing limits when production is subcontracted across many factories.
- The disaster also illustrated how local enforcement capacity and corruption risks can compound engineering failures.
