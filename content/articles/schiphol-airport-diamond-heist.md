## Summary

Armed men stole $118 million worth of diamonds from an armoured KLM cargo truck at Amsterdam's Schiphol Airport. Most of the diamonds were never recovered.

## What Happened (High-Level)

The Schiphol Airport diamond heist refers to a high-value cargo theft that took place on **25 February 2005** at **Amsterdam Airport Schiphol**.

Key points commonly reported in summaries:

- The target was a shipment of diamonds being transported in a **KLM cargo** context.
- The thieves used **weapons and intimidation** to seize the cargo.
- The estimated value reported in press and secondary sources is **about US$118 million**.
- **Most of the stones were not recovered**, which is a major reason the incident is frequently cited in lists of notable heists.

## Method and Tactics

Public accounts emphasize that the operation appeared planned and time-sensitive.

Tactics described in coverage include:

- **Disguises/impersonation** (often described as appearing like police or security personnel) to reduce initial suspicion.
- **Rapid interception** of an armored/secured cargo vehicle during airport-side logistics.
- **Fast exit** from the airport perimeter, consistent with a plan that prioritized speed over prolonged confrontation.

Because many operational details come from investigative reporting and later reconstructions, it is safest to treat precise step-by-step descriptions as *reported* rather than fully verifiable from a single public summary.

## Investigation and Aftermath

After the theft:

- Authorities pursued suspects and attempted to trace the stones through fencing networks.
- Reporting commonly notes that **only a small portion** of the diamonds was recovered.

The case is often referenced as an example of the difficulty of recovering high-value, easily divisible goods (like diamonds) once they enter illicit markets.

## Security and Industry Impact

Schiphol is one of Europe's major logistics hubs, and the incident is frequently discussed as a stress test for:

- **Airport perimeter security** and controlled access areas.
- **Chain-of-custody** practices for high-value cargo.
- The balance between efficient cargo operations and layered security measures.

In broader context, the heist illustrates how insider knowledge, timing, and credible disguises can undermine systems that are otherwise robust on paper.
