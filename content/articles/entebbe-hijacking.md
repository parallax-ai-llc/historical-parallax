## Summary

Hijacking of Air France Flight 139 by PFLP and German revolutionaries on June 27, 1976. The plane was flown to Entebbe, Uganda where Israeli commandos carried out a dramatic rescue of 102 hostages.

## Additional Context

- **Route and seizure:** Air France Flight 139 was operating a Tel Aviv–Paris service with a stopover; the aircraft was taken over shortly after departing Athens on 27 June 1976 and was first forced to land in **Benghazi, Libya** before being flown onward to **Entebbe, Uganda**.
- **Perpetrators:** The hijacking was carried out by members of the **Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine (PFLP)** together with **German militant** collaborators (often identified in reporting as members of the Revolutionary Cells).
- **Hostage separation:** Once at Entebbe, hostages were held in the old terminal and, according to widely cited accounts, **Israeli/Jewish passengers** were separated from others and threatened with execution if demands were not met.
- **Ugandan role:** Uganda’s leader **Idi Amin** was accused of aiding the hijackers and providing a permissive environment at Entebbe; the episode became a major international incident.
- **Rescue operation:** On the night of **3–4 July 1976**, Israeli forces conducted **Operation Entebbe (also known as Operation Thunderbolt)**, flying in commandos to free the hostages. The raid is noted for long-range airlift, rapid assault planning, and deception tactics used to approach the terminal.
- **Aftermath and losses:** Most hostages were rescued; several hijackers were killed in the assault, and a small number of hostages and Israeli personnel died. Ugandan military assets, including aircraft at the airfield, were reportedly destroyed during the withdrawal.

## Why It Matters

The Entebbe episode is frequently cited in discussions of:

- **Counterterrorism doctrine** and the risks/benefits of military rescue versus negotiation.
- **State sponsorship or protection** of non-state armed groups.
- The evolution of **aviation security** practices in the 1970s.
