## Summary

Italian anti-Mafia judge assassinated by Cosa Nostra just 57 days after his colleague Giovanni Falcone. A car bomb killed him and five police escorts.

## Early Life and Legal Career

Paolo Borsellino was born in Palermo and trained as a magistrate in the Italian judicial system. He became known for working on organized crime cases during a period when the Sicilian Mafia (Cosa Nostra) exercised significant violent and political influence.

## Anti-Mafia Work

Borsellino is closely associated with the “pool” approach in Palermo, where magistrates shared information and coordinated strategy to reduce the risk that any single investigator could be isolated or compromised.

He worked alongside Giovanni Falcone and others in building cases that culminated in the Maxi Trial, a landmark prosecution that relied heavily on evidence, financial tracing, and testimony from pentiti (turncoat witnesses).

## Assassination

Borsellino was killed in the Via D'Amelio bombing in Palermo. The attack also killed members of his police escort and became one of the most notorious assassinations in modern Italian history.

## Investigations and Public Debate

The aftermath included extensive investigations, trials, and controversies over the handling of evidence and the search for those responsible. The case became a focal point for Italian debates about:

- the relationship between organized crime and politics
- state capacity and institutional integrity
- the protection of magistrates and witnesses

## Legacy

Borsellino is widely remembered as a symbol of civic resistance to mafia violence. Commemorations, educational initiatives, and anti-mafia organizations often invoke his work to emphasize the importance of rule-of-law institutions and sustained public accountability.
