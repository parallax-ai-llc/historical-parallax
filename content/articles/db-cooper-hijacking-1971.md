## Summary
On November 24, 1971, a man using the alias Dan Cooper hijacked a Northwest Orient Airlines flight, extorted $200,000, and parachuted from the plane over the Pacific Northwest. He was never found. It remains the only unsolved hijacking in US commercial aviation history.

## Investigation and Evidence
The FBI opened an extensive investigation, gathering witness statements, tracking the ransom money serial numbers, and searching likely jump zones. In 1980, a child found a small cache of deteriorated ransom bills along the Columbia River, providing one of the few physical links to the hijacking.

## Aviation Security Changes
The hijacking occurred during a period of frequent airline hijackings in the United States. The case contributed to long-term attention on cabin security and aircraft design, including the development and later mandated installation of devices intended to prevent unauthorized deployment of airstairs in flight (commonly associated with the “Cooper vane”).

## Cultural Legacy
“D. B. Cooper” became a durable American mystery referenced in books, documentaries, and popular culture. Numerous suspects have been proposed, but no definitive identification has been accepted by authorities.
