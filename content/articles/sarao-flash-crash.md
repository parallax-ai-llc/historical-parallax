## Summary

The May 6, 2010 Flash Crash that briefly wiped $1 trillion from US markets was partly caused by British trader Navinder Sarao who used spoofing techniques from his parents house in London.

## The 2010 Flash Crash (market context)

On May 6, 2010, U.S. equity and futures markets experienced an extreme, rapid drop and rebound over minutes.
Many securities traded at anomalous prices, and liquidity temporarily disappeared in parts of the market.
The event became a reference point for debates about high-frequency trading, market fragmentation, and automated risk controls.

## Spoofing and alleged conduct

“Spoofing” generally refers to placing orders without intent to execute in order to create a misleading impression of supply or demand.
Regulators and prosecutors argued that certain order patterns in E-mini S&P 500 futures contributed to instability during the crash.
The Sarao case is frequently cited as a prominent enforcement action involving spoofing allegations.

## Enforcement and outcomes

The case involved U.K. arrest proceedings and U.S. extradition, followed by U.S. federal criminal and CFTC-related actions.
Public reporting has described cooperation, legal negotiations, and questions about the relative contribution of one trader versus broader market dynamics.

## Regulatory impact

After the Flash Crash, exchanges and regulators implemented or strengthened mechanisms such as circuit breakers and limit-up/limit-down rules.
The incident also accelerated research into how automated strategies interact under stress and how to detect manipulation in high-volume order books.
