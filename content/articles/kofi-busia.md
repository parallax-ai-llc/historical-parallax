## Summary

Prime Minister of Ghana whose government was overthrown for corruption and economic mismanagement. The Busia government implemented the Alien Compliance Order expelling foreign workers.

## Early Career

Busia was a Ghanaian academic and political leader who became prominent in the years surrounding independence. He is often described as a sociologist by training and an influential opposition figure during Ghana’s early postcolonial political experiments.

## Prime Ministership (Second Republic)

Busia served as Prime Minister after the 1969 elections, leading a government that sought to restore civilian rule following earlier military intervention in politics. His administration faced significant economic constraints, including foreign-exchange shortages, fiscal pressures, and debates over the appropriate balance between state planning and market-oriented reforms.

## Major Policies

Several policy areas are frequently associated with his government:

- Economic stabilization measures, including contested currency and budget decisions
- Public-sector and trade policies aimed at improving macroeconomic balance
- The Alien Compliance Order, which affected migrant communities and labor markets

## Overthrow and Context

In 1972, Busia’s government was removed in a military coup. Contemporary and later analyses connect the coup to a mix of economic discontent, civil–military tensions, and political polarization. Public narratives about the period vary, with supporters emphasizing the difficulties of governing amid structural economic problems and critics focusing on perceived policy failures and patronage.

## Legacy

Busia remains a significant figure in Ghana’s political history, often discussed in relation to democratic governance, economic reform debates, and the treatment of migrants and non-citizens. His tenure is also used as a case study of how fragile civilian administrations can be when economic shocks and institutional tensions converge.
