## Summary

Clovis I (c. 466–511) was the first king of the Franks to unite all of the Frankish tribes under one ruler, changing the form of leadership from a group of royal chieftains to rule by a single king and ensuring that the kingship was passed down to his heirs.[^1] He is considered the founder of the Merovingian dynasty, which ruled the Frankish kingdom for the next two centuries, and his conversion to Catholicism in 496 was a pivotal moment in European history.[^2] Clovis's military conquests expanded Frankish territory to include most of the former Roman province of Gaul (modern-day France and parts of Germany), establishing the foundation for what would become the Kingdom of France.[^3] His reign marked the transition from late antiquity to the early Middle Ages in Western Europe, and his legacy profoundly influenced the development of Christian Europe.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Clovis I was born around 466 in the region of Tournai, in what is now Belgium, into the Merovingian dynasty of Frankish kings.[^5] He was the son of Childeric I, king of the Salian Franks, and Basina, a Thuringian princess.[^6] The Franks were a confederation of Germanic tribes that had settled in the Roman provinces of Gaul during the declining years of the Western Roman Empire.[^7] At the time of Clovis's birth, the Frankish people were divided into several independent kingdoms, each ruled by its own king or chieftain.[^8]

Clovis's father, Childeric I, had been a powerful military leader who maintained a complex relationship with the remnants of Roman authority in Gaul, sometimes serving as a Roman ally and sometimes acting independently.[^9] When Childeric died in 481 or 482, Clovis succeeded him as king of the Salian Franks at approximately fifteen years of age.[^10] The young king inherited a relatively small kingdom centered around Tournai and the surrounding region, but he also inherited his father's ambitions and military prowess.[^11]

Little is known about Clovis's early education or upbringing, as written records from this period are scarce and often unreliable.[^12] The primary source for Clovis's life is the "History of the Franks" written by Gregory of Tours in the late sixth century, several decades after Clovis's death.[^13] Gregory portrayed Clovis as a divinely favored ruler whose conversion to Christianity was part of God's plan for the Franks.[^14] Modern historians recognize that Gregory's account contains hagiographical elements and must be read critically, but it remains the most comprehensive contemporary source available.[^15]

As a young king in a turbulent time, Clovis would have been trained in warfare, leadership, and the political intrigues necessary to survive in the fractured post-Roman world.[^16] The Frankish culture of the time valued martial prowess, loyalty to one's warband, and the distribution of plunder to maintain the support of warriors.[^17] These values would shape Clovis's approach to kingship throughout his reign.[^18]

## Rise to Power

Clovis's rise to prominence began almost immediately after his accession to the throne in 481/482.[^19] His first major military campaign came in 486, when he attacked the last significant remnant of Roman authority in northern Gaul: the Domain of Soissons, ruled by the Roman general Syagrius.[^20] In the Battle of Soissons, Clovis decisively defeated Syagrius, who fled to the Visigothic king Alaric II for protection.[^21] Under threat from Clovis, Alaric handed over Syagrius, whom Clovis then executed, eliminating a major rival and expanding his territory significantly.[^22]

The conquest of Syagrius's domain gave Clovis control over a wealthy and strategically important region and brought him into contact with the Gallo-Roman aristocracy and Catholic bishops who had served under Roman rule.[^23] Unlike the Visigoths, Burgundians, and other Germanic peoples who had adopted Arian Christianity, Clovis and his Franks remained pagan, which initially complicated his relationship with the Catholic Church but would later become crucial to his success.[^24]

Between 486 and 496, Clovis systematically expanded his power through a combination of military conquest, political marriages, and strategic alliances.[^25] Around 493, he married Clotilde, a Burgundian princess who was a devout Catholic.[^26] This marriage alliance connected Clovis to the powerful Burgundian kingdom and exposed him directly to Catholic influence through his wife, who reportedly urged him to convert.[^27]

A pivotal moment in Clovis's rise came around 496, when he faced the Alemanni, a confederation of Germanic tribes threatening Frankish territory from the east.[^28] According to Gregory of Tours, during the Battle of Tolbiac, when Clovis's forces were on the verge of defeat, he prayed to his wife's God, promising to convert if granted victory.[^29] The Franks rallied and won the battle, and Clovis subsequently converted to Catholic Christianity, being baptized by Saint Remigius (Remi), the Bishop of Reims, on December 25, 496 (though some historians place this event as late as 508).[^30]

This conversion was a masterstroke of political strategy.[^31] By adopting Catholic Christianity rather than Arian Christianity, Clovis aligned himself with the Gallo-Roman population and the powerful Catholic episcopate, gaining allies who could help legitimize and administer his expanding kingdom.[^32] It also differentiated him from other Germanic rulers and provided ideological justification for his subsequent conquests of Arian kingdoms.[^33]

Following his conversion, Clovis launched campaigns against the Burgundians (500), though this was inconclusive, and more successfully against the Visigoths.[^34] In 507, at the Battle of Vouillé near Poitiers, Clovis's forces defeated the Visigothic king Alaric II, who was killed in the battle, possibly by Clovis himself.[^35] This victory gave Clovis control over most of Aquitaine and pushed Visigothic power south of the Pyrenees into Hispania.[^36]

By the end of his reign, Clovis had also eliminated rival Frankish kings, including Sigobert the Lame, king of the Ripuarian Franks, and Ragnachar of Cambrai, consolidating all Frankish tribes under his sole rule.[^37] These actions, often achieved through treachery and assassination according to Gregory of Tours, established Clovis as the undisputed king of all the Franks.[^38]

## Major Achievements

Clovis I's most significant achievement was the unification of the Frankish tribes under a single monarchy, creating a powerful kingdom that would dominate Western Europe for centuries.[^39] Before Clovis, the Franks were divided into numerous petty kingdoms and tribal confederations; after his reign, they were a unified political entity with a clear line of dynastic succession.[^40] This unification provided stability and strength that enabled the Franks to resist external threats and expand their territory.[^41]

The territorial expansion under Clovis was remarkable. When he became king, his domain consisted of a small territory around Tournai; by his death in 511, he controlled most of what is now France, Belgium, and parts of Germany and Switzerland.[^42] His conquests included the Domain of Soissons (486), much of the Alemanni territory (496-506), significant portions of Burgundy (500-534, completed by his sons), and most of Visigothic Gaul (507-511).[^43] This territorial expansion created the foundation for the Frankish Empire and, ultimately, for medieval France.[^44]

Clovis's conversion to Catholic Christianity was not merely a personal religious decision but a transformative political act with profound historical consequences.[^45] By becoming Catholic rather than Arian, Clovis gained the support of the Catholic Church and the Gallo-Roman population, who formed the administrative backbone of his kingdom.[^46] This alliance between the Frankish monarchy and the Catholic Church became a defining feature of medieval European politics.[^47] The Catholic Church provided legitimacy, administrative expertise, and ideological support for Clovis's rule, while Clovis protected the Church and promoted its interests.[^48]

The baptism of Clovis and his warriors (reportedly 3,000 were baptized with him) initiated the Christianization of the Frankish people and set in motion a process that would eventually make the Franks the "eldest daughter of the Church" and champion of Catholic Christianity in Western Europe.[^49] This religious identity would later justify Charlemagne's coronation as Holy Roman Emperor and the Crusades, among other pivotal events.[^50]

Clovis also established important administrative and legal precedents. Around 507-511, he promulgated the Lex Salica (Salic Law), a written legal code that codified Frankish customary law.[^51] While the text that survives today was likely modified by later editors, the core legal principles can be attributed to Clovis's reign.[^52] The Salic Law covered various aspects of Frankish life, including inheritance, property rights, and criminal penalties, and it would influence European legal development for centuries.[^53] One famous provision, which later interpreters claimed excluded women from royal succession, would be invoked during succession disputes in medieval and early modern France.[^54]

Clovis established Paris as his capital around 508, moving his seat of power from Tournai.[^55] This choice was strategically brilliant, as Paris was centrally located in his expanded kingdom and was an important city with Roman roots.[^56] The establishment of Paris as the Frankish capital set the stage for its eventual status as the capital of France.[^57]

In recognition of his services to the Catholic cause, Clovis received honors from the Byzantine Emperor Anastasius I, who granted him the title of honorary consul in 508.[^58] This recognition connected Clovis to the prestige of the Roman Empire and provided additional legitimacy for his rule.[^59]

Clovis's reign also saw the beginning of a productive collaboration between Frankish rulers and the Gallo-Roman aristocracy and clergy.[^60] Rather than completely replacing Roman institutions with Germanic ones, Clovis adapted and incorporated existing structures, creating a hybrid system that drew on both traditions.[^61] This pragmatic approach to governance contributed to the stability and longevity of the Frankish kingdom.[^62]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his historical significance and the reverence accorded to him by medieval chroniclers, Clovis's methods and character have been subject to substantial criticism, both by contemporary observers and modern historians.[^63] Gregory of Tours, while generally favorable to Clovis as the founder of Catholic Frankish power, nevertheless recorded numerous instances of treachery, violence, and ruthlessness that paint a darker picture of the king.[^64]

One of the most persistent criticisms concerns Clovis's systematic elimination of rival Frankish kings, often through treachery and assassination rather than open warfare.[^65] Gregory of Tours recounts how Clovis orchestrated the murders of Sigobert the Lame and his son Chloderic, kings of the Ripuarian Franks, by encouraging Chloderic to kill his father and then having Chloderic assassinated, after which Clovis claimed the kingdom for himself.[^66] Similarly, Clovis had Ragnachar of Cambrai and his brothers killed after promising them safety, gaining their territories through betrayal.[^67]

Gregory attributes to Clovis a famous lamentation near the end of his life: "Woe to me, who have remained as a traveler among strangers, and have none of my own relations left to help me in time of adversity."[^68] However, Gregory notes that Clovis said this not out of genuine grief but "craftily... in the hope of discovering some relative still in the land of the living whom he could kill."[^69] This portrayal, even from a generally sympathetic source, reveals the brutal and paranoid character of Clovis's rule.

The sincerity of Clovis's conversion to Christianity has been questioned by some historians, who view it primarily as a political calculation rather than a genuine spiritual transformation.[^70] While Gregory of Tours presents the conversion as divinely inspired, the timing and consequences of the conversion—providing Clovis with powerful allies and justification for further conquests—suggest strategic motives.[^71] Clovis's actions after conversion, including continued violence and treachery, seem inconsistent with Christian moral teachings, though this inconsistency was common among early medieval rulers.[^72]

Clovis's treatment of the Gallo-Roman population and other conquered peoples was often harsh.[^73] While he cultivated alliances with the Catholic Church and Gallo-Roman aristocracy, his Frankish warriors engaged in the typical pillaging and violence of early medieval warfare.[^74] The famous story of the "Vase of Soissons," in which Clovis split a warrior's skull with an axe for violating his order about the distribution of plunder, illustrates both Clovis's authority and the violent culture of his warband.[^75]

Modern historians have criticized the romanticization of Clovis in French nationalist historiography, which has sometimes portrayed him as the founder of France and a noble Christian hero.[^76] These interpretations often downplay the violence and instability of his reign and the fundamentally different nature of early medieval kingship compared to later nation-states.[^77] Clovis was not creating a "nation" in the modern sense but rather building a personal domain through military conquest and political opportunism.[^78]

The legal code attributed to Clovis, the Salic Law, has also been controversial, particularly in its later interpretations.[^79] While the original code was relatively straightforward customary law, later medieval and early modern lawyers interpreted certain provisions to exclude women from royal succession and property inheritance, contributing to legal inequalities that persisted for centuries.[^80] Whether Clovis intended such interpretations is unknown, but the law bearing his name became associated with gender discrimination.[^81]

Some scholars have criticized the centralization of power under Clovis as ultimately destabilizing, arguing that Frankish traditions of dividing kingdoms among heirs led to constant civil wars among Clovis's descendants.[^82] The Merovingian dynasty that Clovis founded was characterized by fratricidal conflicts, palace intrigues, and eventual decline into powerlessness, with the later "do-nothing kings" being mere puppets of their mayors of the palace.[^83]

## Personal Life

Clovis's personal life, like much of early medieval history, is imperfectly documented, but several important details emerge from the historical record.[^84] His most significant personal relationship was his marriage to Clotilde (also called Clothilde or Chrotilda), a Burgundian princess and devout Catholic, which took place around 492 or 493.[^85] Clotilde was the daughter of Chilperic II, king of Burgundy, who had been murdered along with his wife by his brother Gundobad.[^86] Clotilde had survived the massacre and taken refuge in Geneva, where she lived under the guardianship of her uncle Gundobad, who had killed her parents.[^87]

The marriage between Clovis and Clotilde was both a political alliance and, according to Gregory of Tours, a genuine partnership.[^88] Clotilde is portrayed as having been instrumental in Clovis's conversion to Christianity, persistently urging him to abandon paganism and embrace the Catholic faith.[^89] When their first son was baptized at Clotilde's insistence and died shortly after, Clovis blamed Christianity, but Clotilde remained steadfast in her faith.[^90] After Clovis's miraculous victory over the Alemanni and his subsequent conversion, Clotilde's influence at court increased significantly.[^91]

Clovis and Clotilde had four sons who survived to adulthood: Ingomer (who died in infancy), Chlodomer, Childebert I, Chlothar I, and Chlodomir, who would inherit different portions of their father's kingdom.[^92] They also had a daughter, Clotilde (named after her mother), who married Amalaric, king of the Visigoths.[^93] Before his marriage to Clotilde, Clovis had a relationship with an unnamed woman by whom he had a son, Theuderic I, who also became king of a portion of the Frankish realm.[^94]

The division of Clovis's kingdom among his four surviving sons after his death followed Frankish custom, which treated the kingdom as personal property to be divided among heirs rather than an indivisible political entity.[^95] This practice would lead to significant conflicts among Clovis's descendants and characterize Merovingian politics for generations.[^96]

Gregory of Tours provides some glimpses into Clovis's personality and character. He was evidently a charismatic and effective military leader, able to inspire loyalty among his warriors and terror among his enemies.[^97] The "Vase of Soissons" incident demonstrates his concern for maintaining authority and his willingness to use dramatic violence to enforce discipline.[^98] His systematic elimination of rival kings shows strategic cunning and ruthlessness.[^99]

Clovis appears to have had a pragmatic and calculating personality, willing to use any means—military force, political marriages, religious conversion, or assassination—to achieve his goals.[^100] At the same time, he showed respect for the Catholic Church after his conversion and worked to build collaborative relationships with bishops and Gallo-Roman elites.[^101]

After his death on November 27, 511, Clovis was buried in the Church of the Holy Apostles in Paris, which he and Clotilde had founded.[^102] This church, later renamed Sainte-Geneviève, became an important religious site.[^103] Clotilde survived her husband by many years, dying around 545, and was eventually recognized as a saint by the Catholic Church for her role in converting the Franks to Christianity.[^104]

The personal lives of Clovis's descendants were marked by violence and intrigue that, in some ways, reflected the methods Clovis himself had used.[^105] His grandsons murdered two of Chlodomer's sons to prevent them from sharing in their inheritance, an act that echoed Clovis's own elimination of rival claimants.[^106] The brutal patterns established during Clovis's reign continued to shape Merovingian political culture.[^107]

## Legacy

Clovis I's legacy is profound and multifaceted, influencing European political, religious, and cultural development for more than a millennium after his death.[^108] As the founder of the Merovingian dynasty and the first king of all the Franks, Clovis established the Frankish kingdom as the dominant power in Western Europe, a position it would maintain and expand under the Carolingians.[^109]

Perhaps Clovis's most enduring legacy was his conversion of the Franks to Catholic Christianity, which created a lasting alliance between the Frankish monarchy and the Catholic Church.[^110] This alliance became a defining feature of European medieval civilization and set the Franks apart from other Germanic peoples who had adopted Arianism.[^111] When the Arian kingdoms (Visigoths, Vandals, Ostrogoths, Burgundians) eventually fell or converted, the Franks emerged as the champions of Catholic orthodoxy in the West.[^112]

The relationship between the Frankish kings and the papacy, which began to develop during and after Clovis's reign, would later result in the coronation of Charlemagne as Holy Roman Emperor in 800, establishing a political structure that endured until 1806.[^113] The concept of Christian kingship that Clovis helped establish—the idea that kings ruled by divine right and in partnership with the Church—shaped European political theory throughout the Middle Ages and beyond.[^114]

Clovis's territorial conquests laid the foundation for the Kingdom of France.[^115] While the Frankish kingdom was repeatedly divided and reunited among Clovis's descendants, the core territory he established—essentially Roman Gaul minus the Mediterranean coast—formed the basis for the French state that would emerge in the later Middle Ages.[^116] French monarchs and nationalists from the Middle Ages through the nineteenth century claimed Clovis as the founder of France, and he occupied a prominent place in French national mythology.[^117]

The baptism of Clovis became a foundational event in French royal ceremony and ideology.[^118] Later French kings were crowned and anointed in Reims, the site of Clovis's baptism, using (supposedly) the same holy oil (the "Holy Ampulla") that had been used to baptize Clovis.[^119] This tradition continued until the French Revolution and was even briefly revived during the Restoration.[^120]

The Salic Law, codified during Clovis's reign, had a lasting impact on European legal development.[^121] It influenced legal codes throughout the Frankish territories and beyond, and its principles were incorporated into later legal systems.[^122] The supposed "Salic Law" excluding women from succession, while likely a later interpretation, was invoked during major historical events, including the Hundred Years' War (when Edward III of England claimed the French throne through his mother, but the French invoked Salic Law to exclude his claim).[^123]

Clovis's model of kingship—combining military prowess, political cunning, control of patronage, and religious legitimacy—became the template for early medieval European monarchy.[^124] Later Frankish rulers, including Charlemagne, consciously invoked Clovis as a model and precedent for their own rule.[^125]

In religious terms, Clovis's conversion initiated the process of Christianizing the Frankish people and, by extension, much of Northern Europe.[^126] The Frankish Church became a center of missionary activity, sending monks and bishops to convert the Saxons, Frisians, and other Germanic peoples.[^127] Saints like Boniface, who evangelized Germany in the eighth century, worked under Frankish royal protection and patronage, a pattern established by Clovis.[^128]

Culturally, Clovis's reign marked a crucial moment in the fusion of Germanic and Roman-Christian elements that characterized medieval European civilization.[^129] The Frankish kingdom under Clovis and his successors preserved elements of Roman administration, law, and culture while infusing them with Germanic traditions, creating a new synthesis that was neither purely Roman nor purely Germanic.[^130]

Modern historians debate the extent to which Clovis should be considered the "founder of France" versus simply an important early medieval king whose kingdom happened to occupy the territory that later became France.[^131] Nationalist interpretations have given way to more nuanced views that recognize both the significance of Clovis's achievements and the fundamental differences between his kingdom and later medieval France.[^132]

Regardless of historiographical debates, Clovis's impact on the trajectory of European history is undeniable.[^133] The Frankish kingdom he created became the foundation for Charlemagne's empire, the Holy Roman Empire, and ultimately the modern nations of France, Germany, and the Low Countries.[^134] The alliance he forged between Germanic warriors and the Catholic Church shaped European political and religious culture for a thousand years.[^135]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Historians and scholars who view Clovis favorably emphasize his role as a unifier, state-builder, and the founder of a lasting political and religious order in Western Europe.[^136] J.M. Wallace-Hadrill, a prominent historian of the Frankish kingdoms, argued that Clovis possessed exceptional political acumen and an understanding of how to build lasting power by combining military force with institutional legitimacy.[^137] Wallace-Hadrill noted that Clovis's conversion to Catholicism showed sophisticated strategic thinking and a willingness to adapt to the political realities of ruling a predominantly Gallo-Roman population.[^138]

Edward James, another influential historian of the Merovingians, praised Clovis for creating a workable synthesis between Frankish and Roman traditions, arguing that this achievement required considerable political skill and flexibility.[^139] James contends that Clovis should be credited with recognizing that the future of his kingdom depended on incorporating Roman administrative practices and gaining the support of the Catholic episcopate.[^140]

Defenders of Clovis's historical reputation argue that his methods, while violent by modern standards, were typical of early medieval political life and that judging him by modern ethical standards is anachronistic.[^141] All early medieval rulers employed violence, treachery, and assassination; Clovis's distinction was that he used these methods more effectively and with better strategic judgment than his contemporaries.[^142]

Some scholars emphasize Clovis's importance in preserving Christian civilization during the chaotic period following the fall of the Western Roman Empire.[^143] By creating a strong, Catholic kingdom in Gaul, Clovis provided a bulwark against both pagan Germanic tribes and Arian Christian kingdoms, ensuring the survival and eventual dominance of Catholic orthodoxy in Western Europe.[^144]

Patrick Geary, a historian of medieval Europe, has argued that Clovis's legacy should be understood not in nationalist terms but as part of the complex process of ethnogenesis and state formation in the post-Roman world.[^145] From this perspective, Clovis was a successful political entrepreneur who created a new political identity (the Franks) that incorporated diverse peoples and traditions.[^146]

Catholic historians and hagiographers have traditionally venerated Clovis as the first Catholic Germanic king and the ruler who brought the Frankish people into the Christian fold.[^147] The Catholic Church has long honored Clotilde as a saint for her role in Clovis's conversion, and Clovis himself, while never formally canonized, has been regarded with reverence in Catholic tradition.[^148]

French historians, particularly during the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, celebrated Clovis as the founder of the French nation and a great national hero.[^149] François Guizot, the nineteenth-century French historian and statesman, wrote admiringly of Clovis as a nation-builder whose vision unified France.[^150] While this nationalist interpretation has been challenged by more recent scholarship, it demonstrates the enduring power of Clovis's image in French cultural memory.[^151]

---

### Negative Views

Critics of Clovis have focused on his violence, treachery, and the questionable sincerity of his religious conversion, as well as on the problematic nature of his legacy.[^152] Some historians argue that the romanticization of Clovis in French nationalist historiography has obscured the brutal realities of his rule and his fundamentally opportunistic character.[^153]

Modern scholars have questioned the depth and sincerity of Clovis's Christian faith, suggesting that his conversion was primarily a political calculation designed to gain allies and legitimacy rather than a genuine spiritual transformation.[^154] The fact that Clovis continued to employ assassination, treachery, and violence after his baptism, including the systematic murder of rival Frankish kings, suggests that Christian moral teachings had little impact on his behavior.[^155]

Some historians, particularly those influenced by the Annales school and social history, have criticized the traditional focus on Clovis as a "great man" making history through individual actions.[^156] These scholars argue that broader social, economic, and demographic forces were more important than individual rulers in shaping historical development, and that the emphasis on Clovis reflects an outdated "kings and battles" approach to history.[^157]

Feminist historians and legal scholars have criticized the Salic Law attributed to Clovis for its role in establishing and perpetuating legal inequalities between men and women.[^158] While the extent to which Clovis himself bears responsibility for later interpretations of the law is debatable, the legal code promulgated during his reign has been used to justify gender discrimination for centuries.[^159]

Critics have also pointed out that the Merovingian dynasty Clovis founded was characterized by endemic violence, instability, and eventual decline into powerlessness.[^160] Rather than creating a stable political order, Clovis's establishment of the principle that the kingdom should be divided among heirs led to constant civil wars among his descendants.[^161] The later Merovingian kings, known as rois fainéants ("do-nothing kings"), were largely powerless figureheads, suggesting that Clovis's political legacy was ultimately a failure.[^162]

Some historians have questioned whether Clovis's military achievements were as significant as traditional accounts suggest.[^163] Recent scholarship has emphasized that the battles attributed to Clovis may have been smaller-scale affairs than later sources suggest, and that the process of Frankish expansion was more gradual and less dependent on individual military genius than traditional narratives imply.[^164]

Post-colonial and critical historians have noted that the celebration of Clovis as a founder of European Christian civilization reflects a Eurocentric and religiously biased perspective that valorizes conquest and forced conversion.[^165] From this critical standpoint, Clovis's legacy includes the violent suppression of pagan traditions and the beginning of a pattern of European expansionism justified by religious mission.[^166]

Scholars studying late antiquity have sometimes portrayed Clovis less favorably than medievalists, emphasizing the destructive aspects of the Frankish conquests and their role in the final collapse of Roman civilization in Gaul.[^167] While Clovis adapted some Roman institutions, his reign also represented a significant break with Roman traditions and a step toward the fragmentation and localization of power characteristic of the early Middle Ages.[^168]

## Famous Quotes

> "God of Clotilde, if you grant me victory over these enemies, I will be baptized in your name." - Attributed to Clovis during the Battle of Tolbiac, according to Gregory of Tours[^169]

> "Woe to me, who have remained as a traveler among strangers, and have none of my own relations left to help me in time of adversity." - Clovis lamenting the lack of relatives (though Gregory notes this was said "craftily" to discover more relatives to kill)[^170]

> "It grieves me that these Arians should hold a part of Gaul. Let us go forth, with God's help, and conquer them and bring the land under our control." - Clovis before his campaign against the Visigoths, as recorded by Gregory of Tours[^171]

> "Remember, brave Sicambri, bow your head humbly; worship what you have burned, and burn what you have worshipped." - Saint Remigius to Clovis at his baptism, according to later tradition (though historians doubt the authenticity of this quote)[^172]

> "How sad it is that I live among strangers like some refugee, and that I have no relatives to help me when disaster threatens!" - Alternative translation of Clovis's feigned lament about lacking relatives[^173]
