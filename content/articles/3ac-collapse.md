## Background

Three Arrows Capital (often abbreviated “3AC”) was a prominent crypto-focused hedge fund that operated during the rapid expansion of digital-asset markets in the late 2010s and early 2020s. The firm became widely known for directional bets on major cryptoassets and for participating in the broader ecosystem of lending, borrowing, and liquidity provision that connected trading firms, lenders, and exchanges.

Because many participants in the crypto-credit market relied on short-term funding and pledged collateral whose value could change rapidly, the system was vulnerable to sudden price declines and liquidity shocks. In that environment, large, leveraged positions could become difficult to unwind without triggering further losses.

## What “Collapse” Means in This Context

In high-level terms, the “3AC collapse” refers to the fund’s failure to meet obligations to counterparties and creditors, followed by legal and insolvency processes intended to recover assets and resolve claims. In practice this can include:

- Missed margin calls or collateral shortfalls on loans.
- Defaults to lenders and trading counterparties.
- Liquidation or wind-down procedures under relevant jurisdictions.
- Efforts by court-appointed liquidators to locate, secure, and distribute remaining assets.

## Contributing Factors (High-Level)

Public reporting has commonly described the downfall as tied to a combination of market drawdowns and concentrated risk exposure. Typical risk drivers in crypto-credit crises include:

- Leverage: Borrowing against volatile collateral amplifies both gains and losses.
- Concentration: Large, correlated bets (e.g., “risk-on” crypto themes) can fail together.
- Liquidity mismatch: Assets that are hard to sell quickly may back short-term liabilities.
- Counterparty interdependence: Failures at one large firm can spread through lenders and platforms.

## Impact on the Crypto Market

The 3AC failure was frequently cited as a major event in the 2022 crypto credit downturn. When a large borrower defaults, lenders may face balance-sheet stress and are forced to reduce exposure, tighten terms, or halt withdrawals. This can contribute to:

- Losses at crypto lending desks and platforms.
- Contagion effects as creditors seek liquidity simultaneously.
- Greater scrutiny of risk management practices in the sector.
- Calls for clearer disclosure around leverage, collateral, and rehypothecation practices.

## Aftermath and Legal Proceedings

In widely covered accounts, 3AC’s founders, Su Zhu and Kyle Davies, were reported to have left jurisdictions where creditors and liquidators were pursuing recovery efforts. Zhu’s later arrest in Singapore was reported as part of enforcement actions connected to the winding-down process.

In insolvency cases involving global digital-asset operations, the recovery process can be complex because assets may be held across multiple exchanges, wallets, jurisdictions, and legal entities. Liquidators typically attempt to trace funds, identify transferable assets, and resolve disputes over ownership and claims.
