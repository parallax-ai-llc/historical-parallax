## Summary

Indian bandit queen who ordered the Behmai massacre of 22 upper-caste men. Later became a member of parliament.

## Additional Context

### Early Life and Social Setting

Phoolan Devi's life story is often framed in terms of rural poverty, gender violence, and caste politics in north India. Many accounts emphasize the limited protections available to marginalized women in the region and period, and how those conditions shaped later events.

### Banditry and Notoriety

She became associated with bandit groups operating in the Chambal ravines, an area historically linked to outlaw activity. The Behmai massacre is one of the most cited episodes connected to her name, and remains a focal point of controversy and competing narratives in coverage.

### Surrender and Custody

Her surrender in the early 1980s is frequently described as a highly publicized event involving negotiations with authorities. Secondary sources note that she spent years in custody and that legal proceedings extended over a long period, contributing to her transformation from outlaw figure to political actor.

### Entry into Politics

After release, Phoolan Devi entered electoral politics and served as a Member of Parliament. Profiles often interpret this as a striking example of how notoriety, identity-based mobilization, and local grievance politics can intersect in democratic systems.

### Assassination and Legacy

She was assassinated in 2001 in New Delhi. Her legacy remains contested: she is portrayed variously as a criminal, a symbol of revenge, and a figure of resistance for some communities, with interpretations shaped by caste, gender, and political affiliation.

## Notes on Sources

Popular treatments include biographies, journalism, and film portrayals that may dramatize events. Court records, contemporaneous reporting, and scholarly work on caste and banditry provide more stable grounding.
