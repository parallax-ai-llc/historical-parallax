## Scientific Contributions (High-Level)

- **Solar neutrino detection:** Davis is best known for leading one of the earliest successful efforts to detect neutrinos produced by nuclear fusion reactions in the Sun.
- **Radiochemical method:** His work used a **radiochemical detection** approach: neutrino interactions converted a target nucleus into a different isotope that could be chemically extracted and counted.

## The Homestake Experiment

Davis’s most famous program was the **Homestake Solar Neutrino Experiment** in the Homestake Mine (South Dakota).

- **Why underground:** The detector was placed deep underground to reduce background signals from cosmic rays.
- **What was measured:** A large tank of cleaning fluid (rich in chlorine) was used so that neutrino interactions could produce argon atoms.
- **What it showed:** The measured solar neutrino flux was significantly lower than many theoretical expectations at the time.

## The Solar Neutrino Problem and Resolution

The long-standing mismatch between predicted and detected neutrino counts became known as the **solar neutrino problem**.

- Over time, results from multiple experiments supported the conclusion that the deficit was real.
- Later neutrino physics (including **neutrino oscillations**) explained how neutrinos can change “flavor” between production in the Sun and detection on Earth.

## Recognition and Legacy

- Davis shared the **2002 Nobel Prize in Physics** for pioneering contributions to astrophysical neutrino detection.
- His work helped establish **neutrino astronomy** as a credible way to study processes that are otherwise difficult to observe directly.
