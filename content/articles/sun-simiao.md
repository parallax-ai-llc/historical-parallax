## Summary

Sun Simiao was one of the most revered physicians in Chinese history, known as the 'King of Medicine.' His encyclopedic work Beiji Qianjin Yaofang (Essential Prescriptions Worth a Thousand Gold) compiled medical knowledge from all Chinese medical traditions. He emphasized medical ethics centuries before Hippocratic ideals reached China and lived to over 100 years old.

## Additional context

### Medical writings

- *Beiji qianjin yaofang* (“Essential Prescriptions Worth a Thousand Gold”) is a large compilation that preserved clinical guidance, herbal formulas, and therapeutic approaches from earlier traditions.
- *Qianjin yifang* (“Supplement to Essential Prescriptions Worth a Thousand Gold”) extended the earlier work with additional prescriptions and commentary.
- The books are important historical sources for the study of classical Chinese medicine, including how diagnoses and treatments were organized and taught.

### Ethics and physician conduct

- Sun Simiao is often quoted for writings on the responsibilities of physicians, including diligence, compassion, and an emphasis on treating patients without discrimination.
- These passages are frequently presented as early formulations of medical ethics within Chinese intellectual history.

### Historical context and legacy

- His life spanned the Sui and Tang periods, eras associated with administrative consolidation and cultural flourishing in China.
- Later generations elevated Sun Simiao as a model healer; temples, folklore, and medical lineages sometimes refer to him with honorific titles such as “King of Medicine.”
- Modern discussions of traditional Chinese medicine sometimes cite his works as examples of early encyclopedic synthesis.
