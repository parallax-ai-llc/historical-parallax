## Summary

Afghan ruler who launched 17 devastating raids into India primarily to loot Hindu temples. His destruction of the Somnath temple became one of the most traumatic events in Indian historical memory.

## Historical setting

Mahmud of Ghazni was a leading ruler of the Ghaznavid dynasty, based in Ghazni (in present-day Afghanistan). His reign is commonly placed in the broader expansion of Turkic-Persianate polities across Central and South Asia in the late 10th and early 11th centuries.

## State-building and administration

Sources describe Mahmud as consolidating a militarized state with a strong court and revenue system, supported by campaigns and tribute. Persian administrative and literary culture was influential at the Ghaznavid court, and the dynasty is often discussed as part of the Persianate world.

## Campaigns in South Asia

Mahmud is especially known for repeated expeditions into northern India. Interpretations differ on the mix of strategic aims, frontier politics, and the extraction of wealth, but temple raids and the taking of booty are central elements in many narratives of these campaigns.

## Somnath in historiography

The raid on Somnath has become a frequently referenced episode in later historical memory and political discourse. Modern scholarship often emphasizes the need to separate contemporaneous evidence from later retellings and to situate the event within the norms of medieval warfare and state finance.

## Cultural patronage

Ghaznavid rule is associated with patronage of Persian literature and scholarship. The court's cultural output is sometimes treated as one of the dynasty's enduring legacies, alongside the political and military footprint of its conquests.
