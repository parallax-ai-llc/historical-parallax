## Summary

Marie Joseph Louis Adolphe Thiers (1797-1877) was a French statesman, historian, and journalist who served as the first President of the French Third Republic from 1871 to 1873.[^1] A key figure in the July Revolution of 1830 that overthrew Charles X, Thiers held numerous ministerial positions during the July Monarchy and became one of France's most influential political figures across five decades.[^2] He is best known for his brutal suppression of the Paris Commune in 1871 and for negotiating peace with Germany after the Franco-Prussian War, as well as for his monumental historical works on the French Revolution and Napoleon.[^3] His political legacy remains deeply contested, praised by some as the savior of the Republic and condemned by others as a ruthless reactionary who crushed the working-class movement.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Adolphe Thiers was born in Marseille on April 15, 1797, under circumstances that would mark him throughout his life. He was born out of wedlock, arriving a month before his parents married on May 13, 1797.[^5] His father, Pierre-Louis-Marie Thiers, was a businessman and occasional government official under Napoleon who led a life of debauchery and was frequently in trouble with the law.[^6] Four months after the marriage, his father abandoned both Adolphe and his mother, Marie-Madeleine Amic, leaving them in poverty.[^7] This early abandonment left Thiers with a lifelong inclination to seek support and approval from older women, a psychological tendency that would shape his personal relationships.[^8]

Raised in poverty by his mother and maternal grandmother in Marseille, Thiers nevertheless received a good education thanks to financial aid from an aunt and a godmother.[^9] He won admission to the Lycée of Marseille through competitive examination, demonstrating early the exceptional intelligence and ambition that would define his career.[^10] In November 1815, with help from his relatives, he entered the faculty of law at Aix-en-Provence, where he met François Mignet, who would become a lifelong friend and fellow historian.[^11] Both were admitted to the bar in 1818, though Thiers made only a precarious living as a lawyer for three years, hampered by his unprepossessing physical appearance.[^12]

Thiers was remarkably short, standing just five feet two inches tall, with plain features, ungainly gestures and manners, severe nearsightedness, and a disagreeable, reedy voice.[^13] His almost gnomish appearance and lack of physical presence meant he struggled to attract clients during his brief legal career.[^14] Yet what he lacked in physical stature, he more than compensated for with his intellectual brilliance, tireless work ethic, and burning ambition to overcome the circumstances of his birth.[^15] The experience of poverty, illegitimacy, and paternal abandonment forged in Thiers a determination to succeed that would drive him throughout his extraordinarily long political career, spanning from the 1820s to the 1870s—a period encompassing four changes of regime in France.[^16]

---

## Rise to Power

In 1821, at age 24, Thiers made the decisive move to Paris that would launch his career, becoming a contributor to the influential Liberal newspaper Le Constitutionnel.[^17] Within four months of his arrival in the capital, Thiers was one of the most-read journalists in Paris, writing prolifically about politics, art, literature, and history.[^18] His journalism established his reputation, but it was his historical writing that first brought him fame and financial security. Beginning in 1823, he published his Histoire de la Révolution française (History of the French Revolution) in ten volumes, completed in 1827.[^19] This work was a huge commercial success and made Thiers's name as a historian, particularly popular in liberal circles and among younger Parisians for its praise of the principles and accomplishments of the 1789 Revolution while condemning the monarchy, aristocracy, and clergy.[^20]

The success of his historical work earned Thiers election to the prestigious Académie Française in 1834, cementing his status as one of France's leading intellectuals.[^21] However, it was political journalism rather than historical scholarship that made Thiers a power broker. In January 1830, he helped found Le National, a new opposition newspaper that almost openly advocated a change of dynasty should the reactionary King Charles X attempt to circumscribe public liberties.[^22] When Charles X issued the repressive July Ordinances on July 26, 1830—dissolving the Chamber of Deputies and restricting press freedom—Thiers accused the king of planning a coup d'état and protested vigorously from the pages of Le National.[^23]

During the July Revolution of July 27-29, 1830, Thiers initially took refuge in a Paris suburb, fearing failure and renewed exile.[^24] But on July 29, he returned to the capital to play a crucial role in winning support for Louis-Philippe, Duke of Orléans, as the new constitutional monarch.[^25] Thiers undertook the delicate task of persuading the reluctant duke to accept the crown and succeeded, helping to establish the July Monarchy.[^26] His reward was immediate: he was made a member of the Council of State and elected to the Chamber of Deputies for Aix-en-Provence.[^27] Under the Orleanist monarchy of Louis-Philippe, Thiers rapidly accumulated ministerial positions, serving as undersecretary of state for the treasury (1830), minister of the interior (1832 and 1834-36), minister of trade and public works (1833-34), and prime minister in 1836 and 1840.[^28] During his tenure, he dedicated the Arc de Triomphe and arranged the ceremonial return to France of Napoleon's remains from Saint Helena, powerful symbolic gestures that enhanced his popularity.[^29]

---

## Major Achievements

Thiers's political career was marked by significant accomplishments across multiple domains. As a minister and prime minister during the July Monarchy, he represented the liberal reformist faction known as le parti du Mouvement (the Party of Movement), positioned against the more conservative faction led by François Guizot.[^30] His most enduring legacy from this period was his cultural and symbolic work, including the dedication of the Arc de Triomphe and the repatriation of Napoleon's remains to Les Invalides in 1840, which stirred powerful nationalist sentiments among the French populace.[^31]

As a historian, Thiers produced two monumental works that shaped French historical consciousness for generations. His Histoire de la Révolution française (1823-1827) was the first large-scale history of the French Revolution and remained influential despite its partisan perspective.[^32] Even more ambitious was his twenty-volume Histoire du Consulat et de l'Empire (History of the Consulate and Empire), written between 1845 and 1862, which chronicled the Napoleonic period in exhaustive detail.[^33] While modern historians criticize these works for their diffuseness, bias, and omission of inconvenient facts, they were enormously popular in their time and established Thiers as one of the most significant popular historians of nineteenth-century France.[^34]

Thiers's greatest political achievement came during the crisis that followed France's defeat in the Franco-Prussian War. Elected chief of the executive power of the French Republic by the National Assembly in Bordeaux in February 1871, he faced the monumental task of negotiating peace with a triumphant Germany while simultaneously dealing with revolutionary upheaval in Paris.[^35] Thiers volunteered to negotiate the armistice and successfully concluded the Treaty of Versailles on February 26, 1871, signed with Otto von Bismarck.[^36] Though the terms were harsh—Germany annexed Alsace and half of Lorraine, including Metz, and imposed an indemnity of five billion francs plus occupation costs—Thiers managed to prevent even worse terms and began the process of national reconstruction.[^37]

In August 1871, Thiers was formally named President of the Republic, becoming the first president of the Third French Republic.[^38] His most significant achievement as president was the rapid repayment of the war indemnity to Germany. Though he had initially argued that France could pay only 1.5 billion francs, he committed himself to early repayment of the full five billion.[^39] Through successful bond subscriptions, France succeeded in repaying Germany ahead of schedule in early September 1873, two years before the 1875 deadline.[^40] This remarkable financial achievement allowed the withdrawal of German occupation forces and restored French sovereignty and national pride.[^41] Thiers's political legacy—a combination of patriotism, liberalism, and conservatism—had a profound effect on the establishment and stabilization of the Third Republic.[^42]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his achievements, Thiers's career was marked by profound controversies that continue to divide historical opinion. His most notorious action was the brutal suppression of the Paris Commune in 1871, an event that earned him lasting condemnation from the political left. When the Paris Commune seized power in March 1871, composed largely of workers and radicals who had defended Paris during the Prussian siege, Thiers decided to disarm the National Guard and ordered government troops to suppress the revolutionary government.[^43] After pulling the French national army out of the city on March 18, Thiers methodically planned the reconquest of the capital.[^44]

On May 21, 1871, government troops entered Paris, initiating la semaine sanglante—"Bloody Week"—one of the most violent episodes in French history.[^45] Over the course of the week, regular troops crushed the Communards with overwhelming force. Approximately 20,000 insurrectionists were killed, compared to about 750 government troops.[^46] Thiers ruthlessly used troops to defeat the Paris insurrection, working in concert with the occupying Germans, thus destroying for many years the strength of French Socialist and workers' movements.[^47] The massacre left deep scars in French society and made Thiers a hated figure among leftists and workers, who viewed him as a class traitor willing to collaborate with foreign occupiers to crush French workers.[^48]

Thiers's historical writings also drew substantial criticism from scholars and contemporaries alike. His works suffered from "diffuseness, casuistry, bias against those with whom he disagreed, and omission of inconvenient facts," which evoked protests from many participants in the events he described, who claimed he had treated them and their causes unjustly.[^49] His partisan approach to history reflected his political convictions but compromised the scholarly objectivity that would have given his works lasting academic value.[^50]

His political conduct during the July Monarchy and Second Republic also attracted criticism. During his second term as premier and minister of foreign affairs (1840), his support for the Egyptian pasha Muhammad Ali's territorial ambitions almost led to war with England, forcing his resignation when King Louis-Philippe chose to avert conflict.[^51] Later, Thiers made what many consider the greatest political miscalculation of his career by supporting Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte in the 1848 presidential election, believing he could control the supposedly dim-witted prince (Thiers had called him a "cretin whom we will lead by the nose").[^52] Louis-Napoleon proved himself far more cunning than Thiers anticipated, eventually staging a coup d'état on December 2, 1851, after which Thiers was briefly imprisoned and then forced into exile in Brussels, London, and Switzerland until August 1852.[^53]

Contemporary critics also noted Thiers's difficult personality: his "temper at once imperious and intriguing, his inveterate inclination towards brigue—that is to say, underhand rivalry and caballing for power and place."[^54] His political opportunism and willingness to shift positions for tactical advantage earned him a reputation for untrustworthiness among both allies and adversaries.[^55]

## Personal Life

Thiers's personal life was unconventional and reflected the complexities of his character. Without fortune or social standing, he was helped in his early career by his wealthy married mistress, Eurydice Dosne, who was considerably older than him.[^56] This relationship echoed the psychological pattern established by his mother's early support and his lifelong tendency to seek approval from older women.[^57] In 1833, an unusual marriage was arranged between Thiers and Eurydice's daughter, Elise Dosne, who was only sixteen years old at the time, while Thiers was thirty-six.[^58] This marriage of convenience consolidated his connection to the Dosne family fortune while maintaining his relationship with the mother.[^59]

Thiers and Elise had one daughter, who tragically predeceased him.[^60] He left no other children, though his wife and sister-in-law, Mademoiselle Dosne, were his constant companions throughout his life.[^61] In 1849, a cholera epidemic struck Paris, claiming Thiers's father-in-law among its victims; Thiers and his wife inherited a substantial fortune that secured his financial independence.[^62] This inheritance freed him from economic concerns and allowed him to focus entirely on his political and historical work during the latter part of his career.[^63]

Physically, Thiers cut an unprepossessing figure. His very short stature of five feet two inches, plain features, ungainly gestures and manners, severe nearsightedness requiring thick spectacles, and unpleasant, reedy voice made him "remarkable, and not imposing."[^64] His near-gnomish appearance was the subject of numerous caricatures and political cartoons.[^65] Yet what he lacked in physical presence, he compensated for with extraordinary intellectual energy, an encyclopedic memory, conversational brilliance, and relentless ambition.[^66]

His personality was marked by contradictions. He could be charming and persuasive in conversation, but also imperious and manipulative in political dealings.[^67] His inveterate tendency toward intrigue and behind-the-scenes maneuvering for power made him both an effective political operator and a figure of distrust.[^68] Despite his ambition and often ruthless political conduct, Thiers maintained deep and lasting friendships, particularly with his fellow historian François Mignet, whom he had known since his student days in Aix-en-Provence.[^69] These friendships, along with his devotion to his wife and extended family, revealed a capacity for loyalty and affection that contrasted with his public image as a cold political calculator.[^70]

## Legacy

Adolphe Thiers died on September 3, 1877, at the age of eighty.[^71] His death became a major political event in France, with his funeral transforming into a massive demonstration of republican solidarity. The funeral procession was led by two of the most prominent leaders of the republican movement: the celebrated writer Victor Hugo and the politician Léon Gambetta, who at the time of Thiers's death were his allies against conservative monarchist forces.[^72] The state funeral was lavish, reflecting Thiers's status as a founding father of the Third Republic.[^73]

Thiers's legacy is profoundly contradictory, reflecting the tumultuous nature of nineteenth-century French politics. As a founder and first president of the Third Republic, he played a crucial role in establishing republican institutions after the collapse of the Second Empire.[^74] His successful negotiation of peace with Germany and rapid repayment of the war indemnity were genuine achievements that helped restore French sovereignty and national pride during an exceptionally difficult period.[^75] The Third Republic that he helped establish would endure until 1940, making it the longest-lasting French regime since the Revolution of 1789.[^76]

His exceptionally long career, spanning from the 1820s to the 1870s and surviving four regime changes, indicates remarkable political adaptability and reveals the underlying continuities in French politics despite surface transformations.[^77] Thiers exemplified the close connections between journalism, historical writing, and politics that characterized nineteenth-century France, demonstrating how intellectual work and political power could reinforce each other.[^78] His model of the "literary statesman" influenced subsequent generations of French political figures.[^79]

However, Thiers's legacy is indelibly stained by his suppression of the Paris Commune. For the French left, workers' movements, and socialists, Thiers remains the "butcher of the Commune," the man who massacred 20,000 Parisians and destroyed the workers' movement for a generation.[^80] Karl Marx and other socialist theorists condemned Thiers as the embodiment of bourgeois reaction, willing to collaborate with foreign occupiers to crush domestic working-class revolution.[^81] This aspect of his legacy ensured that he would never be a unifying national figure but would remain a symbol of class conflict.[^82]

His historical works, once immensely popular, have not maintained their scholarly reputation. Modern historians criticize them for partisan bias, factual omissions, and lack of analytical rigor, though they acknowledge their importance as primary sources reflecting the political consciousness of French liberals in the early nineteenth century.[^83] Nevertheless, these massive works remain monuments to nineteenth-century historical writing and demonstrate Thiers's remarkable industry and literary productivity.[^84]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Admirers of Thiers emphasize his crucial role in establishing the Third Republic and preventing a monarchist restoration in the 1870s. He is credited as "the most gifted of the literary statesmen who were an important feature of 19th-century French political life."[^85] His defenders point to his genuine patriotism, demonstrated by his negotiation of the best possible terms with Germany in 1871 and his successful early repayment of the war indemnity that freed France from occupation.[^86]

Supporters argue that his suppression of the Commune, while brutal, prevented civil war and possible foreign intervention that could have destroyed France entirely.[^87] They maintain that he faced an impossible situation in 1871, with France defeated, occupied, and on the brink of disintegration, and that his decisive action, however harsh, saved the nation and allowed the establishment of stable republican government.[^88] His political legacy of combining patriotism, liberalism, and conservatism provided a framework for the Third Republic that allowed it to endure for seventy years.[^89]

His historical works, despite their flaws, are recognized for making the history of the French Revolution and Napoleonic period accessible to a broad public audience and for shaping French historical consciousness.[^90] His election to the Académie Française in 1834 and the enormous commercial success of his books testify to his impact as a public intellectual.[^91] As a self-made man who overcame illegitimate birth, poverty, and physical disadvantages through intelligence and determination, Thiers embodied the liberal ideal of meritocracy and individual achievement.[^92]

Scholars recognize that his skills were most effective in opposition, as a journalist, critic, and parliamentary debater, where his intellectual brilliance and rhetorical abilities could shine.[^93] His ability to survive and thrive across multiple regimes demonstrates remarkable political intelligence and adaptability.[^94] His famous saying, "The king reigns but does not govern," articulated an important principle of constitutional monarchy that influenced political thought throughout Europe.[^95]

### Negative Views

Critics of Thiers view him as a political opportunist who betrayed every principle for personal advancement and power. The massacre of the Commune remains the central indictment: his willingness to kill 20,000 French citizens, working in collaboration with the occupying German army, reveals him as a reactionary committed to preserving bourgeois class power at any cost.[^96] Socialist historians argue that Thiers deliberately provoked the Commune by attempting to disarm the National Guard, then used the uprising as a pretext to crush the workers' movement and eliminate the possibility of social revolution in France.[^97]

His historical works are criticized as partisan propaganda disguised as scholarship, suffering from "diffuseness, casuistry, bias against those with whom he disagreed, and omission of inconvenient facts."[^98] Many participants in the events he described protested that he had treated them and their causes unjustly, manipulating history to serve his political agenda.[^99] Modern historians find his works unreliable as objective historical sources, though valuable as documents of liberal political ideology.[^100]

Critics point to his disastrous miscalculation in supporting Louis-Napoleon in 1848, which he believed he could control, only to be outmaneuvered and forced into exile after Napoleon's coup.[^101] This episode reveals both his political arrogance and his poor judgment of character.[^102] His nearly provoking war with England in 1840 through reckless foreign policy demonstrates similar poor judgment in international affairs.[^103]

His personality—marked by "temper at once imperious and intriguing, his inveterate inclination towards brigue"—made him a difficult colleague and untrustworthy ally.[^104] His constant scheming and maneuvering for power and position earned him widespread distrust across the political spectrum.[^105] Even physical descriptions emphasize his unprepossessing appearance and disagreeable voice, suggesting that contemporaries found him personally unappealing as well as politically suspect.[^106]

For the left, Thiers represents everything wrong with bourgeois liberalism: rhetorical commitment to liberty and progress combined with brutal repression of workers and the poor when class interests are threatened.[^107] His legacy as the butcher of the Commune ensures that he will never be rehabilitated in progressive or socialist historical memory.[^108]

## Famous Quotes

> "The king reigns but does not govern."

This is Thiers's most famous saying, articulating the principle of constitutional monarchy that influenced political thought throughout nineteenth-century Europe.[^109]

> "In politics it is necessary to take nothing tragically and everything seriously."

This quote reflects Thiers's pragmatic and calculated approach to political life, emphasizing the need for emotional detachment combined with careful attention to detail.[^110]

> "For those who govern, the first thing required is indifference to newspapers."

An ironic observation from a man who made his career in journalism and used newspapers as instruments of political power.[^111]

> "There are no more mistakes for you to commit." (Il n'y a plus une seule faute à commettre)

Concluding words from his famous speech of March 14, 1867, warning the government about the perils of their policies.[^112]

> "A cretin whom we will lead by the nose."

Thiers's disastrously mistaken assessment of Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte in 1848, reflecting his political arrogance and poor judgment of character.[^113]
