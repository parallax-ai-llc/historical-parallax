## Summary

Toxic chemicals in humidifier disinfectants killed over 1,600 people and injured 6,000 in South Korea, mostly children and pregnant women. Companies including Oxy hid the dangers.

## Background (High Level)

- Household humidifiers are commonly used in South Korea, particularly during dry seasons.
- Some products were marketed as “disinfectants” or additives intended to keep humidifier water clean.
- In retrospect, this created a route for inhalation exposure when aerosolized particles were dispersed into indoor air.

## Public Health Response

- Reports of unusual lung injuries and deaths led to scientific and governmental investigation.
- The issue is often cited as a major consumer-safety failure involving chemicals used in everyday products.
- Public attention grew around the lag between early harm signals and effective regulatory action.

## Accountability and Legal Proceedings

- The scandal became associated with investigations into corporate conduct, product safety testing, and disclosure.
- Criminal prosecutions and civil actions were pursued over subsequent years.
- Public discussions also focused on institutional responsibility: regulation, oversight, and risk communication.

## Ongoing Issues

- Recognition of victims and eligibility criteria have been recurring points of contention.
- Compensation frameworks and long-term health monitoring have been central to remediation efforts.
- The case influenced broader debates about chemical safety standards and product approval processes.
