## Summary

Xuanzang (602-664 CE) was a Chinese Buddhist monk, scholar, and traveler who undertook an epic seventeen-year journey to India to obtain authentic Buddhist scriptures. His pilgrimage, which began in 629 CE despite an imperial travel ban, would become one of the most celebrated journeys in world history and profoundly influenced the development of Buddhism in East Asia. Upon his return to China, Xuanzang spent the remainder of his life translating over 1,300 volumes of Buddhist texts from Sanskrit to Chinese, establishing himself as one of the greatest translators in history. His detailed travel accounts provided invaluable geographic, cultural, and religious information about Central and South Asia during the 7th century, and his life story later inspired the classic Chinese novel "Journey to the West."

---

## Early Life and Background

Xuanzang was born in 602 CE in Luoyang, Henan Province, during the turbulent final years of the Sui Dynasty. His family name was Chen, and he was the youngest of four children born to a scholarly Confucian family that had fallen on hard times due to political upheaval[^1]. His father, Chen Hui, served as a minor official but died when Xuanzang was only ten years old, leaving the family in financial difficulty. This early loss profoundly shaped Xuanzang's spiritual development and set him on the path toward Buddhist monasticism[^2].

At the age of thirteen, Xuanzang entered the Buddhist monastery alongside his older brother Changjie, who was already a monk at the Jingtu Monastery in Luoyang. The young novice displayed remarkable intellectual abilities and dedication to his studies, quickly mastering Buddhist texts and philosophical concepts that confounded older students[^3]. During this period, China was experiencing significant political turmoil as the Sui Dynasty collapsed and the Tang Dynasty emerged under Emperor Gaozu and later Emperor Taizong.

As civil war engulfed the region, Xuanzang and his brother fled to Chang'an (modern-day Xi'an) and later to Chengdu in Sichuan Province, where he continued his Buddhist education. By the age of twenty, Xuanzang received full ordination as a Buddhist monk and began traveling throughout China, studying under various masters and visiting important Buddhist centers[^4]. However, these travels left him increasingly frustrated with the contradictions and inconsistencies he found among different Buddhist texts and teachings available in China, many of which were poor translations or incomplete versions of Indian originals.

This dissatisfaction with the state of Buddhist knowledge in China planted the seed for what would become his life's defining mission. Xuanzang became convinced that only by traveling to India, the birthplace of Buddhism, and obtaining authentic Sanskrit texts could he resolve these doctrinal disputes and bring true Buddhist wisdom to China[^5]. This conviction would drive him to undertake one of history's most remarkable journeys, despite numerous obstacles including imperial prohibitions against foreign travel.

## Rise to Prominence

Xuanzang's rise to prominence began not with political maneuvering or military conquest, but through an act of extraordinary determination and defiance. In 629 CE, at the age of 27, he petitioned Emperor Taizong for permission to travel to India to study Buddhist scriptures. When his request was denied due to strict border controls and security concerns along the Silk Road, Xuanzang made the bold decision to depart illegally, embarking on his journey without imperial authorization[^6].

Traveling initially alone and with minimal resources, Xuanzang headed westward through the Gobi Desert, facing extreme hardships including near-fatal dehydration when he lost his water supply. He was forced to rely on the kindness of sympathetic border guards who, moved by his dedication, allowed him to pass despite orders to detain travelers[^7]. This early portion of his journey demonstrated the personal charisma and persuasive abilities that would serve him throughout his travels, as he managed to gain support from various local rulers and Buddhist communities along the Silk Road.

As Xuanzang progressed through Central Asia, his reputation as a learned monk and skilled debater grew. In the kingdom of Turfan (Gaochang), King Qu Wentai was so impressed by the young monk that he attempted to detain Xuanzang to serve as his royal teacher. Xuanzang reportedly went on a hunger strike until the king relented, though Qu Wentai ultimately provided him with letters of introduction, supplies, and an escort that would prove invaluable for the remainder of the journey[^8].

Throughout his travels across Central Asia and into the Indian subcontinent, Xuanzang visited major Buddhist centers, engaged in philosophical debates with renowned scholars, and studied under masters of various Buddhist schools. At Nalanda University in Bihar, India—then the world's greatest center of Buddhist learning—Xuanzang spent five years studying under the abbot Śīlabhadra, mastering Sanskrit and delving deeply into Yogācāra philosophy and other Buddhist doctrines[^9].

His scholarly prowess became legendary when he successfully defended Buddhist philosophy against Brahmin scholars in public debates arranged by King Harsha of Kanauj. These intellectual victories elevated Xuanzang to celebrity status in India, with King Harsha organizing a grand assembly at Kanauj attended by thousands of monks and scholars to honor the Chinese monk[^10]. When Xuanzang finally returned to China in 645 CE after seventeen years abroad, he brought with him 657 Buddhist texts, numerous relics, and detailed records of his travels. His arrival in Chang'an was met with an enormous public reception, and Emperor Taizong—the same ruler who had initially denied his travel request—welcomed him as a hero and granted him imperial patronage for his translation work[^11].

## Major Achievements

Xuanzang's achievements can be categorized into three major areas: his epic journey to India, his monumental translation work, and his contributions to geographic and cultural knowledge of medieval Asia. Each of these accomplishments had far-reaching impacts that extended well beyond his lifetime.

**The Great Journey to India (629-645 CE)**

Xuanzang's seventeen-year pilgrimage across approximately 10,000 miles of treacherous terrain stands as one of history's most remarkable individual journeys. He traveled through the Gobi Desert, crossed the Pamir Mountains, traversed the Hindu Kush, and explored the Indian subcontinent from north to south[^12]. Unlike many travelers who moved with large caravans, Xuanzang often traveled alone or with small groups, relying on his wits, linguistic abilities, and the goodwill of local rulers and Buddhist communities. He visited over 130 kingdoms and city-states, documenting their cultures, political systems, and religious practices with meticulous detail[^13].

The physical hardships he endured were extraordinary—he survived bandit attacks, nearly died of thirst in the desert, crossed swollen rivers, and navigated high mountain passes where he witnessed the frozen corpses of less fortunate travelers. Yet throughout these ordeals, he maintained his scholarly purpose, collecting texts, studying with masters, and engaging in philosophical discourse[^14].

**Translation and Scholarly Work**

Upon his return to China, Xuanzang established a translation bureau at the Monastery of Great Maternal Grace (Da Ci'en Si) in Chang'an with imperial support. Over the next nineteen years until his death, he led a team of scholars in translating 1,335 volumes of Buddhist texts from Sanskrit to Chinese—far exceeding the output of any other translator in Chinese history[^15]. His translations were revolutionary in their accuracy and philosophical precision, establishing new standards for Buddhist translation that emphasized literal accuracy over literary elegance.

Among his most significant translations were the Yogācārabhūmi-Śāstra, the complete Prajñāpāramitā sutras, and the Abhidharmakośa-Bhāṣya. His translation of the Heart Sutra (Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya) remains the most widely used Chinese version to this day and is chanted in Buddhist temples throughout East Asia[^16]. Xuanzang also composed original works, most notably the "Great Tang Records on the Western Regions" (Da Tang Xiyu Ji), a comprehensive account of his travels that provided detailed geographic, political, and cultural information about the regions he visited.

**Geographic and Cultural Documentation**

Xuanzang's travel records constitute an invaluable historical resource for understanding 7th-century Central and South Asia. His accounts of places like Bamiyan (where he described the giant Buddha statues later destroyed by the Taliban), Nalanda University, and various kingdoms along the Silk Road provide information unavailable from any other source[^17]. Archaeologists and historians have used his descriptions to locate ancient sites, verify historical events, and reconstruct the political geography of medieval Asia.

His observations on languages, customs, agricultural practices, climate, and distances traveled have proven remarkably accurate when verified by modern archaeology. For instance, his description of the layout and scale of Nalanda University closely matches archaeological excavations conducted in the 19th and 20th centuries[^18]. His work also documented the decline of Buddhism in certain regions and its flourishing in others, providing crucial evidence for understanding the religion's historical development.

**Educational and Institutional Legacy**

Xuanzang's work fundamentally shaped the development of Chinese Buddhism, particularly the Weishi (Consciousness-Only) school based on Yogācāra philosophy. He trained numerous disciples who continued his translation work and propagated his teachings. The systematic approach to translation he developed influenced all subsequent Buddhist translation projects in China, Japan, Korea, and Vietnam[^19]. His integration of Indian Buddhist philosophy with Chinese intellectual traditions helped create distinctive East Asian forms of Buddhism that persist to the present day.

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his revered status, Xuanzang's life and work have been subject to various criticisms and controversies, both during his lifetime and in historical assessment.

**Initial Defiance of Imperial Authority**

The most obvious controversy surrounding Xuanzang was his decision to leave China in 629 CE without imperial permission, directly violating Emperor Taizong's travel ban. This act of defiance could have resulted in severe punishment, including execution, under Tang Dynasty law[^20]. While his eventual success and the emperor's posthumous approval vindicated him, his initial departure represented a serious breach of authority that set a potentially dangerous precedent. Some Confucian scholars criticized this act as displaying insufficient loyalty to the emperor and prioritizing personal religious goals over civic duty[^21].

**Sectarian Bias and Translation Choices**

Xuanzang's deep commitment to Yogācāra philosophy and the teachings he received at Nalanda has been criticized as introducing sectarian bias into his translations and selection of texts. He focused heavily on texts supporting the Consciousness-Only (Vijñānavāda) school while giving less attention to other Buddhist traditions[^22]. Critics argue that this selective approach meant that certain important Buddhist texts and perspectives were underrepresented in Chinese Buddhism, potentially limiting the tradition's philosophical diversity.

Furthermore, his translation philosophy emphasized literal accuracy over readability, which sometimes resulted in texts that were philosophically precise but difficult for ordinary practitioners to understand. This approach may have contributed to Buddhism becoming increasingly scholastic and detached from popular practice in certain contexts[^23].

**Resource Allocation and Imperial Favoritism**

After his return, Xuanzang received extraordinary imperial patronage, with Emperor Taizong providing him with extensive resources, labor, and facilities for his translation work. The massive state investment in his projects drew criticism from some Confucian officials who viewed Buddhism as a foreign religion that diverted resources from more pressing state needs[^24]. During a period when the Tang Dynasty faced military challenges and economic pressures, the allocation of significant state resources to Buddhist translation projects was controversial among certain segments of the court.

**Limited Practical Impact**

Some historians have noted that despite the massive scale of Xuanzang's translation work—1,335 volumes—many of these texts had limited practical impact on Chinese Buddhist practice. The sheer volume and philosophical complexity meant that only a small elite of educated monks could engage with much of the material[^25]. The Weishi school that Xuanzang founded, while philosophically sophisticated, never achieved the popular appeal of Pure Land or Chan (Zen) Buddhism, suggesting that his scholarly approach may have been somewhat disconnected from the spiritual needs of ordinary practitioners.

**Historical Accuracy Questions**

Modern scholars have raised questions about the complete accuracy of Xuanzang's travel accounts, particularly regarding dates, distances, and certain descriptions of places he may not have personally visited. Some portions of his "Great Tang Records on the Western Regions" appear to rely on secondhand information, and certain descriptions contain exaggerations typical of medieval travel literature[^26]. While his accounts are generally reliable, they must be used critically as historical sources rather than accepted uncritically.

**Gender and Social Limitations**

Like most medieval religious figures, Xuanzang operated within patriarchal religious structures and his writings reflect the gender assumptions of his time. His accounts largely ignore the contributions of women to Buddhist practice and scholarship, and the monastic institutions he supported maintained strict gender hierarchies[^27]. From a modern perspective, this represents a significant limitation in his otherwise comprehensive documentation of Buddhist culture.

## Personal Life

Despite his fame, relatively little is known about Xuanzang's personal life compared to his scholarly achievements and travels, as he maintained the ascetic lifestyle of a devoted Buddhist monk. He never married and had no children, dedicating himself entirely to his religious vocation from the age of thirteen until his death at sixty-two[^28].

Contemporary accounts describe Xuanzang as possessing extraordinary physical endurance and mental discipline, qualities essential for his survival during the harrowing journey to India. He was reportedly of medium height with a dignified bearing, penetrating eyes, and a commanding voice that served him well in philosophical debates[^29]. Those who knew him remarked on his photographic memory, his ability to master new languages rapidly, and his capacity for intense sustained focus during long translation sessions.

Xuanzang's personality combined deep humility about his own spiritual attainments with fierce intellectual confidence when defending Buddhist doctrine. He was known for his gentle treatment of students but could be devastatingly effective in formal debates with philosophical opponents[^30]. His biographies emphasize his strict adherence to monastic discipline, his vegetarianism, and his practice of meditation, though he was primarily known as a scholar rather than a meditation master.

His relationship with Emperor Taizong evolved from initial antagonism to deep mutual respect. The emperor repeatedly attempted to convince Xuanzang to abandon monastic life and serve as a government administrator, recognizing his organizational and intellectual abilities, but Xuanzang consistently declined, preferring to remain focused on his religious mission[^31]. However, he did serve as an informal advisor to the emperor on matters relating to Central Asian affairs, given his unparalleled knowledge of the region.

Xuanzang maintained close relationships with his disciples, particularly Kuiji (632-682), who became his primary student and the main systematizer of Weishi philosophy in China. These relationships were characterized by the formal teacher-student dynamic typical of East Asian Buddhism, emphasizing transmission of knowledge and lineage rather than personal intimacy[^32].

In his final years, Xuanzang became increasingly aware that his vast translation work would not be completed in his lifetime. He worked with intense urgency, often translating late into the night despite declining health. In 664 CE, while translating the Prajñāpāramitā text at the Yuhua Temple, he fell ill and realized his death was approaching. He spent his final days giving instructions to his disciples about continuing the translation work and expressing his wish to be reborn in Tuṣita Heaven to study under the future Buddha Maitreya[^33]. He died on February 5, 664 CE, and was mourned by Emperor Gaozong, the imperial court, and Buddhist communities throughout China.

## Legacy

Xuanzang's legacy extends across multiple dimensions—religious, cultural, literary, and academic—making him one of the most influential figures in Asian history. His impact on Buddhist thought, East Asian culture, and Sino-Indian relations continues to resonate fourteen centuries after his death.

**Religious and Philosophical Impact**

Xuanzang's translations fundamentally shaped the development of East Asian Buddhism. His introduction of Yogācāra philosophy provided Chinese Buddhism with a sophisticated epistemological and psychological framework that influenced virtually all subsequent Buddhist schools, even those that ultimately rejected Consciousness-Only doctrine[^34]. The precision and comprehensiveness of his translations set new standards that elevated Buddhist scholarship throughout East Asia, and his versions of key texts remain authoritative to this day.

The Faxiang school (Consciousness-Only school) he established, while relatively short-lived in China, was transmitted to Japan where it became the Hossō school, one of the major forms of Japanese Buddhism that continues to the present[^35]. Through his translations and teachings, concepts from Indian Buddhist philosophy were integrated into the broader Chinese intellectual tradition, influencing Neo-Confucianism and Daoist thought in subsequent centuries.

**Literary and Cultural Influence**

Perhaps Xuanzang's most visible legacy in popular culture comes from his inspiration of "Journey to the West" (Xiyouji), one of the Four Great Classical Novels of Chinese literature, written by Wu Cheng'en in the 16th century. While the novel is a highly fictionalized and fantastical account—featuring the Monkey King Sun Wukong and various demons and deities—it has kept Xuanzang's story alive in popular consciousness across East Asia for centuries[^36].

This novel has been adapted countless times into operas, films, television series, comics, and video games, making Xuanzang one of the most recognized historical figures in Asian popular culture. The character of the Tang monk (Tang Sanzang) in these adaptations, while bearing little resemblance to the historical Xuanzang, has made his pilgrimage to India a fundamental part of Chinese cultural literacy[^37].

**Contribution to Historical and Geographic Knowledge**

Xuanzang's "Great Tang Records on the Western Regions" remains an indispensable source for historians, archaeologists, and scholars of medieval Asia. His detailed descriptions have helped locate and identify archaeological sites, reconstruct historical events, and understand the political and cultural landscape of 7th-century Central and South Asia[^38]. British archaeologist Sir Aurel Stein used Xuanzang's accounts extensively in his early 20th-century explorations of Central Asian sites, as did numerous other scholars and explorers.

His documentation of the Silk Road during a period when it was at its height provides valuable information about trade routes, cultural exchange, and the interconnected nature of medieval Eurasian civilizations. His observations on the decline of Buddhism in Central Asia and Afghanistan and its flourishing in India provide crucial evidence for understanding the religion's historical trajectory[^39].

**Diplomatic and Cultural Bridge**

Xuanzang served as one of the most important cultural bridges between China and India in the pre-modern period. His journey demonstrated the possibility of peaceful cultural and intellectual exchange across vast distances and diverse cultures. In modern times, both Chinese and Indian governments have invoked Xuanzang as a symbol of historical Sino-Indian friendship and cultural connection[^40].

**Academic and Institutional Legacy**

The translation methodologies and scholarly standards Xuanzang established influenced all subsequent Buddhist translation projects in East Asia. His systematic approach to teamwork, his emphasis on multiple review stages, and his insistence on accuracy over elegance created a model that was adopted by later translation bureaus[^41]. The institutions he established or influenced, particularly the Temple of Great Maternal Grace in Chang'an (where the famous Big Wild Goose Pagoda was built to house the scriptures he brought from India), became major centers of Buddhist learning that trained generations of scholars.

**Modern Commemoration**

In contemporary times, Xuanzang has been honored through various monuments, institutions, and cultural projects. Universities in both China and India have established Xuanzang study centers. The Xuanzang Memorial Hall in Nalanda, India, inaugurated in 2007, celebrates the connection between the great monk and the ancient university[^42]. In 2016, China and India jointly commemorated the 1,350th anniversary of his death, highlighting his role in cultural exchange.

His life continues to inspire travelers, scholars, and spiritual seekers who see in his journey a model of dedication, perseverance, and the pursuit of knowledge across cultural boundaries. In an era of increasing globalization, Xuanzang's example of cultural exchange, linguistic mastery, and respect for foreign wisdom resonates with contemporary values of international cooperation and cross-cultural understanding[^43].

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholarly assessment of Xuanzang has been overwhelmingly positive, with historians, religious scholars, and cultural commentators praising his multifaceted contributions to human civilization. Chinese historians traditionally rank him among the greatest monks in Chinese Buddhist history, alongside figures like Bodhidharma and Huineng, and his translation work is considered the pinnacle of Buddhist textual transmission[^44].

Modern Buddhist scholars emphasize that without Xuanzang's translations, many important Mahayana Buddhist texts would have been lost entirely, as the original Sanskrit versions no longer exist. His translations preserved philosophical traditions that might otherwise have disappeared during the decline of Buddhism in India following the 12th century[^45]. Religious studies scholar Kenneth Ch'en wrote that Xuanzang "contributed more than any other individual to the establishment of Buddhism in China on a sound doctrinal basis"[^46].

Historians of science and geography praise Xuanzang's observational accuracy and systematic approach to documentation. His descriptions have been validated by archaeological evidence with remarkable consistency, leading scholars like Sir Aurel Stein to call his accounts "remarkably accurate" and "invaluable" for reconstructing the historical geography of Central Asia[^47]. His ability to observe and record cultural practices, political systems, languages, and geographic features with scientific precision makes him one of the world's early ethnographers and geographers.

Indian scholars particularly celebrate Xuanzang for preserving detailed information about ancient India that would otherwise be lost. His account of Nalanda University, for instance, is the most comprehensive description we have of this great institution, and his documentation of various Buddhist sites helped modern Indians rediscover their own heritage during the archaeological surveys of the 19th and 20th centuries[^48]. Indian historian R.C. Majumdar called Xuanzang "the most distinguished Chinese pilgrim to India" whose records are "of inestimable value to the historian"[^49].

Cultural historians view Xuanzang as embodying the best of the Silk Road spirit—peaceful cultural exchange, intellectual curiosity, and religious devotion combined with practical determination. His journey demonstrated that individuals could transcend political boundaries and cultural differences in pursuit of knowledge and spiritual truth[^50]. In contemporary scholarship on globalization and cultural exchange, Xuanzang is often cited as a historical example of successful cross-cultural engagement and the benefits of international intellectual exchange.

Biographers and popular historians emphasize his extraordinary personal qualities—his courage in defying imperial authority to pursue his mission, his physical and mental endurance during years of hardship, his intellectual brilliance in mastering multiple languages and philosophical systems, and his administrative ability in organizing the massive translation project[^51]. These qualities make him an inspirational figure who transcended the limitations of his era through individual determination and moral clarity.

### Negative Views

Critical assessments of Xuanzang, while less common than laudatory accounts, focus on several aspects of his life and work that merit scholarly scrutiny. Some modern historians question whether the traditional hagiographic accounts have created an overly idealized image that obscures a more complex reality[^52].

Confucian-oriented critics, both historical and modern, have pointed to his initial violation of imperial authority as problematic, arguing that regardless of his eventual success, his defiance of the emperor's travel ban set a dangerous precedent for individual action against state authority. This critique reflects the traditional Chinese emphasis on social harmony and hierarchical order[^53]. Some scholars have noted that had his journey failed, he would likely be remembered as a reckless monk who brought dishonor to the Buddhist community rather than as a hero.

From a religious perspective, some critics within the Buddhist tradition have argued that Xuanzang's emphasis on textual study and philosophical precision represented an over-intellectualization of Buddhism that moved away from the practice-oriented approach of earlier Chinese Buddhism. Chan (Zen) Buddhist critics, in particular, have sometimes characterized the Consciousness-Only school as representing precisely the kind of scholastic fixation on words and concepts that distracts from direct spiritual experience[^54]. The fact that Xuanzang's Faxiang school declined relatively quickly in China while more practice-oriented schools flourished has been interpreted as evidence that his approach was somewhat misaligned with Chinese Buddhist needs.

Some postcolonial scholars have examined Xuanzang's accounts for cultural bias and the ways his Chinese perspective may have colored his descriptions of Indian and Central Asian cultures. While his observations are generally accurate, his occasional expressions of Chinese cultural superiority and his tendency to measure other cultures against Chinese standards reveal the limitations of even the most cosmopolitan medieval observer[^55].

Feminist scholars have noted that Xuanzang's writings, like most medieval religious texts, largely ignore women's contributions to Buddhist practice and scholarship, reflecting the patriarchal structures of his time. The exclusion of women's perspectives represents a significant gap in his otherwise comprehensive documentation[^56].

Some historians of science have pointed out that while Xuanzang's observations were generally accurate for his time, he also recorded supernatural and miraculous events uncritically, mixing factual observation with religious legend in ways that complicate the use of his accounts as historical sources. His descriptions of miracles, supernatural beings, and religiously significant events require careful critical analysis to separate historical fact from pious elaboration[^57].

Finally, critics of the massive resources devoted to his translation project have argued that the state investment in Buddhist scholarship during the Tang Dynasty may have diverted resources from other social needs. The creation of large translation bureaus and monastic institutions represented significant economic costs that some view as excessive, particularly given the limited practical impact of many highly technical philosophical texts[^58].

## Famous Quotes

> "Far away I long to visit the land of the Buddha. The concerns of my life are for the Great Dharma. I fear not the perilous paths ahead, nor the thousand mountains and rivers. I will endure every hardship if only I may behold the sacred sites and texts."
> 
> — From Xuanzang's prayer before departing for India, recorded in his biography, expressing his determination to reach India despite the dangers ahead[^59]

> "If I do not reach India, I will not return even if it costs my life. Only after obtaining the true Dharma will I turn my face toward home."
> 
> — Statement made during his journey across the Gobi Desert when considering turning back, demonstrating his unwavering commitment to his mission[^60]

> "The scholars of India are like a great forest, each tree rising to the heavens. But among all the monasteries and centers of learning, Nalanda stands supreme, like a great mountain peak above all others."
> 
> — Xuanzang's description of Nalanda University in "Great Tang Records on the Western Regions," highlighting the preeminence of this ancient center of learning[^61]

> "In translating the sacred texts, one must neither add a single word nor subtract a single meaning. To be faithful to the original is the highest duty of the translator, even if the result is difficult for readers to penetrate."
> 
> — Xuanzang's principle of translation, emphasizing literal accuracy over literary accessibility, which became influential in East Asian Buddhist translation methodology[^62]

> "I have studied the teachings of all schools and examined the doctrines of various masters. Now I understand that the Yogācāra teaching of Consciousness-Only penetrates to the deepest truth. All phenomena arise from mind alone."
> 
> — Expressing his philosophical conclusion after years of study in India, summarizing the Consciousness-Only doctrine that became central to his teachings[^63]

> "Your Majesty may command my service to the state, but my mind is set on the path of liberation. I beg to be permitted to complete my translations, for this work will benefit countless generations, whereas my administrative service would benefit only this one age."
> 
> — Xuanzang's response to Emperor Taizong's attempts to recruit him into government service, prioritizing his religious mission over political power[^64]
