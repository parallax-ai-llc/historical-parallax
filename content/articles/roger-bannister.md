## Summary

Roger Bannister ran the first sub-4-minute mile on May 6, 1954, one of the most iconic moments in sports history.

## The Four-Minute Mile

Before 1954, the four-minute barrier was widely treated as a physiological and psychological limit for middle-distance runners.
Bannister’s run at Oxford’s Iffley Road track was carefully planned with pacing support and favorable conditions.
Soon after, other athletes also broke four minutes, reinforcing the idea that perceived limits can shift once shown to be attainable.

## Training and Approach

Unlike many elite runners, Bannister balanced athletics with demanding medical studies.
His preparation emphasized efficiency: high-intensity sessions and race-specific work rather than very high weekly mileage.
The achievement is often cited in discussions of sports science, pacing, and performance psychology.

## Medical and Academic Career

After his running career, Bannister became a neurologist and held senior roles in British medicine.
He contributed to clinical practice and leadership in healthcare, and his public profile helped popularize the idea of athlete-scholars.

## Legacy

Bannister’s mile is remembered as a defining milestone in modern athletics.
The “Miracle Mile” race later in 1954, against Australian runner John Landy, became another famous chapter in mile-running history.
His story continues to be used as a metaphor for breakthrough achievement in sports and beyond.
