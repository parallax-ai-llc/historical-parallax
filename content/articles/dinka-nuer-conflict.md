## Summary

The power struggle between President Kiir (Dinka) and VP Machar (Nuer) triggered a civil war along ethnic lines. Both sides committed systematic atrocities against civilians.

## Background

South Sudan became independent in 2011 after decades of war with Sudan. Soon after independence, political competition inside the ruling Sudan People's Liberation Movement (SPLM) intensified, overlapping with existing community tensions and the legacies of earlier armed mobilization.

The terms “Dinka” and “Nuer” refer to two of South Sudan’s largest ethnic groups, but the conflict is widely described as political in origin, with ethnic identity becoming a major driver of mobilization, targeting, and retaliation once violence escalated.

## Outbreak and Escalation (2013)

Fighting in December 2013 in Juba rapidly spread to multiple regions. Armed forces, allied militias, and opposition groups fractured and re-formed across shifting front lines. Many accounts emphasize cycles of reprisal attacks, atrocities against civilians, and the displacement of large populations.

## Humanitarian and Regional Dimensions

The civil war produced severe humanitarian consequences, including:

- Large-scale internal displacement and refugees to neighboring countries.
- Food insecurity and repeated warnings of famine conditions.
- Widespread sexual violence and attacks on civilian sites.

International actors, including the United Nations Mission in South Sudan (UNMISS), operated protection sites and supported humanitarian access, while also documenting abuses.

## Peace Processes and Power-Sharing

Regional mediation efforts, often associated with IGAD (Intergovernmental Authority on Development), led to multiple ceasefire and power-sharing attempts. The 2018 agreement is often referenced as a major milestone, but implementation has been described as uneven, with persistent insecurity, contested governance arrangements, and delays in security-sector integration.

## Significance

The Dinka–Nuer conflict is frequently cited as a case in which elite political rivalry contributed to state fragmentation, with long-lasting impacts on national institutions, community relations, and prospects for durable peace.
