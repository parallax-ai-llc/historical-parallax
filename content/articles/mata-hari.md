## Summary

Dutch exotic dancer and courtesan who was convicted of spying for Germany during WWI. She used her social connections to gather intelligence. Her guilt remains debated. Executed by French firing squad. She blew a kiss to her executioners.

## Background

Mata Hari was the stage name of **Margaretha Geertruida Zelle**, a Dutch woman who became a celebrity performer in early-20th-century Europe. She built a public persona around “Orientalist” dance routines and cultivated relationships within elite social circles.

The name “Mata Hari” is often glossed in popular sources as meaning “eye of the day” (the sun) in Malay.

## World War I Espionage Allegations

During **World War I**, intelligence services across Europe recruited informants and intermediaries, and high-profile travelers were often viewed with suspicion. Mata Hari’s cross-border movements and contacts with military and diplomatic figures made her a target for counterintelligence scrutiny.

Open sources generally describe her as being accused of acting as a German agent (often referenced as **H-21**) while also allegedly offering information to French authorities, a pattern that has been interpreted as either double-dealing or opportunistic bargaining.

## Arrest, Trial, and Execution

- **Arrest (1917)**: French authorities arrested her amid a wartime atmosphere of paranoia and political pressure.
- **Trial**: She was tried by a French military court. Later commentary has questioned the strength of the evidence presented and the degree to which she became a symbolic scapegoat.
- **Execution (1917)**: She was executed by firing squad. Popular retellings often emphasize her composure at the execution, though details vary by account.

## Reputation and Legacy

Mata Hari became an enduring cultural figure in books and films, often portrayed as a glamorous femme fatale. Historians and biographers continue to debate whether she was a significant intelligence asset or a minor participant whose legend outgrew the documented record.
