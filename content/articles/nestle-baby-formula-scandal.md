## Summary

Nestlé aggressively marketed baby formula in developing countries, discouraging breastfeeding. The practice led to millions of infant deaths from contaminated water used to mix formula.

## Why It Mattered

The controversy became an early, globally visible test case for corporate responsibility in public health. It linked advertising and distribution practices to real-world outcomes in settings where clean water, literacy, and access to medical advice could not be assumed.

## Key Issues Highlighted

- **Marketing vs. medical guidance:** Promotional campaigns were criticized for being presented as authoritative health advice.
- **Infrastructure constraints:** Formula feeding can be dangerous when households lack reliable access to safe water, fuel for sterilization, and consistent supply.
- **Regulatory gaps:** The episode helped show how weak consumer-protection rules in some countries could be exploited by multinational firms.

## Policy & Industry Aftermath

International attention contributed to the adoption and later strengthening of the **WHO International Code of Marketing of Breast-milk Substitutes** and related national policies. The boycott and subsequent monitoring efforts also influenced later NGO strategies aimed at changing multinational corporate behavior.

## Ongoing Relevance

The scandal is still referenced in debates about how companies should market health-adjacent products in low-resource settings, and about whether voluntary codes, public pressure, or binding regulation best changes behavior at scale.

It also helped normalize independent monitoring of corporate compliance claims in global health.
