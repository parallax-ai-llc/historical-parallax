## Summary

Canadian cryptocurrency exchange whose founder Gerald Cotten died mysteriously in India with the sole passwords to $190 million in customer funds. Suspected to have faked his death.

## What Happened (High Level)

- QuadrigaCX operated as a centralized crypto exchange, so customer access ultimately depended on internal custody of private keys and operational controls.
- After Gerald Cotten’s reported death in late 2018, the company said critical passwords and key material were unavailable, preventing withdrawals.
- The situation triggered creditor actions, insolvency proceedings, and third-party monitoring of the firm’s remaining assets.

## Investigations and Findings

- Court-supervised monitoring and forensic reviews reported that many “cold wallets” the company claimed to control were empty or had been drained earlier.
- Regulators and investigators described the exchange as effectively operating without normal corporate governance and financial controls.
- Public reporting around the case frequently emphasized commingling of funds, unreliable internal records, and the difficulty of tracing crypto flows across addresses and exchanges.

## Aftermath and Significance

- The collapse became a widely cited example of key-person risk and opaque custody in the cryptocurrency industry.
- It accelerated interest in proof-of-reserves, independent audits, and better segregation of customer assets.
- The case is often discussed alongside broader calls for consumer protection and clearer oversight of crypto intermediaries.

## Key Themes

- Custody and access to keys (who can move funds, and under what controls)
- Transparency and verifiable reserves
- Governance, recordkeeping, and separation of customer and company assets
