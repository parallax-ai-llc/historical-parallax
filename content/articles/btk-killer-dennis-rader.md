## Summary

American serial killer known as BTK (Bind, Torture, Kill) who murdered 10 people in Wichita, Kansas between 1974 and 1991. He was a church president and Boy Scout leader who taunted police with letters for decades.

## Modus and Public Communications

The "BTK" label was derived from the killer’s own communications, which included letters to media and law enforcement.
Those communications influenced how the case was discussed publicly and kept attention on the investigation across long gaps between known attacks.

## Investigation and Capture (High Level)

After years without an arrest, renewed communication in the early 2000s helped authorities re-focus the case.
Investigators were able to link information from a computer disk to Rader, leading to his arrest in 2005.
The case is frequently cited as an example of how digital forensics and operational mistakes can become decisive in long-running investigations.

## Legal Outcome

Rader pleaded guilty in Kansas state court and received multiple life sentences.
Because the crimes occurred across many incidents and victims, sentencing reflected consecutive terms intended to ensure he would remain incarcerated.

## Cultural Impact

The BTK case has been referenced in books, documentaries, and fictional works, often as a cautionary story about media attention, public fear, and the limits of traditional investigative techniques before modern forensic tools.
