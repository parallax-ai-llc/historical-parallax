## Summary

Canadian mass murderer who shot and killed 14 women at École Polytechnique in Montreal on December 6, 1989, specifically targeting women engineering students. He blamed feminists for his failures. The massacre led to Canadian gun control reforms.

## Additional Context

### Background

Marc Lépine (born **Gamil Rodrigue Liass Gharbi**) grew up in Montreal. Biographical accounts commonly emphasize a difficult home environment, social isolation, and repeated setbacks in education and employment.

### Stated ideology and targeting

The attack is widely interpreted as misogynist violence: during the shooting he separated men and women, explicitly directed violence at women, and left statements framing the act as opposition to feminism.

### Public response and commemoration

The École Polytechnique massacre became a national point of reference in Canada for debates about violence against women, misogyny, and public safety. December 6 is observed in Canada as the **National Day of Remembrance and Action on Violence Against Women**.

### Policy and advocacy impact

The event is frequently cited in discussions of Canadian firearms policy and regulatory changes, as well as in the history of organized advocacy for gun control. It also shaped institutional approaches to campus safety and emergency response planning.

### Ongoing debates

Later analysis has explored how to interpret motive and causation, including mental-health framing versus ideological framing, and how public narratives can influence prevention strategies.

The case remains heavily studied in criminology and gender-violence research.
