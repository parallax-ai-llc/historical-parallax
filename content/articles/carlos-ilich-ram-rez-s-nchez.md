## Summary

Venezuelan revolutionary known as Carlos the Jackal who was one of the world's most notorious terrorists during the 1970s-80s. He led the OPEC siege, carried out bombings in France, and eluded capture for decades.

## Early involvement in militant networks

Ramírez Sánchez emerged from the milieu of Cold War-era revolutionary movements that linked ideologically motivated militants, state intelligence services, and transnational logistics. He is commonly described as having worked with the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine (PFLP) and related networks that operated across the Middle East and Europe.

## OPEC siege (1975)

The 1975 raid on OPEC headquarters in Vienna made him internationally notorious. The operation involved taking hostages and forcing the attackers’ demands into global media attention. The episode is frequently cited as an example of “spectacular” political violence designed to gain leverage through publicity rather than battlefield success.

## Later bombings and manhunt

In the following years he was associated with attacks and bombings in Europe, particularly in France, and became a high-profile target for security services. His ability to move across borders and operate with support networks contributed to a long-running international manhunt.

## Capture and prosecution

In 1994 he was captured in Sudan and transferred to France. French courts later convicted him in multiple cases, including those connected to killings and bombings, and imposed life sentences. He spent decades in French custody, making him one of the better-known examples of a militant figure from the 1970s being prosecuted years later.

## Historical significance

His career is often used to illustrate how political violence in the 1970s and 1980s combined ideology with opportunistic alliances, and how counterterrorism shifted toward long-term investigation, international cooperation, and prosecutions.

The nickname “Carlos the Jackal” became part of his public image through media coverage, reinforcing the way sensational labeling can shape the popular memory of politically motivated violence.
