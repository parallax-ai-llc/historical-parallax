## Summary

Fritz Strassmann co-discovered nuclear fission with Otto Hahn in 1938, one of the most important discoveries of the 20th century.

## Additional Context

### Role in the discovery of fission

- In late 1938, experiments by Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann on neutron-irradiated uranium produced chemical evidence of barium among the products.
- This result was inconsistent with the expectation of producing only nearby heavy elements, and it implied that the uranium nucleus had split into lighter elements.
- Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch provided the physical interpretation and coined the term “nuclear fission,” connecting the chemical findings to a new nuclear process.

### Scientific and historical impact

- The identification of fission rapidly reshaped nuclear physics and chemistry and ultimately enabled both nuclear reactors and nuclear weapons programs.
- Strassmann’s contributions are often described as crucial experimental chemistry: careful separation, identification, and insistence on what the results meant.

### Later career (high level)

- Postwar biographies typically note his continuing work in radiochemistry and his professional recognition within German scientific institutions.

## Notes

- Short summaries sometimes compress the multi-person nature of the fission discovery; chemistry (Hahn/Strassmann) and physics interpretation (Meitner/Frisch) were both essential.
- Many popular accounts focus on the Manhattan Project era, but the key experimental observation predates wartime industrialization of nuclear research.
