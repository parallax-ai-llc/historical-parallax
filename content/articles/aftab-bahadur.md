## Summary

Pakistani man executed for a triple murder committed when he was 15, in a case plagued by allegations of forced confession and torture. His execution despite being a juvenile at the time drew international condemnation.

## Legal and Human-Rights Context

Bahadur's case became widely cited in discussions about the application of capital punishment to people who were minors at the time of the alleged offense. International human-rights norms generally prohibit the death penalty for crimes committed by juveniles, and Pakistan's domestic law and documentation of defendants' ages have been points of contention in several high-profile cases.

## Case Controversies (High-Level)

Reporting around the case has emphasized several recurring issues often raised in wrongful-conviction and death-penalty debates:

- **Age determination:** disputes over whether the defendant was under 18 at the time of the crime.
- **Confession reliability:** allegations that statements were obtained under coercion or torture.
- **Due process concerns:** claims of inadequate legal representation and limited avenues for review.

## Legacy

Following his execution in 2015, the case was frequently referenced by advocacy organizations and commentators as an example of the stakes involved in death-penalty cases where juvenile status, evidentiary reliability, and alleged mistreatment are disputed.
