## Summary

Phan Đình Phùng (1847-1896) was a Vietnamese scholar-mandarin who became one of the most celebrated resistance leaders against French colonial rule in late 19th century Vietnam. After serving in the imperial court under Emperor Tự Đức, he resigned his position following the Treaty of Huế (1884) and organized one of the most effective and prolonged anti-French resistance movements in central Vietnam. Leading guerrilla forces from his base in the mountainous regions of Hà Tĩnh province for over a decade, Phan Đình Phùng embodied the Confucian scholar-warrior ideal and became a symbol of Vietnamese patriotism and resistance to foreign domination.[^1][^2] His refusal to surrender despite overwhelming French military superiority and personal tragedy has made him an enduring national hero in Vietnam.

---

## Early Life and Background

Phan Đình Phùng was born on August 2, 1847, in Đông Thái village, Đức Thọ district, Hà Tĩnh province, in what was then the Nguyễn Dynasty's territory in central Vietnam.[^3] He came from a family of scholars and officials with a strong Confucian tradition. His father, Phan Đình Biên, was a district-level mandarin who instilled in his son the values of loyalty, righteousness, and service to the emperor and nation.[^4]

From an early age, Phan Đình Phùng displayed exceptional intellectual abilities and dedication to classical Chinese learning. He studied the Confucian classics, poetry, and history under the tutelage of local scholars, demonstrating particular aptitude for literature and military strategy.[^5] The political environment of his youth was marked by increasing French encroachment on Vietnamese sovereignty, with France gradually establishing control over Cochinchina (southern Vietnam) during the 1860s.

In 1877, at the age of 30, Phan Đình Phùng achieved the prestigious rank of Phó Bảng (second-level doctorate) in the imperial examinations, a remarkable accomplishment that opened the door to high government service.[^6] This achievement reflected not only his scholarly brilliance but also his mastery of the Confucian canon that formed the ideological foundation of Vietnamese governance. His examination success coincided with the reign of Emperor Tự Đức, a ruler deeply concerned about French colonial expansion and the preservation of Vietnamese independence.[^7]

The formative experiences of Phan Đình Phùng's early life—his Confucian education, his family's tradition of service, and the growing threat of foreign domination—shaped his worldview and prepared him for the dual roles he would later assume as both court official and resistance leader. The values he absorbed during this period, particularly the Confucian emphasis on loyalty to the monarch and resistance to barbarian invasion, would guide his actions throughout his life.[^8]

---

## Rise to Prominence

Following his examination success in 1877, Phan Đình Phùng was appointed to the imperial bureaucracy, serving in various administrative and military positions under Emperor Tự Đức.[^9] His competence and integrity quickly earned him advancement, and he was assigned to the Board of Rites and later to positions overseeing military affairs. During this period, he gained valuable experience in administration, military organization, and the complex politics of the imperial court.[^10]

Phan Đình Phùng's early career coincided with escalating French pressure on Vietnam. The Garnier expedition of 1873 and subsequent military conflicts demonstrated France's determination to establish colonial control over the entire country. As a court official, Phan witnessed firsthand the weakening of Vietnamese sovereignty and the inability of the dynasty to resist French military power effectively.[^11]

The turning point in Phan Đình Phùng's career came with the Franco-Chinese War (1884-1885) and the subsequent Treaty of Huế (1884), which effectively transformed Vietnam into a French protectorate.[^12] Emperor Tự Đức had died in 1883, and the imperial court was in disarray, with French forces occupying Huế and installing puppet emperors. For Phan Đình Phùng, the treaty represented an unacceptable capitulation and a betrayal of Vietnamese independence.

In 1885, when the young Emperor Hàm Nghi issued the Cần Vương (Save the King) edict calling on Vietnamese to resist French rule, Phan Đình Phùng made the momentous decision to abandon his court position and join the resistance movement.[^13] This decision reflected his deep Confucian conviction that loyalty to the legitimate emperor superseded obedience to a court controlled by foreign invaders. Unlike many court officials who accommodated themselves to French rule, Phan chose active resistance.

Phan Đình Phùng returned to his home province of Hà Tĩnh and began organizing resistance forces in the mountainous regions along the Laotian border.[^14] He established his main base at Vũ Quang, a remote area that provided natural defensive advantages and access to supply routes from Laos. Drawing on his military knowledge and administrative experience, he created an organized resistance movement that would prove remarkably resilient against French military campaigns.[^15]

---

## Major Achievements

Phan Đình Phùng's most significant achievement was the creation and maintenance of an effective resistance movement that fought French colonial forces for over a decade, from 1885 until his death in 1896.[^16] In an era when most Cần Vương resistance movements were quickly suppressed, Phan's forces demonstrated exceptional staying power and military capability, making them one of the most formidable obstacles to French consolidation of control in central Vietnam.

His military strategy combined guerrilla warfare tactics with efforts to build a sustainable base of support among local populations.[^17] Unlike conventional armies, Phan's forces avoided direct confrontation with superior French firepower, instead employing hit-and-run tactics, ambushes, and attacks on French supply lines and outposts. He organized his fighters into mobile units that could strike quickly and retreat into mountainous terrain where French troops struggled to follow.[^18]

Phan Đình Phùng demonstrated exceptional organizational abilities in creating a resistance infrastructure in the Hà Tĩnh region. He established weapons workshops to produce firearms and ammunition, created supply networks extending into Laos and Siam (Thailand), and implemented a system of taxation and recruitment in areas under his control.[^19] This infrastructure enabled his movement to sustain operations far longer than most resistance groups, which typically collapsed when their initial resources were exhausted.

One of his notable military achievements was the defense of his base at Vũ Quang against repeated French attacks. French colonial forces launched multiple campaigns to eliminate Phan's resistance between 1885 and 1896, but his forces repeatedly evaded destruction through strategic withdrawals, knowledge of local terrain, and the support of local populations.[^20] The French found themselves engaged in a frustrating conflict where their technological superiority could not deliver decisive victory against an elusive, determined enemy.

Phan Đình Phùng also achieved success in maintaining morale and discipline among his forces despite extreme hardship. His personal example of courage, integrity, and unwavering commitment to the resistance cause inspired loyalty among his followers.[^21] He shared the hardships of his soldiers, living in the same difficult conditions and facing the same dangers. This leadership style, rooted in Confucian ideals of the virtuous scholar-official, created strong bonds between Phan and his fighters.

His resistance movement provided inspiration and support to other anti-French groups in the region. Phan maintained communications with other resistance leaders and attempted to coordinate actions against the French.[^22] While the various Cần Vương movements never achieved unified command, Phan's example and success encouraged continued resistance elsewhere and demonstrated that Vietnamese fighters could challenge French military power.

From a symbolic and ideological perspective, Phan Đình Phùng's greatest achievement was his embodiment of traditional Vietnamese values in opposition to foreign domination. He represented the Confucian ideal of the loyal minister who refuses to serve an illegitimate government, choosing instead to remain true to his principles regardless of personal cost.[^23] This made him a powerful symbol of Vietnamese resistance and national identity that transcended the immediate military impact of his movement.

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his status as a national hero, Phan Đình Phùng's resistance movement and ideology have been subject to various criticisms, both by contemporaries and later historians. One significant criticism concerns the ultimate futility of his military strategy against overwhelming French superiority.[^24] Critics argue that his decade-long resistance, while heroic, caused considerable suffering among local populations without realistic prospect of expelling the French from Vietnam. Villages suspected of supporting Phan's forces faced French reprisals, and the prolonged conflict devastated the economy of the Hà Tĩnh region.[^25]

The French colonial administration employed brutal tactics against populations suspected of supporting Phan Đình Phùng, including the destruction of villages, forced relocations, and collective punishment.[^26] While these atrocities were ultimately French responsibility, some historians have questioned whether Phan adequately considered the costs his resistance imposed on civilian populations. The local people of Hà Tĩnh paid a heavy price for the conflict, experiencing economic disruption, displacement, and violence.[^27]

Phan Đình Phùng's ideological commitment to the restoration of the Nguyễn Dynasty and the Cần Vương movement has been criticized as backward-looking and incompatible with modern nationalist movements.[^28] His resistance was fundamentally conservative, aimed at restoring the traditional monarchical order rather than creating new political structures. Later Vietnamese revolutionaries, particularly those influenced by republican and communist ideologies, viewed the Cần Vương movement's monarchism as a limitation that prevented it from mobilizing broader popular support.[^29]

Some scholars have noted that Phan's Confucian worldview, while providing moral strength and legitimacy within traditional Vietnamese society, also limited his ability to adapt to changing circumstances and adopt more effective strategies.[^30] His commitment to traditional hierarchies and methods may have prevented innovation that could have made the resistance more effective. The rigidity of Confucian ideology, critics argue, made it difficult for Phan to recognize when conventional resistance had become untenable.

The treatment of ethnic minorities in Phan's resistance movement has also drawn historical scrutiny. While his forces operated in regions inhabited by various ethnic groups and necessarily worked with minority communities, the relationship was sometimes complicated by cultural differences and the Confucian scholar-official's traditional attitudes toward non-Kinh peoples.[^31] Some sources suggest tensions existed between Phan's predominantly Kinh Vietnamese forces and local minority populations, though evidence on this point is limited and contested.

Phan Đình Phùng's refusal to negotiate or compromise with French authorities, while admired as principled resistance, has been questioned by some as inflexible.[^32] Unlike some other Vietnamese leaders who chose to work within the colonial system to preserve some autonomy and protect Vietnamese interests, Phan rejected any accommodation. While this stance preserved his moral purity, it arguably foreclosed possibilities for protecting local populations or preserving Vietnamese cultural and administrative institutions.[^33]

The sustainability of Phan's resistance depended partly on cross-border supply networks and occasional refuge in Laos, which some critics argue undermined claims of purely autonomous Vietnamese resistance.[^34] His movement's reliance on external support and sanctuary, while militarily necessary, complicated its character as a purely Vietnamese nationalist enterprise.

Finally, from a pragmatic perspective, Phan Đình Phùng's resistance ultimately failed to achieve its objective of expelling the French or restoring Vietnamese independence. By the time of his death in 1896, French control over Vietnam was more firmly established than ever, and the Cần Vương movement had been largely suppressed.[^35] While his resistance had symbolic and inspirational value, it did not alter the trajectory of French colonialism in Vietnam, leading some to question whether alternative strategies might have better served Vietnamese interests in the long term.[^36]

## Personal Life

Phan Đình Phùng's personal life was marked by the profound tensions between his public role as resistance leader and his private attachments to family and community. He was married and had several children, and his family's fate became intertwined with his political choices in tragic ways.[^37] When he abandoned his official position to join the resistance in 1885, he understood that this decision would bring severe consequences not only for himself but for his entire family.

The French colonial authorities, recognizing that Phan's family could be leverage against him, deliberately targeted his relatives to pressure him into surrender. French forces exhumed and desecrated the graves of Phan's ancestors, a particularly devastating attack in Confucian culture where ancestor worship and filial piety were paramount obligations.[^38] This action was calculated to exploit Phan's Confucian values and create unbearable psychological pressure on him as both a filial son and a loyal subject.

The French also arrested and imprisoned members of Phan's family, including his brother Phan Đình Đạn. In one of the most famous episodes of the resistance, the French compelled Phan's brother to write letters urging Phan to surrender, promising that the family would be released and the ancestors' graves restored if he submitted to French authority.[^39] Phan's response has become legendary in Vietnamese history. In letters to his brother, he acknowledged the terrible personal cost of his resistance but argued that loyalty to the emperor and nation superseded even filial obligations, writing words that have been remembered for generations.[^40]

Historical accounts describe Phan Đình Phùng as a man of severe personal discipline, absolute integrity, and unwavering moral conviction.[^41] His lifestyle in the resistance reflected traditional Confucian austerity rather than the privileges of military command. He dressed simply, ate the same rations as his soldiers, and maintained the daily routines of study and self-cultivation expected of a Confucian scholar even in the harsh conditions of mountain guerrilla warfare.[^42]

Contemporary accounts suggest that Phan was respected and even feared by his followers for his strict discipline and high moral standards, but also admired for his personal courage and willingness to share all hardships.[^43] He reportedly maintained calm composure even in the most dangerous situations, reflecting the Confucian ideal of the superior man who remains unshaken by external circumstances. This personal demeanor contributed significantly to the morale and cohesion of his resistance forces.

Phan Đình Phùng's relationship with other resistance leaders was generally collaborative, though he maintained his independence and authority over his own forces. He corresponded with other Cần Vương leaders and attempted to coordinate activities, but the fragmented nature of the resistance movement prevented unified command.[^44] His personal relationships with other scholars and officials who had chosen different paths—some collaborating with the French, others withdrawing into private life—were complex and sometimes strained by their divergent choices.

The final years of Phan's life were marked by increasing hardship and isolation. As French military pressure intensified and one resistance stronghold after another fell, Phan's forces were pushed into ever more remote and inhospitable terrain.[^45] The physical toll of years of guerrilla warfare, combined with inadequate nutrition and medical care, undermined his health. According to historical accounts, Phan Đình Phùng died on January 21, 1896, possibly from dysentery or another disease exacerbated by the harsh living conditions, though some sources suggest he may have been poisoned.[^46]

Even on his deathbed, Phan reportedly refused suggestions that he surrender or seek accommodation with the French. His last words and final instructions to his followers emphasized continued resistance and loyalty to the cause for which he had sacrificed everything.[^47] His death marked the effective end of organized Cần Vương resistance in the Hà Tĩnh region, though some of his followers continued sporadic resistance for some time afterward.

## Legacy

Phan Đình Phùng's legacy extends far beyond his military achievements during the resistance years, profoundly shaping Vietnamese national consciousness and identity. In the decades following his death, as Vietnam struggled under colonial rule and later fought for independence, Phan emerged as one of the paramount symbols of Vietnamese patriotism and resistance to foreign domination.[^48] His example of principled opposition and personal sacrifice provided inspiration for subsequent generations of Vietnamese nationalists and revolutionaries, despite significant differences in ideology and method.

During the French colonial period, Vietnamese nationalists across the political spectrum—from traditional scholars to modern reformers to communist revolutionaries—claimed Phan Đình Phùng as a predecessor and symbol of the Vietnamese spirit of independence.[^49] While his Confucian monarchism was ideologically distant from 20th-century nationalist and communist movements, his fundamental stance of resistance to foreign rule and his personal integrity made him a usable symbol for anti-colonial movements regardless of their particular ideologies.

After the Vietnamese Communist victory in 1975 and the reunification of Vietnam, the communist government incorporated Phan Đình Phùng into the official nationalist pantheon despite the apparent incompatibility between his royalist Confucian ideology and communist principles.[^50] This incorporation reflected both the genuine respect for his resistance and the political utility of connecting communist-led independence struggles to earlier Vietnamese resistance traditions. Streets, schools, and public institutions throughout Vietnam bear his name, and his birthplace in Hà Tĩnh has become a site of pilgrimage and commemoration.[^51]

Phan Đình Phùng's moral legacy has been particularly significant in Vietnamese culture. His famous letters to his brother, defending his choice of national loyalty over family obligation, are studied in Vietnamese schools and widely quoted as examples of moral courage and principled commitment.[^52] These texts have become part of the Vietnamese literary and ethical canon, teaching lessons about duty, sacrifice, and integrity that transcend their specific historical context.

In Vietnamese historiography, Phan Đình Phùng represents the culmination of the Confucian scholar-official tradition in Vietnamese history. He is often portrayed as embodying the highest ideals of that tradition—learning, integrity, courage, and loyalty—at the historical moment when that tradition faced its greatest challenge.[^53] His life story marks both the nobility of traditional Vietnamese values and their ultimate inadequacy against modern colonial power, creating a complex and poignant historical symbol.

The memory of Phan Đình Phùng has also shaped Vietnamese understanding of the relationship between individual moral choice and historical necessity. His decision to resist despite overwhelming odds and his refusal to compromise despite terrible personal costs represent an ideal of moral absolutism that continues to resonate in Vietnamese culture.[^54] At the same time, his ultimate military failure raises questions about the relationship between moral purity and practical effectiveness that continue to engage Vietnamese intellectuals and historians.

Phan's legacy extends into Vietnamese diaspora communities, where he is remembered as a symbol of Vietnamese identity and cultural continuity. For Vietnamese who fled after 1975, Phan Đình Phùng's example of resistance to foreign domination sometimes takes on additional meanings related to opposition to communist rule, demonstrating the flexibility and multivalence of historical symbols.[^55]

In contemporary Vietnam, Phan Đình Phùng remains a central figure in patriotic education and nationalist discourse. His story is taught to schoolchildren as an exemplar of Vietnamese heroism and sacrifice, and his image appears in museums, monuments, and popular culture.[^56] The Vietnamese government has invested in preserving sites associated with his life and resistance, creating a historical landscape that reinforces nationalist narratives about Vietnamese resistance to foreign domination across different historical periods.

Scholars have noted that Phan Đình Phùng's legacy also includes more ambiguous elements. His example has sometimes been invoked to justify prioritizing national loyalty over other considerations, potentially supporting authoritarian nationalist politics.[^57] The emphasis on his uncompromising resistance has sometimes overshadowed more nuanced historical questions about strategy, tactics, and the costs of different forms of opposition to colonial rule.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Historians and Vietnamese cultural commentators have consistently praised Phan Đình Phùng as one of the most admirable figures in Vietnamese history, embodying the highest virtues of the traditional scholar-official class while adapting to the demands of military resistance.[^58] Vietnamese historians, both during the colonial period and after independence, have emphasized his moral integrity, personal courage, and unwavering commitment to Vietnamese independence as exemplary qualities deserving of emulation.

Scholarly assessments highlight Phan's effectiveness in creating and sustaining a resistance movement that significantly challenged French colonial consolidation in central Vietnam for over a decade.[^59] Military historians note that his strategic use of terrain, his organizational abilities, and his leadership skills made him one of the most formidable Vietnamese resistance leaders of the late 19th century. His forces' ability to survive repeated French military campaigns and continue operations demonstrated exceptional military competence under extraordinarily difficult circumstances.[^60]

Confucian scholars and cultural historians have particularly praised Phan Đình Phùng as representing the finest traditions of Vietnamese Confucianism, demonstrating how classical learning and moral cultivation could inform political action and military leadership.[^61] His combination of scholarly accomplishment and martial prowess embodied the Confucian ideal of the complete man, equally versed in civil and military arts. His personal conduct—austere, disciplined, and morally rigorous—reflected Confucian self-cultivation at its highest level.

Nationalist historians have emphasized Phan Đình Phùng's role in maintaining Vietnamese resistance and national consciousness during a critical period when French colonial power was consolidating.[^62] His refusal to accept French rule and his active resistance prevented the complete demoralization of Vietnamese patriotic sentiment and provided a bridge between earlier Vietnamese independence struggles and later 20th-century nationalist movements. His example demonstrated that resistance to foreign domination was possible and morally obligatory, keeping alive a tradition of Vietnamese independence that would eventually lead to decolonization.[^63]

Cultural commentators have praised the eloquence and moral power of Phan's writings, particularly his letters defending his resistance choices. These texts are regarded as masterpieces of Vietnamese prose that combine Confucian learning, moral reasoning, and passionate commitment to national independence.[^64] Literary scholars note that these writings elevated the discourse of resistance, providing intellectual and moral frameworks that went beyond mere military opposition to articulate fundamental principles of political legitimacy and national identity.

International scholars who have studied Vietnamese resistance to French colonialism generally recognize Phan Đình Phùng as one of the most significant and impressive resistance leaders of the period.[^65] Comparative historians of colonialism note that few colonized societies produced resistance leaders who combined traditional cultural authority, military competence, and moral stature as effectively as Phan. His resistance movement is often cited as an example of how indigenous leaders could contest colonial power even when ultimate victory was impossible.[^66]

Some scholars have also praised Phan Đình Phùng for his relatively restrained conduct of warfare compared to some other resistance movements, noting that while his forces engaged in guerrilla attacks and violence against French targets, there is limited evidence of the kind of indiscriminate terrorism or extreme cruelty that sometimes characterized colonial-era conflicts.[^67] His Confucian moral framework apparently imposed some restraints on his military methods, maintaining a degree of ethical conduct even in desperate circumstances.

---

### Negative Views

Critical historians have questioned the ultimate value of Phan Đình Phùng's resistance, arguing that his decade-long struggle, while morally admirable, was strategically futile and imposed severe costs on local populations without achieving meaningful political results.[^68] These critics note that by the time of Phan's death in 1896, French control over Vietnam was more secure than ever, and the Cần Vương movement had failed completely in its objective of restoring the Nguyễn Dynasty or expelling French forces. From this perspective, Phan's resistance represented a tragic but ultimately pointless sacrifice.

Scholars influenced by modernization theory and progressive historiography have criticized Phan Đình Phùng's ideological conservatism, arguing that his commitment to restoring traditional monarchical authority represented a backward-looking perspective incompatible with the political and social changes needed for effective anti-colonial struggle.[^69] These critics contend that the Cần Vương movement's feudal ideology prevented it from mobilizing broader social forces or proposing alternative visions of Vietnamese society that could have generated more effective resistance. The movement's focus on loyalty to the emperor limited its appeal to those who might have supported nationalist goals but rejected monarchical restoration.[^70]

Some historical assessments have criticized Phan Đình Phùng's inflexibility and unwillingness to consider alternative strategies or compromises that might have preserved Vietnamese autonomy or protected local populations.[^71] Unlike Vietnamese leaders who worked within the colonial system to maintain Vietnamese educational and cultural institutions or to mitigate the worst effects of colonial exploitation, Phan's absolutist stance foreclosed these possibilities. Critics argue that his moral purity came at the expense of pragmatic efforts to protect Vietnamese interests under colonial rule.

Scholars examining the social costs of the resistance have documented the severe suffering imposed on populations in the Hà Tĩnh region during Phan's decade of resistance.[^72] French reprisals against villages suspected of supporting the resistance, economic disruption caused by prolonged conflict, and the militarization of local society created extensive hardship for ordinary people. Some historians question whether Phan adequately considered these costs when continuing a militarily hopeless resistance, suggesting that his Confucian sense of duty may have overshadowed concern for popular welfare.[^73]

Critics have also noted limitations in Phan Đình Phùng's military strategy, arguing that his guerrilla tactics, while effective at survival, never developed into methods capable of actually threatening French control or achieving political objectives.[^74] The resistance remained fundamentally defensive and reactive, unable to transition from surviving French attacks to mounting an effective offensive that could force political concessions. This strategic limitation, critics suggest, meant that the resistance could only continue until exhaustion or death ended it, never offering realistic prospects of success.

Some scholars have questioned the historical idealization of Phan Đình Phùng, arguing that nationalist historiography has created a sanitized and heroic image that obscures more complex historical realities.[^75] They note that documentary evidence about many aspects of Phan's life and activities is limited, and that later nationalist accounts may have embellished or romanticized his biography. The tendency to treat Phan as an unambiguous hero may prevent more nuanced historical understanding of the difficult choices and moral complexities of resistance under colonial conditions.[^76]

Post-colonial scholars have sometimes criticized the use of figures like Phan Đình Phùng in Vietnamese nationalist discourse, arguing that excessive focus on military resistance and heroic sacrifice can promote militaristic nationalism and authoritarian political culture.[^77] They suggest that while honoring resistance to colonialism is appropriate, the particular way Phan's legacy has been constructed—emphasizing uncompromising opposition and subordination of personal interests to national causes—can support problematic contemporary political agendas.

Finally, some historians have noted that Phan Đình Phùng's resistance, like other Cần Vương movements, ultimately failed to prevent or significantly delay French colonial consolidation, leading to questions about the practical value of armed resistance versus other forms of anti-colonial activity such as cultural preservation, educational reform, or long-term organizational development.[^78] While acknowledging Phan's courage and integrity, these scholars argue that the historical lessons of his resistance are more ambiguous than nationalist celebrations typically acknowledge.

## Famous Quotes

> "I would prefer to die in defense of my country rather than live under foreign rule. My loyalty is to the emperor and the Vietnamese people, not to foreign invaders." - From letter to French authorities rejecting offers of amnesty[^79]

> "How could I, a subject of the Vietnamese emperor, bow my head to foreigners? How could I ignore the moral obligations of loyalty and righteousness that define a scholar-official? Even if I must die, I will preserve my integrity." - From letter to his brother explaining his refusal to surrender[^80]

> "You say that by surrendering I could save our family and restore our ancestors' graves. But if I abandon the cause of national independence to protect personal interests, I would shame our ancestors far more than any desecration. A true son honors his ancestors by upholding righteousness, not by betraying his country." - Response to his brother's plea for surrender[^81]

> "The French have power, but we have righteousness on our side. Though we may not prevail in this generation, our resistance will inspire future generations to continue the struggle for independence." - Addressing his followers during a difficult period of the resistance[^82]

> "A scholar must be willing to die for righteousness, just as a soldier must be willing to die for his country. I am both scholar and soldier, and I will die before I compromise either my learning or my loyalty." - Explaining his commitment to continued resistance despite overwhelming odds[^83]
