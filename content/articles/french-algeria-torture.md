## Summary

French forces systematically tortured Algerian independence fighters using waterboarding, electric shocks, and sexual violence. General Aussaresses later admitted to the widespread torture.

## Context

- The term refers to documented practices of torture and coercive interrogation by French security forces during the Algerian War of Independence (1954–1962).
- Methods reported in survivor testimony, journalism, and later investigations included electric shocks, simulated drowning, beatings, stress positions, and sexual violence.

## Battle of Algiers and Counterinsurgency

- The 1957 Battle of Algiers is frequently cited as a focal point, with paratrooper units conducting mass arrests and interrogations to dismantle FLN networks.
- Supporters framed these methods as necessary for intelligence collection; critics argued they constituted systematic violations of law and fueled further radicalization.

## Legal and Political Aftermath

- In France, public debate intensified over time through memoirs, court cases, and historical scholarship.
- Some officials later acknowledged participation or knowledge of torture, while the state’s stance evolved unevenly amid competing narratives of wartime necessity versus criminality.

## Why It Matters

- The episode is often used in human-rights and security-policy discussions as a case study in how torture:
  - Produces contested intelligence quality.
  - Damages institutional legitimacy.
  - Leaves long-term social and political trauma.
