## Summary

Guatemalan military dictator whose 1982-1983 regime committed genocide against the Ixil Maya people. An estimated 1,771 indigenous people were killed under his scorched earth campaign. Briefly convicted of genocide in 2013.

## Regime and Conflict (High Level)

- **Short but violent rule:** Although his time in power was brief, Ríos Montt's government is widely associated with an intense phase of counterinsurgency operations.
- **Scorched-earth strategy:** Security forces targeted communities suspected of supporting guerrillas; historians and human-rights organizations describe mass killings and displacement.
- **Ixil Maya genocide finding:** Courts and investigators focused on violence against the Ixil population as a central case for genocide allegations.

## Legal Proceedings

- **2013 trial:** Ríos Montt became one of the first former heads of state to be convicted of genocide by a domestic court (the verdict was later annulled on procedural grounds).
- **Retrial:** Proceedings continued in altered form due to legal and health considerations until his death.

## Significance

- **Transitional justice milestone:** The case is often cited in Latin American human-rights law as a landmark attempt at domestic accountability.
- **Ongoing controversy:** Debates persist in Guatemala over historical memory, military responsibility, and the political legacy of the civil war.

## Key Takeaways

- **Domestic prosecution:** The trial demonstrated that, under some conditions, national courts can attempt to prosecute former leaders for mass atrocities.
- **Evidence challenges:** Such cases rely on survivor testimony, military documentation (when available), and patterns of operations across regions.
- **Memory politics:** Competing narratives about the civil war continue to influence Guatemalan politics and institutions.
