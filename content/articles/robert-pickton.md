## Summary
Robert William Pickton was a Canadian serial killer and pig farmer convicted of six second-degree murders of women from Vancouver's Downtown Eastside. He was charged with 26 murders total. He fed victims' remains to his pigs. His case exposed police failures to investigate missing Indigenous and marginalized women.

## Early Life
Born October 24, 1949, in Port Coquitlam, British Columbia. He operated a pig farm with his brother. The farm was also used as a venue for wild parties. He had prior assault charges that were stayed. [^1]

## Criminal Career
Between the 1990s and 2001, Pickton murdered women from Vancouver's Downtown Eastside, a neighborhood marked by poverty, drug addiction, and sex work. Many victims were Indigenous women. He brought them to his pig farm, killed them, and disposed of their remains by feeding them to pigs or mixing them with pork sold commercially. [^2]

## Capture / Downfall
Pickton was arrested on February 5, 2002, after police obtained a warrant to search his farm on firearms charges. DNA of numerous missing women was found on the property. He was charged with 26 murders but tried on only six due to legal complexity. Convicted of six second-degree murders in 2007 and sentenced to life with no parole for 25 years. [^3]

## Positive Perspectives
- His case led to the National Inquiry into Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls [^4]
- Prompted major reforms in Vancouver police handling of missing persons cases
- Highlighted systemic racism and neglect of Indigenous women

## Negative Perspectives
- Convicted of six murders, charged with 26, and suspected of more [^5]
- Fed victims' remains to pigs
- Police ignored reports of missing women for years due to their marginalized status
- His case revealed deep systemic racism against Indigenous women
- Remains may have entered the commercial food supply
