## Summary

Military commander of the Delhi Sultanate who led devastating campaigns across southern India plundering Hindu temples and kingdoms. He was murdered by palace nobles after attempting to seize power.

## Background and rise

Malik Kafur (also rendered as Kafur or Kafuri) rose within the Delhi Sultanate during the reign of Alauddin Khalji. Many narratives describe him as having entered the court from a position of servitude and then gaining influence through military success and proximity to the sultan.

## Military role under Alauddin Khalji

In the early 14th century, the Khalji state pursued aggressive expansion and revenue extraction. Kafur became a key commander in campaigns that combined territorial control, coercive tribute-taking, and high-value plunder. Chroniclers and later historians often treat these expeditions as both strategic (projecting power) and fiscal (feeding the sultanate’s treasury).

## Southern campaigns (high level)

Accounts of Kafur’s southern expeditions emphasize fast-moving raids into regions outside direct Delhi administration at the time. Targets included major South Indian polities and wealthy temple centers.

Commonly cited features include:

- Long-distance logistics across the Deccan and into the far south
- The extraction of tribute, captives, and portable wealth
- The political aim of forcing acknowledgement of Delhi’s supremacy without necessarily establishing durable direct rule

## Court politics and succession crisis

As Alauddin Khalji’s health declined, factional struggles intensified among nobles, military commanders, and palace officials. Kafur is frequently depicted as an important power broker in the last phase of the reign, with sources suggesting he sought to shape (or control) the succession and the regency around a minor heir.

## Death

Kafur’s fall was rapid. After Alauddin’s death in 1316, rival factions within the court reportedly moved against him, and he was killed during the ensuing purge. The episode is often cited as an example of how personal power in the sultanate depended on a fragile coalition at court.

## Historical significance

Kafur’s career is remembered primarily for:

- The scale and reach of Khalji-era campaigning
- The role of raid-and-tribute warfare in Delhi’s southern policy
- The volatility of succession politics in premodern imperial courts

## Notes on sources

Much of what is known is filtered through court chronicles and later historical reconstructions. Interpretations vary on the balance between strategic objectives and the immediate fiscal motives of the campaigns, and on the reliability of specific claims about court intrigue.
