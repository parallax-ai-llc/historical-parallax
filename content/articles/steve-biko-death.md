## Summary

Anti-apartheid activist Steve Biko was beaten to death in police custody. He was driven 1,100km naked in the back of a police van. No officers were prosecuted.

## Context and Background

Steve Biko was a prominent South African anti-apartheid activist and a founder of the Black Consciousness Movement, which emphasized psychological liberation and Black self-determination under apartheid.[^2]

By the mid-1970s, Biko was subject to state restrictions and surveillance, including banning orders that limited his ability to speak publicly and participate in political organizing.[^2]

## What Happened (High-level)

In 1977, Biko was detained by South African security police. Accounts summarized in secondary sources describe severe physical assault during interrogation and a subsequent period where he received inadequate medical attention while in custody.[^2]

Biko died in detention on 12 September 1977. The case became internationally notorious because of the circumstances of his injuries, the conditions of transport between detention facilities, and the state’s initial explanations for his death.[^2]

## Aftermath and Investigations

An inquest into Biko’s death concluded that he died from brain injuries sustained while in police custody, but it did not result in criminal convictions of the officers involved at the time.[^2]

The role of medical professionals who examined Biko while he was injured has also been widely discussed; professional disciplinary processes and later public scrutiny contributed to the broader debate about medical ethics under apartheid.[^2]

In the post-apartheid era, the case was revisited in public forums, including Truth and Reconciliation Commission-related processes, and it remained a symbol of impunity and systemic violence in the apartheid security apparatus.[^2]

## Legacy

Biko’s death became a rallying point for anti-apartheid activism, strengthening international pressure on the apartheid government and cementing Biko’s status as a political martyr for many South Africans.[^2]

The phrase "the spirit of Biko" is often used to refer to enduring themes associated with Black Consciousness: dignity, self-respect, and resistance to racial domination.[^2]
