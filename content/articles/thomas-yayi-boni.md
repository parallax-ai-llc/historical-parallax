## Summary

Former President of Benin whose second term was marred by corruption scandals including the ICC Services Ponzi scheme that defrauded thousands. His government lost billions in the fraud.

## Political and professional background

Yayi Boni is commonly described as an economist and banker by training, with experience in regional and international finance institutions prior to entering electoral politics. He won the presidency in 2006 and served two terms, a period often discussed in relation to economic management, governance reforms, and the limits of anti-corruption enforcement in Benin’s political system.

## Governance and policy themes

His presidency included efforts to project stability and attract investment, while critics focused on patronage and the concentration of influence around the executive. Like many West African governments, his administration faced the challenge of balancing donor-backed reforms with domestic political coalitions.

## ICC Services scandal

The ICC Services case was described in public reporting as a large Ponzi scheme that collected deposits from citizens and promised high returns. When the scheme collapsed, it affected a wide cross-section of the population and became a national political crisis.

The scandal raised questions about regulatory oversight, political protection networks, and whether state actors benefited from or ignored warning signs. It also amplified public anger toward elites when losses were socialized and accountability appeared limited.

## Controversies and political aftermath

Yayi Boni’s later years in office included recurring allegations of corruption and political intimidation from opponents, while supporters argued that he was targeted by rivals and that governance challenges were structural.

After leaving office, he remained a prominent political figure, occasionally commenting on national politics and participating in opposition or mediation efforts depending on the political moment.
