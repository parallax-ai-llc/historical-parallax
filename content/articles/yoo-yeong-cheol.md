## Summary

South Korean serial killer who mutilated victims' bodies and cannibalized some remains in Seoul between 2003 and 2004. He targeted both wealthy elderly people and sex workers, killing 21 people.

## Background

Yoo Yeong-cheol is one of the most widely reported serial offenders in modern South Korean criminal history. The case is often referenced in media studies and criminology discussions because of the scale of the crimes, the public fear they generated, and the subsequent debates about policing, social safety, and punishment.

## Investigation and Arrest

The killings took place primarily in the Seoul metropolitan area during 2003–2004. Investigators connected multiple incidents through evidence and witness accounts, and the case became a major national manhunt. Reports commonly describe the arrest as resulting from a combination of investigative work and information derived from victims and survivors.

## Trial and Sentencing

South Korean courts sentenced Yoo to death in 2005. South Korea, however, has maintained a long-standing de facto moratorium on executions, meaning that death sentences have generally not been carried out for decades even though the penalty remains in law.

## Social Impact

The case contributed to extensive public discussion about the vulnerabilities of certain groups and about patterns of violence linked to social isolation and exploitation. It also intensified attention to emergency reporting, neighborhood safety, and the resources available to people at risk of violence.

## Media and Cultural Legacy

Yoo Yeong-cheol's crimes have been covered in documentaries and true-crime media, and the name is frequently invoked in broader narratives about notorious criminal cases in South Korea. Commentators sometimes note the need for responsible coverage to avoid sensationalism while still acknowledging the experiences of victims and communities.
