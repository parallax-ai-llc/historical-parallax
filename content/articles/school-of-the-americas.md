## Summary

US military training school in Panama (later Georgia) that trained Latin American soldiers later implicated in human rights abuses. Graduates include dictators, death squad leaders, and torturers.

## Mission and Institutional History

- The U.S. Army School of the Americas (SOA) was created to train military personnel from Latin America, initially operating in the Panama Canal Zone.
- Training curricula evolved over time and included a range of military and security subjects.
- In 2001 the institution was rebranded as the **Western Hemisphere Institute for Security Cooperation (WHINSEC)**.

## Core Controversy

- Critics argue the school provided training to individuals and units later involved in coups, death squads, and human rights abuses.
- The controversy is often framed around:
  - who attended and what roles they later held
  - the content and oversight of training materials
  - accountability for graduates implicated in abuses

## Public Response

- The SOA/WHINSEC has been a long-running focus of protests and advocacy campaigns in the United States.
- Supporters argue the program professionalizes partner militaries and can promote lawful conduct; opponents argue it enabled abusive security forces.

## Why It Matters

- The debate around SOA is frequently used as a case study in how security assistance intersects with human rights and foreign policy outcomes.
