## Summary

Aamir Khan is one of Indian cinema's most celebrated and commercially successful actors, widely regarded as "Mr. Perfectionist" for his meticulous approach to craft and role preparation. Born on March 14, 1965, in Mumbai, he has spent over four decades building a filmography defined by ambitious storytelling, social commentary, and box office dominance. His films consistently challenge mainstream Bollywood conventions while reaching mass audiences across India and internationally.

Beyond acting, Khan has established himself as a producer and filmmaker of considerable influence, championing projects that address caste discrimination, educational inequality, gender norms, and national identity. His television show *Satyamev Jayate* (2012) brought serious social issues to primetime Indian television, demonstrating a commitment to public discourse that extends well beyond entertainment.

## Early Life & Background

Aamir Khan was born into a film family in Mumbai. His uncle, Nasir Hussain, was a prominent Hindi film director and producer, giving Khan early exposure to the industry. He made his debut as a child actor in *Yaadon Ki Baaraat* (1973). Khan attended J.B. Petit School and later Bombay Scottish School, where he developed an interest in theatre. He studied briefly at Narsee Monjee College before leaving to pursue acting full-time. His formative years were shaped by the creative environment of a film household and a personal drive for excellence that would define his later work.

## Rise to Prominence

Khan's adult acting debut came with *Qayamat Se Qayamat Tak* (1988), directed by his cousin Mansoor Khan. The film was a massive commercial and critical success, earning him a Filmfare Award for Best Male Debut. Through the 1990s he delivered a string of hits including *Jo Jeeta Wohi Sikandar* (1992), *Rangeela* (1995), and *Raja Hindustani* (1996). A pivotal shift came with *Lagaan* (2001), which he produced and starred in — an epic period drama nominated for the Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film, marking India's third such nomination. This cemented his reputation as an actor willing to take unconventional risks.

## Key Achievements

Khan's post-2000 career produced some of Bollywood's highest-grossing films. *Dhoom 3* (2013), *PK* (2014), and *Dangal* (2016) each broke domestic box office records at the time of their release. *Dangal*, based on wrestler Mahavir Singh Phogat, grossed over ₹2,000 crore worldwide, becoming one of the highest-grossing Indian films ever and a massive success in China. He has won three National Film Awards and multiple Filmfare Awards. The Government of India honored him with the Padma Shri (2003) and Padma Bhushan (2010).

## Controversies & Criticism

In 2015, Khan sparked significant backlash when he remarked that his wife Kiran Rao had considered leaving India due to rising intolerance, comments made during the "Award Wapsi" debate. The statement drew criticism from political leaders and public figures who accused him of misrepresenting the country's social climate. Some critics have also argued that his public persona of social consciousness is at odds with commercial compromises in certain film choices. His selective release strategy — spacing films years apart — has occasionally frustrated distributors and collaborators.

## Legacy & Influence

Aamir Khan redefined what a mainstream Hindi film star could represent — someone who combines mass appeal with artistic ambition and civic responsibility. His model of deep character immersion, including dramatic physical transformations for *Ghajini* (2008) and *Dangal*, influenced an entire generation of Indian actors. His success in China opened discussions about Indian cinema's global reach. Through his production company Aamir Khan Productions, he continues to develop projects centered on underrepresented stories, ensuring his influence on Indian cinema extends behind the camera as well.
