## Summary

Mass shooting at Dunblane Primary School in Scotland on March 13, 1996 killing 16 children and their teacher. It led to strict gun control laws in the UK.

## Background

- The attack occurred in Dunblane, Stirling, at Dunblane Primary School.
- The perpetrator, Thomas Hamilton, used legally held handguns and killed 16 pupils and one teacher; a number of others were injured.
- Hamilton died at the scene, and the incident became one of the deadliest shootings in modern British history.

## Investigation and inquiry

- The UK government convened a public inquiry chaired by Lord Cullen.
- The Cullen Report examined how Hamilton obtained and retained firearms certificates and identified shortcomings in oversight and information-sharing.

## Policy impact

- The public campaign that followed is often associated with the Snowdrop Petition, named after the school’s symbol.
- Parliament passed the Firearms (Amendment) Acts of 1997, which effectively banned private ownership of most handguns in Great Britain.
- The Dunblane case is frequently cited in debates about gun control, public safety, and regulatory enforcement.

## Legacy

- The event had long-lasting effects on victims’ families and on the community, with memorials and ongoing commemorations.
- Dunblane’s legacy is also discussed in relation to later UK gun-crime policy and policing practices.
- In the UK, Dunblane is commonly referenced alongside the Hungerford massacre (1987) when summarizing the evolution of postwar firearms regulation.
