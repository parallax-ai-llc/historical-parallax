## Overview

Reports of war crimes and serious violations of international humanitarian law have been a persistent feature of the Syrian conflict since 2011. Allegations have been made against multiple parties, including state forces, allied militias, opposition armed groups, and jihadist organizations.

## Commonly Cited Categories of Violations

High-level reporting and investigations often group alleged violations into recurring categories:

- **Indiscriminate or disproportionate attacks**: shelling, aerial bombardment, and the use of explosive weapons in densely populated areas
- **Detention abuses**: torture, enforced disappearances, and deaths in custody
- **Siege warfare and deprivation**: starvation tactics and obstruction of humanitarian access
- **Chemical weapons allegations**: incidents investigated by international bodies, with disputed narratives in some cases
- **Targeting of medical facilities and personnel**: repeated attacks on hospitals and health workers reported by monitoring groups
- **Summary executions and massacres**: killings of civilians or captured fighters attributed to different armed factions

## Documentation Efforts

A large body of documentation has been assembled by Syrian civil-society groups, international NGOs, journalists, and UN-mandated mechanisms. These efforts typically rely on witness testimony, satellite imagery, open-source video verification, medical records, and recovered documents.

## International Mechanisms and Investigations

Several international processes have sought to investigate and preserve evidence, including:

- UN commissions of inquiry focused on mapping patterns of abuse
- OPCW processes related to chemical weapons allegations
- The International, Impartial and Independent Mechanism (IIIM) established to support future prosecutions

## Accountability and Legal Proceedings

While Syria is not a party to the Rome Statute, accountability has been pursued through other pathways, including domestic prosecutions in third countries under universal-jurisdiction principles. Such cases often focus on specific defendants, chain-of-command evidence, and documented detention practices.

## Notes on Terminology

“War crimes” is a legal term that depends on fact-finding and judicial standards. Many public reports describe alleged crimes; definitive legal conclusions generally require court judgments or similarly authoritative determinations.
