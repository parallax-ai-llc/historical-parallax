## Summary

Purdue Pharma's aggressive marketing of OxyContin and other pharmaceutical companies' practices caused an opioid epidemic killing over 500,000 Americans. The Sackler family earned $13 billion.

## Overview

The opioid crisis refers to overlapping waves of opioid-related harms, including addiction, overdose, and community-level impacts. It has involved prescription opioids, heroin, and synthetic opioids, with patterns varying by region and time.[^1]

## Key Drivers (Commonly Cited)

High-level factors frequently discussed include:

- **Medical and regulatory context**: changing approaches to pain, prescribing norms, and monitoring.[^1]
- **Pharmaceutical marketing and supply**: promotion, distribution, and diversion of prescription opioids.[^1]
- **Illicit drug markets**: transitions from prescription opioids to heroin and later to fentanyl and analogues.[^1]
- **Socioeconomic stressors**: unemployment, disability, and mental-health comorbidity in some affected communities.[^1]

## Corporate and Legal Accountability

The crisis led to extensive litigation involving manufacturers, distributors, and pharmacies, as well as major settlements and restructuring efforts. Public debate has also focused on how to balance pain treatment with risk mitigation.[^1]

## Public Health Response

Responses have included:

- expansion of medication-assisted treatment (e.g., methadone, buprenorphine),
- naloxone access to reverse overdoses,
- prescription drug monitoring programs,
- harm reduction measures and public education.
