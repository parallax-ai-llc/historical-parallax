## Summary

Terrorist attack on the Indian Parliament in New Delhi on December 13, 2001 by Jaish-e-Mohammed and Lashkar-e-Taiba militants. 14 people were killed including all 5 attackers.

## Background and Context

- The attack occurred amid heightened India–Pakistan tensions and ongoing militancy in Jammu and Kashmir.
- Indian authorities attributed the operation to Pakistan-based militant organizations, which contributed to a major diplomatic crisis between the two countries.

## Immediate Aftermath

- The assault led to an extensive security response in New Delhi and a reassessment of parliamentary and government security procedures.
- In the weeks following, India mobilized large numbers of troops to its border areas, escalating regional tensions.

## Investigation and Legal Proceedings

- Indian investigators pursued a broader conspiracy case beyond the attackers killed at the scene.
- The case became one of India’s most prominent terrorism prosecutions, involving debates over evidence, due process, and the use of special security legislation.

## Significance

- The incident is frequently cited as a turning point in India’s counterterrorism posture in the early 2000s.
- It remains a reference point in discussions of internal security, intelligence coordination, and India–Pakistan crisis management.
