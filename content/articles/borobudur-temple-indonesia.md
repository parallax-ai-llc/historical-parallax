## Summary

Borobudur is the largest Buddhist temple in the world and one of the greatest monuments of
human civilization, rising from the fertile plains of central Java, Indonesia,
approximately 40 kilometers northwest of Yogyakarta. Built in the 9th century CE during
the reign of the Sailendra dynasty, the monument is a massive three-dimensional mandala —
a cosmological diagram rendered in stone — representing the Buddhist path from ignorance
to enlightenment. It stands 35 meters tall and consists of nine stacked platforms (six
square and three circular), crowned by a central dome (stupa). The structure contains 504
Buddha statues, 72 latticed stupas on the upper circular terraces, and nearly 2,672
individual relief panels covering a total of approximately 2,500 square meters — one of
the most extensive sculptural programs in the world.

Borobudur was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 1991 and was recognized as
one of the greatest archaeological achievements of the ancient world. After lying largely
forgotten and buried under volcanic ash and jungle vegetation for several centuries, it
was rediscovered in the early 19th century and has since undergone two major international
restoration projects, the most recent completed in 1983 with the assistance of UNESCO.

The monument's name is believed to derive from the Sanskrit words "vihara Buddha uhr"
meaning "Buddhist monastery on the hill" or possibly from the Old Javanese "bara" (temple)
and "budur" (on the hill or above).

## History & Origins

Borobudur was built during the reign of the Sailendra dynasty, a powerful Buddhist royal
house that dominated central Java from the late 8th to the 9th century CE. Construction is
generally dated to between approximately 750 and 842 CE, based on analysis of
inscriptions, architectural styles, and comparison with other dated monuments from the
period.

The precise circumstances of its construction — who commissioned it, how the workforce was
organized, what its dedicated function was — remain matters of scholarly debate, since no
contemporary written records specifically documenting its building have survived. The most
widely accepted theory attributes the monument's construction to the Sailendra king
Samaratungga, with its completion overseen by his daughter Pramodhavardhani. An
inscription from 824 CE mentions the construction of a monument of extraordinary scale in
the region.

The construction required an enormous labor force and the quarrying and transport of
approximately 2 million blocks of andesite stone from nearby riverbeds and hills. The
stones were fitted together without mortar, using an interlocking system. At its height of
construction, estimates suggest many thousands of workers — craftsmen, stone carvers,
laborers, and engineers — were involved.

The Sailendra dynasty was also responsible for other significant Buddhist monuments in the
region, including the temples of Mendut and Pawon, which lie along a straight line with
Borobudur and are believed to have formed a sacred processional route.

By the 10th or 11th century, Borobudur had apparently fallen out of active use. The
reasons are debated: the shift of the political center of Java eastward, the growing
influence of Hinduism, and possibly the catastrophic eruption of Mount Merapi (one of the
world's most active volcanoes, located nearby) are all proposed factors. Over the
following centuries, the monument was gradually obscured by volcanic ash deposits,
landslides, and the encroachment of tropical vegetation.

A Dutch colonial officer, Herman Cornelius, led the first modern survey of the site in
1814, at the direction of Thomas Stamford Raffles, the British Lieutenant-Governor of
Java. Raffles, a keen antiquarian, is often credited with the "rediscovery" of Borobudur
to the Western world, though local Javanese people had knowledge of the buried ruins
throughout the centuries of its obscurity.

Between 1817 and 1835, Dutch colonial authorities conducted initial clearance of the site,
revealing the monument's general form. A more comprehensive restoration effort was
undertaken between 1907 and 1911 under the direction of Theodoor van Erp, which restored
many of the upper terraces and stupas. However, the fundamental problems of water
infiltration and structural instability were not addressed, and by the mid-20th century,
the monument was again in serious deterioration.

The most significant restoration project ran from 1975 to 1982, coordinated by the
Indonesian government with the support of UNESCO and contributions from many countries.
The entire monument was effectively dismantled and reconstructed: 1,460,000 stone blocks
were removed, cleaned, treated for preservation, and reassembled on a new reinforced
concrete foundation. The project cost approximately USD $25 million and involved the work
of hundreds of specialists and thousands of laborers.

## Religious Significance

Borobudur was built as a Mahayana Buddhist monument and functions on multiple levels
simultaneously: as a stupa (a mound containing sacred relics), as a temple (a place of
ritual activity), and as a mandala (a diagrammatic representation of the cosmos and the
path to enlightenment).

The monument's design embodies the Buddhist cosmological model of Mount Meru, the cosmic
axis around which the universe revolves. Moving from the base to the summit represents the
journey of the soul from the lowest realm of existence to the highest state of spiritual
attainment — nirvana.

The monument is divided into three zones corresponding to the three realms of Buddhist
cosmology:

**Kamadhatu** (the realm of desire) — The hidden base of the monument, partially buried, contains 160 relief panels depicting the consequences of human desires and passions, illustrating the law of cause and effect (karma).

**Rupadhatu** (the realm of form) — The five square terraces, covered in 1,300 narrative relief panels that tell the stories of the Buddha's previous lives (Jataka tales) and the lives of bodhisattvas, as well as scenes from the Lalitavistara (the life of Siddhartha Gautama) and the Gandavyuha (the journey of the pilgrim Sudhana). The 432 niches on these terraces contain seated Buddha statues, each making one of five specific hand gestures (mudras) corresponding to the five directions.

**Arupadhatu** (the realm of formlessness) — The three circular upper terraces, stripped of all decorative relief panels, contain 72 latticed bell-shaped stupas, each enclosing a seated Buddha statue. These stupas are in various states of openness: some have wide diamond-shaped lattices (representing the realm of water), some have square lattices (the realm of fire). The central dome at the summit, the largest stupa, is empty — representing nirvana, the absence of form and desire.

This architectural progression from the ornate and narrative lower levels to the spare and
abstract upper levels physically enacts the Buddhist meditative journey from conceptual
thinking toward direct, non-conceptual experience of reality.

The 504 Buddha statues represent different aspects and manifestations of the Buddha. The
five mudras — Bhumisparsha (earth touching), Dhyana (meditation), Vara (gift-giving),
Abhaya (fearlessness), and Vitarka (teaching) — correspond to the five Dhyani Buddhas of
Mahayana Buddhism: Aksobhya, Amitabha, Ratnasambhava, Amoghasiddhi, and Vairochana.

## Architecture & Description

Borobudur's footprint is approximately 123 by 123 meters. The monument rises in nine
graduated platforms — six square and three circular — to a total height of approximately
35 meters above ground level (42 meters including the central spire, of which only the
base remains).

The relief panels on the galleries of the square terraces are among the most extensive and
detailed sculptural programs in the world. Walking through the galleries in the prescribed
clockwise direction (pradakshina), a pilgrim would cover approximately 4.8 kilometers of
walkway and encounter nearly 2,700 individual relief panels. These panels depict scenes
from Buddhist scripture with extraordinary narrative detail and artistic sophistication:
figures of princes, peasants, merchants, soldiers, monks, and deities interact in scenes
of cosmological significance and everyday Javanese life.

The andesite stone used throughout Borobudur was not polished but carved with remarkable
precision using iron tools. The stone's natural dark grey color contrasts with the bright
sky and surrounding green agricultural landscape, giving the monument a powerful visual
presence that changes dramatically with the light conditions throughout the day.

Borobudur does not have interior spaces in the conventional sense — it is not a hollow
building but a solid monument with open-air gallery walkways wrapping around each level.
There are no rooms, no altar chambers, no permanent dwelling spaces. It is designed
entirely as a circumambulatory pilgrimage monument.

The drainage system built into the structure is another remarkable engineering
achievement: more than 100 ornamental waterspouts, carved as kala (monstrous faces) or
makaras (mythical sea creatures), drain rainwater from the terraces through a network of
channels and pipes.

## Pilgrimage & Visitors

Borobudur receives approximately 3 to 4 million visitors per year, making it one of the
most-visited monuments in Indonesia and Southeast Asia. It is the single most visited
tourist destination in Indonesia.

The most sacred pilgrimage event at Borobudur is Waisak (Vesak), the Buddhist holiday
commemorating the birth, enlightenment, and death of the Buddha, celebrated on the full
moon of the fourth month of the Buddhist calendar (usually May). On Waisak, thousands of
Buddhist monks and lay practitioners from across Indonesia and from many other countries
gather at Borobudur for a ceremonial procession and meditation. The procession
traditionally travels from Mendut Temple to Pawon Temple to Borobudur, reenacting the
sacred alignment of these three monuments. At Borobudur, a ceremony is held at sunrise on
the upper terrace, with the release of lanterns and the offering of flowers.

In recent years, the Indonesian government and UNESCO have worked to balance the
requirements of mass tourism with the preservation of the monument and respect for its
religious significance. A ticketing system limits daily visitor numbers, and restoration
work continues on an ongoing basis.

The Indonesian government has also developed a broader "Borobudur Cultural Landscape"
management plan that addresses the relationship between the monument, the surrounding
villages, the Merapi volcano, and the regional ecosystem.

## Modern Significance

Borobudur is central to Indonesian national identity, appearing on the 20,000 rupiah
banknote and serving as a symbol of the country's pre-Islamic cultural heritage and
artistic achievement. For the Buddhist minority in Indonesia (approximately 0.7% of the
population, concentrated in Java, Bali, and among the ethnic Chinese community), it is the
most sacred site in the country.

The monument's extraordinary sculptural program has been the subject of intensive
scholarly study since the late 19th century. The Dutch scholar N.J. Krom produced the
first comprehensive scholarly survey of Borobudur's reliefs in the early 20th century, and
subsequent generations of art historians, archaeologists, and religious studies scholars
have continued to analyze its iconographic program, architectural geometry, and historical
context.

The 1983 restoration completed by UNESCO is widely cited as a landmark in international
heritage conservation, demonstrating what can be achieved through coordinated
international effort in the face of the deterioration of irreplaceable cultural monuments.
The lessons learned at Borobudur influenced subsequent UNESCO conservation projects in
Angkor (Cambodia), Bagan (Myanmar), and elsewhere.

A 2006 earthquake centered near Yogyakarta caused minor damage to the monument but did not
affect its structural integrity. Volcanic eruptions of Mount Merapi in 2010 deposited
volcanic ash on the monument, requiring emergency cleaning operations.
