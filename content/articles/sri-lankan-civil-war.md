## Summary

Civil war from 1983-2009 between the Sri Lankan government and LTTE that killed over 100,000 people. The wars final phase saw alleged war crimes by both sides in the Vanni region.

## Background and Causes

- The conflict is commonly traced to decades of political tension over language policy, minority rights, and national identity.
- Anti-Tamil violence in 1983 (often associated with “Black July”) is widely treated as a turning point that escalated insurgency and civil conflict.

## Main Actors

- The Sri Lankan state fought against several militant groups over time, with the Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE) becoming the dominant insurgent force.
- The LTTE sought an independent Tamil state and built conventional and guerrilla capabilities, including naval and suicide-attack units.

## Phases of the War

- The war included periods of heavy fighting as well as ceasefires and negotiations.
- International mediation efforts, including a Norwegian-brokered ceasefire, attempted to create a political settlement but ultimately failed.

## Humanitarian and Legal Concerns

- Civilian displacement, attacks on noncombatants, and restrictions on humanitarian access were recurring concerns.
- The final phase of fighting in the north (including the Vanni) drew allegations of serious violations by multiple parties.

## End of the Conflict and Aftermath

- The war ended in 2009 with the military defeat of the LTTE and the death of its leader, Velupillai Prabhakaran.
- Post-war Sri Lanka faced ongoing debates about accountability, reconciliation, demilitarization, and the political status of Tamil-majority areas.

## Historical Significance

- The conflict shaped Sri Lanka’s modern politics, security institutions, and diaspora communities.
- It remains a major case study in counterinsurgency, negotiated peace processes, and post-conflict justice.
