## Summary

Thomas Sudhof is a German-American biochemist who shared the 2013 Nobel Prize for discoveries of vesicle traffic machinery.

## Scientific Contributions (High-Level)

- Sudhof’s research is best known for explaining how **cells package, traffic, and release molecules** using membrane-bound vesicles.
- A major focus of his work has been **neuronal synapses**, where vesicle release underlies communication between nerve cells.
- His findings helped clarify how proteins coordinate vesicle docking, priming, and fusion with target membranes.

## Nobel Prize (2013)

- In 2013, Sudhof shared the **Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine** for work on the machinery that governs vesicle trafficking.
- The prize recognized foundational discoveries about how vesicles move and fuse—processes essential to secretion, neurotransmission, and many aspects of cellular organization.
- The award was shared with **James E. Rothman** and **Randy W. Schekman**.

## Academic Career

- Sudhof has held senior research and faculty positions in the United States and is widely associated with **Stanford University**, where he joined the faculty in 2008.
- He has trained and collaborated with many researchers in neuroscience and cell biology, contributing to a large body of highly cited work.

## Impact

- Vesicle trafficking is central to normal physiology; disruptions are implicated in a range of diseases, including neurological disorders.
- Sudhof’s research is frequently cited in modern neuroscience for its role in linking **molecular mechanisms** to **synaptic function**.
