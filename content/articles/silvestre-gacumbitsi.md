## Summary

Rwandan bourgmestre who directed massacres in Rusumo commune during the genocide, personally encouraging sexual violence against Tutsi women. He was convicted by the ICTR.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |

## Role and Office

In Rwanda’s administrative system at the time, a **bourgmestre** (mayor) held significant local authority over security and civil administration. ICTR cases involving local officials often focus on how administrative power, public meetings, and coordination with armed groups contributed to mass violence.

## ICTR Conviction (High-Level)

The International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR), established by the United Nations, prosecuted individuals alleged to bear responsibility for the 1994 genocide against the Tutsi. In Gacumbitsi’s case, judgments are commonly summarized as finding responsibility for directing or encouraging attacks and for acts of sexual violence committed in the context of the genocide.

## Legal Significance

The case is sometimes referenced in discussions of:

- **Incitement and leadership responsibility** at the local level.
- **Sexual violence as an instrument of genocide**, and how international tribunals document and characterize such crimes.
- **Appellate review**, including adjustments of sentences based on findings and legal standards.

## Context: Rusumo Commune

Rusumo, in eastern Rwanda near the Tanzanian border, is frequently mentioned in accounts of population movement and localized massacres during the genocide period. Tribunal findings typically connect specific events to dates, locations, and chains of authority to establish individual criminal responsibility.

These records are often used by historians and legal scholars to study how mass violence was organized at the communal level.
