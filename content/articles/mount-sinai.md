## Summary

Mount Sinai — known in Arabic as Jabal Musa (Mountain of Moses) — is a mountain in the Sinai Peninsula of Egypt traditionally identified as the location where the Prophet Moses received the Ten Commandments from God, an event described in the biblical Book of Exodus. The mountain is sacred to Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, making it one of the few sites in the world of profound significance to all three Abrahamic religions simultaneously.

The most commonly identified location for Mount Sinai is the peak known as Jabal Musa, rising 2,285 meters above sea level in the southern Sinai Peninsula. At its base lies the ancient Saint Catherine's Monastery, one of the oldest Christian monasteries in the world, continuously inhabited since the 6th century CE. The monastery is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

The Sinai Peninsula itself is a place of extraordinary importance in religious history, described in the Hebrew Bible as the setting for forty years of Israelite wandering following the Exodus from Egypt. The landscape — rugged granite peaks, narrow wadis, and sparse desert vegetation — retains a stark grandeur that pilgrims, travelers, and mystics have found conducive to spiritual reflection for thousands of years.

## History & Origins

The identification of a specific mountain in the Sinai Peninsula as the biblical Mount Sinai where Moses received the Law has been debated by scholars for centuries, and no archaeological evidence definitively confirms the identification. The biblical account in Exodus describes the mountain in terms that have led different scholars to propose a variety of locations, including sites in northwestern Saudi Arabia and the Sinai highlands.

Nevertheless, the tradition locating Mount Sinai at Jabal Musa in the southern Sinai has been accepted by the major Abrahamic religious traditions since at least the 4th century CE, when Christian monks began settling in the region. According to the Book of Exodus, Moses led the Israelites out of Egypt, crossed the Red Sea (or "Reed Sea"), and journeyed through the wilderness to the holy mountain — identified in different biblical texts as Sinai or Horeb. At the mountain, God appeared in fire and smoke, and Moses received the Ten Commandments inscribed on stone tablets, as well as the broader corpus of Law (Torah) that would define the covenant between God and Israel.

The narrative of Mount Sinai extends beyond the Ten Commandments. The mountain is also the site where Moses encountered God in the burning bush (Exodus 3), where the prophet Elijah fled and heard the "still small voice" of God (1 Kings 19), and where, in the New Testament interpretation, the giving of the Law at Sinai is interpreted typologically in relation to the new covenant. Islamic tradition (the Quran) also references "Tur Sinai" and Moses's encounter with God, describing the revelation as a foundational act of prophetic communication.

The first Christian hermits arrived in the Sinai region in the 3rd century CE, drawn by the biblical associations of the landscape. By the 4th century, organized communities of monks had established themselves. Around 330 CE, the Empress Helena (mother of Constantine), who made a major pilgrimage to the Holy Land and is credited with identifying many biblical sites, commissioned a small chapel at the traditional site of the burning bush.

The Emperor Justinian I (527–565 CE) ordered the construction of a fortified monastery at the site, partly to protect the Christian monks from raids and partly to mark the site's imperial religious significance. This monastery — dedicated to the Transfiguration of Christ and later renamed for Saint Catherine of Alexandria — is essentially the same complex that stands at the foot of Jabal Musa today, making it one of the oldest continuously operating Christian monasteries in the world.

## Religious Significance

For Jews, Mount Sinai/Horeb is the site of the most important event in their religious history: the giving of the Torah. The covenant made at Sinai between God and Israel is the foundational act that constitutes the Jewish people as a covenantal community. The Ten Commandments received at Sinai are the basis of Jewish (and indeed much of Western) law and ethics. The Sinai revelation is recalled in the Jewish liturgy daily and features prominently in the reading of the Torah and in the observance of Shavuot (the Festival of Weeks), which commemorates the giving of the Torah.

For Christians, Sinai carries complex typological significance. The law given through Moses at Sinai is seen in Christian theology as foreshadowing and being superseded by the "new law" of grace given through Jesus Christ. The Epistle to the Galatians develops a detailed allegory contrasting Sinai (representing the law of slavery) with Jerusalem (representing the freedom of the Gospel). Nevertheless, Sinai retains its status as a holy mountain — a place where God was revealed in fire and storm — and has been a destination for Christian pilgrims since antiquity.

For Muslims, Moses (Musa) is one of the greatest prophets in Islam, and the Quranic accounts of his encounter with God at "the sacred valley of Tuwa" and at the burning bush are important revelations. The Quran (7:143) describes Moses asking to see God directly and being told this is impossible — God's appearance on the mountain causes it to crumble and Moses to fall unconscious. The tradition of Moses at Sinai is thus woven into Islamic prophetology.

## Architecture & Description

Saint Catherine's Monastery, at the foot of Jabal Musa at an elevation of approximately 1,570 meters, is the primary architectural complex at the site. It is one of the oldest and most important Christian monasteries in the world, continuously inhabited since Justinian's construction in the 6th century.

The monastery is enclosed by massive granite walls, 10–15 meters high, built by Justinian's engineers. Within these walls is a complex of buildings including the Basilica of the Transfiguration — a remarkably well-preserved early Byzantine church — and numerous other structures including a mosque (for the use of local Muslim guides and workers, built in the 11th century), cells for the monks, a library, and a refectory.

The Basilica of the Transfiguration is one of the most important surviving examples of early Christian architecture and art. The apse mosaic — depicting the Transfiguration of Christ — dates from the Justinianic period (6th century) and is one of the finest Byzantine mosaics in the world. The monastery also contains an extraordinary collection of early Christian icons, many of which are among the oldest surviving panel paintings in the world. The monastery's library holds approximately 3,300 manuscripts, including the famous Codex Sinaiticus — a 4th-century Greek manuscript of the Bible, one of the oldest and most complete biblical manuscripts known. (The Codex itself was taken by German biblical scholar Constantin von Tischendorf in the 19th century and is now divided between the British Library, the Russian National Library, the Leipzig University Library, and the monastery itself.)

The Chapel of the Burning Bush is located behind the apse of the main church, marking the traditional site of Moses's encounter with the burning bush. Access to this chapel is restricted, as shoes must be removed and the space is considered particularly sacred.

The burning bush itself — a living specimen of Rubus sanctus (sacred bramble) — grows in the monastery courtyard, tended by the monks. It is claimed to be the original bush or a descendant of it; the monastery records no instance of the plant having died and been replaced. Whether or not this claim is literally true, the bush has been growing at the monastery since at least the 4th century.

Jabal Musa itself rises above the monastery. The summit can be reached by two routes: the "Steps of Repentance" — approximately 3,750 stone steps cut into the granite rock face, traditionally assembled by monks as an act of penance — or a longer, gentler camel path. At the summit, approximately 2,285 meters, is a small 19th-century chapel and a mosque. The views from the summit across the Sinai highlands are spectacular.

## Pilgrimage & Rituals

Pilgrimage to Mount Sinai is deeply embedded in the devotional practice of Jews, Christians, and Muslims. The summit is accessible year-round, and thousands of pilgrims and tourists make the ascent annually, most choosing to climb at night to arrive at the summit for sunrise — an experience of extraordinary natural beauty and spiritual resonance.

The Christian monastic tradition of Saint Catherine's continues to this day, with a small community of Greek Orthodox monks from the Sinai Church (a branch of the Greek Orthodox Church of Jerusalem) maintaining the monastery and receiving pilgrims. The monastery is open to visitors for part of each morning, except on Sundays and feast days.

For Christian pilgrims, the visit typically includes a tour of the monastery, veneration of the burning bush, and the ascent of Jabal Musa. During major Christian festivals, the monks conduct liturgies at the summit chapel.

Jewish pilgrimage to Mount Sinai has been less institutionalized than Christian practice, partly because the exact location is uncertain and partly because of Israel's strained relations with Egypt for much of modern history (Israeli tourists began visiting the Sinai freely only after the peace treaty of 1979 and Sinai's return to Egypt in 1982). Nevertheless, Jews with connections to the biblical narrative make the ascent as an act of spiritual connection with the foundational event of Jewish history.

## Modern Significance

Saint Catherine's Monastery and the surrounding Sinai landscape were designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2002, recognizing the site's outstanding universal value. The designation covers the monastery complex and its natural and cultural surroundings, acknowledging the extraordinary concentration of Byzantine art, early Christian manuscripts, and sacred landscape.

The Sinai Peninsula's political status has shaped pilgrimage patterns in the modern era. Israel captured the Sinai from Egypt during the 1967 Six-Day War, and the region was under Israeli control until its phased return to Egypt following the 1979 peace treaty, completed in 1982. During the Israeli period, archaeological surveys and tourist infrastructure were developed.

Since Egypt regained full control, tourism has developed significantly, though the broader Sinai Peninsula has faced security challenges in the 21st century from militant groups active in the region. Terrorist attacks targeting tourists in the Sinai — including the 2004 Taba bombings and the 2005 Sharm el-Sheikh bombings — affected visitor numbers, and ongoing instability in northern Sinai has created security concerns.

The monastery remains one of Egypt's most significant cultural and religious tourism destinations and a place of ongoing scholarly importance, with digitization projects capturing the monastery's vast manuscript collection for global access.

## Timeline

- **c. 1446 BCE (traditional)**: Moses receives the Ten Commandments at Sinai, according to biblical chronology.
- **c. 330 CE**: Empress Helena commissions a chapel at the site of the burning bush.
- **3rd–4th century CE**: Christian hermits and monks begin settling in the Sinai.
- **565–569 CE**: Emperor Justinian I builds the fortified monastery at the foot of Jabal Musa.
- **11th century CE**: A mosque is added within the monastery walls for local Muslim workers.
- **1844**: Constantin von Tischendorf visits the monastery; discovers pages of the Codex Sinaiticus.
- **1859**: Tischendorf obtains the Codex Sinaiticus; later gives it to the Russian Tsar.
- **1967**: Israel captures the Sinai Peninsula in the Six-Day War; archaeological surveys conducted.
- **1979**: Egypt-Israel peace treaty; phased Israeli withdrawal from Sinai begins.
- **1982**: Sinai Peninsula fully returned to Egypt; the monastery comes under Egyptian administration.
- **2002**: UNESCO inscribes Saint Catherine's Area as a World Heritage Site.
- **2004–2005**: Terrorist bombings in the Sinai affect tourism.
- **Present**: The monastery remains active; ongoing digitization of manuscripts and pilgrimage continues.
