## Summary

Russian poet who was one of the greatest of the 20th century. His refusal to accommodate Soviet power led to arrest and death in a transit camp. His poetry combines classical learning with modernist innovation.

## Additional Context

### Literary Movement

- Mandelstam is commonly associated with **Acmeism**, a Russian poetic movement that emphasized clarity, craft, and concrete imagery in contrast to Symbolist mysticism.
- His work is often described as combining classical allusion with modernist compression and musicality.

### Conflict with Stalinist Power

High-level, widely cited elements of his persecution include:

- A privately circulated **anti-Stalin satirical poem** (often called the "Stalin epigram") that led to arrest and internal exile.
- Continued surveillance and repeated punishment during the period of heightened repression.

### Nadezhda Mandelstam and Preservation of the Work

- Mandelstam's widow, **Nadezhda Mandelstam**, is frequently credited with helping preserve his poems through memorization and clandestine copying when written texts were dangerous to keep.
- Her later memoirs became influential accounts of Soviet cultural repression.

### Legacy

- Today he is widely studied in world literature curricula as a major 20th-century poet and as a symbol of artistic resistance under totalitarian systems.
- Translations and critical editions continue to shape how his work is read outside Russian.
