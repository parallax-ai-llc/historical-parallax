## Summary

Nuclear reactor explosion at the Chernobyl plant on April 26, 1986, the worst nuclear disaster in history. It released 400 times more radiation than the Hiroshima bomb and displaced 350,000 people.

## Background

The Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant was located near Pripyat in the Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic (now Ukraine). Reactor 4 was an RBMK-design graphite-moderated reactor. The accident occurred during a late-night safety test involving operational changes and disabling of certain safety systems.

## Accident and immediate response

The explosion and subsequent fire released radioactive material into the atmosphere. Plant workers, firefighters, and other first responders were exposed while attempting to contain the situation. Authorities evacuated Pripyat and later expanded evacuations and resettlement from surrounding areas as contamination patterns became clearer.

## International impacts

Radioactive fallout affected parts of Ukraine, Belarus, and Russia, and measurable contamination was detected across Europe. The event influenced international nuclear safety cooperation, including incident reporting norms and the strengthening of safety culture discussions.

## Health and environmental effects

Estimates of long-term health impacts vary depending on methodology and which exposures are included. Certain outcomes, such as increased thyroid cancer risk among exposed populations (particularly children) in affected regions, are frequently discussed in public-health literature.

## Containment and the exclusion zone

The damaged reactor was initially enclosed in a “sarcophagus,” later supplemented by the New Safe Confinement structure designed to reduce releases and enable safer decommissioning work. The surrounding exclusion zone became a long-term restricted area managed for contamination control, monitoring, and limited industrial activity.

## Legacy

Chernobyl remains a reference case for the interaction between engineering design, operational decision-making, emergency preparedness, and government communication during disasters. It also continues to shape public perception of nuclear power and risk.
