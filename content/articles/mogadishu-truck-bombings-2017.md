## Summary

Two truck bombs detonated in Mogadishu killing 587 people in the deadliest terrorist attack in African history. A single blast at the Zoobe junction destroyed an entire block.

## Background

Mogadishu had experienced years of insurgent violence, with vehicle-borne explosive devices used periodically against government, security, and civilian targets. By 2017, the militant group al-Shabaab remained the dominant insurgent actor in Somalia, and major attacks had continued despite ongoing security operations.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- A large truck carrying explosives detonated near the Zoobe junction (KM5) in Mogadishu.
- The blast and resulting fires caused extensive damage to buildings, vehicles, and nearby infrastructure.
- A second explosion was reported elsewhere in the city the same day, compounding the emergency response.

## Casualties and Impact

The attack is widely reported as the deadliest terrorist incident in Somalia’s history and among the deadliest in Africa. Large numbers of civilians were killed and injured, with many victims affected by the intensity of the blast and subsequent fires.

## Response

- Somali authorities coordinated emergency medical care, blood donations, and recovery operations.
- The government declared national mourning and held public memorials.
- International governments and organizations issued condemnations and offered assistance.

## Attribution (Reported)

No group immediately and formally claimed responsibility in the aftermath. Somali officials and many media reports nevertheless pointed to al-Shabaab as the likely perpetrator based on prior attack patterns and the group’s operational presence in the country.

## Legacy

The bombings became a defining reference point in discussions about Somalia’s security situation, urban resilience, and the humanitarian toll of insurgent violence. The scale of the casualties and destruction also prompted renewed attention to victim identification, survivor support, and long-term memorialization.
