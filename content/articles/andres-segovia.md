## Summary

Andrés Segovia Torres (1893–1987) was a Spanish classical guitarist widely regarded as the father of the modern classical guitar. Through decades of relentless performance and advocacy, he single-handedly elevated the guitar from a parlor instrument associated with folk and popular music to a respected concert instrument capable of filling the world's greatest halls. His technical innovations, transcriptions of Baroque and Renaissance repertoire, and insistence on professional standards transformed how the instrument was taught, played, and perceived.

Segovia's impact extended far beyond his own performances. He commissioned new works from prominent composers, established a global pedagogy through masterclasses, and recorded prolifically, producing a catalog that remains a reference point for guitarists today. Without his efforts, the classical guitar might never have entered the mainstream concert repertoire or the conservatory curriculum.

## Early Life & Background

Segovia was born on February 21, 1893, in Linares, Jaén, in southern Spain. Raised largely by an aunt and uncle in Granada after his parents relocated, he grew up surrounded by the deep Andalusian guitar tradition. His family opposed his interest in the guitar, considering it a low-class instrument, and pushed him toward the piano or cello. Undeterred, Segovia largely taught himself, rejecting the flamenco technique dominant in Granada and developing his own right-hand fingerstyle approach that prioritized tonal clarity and projection.

He studied briefly at the Granada Musical Center but was essentially self-taught, a fact he emphasized throughout his life as evidence of the guitar's exclusion from formal musical institutions. By his early teens he had resolved to make the guitar a legitimate concert instrument.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Segovia gave his first public recital in Granada in 1909 at the age of 16. His Madrid debut followed in 1912, and a Barcelona recital in 1916 drew critical attention. In 1919 he performed in South America, beginning an international touring career. His Paris debut in 1924 was a landmark event; the audience included Pablo Casals and other leading musicians, and the critical reception confirmed his European standing.

In 1928 he made his first tour of the United States, performing at Town Hall in New York to considerable acclaim. Throughout the 1920s and 1930s he commissioned works from composers including Manuel Ponce, Federico Moreno Torroba, Joaquín Turina, Heitor Villa-Lobos, and Mario Castelnuovo-Tedesco, substantially expanding the original guitar repertoire.

---

## Key Achievements

Segovia produced landmark transcriptions of Bach lute suites and keyboard works, making Baroque counterpoint a cornerstone of the guitar repertoire. His 1927 recordings for HMV were among the first classical guitar recordings of lasting significance. He developed the Segovia scales, a systematic technical exercise still used in conservatories worldwide. In 1981 he was awarded the Gold Medal of the Royal Philharmonic Society, and King Juan Carlos I of Spain granted him the title Marqués de Salobreña in 1981.

---

## Controversies & Criticism

Segovia has been criticized for the liberties he took in his Bach transcriptions, altering rhythms, dynamics, and ornamentation in ways subsequent scholars considered historically unfaithful. Some contemporaries and later guitarists, including Julian Bream and John Williams, diverged from his interpretive approach. He was also accused of gatekeeping — controlling access to publishers and concert opportunities for guitarists who did not conform to his aesthetic standards, and of being dismissive of flamenco and electric guitar traditions.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Segovia taught masterclasses at Santiago de Compostela from 1957 onward and in Siena, shaping generations of guitarists including John Williams, Oscar Ghiglia, and Alirio Díaz. His advocacy transformed conservatory curricula across Europe and the Americas. He performed actively into his nineties, giving what proved to be his final recital in 1987, the year of his death on June 2 in Madrid. The Andrés Segovia International Guitar Competition, held in Linares, continues to bear his name.
