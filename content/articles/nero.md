## Summary

Nero Claudius Caesar Augustus Germanicus (December 15, AD 37 – June 9, AD 68) was the fifth Roman Emperor, ruling from AD 54 to 68 and the last emperor of the Julio-Claudian dynasty.[^1] Born Lucius Domitius Ahenobarbus, he ascended to the throne at just sixteen years old following the death of his adoptive father, Emperor Claudius.[^2] His reign is characterized by a stark contrast: the first five years, known as the "Quinquennium Neronis," were marked by competent governance and fiscal reform, while his later years descended into alleged tyranny, extravagance, and violence, culminating in matricide, the persecution of Christians following the Great Fire of Rome, and ultimately his suicide in AD 68.[^3] Though ancient sources overwhelmingly portray him as a debauched tyrant, modern scholarship has reassessed his legacy, recognizing both his administrative achievements and his passionate, if controversial, patronage of the arts.[^4]

## Early Life and Background

Nero was born on December 15, AD 37, at Antium (modern Anzio), a coastal town south of Rome, to Gnaeus Domitius Ahenobarbus and Agrippina the Younger, the great-granddaughter of Emperor Augustus.[^5] His father, Domitius, was reportedly a man of violent temper and questionable character; when congratulated by friends on the birth of his son, he allegedly replied that any child born to him and Agrippina would have a detestable nature and become a public danger.[^6] This ominous prediction would later be used to frame Nero's character in ancient historical accounts. Domitius died when Nero was only three years old, in AD 41, leaving the young boy without a father during his formative years.[^7]

Nero's early childhood was marked by instability and hardship. After Emperor Caligula's death and the ascension of Claudius, Nero's mother Agrippina was accused of plotting against the emperor and was exiled to the remote island of Pontia in the Mediterranean Sea.[^8] Nero's inheritance was seized, and he was sent to live with his paternal aunt, Domitia Lepida, who provided him with minimal education. During this period, his tutors consisted primarily of a dancing master and a barber—a far cry from the classical education expected of Roman nobility.[^9] This neglect would later be remedied, but it reflected the uncertain political position of the young Nero during Claudius's early reign.

Nero's fortunes changed dramatically when Claudius recalled Agrippina from exile and restored her property and status. In AD 49, when Nero was eleven years old, Agrippina married Emperor Claudius, becoming empress.[^10] This marriage was politically strategic: Agrippina was determined to secure power for herself and her son. In AD 50, Claudius formally adopted Nero, who then took the name Nero Claudius Caesar Drusus Germanicus.[^11] This adoption placed Nero ahead of Claudius's biological son, Britannicus, in the line of succession—a move that would have fatal consequences for Britannicus. Following his adoption, Nero received a proper education under the tutelage of the famous Stoic philosopher Seneca the Younger, who would become one of his principal advisors.[^12] Nero also married Claudius's daughter Octavia in AD 53, further cementing his position as heir apparent.[^13]

## Rise to Power

On October 13, AD 54, Emperor Claudius died suddenly after eating a dish of mushrooms.[^14] Many ancient historians, including Tacitus and Suetonius, claim that Agrippina poisoned Claudius to ensure Nero's succession, though this remains a matter of historical debate.[^15] Regardless of the true cause of Claudius's death, Nero, at just sixteen years old, was swiftly proclaimed emperor with the backing of the Praetorian Guard—whose loyalty Agrippina had secured through bribes and political maneuvering—and the Roman Senate.[^16] For the first time in Roman history, absolute imperial power was vested in a mere teenager.[^17]

The early years of Nero's reign, from AD 54 to 59, are often referred to as the "Quinquennium Neronis" (Nero's Five Years) and are regarded by historians as a period of competent, even enlightened governance.[^18] This was largely due to the influence of three key figures: Nero's mother Agrippina the Younger, his tutor Seneca the Younger, and Sextus Afranius Burrus, the prefect of the Praetorian Guard.[^19] These advisors effectively governed the empire on Nero's behalf, guiding him through the complexities of imperial administration and keeping his more erratic tendencies in check.

During this period, Nero implemented several progressive reforms. He put an end to the secret trials and influence of corrupt freedmen that had characterized the later years of Claudius's reign, and he granted the Senate greater independence in legislative and judicial matters.[^20] His government forbade gladiatorial contests involving bloodshed, banned capital punishment in certain circumstances, reduced taxes, and granted slaves the right to bring civil complaints against unjust masters.[^21] Nero also instituted fiscal reforms, placing tax collectors under stricter supervision by establishing local offices to monitor their activities, which reduced corruption and was well received by the populace.[^22] He provided assistance to cities that had suffered disasters and, at the request of the Jewish historian Flavius Josephus, extended aid to the Jewish population.[^23]

However, Nero's relationship with his mother Agrippina became increasingly strained. Agrippina had expected to wield power through her son, but Nero, encouraged by Seneca and Burrus, sought to assert his independence.[^24] Agrippina's interference in state affairs and her attempts to control Nero's personal life—particularly his romantic relationship with Poppaea Sabina—created tension that would ultimately have tragic consequences.[^25]

## Major Achievements

Nero's reign saw significant achievements in military, diplomatic, cultural, and infrastructural domains, particularly during the first decade of his rule. On the military front, his general Gnaeus Domitius Corbulo successfully concluded the Roman-Parthian War of 58–63 AD, securing a favorable peace treaty that resolved the ongoing conflict over Armenia.[^26] According to the terms approved by Nero, the Parthian candidate Tiridates remained king of Armenia but had to renounce his allegiance to Parthia and recognize Roman suzerainty, effectively making Armenia a Roman client state.[^27] This diplomatic achievement demonstrated Nero's capacity for strategic compromise and secured Rome's eastern frontier for years to come.

In the western provinces, the Roman general Suetonius Paulinus successfully quashed a major revolt in Britain led by Queen Boudica in AD 60–61, preserving Roman control over the province despite suffering initial setbacks.[^28] Though the rebellion was costly and brutal, the eventual Roman victory consolidated imperial authority in Britain and demonstrated the effectiveness of Rome's military apparatus under Nero's reign.

Nero's most distinctive achievements lay in his passionate patronage of the arts and culture, which set him apart from his predecessors. He was a skilled lyre player, singer, and poet who not only appreciated the arts but actively participated in public performances and artistic competitions—behavior considered scandalous and beneath the dignity of a Roman emperor by the senatorial elite.[^29] Nero organized lavish festivals, sponsored theatrical productions, and encouraged the work of prominent artists and poets throughout the empire.[^30] He inaugurated the Neronian Games in AD 60, a quinquennial competition modeled on Greek athletic and artistic festivals, featuring contests in poetry, theater, music, and athletics as counterattractions to traditional gladiatorial combat.[^31] These games reflected Nero's Hellenistic cultural values and his desire to elevate Rome's cultural life to match the sophistication of Greek civilization.

In AD 67, Nero traveled to Greece, where he participated in various artistic and athletic competitions, reportedly winning over 1,800 first prizes—though these victories were undoubtedly influenced by his imperial status.[^32] Nevertheless, his genuine passion for Greek culture was evident, and he declared Greece free and exempt from Roman taxation, a gesture that, while fiscally irresponsible, demonstrated his philhellenism.[^33]

Nero also initiated several ambitious public works projects. Following the Great Fire of Rome in AD 64, which destroyed much of the city, Nero oversaw extensive reconstruction efforts with improved urban planning, including wider streets, regulated building heights, and the use of fire-resistant materials.[^34] The most famous—and controversial—of his building projects was the Domus Aurea (Golden House), an extravagant palace complex that covered a vast area in the heart of Rome, featuring elaborate frescoes, rotating dining rooms, and artificial lakes.[^35] While the opulence of the Domus Aurea was resented by the public and later emperors, who dismantled it, the palace represented an extraordinary architectural achievement and a testament to Nero's artistic vision.[^36]

Nero's infrastructure projects extended beyond Rome. He commissioned the construction of roads, bridges, and aqueducts throughout the empire, and he even planned—though never completed—a canal across the Isthmus of Corinth to facilitate maritime trade.[^37] His administration also saw continued development of the Roman economy through trade expansion and monetary reforms, though these were later undermined by his extravagant spending.[^38]

The period of Nero's reign, particularly from AD 54 to 64, was described by some later historians as "one of the brightest pages in the history of Roman art," as he commissioned the building of theaters and promoted cultural development throughout the empire.[^39] His practical contributions to Rome's governance focused on diplomacy, trade, and culture, creating a legacy that, while complicated by his later excesses, included genuine administrative and cultural accomplishments.[^40]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Nero's reign was marred by a series of violent acts, alleged crimes, and extravagant behaviors that scandalized Rome and dominated ancient historical narratives. The first major controversy was his treatment of family members, beginning with the death of his stepbrother Britannicus in AD 55.[^41] Britannicus, the biological son of Emperor Claudius, died suddenly during a dinner feast, allegedly after drinking poisoned wine.[^42] Ancient sources, particularly Tacitus and Suetonius, claim that Nero ordered the assassination to eliminate a potential rival to the throne, though definitive proof has never been established.[^43]

The most shocking act of Nero's reign was the murder of his own mother, Agrippina the Younger, in AD 59.[^44] After years of increasing tension and Agrippina's persistent interference in state affairs—particularly her opposition to Nero's relationship with Poppaea Sabina—Nero decided to eliminate her.[^45] According to ancient accounts, he made several failed attempts, including a sabotaged boat designed to collapse and drown her, before finally sending assassins to her villa, where she was brutally stabbed to death.[^46] This act of matricide scandalized Roman society and permanently damaged Nero's reputation, marking a turning point in his reign from competent administrator to perceived tyrant.[^47]

Nero's treatment of his wives was equally controversial. He divorced and exiled his first wife, Octavia (daughter of Claudius), in AD 62 to marry his mistress Poppaea Sabina, and later had Octavia executed on false charges of adultery.[^48] Poppaea herself died in AD 65, allegedly kicked to death by Nero while she was pregnant, though some sources suggest she died from complications of childbirth.[^49] Nero subsequently married Statilia Messalina, but not before having her husband executed.[^50]

The Great Fire of Rome in July AD 64 remains one of the most debated events of Nero's reign.[^51] The fire began near the Circus Maximus on the night of July 18 and burned for six days before reigniting, ultimately lasting nine days.[^52] Of Rome's fourteen districts, three were completely destroyed, seven were severely damaged, and only four escaped unscathed.[^53] Ancient historians Tacitus and Suetonius suggest that Nero was either indifferent to the catastrophe or even deliberately started the fire to clear land for his palatial building projects.[^54] The famous image of Nero "fiddling while Rome burned" (though the fiddle had not yet been invented) became an enduring symbol of imperial indifference and decadence.[^55]

However, modern scholarship challenges this narrative. Nero was not even in Rome when the fire began—he was at his villa in Antium and rushed back to the city to organize relief efforts.[^56] He opened his palaces to homeless citizens, arranged for food supplies, and implemented new building codes to prevent future fires.[^57] Nevertheless, to deflect blame and public suspicion, Nero scapegoated the Christian community, initiating the first major persecution of Christians in Roman history.[^58] According to Tacitus, Christians were arrested and subjected to grotesque public executions: some were torn apart by hunting dogs, others were crucified, and still others were burned alive as human torches to illuminate Nero's gardens at night.[^59] This persecution established a precedent for Roman treatment of Christians and secured Nero's place in Christian tradition as a figure of evil—some early Christians even identified him with the Antichrist.[^60]

The end of Nero's reign was marked by political trials and conspiracies. The Pisonian conspiracy in AD 65, a plot by senators and equestrians to assassinate Nero and replace him with Gaius Calpurnius Piso, was discovered and brutally suppressed.[^61] Nero executed numerous alleged conspirators, including Seneca, his former tutor and advisor, who was forced to commit suicide despite uncertain evidence of his involvement.[^62] Other victims included the poet Lucan and the Stoic philosopher Thrasea Paetus.[^63] These purges created an atmosphere of fear and paranoia in Rome's upper classes and further alienated the Senate from Nero's rule.

Nero's extravagance was another source of criticism. According to Suetonius, Nero declared that "there is no other way of enjoying riches and money than by riotous extravagance," and he kept no account of his spending.[^64] His construction of the Domus Aurea was particularly resented, as it consumed vast public lands in the heart of Rome for the emperor's private pleasure.[^65] Graffiti appeared on the palace walls reading, "Rome is becoming one house. Flee to Veii, citizens, if this house doesn't seize Veii too!"[^66]

## Personal Life

Nero's personal life was characterized by complex relationships, artistic passions, and behaviors that scandalized Rome's traditional elite. His relationship with his mother Agrippina dominated his early years; she had orchestrated his rise to power and initially wielded considerable influence over him.[^67] However, as Nero matured, he increasingly resented her controlling nature and interference in both political and personal matters.[^68] Their relationship deteriorated dramatically when Agrippina opposed his affair with Poppaea Sabina, ultimately leading to her murder in AD 59.[^69]

Nero's romantic life was tumultuous and marked by violence. His first marriage to Octavia, daughter of Emperor Claudius, was a political arrangement that produced no children and was reportedly loveless.[^70] Nero openly conducted affairs and eventually divorced Octavia in AD 62 to marry his mistress Poppaea Sabina, whom he had been pursuing for years.[^71] Poppaea bore him a daughter, Claudia Augusta, who died in infancy in AD 63.[^72] Poppaea herself died in AD 65 under controversial circumstances—ancient sources claim Nero kicked her to death while she was pregnant, though modern historians suggest she may have died from childbirth complications.[^73]

After Poppaea's death, Nero's behavior became increasingly erratic. According to Suetonius, he sought out boys who resembled Poppaea and even had a young freedman named Sporus castrated and married him in a public ceremony, dressing him in the regalia of an empress.[^74] Nero also reportedly married a freedman named Pythagoras, in which Nero played the role of the bride.[^75] These relationships, whether accurately reported or exaggerated by hostile sources, were considered deeply scandalous by Roman standards.

Nero's greatest passion was the arts. He fancied himself a talented musician, singer, poet, and actor, devoting considerable time to artistic pursuits.[^76] He took his performances seriously, training his voice with vocal coaches, lying on his back with lead weights on his chest to strengthen his diaphragm, and observing strict dietary regimens to preserve his vocal quality.[^77] Nero performed publicly in theaters and competitions, which was considered deeply shameful for a Roman emperor—the elite viewed theatrical performance as the domain of slaves and lowborn entertainers.[^78] Nevertheless, Nero declared that "the Greeks were the only ones who had an ear for music and that they alone were worthy of his efforts," reflecting his admiration for Greek culture.[^79]

His personality was reportedly complex and contradictory. Early in his reign, under the guidance of Seneca and Burrus, he showed signs of clemency and administrative competence.[^80] However, as he eliminated his advisors and asserted his independence, he became increasingly autocratic, paranoid, and prone to violence.[^81] Ancient sources describe him as vain, theatrical, and desperate for public acclaim, yet also capable of charm and generosity.[^82] He was known to wander the streets of Rome at night in disguise, engaging in pranks and brawls with ordinary citizens.[^83]

Nero's relationship with religion was unconventional. He was fascinated by mystery cults and Eastern religions, and his later persecution of Christians stemmed more from political expediency than religious conviction.[^84] He reportedly dabbled in various philosophical and spiritual practices, though he showed little deep commitment to any particular tradition.[^85]

## Legacy

Nero's legacy is among the most contested and complex in Roman history, shaped by drastically different perspectives from ancient sources, Christian tradition, popular memory, and modern scholarship. In the immediate aftermath of his death, the Roman Senate declared him a public enemy (hostis publicus) and ordered his images destroyed in a practice known as damnatio memoriae.[^86] His suicide on June 9, AD 68, ended the Julio-Claudian dynasty and plunged Rome into civil war—the Year of the Four Emperors—which saw four men claim the throne in rapid succession before Vespasian established the Flavian dynasty.[^87]

Ancient Roman historians—particularly Tacitus, Suetonius, and Cassius Dio—portrayed Nero as the archetypal bad emperor: tyrannical, matricidal, debauched, and incompetent.[^88] These accounts, written during or after the Flavian dynasty, served partly as propaganda to legitimize Nero's successors by contrasting their virtues with his alleged vices.[^89] The image of Nero as a monster became deeply embedded in Roman collective memory and was later amplified by Christian writers, who remembered him as the first emperor to persecute their faith and identified him with apocalyptic evil—some even speculating he was the Beast of Revelation or an incarnation of the Antichrist.[^90]

However, Nero's legacy was not uniformly negative among all segments of Roman society. Modern scholarship has revealed that while the Senate and wealthy classes welcomed his death, the general populace remained "loyal to the end and beyond," with later emperors Otho and Vitellius both appealing to nostalgia for Nero to gain popular support.[^91] In the eastern provinces, particularly in Greece and Asia Minor, Nero was remembered fondly for his philhellenism, his declaration of Greek freedom, and his patronage of culture.[^92] So persistent was popular affection for Nero that several "false Neros" appeared in the decades following his death, pretenders who claimed to be the emperor returned from exile, attracting significant followings before being suppressed.[^93]

Nero's cultural legacy is significant but complicated. His passionate promotion of the arts, establishment of Greek-style festivals, and personal performances represented a democratization of culture that challenged Roman aristocratic norms.[^94] The architectural innovations seen in the Domus Aurea influenced later Roman building, and his urban planning reforms following the Great Fire—wider streets, height restrictions, fire-resistant materials—improved Rome's infrastructure despite the controversy surrounding his palace.[^95] His reign is described as "one of the brightest pages in the history of Roman art," reflecting genuine cultural achievements alongside political failures.[^96]

In medieval and Renaissance Europe, Nero became a symbol of tyranny and artistic excess, featured in literature, drama, and moral treatises as a cautionary tale.[^97] His image as a persecutor of Christians ensured his vilification in Christian-dominated societies, while his supposed role in burning Rome made "Nero" synonymous with destructive leadership.[^98]

Modern archaeological and historical research has complicated this picture considerably. Scholars now recognize that many accounts of Nero's crimes were exaggerated or fabricated by hostile sources with political motivations.[^99] Re-examination of the Great Fire evidence suggests Nero likely did not start it and actually responded competently to the crisis, even if his subsequent building projects were self-serving.[^100] His administrative reforms, diplomatic achievements with Parthia, and fiscal policies during the "Quinquennium Neronis" demonstrate capacity for effective governance when properly advised.[^101]

Nero's legacy ultimately embodies the tension between autocracy and republican values, between Hellenistic culture and Roman tradition, and between artistic passion and political responsibility. He remains a figure of endless fascination precisely because he defies simple categorization—neither purely monster nor misunderstood visionary, but a complex, flawed individual whose reign revealed both the possibilities and dangers of imperial power.[^102]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Modern scholarly reassessment has significantly challenged the uniformly negative portrayal of Nero found in ancient sources. Historians now recognize that the traditional image of Nero as an unmitigated tyrant "rests on extremely tenuous and in many cases untenable assumptions."[^103] Several factors have contributed to this revisionist perspective.

First, scholars have questioned the reliability and objectivity of ancient sources. Tacitus, Suetonius, and Cassius Dio all wrote during or after the Flavian dynasty, which had a vested interest in portraying the last Julio-Claudian emperor negatively to legitimize its own rule.[^104] These historians also belonged to the senatorial class, which had been alienated by Nero's autocratic style and preference for popular entertainment over traditional aristocratic values.[^105] Recent scholarship suggests that "depictions by ancient historians were biased and may have been influenced by storytelling tropes and political motivations."[^106]

Second, modern historians emphasize Nero's genuine administrative and diplomatic accomplishments, particularly during the first decade of his reign. The "Quinquennium Neronis" saw progressive reforms including fiscal responsibility, judicial improvements, reduced taxation, expansion of rights for slaves, and efficient administration overseen by competent advisors.[^107] His successful conclusion of the Parthian conflict through diplomacy rather than prolonged warfare demonstrated strategic wisdom, and his response to the Great Fire—organizing relief, implementing new building codes, and reconstructing the city with improved urban planning—showed administrative competence despite later controversial building projects.[^108]

Third, Nero's cultural policies and artistic patronage receive more favorable evaluation from scholars who recognize his genuine passion for Hellenistic culture. His establishment of Greek-style festivals, promotion of theatrical and athletic competitions, and personal participation in the arts, while scandalous to Roman aristocrats, represented a democratization of culture and a cosmopolitan vision of empire.[^109] Some scholars argue that Nero's spending on public works and charities was intended to ease economic troubles caused by deflation, suggesting a more nuanced understanding of his fiscal policy than simple extravagance.[^110]

Fourth, historians note the significant popular support Nero enjoyed, particularly among the lower classes and in the eastern provinces. The appearance of multiple "false Neros" after his death and the fact that later emperors appealed to his memory to gain popular backing indicates his reign was not universally despised.[^111] In Greece and Asia Minor, Nero was remembered as a benefactor and philhellene, suggesting that negative assessments were concentrated among Roman elites rather than the broader population.[^112]

Contemporary scholars continue to debate specific controversial events, including the extent of Nero's responsibility for the Great Fire, the circumstances of various family members' deaths, and the degree to which later sources exaggerated his theatrical performances and sexual behavior.[^113] This ongoing scholarly reassessment has produced not a complete rehabilitation of Nero, but a recognition that he was a more complex and capable figure than ancient propaganda suggested.[^114]

### Negative Views

Despite modern scholarly reassessment, substantial evidence supports severe criticism of Nero's character and reign, particularly during his final years. Even sympathetic historians acknowledge that Nero committed acts of extraordinary cruelty and demonstrated increasingly erratic and autocratic behavior as his reign progressed.[^115]

The murder of Agrippina in AD 59 remains one of the most shocking crimes in Roman history. While modern scholars debate many aspects of Nero's reign, the matricide is well-attested in multiple independent sources and represents an unambiguous moral transgression that violated fundamental Roman values of pietas (duty to family).[^116] The elaborate and cruel methods Nero employed—including the failed collapsing boat scheme before finally sending assassins—suggest premeditation and callousness.[^117]

The persecution of Christians following the Great Fire, while possibly exaggerated in Christian sources, is corroborated by the reputable Roman historian Tacitus, who described grotesque public executions including burning Christians alive as human torches.[^118] Even if Nero did not start the fire himself, his scapegoating of an unpopular minority group and authorization of spectacular cruelties represents a moral failure and abuse of power.[^119]

The political purges following the Pisonian conspiracy in AD 65 demonstrate Nero's growing paranoia and autocratic tendencies. The forced suicides of Seneca, Lucan, Petronius, and numerous others, including those with dubious connections to the actual plot, created an atmosphere of terror among Rome's elite.[^120] These purges eliminated many of Rome's finest minds and contributed to the alienation of the senatorial class that ultimately doomed Nero's reign.[^121]

Nero's fiscal irresponsibility is difficult to defend. While some spending on infrastructure and disaster relief was legitimate, the extravagance of the Domus Aurea and other personal projects drained the imperial treasury and required debasement of the currency, which had long-term economic consequences.[^122] Suetonius's report that Nero kept no account of his expenses and believed wealth should be enjoyed through "riotous extravagance" reflects an unsustainable approach to imperial finance.[^123]

Critics also point to Nero's abandonment of traditional Roman values in favor of theatrical performances and Greek cultural practices. While modern observers may view this cosmopolitanism favorably, contemporary Romans saw it as degrading to imperial dignity and undermining the cultural foundations of Roman power.[^124] His public performances, whatever their artistic merit, were considered shameful for an emperor and damaged Rome's prestige in the eyes of traditional observers.[^125]

The manner of Nero's death—fleeing Rome in disguise and requiring assistance to commit suicide while crying "What an artist dies in me!"—epitomizes for critics his self-absorption and failure to accept responsibility for his actions.[^126] His final words focused on his artistic self-image rather than the empire he had sworn to serve, suggesting to detractors a fundamental unsuitability for imperial leadership.[^127]

Even scholars who acknowledge bias in ancient sources generally concede that Nero's later reign was characterized by increasing violence, paranoia, and poor judgment that contributed to civil war and the collapse of the Julio-Claudian dynasty.[^128] While modern reassessment has complicated the simplistic "monster" narrative, it has not exonerated Nero from serious and legitimate criticisms of his rule.[^129]

## Famous Quotes

> "Qualis artifex pereo!" ("What an artist dies in me!")
> 
> Nero's reported last words before committing suicide on June 9, AD 68, reflecting his self-perception as an artist above all else and his vanity even in his final moments.[^130]

> "Hidden talents count for nothing."
> 
> This quote embodies Nero's belief in public performance and the necessity of displaying one's abilities, justifying his controversial decision to perform publicly despite the shame Roman aristocrats attached to theatrical performance.[^131]

> "There is no other way of enjoying riches and money than by riotous extravagance, declaring that only stingy and niggardly fellows kept a correct account of what they spent."
> 
> Reported by Suetonius in his biography, this quote captures Nero's attitude toward imperial finances and his justification for lavish spending on artistic projects and personal pleasure.[^132]

> "The Greeks were the only ones who had an ear for music and that they alone were worthy of his efforts."
> 
> This statement reflects Nero's philhellenism and his preference for Greek cultural values over traditional Roman aristocratic sensibilities, explaining his particular fondness for performing before Greek audiences.[^133]

> "Mushrooms must be the food of the gods."
> 
> A darkly ironic quote attributed to Nero, referencing the poisoned mushroom dish that allegedly killed Emperor Claudius and brought Nero to power, demonstrating his cynical humor about his path to the throne.[^134]
