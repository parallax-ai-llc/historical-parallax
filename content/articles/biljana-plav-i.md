## Summary

Biljana Plavšić was a Bosnian Serb political figure who served as president of Republika Srpska after the Bosnian War. At the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY), she pleaded guilty to crimes against humanity and received a prison sentence. She is often noted as one of the highest-ranking women convicted by an international war-crimes tribunal.

## Background

Before entering high-level politics, Plavšić had an academic career and is commonly described as having worked as a university professor in the sciences. Her transition from academia to nationalist politics is part of what made her public profile distinctive during the 1990s.

## Political Role (High Level)

During the breakup of Yugoslavia and the Bosnian War, Plavšić held senior positions in Bosnian Serb political structures. In historical accounts of the conflict, these institutions are discussed in connection with campaigns of persecution and forced displacement.

## ICTY Proceedings

- Plavšić surrendered to the tribunal and entered a guilty plea to crimes against humanity.
- The case is frequently summarized as an example of plea bargaining in international criminal justice, where admissions of responsibility may be weighed against other factors in sentencing.
- She served her sentence in Sweden and was released before completing the full term.

## Legacy and Controversy

Plavšić’s conviction is often referenced in discussions of accountability for political leaders during the Yugoslav wars. At the same time, debates over her public statements before and after conviction illustrate how legal judgments and post-war narratives can remain contested long after a conflict ends.

In transitional justice literature, her case is sometimes discussed alongside questions about the limits of individual trials in addressing collective violence and the politics of reconciliation.

It also highlights the role of senior political officeholders in enabling or constraining wartime policy.
