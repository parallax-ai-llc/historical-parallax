## Background: Colombia’s post-cartel trafficking landscape

- After the fragmentation of large cartels in the 1990s, trafficking networks in Colombia often became more decentralized, with regional groups controlling corridors, ports, and relationships with international buyers.
- In the 2000s, paramilitary demobilization and reorganization contributed to new criminal structures competing over territory and drug routes.
- Extradition to the United States has been a central enforcement tool in Colombia–US counternarcotics cooperation, especially for large-scale trafficking cases.

## What “drug lord” usually implies in this context

- Leadership typically involves control of logistics (production zones, labs, transport), financing, and protection networks.
- Many organizations function as alliances of cells rather than a single hierarchical cartel, which can make attribution and prosecution complex.
- Rivalries can drive cycles of violence as groups fight for control of strategic corridors.

## Arrest, extradition, and prosecution (high level)

- Arrests in Colombia can involve specialized police units and coordination with prosecutors and international partners.
- Extradition cases generally depend on treaty frameworks, domestic court approvals, and executive authorization.
- In US federal court, narcotics prosecutions commonly focus on conspiracy and trafficking charges with sentencing influenced by quantity, role, and cooperation.
