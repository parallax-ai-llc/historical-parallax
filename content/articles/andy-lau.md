## Summary

Andy Lau Tak-wah (born September 27, 1961) is one of Hong Kong's most enduring entertainment figures, having sustained a top-tier career across acting, singing, and film production for over four decades. He is one of the "Four Heavenly Kings" of Cantopop — a designation given in the early 1990s to the four dominant male pop stars in Hong Kong — alongside Jacky Cheung, Leon Lai, and Aaron Kwok.

His reach extends well beyond Hong Kong. Lau has released albums in Cantonese, Mandarin, and Japanese, sold an estimated 30 million records worldwide, and appeared in more than 100 films. He remains one of the highest-grossing actors in Chinese-language cinema history and a rare example of simultaneous, sustained success in both music and film.

## Early Life & Background

Andy Lau was born in Tai Po, New Territories, Hong Kong, the second of five children in a working-class family. His father drove a taxi; his mother managed the household. The family lived in public housing, and Lau has spoken openly about the financial pressures of his upbringing as a motivating force throughout his career.

He attended Pui Kiu Middle School and later enrolled in the Hong Kong TVB Artist Training Class in 1981, the same program that produced many of Hong Kong's most prominent entertainers. His time at TVB gave him foundational training in both acting and on-screen presentation.

## Rise to Prominence

Lau signed with TVB and quickly earned lead roles in television dramas during the early 1980s. His breakout came with the 1982 series *The Duke of Mount Deer*, which gave him widespread household recognition across Hong Kong.

His film career accelerated through collaborations with director John Woo and actor Chow Yun-fat, including *A Better Tomorrow* (1986), a film that defined Hong Kong's heroic bloodshed genre. He continued building his filmography throughout the late 1980s and 1990s while simultaneously launching a music career, releasing his debut Cantonese album in 1985.

By the early 1990s, he was among the best-selling recording artists in Asia and a consistent box-office draw. His 2002 film *Infernal Affairs*, co-starring Tony Leung, became a landmark of Hong Kong cinema and served as the basis for Martin Scorsese's *The Departed* (2006).

## Key Achievements

- Over 100 films as an actor, with several exceeding HK$100 million at the box office
- Estimated 30 million records sold worldwide across Cantonese, Mandarin, and Japanese releases
- Hong Kong Film Award for Best Actor for *Running Out of Time* (1999) and *Infernal Affairs* (2003)
- Founded Focus Films in 2000, producing critically and commercially successful Chinese-language cinema
- Named one of the "Four Heavenly Kings" of Cantopop — a title that defined an era of Hong Kong pop culture
- Multiple sold-out world tours, including a 2010 tour that spanned Asia, North America, and Europe

## Controversies & Criticism

Lau maintained a long-secret marriage to Malaysian model Carol Zhu Liqian, which became public in 2008 after press reports. Fans reacted strongly; some felt deceived given his carefully managed public image as perpetually single. He issued a public apology, acknowledging he had kept the relationship private to protect his family.

He has also faced scrutiny over financial losses suffered by fans who invested in a film fund he promoted in the mid-2000s, though he was not found legally liable.

## Legacy & Influence

Andy Lau represents a model of longevity in an industry known for short careers. His ability to navigate the commercial demands of both pop music and prestige cinema over four decades has made him a benchmark for ambition and discipline in Hong Kong entertainment. Younger generations of Chinese-language artists frequently cite him as a formative influence. His production work through Focus Films has helped sustain Hong Kong cinema's international profile during periods of industry contraction.
