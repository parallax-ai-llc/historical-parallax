## Summary

Dick Fosbury invented the 'Fosbury Flop' and won 1968 Olympic gold. His technique revolutionized high jumping worldwide.

## Background

Fosbury was an American track and field athlete best known for transforming the high jump. Prior to his rise, many elite jumpers used techniques such as the straddle or scissors style, approaching the bar and clearing it in ways that kept the torso facing downward or upright.

## The Fosbury Flop (Technique)

The Fosbury Flop is characterized by:

- A curved approach that sets up rotation.
- Takeoff that turns the jumper so the back faces the bar.
- Back-first clearance, allowing the jumper's hips to rise while the center of mass can pass below the bar.

This method took advantage of improved foam landing pits that made back landings safer than in earlier eras.

## Impact on the Sport

After Fosbury's Olympic success, the flop became the dominant technique in high jumping at the international level. Its widespread adoption is often cited as a clear example of how a technical innovation can rapidly reshape competitive standards in a mature sport.

Coaches and athletes integrated the flop into training systems, including drills for the curved approach, takeoff mechanics, and bar clearance timing.

## Later Life and Recognition

Beyond competition, Fosbury remained associated with the high jump community and was frequently referenced in discussions about sports innovation and biomechanics. His name became synonymous with the technique itself, and his Olympic win is commonly presented as the moment that validated the approach on the largest stage.
