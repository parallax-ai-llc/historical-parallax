## Summary

Dame Margot Fonteyn de Arias (1919–1991) was the preeminent British ballerina of the twentieth century and one of the most celebrated classical dancers in history. As the defining ballerina of the Royal Ballet for over three decades, she set a standard of technical refinement, dramatic intelligence, and stage presence that shaped the company's identity and elevated British ballet to international prominence.

Her partnership with Rudolf Nureyev, beginning in 1962, became one of the most celebrated collaborations in performing arts history. Although Fonteyn was 42 when they first danced together — an age at which most ballerinas had long retired — the pairing extended her career by more than a decade and produced performances of extraordinary intensity that remain legendary among dance historians and audiences alike.

## Early Life & Background

Born Margaret Evelyn Hookham on 18 May 1919 in Reigate, Surrey, Fonteyn showed early aptitude for dance. Her mother, a part-Irish, part-Brazilian woman with a love of the arts, encouraged her training from childhood. The family moved to Shanghai in the late 1920s, where Fonteyn studied under George Goncharov, a former member of the Ballets Russes. Returning to England in 1933, she enrolled at the Sadler's Wells Ballet School under the tutelage of Ninette de Valois. She studied with the Russian émigré teacher Serafina Astafieva and came under the formative influence of choreographer and teacher Nicholas Sergeyev.

## Rise to Prominence

Fonteyn joined the Vic-Wells Ballet (later Sadler's Wells, then the Royal Ballet) in 1934 at age 15. Her rise was rapid: by 1939 she was the company's undisputed principal ballerina. The choreographer Frederick Ashton became central to her development, creating roles specifically for her in works including *Ondine* (1958) and *Daphnis and Chloë* (1951). She danced the title roles in *Giselle*, *Swan Lake*, and *The Sleeping Beauty* to international acclaim, and her 1949 New York debut with Sadler's Wells was a landmark moment for British ballet in America.

## Key Achievements

Fonteyn's partnership with Rudolf Nureyev produced some of the most famous performances in ballet history, including their celebrated *Romeo and Juliet* (1965) choreographed by Kenneth MacMillan. She received a record 89 curtain calls after one performance of *Swan Lake* at the Vienna State Opera in 1964. She was appointed CBE in 1951 and created DBE (Dame Commander) in 1956. In 1979 she was named Prima Ballerina Assoluta by the Royal Ballet — a title of the highest distinction awarded only three times in the company's history.

## Controversies & Criticism

Fonteyn's personal life attracted significant press attention. Her husband, Panamanian politician Roberto Arias, was shot in 1964 and left quadriplegic; Fonteyn devoted considerable time and resources to his care. She also faced scrutiny over her involvement in a 1959 political coup attempt in Panama, for which she was briefly detained before being released without charge. Some critics argued that her extended career in partnership with Nureyev prolonged her beyond her peak technical years, though audiences and most reviewers disagreed.

## Legacy & Influence

Fonteyn's legacy is inseparable from the identity of the Royal Ballet and the international reputation of British classical dance. She demonstrated that a dancer trained outside the Russian tradition could achieve the highest world standards, and her partnership with Nureyev proved that ballet could command mainstream cultural attention. She served as President of the Royal Academy of Dance from 1954 to 1991. Fonteyn died on 21 February 1991 in Panama from ovarian cancer. The Margot Fonteyn International Ballet Competition continues to bear her name, and her performances remain among the most studied in ballet pedagogy.
