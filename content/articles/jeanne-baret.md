## Summary

Jeanne Baret was the first woman to complete a circumnavigation of the globe, disguising herself as a man to join Bougainville's expedition in 1766.

## Additional Context

## Historical Setting

- European scientific expeditions in the 18th century often combined naval exploration with systematic collection of plants, animals, and geographic observations.
- Women were frequently barred from serving on naval ships, which made Baret’s participation exceptional and risky.

## Role on the Bougainville Expedition

- Baret traveled as an assistant to the expedition’s naturalist (commonly associated with botanist Philibert Commerson).
- Her work is typically described as involving the gathering, preparing, and cataloging botanical specimens during landfalls.

## Discovery and Recognition

- Accounts of Baret’s journey have been reconstructed from ship logs, later writings, and the history of scientific collecting.
- Many specimens collected during the voyage contributed to European botanical knowledge, even when crediting practices of the era obscured assistants’ contributions.

## Legacy

- Baret is remembered as a symbol of the barriers women faced in exploration and science, and the ingenuity used to navigate them.
- In modern historical scholarship, her story is often used to discuss gender, labor, and recognition in the early history of natural science.
