## Summary

Controversial 1971 psychology experiment at Stanford where students assigned as guards became abusive toward students assigned as prisoners. The experiment was ended after 6 days due to the guards cruelty.

## Setup and conduct (high level)

The study was run in the basement of Stanford University’s psychology building and is closely associated with psychologist Philip Zimbardo. Participants were college students who were randomly assigned roles as “guards” or “prisoners” within a simulated jail environment.

## Why it became infamous

Within days, the situation escalated: some participants acting as guards engaged in degrading or coercive behavior, while some prisoners showed signs of severe stress. The study was terminated early, and it became widely cited in discussions of how social roles and institutional contexts can influence behavior.

## Ethical issues

The experiment is frequently used as a case study in research ethics, including concerns about informed consent, participant safety, and the difficulty of maintaining appropriate oversight when researchers are embedded in the situation being studied.

## Criticism and reassessment

Over time, researchers and journalists raised questions about the study’s methodology and the extent to which participant behavior may have been shaped by demand characteristics, coaching, or other experimental pressures. These critiques affected how the experiment is interpreted in psychology and in the public imagination.

## Legacy

Regardless of interpretation, the Stanford Prison Experiment remains a touchstone for debates about power, conformity, and the responsibilities of institutions—especially in education about ethics, prisons, and human-subjects research.
