## What the "demon" refers to

- **Maxwell's demon** is a famous *thought experiment* introduced by **James Clerk Maxwell** to illustrate a puzzle about the **second law of thermodynamics**.
- It imagines a tiny, intelligent "demon" that can observe individual molecules in a gas and operate a small door between two compartments.
- By selectively allowing fast molecules to pass one way and slow molecules the other way, the demon appears to create a **temperature difference** (and thus usable work) **without expending energy**, which would seem to reduce entropy.

## Historical origin

- Maxwell discussed the idea in the context of statistical mechanics, emphasizing that the second law has a **statistical** character rather than an absolute mechanical prohibition.
- The scenario became a standard reference point in debates about whether microscopic information and measurement could "beat" macroscopic irreversibility.

## Why it matters (thermodynamics & information)

- The paradox helped motivate a modern view: **information has thermodynamic consequences**.
- Later analyses argue that while the demon can in principle sort molecules, the full cycle (including **acquiring, storing, and resetting information**) restores compliance with the second law.
- A common resolution connects the minimum heat cost of erasing information (often discussed via **Landauer's principle**) to the entropy budget of the demon.

## Common modern interpretations

- **Measurement alone** is not always treated as the main cost; instead, the unavoidable cost can be placed on **memory reset/erasure** for a cyclic demon.
- The demon is often used as a bridge between:
  - classical thermodynamics
  - statistical mechanics
  - computation and information theory

## Related concepts and follow-ups

- **Szilard engine (1929)**: a one-molecule engine that makes the information/entropy bookkeeping explicit.
- **Brownian ratchet (Feynman)**: another illustration showing that thermal fluctuations prevent a perpetual-motion-like device.
- **Information thermodynamics**: modern research on feedback control, nonequilibrium systems, and bounds on work extraction with information.
