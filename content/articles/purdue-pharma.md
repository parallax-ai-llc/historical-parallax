## Background

Purdue Pharma is most closely associated with the marketing of prescription opioid painkillers in the United States, particularly OxyContin, during a period when opioid prescribing increased sharply. The company’s practices became a central focus of investigations into the broader opioid epidemic.

## Marketing and controversy

Critics and prosecutors alleged that Purdue promoted opioid products in ways that understated addiction risk and encouraged wider prescribing. Purdue has disputed some characterizations over time, but multiple legal actions and settlements documented serious misconduct claims.

## Litigation, bankruptcy, and settlements

Facing extensive lawsuits from states, municipalities, tribes, and other entities, Purdue sought bankruptcy protection and pursued a restructuring plan intended to channel claims into a settlement framework. These proceedings became a major test case for how mass-tort liabilities can be resolved through bankruptcy.

## The Sackler family

The Sackler family, owners of Purdue, became the subject of public scrutiny and legal arguments over accountability, financial transfers, and the scope of any legal releases. Debates over whether non-debtor releases should shield owners from civil liability have been especially prominent.

## Why it matters

The Purdue case is frequently cited in policy discussions about pharmaceutical marketing, opioid regulation, corporate criminal enforcement, and the design of compensation mechanisms for public-health harms.
