## Summary

Liberian warlord who captured and tortured President Samuel Doe to death while drinking beer, in a scene captured on video. He later became a senator and was never prosecuted for war crimes.

## Historical Context (Liberian Civil Wars)

Prince Johnson is primarily known in connection with the First Liberian Civil War (1989–1997), a conflict that involved multiple armed factions, foreign involvement, and widespread atrocities. The war followed years of political instability after the 1980 coup that brought Samuel Doe to power.

## Role as a Faction Leader

Johnson became a prominent commander within the insurgency against Doe and is associated with the Independent National Patriotic Front of Liberia (INPFL), a splinter faction from Charles Taylor's NPFL. Accounts of the period emphasize shifting alliances and factional control of territory, with armed groups competing for power in and around Monrovia.

## Capture of Samuel Doe and Aftermath

Johnson's capture of President Doe in 1990 became internationally notorious, in part because recordings of the event circulated widely. The episode has often been cited in discussions of impunity and the difficulty of establishing accountability during and after Liberia's wars.

## Postwar Politics

After periods abroad, Johnson re-entered Liberian politics and later served as a senator. His political career illustrates a broader post-conflict pattern in which former commanders and wartime figures were able to transition into formal political roles.

## Accountability and Transitional Justice

Liberia's Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) collected testimony about abuses committed during the conflict era and made recommendations that included prosecutions and other measures. Implementation has been contested and uneven, and debates over justice, reconciliation, and political stability have remained active in Liberian public life for years.
