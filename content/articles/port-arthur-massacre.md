## Summary

Mass shooting in Port Arthur, Tasmania on April 28, 1996 killing 35 people. It was Australias deadliest mass shooting and led to comprehensive gun reform.

## Event Overview

The Port Arthur massacre occurred at and around the Port Arthur Historic Site in Tasmania on 28 April 1996. The attack killed 35 people and injured many others, and it remains one of the deadliest mass shootings in modern Australian history.

## National and Political Impact

The event produced an unusually rapid national political response. Australia’s federal and state governments coordinated to pursue tighter firearms regulation, despite differences in jurisdictional authority over gun laws.

## National Firearms Agreement (NFA)

A key policy outcome was the **National Firearms Agreement**, which is commonly summarized as including:

- **Bans/restrictions** on certain rapid-firing long guns.
- **Uniform licensing and registration** frameworks across states and territories.
- **Safe storage** requirements and additional controls on sales and transfers.
- A large-scale **buyback program** to remove prohibited firearms from circulation.

## Longer-Term Legacy

Port Arthur is frequently referenced in international debates about gun policy as an example of sweeping reform following a single catastrophic event. Analysts often focus on the combination of political consensus, coordinated federal–state action, and the practical mechanism of a buyback.

## Notes on Terminology

In Australian public discussion, the massacre is sometimes discussed alongside broader questions of firearm access, public safety, and the balance between individual ownership and collective risk.
