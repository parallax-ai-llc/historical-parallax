## Context

The **Khasavyurt Accord** (often called the *Khasavyurt Agreement*) was signed on **31 August 1996** in Khasavyurt, Dagestan, to end active hostilities in the **First Chechen War**.

## Main Parties and Signatories

The agreement is commonly described as being signed by the lead representatives of:

- **Russian Federation** — **Alexander Lebed**, Secretary of the Security Council of the Russian Federation at the time.
- **Chechen Republic of Ichkeria** — **Aslan Maskhadov**, Chief of Staff (and later President) of the Chechen separatist leadership.

> Note: Many summaries focus on these two principal signatories; other officials participated in negotiations and implementation, but the widely cited signature pairing is Lebed–Maskhadov.

## What the Accord Did (High-Level)

- Established a framework to **stop large-scale fighting** and move toward a political settlement.
- Contributed to the **withdrawal of Russian forces** from Chechnya.
- **Deferred** a final decision on Chechnya’s political status to a later date (often described as postponing the status question until the early 2000s).

## Aftermath and Legacy

- The accord helped produce a period of **de facto separation** of Chechnya from direct Russian control in the late 1990s.
- The unresolved status and continued instability are frequently cited as factors in the lead-up to the **Second Chechen War** (beginning in 1999).

## Negotiation and implementation (high-level)

### Why the signatories matter

- **Alexander Lebed** was a prominent Russian security official at the time, often portrayed as playing a decisive role in pushing for an end to active combat.
- **Aslan Maskhadov** represented the Chechen separatist side’s military leadership and later became a key political figure.

### What was "deferred"

- Many accounts emphasize that the accord did not fully resolve sovereignty.
- Instead, it established an interim arrangement intended to stop fighting while postponing the political settlement.

### Why the accord is debated

- Supporters describe it as a pragmatic cessation of hostilities that reduced immediate casualties.
- Critics argue that deferring the status question left fundamental issues unresolved, contributing to later instability.
