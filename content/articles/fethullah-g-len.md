## Summary

Turkish cleric accused by the Turkish government of masterminding the 2016 coup attempt. He led a vast transnational movement with schools and media worldwide. Lived in self-exile in Pennsylvania since 1999. Turkey classified his movement as terrorist.

## Movement and Institutions

- **Hizmet ("Service")**: Gülen is associated with a religiously inspired civic network often described as the Gülen movement, emphasizing education, dialogue initiatives, and philanthropy.
- **Education footprint**: Supporters established schools, tutoring centers, and affiliated organizations in Turkey and internationally, which became a major source of the movement’s influence.
- **Media and business ties**: The movement was also linked to media outlets and business associations, contributing to a broad, transnational presence.

## Relationship with Turkish Politics

- **Early alignment and later conflict**: Analysts commonly describe an initial period of overlap between Gülen-aligned actors and Turkey’s governing AKP, followed by a sharp split.
- **2013–2014 rupture**: The political break escalated amid corruption investigations and power struggles within state institutions.
- **Government designation**: The Turkish government labels the movement as a terrorist organization and attributes significant state infiltration to Gülen-linked networks.

## Coup Allegations and Extradition Dispute

- **2016 coup attempt**: Following the failed coup, Turkish authorities accused Gülen of directing or orchestrating the attempt; Gülen denied involvement.
- **International legal process**: Turkey sought his extradition from the United States, leading to a prolonged legal and diplomatic dispute.
- **Broader crackdown**: The post-coup period included mass arrests, dismissals, and closures of institutions alleged to be affiliated with the movement.

## Exile and Later Years

- **U.S. residency**: Gülen lived in Pennsylvania from 1999 until his death, issuing statements and writings from abroad.
- **Public posture**: He framed the movement as nonviolent and education-focused, while critics and the Turkish state argued it operated as a covert power network.
