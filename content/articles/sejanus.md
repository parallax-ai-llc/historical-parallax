## Summary

Praetorian Guard prefect who effectively controlled Rome for the reclusive Emperor Tiberius. Executed with his family for plotting to seize the throne.

## Background and rise

Lucius Aelius Sejanus rose within the equestrian order and became prefect of the Praetorian Guard, the elite unit tasked with protecting the emperor. Under Tiberius, the Guard’s political importance increased, and Sejanus is portrayed in ancient sources as consolidating influence through appointments, patronage, and control over access to the emperor.

## Consolidation of power

Ancient narratives describe Sejanus as encouraging Tiberius’s withdrawal from day-to-day public life, while building a position as a de facto gatekeeper in Rome. He is also associated with political prosecutions and treason trials (*maiestas*), a feature of the period that later writers treated as emblematic of fear and factional struggle.

## Allegations and sources

Many specific claims—such as the alleged poisoning of Drusus—come from later historians (notably Tacitus and Suetonius) who wrote with their own agendas and limited access to imperial decision-making. Modern historians often treat details cautiously while still recognizing Sejanus as a central figure in the politics of Tiberius’s reign.

## Fall and execution

In 31 CE, Sejanus was abruptly denounced and arrested; he was executed after a rapid political reversal orchestrated through imperial correspondence and Senate action. Accounts describe subsequent punishment and reprisals against his associates and family.

## Legacy

Sejanus became a long-lasting symbol of the dangerous favorite or minister who accumulates power near the ruler, appearing in later literature and political commentary as a cautionary archetype.
