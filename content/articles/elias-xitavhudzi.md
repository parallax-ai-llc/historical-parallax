## Summary

South African serial killer who murdered at least 18 women in the Limpopo province during the 1990s. He was a traditional healer who killed his victims for muti (traditional medicine) purposes.

## Context

Xitavhudzi is often discussed in South African crime reporting alongside the phenomenon sometimes labeled “muti killings,” a term used in media and court contexts for murders linked—allegedly or demonstrably—to beliefs about harvesting body parts for rituals or traditional medicine. It is important to distinguish these criminal claims from the broader and legitimate practice of traditional healing, which is a recognized part of many communities.

## Pattern of Crimes

Summaries generally describe a multi-year series of murders in Limpopo during the 1990s, with victims primarily being women. High-level accounts emphasize repeated victimization over time and the difficulty of detecting a serial pattern across rural areas with limited investigative resources.

## Investigation and Trial

Authorities eventually linked cases through investigative work and evidence gathered over multiple incidents. After arrest, Xitavhudzi was prosecuted and later convicted, receiving multiple life sentences. Detailed court findings and the precise evidentiary narrative are typically captured in contemporaneous reporting rather than short encyclopedia summaries.

## Public Impact

The case is frequently cited in discussions of:

- the challenges of policing and forensic capacity in dispersed rural regions,
- community fear and rumor during unresolved serial-crime periods,
- how sensational framing can affect understanding of cultural practices.
