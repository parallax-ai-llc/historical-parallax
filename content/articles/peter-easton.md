## Summary

One of the most successful pirates ever who commanded 40 ships and terrorized the Atlantic. Retired as a wealthy marquis in the French Riviera, never captured or prosecuted.

## Background

- **Era:** Easton operated during the early 17th century, when Atlantic shipping lanes and fishing fleets created opportunities for piracy, privateering, and maritime raiding.
- **Origins:** He is typically described as English by origin, with activity tied to the North Atlantic, including waters near Newfoundland.

## Piracy and Power (High Level)

- **Fleet command:** Accounts credit him with commanding a substantial pirate fleet at his peak, reflecting how some pirate leaders functioned more like naval commanders than lone raiders.
- **Targets and tactics:** Successful pirates in this period often focused on seizing cargo and extracting ransoms, using intimidation and superior numbers rather than set-piece battles.

## Retirement and Patronage

- **Transition out of piracy:** Some pirates secured pardons or protection by changing allegiance or entering service of a European power; Easton is often presented as an example of a pirate who retired without facing a major trial.
- **Nobility claims:** Mentions of titles such as “Marquis of Savoy” appear in popular summaries; the precise status and nature of any honors should be treated as source-dependent.

## Significance

- Easton is often cited in lists of notable pirates because his story illustrates how piracy, state power, and commerce overlapped in the early modern Atlantic.
