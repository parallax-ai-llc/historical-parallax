## Summary

Leader of the Gangster Disciples who ran a multimillion-dollar drug empire from federal prison.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1973 | Convicted in Illinois for ordering the murder of William “Pooky” Young; sentenced to a long prison term |
| 1995 | Federal indictment alleging he led the Gangster Disciples’ criminal enterprise while incarcerated |
| 1997 | Convicted of running drug empire from prison |

## Background

- Larry Hoover is widely described as a key leader associated with the Gangster Disciples, a Chicago-based street gang.
- Public reporting commonly places his rise to prominence in the late 1960s and 1970s, alongside the broader formation and consolidation of large Chicago gang coalitions.

## Gangster Disciples (high-level)

- The Gangster Disciples have been characterized by law enforcement and journalists as a large, hierarchical organization with operations extending beyond Illinois.
- Accounts often describe the group as involved in narcotics trafficking and other organized criminal activity.
- Discussions of the organization frequently emphasize its internal ranks, rules, and efforts to maintain discipline across chapters.

## Convictions and incarceration

- Hoover has served decades in prison following state and federal convictions.
- In addition to the Illinois murder conviction (1973), he was later prosecuted federally for allegedly directing gang activity while imprisoned.
- The 1997 federal conviction is often summarized as a finding that he continued to lead or influence a criminal enterprise from custody.

## Influence and public attention

- Hoover’s name remains prominent in discussions about gang leadership, prison influence, and the difficulties of disrupting criminal networks when leaders are incarcerated.
- He is also referenced in popular culture and in public advocacy conversations about sentencing, prison conditions, and clemency.
