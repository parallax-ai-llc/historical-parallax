## Summary

Collapse of Britains oldest merchant bank in 1995 after rogue trader Nick Leeson lost $1.3 billion through unauthorized speculative trading in Singapore.

## Background

- Barings Bank was founded in the 18th century and became a prominent merchant bank in the United Kingdom.
- In the early 1990s, Barings expanded activities in derivatives trading, including operations in Asia.

## What happened (high-level)

- Nick Leeson, based in Singapore, accumulated large positions in derivatives tied to the Japanese equity market (often described in connection with Nikkei-linked futures and options).
- Losses were concealed for an extended period through accounting manipulation and the use of an “error” or suspense account referenced in many summaries of the case.
- The exposure grew to a level that exceeded Barings’ capital, making the bank unable to absorb the losses once they were recognized.

## Control failures

- The collapse is widely treated as a classic example of breakdowns in internal controls, including:
  - weak segregation of duties (front office vs. back office),
  - insufficient independent risk oversight,
  - inadequate reconciliation and reporting,
  - an organizational culture that relied too heavily on apparent profit performance.

## Outcome

- Barings became insolvent and was ultimately sold to ING for a nominal amount, with ING assuming liabilities.
- Leeson was prosecuted and imprisoned; he was later released after serving a portion of his sentence.

## Why it matters

- The Barings case is frequently cited in risk-management education as a cautionary tale about operational risk and the limits of reputation.
- It helped shape later discussions about:
  - real-time risk monitoring,
  - governance of complex products,
  - stress testing and capital adequacy,
  - accountability for supervisory failures.
