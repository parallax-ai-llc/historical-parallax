## Summary

First president of Guinea who became increasingly authoritarian, establishing a one-party state with a vast network of prison camps. An estimated 50,000 people were killed under his regime, including many at the notorious Camp Boiro.

## Rise to Power and Independence Context

- Touré emerged from trade union politics and anti-colonial organizing during the late French colonial period.
- He is most closely linked to Guinea’s 1958 break from France, after which the country pursued a distinct post-colonial trajectory compared with many neighboring states.

## Governance and Political System (High-Level)

- Major summaries describe Guinea under Touré as a centralized one-party state.
- Political opposition was treated as a security threat, and the state’s internal-security apparatus grew in influence.

## Repression and Camp Boiro

- Camp Boiro is frequently referenced in accounts of the period as a symbol of detention, interrogation, and political violence.
- Estimates of deaths and abuses vary by source, but the overall characterization in prominent narratives is of systematic repression affecting political rivals and perceived dissenters.

## Foreign Policy and Regional Role

- Touré’s Guinea navigated Cold War pressures, seeking external support while asserting national sovereignty.
- The government’s security focus and suspicion of coups shaped both domestic policy and Guinea’s relations with neighbors and external powers.

## Legacy

- Touré remains a polarizing figure: associated with independence-era nationalism on one hand, and severe authoritarian repression on the other.
- Post-1984 Guinean politics often referenced the Touré period as a cautionary baseline for governance and human-rights debates.
