## Summary

Swedish PM Olof Palme was shot dead while walking home from a cinema without bodyguards. The assassination remained unsolved for 34 years until suspect Stig Engström was named in 2020.

## Context

- **Victim:** Olof Palme, Prime Minister of Sweden.
- **Date & place:** Late evening of **28 February 1986**, central Stockholm, after leaving a cinema.
- **Notable circumstance:** Palme was walking in public **without a security detail**, which was consistent with Sweden’s comparatively informal protection practices at the time.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- Palme and his wife Lisbeth left the cinema and walked along Sveavägen.
- A gunman approached from behind and fired at close range.
- Palme was fatally wounded; Lisbeth Palme was also injured.
- The attacker escaped on foot into the surrounding streets.

## Investigation Overview

- The case became one of Sweden’s largest criminal investigations.
- Early investigative work included:
  - Large-scale witness canvassing and tip intake.
  - Forensic examination of the crime scene.
  - Pursuit of multiple suspect tracks over decades.
- The long duration of the inquiry made it a major national issue, frequently revisited in media and politics.

## Prominent Lines of Inquiry (Summary)

- **Christer Pettersson:** Convicted in a lower court and later acquitted on appeal; the case remained open afterward.
- **International/political theories:** Various hypotheses circulated over time, reflecting Palme’s international profile and Sweden’s Cold War-era context.
- **Stig Engström (“Skandia Man”):** A person present near the scene who later became a central focus in the 2020 prosecutorial conclusion.

## 2020 Prosecutorial Conclusion

- In 2020, Swedish prosecutors publicly identified **Stig Engström** as the most likely perpetrator.
- The investigation was **closed** because Engström was **deceased**, making prosecution impossible.
- This conclusion did not involve a trial verdict; it was presented as the state’s final assessment based on the available material.

## Why the Case Matters

- The assassination influenced Swedish debates about:
  - **Personal security** for public officials.
  - The balance between an open society and protective measures.
  - Institutional confidence in the justice system when high-profile cases remain unresolved.
