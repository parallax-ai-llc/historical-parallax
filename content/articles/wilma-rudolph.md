## Summary

Wilma Rudolph overcame childhood polio to become the first American woman to win three gold at one Olympics (1960 Rome).

## Early Life and Health

Rudolph was born in Tennessee and experienced significant health challenges in childhood, including **polio** and related complications. With family support and extensive treatment, she regained the ability to walk normally and later developed into an elite sprinter.

## Athletic Achievements

- **1960 Rome Olympics:** Became a global sports icon after winning **three gold medals** in sprint events.
- **World recognition:** Her performance helped popularize women’s track and field in the United States during a period when media coverage and sponsorship were far more limited than today.

## Style and Significance

- Known for exceptional speed and smooth running mechanics in the **100 m** and **200 m** sprints.
- Her story is frequently cited as an example of resilience: a transition from childhood disability and poverty to Olympic dominance.

## Life After Competition

After retiring from top-level competition, Rudolph worked in education and youth programs and remained a prominent public figure associated with civil rights–era symbolism and opportunity in American sport.

## Legacy

Rudolph’s achievements endure as a landmark moment in Olympic history, particularly for American women athletes, and her biography remains widely taught and referenced in sports history.
