## Expanded Overview

This entry duplicates the better-known spelling "Srinivasa Ramanujan"; however, the biographical facts refer to the same person: the Indian mathematician whose work in number theory and analysis became foundational to several modern areas of mathematics.[^1]

### Context and Early Life

- Ramanujan grew up in South India and developed advanced mathematical ideas largely outside formal Western training.
- His early notebooks contained numerous identities and results, often stated without full proofs, reflecting a style focused on discovery and pattern.

### Work and Collaboration

- Ramanujan is closely associated with the University of Cambridge period in which he collaborated with G. H. Hardy and other mathematicians.
- Hardy helped present and contextualize Ramanujan's results for the broader mathematical community, while Ramanujan continued producing original theorems and conjectures.

### Notable Mathematical Themes (High-Level)

- Integer partitions and asymptotic formulas.
- Modular forms and q-series (including what later became known as mock theta functions).
- Infinite series, continued fractions, and striking numerical identities.

### Health, Return, and Legacy

- Ramanujan's most productive years were constrained by significant health problems.
- He returned to India and died at a young age (1920), but his unpublished notes continued to yield new results for decades.
- His life is frequently cited as an example of extraordinary mathematical intuition and the cross-cultural transmission of scientific knowledge.
