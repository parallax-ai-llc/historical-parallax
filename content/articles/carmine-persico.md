## Summary
Carmine 'The Snake' Persico was the longtime boss of the Colombo crime family. He led the family for decades from prison. Sentenced to 139 years. He died in prison.

## Background
- Persico was associated with organized crime in New York City and is most commonly linked with the Colombo crime family, one of the "Five Families" that dominated the American Mafia in the post–World War II era.[^2]
- In many accounts he is described as a strong internal power broker within his family, with influence continuing even during extended periods of incarceration.[^2]

## Colombo crime family leadership
- The Colombo family (formerly the Profaci family) experienced multiple internal conflicts over leadership in the 1960s–1980s; Persico is often cited as a central figure in the factional struggles that shaped the organization.[^2]
- Persico’s leadership is frequently characterized as "from prison"—delegating day-to-day activity to street-level leadership while retaining final authority and influence through intermediaries and trusted associates.[^2]

## Legal cases and imprisonment
- Persico was convicted in major federal prosecutions during the 1980s, including cases that targeted senior organized-crime figures using racketeering statutes (RICO).[^2]
- He received a long federal sentence (commonly reported as 139 years) and spent the remainder of his life in U.S. federal custody, where he died in 2019.[^2]

## Notes
- Nickname spellings and attributions (including "The Snake") vary by source; biographical summaries commonly use it as a shorthand identifier rather than a formal title.[^2]
- Public reporting generally treats Persico as one of the more prominent late-20th-century New York Mafia leaders due to the length of his sentence and the notoriety of the Colombo family’s internal conflicts.[^2]
