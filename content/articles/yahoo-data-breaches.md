## Summary

Two massive data breaches at Yahoo in 2013 and 2014 that compromised all 3 billion user accounts. Russian intelligence officers and criminal hackers were charged.

## What Happened (High Level)

The term “Yahoo data breaches” commonly refers to intrusions into Yahoo’s user account systems that occurred in 2013 and 2014 and were later publicly disclosed. The incidents became notable for their scale and for the way they unfolded over several years, with the scope of affected accounts revised upward over time.

## Disclosure, Attribution, and Charges

Public reporting and subsequent U.S. legal actions connected parts of the activity to a mix of criminal hackers and Russian intelligence-linked actors. In broad terms, the case is often discussed as an example of:

- **Credential and account data theft** on an industrial scale
- **Long dwell time** between compromise and full public understanding
- **Complex attribution** involving multiple participants and motivations

## Business and Regulatory Impact

The breaches had material consequences beyond technical remediation. They were widely covered in the context of Yahoo’s acquisition by Verizon, where the incidents influenced negotiation, risk allocation, and the final deal terms reported in the press.

## Why It Matters

Security practitioners and business leaders often cite the Yahoo breaches when talking about:

1. The importance of strong account security (hashed passwords, salting, modern algorithms)
2. Detection and incident response capabilities at large consumer platforms
3. The downstream costs of delayed disclosure (legal exposure, reputational damage, transaction risk)
