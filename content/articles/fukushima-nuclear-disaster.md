## Summary

Nuclear meltdown at the Fukushima Daiichi plant on March 11, 2011 triggered by earthquake and tsunami. Three reactors melted down in the worst nuclear disaster since Chernobyl.

## Context and Causes

The Fukushima Daiichi accident occurred after the Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami (11 March 2011). The earthquake caused a loss of off-site power, and the subsequent tsunami flooded backup diesel generators and electrical switchgear. With power and many cooling systems disabled, operators struggled to remove decay heat from the reactor cores.

While the initiating hazard was natural, several risk factors are often highlighted in post-accident reporting: the plant’s seawall height relative to the tsunami, the placement and protection of emergency power equipment, and the challenge of managing a prolonged station blackout.

## Emergency Response (High-Level)

In the days after the tsunami, emergency actions included venting to reduce pressure, injecting seawater as an improvised cooling measure, and establishing exclusion zones. Hydrogen generated during overheating and zirconium-steam reactions accumulated in reactor buildings and contributed to multiple explosions that damaged structures and complicated response operations.

Authorities issued evacuation orders for surrounding communities, with displacement and disruption lasting for years for some residents.

## Longer-Term Impacts

- **Decontamination and waste management:** Cleanup included removal of contaminated topsoil and extensive monitoring, producing large volumes of radioactive waste requiring storage and processing.
- **Decommissioning:** Work has involved stabilizing damaged reactors, treating contaminated water, and planning for retrieval of fuel debris—an engineering effort expected to take decades.
- **Policy effects:** The accident influenced nuclear safety regulation in Japan and internationally, including stress tests, reassessment of severe-accident management, and changes in energy policy debates.

## Notes on Treated Water

A major long-running issue has been the management of water used for cooling and groundwater ingress. Water treatment systems remove many radionuclides, while tritium remains difficult to separate at scale. Releases of treated water have been framed by operators and regulators as meeting discharge standards, while also drawing public concern, regional political debate, and scrutiny by environmental groups.
