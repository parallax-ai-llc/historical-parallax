## Summary

MS-13 and Barrio 18 gangs originated in Los Angeles and were exported to Central America through deportations. They control territory, extort businesses, and have killed tens of thousands.

## Origins and Transnational Spread

- The best-known groups in this context include **MS-13 (Mara Salvatrucha)** and **Barrio 18**, which developed significant presence in Los Angeles before expanding.
- Expansion into Central America is frequently linked to **deportation policies** and the return of gang-involved individuals to countries with weaker institutions and limited reintegration capacity.

## Organization and Methods (High-Level)

- Many gangs combine neighborhood-level territorial control with more networked structures that facilitate coordination across cities and borders.
- Revenue streams commonly include **extortion**, drug retail, robbery, and coercive "taxation" of transport routes and local businesses.

## Social and Political Effects

- High homicide rates, intimidation of communities, and forced displacement have been associated with gang activity in several countries.
- Gangs have also influenced local governance through corruption, threats against officials, and coercion of witnesses.

## State Responses

- Governments in the region have used a range of approaches, including aggressive policing and incarceration strategies often described as "mano dura" policies.
- These responses have been debated for their effectiveness and for potential human-rights impacts, particularly in overcrowded prison systems.

## International Dimension

- Migration flows, asylum claims, and cross-border law-enforcement cooperation are frequently discussed in relation to the gang problem.
- The issue is often framed as both a security challenge and a governance challenge tied to poverty, exclusion, and institutional weakness.
