## Summary

Naomi Campbell is a British supermodel widely regarded as one of the most iconic figures in the history of fashion. Rising to global prominence in the late 1980s, she became one of the original "Big Six" supermodels alongside Cindy Crawford, Linda Evangelista, Christy Turlington, Tatjana Patitz, and Claudia Schiffer — a cohort that redefined the commercial and cultural power of the modeling profession. Her career has spanned over four decades, making her one of the most enduring figures in the industry.

Beyond modeling, Campbell has built a multifaceted public profile through television, philanthropy, and business ventures. She was among the first Black models to appear on the covers of major fashion magazines including French Vogue and Time, breaking barriers at a time when racial diversity in high fashion was severely limited. Her influence on representation in the industry remains a defining part of her legacy.

---

## Early Life & Background

Naomi Elaine Campbell was born on May 22, 1970, in Streatham, south London, to Valerie Morris, a Jamaican-born dancer. Her father was absent from her life, and she was raised largely by her mother and grandmother. She attended the Barbara Speake Stage School in London from the age of three and later studied at the Italia Conti Academy of Theatre Arts, where she trained in dance and performance.

At age 15, while on a trip to Covent Garden, she was spotted by modeling agency head Beth Boldt and signed to the Synchro Model Agency. Within weeks, she had landed the cover of British fashion magazine *Elle* in 1985, launching what would become one of the most storied careers in fashion history.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Campbell's international breakthrough came in 1988 when she appeared on the cover of French *Vogue*, becoming the first Black woman to do so. That same year, she signed with Elite Model Management and began appearing in major campaigns for brands including Versace, Chanel, and Ralph Lauren. By the early 1990s, she had achieved supermodel status, commanding fees that matched or exceeded those of her white counterparts — a landmark in the industry.

She formed a close professional and personal relationship with designer Gianni Versace, who championed her career extensively. Her appearance in George Michael's 1992 music video "Too Funky" further broadened her cultural reach. She also co-authored a novel, *Swan* (1994), and released a pop album, *Baby Woman* (1994).

---

## Key Achievements

- First Black model on the cover of French *Vogue* (1988) and *Time* magazine (1998).
- Appeared on over 500 magazine covers throughout her career.
- Named one of the "Big Six" supermodels of the 1990s.
- Founded Fashion for Relief in 2005, a charity raising funds through fashion shows for disaster victims, including Hurricane Katrina and the 2010 Haiti earthquake.
- Launched *The Face* magazine reboot in 2019 as creative director.

---

## Controversies & Criticism

Campbell's career has been marked by well-documented personal difficulties. Between 2000 and 2008, she faced multiple legal actions related to assaulting personal assistants and staff, resulting in community service sentences and anger management requirements. She has publicly acknowledged these behavioral issues in interviews. In 1997, she testified in the trial of alleged arms dealer Viktor Bout after receiving a gift of diamonds from him at a 1997 dinner hosted by Nelson Mandela — a matter that drew significant media scrutiny.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Campbell's impact on fashion and racial representation is substantial and ongoing. She has consistently advocated for greater diversity in the industry and used her platform to spotlight underrepresented designers and models. In 2023, the Victoria and Albert Museum in London mounted a major retrospective exhibition dedicated to her career, the first such honor for a model at the institution. She remains active on the runway and in editorial work well into her fifties, challenging industry norms around age and beauty.
