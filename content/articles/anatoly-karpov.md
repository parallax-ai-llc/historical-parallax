## Summary

Anatoly Yevgenyich Karpov (born May 23, 1951, in Zlatoust, Soviet Union) is one of the greatest chess players in history, having served as World Chess Champion from 1975 to 1985. A positional genius renowned for his prophylactic and strategic style, Karpov won the title by default when Bobby Fischer refused to defend his crown, then proved his legitimacy through a decade of dominant competitive results. He held the FIDE world title again from 1993 to 1999 and amassed more tournament victories than virtually any other player in the modern era.

Beyond chess, Karpov has been active in Russian politics and humanitarian work. A longtime member of the State Duma and a UNICEF Goodwill Ambassador, he has used his public profile to advocate for environmental causes and chess education worldwide. His rivalry with Garry Kasparov — spanning five world championship matches between 1984 and 1990 — stands as one of the most intense and historically significant contests in sports history.

## Early Life & Background

Karpov was born on May 23, 1951, in Zlatoust, in the Ural region of the Soviet Union. He learned chess at age four from his father and quickly demonstrated exceptional talent. By age nine he had achieved the rank of candidate master, and at fifteen he became the Soviet Union's youngest chess master at the time. He studied economics at Leningrad State University, graduating in 1978, though his chess career had already taken international precedence long before his academic completion.

## Rise to Prominence

Karpov rose through the Soviet chess system with remarkable consistency. He won the World Junior Chess Championship in 1969 and earned the grandmaster title in 1970 at age nineteen. He won the strong Hastings tournament in 1971–72 and by 1974 had defeated Boris Spassky and Viktor Korchnoi in the Candidates matches to earn the right to challenge Fischer. When Fischer forfeited the title in 1975 rather than accept FIDE match conditions, Karpov was declared champion — a circumstance that motivated him to prove his worthiness through action. He did exactly that, embarking on a nearly unbroken streak of tournament victories throughout the late 1970s.

## Key Achievements

- **World Chess Champion**, FIDE title: 1975–1985 and 1993–1999
- Won over **160 international tournaments**, more than any other player of his era
- Three World Championship matches against Viktor Korchnoi (1978, 1981) both won
- Five consecutive World Championship matches against Garry Kasparov (1984–1990)
- The 1984–85 match was abandoned after five months with Karpov leading 5–3 in wins, the longest championship match in history
- Three-time recipient of the **Chess Oscar** award for player of the year (1975, 1976, 1977)
- Maintained a FIDE rating above 2700 for over two decades

## Controversies & Criticism

The manner in which Karpov obtained the world title in 1975 — without playing Fischer — drew lasting skepticism, though his subsequent results silenced most doubters. The abandonment of the 1984–85 match against Kasparov remains contentious; FIDE president Florencio Campomanes halted the match, with critics suggesting the decision protected Karpov from an increasingly dangerous Kasparov. Karpov's close alignment with Soviet authorities throughout his career also attracted scrutiny, with some observers noting preferential treatment in scheduling and opponent selection.

## Legacy & Influence

Karpov's positional style — characterized by microscopic advantages accumulated over long games — has been studied and emulated by generations of players. His body of theoretical contributions to the Caro-Kann Defense and numerous other openings remains foundational. He served in the Russian State Duma representing the Communist Party and has promoted chess education globally through the Karpov Chess Schools foundation. His rivalry with Kasparov is widely credited with popularizing chess internationally during the 1980s, elevating the game's cultural profile far beyond its traditional audience.
