## Summary

US Army lieutenant convicted for the 1968 My Lai Massacre in Vietnam, where American soldiers killed between 347 and 504 unarmed Vietnamese civilians. He was the only person convicted. Originally sentenced to life, he served 3.5 years of house arrest.

## Additional Context

- **My Lai as a watershed**: The My Lai Massacre became one of the most consequential events shaping U.S. and global perceptions of the Vietnam War, intensifying debate about military conduct and accountability.
- **Command responsibility debate**: Calley’s case was frequently discussed alongside broader questions of chain-of-command responsibility, rules of engagement, and the treatment of civilians in counterinsurgency operations.

## Legal and Political Aftermath

- **Court-martial and sentence**: Calley was convicted by court-martial; his sentence was later reduced, and he did not serve the life term in a conventional prison setting.
- **Public controversy**: The case produced polarized reactions in the United States, including public sympathy campaigns as well as condemnation, reflecting broader divisions over the war.
- **Long-term significance**: The proceedings are often referenced in discussions of military justice, war crimes prosecutions, and how democracies investigate alleged atrocities by their own forces.

## Notes on Historical Impact

My Lai and the Calley trial remain central reference points in journalism, scholarship, and policy debates on civilian protection, battlefield ethics, and the practical challenges of enforcing international humanitarian norms during war.
