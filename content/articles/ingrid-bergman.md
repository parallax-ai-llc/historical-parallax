## Summary

Ingrid Bergman (1915–1982) was a Swedish actress widely regarded as one of the greatest performers in cinema history. With a career spanning five decades across Hollywood, Europe, and the stage, she brought an unaffected naturalism to her roles at a time when stylized glamour was the industry norm. Her collaborations with directors like Alfred Hitchcock and Roberto Rossellini, and her iconic appearance in *Casablanca* (1942), cemented her place as an enduring symbol of both talent and beauty.

Bergman's significance extends beyond her filmography. She navigated a deeply personal and professional exile from Hollywood in the early 1950s following a public scandal, then staged one of the most remarkable career comebacks in entertainment history. Her three Academy Awards, two Emmy Awards, and a Tony Award testify to her extraordinary range across film, television, and theater.

---

## Early Life & Background

Ingrid Bergman was born on August 29, 1915, in Stockholm, Sweden. Her mother, Friedel Adler, a German woman, died when Ingrid was two years old; her father, Justus Bergman, a painter and photography enthusiast, died when she was twelve. Raised by relatives, Bergman found solace in theater and performance from an early age.

She enrolled at the Royal Dramatic Theatre School in Stockholm in 1933, but her film career accelerated quickly enough that she left after only one year. She appeared in a series of Swedish films throughout the mid-1930s, attracting the attention of Hollywood producer David O. Selznick, who brought her to the United States in 1939.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Bergman's American debut came with *Intermezzo: A Love Story* (1939), a remake of her 1936 Swedish film. Her breakout on the world stage arrived with *Casablanca* (1942), opposite Humphrey Bogart, which became one of the most celebrated films ever made. She followed that with *For Whom the Bell Tolls* (1943) and won her first Academy Award for Best Actress for *Gaslight* (1944).

Her collaboration with Alfred Hitchcock produced two notable thrillers: *Spellbound* (1945) and *Notorious* (1946), both of which showcased her ability to convey psychological complexity. She won a second Best Actress Oscar for *Anastasia* (1956), marking her return to mainstream Hollywood acceptance after years of exile.

---

## Key Achievements

- **Three Academy Awards**: Best Actress for *Gaslight* (1944) and *Anastasia* (1956); Best Supporting Actress for *Murder on the Orient Express* (1974)
- **Two Emmy Awards** and a **Tony Award** (1947, for *Joan of Lorraine*)
- Starred in over 50 films across Swedish, American, Italian, French, and British cinema
- Named the fourth greatest female screen legend by the American Film Institute in 1999

---

## Controversies & Criticism

In 1949, Bergman began a love affair with Italian director Roberto Rossellini while still married to her first husband, Dr. Petter Lindström. She became pregnant with Rossellini's child, and the ensuing scandal led to a formal condemnation on the floor of the U.S. Senate by Senator Edwin C. Johnson. Hollywood studios effectively blacklisted her, and she spent the early 1950s working in Europe with Rossellini on a series of films that were poorly received commercially and critically at the time. Though she and Rossellini married in 1950 and had three children together, they divorced in 1957. Her rehabilitation in American public opinion came gradually, aided by her Oscar win for *Anastasia*.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Bergman's naturalistic acting style influenced generations of performers who followed. Her willingness to prioritize artistic risk — working with Rossellini on uncommercial neo-realist films at the height of her fame — reflects a conviction that shaped her reputation in European cinema. Her later work, including her portrayal of Israeli Prime Minister Golda Meir in the 1982 television film *A Woman Called Golda*, earned her a posthumous Emmy; she died of breast cancer on her 67th birthday, August 29, 1982. The Ingrid Bergman Award is given annually at the San Sebastián International Film Festival in her honor.
