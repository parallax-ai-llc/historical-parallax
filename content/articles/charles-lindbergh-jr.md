## Summary

Toddler son of aviator Charles Lindbergh who was kidnapped from his crib in 1932 in what was called the Crime of the Century. His body was found 10 weeks later and Bruno Hauptmann was convicted and executed for the crime.

## Additional Context

### The kidnapping and investigation

- The kidnapping occurred at the Lindbergh family home near Hopewell, New Jersey. A ransom note was left, and the case quickly became a national media spectacle.
- Investigators focused on physical evidence such as a homemade wooden ladder believed to have been used to access the nursery and the chain of ransom notes and payments.

### Ransom, publicity, and security

- The ransom demand and the involvement of intermediaries became heavily covered by newspapers, shaping the public perception of modern “celebrity crime.”
- The intensity of press attention also raised questions about privacy, policing, and the handling of sensitive evidence in high-profile cases.

### Legal and historical significance

- The case helped spur federal involvement in kidnapping crimes in the United States; Congress passed the Federal Kidnapping Act of 1932, often called the “Lindbergh Law,” expanding federal jurisdiction when victims were transported across state lines.

### Trial and ongoing debate

- Bruno Richard Hauptmann was convicted and executed, but the case has remained the subject of later books and debates about evidence, procedure, and possible alternative explanations.
