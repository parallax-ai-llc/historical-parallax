## Summary

Élie Joseph Cartan (1869–1951) was one of the most original and influential mathematicians of the twentieth century, whose work fundamentally reshaped differential geometry, Lie group theory, and modern theoretical physics. Working across an exceptionally broad range of topics, he developed tools and concepts — exterior differential forms, moving frames, and the theory of spinors — that remain central to mathematics and physics today.

Cartan's contributions were so ahead of their time that many were not fully appreciated until decades after their publication. His synthesis of algebraic and geometric thinking produced a body of work of remarkable depth and coherence, earning him recognition late in life as one of the towering figures of modern mathematics.

---

## Early Life & Background

Élie Cartan was born on 9 April 1869 in Dolomieu, a small village in the Isère department of southeastern France, the son of a blacksmith. His exceptional mathematical talent was recognized early by a school inspector, Antonin Dubost, who helped secure a scholarship enabling Cartan to attend the Lycée in Grenoble and later the École Normale Supérieure in Paris, which he entered in 1888.

At the École Normale, Cartan encountered the full breadth of contemporary mathematics and came under the influence of leading French mathematicians. He graduated in 1891 and completed his doctoral thesis in 1894, a work that would immediately establish his reputation.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Cartan's 1894 doctoral dissertation completed the classification of all finite-dimensional simple Lie algebras over the complex numbers, finishing work begun by Wilhelm Killing. The thesis corrected errors in Killing's analysis and provided a rigorous, complete account — a foundational achievement in algebra.

He held positions at Montpellier (1894), Lyon (1896), Nancy (1903), and finally Paris, where he joined the Sorbonne in 1909 and remained until his retirement in 1940. Over these decades he produced a continuous stream of major papers, averaging well over ten significant publications per year at his peak.

---

## Key Achievements

Cartan's contributions span several fields. He developed the **theory of exterior differential forms** and the **exterior derivative**, providing a calculus intrinsically suited to manifolds and now standard in differential geometry and physics. His **method of moving frames** (repère mobile) gave a powerful and flexible approach to the geometry of curves, surfaces, and higher-dimensional spaces.

He classified **symmetric spaces** (1926–1927), a monumental project producing a complete list of Riemannian symmetric spaces that deeply influenced geometry and harmonic analysis. His work on **Cartan connections** generalized the notion of a connection on a manifold, unifying and extending earlier ideas by Levi-Civita and Weyl.

Cartan also introduced **spinors** into geometry in 1913, objects that proved essential to quantum mechanics and relativistic field theory. His correspondence with Albert Einstein in the 1920s explored applications of his geometric ideas to unified field theories.

---

## Controversies & Criticism

Cartan's work suffered from a prolonged lack of recognition, partly due to its difficulty and unconventional style. Hermann Weyl remarked that Cartan's papers were notoriously hard to read. For much of his career, his influence was felt more through those who decoded his work than through immediate widespread adoption. There were no significant personal controversies, but the mathematical community's slow absorption of his ideas represents a notable episode in the sociology of mathematics.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Cartan was elected to the French Academy of Sciences in 1931 and received the Wolf Prize's precursor honors and the Sylvester Medal in 1937. His son Henri Cartan became a distinguished mathematician in his own right and a founding member of Bourbaki.

The concepts Cartan developed — differential forms, fiber bundles, connections, symmetric spaces, and spinors — are indispensable in contemporary differential geometry, topology, and theoretical physics, including general relativity and gauge theory. His work is cited as a direct antecedent of much of twentieth-century geometry.
