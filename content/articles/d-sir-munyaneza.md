## Summary

Rwandan militia leader convicted of genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity by a Canadian court. He was the first person convicted under Canada's Crimes Against Humanity and War Crimes Act.

## Background

Désiré Munyaneza is associated with violence during the 1994 Rwandan genocide, in which extremist-led forces and militias carried out mass killings targeting Tutsi and others. After the genocide, some suspects lived abroad, leading to cases based on universal-jurisdiction-style statutes in countries such as Canada.

## Canadian prosecution

- Munyaneza was arrested in Canada and prosecuted under Canada's Crimes Against Humanity and War Crimes Act.
- The case is historically notable because it was the first conviction under that Act.
- The court found him guilty of genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes for actions connected to events in the Butare region.

## Legal significance

The prosecution is often referenced for:

- Demonstrating that domestic courts can try international crimes committed abroad
- Building complex cases using survivor testimony, expert evidence, and international cooperation
- The evidentiary and logistical challenges involved in prosecuting atrocities years after the fact

## Legacy

- The case is cited in discussions of accountability for genocide and the role of diaspora states in pursuing justice.
- It also illustrates how immigration and criminal law can intersect when alleged perpetrators relocate after conflicts.
