## Summary

Russian ransomware group that pioneered double extortion by stealing data before encrypting systems. They attacked Cognizant, Canon, and Xerox before shutting down in 2020.

## Context

Maze is commonly discussed in cybersecurity as a turning point in the mainstreaming of “double extortion” ransomware: attackers both encrypt systems and threaten to publish stolen data to increase pressure on victims.

## What “double extortion” changes

- **Confidentiality risk**: the incident is no longer only about downtime; it also becomes a data-breach and privacy problem.
- **Regulatory exposure**: disclosure obligations and potential penalties can raise the total cost beyond technical recovery.
- **Negotiation leverage**: leak threats can be used even if an organization restores from backups.

## Tactics often associated with modern ransomware operations

- Initial access via compromised credentials, exposed remote services, or phishing.
- Lateral movement inside a network to reach high-value systems.
- Exfiltration of sensitive files prior to encryption.
- Public “name-and-shame” posting to apply reputational pressure.

## Impact and responses

- Maze’s approach influenced later ransomware groups, although attribution between groups can be contested.
- Defensive guidance commonly emphasizes segmented networks, tested backups, rapid patching, and incident-response playbooks.
- Law-enforcement actions and private-sector disruption have periodically degraded ransomware infrastructure, but the broader ecosystem persists.
