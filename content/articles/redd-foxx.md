## Summary

Redd Foxx was an American stand-up comedian and actor whose pioneering work in adult comedy and television made him one of the most influential entertainers of the 20th century. Born John Elroy Sanford in St. Louis, Missouri, he built a reputation through decades of raunchy "party records" before achieving mainstream fame as Fred Sanford in the NBC sitcom *Sanford and Son* (1972–1977). His raw, unfiltered style laid groundwork for generations of Black comedians and helped reshape what American audiences would accept on primetime television.

Foxx's career bridged the underground Black comedy circuit and mainstream entertainment at a time when racial barriers in show business were still formidable. His success demonstrated that unapologetically Black humor could command mass audiences, influencing figures including Richard Pryor, Eddie Murphy, and Chris Rock. He remained a working comedian and performer until the end of his life, dying on set in 1991.

## Early Life & Background

John Elroy Sanford was born on December 9, 1922, in St. Louis, Missouri. His father abandoned the family when he was young, and he was largely raised by his mother and grandmother. He moved to Chicago as a teenager and then to New York City around 1939, where he hustled to survive, working odd jobs and playing washboard in street bands in Harlem. It was during this period that he adopted the stage name "Redd Foxx" — "Redd" for his light complexion and reddish hair, and "Foxx" as a nod to baseball player Jimmie Foxx.

He befriended Malcolm Little (later Malcolm X) during his Harlem years, a connection he referenced throughout his life. The streets of New York and the vibrant Black entertainment scene of Harlem shaped his comedic sensibility and gave him material he would mine for decades.

## Rise to Prominence

Foxx began performing on the Chitlin' Circuit, the network of Black-owned venues across the segregated United States, honing a blue, adult-oriented act. From the early 1950s onward, he recorded a series of explicit comedy albums — often called "party records" — that sold millions of copies through word of mouth and independent distributors, making him enormously popular in Black communities while remaining largely invisible to white mainstream media. He recorded over 50 such albums.

His crossover came when producer Norman Lear cast him as the cantankerous junk dealer Fred Sanford in *Sanford and Son*, which premiered on NBC on January 14, 1972. The show was an immediate hit, frequently ranking as the second most-watched program in America during its run. Foxx earned a Golden Globe nomination for his performance and became a household name.

## Key Achievements

- Starred in *Sanford and Son* for six seasons (1972–1977), one of the highest-rated sitcoms of the era
- Recorded over 50 comedy albums, selling millions of copies across the 1950s–1970s
- Received a star on the Hollywood Walk of Fame
- Returned to television in *The Redd Foxx Show* (1986) and *The Royal Family* (1991), the latter alongside Della Reese
- Inducted into the Comedy Hall of Fame

## Controversies & Criticism

Foxx's explicit material was controversial throughout his career, earning him bans from certain venues and radio stations. His party records, while celebrated in Black communities, were considered obscene by mainstream standards of the time. He also had well-documented financial troubles; the IRS seized his home and belongings in 1989 due to approximately $3.6 million in unpaid taxes. His personal life included four marriages and periods of public conflict with producers over salary disputes during *Sanford and Son*.

## Legacy & Influence

Foxx is widely credited as a forefather of modern Black stand-up comedy. His willingness to speak candidly about race, sexuality, and urban life cleared a path for Richard Pryor and Eddie Murphy, both of whom acknowledged his influence directly. *Sanford and Son* remains a landmark in American television history for its portrayal of working-class Black life with authenticity and humor.

He died on October 11, 1991, on the set of *The Royal Family* in Hollywood after suffering a heart attack — which cast members initially mistook for one of his trademark fake heart attacks from *Sanford and Son*. He was 68.
