## Summary

Bangalore-based don who controlled organized crime in southern India including real estate extortion and the sandalwood mafia. Linked to the assassination of multiple rivals.

## Background

D. K. Rao is described in reporting and secondary sources as a figure in Bengaluru's (Bangalore's) underworld during a period when rapid urbanization and real‑estate expansion created opportunities for extortion, contract violence, and protection rackets.

## Criminal activities (as alleged)

Accounts commonly associate him with:

- Real‑estate intimidation and extortion tied to construction and land disputes
- Networks involved in smuggling and other illicit markets in southern India
- Rivalries with other underworld groups, sometimes resulting in targeted killings

## Law-enforcement attention

- Rao was reportedly arrested on multiple occasions and faced various criminal cases.
- Organized-crime enforcement in India often involves preventive detention, surveillance, and the use of specialized police units to disrupt violent gangs.

## Context and significance

The term "don" in Indian cities has historically referred to a crime boss who combines violence, economic coercion, and political patronage. Profiles like Rao's are frequently discussed in relation to:

- The intersection of real‑estate money and coercive power
- Weak witness protection and the difficulty of securing convictions
- The way local gangs can evolve into broader networks with financial and political ties
