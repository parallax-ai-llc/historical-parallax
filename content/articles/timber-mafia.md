## What “Timber Mafia” Typically Refers To

- A shorthand label used in reporting and advocacy for organized criminal networks that profit from illegal logging and timber trafficking.
- The term is used across multiple countries and contexts; it does not describe a single unified organization.

## Common Operating Methods (High Level)

- **Corruption and protection**: bribery, political patronage, or intimidation to enable harvesting, transport, and export.
- **Document fraud**: falsified permits, laundering of illegally cut wood through legal supply chains, and mislabeling of origin/species.
- **Violence and coercion**: threats against indigenous communities, local residents, journalists, and environmental defenders in some regions.
- **Cross-border logistics**: use of intermediaries, shell companies, and complex routing to move timber from source to end markets.

## Environmental and Social Impacts

- Loss of biodiversity and accelerated habitat destruction, particularly in tropical forests.
- Increased carbon emissions from deforestation and forest degradation.
- Land-rights conflicts involving indigenous and forest-dependent communities.
- Reduced government revenue and weakened rule of law in affected regions.

## Enforcement and Policy Responses

- International cooperation efforts have included customs enforcement, anti-corruption investigations, and financial crime probes targeting proceeds.
- Some jurisdictions use import controls and “due diligence” rules to reduce the market for illegally sourced timber.
- NGOs and investigative journalism have played a major role in documenting supply chains and identifying hotspots.

## Notes on Numbers

- Estimates of the size of illegal logging markets vary widely by methodology, geography, and year; figures are often cited as ranges rather than point estimates.
