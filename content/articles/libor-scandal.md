## Summary

Global manipulation of the London Interbank Offered Rate by major banks from 2003-2012. Banks colluded to fix the benchmark rate that affects $350 trillion in financial products.

## What LIBOR Was

- LIBOR (London Interbank Offered Rate) functioned as a benchmark interest rate intended to reflect the cost of unsecured interbank borrowing.
- It was widely referenced in contracts ranging from derivatives to loans and mortgages, making integrity of the benchmark systemically important.

## Nature of the Manipulation

- Investigations described two overlapping issues: traders attempting to influence submissions for profit, and banks submitting rates that reduced perceived funding stress during the crisis.
- Because LIBOR was based on submissions rather than transactions, it was vulnerable to misreporting and coordination.

## Enforcement and Penalties

- Multiple banks faced fines and settlements across jurisdictions.
- Individual prosecutions and convictions occurred in several countries, reflecting the scandal’s cross-border nature.

## Benchmark Reform

- The scandal helped drive a shift toward more transaction-based reference rates.
- In many markets, regulators and industry groups promoted alternative rates and governance structures to reduce manipulation risk.

## Why It Still Matters

- LIBOR became a case study in how “small” benchmark distortions can propagate into enormous value transfers when tied to large contract volumes.
- It also reshaped expectations for audit trails, compliance, and oversight in benchmark-setting processes.
