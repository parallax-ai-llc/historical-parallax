## Summary

Industrial disaster at the Union Carbide pesticide plant in Bhopal on December 3, 1984. Toxic methyl isocyanate gas killed over 3,700 people immediately and affected over 500,000 residents.

## What Happened

- Water entered a storage tank containing methyl isocyanate (MIC), triggering an exothermic reaction and rapid pressure increase.
- Safety systems (refrigeration, gas scrubber, flare tower) were reportedly unavailable, degraded, or insufficient at the time of the release.
- The dense toxic cloud spread over nearby neighborhoods during the night, when many residents were sleeping and warning/evacuation capacity was limited.

## Impact

- Acute effects included respiratory injury, eye damage, and chemical burns; many survivors experienced chronic illness.
- Long-term impacts reported in studies include persistent respiratory disease, reproductive and developmental effects, and mental health consequences.
- Environmental contamination concerns have persisted around the former plant site, including soil and groundwater issues.

## Aftermath and Accountability

- The disaster triggered international scrutiny of industrial safety, emergency preparedness, and corporate responsibility in hazardous chemical operations.
- Legal and compensation processes extended for decades and remain contested in terms of adequacy relative to harms.
- The event is frequently referenced in risk management as a case study in process safety, maintenance, and community impact.

## Legacy

- Bhopal is used in safety engineering and policy discussions to emphasize layered protections, transparent reporting, and robust disaster response planning.
- It also influenced public awareness about the siting of hazardous facilities near dense populations.
