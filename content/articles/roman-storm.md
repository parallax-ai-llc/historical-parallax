## Context

- **Tornado Cash** is an open-source cryptocurrency “mixer” that uses smart contracts to help users obscure on-chain transaction links.
- The project became a major policy flashpoint after U.S. authorities alleged it was used at scale to launder proceeds from hacks and other cybercrime.

## What Roman Storm is known for

- Widely described as a **developer and co-founder** associated with Tornado Cash.[^1]
- Became a prominent example in the debate over **whether publishing and maintaining open-source privacy tooling can create criminal liability** when third parties misuse it.

## Legal case (high-level)

- In 2023, U.S. prosecutors announced criminal charges against Storm tied to **alleged money laundering and sanctions-related violations** connected to Tornado Cash.[^1]
- Public reporting around the case frequently frames it as part of a broader U.S. enforcement push against crypto services alleged to facilitate illicit finance.

## Why it matters

- **Open-source precedent risk:** The case is often cited in discussions of how far criminal law can reach into software development and neutral tooling.
- **Privacy vs. compliance:** It sits at the intersection of financial surveillance, sanctions enforcement, and privacy-preserving technology.
- **Chilling effects:** Developers and civil liberties groups have argued that aggressive enforcement may deter publication of privacy tech; regulators argue that “willful facilitation” and operational involvement can cross a legal line.

## Related entities and terms

- **Tornado Cash** — the smart contract system and surrounding infrastructure (front-ends, relayers, governance) discussed in enforcement actions.

- **Money laundering** — broadly, the process of concealing the origin of illicit funds; in crypto contexts, often discussed in relation to mixers and bridges.
