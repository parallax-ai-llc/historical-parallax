## Summary

Uprising against Gaddafi escalated into civil war. NATO intervened; Gaddafi was captured hiding in a drainage pipe and killed by rebels. Libya descended into chaos.

## Background (high-level)

- The war grew out of the broader wave of regional protests in early 2011 and long-running domestic grievances over governance, repression, and corruption.
- Demonstrations and armed clashes spread across multiple cities, with rapid fragmentation of security forces and local control.

## Main Parties and Actors

- Government forces loyal to Muammar Gaddafi, including military units and security services.
- Anti-Gaddafi forces that coalesced under the National Transitional Council (NTC), alongside various local militias.
- Civilian communities caught between shifting front lines and competing armed groups.

## International Involvement

- The United Nations Security Council authorized measures to protect civilians, including a no-fly zone.
- A NATO-led coalition conducted air operations that targeted Libyan government military assets and supported the protection-of-civilians mandate.
- International recognition and diplomatic support shifted toward the NTC as the conflict progressed.

## Key Phases (2011)

- Early unrest and defections turned protests into an armed uprising.
- Fighting focused on strategic cities and supply routes, with control changing hands in several areas.
- The fall of Tripoli marked a decisive turning point in the collapse of the central regime.
- Final battles culminated in the capture and killing of Gaddafi near Sirte.

## Outcome and Aftermath

- The Gaddafi government fell, but a stable post-war political settlement did not consolidate.
- Armed groups retained significant autonomy, contributing to persistent insecurity and governance challenges.
- Subsequent years saw renewed conflict, competing administrations, and intermittent peace processes.

## Why It Matters

- The conflict is frequently cited in discussions of humanitarian intervention, regime change dynamics, and post-conflict state-building.
- Libya's fragmentation had regional spillover effects, including weapons proliferation and cross-border militant activity.
