## Summary

Jules Hoffmann is a French biologist who shared the 2011 Nobel Prize for discoveries concerning innate immunity.

## Additional Context

### Background and Education

Hoffmann was born in **Echternach, Luxembourg** and later pursued higher education in France. Open biographies describe an early interest in insects and zoology, and he completed undergraduate studies in **biology and chemistry** at the **University of Strasbourg**, later earning a Ph.D. there.

### Research Focus: Innate Immunity in Model Organisms

A central theme of his work is the use of **insects (notably *Drosophila*) as model organisms** to understand the immune system. With collaborators including Bruno Lemaitre, Hoffmann helped establish that the **Toll pathway**—first studied in fruit flies—has a key role in **innate immune signaling**, and that related mechanisms extend to mammals through **Toll-like receptors**.

### Institutional and Leadership Roles

Hoffmann has been associated with **CNRS** and the **University of Strasbourg** as a research director and faculty member. He also served in leadership roles within the **French Academy of Sciences**, including terms as vice-president and president.

### Major Recognition

In addition to the 2011 Nobel Prize (shared with Bruce Beutler; the other half awarded to Ralph Steinman), Hoffmann has received other international science awards, reflecting the broad impact of innate-immunity research across infectious disease and inflammatory biology.

### Why the Discovery Mattered

Work on innate immunity helped clarify how organisms detect pathogens quickly and trigger early defensive responses. The conceptual shift—treating innate immunity as an active, genetically encoded detection system—became foundational for later research into inflammation, sepsis, and host–pathogen interactions.
