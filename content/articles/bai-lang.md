## Summary

Chinese bandit leader known as the 'White Wolf' who led a massive peasant rebellion across five provinces during the early Republic era. His army grew to 20,000 before government forces defeated him.

## Additional Context

### Origins and setting

Bai Lang (白朗, commonly rendered in English sources as “Bai Lang,” literally “White Wolf”) emerged during a period of deep instability in North China following the fall of the Qing dynasty (1911) and the fragmentation of authority in the early Republic.

### Rebellion and operations

His forces were often described as a large, mobile bandit-rebel army that moved rapidly across provincial borders, taking advantage of weak local garrisons, poor communications, and the breakdown of rural administration.

Contemporary reporting and later summaries emphasize the mixture of motives typical of the era’s bandit movements: local grievances, opportunistic raiding for supplies and cash, and political slogans that helped attract recruits as the rebellion expanded.

### Government response

The campaign against Bai Lang involved regular troops and coordinated provincial suppression efforts, reflecting the Republic’s priority to reassert control over the countryside and protect transport routes, market towns, and county seats.

### Historical significance

Bai Lang is often cited in overviews of early Republican China as an example of how quickly armed movements could scale in conditions of economic stress and weak state capacity, and how “banditry” and “rebellion” frequently overlapped in both practice and official labeling.
