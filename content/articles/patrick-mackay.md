## Summary

British serial killer who murdered at least 3 people in London in 1974-1975, though suspected of up to 11. He targeted elderly victims and a Catholic priest. One of Britain's longest-serving prisoners.

## Early Life and Background

Accounts of Mackay's early life describe instability and episodes of institutionalization before the murders for which he was convicted. This background is often cited in discussions of how social services and mental health systems interacted with repeat offending in the period.

## Known Victims and Modus Operandi

The confirmed murders were committed in London in the mid-1970s. Reporting about the case has emphasized opportunistic attacks and the selection of vulnerable victims, including elderly individuals. Investigators also examined links to additional unsolved crimes, which contributed to higher speculative victim counts.

## Trial, Sentencing, and Imprisonment

Mackay was convicted of three murders and received a life sentence. He is frequently referenced as one of the United Kingdom's longest-serving prisoners, with parole decisions shaped by assessments of risk, responsibility, and the management of violent offenders over long periods.

## Public and Criminal-Justice Significance

The case is sometimes used in broader commentary about:

- how police attribute crimes when multiple unsolved offenses share partial similarities,
- the limits of evidence in historical investigations, and
- the long-term incarceration of individuals convicted of serious violent crimes.
