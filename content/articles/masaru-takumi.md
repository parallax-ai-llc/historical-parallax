## Summary

Fourth kumicho of the Yamaguchi-gumi who led during a bloody war with the Ichiwa-kai splinter group. The Yama-Ichi War resulted in 25 deaths and over 70 injuries.

## Organizational context

The Yamaguchi-gumi is widely described as Japan's largest yakuza syndicate.
Its leadership succession (kumicho line) has historically involved internal factions and patronage networks.
Public attention tends to increase when disputes spill into visible violence.

## The Yama-Ichi War (high-level)

The conflict referred to as the Yama-Ichi War is commonly summarized as a turf and legitimacy struggle.
It involved the main Yamaguchi-gumi organization and the Ichiwa-kai splinter group.
Retaliatory attacks, assassinations, and ambushes contributed to the casualty figures often cited.
Japanese authorities and media treated the conflict as a major organized-crime incident of the period.

## Leadership and impact

As kumicho, Takumi is generally portrayed as presiding over consolidation during and after the conflict.
Leadership in such groups typically mixes internal discipline, alliance management, and relations with rival syndicates.
The war's aftermath influenced policing tactics and public policy toward organized crime.
It also became part of the broader narrative that later supported anti-yakuza legislation and enforcement.
