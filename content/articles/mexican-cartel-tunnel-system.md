## Overview

Cross-border tunnels have been used by multiple criminal organizations as a method to move narcotics, cash, weapons, and people across the U.S.–Mexico border while reducing exposure to surface-level interdiction.

## Typical characteristics

While tunnel designs vary, frequently reported features include:

- concealed entrances in warehouses, homes, or commercial buildings
- reinforcement, lighting, and ventilation to enable longer construction and use
- rails or carts to move heavy loads efficiently
- exits positioned near border infrastructure on the U.S. side

## Discovery and countermeasures

Law-enforcement agencies have used a mixture of surveillance, human intelligence, and geophysical detection methods to locate tunnels. Once discovered, tunnels are typically mapped, documented, and then sealed or destroyed to prevent re-use.

## Notable patterns

Observers often note that tunnel activity increases when surface routes face heightened enforcement pressure, creating an adaptation cycle between traffickers and authorities. The sophistication of some tunnels suggests access to engineering expertise, capital, and sustained operational planning.

## Broader implications

Tunnel systems are discussed in policy debates about border security, corruption risks, and the economics of illicit trafficking, particularly in high-volume corridors where the incentives to invest in infrastructure are strongest.
