## Summary

Kenneth Lay and Jeffrey Skilling led Enron's massive accounting fraud that became the largest corporate scandal in US history at the time. The company's collapse cost investors $74 billion and employees their savings.

## Additional Context

- Enron's collapse became a landmark case in modern corporate governance, illustrating how complex financial structures can mask risk and losses.
- The scandal was driven by a broader network than two individuals, involving senior leadership, finance teams, and external gatekeepers.

## How the Fraud Worked (High Level)

- Enron used off-balance-sheet entities and aggressive accounting to move debt and underperforming assets away from the core financial statements.
- The company emphasized reported earnings and stock price growth, which amplified incentives to maintain the appearance of profitability.
- Internal controls and board oversight failed to adequately challenge the company's reported performance and risk exposure.

## Consequences

- Arthur Andersen, Enron's auditor, collapsed after its conviction (later overturned) and reputational damage, reshaping the auditing industry.
- The US response included the SarbanesOxley Act (2002), which tightened requirements for public-company reporting, internal controls, and executive accountability.
- The case is frequently used in business education as an example of ethical failure, incentive misalignment, and governance breakdown.

## Legacy

Enron remains a reference point for discussions about financial transparency, regulatory design, and the limits of market discipline when information is distorted.
