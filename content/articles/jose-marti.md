## Summary

José Julián Martí Pérez (January 28, 1853 – May 19, 1895) was a Cuban poet, essayist, journalist, philosopher, and revolutionary leader who organized the Cuban War of Independence (1895) against Spanish colonial rule. Considered Cuba's national hero ("Apóstol de la Independencia") and one of Latin America's most influential literary figures, Martí died in his first battle but left behind a vast body of writing that has been claimed by both communist and anti-communist Cubans, becoming a contested but universal symbol of Cuban identity.

---

## Early Life and Education

Born January 28, 1853, in Havana, Cuba:

**Background**:
- Father Mariano Martí, Spanish soldier from Valencia
- Mother Leonor Pérez, from Canary Islands
- Modest family of Spanish immigrants
- Eldest of seven siblings (six sisters)

**Education**:
- Municipal school in Havana
- Tutored by Rafael María de Mendive — major formative influence
- Mendive radicalized young Martí toward independence

---

## Political Awakening

### First Imprisonment (1869)

**At Age 16**:
- Wrote letter accusing fellow student of "apostasy" for joining Spanish forces
- Arrested by Spanish authorities
- Sentenced to six years hard labor at San Lázaro quarry
- Permanent leg injuries from chain gang

**Exile to Spain** (1871):
- Sentence commuted to exile
- Studied in Madrid and Zaragoza
- Earned degrees in Law (1874) and Philosophy and Letters (1874)
- Wrote "El presidio político en Cuba" — exposed Spanish prison brutality

---

## Years of Exile

### Latin American Travels

**Mexico** (1875-1877):
- Worked as journalist
- Met future wife Carmen Zayas Bazán
- Engaged with Mexican intellectual life

**Guatemala** (1877-1878):
- Professor of literature
- Married Carmen Zayas Bazán (1877)
- Returned briefly to Cuba (1878) under amnesty

**Second Exile**:
- Re-arrested 1879 for political activities
- Returned to Spain, then Venezuela (1881)

### New York Years (1881-1895)

**Center of Activity**:
- Settled in New York City
- Became hub for Cuban exile community
- Worked as journalist, translator, diplomat
- Consul for Uruguay, Argentina, Paraguay

**Journalism**:
- Correspondent for Latin American newspapers
- "La Nación" (Buenos Aires)
- Acute observer of US politics, society, capitalism
- Reported on Coney Island, US presidents, Statue of Unveiling

---

## Literary Achievements

### Poetry

**Major Collections**:
- *Ismaelillo* (1882) — for his son José Francisco
- *Versos sencillos* (1891) — most famous, includes "Guantanamera" lyrics
- *Versos libres* (posthumous) — modernist innovations

**Style**:
- Pioneer of modernismo movement
- Rich symbolism, musicality
- Bridge between Romanticism and Modernism

### Prose

**Essays**:
- "Nuestra América" (1891) — defining Latin American identity manifesto
- Critique of US expansionism
- Concept of "Mestizo America" against European/Anglo dominance

**Children's Magazine**:
- *La Edad de Oro* (1889) — illustrated magazine for Latin American children
- Modeled progressive education values

**Crónicas** (chronicles):
- Reports on US life for Latin American audiences
- Death of US presidents, urban life, social commentary
- Among the most important journalism of the era

## Founding the Independence Movement

### Cuban Revolutionary Party (PRC)

**Organization** (1892):
- Founded Partido Revolucionario Cubano in New York
- Unified divided Cuban exile factions
- Built organization across Cuban diaspora (US, Mexico, Caribbean)
- Newspaper *Patria* as voice of movement

### War Planning

**Strategic Vision**:
- Reconciled with veteran generals Máximo Gómez and Antonio Maceo
- Coordinated military and political strategy
- Raised funds among tobacco workers in Florida
- Avoided premature uprising despite pressure

### Failed First Attempt

**Fernandina Plan** (1895):
- US authorities seized weapons in Florida
- Major setback; near collapse of plan
- Refused to abandon mission
- Issued "Manifesto of Montecristi" with Gómez (March 1895)

---

## Cuban War of Independence (1895)

### Landing in Cuba

**Return**:
- Landed at Playitas de Cajobabo, Oriente, April 11, 1895
- First time on Cuban soil in over a decade
- Joined existing rebel forces under Gómez

### Death at Dos Ríos

**Battle of Dos Ríos** (May 19, 1895):
- Spanish forces ambushed rebel column
- Martí, against Gómez's orders, charged Spanish lines
- Killed by three rifle shots
- Body briefly captured by Spanish, later recovered

**Significance**:
- Died in his first battle, age 42
- Death immediately mythologized
- War continued without him for three more years
- US intervention in 1898 ended Spanish rule (controversial outcome he had warned against)

## Political Thought

### Anti-Imperialism

**Warning About US**:
- Famously wrote: "I have lived in the monster, and know its entrails"
- Warned of US economic and political domination
- Predicted US would absorb Cuba if independence delayed
- Argued for Latin American unity against US expansion

### Vision for Cuba

**Republican Ideals**:
- Inclusive racial society — "Cuban is more than white, more than black"
- Universal suffrage and education
- Gradual social reforms
- Civilian (not military) governance after independence
- Rejection of caudillismo

### "Nuestra América"

**Concept**:
- Latin America as distinct civilization
- Mestizo identity, indigenous heritage
- Rejection of European/Yankee imperialism
- Education and reason as basis for nation-building

## Personal Life

### Family

**Marriage**:
- Wife Carmen Zayas Bazán
- Son José Francisco "Pepito" Martí (1878-1945)
- Marriage strained by exile and politics
- Separated for long periods

### Other Relationships

**María Mantilla**:
- Daughter of Carmen Miyares (close friend in NY)
- Some scholars believe Martí was her biological father
- Wrote "Ismaelillo"-style verses for her
- Mother of actor César Romero

## Legacy

### Cuban National Hero

**Universal Veneration**:
- Recognized as Cuba's "Apóstol"
- Statues, monuments, schools, streets named for him
- Image on Cuban currency
- January 28 (his birthday) celebrated nationally

### Contested Symbol

**Multiple Cubas Claim Him**:
- Cuban Revolution under Castro adopted him as ideological forefather
- Cuban exile community in Miami also claims him
- His complete writings cited by both sides
- Genuinely defies single political appropriation

### Latin American Influence

**Beyond Cuba**:
- "Apostle of Latin American Independence"
- Influence on Modernismo literary movement
- Read across Spanish-speaking world
- Inspiration for later anti-imperialist movements

---

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

- Visionary independence leader
- Major literary innovator
- Prophet warning of US imperialism
- Ethical political thinker rejecting demagoguery
- Authentic martyr for cause

### Critical Views

- Romantic vision exceeded political pragmatism
- Death may have prevented greater achievements
- Some accuse posthumous appropriation by Castro regime
- Personal life complications
- Idealism vs. realities of revolutionary war
