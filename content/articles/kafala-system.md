## Summary

Sponsorship system in Gulf states that ties workers to employers, enabling exploitation. Workers face confiscated passports, unpaid wages, abuse, and inability to change employers or leave.

## Overview

The kafala (sponsorship) system refers to legal and administrative arrangements used in parts of the Middle East—especially Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) states—to regulate the employment and residency of migrant workers. In many implementations, a worker’s immigration status is linked to a specific sponsor (typically an employer), which can produce strong dependency.

## How the System Works (High-Level)

While details differ by country and sector, descriptions of kafala-style arrangements commonly include:

- a sponsor’s role in initiating or maintaining a worker’s legal residency status
- employer control over job transfer processes (such as changing employers)
- administrative requirements that can make leaving a job or exiting the country difficult without sponsor cooperation

When combined with recruitment fees, language barriers, and limited access to legal remedies, these features can increase vulnerability to exploitation.

## Commonly Reported Abuses

Human-rights reporting and investigative journalism often cite patterns such as:

- withholding or delayed payment of wages
- excessive working hours and unsafe conditions
- retention of passports and other identity documents
- threats of retaliation through deportation or legal complaints

Not every worker experiences these outcomes, but the structural incentives and power imbalance are a central reason the system is controversial.

## Reforms and Enforcement Gaps

Various states have announced reforms over time, including changes to exit permits, job mobility rules, or complaint mechanisms. A common critique is that formal rule changes may not translate into practice if:

- workers cannot access enforcement institutions safely
- penalties for abusive employers are weak or inconsistently applied
- information and translation support are limited

## Why It Matters

The kafala system is frequently discussed in the context of labor migration, global supply chains, and major construction or domestic-work sectors that rely heavily on migrant labor.
