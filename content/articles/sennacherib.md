## Summary

Assyrian king who destroyed Babylon and boasted of massacring populations, later assassinated by his own sons.

Sennacherib was king of the Neo-Assyrian Empire (reigned **705–681 BCE**) and is remembered for both military campaigns and major building projects, especially at **Nineveh**. Much of what is known comes from royal inscriptions (which were politically motivated) alongside archaeology and later historical traditions.

The label "war criminal" is a modern moral category; however, ancient sources and modern scholarship do describe Assyrian imperial warfare as frequently involving forced deportations, severe reprisals, and the public display of violence as deterrence.

## Background

- **Dynasty**: Son of Sargon II, inheriting a large imperial system spanning Mesopotamia and the Levant.
- **Capital and administration**: Strongly associated with the development of Nineveh as a royal center.
- **Sources**: Royal annals, reliefs, and later accounts (including biblical and classical traditions) often overlap but also conflict.

## Major Campaigns (High-Level)

- **Babylonia**: A recurring strategic and symbolic focus; culminated in the sack of Babylon in **689 BCE**.
- **Levant (including Judah)**: Campaigns in the western provinces are commonly associated with the **701 BCE** campaign and the siege of Jerusalem under King Hezekiah (noted in Assyrian records and in the Hebrew Bible).
- **Imperial control**: Like other Assyrian rulers, relied on tribute systems, provincial restructuring, and deportations to manage revolt-prone regions.

## Building and Legacy

- Oversaw large-scale construction at **Nineveh**, including palatial complexes and urban works that later sources associate with the grandeur of the Assyrian court.
- Remembered as an emblematic Assyrian imperial monarch: effective at projecting power, but also associated with extreme punitive violence in both ancient propaganda and later memory.
