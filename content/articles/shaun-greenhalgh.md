## Summary

British art forger who created fakes spanning 2,000 years of art history including an Egyptian statue, a Roman silver plate, and works by LS Lowry and Thomas Moran. He was called the most diverse art forger ever caught.

## The "Greenhalgh family" operation (high-level)

- Public accounts often describe Greenhalgh working with close family members to authenticate and sell forged artifacts and artworks.
- The scheme leveraged plausible provenance stories and the willingness of collectors and institutions to acquire rare items.

## Methods and range

- Greenhalgh's forgeries were notable for spanning a wide range of periods and media, from supposed ancient objects to modern paintings.
- Reports emphasized an ability to imitate materials and styles, and to craft an accompanying backstory that fit what buyers wanted to believe.
- The diversity of objects also complicated detection: expertise is often highly specialized, and cross-period scrutiny can be inconsistent.

## Detection and investigation

- The exposure of the forgeries is commonly linked to expert re-examination, scientific testing, and inconsistencies in provenance.
- The high-profile case involving the so-called **"Amarna Princess"** highlighted the vulnerability of regional museums and smaller institutions operating with limited resources.

## Legal outcome and aftermath

- Greenhalgh was convicted and served a prison sentence, with the case becoming a cautionary tale for the art market.
- The scandal contributed to broader emphasis on due diligence, provenance research, and the use of scientific methods in authentication.
- It is frequently cited in discussions of how fraud exploits gaps between connoisseurship, paperwork, and institutional incentives.
