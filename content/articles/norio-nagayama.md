## Summary

Japanese spree killer who murdered four people with a stolen gun in 1968 at age 19. His case became a landmark in Japanese criminal law, establishing the 'Nagayama criteria' for when the death penalty is applied. He became an author in prison.

## Background

Nagayama grew up in postwar Japan and, by his late teens, was living on his own and doing irregular work. The killings occurred during a period of instability in his life and immediately drew national attention because of the unusually young age of the offender and the use of a stolen firearm.

## Case and Trial

His case moved through Japan’s courts over many years, with multiple rulings and appeals. Public debate around the trial often centered on how to weigh the gravity of multiple murders against the defendant’s youth and circumstances.

## Legal Impact: the “Nagayama Criteria”

Japanese courts later summarized a set of factors commonly referred to as the *Nagayama criteria* when considering capital punishment. They are typically described at a high level as including the number of victims, the nature and seriousness of the crime, motive, impact on society, the bereaved families’ feelings, the offender’s prior record, and the offender’s age and prospects for rehabilitation.

## Writing and Legacy

While incarcerated, Nagayama wrote and published works that kept his case in the public eye beyond the courtroom. The longevity of the proceedings and the legal framework associated with them made the case a reference point in discussions of sentencing and the death penalty in Japan.

## Key Takeaways

- The case is remembered less for complex investigative technique than for its long legal afterlife.
- “Nagayama criteria” is commonly used as shorthand for the multi-factor approach courts cite when addressing capital sentencing.
- It remains a frequently referenced example in Japanese debates about punishment, youth, and rehabilitation.
