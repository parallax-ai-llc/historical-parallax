## Summary

The Travis Walton incident is widely considered the most famous and controversial "alien abduction" case on record. Unlike typical "bedroom abductions," this event began as a multiple-witness sighting involving a logging crew of seven men in the Apache-Sitgreaves National Forests in Arizona. 

On November 5, 1975, the crew witnessed a glowing disc-shaped craft. Travis Walton (22) approached it and was allegedly struck by a beam of light. His panicked coworkers fled, leaving him behind. Walton disappeared for five days, triggering a massive manhunt and murder investigation. He returned dehydrated and traumatized, claiming to have been taken aboard a craft. The case gained lasting fame due to the fact that the six witnessing coworkers passed lie detector tests (polygraphs) regarding his disappearance, and it later inspired the Hollywood movie *Fire in the Sky*.[^1]

## The Incident

### The Sighting (Nov 5, 1975)
A logging crew of seven men—**Mike Rogers** (foreman), **Travis Walton**, Allan Dalis, Dwayne Smith, John Goulette, Kenneth Peterson, and Steve Pierce—finished work around 6:00 PM.
*   **The Object:** While driving their truck down a fire road, they saw a "luminous object," shaped like a flattened disc, hovering above a clearing. It glowed with a yellowish light.
*   **The Beam:** Walton, entranced, jumped out of the truck to get a closer look. The crew yelled at him to stop. As he stood beneath it, the craft began to wobble and make a loud noise. Suddenly, a blue-green beam of light shot out and struck Walton in the chest, throwing him backward several feet.
*   **The Panic:** Believing Walton was dead or "zapped," Mike Rogers stepped on the gas, and the crew fled in terror.[^2]

### The Search
After driving a few miles, the crew composed themselves and returned to the site. The craft was gone, and so was Walton. They reported the incident to the Navajo County Sheriff.
*   **Suspicion:** The authorities suspected foul play. They believed the "UFO story" was a cover-up for a drug deal gone wrong or a murder. A massive search party (helicopters, jeeps, men on horseback) combed the forest but found no trace.[^3]

---

## Five Days Missing

For five days, Walton was gone. During this time, the Sheriff subjected the six remaining crew members to polygraph examinations to prove they had murdered him.
*   **The Polygraph Results:** Five of the six men passed the test conclusively (proving they firmly believed they saw a UFO and did not kill Walton). The sixth result was inconclusive (due to anxiety), but not deceptive. This result shocked the investigators.[^4]

---

## The Return (Nov 10, 1975)

Travis Walton woke up on the pavement of a highway outside Heber, Arizona, late at night. He watched the craft shoot straight up into the sky. He ran to a phone booth and collapsed, calling his sister.
*   **Condition:** He was dehydrated, had lost weight, and was in a state of shock. He believed he had been gone only a few hours.
*   **The Story:** Under hypnosis and in interviews, Walton recalled waking up in a hospital-like room. He was confronted by three short, bald creatures with large eyes (classic "Greys"). Panicking, he fought them off and ran into another room where he met human-looking figures wearing blue uniforms, who put a mask on him, causing him to black out again.[^5]

## Controversy and *Fire in the Sky*

### The Movie
In 1993, Paramount Pictures released *Fire in the Sky*, based on Walton's book.
*   **Fictionalization:** While the kidnapping scenes were accurate to the witness accounts, the scenes inside the ship were heavily fictionalized by Hollywood to be more horrific and "gooey," depicting a torture-chamber environment that Walton rarely claimed. Walton has spent years clarifying the difference between the movie and his actual memory.[^6]

### Skepticism
Skeptics like **Philip J. Klass** argued the entire event was a hoax concocted to get out of a logging contract that the crew was behind schedule on (the "Force Majeure" clause).
*   **Polygraph Debate:** Critics point out that Walton himself failed a polygraph test administered by the *National Enquirer* shortly after his return (though he passed subsequent ones).
*   **Mike Rogers' Confession (2021):** In a recent twist, decades later, foreman Mike Rogers posted on social media implying the story might have been exaggerated or a hoax, though later clarified he was angry at Walton for excluding him from movie rights/royalties. This internal feud has reignited debates about the case's validity.[^7]
