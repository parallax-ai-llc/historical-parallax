## Summary

Christina Koch is an American astronaut and electrical engineer who became one of NASA's most accomplished spaceflight veterans of her generation. She is best known for setting the record for the longest single spaceflight by a woman, spending 328 consecutive days aboard the International Space Station (ISS) between 2019 and 2020. Her contributions to human spaceflight extend beyond endurance records — she participated in the first all-female spacewalk alongside fellow astronaut Jessica Meir in October 2019, a milestone widely celebrated as a landmark moment for gender representation in space exploration.

Koch's background in electrical engineering and her extensive fieldwork in remote environments made her an ideal candidate for long-duration spaceflight. Her scientific work aboard the ISS encompassed research into fluid physics, plant biology, and human health in microgravity — fields with direct implications for future deep-space missions to the Moon and Mars. In 2024, she was selected as a crew member for NASA's Artemis II mission, which would make her the first woman to travel to lunar orbit.

## Early Life & Background

Christina Hammock Koch was born on January 29, 1979, in Grand Rapids, Michigan, and grew up in Jacksonville, North Carolina. She demonstrated an early aptitude for science and mathematics, going on to earn a Bachelor of Science in Electrical Engineering and a Bachelor of Science in Physics from North Carolina State University in 2001, followed by a Master of Science in Electrical Engineering from the same institution in 2002.

Before joining NASA, Koch worked at the NASA Goddard Space Flight Center developing scientific instruments for space missions. She also served as a field researcher in some of Earth's most extreme environments — including remote Antarctic and Arctic stations — which honed the self-reliance and psychological resilience demanded by long-duration spaceflight.

## Rise to Prominence

Koch was selected as a NASA astronaut in 2013 as part of the agency's 21st astronaut class, a group notable for its gender parity. After completing astronaut training, she supported ISS operations and awaited her first flight assignment. She launched to the ISS in March 2019 aboard a Soyuz spacecraft and would not return to Earth until February 6, 2020 — 328 days later. This exceeded the previous record for women in space held by Peggy Whitson (289 days on a single mission) and placed Koch among the top longest single-mission durations in NASA history.

## Key Achievements

- **328-day spaceflight (2019–2020):** The longest single spaceflight by a woman, providing critical data on long-duration effects of microgravity on the female body.
- **First all-female spacewalk (October 18, 2019):** Conducted with Jessica Meir, the spacewalk lasted 7 hours and 17 minutes and replaced a failed power control unit on the ISS.
- **Six total spacewalks** during her 2019–2020 mission, accumulating over 42 hours of extravehicular activity.
- **Artemis II crew selection (2023):** Named as mission specialist for the Artemis II lunar flyby mission, making her the first woman assigned to a lunar trajectory flight.

## Controversies & Criticism

Koch's career has not been marked by significant personal controversy. A first all-female spacewalk had been planned for March 2019 but was scrubbed due to a lack of two medium-sized spacesuits in the correct configuration — a logistical shortfall that drew criticism of NASA for insufficient preparation for female crew members, though Koch herself avoided public criticism of the agency over the matter.

## Legacy & Influence

Koch's record-setting mission generated substantial physiological data that will inform planning for crewed Mars missions, where flight durations could exceed a year. Her participation in the Artemis program positions her as a symbolic and practical bridge between the ISS era and humanity's return to lunar exploration. She has become a prominent figure in STEM outreach, particularly for young women pursuing careers in science and engineering.
