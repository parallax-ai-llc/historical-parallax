## Summary

American commodities trader who perpetrated the 1963 Salad Oil Scandal by filling tanks with water topped with thin layers of oil. Swindle caused $180 million in losses.

## How the Scheme Worked (High-Level)

The “Salad Oil Scandal” is often cited as a classic example of **collateral fraud**:

- De Angelis’s firm stored soybean oil in large tanks.
- Inspectors and lenders relied on **warehouse receipts** and spot checks to verify inventory.
- Tanks were reportedly filled largely with **water**, with a thin layer of oil on top so that superficial inspection suggested they were full of oil.

By using misleading inventory documentation, the operation could borrow against “oil” that did not exist in the stated quantities.

## Why It Spread

Commodity financing can involve many intermediaries—warehouses, inspectors, banks, and trade counterparties. When documentation is trusted more than physical verification, fraud can scale quickly. In this case, losses cascaded as firms discovered that pledged collateral was worth far less than represented.

## Consequences and Impact

Public accounts link the scandal to:

- major losses for creditors and counterparties,
- bankruptcies and litigation,
- and changes in how commodity inventories and collateral were audited.

The episode is sometimes referenced in discussions of due diligence failures and the limits of “paper-based” controls.

## Legacy

The scandal remains an instructive case for risk management:

- **Independent verification** matters when assets are hard to measure.
- **Concentration risk** (many loans tied to one collateral pool) can turn a single fraud into systemic damage.
- **Incentive design** in inspection and warehousing can create blind spots if oversight is weak.
