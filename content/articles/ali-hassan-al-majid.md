## Summary

Iraqi military commander known as 'Chemical Ali' for his role in the Halabja chemical attack and the Anfal campaign against the Kurds that killed up to 182,000 people. He was Saddam's cousin. Executed by hanging.

## Background

Ali Hassan al-Majid was a senior Iraqi official in the Ba'athist state apparatus and a close relative of Saddam Hussein. He rose through security and party structures during a period when the Iraqi government relied heavily on intelligence services and coercive institutions to manage internal dissent.

## Role in Ba'athist governance

Accounts of al-Majid's career typically emphasize his position within a system that fused party authority, military command, and internal security. His nickname "Chemical Ali" is tied to allegations and findings related to chemical weapons use and mass violence in the late 1980s.

## Anfal campaign and Kurdish repression

The Anfal campaign is widely described as a coordinated effort targeting Kurdish areas, involving forced displacement, mass detention, and large-scale killing. Al-Majid has been portrayed in sources as a key administrator and commander associated with those operations.

## Post-2003 capture, trial, and execution

After the 2003 invasion of Iraq, al-Majid was captured and prosecuted in Iraqi courts. His execution by hanging in 2010 is frequently cited in discussions of transitional justice in post-Ba'athist Iraq.

## Legacy and historical debate

- For many Iraqis and international observers, al-Majid symbolizes the brutality of the Ba'athist security state.
- In legal and historical literature, his cases are referenced in debates about accountability for mass atrocities and the capacity of domestic courts to handle crimes of international concern.
