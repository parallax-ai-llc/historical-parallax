## Summary

Adil "ScreaM" Benrlitom (born July 2, 1994) is a Belgian professional esports player of Moroccan descent, widely regarded as one of the most mechanically gifted aimers in competitive FPS history. He earned the nickname "The Headshot Machine" during his Counter-Strike: Global Offensive career for maintaining headshot percentages that routinely exceeded 60%, a feat virtually unmatched at the professional level.

After over a decade in CS:GO, ScreaM transitioned to Valorant in 2020, where he quickly established himself as one of the game's top duelists. His move to Team Liquid's Valorant roster marked a career resurgence, bringing him renewed relevance and competitive success in Riot Games' tactical shooter. ScreaM's crossover success helped legitimize Valorant as a destination for elite CS:GO talent.

## Early Life & Background

Adil Benrlitom was born on July 2, 1994, in Brussels, Belgium, to a family of Moroccan heritage. He grew up playing Counter-Strike from a young age, developing his signature aim style through countless hours of deathmatch and community servers. His older brother, Nabil "Nivera" Benrlitom, also became a professional esports player, later joining Team Liquid's Valorant roster alongside ScreaM.

## Rise to Prominence

ScreaM began his professional CS:GO career around 2012–2013, competing with French-speaking European teams including VeryGames, Titan, and Epsilon. He gained widespread attention for his one-tap headshot style, which became iconic within the community. His time on Team Envy (formerly LDLC) and later G2 Esports in 2016–2017 represented his peak in CS:GO, where he was considered a world-class rifler.

Despite his raw mechanical talent, ScreaM's CS:GO career was marked by frequent roster changes and inconsistency at the highest level. He cycled through numerous teams, often struggling to find a stable roster that could complement his aggressive playstyle. By 2019, his career in Counter-Strike had stalled.

In June 2020, ScreaM made the pivotal decision to switch to Valorant, joining Team Liquid's newly formed roster. The transition revitalized his career immediately, as Valorant's movement mechanics and gunplay rewarded his precise aim style even more than CS:GO had.

## Key Achievements

In CS:GO, ScreaM maintained a career headshot percentage above 55%, earning him legendary status among aim-focused players. He won multiple regional titles and reached playoffs at several major events with G2 Esports in 2016 and 2017.

In Valorant, he helped lead Team Liquid to multiple VCT EMEA playoff appearances starting in 2021. He was a key contributor to Liquid's qualification for VCT Masters events and consistently ranked among the top-rated duelists in the EMEA region. His performances on agents like Jett and Phoenix set early meta standards for aggressive duelist play.

## Controversies & Criticism

ScreaM's CS:GO career drew criticism for his perceived inability to translate individual brilliance into consistent team success. Analysts noted that his one-tap playstyle, while spectacular, could be punishing in high-pressure rounds where spray control was more reliable. His frequent team changes — playing for over a dozen organizations — also raised questions about team compatibility.

In Valorant, some critics questioned whether former CS:GO players received outsized attention compared to native Valorant talent. However, ScreaM's sustained performance largely silenced these concerns.

## Legacy & Influence

ScreaM's legacy centers on his extraordinary mechanical skill and his successful reinvention across two competitive titles. He proved that elite FPS fundamentals transcend any single game, and his transition to Valorant paved the way for other CS:GO veterans to make similar moves. His headshot-centric playstyle inspired a generation of players to prioritize crosshair placement and precision over spray-heavy approaches. Within the Belgian esports scene, he remains the country's most internationally recognized competitive player.
