## Summary

Turkmen president who succeeded Niyazov and created his own personality cult. He renamed months, built a $5 billion white marble city, and was obsessed with Akhal-Teke horses. He installed his son as successor in 2022.

## Additional Context

## Political Environment

Turkmenistan is frequently described as one of the most politically closed states in the post-Soviet space. During Berdimuhamedow’s years in power, the country maintained tight control over public life, including restrictions on independent journalism, opposition organizing, and many forms of civil society activity.

## Personality Cult and Public Image

State media and official ceremonies cultivated a leader-centered narrative, emphasizing stability, national pride, and social order. Large monuments, choreographed public events, and extensive use of portraits and slogans were recurring features of the public sphere.

## Urban Development and Prestige Projects

A visible component of the era was investment in monumental construction—especially in Ashgabat—often highlighted as a showcase of national prosperity. These projects were sometimes framed as modernization efforts, while outside observers also discussed them in terms of prestige spending and governance priorities.

## Clarifications and Continuities

Some of the most unusual symbolic measures associated with modern Turkmenistan—such as naming and renaming practices—are part of a broader pattern that began under Saparmurat Niyazov. Accounts of Berdimuhamedow’s rule often emphasize that, even when specific policies changed, the overall governing style remained highly centralized and personalized.

## Succession (2022)

The 2022 transfer of the presidency to Serdar Berdimuhamedow is commonly interpreted as a managed transition that preserved regime continuity. Berdimuhamedow retained senior positions and influence after leaving the presidency, underscoring the role of elite control in Turkmenistan’s political system.
