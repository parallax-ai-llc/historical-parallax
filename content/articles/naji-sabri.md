Iraqi former foreign minister who was secretly a CIA source. He provided intelligence before the 2003 invasion that Iraq had abandoned its WMD programs, but his reports were ignored by the Bush administration.

## Background

Naji Sabri al-Hadithi is best known internationally for serving as Iraq’s foreign minister in the final years of Saddam Hussein’s government. In that role he represented Iraq during a period defined by UN sanctions, disputes over inspections, and escalating U.S.–Iraq tensions.

## Role in Pre-2003 Intelligence Reporting

Multiple public accounts describe Sabri as providing information to U.S. intelligence ahead of the 2003 invasion. The most frequently repeated claim is that he indicated Iraq had largely abandoned active weapons of mass destruction programs, a point that later became central to post-invasion debate about the quality of prewar intelligence.

Because intelligence reporting is often fragmentary and filtered through policy processes, summaries of this episode tend to focus on the gap between what some sources reported and what policymakers ultimately presented as justification for war.

## Diplomatic Context (2001–2003)

As foreign minister, Sabri operated in an environment shaped by:

- Negotiations and confrontation over UN weapons inspections
- Diplomatic efforts to end or soften sanctions
- Attempts to manage relations with neighboring states and major powers

## After 2003

Open sources commonly note that many senior officials of the Iraqi state were removed from power following the invasion and the collapse of the Ba’athist government. Reporting about Sabri’s later status and movements has varied over time, and detailed public information is limited compared to more prominent figures.

## Why the Case Is Cited

Sabri is often referenced in discussions about:

- Human intelligence (HUMINT) sources and their reliability
- How intelligence is weighed against political assumptions
- The post-2003 reassessment of WMD claims
