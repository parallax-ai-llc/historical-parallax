## Summary

Lou Gehrig played 2,130 consecutive games for the Yankees, earning the nickname 'The Iron Horse.' He was forced to retire due to ALS, now commonly called 'Lou Gehrig's disease.'

## Early life

Gehrig was born in New York City to German immigrant parents. He attended Columbia University, where he played college baseball before joining the New York Yankees.

## Playing style and achievements

- Primarily a first baseman, Gehrig was known for durability, power hitting, and run production.
- He was a key part of multiple Yankees championship teams during the 1920s and 1930s.
- His consecutive-games streak (2,130) stood as a major league record for decades.

## Illness and farewell

In 1939, Gehrig was diagnosed with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS). His retirement was marked by a widely remembered speech at Yankee Stadium, often summarized by the line about being "the luckiest man on the face of the earth."

## Legacy

- ALS is frequently referred to in the United States as "Lou Gehrig's disease" because of his public association with the condition.
- Gehrig remains a prominent figure in baseball history and Yankees tradition, often cited as an emblem of perseverance and sportsmanship.
