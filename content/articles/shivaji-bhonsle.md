## Summary

Maratha warrior king who carved out an empire through guerrilla warfare against the Mughal and Bijapur sultanates. While a celebrated Hindu hero, his campaigns involved widespread plunder and violence.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1659 | Killed Bijapur general Afzal Khan through deception at a peace meeting |
| 1674 | Crowned himself Chhatrapati establishing Maratha sovereignty |

## Political and military context

- Shivaji emerged in the 17th-century Deccan, where the Mughal Empire, the Bijapur Sultanate, and other powers competed for territory and revenue.
- His forces relied on mobility, local knowledge, and the use of hill forts to contest larger imperial armies.
- Raids and revenue extraction were common features of Deccan warfare in this period, and civilian populations often bore the costs.

## State-building and administration

- Shivaji built administrative institutions to collect revenue, maintain forts, and sustain a standing military.
- His rule is often associated with the promotion of Marathi language and regional political identity.
- Accounts of religious policy vary; many summaries emphasize pragmatism in alliances and governance alongside an enduring symbolic association with Hindu kingship.

## Controversies and competing narratives

- Episodes such as the killing of Afzal Khan and major raids (including sackings) are alternately framed as strategic necessities, acts of treachery, or criminal violence depending on the historian and political tradition.
- Modern commemoration can be politically charged, with debates over how to balance celebration of resistance with acknowledgement of wartime atrocities.

## Legacy

- Shivaji's kingdom became a foundation for the later Maratha Confederacy, which grew into a major power in 18th-century India.
- He remains a key figure in Indian historical memory, with his image invoked in regional, national, and religious politics.
