## Summary

Cryptocurrency hedge fund Three Arrows Capital collapsed with $3 billion in debt after making leveraged bets on Luna and other crypto assets. Founders Su Zhu and Kyle Davies fled.

## Background

Three Arrows Capital (3AC) was a Singapore-based cryptocurrency hedge fund that became a prominent participant in the 2020–2021 crypto bull market. It was known for taking large directional positions and using leverage across multiple crypto venues.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- **Concentrated, leveraged bets:** Reporting and later court filings described 3AC as having significant, leveraged exposure to major crypto themes of the period (including the Terra/Luna ecosystem and other large-cap crypto assets).
- **Market shock and margin calls:** When crypto prices fell sharply in 2022, lenders and trading counterparties issued margin calls. 3AC was widely reported to have failed to meet some of these calls, triggering liquidations and defaults.
- **Insolvency and liquidation:** Liquidators were appointed in mid-2022 as part of formal insolvency proceedings.

## Key People and Entities

- **Su Zhu** — Co-founder of 3AC.
- **Kyle Davies** — Co-founder of 3AC.
- **Creditors / counterparties** — Multiple crypto lenders and exchanges that extended credit or margin to 3AC.
- **Liquidators** — Court-appointed professionals tasked with identifying assets, reconstructing records, and pursuing recovery for creditors.

## Legal and Regulatory Proceedings (Overview)

Public reporting has covered a mix of civil insolvency matters and related enforcement actions, including cross-border coordination because 3AC’s operations, assets, and counterparties spanned multiple jurisdictions. Outcomes and allegations have varied by forum and are subject to ongoing legal process.

## Impact

3AC’s collapse became a major contagion event in the 2022 crypto downturn:

- It contributed to losses and liquidity stress at several crypto lenders and trading firms.
- It accelerated risk re-evaluations around unsecured/under-collateralized lending and rehypothecation practices in crypto markets.
- It pushed exchanges, lenders, and funds toward stricter collateral management and transparency demands.

## Notes on Terminology

The term “fraud” is often used in media and online discussions about large financial collapses. In formal contexts, findings depend on specific legal determinations (e.g., misrepresentation, breach of duty, or other claims) made by courts or regulators.
