## Summary

British serial killer who is believed to have murdered up to 21 people, including 11 of her own children, using arsenic poisoning. She killed for insurance money over a 20-year period. Hanged at Durham County Gaol.

## Historical Context

- Cotton's case is often discussed in the context of **Victorian England**, where:
  - infectious disease and child mortality were common,
  - medical diagnostics were limited compared to modern standards, and
  - social welfare systems were minimal.
- These conditions could make suspicious patterns harder to detect quickly, especially within families.

## Method and Detection (High-Level)

- **Arsenic** was a commonly available substance in the 19th century (used in products such as pest control), which made it a frequent agent in historical poisoning cases.
- Many accounts emphasize how poisoning could mimic ordinary illness symptoms, complicating early investigation.

## Motive and Insurance

- Retrospectives frequently frame the alleged motive around **burial clubs** and small insurance policies that paid on death.
- While individual payouts could be modest, repeated claims could create incentives, and the system could be exploited where oversight was weak.

## Investigation and Trial

- The case gained attention through:
  - suspicious clusters of deaths,
  - community reporting, and
  - forensic evidence available at the time.
- In modern discussions, Cotton is also used as a reference point for the evolution of:
  - **toxicology**,
  - coroner systems, and
  - criminal procedure.

## Significance

- Cotton's story remains prominent in British true-crime history and is sometimes cited in broader conversations about:
  - domestic crimes,
  - gendered assumptions in policing, and
  - the early history of insurance fraud and regulation.
