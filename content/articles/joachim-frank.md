## Summary

Joachim Frank is a German-American biophysicist who shared the 2017 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for cryo-electron microscopy.

## Education and Early Career

Frank trained in physics and biophysics and became interested in how noisy, low-contrast electron microscopy images could be combined and processed to recover reliable structural information about biological molecules.[^1]

A recurring theme in his work is statistical treatment of many images: rather than relying on a single “best” micrograph, single-particle methods classify and average large numbers of particle projections to increase signal-to-noise and reconstruct three-dimensional structures.[^1]

## Scientific Contributions

Frank is widely credited with foundational advances in single-particle reconstruction for electron microscopy, including algorithms for aligning, classifying, and averaging particle images. These computational methods became central to modern cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM), where samples are rapidly frozen to preserve native-like conformations for imaging.[^1]

In practical terms, single-particle cryo-EM helps enable structure determination for large macromolecular assemblies that are difficult to crystallize, broadening the set of biological targets that can be studied at high resolution.[^1]

## Nobel Prize in Chemistry (2017)

Frank shared the 2017 Nobel Prize in Chemistry with Jacques Dubochet and Richard Henderson. The prize recognized the combined technical and methodological breakthroughs that transformed cryo-EM into a mainstream tool for structural biology.[^1]

Frank’s portion of the recognition is commonly summarized as the development of image-processing methods that made it feasible to reconstruct detailed 3D structures from many 2D electron micrographs of individual particles.[^1]

## Impact and Legacy

The “cryo-EM revolution” has affected fields ranging from basic molecular biology to drug discovery by accelerating structure determination and enabling the study of dynamic or heterogeneous complexes.[^1]

Frank’s work is frequently cited as an example of how computational and statistical methods can unlock new capabilities from existing imaging hardware by extracting more information from limited data.[^1]
