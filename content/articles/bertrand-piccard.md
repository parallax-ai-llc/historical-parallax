## Summary

Bertrand Piccard (born March 1, 1958) is a Swiss psychiatrist, aeronaut, and explorer who became the first person to circumnavigate the globe nonstop in a balloon and later co-piloted the first solar-powered aircraft to fly around the world. A member of the celebrated Piccard family of scientists and explorers, he has built a career at the intersection of adventure, science, and environmental advocacy. His achievements have made him one of the most recognized figures in modern exploration.

Beyond record-setting feats, Piccard has used his platform to champion clean energy and sustainable technology. He founded Solar Impulse, the project that demonstrated the viability of solar-powered flight, and subsequently launched the Solar Impulse Foundation, which identifies and promotes clean technologies capable of protecting the environment while generating economic growth.

## Early Life & Background

Bertrand Piccard was born in Lausanne, Switzerland, into a family defined by extraordinary ambition. His grandfather, Auguste Piccard, was the first person to reach the stratosphere in a pressurized balloon in 1931; his father, Jacques Piccard, co-piloted the bathyscaphe Trieste to the deepest point in the ocean, the Mariana Trench, in 1960. Growing up surrounded by exploration and scientific inquiry, Bertrand developed an early passion for adventure and flight. He trained as a psychiatrist at the University of Lausanne and also pursued hang gliding and microlight aviation, becoming a European hang gliding champion in 1985.

## Rise to Prominence

Piccard first gained international attention through ballooning. In 1992 he won the first transatlantic balloon race, the Chrysler Challenge. He then set his sights on circumnavigating the globe nonstop by balloon, a feat that had eluded numerous attempts. After two failed attempts in 1997 and 1998, Piccard and British co-pilot Brian Jones departed from Château-d'Oex, Switzerland, on March 1, 1999. Twenty days, hours, and minutes later, on March 21, 1999, they completed the first nonstop round-the-world balloon flight in Breitling Orbiter 3, covering approximately 45,755 kilometers (28,431 miles).

## Key Achievements

The 1999 circumnavigation aboard Breitling Orbiter 3 remains Piccard's most celebrated single achievement, establishing world records for the longest distance and duration of a balloon flight. However, he considers the Solar Impulse project his most meaningful work. Conceived in 2003 and developed over more than a decade, Solar Impulse 2 completed the first solar-powered circumnavigation of the Earth in 2016, piloted alternately by Piccard and André Borschberg across 17 legs over approximately 40,000 kilometers. The aircraft used no fuel, relying entirely on photovoltaic cells to power its motors and charge batteries for night flight. In 2017, Piccard delivered to the United Nations a list of 1,000 solutions — identified by the Solar Impulse Foundation — that are both profitable and environmentally beneficial.

## Controversies & Criticism

Piccard has faced limited controversy, though some critics have questioned whether high-profile adventure projects like Solar Impulse, with their substantial costs and corporate sponsorships, represent the most efficient path to driving clean energy adoption. Others have noted that the aircraft's slow speed and weather dependency make direct commercial application impractical. Piccard has consistently addressed these critiques by framing Solar Impulse as a demonstration of technological possibility rather than a ready-made transport solution.

## Legacy & Influence

Piccard occupies a rare position as both a record-breaking explorer and a credible voice in clean technology policy. His Solar Impulse Foundation's work identifying profitable clean solutions has been cited in discussions at the World Economic Forum and the United Nations. He continues to lecture and advocate globally, carrying forward a multigenerational family legacy of pushing the boundaries of human knowledge and endurance.
