## Summary

Theagenes of Thasos won over 1,300 victories in combat sports. He won Olympic boxing in 480 BC and pankration in 476 BC. His posthumous statue was believed to have healing powers.

## Athletic disciplines

Ancient sources associate Theagenes with multiple combat events:

- **Boxing (pyx):** A striking sport with minimal protective equipment, known for severe injuries.
- **Pankration:** A mixed combat sport combining elements of boxing and wrestling, with few rules.

Victories were recorded not only at Olympia but also at other Panhellenic festivals such as the Pythian, Isthmian, and Nemean Games, reflecting the broader competitive circuit of elite athletes.

## Reputation and hero cult

In Greek city-states, exceptional athletes could become civic symbols. Theagenes’ fame on Thasos was linked to stories of extraordinary strength and a public statue commemorating his achievements.

Later traditions describe a form of **hero cult** around him after death. Accounts of healing and protective powers attributed to his statue fit wider patterns in Greek religion, where heroic figures were believed to offer localized benefits through ritual attention.

## Historical significance

Theagenes is often cited as an example of how athletic success in antiquity could translate into political prestige, collective identity for a polis, and enduring myth-making.

## Notes on sources

Most details about Theagenes come from later literary traditions rather than contemporary records. As a result, precise tallies of victories and some biographical anecdotes are treated cautiously by historians, even while his status as a renowned ancient champion is widely accepted.
