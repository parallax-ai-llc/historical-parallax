## Summary

Timothy McVeigh bombed the Murrah Federal Building on April 19, 1995, killing 168 people.

## Additional Context

### Target and Immediate Impact

The attack destroyed much of the Alfred P. Murrah Federal Building and caused extensive damage in downtown Oklahoma City. It killed 168 people, including children, and injured hundreds, making it one of the deadliest acts of domestic terrorism in U.S. history.

### Investigation and Prosecution

The bombing led to a large-scale federal investigation involving forensic analysis, witness interviews, and the tracing of materials used in the explosive device. Timothy McVeigh was prosecuted and sentenced to death; Terry Nichols was also convicted for his role in the attack.

### Ideological Background (High-Level)

The event is frequently discussed in analyses of U.S. anti-government extremism in the 1990s, including the ways in which grievance narratives, militia subcultures, and conspiratorial worldviews can contribute to political violence.

### Policy and Security Aftermath

In the aftermath, U.S. authorities reviewed security at federal facilities and emergency response coordination. The bombing also influenced public discussion about domestic terrorism, investigative tools, and the balance between civil liberties and security measures.

### Memory, Memorialization, and Public History

Oklahoma City established a memorial and museum to commemorate those killed and to document the event and its consequences. Public memorialization is often cited as part of long-term recovery for communities affected by mass-casualty attacks.
