## Summary

Chilean diplomat and Allende minister who was assassinated in Washington DC by a car bomb planted by DINA, Pinochet's secret police. His American colleague Ronni Moffitt also died. It was the first state-sponsored terrorism in the US capital.

## Expanded Notes

### What happened (high-level)

- On **1976-09-21**, a **car bomb** exploded near **Sheridan Circle** in **Washington, D.C.**
- The blast killed **Orlando Letelier**, a former Chilean foreign/defense minister and diplomat, and **Ronni Karpen Moffitt**, a U.S. citizen working with him.
- **Michael Moffitt** (Ronni's husband) survived.

### Victims and roles

- **Orlando Letelier**: widely described as a **Chilean diplomat and politician**, and a prominent critic of **Augusto Pinochet's** military regime while in exile.
- **Ronni Moffitt**: a U.S. colleague associated with Letelier's policy/advocacy work.

> Note: The frontmatter lists occupation as "Assassin"; in most sources Letelier is characterized as a **diplomat/politician**, and the "assassin" label applies to the perpetrators rather than to Letelier.

### Perpetrators (overview)

- The attack is widely attributed to **DINA** (Dirección de Inteligencia Nacional), Chile's military intelligence service under Pinochet.
- The operation is commonly discussed as part of **transnational repression** against dissidents abroad.

### Why it mattered

- The assassination is often cited as a major instance of **state-linked political violence** carried out on U.S. soil.
- It intensified U.S. attention to human-rights abuses connected to the Pinochet dictatorship.

### Investigation and accountability (summary)

- U.S. investigations connected the bombing to a network involving **Chilean intelligence** and associated operatives.
- The case is frequently referenced in discussions of how democracies pursue accountability for **international political assassinations**.

### Related context

- Letelier's killing is commonly associated with the broader pattern sometimes labeled **Operation Condor**, a campaign of cooperation among certain South American military regimes to target opponents.
