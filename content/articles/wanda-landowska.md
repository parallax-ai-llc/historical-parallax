## Summary

Wanda Landowska (1879–1959) was a Polish-born harpsichordist and pianist widely credited with reviving the harpsichord as a serious concert instrument after nearly two centuries of neglect. Her passionate advocacy for early keyboard music transformed how audiences and musicians understood Baroque repertoire, making works by Bach, Handel, and Scarlatti central to the modern concert hall. She is considered one of the most influential figures in the early music movement of the twentieth century.

Landowska's impact extended beyond performance. She commissioned new harpsichords, wrote extensively on historical performance practice, and attracted commissions from major composers of her era. Her recordings, particularly of Bach's *Goldberg Variations* and *Well-Tempered Clavier*, set standards that shaped generations of performers. Through teaching and writing, she created an intellectual framework for approaching early music that persisted long after her death.

## Early Life & Background

Born in Warsaw on July 5, 1879, Landowska showed exceptional musical talent from childhood. She studied piano at the Warsaw Conservatory and later moved to Berlin, where she trained with Heinrich Urban. By her early twenties she had relocated to Paris, which became her adopted home and the base for her growing career. Her encounter with the music of Bach, combined with historical research into period instruments, ignited her lifelong mission to restore the harpsichord to prominence. In 1900 she married Henry Lew, a folklorist and writer who supported her scholarly interests.

## Rise to Prominence

Landowska gave her first public harpsichord recital in 1903 and began publishing her foundational study *Musique ancienne* in 1909, establishing herself as both a practitioner and theorist of early music. She collaborated with the Pleyel firm in Paris to develop a modern concert harpsichord suited to large halls, first unveiled around 1912. This instrument, though anachronistic by later standards, allowed her to perform in major venues across Europe. She taught at the Berlin Hochschule für Musik from 1913 until World War I disrupted her career. In 1925 she founded the École de Musique Ancienne at Saint-Leu-la-Forêt near Paris, attracting students from around the world.

## Key Achievements

In 1923, Manuel de Falla composed his *El retablo de maese Pedro* with a harpsichord part specifically for Landowska. Francis Poulenc dedicated his *Concert champêtre* (1928) to her, premiering it with her as soloist with the Paris Philharmonic Orchestra in 1929. Her 1933 recording of Bach's *Goldberg Variations* for HMV was a landmark, as was her 1945–1954 RCA Victor recording of the *Well-Tempered Clavier*, made after she had settled in the United States following her flight from Nazi-occupied Europe in 1941. The Nazis had seized her home, library, and instrument collection at Saint-Leu-la-Forêt in 1940.

## Controversies & Criticism

Landowska's approach was not without critics. She performed on the large, pedal-equipped Pleyel harpsichord rather than historically accurate instruments, and her interpretive style was sometimes described as overly Romantic and individualistic. Later scholars of historically informed performance, including Ralph Kirkpatrick and Gustav Leonhardt, challenged her methods as insufficiently rigorous. Her reported remark to a fellow performer — "You play Bach your way and I'll play him his way" — captures both her confidence and the friction her dominance created within the field.

## Legacy & Influence

Landowska died on August 16, 1959, in Lakeville, Connecticut. Despite later critiques, her role in establishing the harpsichord's place in modern concert life is uncontested. She inspired a generation of keyboardists and helped build the institutional infrastructure — schools, recordings, commissions — that made the early music revival possible. Her recordings remain in print and her scholarship laid groundwork that subsequent generations refined rather than discarded.
