## Summary

Albert Spaggiari tunneled into the Société Générale bank vault in Nice from the sewer system. Stole $10 million and left a note: 'Without weapons, without hatred, without violence.'

## Context

The 1976 Société Générale bank heist in Nice became notable less for violence than for planning and execution. It is often cited as an example of a "non-violent" bank burglary where the perpetrators relied on access, timing, and engineering rather than confrontation.

## Method (High-Level)

- **Access route:** The group reached the vault via an underground route connected to the city’s sewer system.
- **Entry approach:** Rather than forcing the front of the building, they targeted the bank’s protected area from below/behind.
- **Time window:** The operation took place over a period when the vault area could be worked on without immediate detection.

## Key Individuals

- **Albert Spaggiari:** Commonly identified as a principal organizer associated with the heist.

## Aftermath and Public Narrative

- The heist entered popular memory partly because of the message reportedly left behind emphasizing an absence of weapons and violence.
- The case also became part of French true-crime history and is frequently discussed in the context of security design: physical defenses can be bypassed when surrounding infrastructure (like underground access) is exploitable.

## Why It Matters (Security Lessons)

- **Perimeter thinking:** Security is not only doors and alarms; it includes adjacent infrastructure (utility tunnels, sewers, neighboring basements).
- **Detection layers:** A vault can be strong while detection/monitoring of unusual underground activity is weak.
- **Human factors:** Non-violent framing can shape how a crime is remembered, even when the financial impact is significant.
