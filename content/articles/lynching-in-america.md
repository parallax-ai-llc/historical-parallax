## Summary

Over 4,400 African Americans were lynched between 1877-1950 primarily in the American South. These were public spectacles of racial terrorism that enforced white supremacy.

## Definition and Features

In U.S. historical writing, “lynching” typically refers to an extrajudicial killing by a mob, often justified with accusations that never received a fair legal process. Many lynchings were public events intended to terrorize communities.

## Historical Context

- The peak era followed the end of Reconstruction, when white supremacist violence was used to enforce racial hierarchy and suppress Black political and economic advancement.
- Local authorities often failed to prosecute perpetrators, contributing to impunity and repetition.

## Resistance and Documentation

Anti-lynching activism included investigative journalism and organized civil-rights advocacy. Figures such as Ida B. Wells and organizations like the NAACP helped document cases, challenge misinformation, and push for federal action.

## Memorialization and Law

- In the 21st century, new research and memorial projects have sought to document victims by name and connect the practice to broader histories of racial violence.
- The 2022 federal anti-lynching law is frequently discussed as a symbolic milestone after decades of failed attempts in Congress.

## Lasting Effects

The legacy of lynching is often discussed in relation to migration patterns, political disenfranchisement, and intergenerational trauma. Scholars also connect it to later forms of racialized violence and unequal legal protection.

## Data and Counts

Lynching tallies depend on definitions and available records; historians and organizations have produced differing counts while agreeing on the broad pattern and scale.
