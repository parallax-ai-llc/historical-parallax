## Background

- Mohammed Atef (also known by the kunya **Abu Hafs al-Masri**) was an Egyptian militant who became a senior figure in **al-Qaeda**.
- Contemporary reporting and later summaries describe him as one of the organization’s key **military** and **security** officials during the late 1990s and early 2000s.
- He is generally described as having been close to **Osama bin Laden** and part of al-Qaeda’s senior leadership circle.

## Role in al-Qaeda (high level)

- Often identified as **al-Qaeda’s military chief**, he was associated with oversight of training, internal security, and operational planning at a strategic level.
- Public sources frequently connect him to the period when al-Qaeda expanded its infrastructure in Afghanistan, especially after relocating there in the mid-to-late 1990s.
- Because many internal details remain disputed or sourced to intelligence reporting, biographies typically emphasize his seniority rather than granular command structures.

## Notable context (late 1990s–2001)

- Atef’s prominence is discussed in the context of al-Qaeda’s rise as a transnational jihadist organization.
- He has been cited in public summaries as being linked to planning and direction of major al-Qaeda actions in this era; however, details vary across sources and are often described in broad terms.
- After the **September 11 attacks** in 2001, al-Qaeda leadership became a primary target of U.S. and allied counterterrorism operations in Afghanistan.

## Death

- Atef was reported killed on **15 November 2001** in Afghanistan during a U.S. airstrike, amid the early phase of the U.S.-led war in Afghanistan.
- Accounts commonly describe the strike as occurring near **Kabul**; precise circumstances are reported differently across summaries.

## Legacy and historical significance

- In biographical and counterterrorism literature, Atef is often cited as an example of how al-Qaeda combined veteran militants from multiple countries into a centralized leadership structure.
- His death is commonly presented as an early, high-profile leadership loss for al-Qaeda during the 2001 campaign.
- Discussions of his impact typically focus on leadership succession and the shifting geography of al-Qaeda’s senior figures after late 2001.
