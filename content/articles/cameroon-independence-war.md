## Summary

France conducted a brutal campaign to suppress Cameroon's independence movement, including napalm bombing villages and concentration camps. An estimated 100,000-400,000 Cameroonians were killed.

## Background

- **Colonial context:** The conflict took place in the late colonial and early post-independence period in Cameroon, in a region shaped by mandates and trusteeship arrangements after World War I.
- **Independence movement:** A key political force associated with the struggle was the **Union des populations du Cameroun (UPC)**, which advocated for independence and reunification.
- **International setting:** The war unfolded alongside broader decolonization in Africa, including armed conflicts and political crises in other French territories.

## Conflict Dynamics (high level)

- **Insurgency and counterinsurgency:** The fighting is often described as a mix of guerrilla warfare by anti-colonial militants and counterinsurgency operations by French forces and allied local actors.
- **Geographic focus:** Violence was particularly associated with parts of southern and western Cameroon, with rural areas bearing much of the impact.
- **Continuity after independence:** Although Cameroon became independent in 1960, aspects of the conflict and repression continued into the 1960s and early 1970s.

## Civilian Harm and Historical Debate

- **Casualty estimates:** Published estimates of deaths vary widely (often cited in the hundreds of thousands), reflecting limited documentation, contested historiography, and differing methodologies.
- **Methods and allegations:** Accounts of the conflict include allegations of mass detention, village destruction, and severe coercive tactics typical of mid-20th-century counterinsurgency campaigns.
- **Memory and recognition:** The war has been described as under-acknowledged internationally compared to other decolonization conflicts, with ongoing debates in Cameroon and France about responsibility, archives, and public commemoration.

## Significance

- The conflict is frequently referenced in studies of **French decolonization**, **state formation in post-colonial Africa**, and the long-term effects of political violence on institutions and civil society.
