## Summary

Jihadist gunmen attacked the village of Solhan in Burkina Faso killing at least 160 people in the deadliest single attack in the country's history. The attackers targeted villagers participating in a self-defense militia.

## Additional Context

### Sahel insurgency backdrop

The Solhan massacre occurred amid a wider security crisis in Burkina Faso and the broader Sahel region, where militant Islamist groups and local armed actors have carried out repeated attacks on civilians, security forces, and local officials. Violence has contributed to large-scale displacement and strained state capacity.

### The attack

Accounts describe an assault on the village of Solhan and nearby areas, with attackers killing civilians and burning property. Reports also noted that the area had local self-defense structures, and that civilians were increasingly caught between militant violence and the pressures of community militarization.

### Humanitarian and political impact

The scale of the killings drew national and international attention and intensified scrutiny of Burkina Faso’s security strategy. Such incidents have been linked to:

- Deepening humanitarian needs (food insecurity, trauma care)
- Increased internal displacement
- Pressure on government institutions and local governance

### Accountability challenges

In many Sahel conflicts, accountability is hindered by limited access to crime scenes, ongoing insecurity, and weak judicial capacity. Documentation by journalists, civil society, and international bodies is often critical for preserving evidence, but can be slow and incomplete.

### Broader significance

The massacre is frequently cited in analyses of the Sahel crisis as an example of how rapidly violence can escalate against rural communities, and how protection gaps can persist even as governments expand military operations.
