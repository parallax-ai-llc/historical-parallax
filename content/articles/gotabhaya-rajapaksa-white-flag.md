## Summary

Gotabaya Rajapaksa's forces allegedly executed surrendering LTTE leaders who came carrying white flags during the final battle in 2009. The incident remains one of the most disputed war crimes allegations.

## Background (High-level)

- The "white flag" allegation is typically discussed in the context of the final phase of the Sri Lankan Civil War, when fighting ended in 2009.
- Under international humanitarian law, persons who surrender or are otherwise hors de combat are protected, and summary executions (if proven) would be serious violations.
- Public debate around this allegation has involved competing accounts, questions of command responsibility, and disputes about what was ordered versus what occurred on the ground.

## Investigations and Dispute

- References to UN and OHCHR reporting indicate that international bodies reviewed available information and described allegations as credible or supported by evidence.
- At the same time, the incident is frequently characterized as disputed, reflecting contestation over witness accounts, documentation, and political narratives.
- For a careful write-up, it is important to distinguish: (1) what is alleged, (2) what is documented by investigations, and (3) what has been adjudicated in court.

## Why It Matters

- **Protection of surrender:** the case is often cited in discussions about how surrender should be handled in end-of-war scenarios.
- **Accountability mechanisms:** it highlights the role and limits of international investigations when domestic accountability is contested.
- **Historical record:** even absent a definitive court judgment, investigative reports shape how the final months of the war are documented.
