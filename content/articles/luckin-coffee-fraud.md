## Summary

Chinese coffee chain fabricated $310 million in sales to inflate stock price on NASDAQ. CEO and COO fired; company delisted. Largest Chinese stock fraud in US markets.

## Context

- Luckin Coffee grew rapidly in China with an app-driven model, heavy subsidies, and aggressive store expansion.
- The company listed on NASDAQ in 2019, attracting international capital and analyst coverage.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- Internal investigations found that certain executives and employees fabricated transactions to inflate revenue.
- The public disclosure stated that fabricated sales totaled roughly **RMB 2.2 billion** (about **US$310 million**) for portions of 2019.
- The scandal triggered trading halts, investigations, management removals, and eventual delisting.

## How the Fraud Worked (Commonly Reported Mechanics)

- **Inflated sales figures**: revenue was overstated through fake or manipulated transaction records.
- **Expense manipulation**: associated costs and marketing expenses were reportedly adjusted to align with the fabricated revenue narrative.
- **Control failures**: rapid scaling, complex vendor relationships, and weak oversight increased the opportunity for misstatement.

## Discovery and Disclosure

- Short-seller and investor scrutiny intensified after the IPO as growth numbers drew skepticism.
- The company announced an internal investigation in early 2020 and disclosed the scale of fabricated sales.

## Consequences

- **Leadership changes**: senior executives (including CEO and COO) were removed following the investigation.
- **Market impact**: shares collapsed after disclosures; trading was halted at points during the crisis.
- **Exchange outcome**: the company was delisted from NASDAQ in 2020.
- **Regulatory and legal exposure**: the case led to multiple investigations and civil litigation related to disclosure and accounting controls.

## Why It Matters

- Became one of the most prominent China-related accounting scandals affecting US-listed equities.
- Highlighted governance and audit challenges in high-growth, cross-border listings.
- Reinforced the importance of internal controls, independent oversight, and skepticism toward subsidy-driven growth metrics.
