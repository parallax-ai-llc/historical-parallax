## Summary

Parmenides of Elea (c. 515-450 BCE) was a pre-Socratic Greek philosopher who fundamentally transformed Western metaphysics and epistemology through his revolutionary doctrine that reality is unchanging, indivisible, and eternal. Born in the Greek colony of Elea in southern Italy, he founded the Eleatic school of philosophy and composed a philosophical poem that argued against the reliability of sensory experience and championed reason as the sole path to truth[^1]. His radical rejection of change and multiplicity, encapsulated in his assertion that "what is, is" and "what is not, is not," profoundly influenced Plato, Aristotle, and the entire subsequent Western philosophical tradition[^2]. Despite the counterintuitive nature of his conclusions, Parmenides' rigorous logical methodology established new standards for philosophical argumentation that continue to resonate in contemporary metaphysics and ontology[^3].

---

## Early Life and Background

Parmenides was born around 515 BCE in Elea (modern-day Velia), a Greek colony on the southwestern coast of Italy in the region known as Magna Graecia[^4]. The city of Elea had been founded around 540 BCE by Phocaean Greeks fleeing Persian conquest, creating a vibrant intellectual community that would later produce several significant philosophers[^5]. Little is definitively known about Parmenides' family background, though ancient sources suggest he came from a wealthy and distinguished family, which would have afforded him the leisure necessary for philosophical pursuits[^6].

According to the 3rd-century CE biographer Diogenes Laërtius, Parmenides was initially a student of the Pythagorean philosopher Ameinias, son of Diochaetas, though this connection remains debated among modern scholars[^7]. The Pythagorean influence may have introduced Parmenides to mathematical reasoning and the concept of unchanging eternal truths, ideas that would later become central to his own philosophy[^8]. Some ancient sources also mention that Parmenides associated with Xenophanes of Colophon, the wandering poet-philosopher who criticized anthropomorphic conceptions of the gods, though the exact nature of their relationship is uncertain[^9].

Parmenides' education would have included the traditional Greek curriculum of poetry, rhetoric, and athletics, but he distinguished himself through his exceptional aptitude for logical reasoning and abstract thought[^10]. The intellectual environment of Elea, situated at the crossroads of Greek colonization and indigenous Italian cultures, exposed him to diverse perspectives and encouraged critical examination of traditional beliefs[^11]. This cosmopolitan atmosphere, combined with the city's relative isolation from the political turmoil affecting mainland Greece, created ideal conditions for sustained philosophical inquiry[^12].

By his early adulthood, Parmenides had developed a reputation for wisdom and was invited to participate in the governance of Elea, demonstrating that his interests extended beyond pure philosophy to practical civic affairs[^13]. Ancient sources credit him with establishing laws for his city that were so admired that the citizens annually required their magistrates to swear to uphold Parmenides' legislation[^14]. This combination of theoretical brilliance and practical political engagement distinguished Parmenides from many other pre-Socratic philosophers and demonstrated the application of rational principles to civic life[^15].

## Rise to Philosophical Prominence

Parmenides achieved philosophical prominence through the composition and dissemination of his revolutionary hexameter poem, traditionally titled "On Nature" (Peri Physeōs), though this title may have been applied by later scholars rather than by Parmenides himself[^16]. Written in the epic verse form associated with Homer and Hesiod, the poem deliberately evoked the authority of traditional Greek poetry while subverting its content through radical philosophical argumentation[^17]. This strategic choice of medium allowed Parmenides to reach a broader audience while lending his counterintuitive conclusions the persuasive power of poetic beauty and memorable phrasing[^18].

The poem's proem describes a mystical journey in which the narrator is transported by horses and divine maidens through the gates of Night and Day to meet an unnamed goddess who promises to reveal both the truth about reality and the deceptive opinions of mortals[^19]. This allegorical framing, reminiscent of shamanistic traditions and mystery religions, gave Parmenides' philosophical arguments a quasi-religious authority that enhanced their impact on contemporaries[^20]. The goddess becomes the voice of reason itself, presenting arguments that demand acceptance based on logical necessity rather than empirical observation or traditional authority[^21].

Parmenides' reputation spread throughout the Greek world, and according to Plato's dialogue "Parmenides," he traveled to Athens around 450 BCE when he was approximately 65 years old, accompanied by his student Zeno of Elea[^22]. During this visit, the young Socrates (then about 20 years old) allegedly engaged in philosophical discussion with Parmenides, though modern scholars debate the historical accuracy of Plato's account[^23]. Whether or not this meeting actually occurred, the story illustrates Parmenides' towering reputation in the ancient philosophical world and his influence on subsequent Athenian philosophy[^24].

The philosophical impact of Parmenides' work was amplified by his student Zeno, who developed a series of paradoxes defending Parmenidean monism against critics who found its denial of plurality and motion absurd[^25]. Zeno's paradoxes, including the famous arguments about Achilles and the tortoise and the arrow in flight, demonstrated that pluralism and motion led to logical contradictions, thereby supporting Parmenides' position through reductio ad absurdum arguments[^26]. This teacher-student collaboration established the Eleatic school as a formidable force in pre-Socratic philosophy and ensured that subsequent thinkers had to grapple with Parmenidean arguments[^27].

Parmenides' prominence was further enhanced by the responses he provoked from other philosophers. Empedocles attempted to reconcile Parmenidean permanence with observable change through his theory of four eternal elements combining and separating[^28]. Anaxagoras proposed his concept of nous (mind) as an organizing principle that could account for apparent change while preserving something like Parmenidean being[^29]. The atomists Leucippus and Democritus developed their theory of atoms moving through void as a pluralistic response to Parmenidean monism that retained the concept of unchanging eternal entities[^30]. Each of these philosophical systems represented an attempt to answer the challenge Parmenides had posed, demonstrating his central position in pre-Socratic thought[^31].

## Major Philosophical Achievements

Parmenides' most significant achievement was his development of a purely rational approach to metaphysics that prioritized logical argumentation over empirical observation, effectively founding Western ontology as a distinct philosophical discipline[^32]. His central argument, known as the "Way of Truth," proceeds from the simple premise that "what is, is" and "what is not, is not," from which he derives the conclusion that reality must be ungenerated, indestructible, unchanging, indivisible, and complete[^33]. This deductive methodology, moving from basic logical principles to comprehensive metaphysical conclusions, established a model for philosophical reasoning that would influence thinkers for millennia[^34].

The poem's famous "two ways" structure presents a fundamental epistemological division between the path of truth, accessible only through reason, and the path of opinion, based on unreliable sensory experience[^35]. Parmenides argues that the path of not-being is "altogether unthinkable," because one cannot know or speak about what does not exist[^36]. This insight led to the principle of the excluded middle and the law of non-contradiction, fundamental logical principles that would be formalized by Aristotle and remain central to Western logic[^37]. By demonstrating that certain statements are necessarily false because they involve logical impossibility, Parmenides established rational necessity as a criterion for truth independent of empirical verification[^38].

Parmenides' arguments against generation and destruction revolutionized Greek cosmology by challenging the assumption that the world came into being through divine creation or natural processes[^39]. He reasoned that if something comes to be, it must come either from what is or from what is not; but it cannot come from what is (since that already exists) nor from what is not (since nothing can come from nothing)[^40]. This argument, which would later influence Christian theology through the doctrine of creation ex nihilo, forced Greek philosophers to reconsider their cosmogonies and led to more sophisticated theories of causation and change[^41].

The philosopher's rejection of plurality represents another major achievement in logical argumentation. Parmenides argues that if there were many things, they would have to be separated by something that is not (empty space or void), but since what is not cannot exist, there can be no genuine separation and therefore no multiplicity[^42]. This argument anticipated modern debates about the nature of space and the continuum, and it forced subsequent philosophers to develop theories of void and atomism that could account for apparent plurality while respecting Parmenidean logical constraints[^43].

Parmenides' treatment of time constitutes a particularly influential achievement, as he argues that true being exists in an eternal present, with no past or future[^44]. His reasoning proceeds from the observation that past and future both involve forms of non-being (what no longer is and what is not yet), and therefore cannot be attributed to what truly is[^45]. This "block universe" conception of time, in which all moments exist simultaneously and change is illusory, has found surprising resonance in modern physics, particularly in interpretations of Einstein's special relativity theory that treat time as a fourth dimension analogous to space[^46].

The philosophical poem's treatment of the relationship between thought and being represents a foundational contribution to epistemology and metaphysics. Parmenides famously declares that "thinking and being are the same," a cryptic statement that has been interpreted as meaning that what can be thought must exist, or that the structure of thought corresponds to the structure of reality[^47]. This principle established a rationalist tradition that would extend through Plato's theory of Forms, Descartes' cogito, and Hegel's idealism, all of which assume a fundamental connection between rational thought and the nature of reality[^48].

Parmenides' influence on Plato represents one of his most important legacies. Plato's theory of Forms, with its distinction between the unchanging realm of eternal truths and the changing world of appearances, directly inherits Parmenidean insights while attempting to provide a more nuanced account that can accommodate both being and becoming[^49]. Plato's dialogue "Parmenides" subjects the theory of Forms to searching criticism, demonstrating both the profound influence of Parmenidean thought and the difficulties involved in moving beyond it[^50]. Through Plato, Parmenidean themes of eternal, unchanging truth became central to Western philosophy, Christian theology, and the development of mathematics and logic[^51].

## Controversies and Criticisms

The most fundamental criticism of Parmenides' philosophy, voiced from antiquity to the present, is that his conclusions are manifestly absurd and contradicted by everyday experience[^52]. His denial of motion, change, plurality, and generation flies in the face of observable reality, leading critics to dismiss his arguments as sophistic word-games rather than genuine insights into the nature of things[^53]. Aristotle, while respecting Parmenides' logical rigor, argued that his philosophy suffered from an overly narrow conception of being and failed to distinguish between different senses in which things can be said to exist[^54]. This criticism suggests that Parmenides' arguments depend on equivocation and can be resolved by clarifying ambiguities in the concept of being[^55].

Ancient critics accused Parmenides of arrogance in trusting reason over the senses and dismissing the entire testimony of human experience as mere "opinion"[^56]. This rationalist hubris, they argued, led him to conclusions that no sensible person could accept and demonstrated the dangers of abstract philosophical reasoning divorced from empirical reality[^57]. The Sophist Gorgias parodied Parmenidean argumentation in his treatise "On Non-Being," using similar logical moves to prove the opposite conclusion that nothing exists, that if anything exists it cannot be known, and that if it can be known it cannot be communicated[^58]. This parody highlighted perceived absurdities in Parmenides' method and suggested that his style of argumentation could prove anything, thereby lacking genuine philosophical value[^59].

Modern philosophers have criticized Parmenides for confusing logical necessity with metaphysical necessity, arguing that his conclusions follow only from linguistic or conceptual constraints rather than from the actual nature of reality[^60]. Bertrand Russell suggested that Parmenides mistakenly inferred that because we cannot think contradictions, reality itself cannot contain contradictions, thus conflating the limits of thought with the limits of being[^61]. This criticism aligns with later developments in modal logic that distinguish between what is logically possible, conceptually possible, and metaphysically possible, categories that Parmenides allegedly conflated[^62].

Critics have also challenged Parmenides' epistemological framework, arguing that his radical devaluation of sensory experience is self-defeating because his own arguments must ultimately be based on some form of experience[^63]. The empiricist tradition, extending from Aristotle through Locke and Hume to contemporary naturalism, rejects the Parmenidean suggestion that pure reason can access truths about reality independent of empirical investigation[^64]. These critics argue that Parmenides' method, if consistently applied, would make all knowledge impossible, since even mathematical and logical truths must be discovered through some form of cognitive engagement with the world[^65].

Some scholars have criticized the cultural and historical impact of Parmenidean philosophy as encouraging an otherworldly focus that devalues the changing, material world in favor of abstract, eternal truths[^66]. This criticism, articulated by Nietzsche and later process philosophers like Whitehead and Bergson, suggests that the Parmenidean tradition contributed to a life-denying asceticism in Western culture by suggesting that true reality lies beyond the world of change and becoming[^67]. By identifying being with permanence and dismissing change as illusory, Parmenides allegedly inaugurated a metaphysical prejudice that has hindered Western philosophy's ability to think adequately about time, process, and creativity[^68].

Feminist philosophers have occasionally criticized the Parmenidean tradition for its association of true being with masculine principles of unity, permanence, and rationality, while relegating change, plurality, and materiality to the realm of feminine "opinion"[^69]. The poem's imagery of the goddess who reveals truth while condemning mortal opinions has been read as participating in gendered hierarchies that privilege abstract reason over embodied, practical knowledge[^70]. While such criticisms are sometimes anachronistic, they point to ways in which Parmenidean metaphysics may have reinforced cultural hierarchies between abstract/concrete, mind/body, and masculine/feminine[^71].

## Personal Life

Little is known with certainty about Parmenides' personal life, as ancient sources provide limited information about the private affairs of pre-Socratic philosophers[^72]. He never married according to available records, instead devoting himself to philosophical contemplation and civic service in Elea[^73]. This celibate lifestyle was not uncommon among ancient philosophers and may have been motivated by the Pythagorean influences in his early education, as Pythagorean communities often emphasized ascetic practices and withdrawal from conventional social roles[^74].

Ancient biographical sources, particularly Diogenes Laërtius, describe Parmenides as a man of exceptional dignity and composure, qualities reflected in the measured, authoritative tone of his philosophical poem[^75]. Unlike some pre-Socratic philosophers known for eccentric behavior or dramatic gestures, Parmenides appears to have maintained a reputation for sober respectability that enhanced his standing as both philosopher and lawgiver[^76]. His personality, as evidenced through his surviving work, suggests a temperament oriented toward abstract contemplation rather than rhetorical performance or public spectacle[^77].

Parmenides' relationship with his student Zeno of Elea was evidently close, with ancient sources describing Zeno as his "beloved" (eromenos), suggesting either a pedagogical relationship in the Greek philosophical tradition or possibly a romantic attachment of the type accepted in ancient Greek culture[^78]. Plato's dialogue "Parmenides" portrays the two philosophers traveling together to Athens and depicts Zeno as fiercely defensive of his teacher's doctrines, developing his famous paradoxes specifically to protect Parmenidean philosophy from ridicule[^79]. This intellectual partnership proved highly productive, as Zeno's paradoxes forced subsequent philosophers to engage more seriously with Parmenidean arguments about motion and plurality[^80].

His involvement in Eleatic politics demonstrates that Parmenides did not retreat entirely into philosophical contemplation but maintained active engagement with his community[^81]. The laws he established for Elea apparently emphasized rational principles and civic harmony, though their specific content has not survived[^82]. This combination of philosophical and political activity suggests a personality that sought to apply theoretical insights to practical governance, anticipating Plato's vision of the philosopher-king[^83].

Later traditions attributed various supernatural abilities to Parmenides, including the power of prophecy and healing, though these attributions likely reflect confusion with shamanistic figures or the mystical imagery of his poem's proem rather than historical fact[^84]. Such legends suggest that later generations viewed him as a quasi-religious figure, a perception encouraged by the poem's revelatory structure and its claims to access truths beyond ordinary human knowledge[^85].

## Legacy

Parmenides' legacy in Western philosophy is profound and multifaceted, with his influence extending from ancient Greek metaphysics through medieval theology to contemporary analytic philosophy and physics[^86]. His establishment of ontology as a distinct philosophical discipline, concerned with the fundamental question "what is being as such?" created a framework that would structure metaphysical inquiry for over two millennia[^87]. Every subsequent Western philosopher has had to position themselves in relation to Parmenidean questions about the nature of existence, change, and reality, making him one of the foundational figures in the philosophical tradition[^88].

The impact on Plato was particularly significant, as Platonic metaphysics represents a sustained engagement with Parmenidean problems about the relationship between being and becoming, unity and multiplicity, permanence and change[^89]. Plato's theory of Forms attempts to preserve Parmenidean insights about eternal, unchanging truth while accommodating the reality of change in the sensible world through a two-world ontology[^90]. The Platonic tradition, extending through Neoplatonism and into Christian theology, transmitted Parmenidean themes of transcendent, immaterial reality that became central to Western religious and philosophical thought[^91].

Aristotle's philosophy, despite criticizing Parmenidean monism, was profoundly shaped by engagement with Parmenidean arguments. Aristotle's sophisticated analysis of being, his distinction between actuality and potentiality, and his theory of substance were all developed partly in response to Parmenidean challenges about change and plurality[^92]. The Aristotelian principle that "being is said in many ways" represents an attempt to overcome Parmenidean monism by recognizing multiple legitimate senses of existence, while still honoring the Parmenidean insight that being has an intelligible structure accessible to reason[^93].

In medieval philosophy, Parmenidean themes were absorbed into both Christian and Islamic theology through their transmission via Platonism and Neoplatonism[^94]. The attributes of divine being—eternal, unchanging, indivisible, perfect—closely parallel Parmenidean characteristics of true being, and medieval theologians drew on Parmenidean argumentation to support doctrines of divine simplicity and immutability[^95]. Thomas Aquinas' distinction between essence and existence and his arguments for God's necessary existence show the continued influence of Parmenidean concerns about the relationship between being and necessity[^96].

The scientific revolution and early modern philosophy saw renewed engagement with Parmenidean themes, particularly in Descartes' rationalism and Spinoza's monism[^97]. Descartes' method of systematic doubt and his search for indubitable truths accessible to pure reason reflect a Parmenidean epistemological orientation, while Spinoza's identification of God with a single infinite substance echoes Parmenidean monism[^98]. Even empiricist critics like Hume engaged with Parmenidean problems, as Hume's analysis of causation and personal identity represented attempts to explain change and identity without invoking Parmenidean substance[^99].

In contemporary philosophy, Parmenides' influence appears in debates about the nature of time, with philosophers divided between "A-theory" advocates who believe in real temporal becoming and "B-theory" defenders of a tenseless, block universe conception of time resembling Parmenidean eternal being[^100]. Physics has unexpectedly vindicated some Parmenidean insights, as Einstein's special relativity implies that simultaneity is relative and all spacetime events exist in a four-dimensional manifold, suggesting that temporal flow may be a subjective illusion rather than an objective feature of reality[^101]. Contemporary physicists and philosophers of physics continue to debate whether time is fundamental or emergent, a question with deep Parmenidean roots[^102].

The legacy extends to logic and analytic philosophy, where Parmenides is recognized as one of the earliest practitioners of rigorous deductive argumentation[^103]. His principle that contradictions cannot be true and his use of reductio ad absurdum arguments anticipated formal logic and set standards for philosophical rigor[^104]. W.V.O. Quine's famous essay "On What There Is" begins by invoking Parmenides' beard, acknowledging the ancient philosopher's foundational role in ontological debates about what exists[^105]. Contemporary metaphysicians working on problems of persistence, composition, and the nature of properties continue to engage with Parmenidean puzzles about identity, change, and the one and the many[^106].

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholarly consensus recognizes Parmenides as one of the most important and influential pre-Socratic philosophers, with many historians of philosophy ranking him second only to Socrates in terms of impact on the Western philosophical tradition[^107]. Classicist Gregory Vlastos argued that Parmenides "discovered logic" by being the first philosopher to demonstrate that certain positions are impossible because they lead to logical contradictions, thereby establishing rational necessity as a criterion for truth[^108]. This achievement, according to Vlastos, transformed philosophy from a tradition of cosmological speculation into a discipline centered on rigorous argumentation and logical analysis[^109].

Contemporary philosopher of physics Carlo Rovelli has praised Parmenides for recognizing that our intuitive understanding of reality can be profoundly mistaken, an insight that anticipated modern physics' discovery that space, time, and causation do not work as common sense suggests[^110]. Rovelli argues that Parmenides' willingness to follow rational arguments to counterintuitive conclusions exemplifies the scientific spirit at its best and that his block universe conception of time has been partially vindicated by relativity theory[^111]. This perspective treats Parmenides not as a mistaken metaphysician but as a visionary who glimpsed truths about reality that would only be confirmed millennia later[^112].

Philosopher Karl Popper defended Parmenides against charges of absurdity, arguing that his philosophy represented a genuine attempt to solve the problem of change through logical analysis[^113]. Popper suggested that Parmenides should be read charitably as recognizing that change requires some unchanging substrate (a view later developed by Aristotle's theory of substance and matter) and that his apparent denial of change was really a sophisticated argument about the logical requirements for identity through change[^114]. This interpretation portrays Parmenides as raising profound questions about persistence and identity that remain central to contemporary metaphysics[^115].

Martin Heidegger regarded Parmenides as one of the most important thinkers in Western history, arguing that his poem contained insights about the nature of being that were subsequently forgotten in the Western metaphysical tradition[^116]. Heidegger's interpretation focuses on Parmenides' identification of being and thinking, which he reads as recognizing that being is disclosed through human understanding rather than existing as an independent, object-like entity[^117]. While Heidegger's reading is controversial, it has inspired generations of continental philosophers to return to Parmenides as a source of alternatives to mainstream Western metaphysics[^118].

Many scholars praise Parmenides for his literary achievement in composing philosophical arguments in hexameter verse, a feat that required synthesizing logical precision with poetic beauty[^119]. Classicist Alexander Mourelatos argues that the poem's form is integral to its philosophical content, as the epic meter and mystical imagery serve to elevate abstract argumentation to the level of revealed truth and make difficult concepts memorable and persuasive[^120]. This aesthetic dimension of Parmenidean philosophy demonstrates that rigorous reasoning need not be divorced from literary artistry, an insight relevant to contemporary debates about the relationship between philosophy and literature[^121].

### Negative Views

Aristotle, despite his respect for Parmenides, criticized him for an overly narrow conception of being that failed to account for the evident reality of change and plurality[^122]. In the "Physics," Aristotle argues that Parmenides was "compelled to follow the phenomena" and that when forced to acknowledge observable facts, he had to abandon his monism, suggesting that the Parmenidean position is ultimately incoherent and unsustainable[^123]. This Aristotelian critique established a tradition of viewing Parmenidean philosophy as an instructive failure that demonstrates the limitations of pure rationalism divorced from empirical observation[^124].

Modern pragmatist philosophers, particularly William James and John Dewey, have criticized the Parmenidean tradition for promoting a static, intellectualist conception of reality that is hostile to genuine novelty, creativity, and temporal becoming[^125]. James argued that Parmenidean monism represents a "block universe" that eliminates genuine possibility and reduces human experience to illusion, a metaphysical position incompatible with lived experience and moral responsibility[^126]. This pragmatist critique suggests that Parmenidean philosophy leads to a world-view that is not only empirically false but existentially stifling and ethically problematic[^127].

Process philosophers influenced by Whitehead and Bergson have argued that the Parmenidean identification of being with permanence reflects a profound misunderstanding of reality, which is fundamentally characterized by process, creativity, and temporal flow rather than static substance[^128]. Henri Bergson's critique of the "spatialization" of time in Western philosophy traces this error back to Parmenides, whose conception of being as complete and unchanging allegedly prevented Western thought from adequately conceptualizing duration and lived temporality[^129]. From this perspective, Parmenidean philosophy represents a wrong turn in Western metaphysics that has impoverished our understanding of time, change, and life[^130].

Some analytic philosophers have argued that Parmenidean arguments rest on elementary logical mistakes, particularly the fallacy of equivocation between different senses of "being" (existential vs. predicative vs. identity)[^131]. Gilbert Ryle's influential analysis suggests that Parmenides confused the "is" of existence with the "is" of predication, leading him to conclude erroneously that whatever can be spoken of must exist[^132]. This line of criticism suggests that Parmenidean puzzles can be dissolved through careful attention to language and that his philosophy represents an early example of being "bewitched by language" rather than genuine metaphysical insight[^133].

Historical materialist critics have occasionally argued that Parmenidean philosophy reflects the ideology of an aristocratic leisure class divorced from productive labor and the material realities of social existence[^134]. By denying the reality of change and becoming, Parmenides allegedly promoted a conservative metaphysics that justified social stasis and undermined political transformation[^135]. While such socioeconomic critiques are less common in contemporary scholarship, they point to possible connections between metaphysical positions and social interests, suggesting that the appeal of Parmenidean permanence may have social and political dimensions beyond pure philosophical argument[^136].

## Famous Quotes

> "For what is, is, and what is not, is not." - Fragment 2, establishing the fundamental principle of his ontology[^137]

> "For thinking and being are the same thing." - Fragment 3, asserting the identity of thought and reality[^138]

> "There is no, and never shall be, any time other than the present, since Fate has chained it so as to be whole and immovable." - Fragment 8, denying the reality of past and future[^139]

> "It is necessary to speak and to think what is; for being is, but nothing is not." - Fragment 6, articulating the impossibility of non-being[^140]

> "How could what is perish? How could it come into being? For if it came into being, it is not; nor is it if ever it is going to be. Thus coming into being is extinguished, and destruction unknown." - Fragment 8, rejecting generation and destruction[^141]

> "Nor is it divided, since it is all alike; nor is there any more or less of it in one place which might prevent it from holding together, but all is full of what is." - Fragment 8, arguing for the indivisibility and homogeneity of being[^142]

> "Mortals have made up their minds to name two forms, one of which they should not name, and that is where they have gone astray." - Fragment 8, criticizing the conventional division of reality into opposites[^143]
