## Summary

Skrillex, born Sonny John Moore on January 15, 1988, in Los Angeles, California, is an American DJ, record producer, and singer who became one of the defining figures of electronic dance music in the 2010s. Rising from the post-hardcore scene as the frontman of From First to Last, he pivoted to electronic music around 2008 and quickly became synonymous with the brostep subgenre of dubstep — a heavier, more abrasive American interpretation of the British sound.

His aggressive sound design, characterized by distorted basslines and stuttering synth "wobbles," broke through into mainstream pop and hip-hop, making electronic production techniques ubiquitous in commercial music. He is among the most decorated producers in Grammy history for an electronic artist, having won eight Grammy Awards across multiple years.

Beyond his solo work, Skrillex co-founded the influential label and collective OWSLA and has collaborated with artists spanning every major genre, cementing his role not just as a dance music act but as a cross-genre production force.

## Early Life & Background

Sonny Moore was born in Los Angeles and raised partly in Northeast Los Angeles and later in the San Fernando Valley. He attended the Kinhaven Music School in Vermont during summers, developing foundational musical skills. At 15, he joined post-hardcore band From First to Last as their lead vocalist, touring extensively and recording albums including *Dear Diary, My Teen Angst Has a Body Count* (2004) and *Heroine* (2006) on Epitaph Records. A serious vocal cord condition forced him to step back from the band around 2007, redirecting his creative output toward solo electronic production.

## Rise to Prominence

After departing From First to Last, Moore began producing electronic music under the Skrillex alias and self-released his debut EP *Gypsyhook* in 2010. His follow-up EPs — *My Name Is Skrillex* and *Scary Monsters and Nice Sprites* (2010) — spread virally online, with the title track of the latter becoming a landmark in dubstep's commercial crossover. His 2011 EP *Bangarang* further solidified his mainstream presence. Skrillex headlined major festivals including Ultra Music Festival and Electric Daisy Carnival and toured internationally, helping push EDM into arena-scale entertainment in North America.

## Key Achievements

Skrillex won six Grammy Awards in 2012 and 2013 alone — the most ever won by an electronic artist in a single Grammy cycle — including Best Dance Recording and Best Dance/Electronica Album multiple times. He co-founded OWSLA Records in 2011, which became a respected tastemaker label releasing music by artists including Rustie, What So Not, and Boys Noize. His collaboration with Diplo as Jack Ü produced "Where Are Ü Now" with Justin Bieber in 2015, winning a Grammy for Best Dance Recording and reaching the top 10 in multiple countries. His 2023 album *Quest for Fire* debuted at number four on the Billboard 200.

## Controversies & Criticism

Skrillex's brostep sound attracted significant criticism from purist dubstep and electronic music communities, who felt his approach commercialized and diluted a more nuanced British genre. Critics also noted the formulaic nature of the drop-centered song structure that his success helped popularize. Some contemporaneous music critics described his work as sonically fatiguing or one-dimensional, though his production sophistication was more broadly acknowledged in later years.

## Legacy & Influence

Skrillex played an instrumental role in mainstreaming electronic dance music in North America during the early 2010s EDM boom. His production techniques influenced a generation of producers across genres, and his willingness to collaborate across hip-hop, pop, and reggaeton broadened the reach of electronic production globally. OWSLA's curatorial influence shaped mid-2010s bass music aesthetics. His later work demonstrated continued evolution, reinforcing his longevity in a genre where careers frequently plateau.
