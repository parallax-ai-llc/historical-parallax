## Summary

Tunisian asylum seeker Anis Amri drove a hijacked truck into a Berlin Christmas market killing 12 people. He fled across Europe before being shot by Italian police.

## Incident overview

The attack took place on 19 December 2016 at the Christmas market at Breitscheidplatz, near the Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church in Berlin.

A heavy truck was driven into the crowd, killing 12 people and injuring dozens more.

The perpetrator, Anis Amri, was widely described as a Tunisian national who had sought asylum in Germany.

## Flight and death of the attacker

After the attack, Amri escaped Germany and traveled across multiple European countries.

He was ultimately shot and killed by police in Italy (near Milan) days after the Berlin attack.

## Immediate aftermath

German authorities launched a major manhunt and conducted cross-border coordination with other European law-enforcement agencies.

The event intensified public debate in Germany about the balance between civil liberties and counterterrorism surveillance.

## Security and policy debates

Reporting and official reviews of the case have commonly focused on issues such as:

- Missed warning signs and information-sharing problems between agencies.
- Challenges of monitoring radicalized individuals who move across jurisdictions.
- The vulnerability of public events to vehicle-ramming attacks and the use of physical barriers.

## Broader significance

The Berlin Christmas market attack became one of Europe’s most prominent jihadist-inspired attacks of the mid-2010s.

It is often referenced in discussions of European counterterrorism policy, asylum and deportation procedures, and the evolution of “low-tech” mass-casualty tactics.
