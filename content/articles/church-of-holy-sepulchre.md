## Summary

The Church of the Holy Sepulchre, located in the Christian Quarter of the Old City of Jerusalem, is the most sacred site in Christianity. The church is built over two locations that are central to Christian faith: Golgotha (Calvary), the hill where Jesus of Nazareth was crucified, and the tomb where he was buried and, according to Christian belief, rose from the dead three days later. For approximately two billion Christians worldwide, this site is literally where the most important events in human history occurred.

The church has stood, in various forms, since the 4th century CE. It has been destroyed, restored, rebuilt, modified, and expanded by successive rulers and religious communities over more than sixteen centuries. Today it is jointly administered by six different Christian denominations — the Greek Orthodox, Roman Catholic (Franciscan), Armenian Apostolic, Coptic, Ethiopian Orthodox, and Syriac Orthodox — under an elaborate arrangement known as the Status Quo that has governed the division of rights and responsibilities since the Ottoman era.

Every year, hundreds of thousands of Christian pilgrims from around the world visit the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, making it one of the most visited religious sites on Earth. For many believers, a visit to the church represents the culmination of a lifetime of faith.

## History & Origins

The site's history reaches back to the crucifixion of Jesus, dated by most scholars to around 30–33 CE. At the time, Golgotha — the "Place of the Skull" — was located just outside the city walls of Jerusalem. The Romans crucified condemned criminals there as a public spectacle. A tomb owned by Joseph of Arimathea, a wealthy Jewish member of the Sanhedrin who had become a follower of Jesus, was located nearby. According to all four Gospels, Jesus was buried in this tomb after the crucifixion, and the tomb was found empty on the third day.

Following the destruction of Jerusalem by the Romans in 70 CE and the subsequent rebuilding of the city as Aelia Capitolina after the Bar Kokhba revolt (132–135 CE), the Roman Emperor Hadrian constructed pagan temples on several sites sacred to the early Christians, including Golgotha. Tradition holds that Hadrian built a temple to Venus over the site, which may have paradoxically helped preserve its location for future generations by marking it prominently.

The transformation of the site into a Christian holy place began under the Emperor Constantine I, who converted to Christianity in 312 CE and made it the religion of the Roman Empire. Around 325–326 CE, Constantine's mother, the Empress Helena, made a pilgrimage to Jerusalem and identified the holy sites, including Golgotha and the tomb. Constantine ordered a great church built on the site. The pagan temple was demolished, and construction began.

The Church of the Holy Sepulchre was consecrated on September 13, 335 CE. Constantine's church was an enormous complex that included a large basilica (the Martyrium), an open courtyard at Golgotha, and a rotunda over the tomb (the Anastasis — "Resurrection"). The construction was elaborate and expensive, reflecting the emperor's desire to create a monument worthy of the holiest site in Christendom.

The church was destroyed by the Sassanid Persian king Khosrow II in 614 CE. The True Cross — the cross on which Jesus was believed to have been crucified, supposedly discovered by Helena — was carried off to Persia. Byzantine Emperor Heraclius defeated the Persians and recovered the Cross in 628 CE, and the church was restored.

In 638 CE, the Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab captured Jerusalem. According to a widely repeated account, when Umar was offered the opportunity to pray inside the church, he refused, explaining that if he did so, Muslims might later claim it as a mosque. He prayed outside, and his agreement with the Patriarch Sophronius guaranteed Christian control of the church.

In 1009, the Fatimid Caliph al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah ordered the church destroyed. The tomb itself was severely damaged — workers reportedly attempted to cut down to bedrock to obliterate it. The destruction was later partially reversed under al-Hakim's successors, and Byzantine Emperor Constantine IX Monomachos funded a rebuilding completed by 1048. The destruction by al-Hakim is cited as one of the factors that contributed to the Crusades.

The Crusaders captured Jerusalem in 1099 and undertook major building work at the church, largely constructing the Romanesque structure that forms the basis of the church today. The various sacred sites — Golgotha, the tomb, Helena's Chapel — were incorporated under a single large roof. The Crusaders lost Jerusalem to Saladin in 1187, but he permitted Christian pilgrims to continue visiting the church.

Subsequent centuries brought earthquakes, fires, and various restorations. A major fire in 1808 destroyed significant portions of the church, and a 19th-century restoration by the Greek Orthodox is responsible for much of the present appearance of the Rotunda.

The current ongoing restoration project — a comprehensive renewal of the Edicule (the shrine encasing the tomb) — was completed in 2017 after years of international fundraising and collaboration between the custodian communities.

## Religious Significance

The Church of the Holy Sepulchre is, for Christians, literally the site of the most important events in history. The crucifixion, death, burial, and resurrection of Jesus Christ are not merely historical events in Christian theology but the pivotal moments of cosmic redemption — the means by which God reconciled humanity to himself. For this reason, the site holds a significance in Christianity that may be unmatched by any single site in any other world religion.

The church contains two focal points of devotion. Golgotha (Calvary), now enclosed within the church building at a slightly raised level and marked by an altar under Greek Orthodox care, is where the cross stood. Pilgrims descend on their knees and reach through an opening beneath the altar to touch the rock of Golgotha. The Edicule, a small shrine in the center of the Rotunda, encases the tomb — a low limestone bench where the body of Jesus was laid. Pilgrims enter the small interior and pray at the bench.

The Via Dolorosa — the processional route through Jerusalem's Old City said to trace Jesus's path to Golgotha — ends at the church, making it the destination of the Stations of the Cross procession observed weekly by Franciscan friars and joined by thousands of pilgrims each Friday.

The ceremony of the Holy Fire, observed annually on Holy Saturday (the day before Eastern Easter), is one of the oldest continuously celebrated Christian rituals. According to tradition, a miraculous fire appears spontaneously in the tomb and is used to light candles carried throughout the church and then across the world. The ceremony draws tens of thousands of Orthodox Christians and is broadcast across Eastern Europe and the Middle East.

## Architecture & Description

The Church of the Holy Sepulchre is a complex, rambling structure that reflects seventeen centuries of building, destruction, and rebuilding by many different hands and in many different architectural styles. It is large but not grandiose by modern standards — the cumulative effect of centuries of modification gives it an organic, almost labyrinthine character.

The primary entrance is through a single doorway in the southern facade — the result of the Crusader-era construction. The facade is Romanesque with twin portals, though one has been permanently blocked. Two towers once flanked the entrance; only the base of one remains.

Immediately inside is the Stone of Unction (also called the Stone of Anointing), a flat rectangular slab of reddish limestone marking the traditional spot where the body of Jesus was prepared for burial. Pilgrims kneel to touch and kiss the stone.

To the right is the stairway ascending to Golgotha. The upper level is divided between two chapels — the Greek Orthodox Chapel at the site of the crucifixion and the Latin Chapel of the Nailing of the Cross to the left. Between them, visible through glass, is the traditional site of the crucifixion — a crack in the bedrock visible beneath the altar.

The central space of the church is the Rotunda, a circular space covered by a large dome. At its center stands the Edicule — the tomb shrine — a structure of Jerusalem stone in a mixture of architectural styles, the result of successive rebuilds. The current Edicule structure dates largely from 1810, with the comprehensive 2017 restoration revealing and stabilizing the ancient limestone tomb itself.

## Pilgrimage & Rituals

Christian pilgrimage to Jerusalem, and specifically to the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, has been practiced since the earliest centuries of Christianity. The church was the destination of Constantine's mother Helena and has drawn emperors, kings, saints, and ordinary believers ever since. In the medieval period, pilgrimage to Jerusalem was considered the most meritorious pilgrimage in Christendom, on a par with or exceeding pilgrimage to Rome and Santiago de Compostela.

Today the church is open daily and receives visitors from every Christian tradition. Lines to enter the Edicule can extend for several hours, particularly during the major holy seasons of Christmas, Easter, and Pentecost. Each of the six custodian communities conducts its own liturgical services according to its own calendar and rite, filling the church with incense, chant, and candlelight at various hours of the day and night.

The coordination between the six communities is governed by the 1852 Status Quo, originally established by Ottoman decree. This arrangement specifies which community controls which space, who may perform which rituals when, and who is responsible for which maintenance. Disputes between communities over rights and responsibilities have occasionally turned physical — a 2008 brawl between Greek Orthodox and Armenian monks required Israeli police intervention.

## Modern Significance

The Church of the Holy Sepulchre remains one of the most visited tourist and pilgrimage destinations in the world. Its location in Jerusalem — a city at the center of one of the world's most intractable political conflicts — means that access to the site has been periodically affected by political tensions.

Israeli control of East Jerusalem since 1967 has created complex dynamics. The Israeli government officially guarantees freedom of access to all holy sites and is responsible for security outside the church. Internal matters are left to the custodian communities.

The church's preservation has long been complicated by the Status Quo arrangement, which makes significant maintenance and restoration work difficult because any action must be agreed upon by multiple contentious parties. The renovation of the Edicule completed in 2017 was a landmark achievement, requiring years of negotiation and the cooperation of all six communities alongside international scientific and archaeological input. During the restoration, researchers were able to examine the original limestone burial bench for the first time in perhaps five centuries.

## Timeline

- **c. 30–33 CE**: Crucifixion, death, and burial of Jesus of Nazareth at the site.
- **135 CE**: Emperor Hadrian builds pagan temple over the site.
- **325–326 CE**: Empress Helena visits Jerusalem, identifies the holy sites.
- **335 CE**: Constantine's Church of the Holy Sepulchre is consecrated.
- **614 CE**: Sassanid Persians destroy the church; the True Cross is captured.
- **628 CE**: Byzantine Emperor Heraclius recovers the True Cross; the church is restored.
- **638 CE**: Jerusalem falls to the Muslim Caliph Umar; the church is guaranteed to Christians.
- **1009 CE**: Fatimid Caliph al-Hakim orders destruction of the church and the tomb.
- **1048 CE**: Byzantine-funded reconstruction of the church is completed.
- **1099 CE**: Crusaders capture Jerusalem and begin major rebuilding of the church.
- **1149 CE**: Consecration of the Crusader-era church in its present general form.
- **1187 CE**: Saladin captures Jerusalem; the church remains in Christian hands.
- **1808 CE**: A fire destroys significant portions of the church.
- **1852 CE**: Ottoman decree formalizes the Status Quo governing the church.
- **2017 CE**: Comprehensive restoration of the Edicule (tomb shrine) is completed.
