## Summary

International military tribunal that tried 24 major Nazi leaders for war crimes, crimes against humanity, and crimes against peace in 1945-1946. Twelve defendants were sentenced to death.

## Origins and Legal Framework

The Nuremberg Trials were organized by the Allied powers after World War II to establish accountability for senior leaders of Nazi Germany. The best-known proceedings were held before the International Military Tribunal (IMT), created by the London Charter of 1945, which defined the tribunal’s jurisdiction and the categories of crimes.

A key historical significance of the IMT was the effort to articulate individual criminal responsibility under international law, including for state officials. The tribunal’s work helped shape later international criminal institutions and the language used in subsequent human-rights and humanitarian-law frameworks.

## Charges and Defendants

The IMT prosecuted major political, military, and economic leaders. The charges were grouped into broad categories commonly summarized as:

- Crimes against peace (planning and waging aggressive war)
- War crimes (violations of the laws or customs of war)
- Crimes against humanity (widespread or systematic persecution and violence)

The proceedings also addressed the role of organizations and institutions within the Nazi system, contributing to an early model for linking bureaucratic structures to criminal conduct.

## Verdicts and Sentences

The tribunal produced a range of outcomes, including acquittals, prison sentences, and death sentences. Public accounts often highlight that twelve defendants were sentenced to death in the main IMT case, with executions carried out in 1946.

## Evidence and Historical Record

Beyond the verdicts, the trials assembled a substantial documentary and testimonial record. This record has been used extensively by historians and educators and remains central to scholarship on the Holocaust and Nazi governance.

## Criticism and Debate

From the outset, the trials drew debate about issues such as:

- “Victor’s justice” concerns (winners judging the defeated)
- Retroactivity arguments about whether some crimes were sufficiently defined beforehand
- The political context and the limits of postwar accountability

These debates continue to be referenced in discussions of modern international criminal tribunals.

## Legacy

The Nuremberg Trials are widely treated as a foundational moment in modern international criminal law. They influenced later tribunals and contributed to norms around accountability for mass atrocities, including the principle that following orders does not automatically absolve individuals of responsibility.
