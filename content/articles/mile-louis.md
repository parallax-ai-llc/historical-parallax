## Summary

French serial killer who murdered seven young disabled women he was supposed to be transporting.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2004 | Convicted of kidnapping and murdering disabled women |

## Background

- Émile Louis was a French criminal whose later convictions centered on the disappearance and murder of vulnerable young women.
- He was associated with transport work involving people with disabilities, which provided access to victims and opportunities to offend.

## Crimes

- The murders attributed to him involved **seven young women with disabilities**.
- Victims were reportedly under his care or in situations where he was responsible for transporting them.
- The selection of targets reflected a pattern of exploiting vulnerability and limited ability to seek help or be immediately located.

## Investigation and identification

- The case is widely described as involving long-standing suspicions and delayed resolution.
- Investigators ultimately linked Louis to multiple disappearances, leading to formal charges.
- Public attention focused on how the victims’ circumstances and institutional settings complicated early detection.

## Trial and sentencing

- In **2004**, Louis was convicted of kidnapping and murdering the victims.
- The convictions established him as a serial killer under French criminal law and media classification.
- He received a lengthy prison sentence (reported as life imprisonment in many summaries).

## Aftermath

- The case is often cited in discussions of safeguarding policies for disabled people and the risks of inadequate oversight in transport and care contexts.
- It remains a reference point in French true-crime reporting due to the number of victims and the vulnerability of those targeted.
