## Summary

Mass execution of 22,000 Polish military officers, police, and intellectuals by Soviet secret police in 1940. The USSR blamed Nazi Germany for decades before admitting responsibility.

## Additional Context

### Background

- After the Soviet invasion of eastern Poland in September 1939, large numbers of Polish officers and state officials were taken prisoner by the NKVD.
- In early 1940, the Soviet leadership approved the execution of selected prisoners held in camps such as Kozelsk, Starobelsk, and Ostashkov.

### Victims and Sites

- The victims included military officers, police, gendarmerie, and members of Poland’s educated and administrative elite.
- The best-known burial site is the Katyn Forest near Smolensk, but related executions and mass graves were also associated with other locations.

### Discovery and Attribution Dispute

- In 1943, German forces announced the discovery of mass graves and used it for wartime propaganda.
- For decades after World War II, Soviet authorities maintained that Nazi Germany was responsible, creating a long-running historical and diplomatic dispute.

### Admission, Documents, and Memory

- Late Soviet-era disclosures culminated in official admissions of Soviet responsibility, followed by the release of key archival materials.
- The massacre remains a central topic in Polish historical memory, commemorated through ceremonies, monuments, and ongoing research.

### Historical Significance

- Katyn became a symbol of state violence, suppressed truth, and the politics of historical accountability.
- It has continued to shape Polish–Russian relations and public debates about transparency, archives, and responsibility for wartime crimes.
