## Summary

Ottoman Grand Vizier who was the principal architect of the Armenian Genocide that killed 1.5 million Armenians. He ordered the deportation and systematic extermination of the Armenian population during World War I.

## Position in the Young Turk Era

Talat Pasha was a leading member of the Committee of Union and Progress (CUP), the ruling political movement that consolidated power in the Ottoman Empire in the years preceding and during World War I. The CUP’s leadership is sometimes described as a de facto triumvirate that coordinated policy across the state’s military and civil institutions.

## Administration and Deportation Machinery

As interior minister and later grand vizier, Talat oversaw key parts of the administrative system that implemented population transfers, policing, and internal security. Scholars of the Armenian Genocide emphasize that mass deportations were not only a military measure but also relied on bureaucratic directives, local enforcement, and the seizure or reassignment of property.

## Aftermath of World War I

Following the Ottoman defeat, Talat left the empire and lived in exile. Postwar debates and legal efforts to address wartime actions became intertwined with the empire’s collapse and the emergence of successor states, making accountability both politically charged and logistically difficult.

## Assassination and Public Memory

His assassination in Berlin in 1921 and the subsequent court proceedings involving Tehlirian became a major episode in the international public’s awareness of the Armenian Genocide. The case is often referenced in discussions of how survivors and diaspora communities sought recognition and justice in the absence of comprehensive international mechanisms at the time.

## Historical Interpretation

Talat Pasha’s legacy is highly contested, but in much of the historical literature he is treated as a central decision-maker whose authority connected top-level political intent with the operational execution of mass violence. The study of his role is also part of broader research on how modern states can mobilize legal, administrative, and security institutions for systematic persecution.
