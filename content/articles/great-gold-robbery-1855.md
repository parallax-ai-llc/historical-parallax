## Summary
The Great Gold Robbery of 1855 was the theft of gold bars and coins worth £12,000 from a South Eastern Railway train traveling from London to Paris. The thieves made wax impressions of the keys to the safes. It was one of the most famous robberies of the Victorian era.

## How the Theft Worked
Accounts of the case emphasize that the cargo was secured with locked boxes and seals, but that the attackers exploited predictable handling routines and limited oversight during transit. The reported use of wax impressions to duplicate keys became an early, widely discussed example of physical security being undermined by key control failures.

## Investigation and Public Attention
Victorian-era newspapers followed the story closely, and later retellings helped fix the robbery in popular memory as a "classic" rail crime. The case is often cited in studies of policing and transport security because it highlighted the need for tighter custody procedures, better audit trails, and separation of duties for valuable shipments.

## Legacy in Transport Security
Later rail and courier practices placed more emphasis on controlled access to keys, tamper-evident packaging, and verification at handoff points. In hindsight, the robbery is frequently summarized as a lesson that the strongest lock is ineffective if the key can be copied or controlled by too few people.
It remains a standard case study in Victorian crime histories.
