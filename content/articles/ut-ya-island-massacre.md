## Summary

Anders Breivik murdered 69 people, mostly teenagers, on the island of Utøya at a Labour Youth camp. Disguised as a police officer, he hunted victims for 72 minutes.

## Context

The Utøya island massacre refers to the mass shooting on 22 July 2011 at a summer camp organized by the Labour Youth (AUF), the youth wing associated with Norway's Labour Party. The victims were predominantly teenagers and young adults attending political and social programming.

The attack is widely discussed alongside the **Oslo government quarter bombing** earlier that day. Together, the events are commonly referred to as the **2011 Norway attacks**.

## What Happened (High-Level)

Open sources consistently describe the following sequence:

- A bombing in central Oslo drew attention and resources to the capital.
- Later, on Utøya island, the attacker arrived and opened fire on camp participants.
- The shooting continued for a prolonged period, with victims attempting to hide, flee, or seek help.

This summary is intentionally high-level and avoids operational detail.

## Aftermath and Impact

The massacre had lasting effects on Norwegian society and politics, including:

- **National mourning and remembrance**, with annual commemorations and memorial discussions.
- **Public inquiries and reforms** focused on coordination, emergency communications, and response readiness.
- **Debates about extremism**, political violence, and the protection of civic spaces.

## Legal Notes (Norway)

Norway's criminal justice system does not use the same "life sentence" framing common in some jurisdictions. In reporting on this case, the key concept is **preventive detention (forvaring)**, which can be extended in set increments if authorities determine the offender remains a threat. [^3]
