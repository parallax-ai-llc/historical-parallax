## Summary

Arrhichion won the Olympic pankration three times. He died during his third victory — his opponent submitted to a joint lock even as Arrhichion was being strangled to death.

## Context: Pankration and the Ancient Olympics

Pankration was a Greek combat sport that combined elements of boxing and wrestling, with relatively few formal prohibitions compared to modern combat sports. Competitors fought without weight classes, and matches could continue until one athlete submitted or was rendered unable to continue.

Arrhichion competed during a period when Olympic victors were celebrated not only as athletes but as civic symbols. Victories could bring prestige to an athlete's home city and were often commemorated with poems, statues, and local honors.

## Accounts of His Death

Ancient writers describe Arrhichion's final match as a dramatic reversal: while he was caught in a chokehold, he applied a joint lock to his opponent. The opponent signaled submission, and the judges awarded Arrhichion the victory, only to discover that Arrhichion had died in the moment of triumph.

Because the story is transmitted through later literary sources rather than contemporary match records, details vary in retelling, but the core motif is consistent: victory was declared due to the opponent's submission even though the champion did not survive the exchange.

## Legacy and Interpretation

Arrhichion's story has been used in later moral and cultural commentary as an example of:

- endurance taken to an extreme,
- the ancient ideal of glory and public recognition,
- the tension between athletic competition and bodily risk.

In the context of ancient Greek athletics, his posthumous victory also illustrates how submission and adjudication could determine outcomes even under chaotic, dangerous conditions.

## Further Reading

Later ancient discussions of Olympic victors and athletic culture include writers such as Pausanias and Philostratus, who are commonly cited in modern summaries of Arrhichion's legend.
