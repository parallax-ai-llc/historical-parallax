## Summary

Imperial Japan forced an estimated 200,000 women from occupied territories into sexual slavery for Japanese soldiers. Victims were primarily Korean, Chinese, and Southeast Asian women.

## System Description

“Comfort women” refers to women and girls forced or coerced into sexual servitude in military-run or military-supported brothels during Imperial Japan’s wartime expansion. Historical accounts describe recruitment through deception, coercion, trafficking, and direct abduction in some cases.

## Geographic Scope

Victims came from multiple occupied and colonized territories, with large numbers identified in Korea and China, as well as Southeast Asia and other regions affected by Japanese military operations.

## Postwar Recognition and Dispute

After World War II, survivor testimony, archival research, and activism brought wider international attention to the system. The topic has remained politically contentious in East Asia, involving debates over official responsibility, terminology, education, and memorialization.

## Human Rights Significance

The comfort women issue is widely discussed as a case study in wartime sexual violence, accountability, and the long-term impacts of trauma and stigma on survivors.


## Key Takeaways
