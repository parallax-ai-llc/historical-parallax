## Summary

Corinthian statesman who killed or allowed the killing of his own brother Timophanes to prevent tyranny.

Timoleon is best known for later leading a Corinthian-backed mission to Sicily, where he helped remove tyrants in Syracuse, fought Carthaginian forces, and sponsored political and civic reconstruction in the mid-4th century BCE.

## Background

Timoleon (Greek: Τιμολέων), son of Timodemus, was associated with the Corinthian oligarchic elite.

Ancient and later sources connect his reputation to two contrasting themes:

- Personal tragedy and political violence in Corinth (the death of his brother Timophanes).
- A later public career in Sicily in which he was portrayed as a liberator and constitutional restorer.

## Conflict with Timophanes

In the mid-360s BCE, Timoleon's brother **Timophanes** seized the acropolis of Corinth and acted as a tyrant.

Accounts summarized in modern reference works describe the sequence as follows:

- Timoleon initially attempted to persuade Timophanes to abandon the attempt at tyranny.
- Timoleon then became involved in Timophanes' assassination.
- While many Corinthians viewed this as a patriotic act, the episode also generated intense backlash and personal consequences.

## Withdrawal from Politics

Following Timophanes' death, sources describe Timoleon withdrawing from public life for an extended period (often given as about twenty years), influenced by:

- Public controversy over the killing.
- Family condemnation (including his mother's hostility in some accounts).
- The broader civic and moral stigma of fratricide.

## Mission to Sicily (344 BCE)

By the 340s BCE, Syracuse and other Sicilian Greek cities were destabilized by internal conflict, local tyrannies, and recurring wars with Carthage.

A Syracusan appeal to Corinth (their "mother city") led Corinth to approve an expedition in **344 BCE**. Timoleon was selected to lead it and sailed with:

- Seven ships.
- A small force of Greek mercenaries (reported as about 700) and supporting Corinthian citizens.

He landed at **Tauromenium** (modern Taormina), then operated in a complex political environment involving:

- **Hicetas** of Leontini, controlling much of Syracuse.
- **Dionysius II**, still holding the island-fortress of **Ortygia**.

## Syracuse: Removal of Tyrants and Civic Reconstruction

After early successes (including victory at Adranum), Timoleon received reinforcements.

Key developments described in reference summaries include:

- Following the siege of Syracuse, **Dionysius II surrendered Ortygia** (343 BCE) and was allowed safe conduct to Corinth.
- Timoleon then began a restoration program in Syracuse, including symbolic acts aimed at ending tyrannical rule (e.g., dismantling tyrant-associated fortifications and re-purposing civic space).
- He encouraged new settlement in depopulated areas and supported a re-established popular government, described as using democratic law traditions associated with Diocles of Syracuse.

## War with Carthage and the Battle of the Crimissus

Carthage intervened repeatedly in Sicilian affairs.

In 340–339 BCE, a large Carthaginian force landed in western Sicily. Timoleon marched west and won a major victory at the **Battle of the Crimissus**, with sources noting factors such as:

- Attacking while the Carthaginian army was still crossing the river.
- A storm that hindered the Carthaginian side.

A later treaty (dated in many accounts to **338 BCE**) is described as restricting Carthaginian influence in Sicily to areas west of the Halycus (Platani) River and limiting Carthaginian support for Sicilian tyrants.

## Later Life and Reputation

Timoleon is described as retiring from active command after achieving his main aims.

Later tradition emphasizes a public image of moderation:

- He remained influential in Syracuse, where he was reportedly carried (by then blind) to the assembly for consultation on important matters.
- He received a civic burial and posthumous honors, including a monument and the name **Timoleonteum** for a gymnasium complex.

Ancient and modern historians have debated whether Timoleon functioned primarily as a democrat, a stabilizing oligarchic reformer, or a "benevolent tyrant" operating through extraordinary power while maintaining democratic forms.
