## Summary

Robert Upshur Woodward (born March 26, 1943) is an American investigative journalist and author who has spent more than five decades at The Washington Post. He is best known for his reporting alongside Carl Bernstein on the Watergate scandal, work that contributed directly to President Richard Nixon's resignation in August 1974 and remains the benchmark for accountability journalism in the United States.

Beyond Watergate, Woodward has built one of the most prolific careers in political journalism, producing twenty-one books — many of them number-one bestsellers — that draw on deep background reporting inside the White House, Pentagon, and Supreme Court. His access to senior officials and his method of reconstructing events through hundreds of on-record and off-record interviews have made him a defining figure of the modern American press.

## Early Life & Background

Woodward was born in Geneva, Illinois, and raised in Wheaton, Illinois, in a conservative Republican family. He attended Yale University on a Naval ROTC scholarship, graduating in 1965 with a degree in history and English literature. He served five years as a U.S. Navy communications officer, reaching the rank of lieutenant, before being discharged in 1970. He briefly enrolled at Harvard Law School but left after just two weeks to pursue journalism, joining the Montgomery County Sentinel in Maryland. After a year of local reporting he was hired by The Washington Post in 1971.

## Rise to Prominence

Woodward's career-defining opportunity came in June 1972 when he and Bernstein were assigned to cover what initially appeared to be a minor break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters in the Watergate complex in Washington, D.C. Over the next two years, the pair connected the burglary to a broader campaign of political espionage and sabotage authorized by the Nixon White House. Their reporting, aided by the anonymous source known as "Deep Throat" — later revealed to be FBI Associate Director Mark Felt in 2005 — helped trigger Senate hearings, the Saturday Night Massacre, and ultimately Nixon's resignation on August 9, 1974. The Post was awarded the Pulitzer Prize for Public Service in 1973 largely on the basis of their work.

## Key Achievements

Woodward and Bernstein co-authored *All the President's Men* (1974), which was adapted into the acclaimed 1976 film starring Robert Redford and Dustin Hoffman. Woodward followed with *The Final Days* (1976), a reconstruction of Nixon's last months in office. He went on to write deeply sourced books on every presidential administration from Reagan through Biden, including *Veil: The Secret Wars of the CIA* (1987), *The Commanders* (1991), *Bush at War* (2002), *Obama's Wars* (2010), and a trilogy on Donald Trump — *Fear* (2018), *Rage* (2020), and *Peril* (2021, co-authored with Robert Costa). *Fear* sold 1.1 million copies in its first week of publication. He has been an associate editor at the Post since 1979.

## Controversies & Criticism

Woodward's methods have drawn sustained criticism. Journalists and scholars have questioned the reliability of extended verbatim dialogue in his books, which is reconstructed from interviews rather than contemporaneous recordings. His 1984 book *Wired*, a biography of John Belushi, was attacked by Belushi's family and collaborators for inaccuracies. Critics have also argued that his access-dependent reporting can shade into hagiography or that sources use him to settle scores. His 2020 revelation that he had on-tape interviews in which Trump admitted downplaying the severity of COVID-19 — interviews conducted months before publication — prompted debate about whether he sat on information of public health importance.

## Legacy & Influence

Woodward's career established investigative political reporting as a prestigious and consequential pursuit within American journalism. The Watergate coverage demonstrated that the press could hold a sitting president accountable and reshaped public expectations of what journalism could achieve. His books have served as primary historical records for multiple administrations and are cited regularly by historians, policymakers, and journalists. He has won virtually every major journalism award, and the archetype of the dogged, source-protecting investigative reporter is, in large part, shaped in his image.
