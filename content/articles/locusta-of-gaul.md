## Summary

Roman professional poisoner who served Emperor Nero and was responsible for some of ancient Rome's most infamous poisonings. She killed Emperor Claudius and his son Britannicus on Nero's orders.

## Historical Context

Poisoning was a recurring motif in Roman imperial politics, where succession disputes and palace intrigue created incentives for covert assassinations.

Professional poisoners appear in ancient sources as specialists who combined knowledge of toxins with the social access needed to administer them.

## Association with Nero

Locusta is most commonly linked to the Julio-Claudian court during Nero's reign, where she is portrayed as an instrument for eliminating political rivals.

Accounts often emphasize that her services were not incidental but integrated into elite power struggles, reflecting the insecurity of imperial succession.

## Claudius and Britannicus (High-Level)

The alleged poisoning of Emperor Claudius is a major narrative element in ancient histories, frequently described through the "poisoned mushrooms" motif.

Britannicus, as a potential rival to Nero, is also described as a poisoning victim during a public setting (a banquet), underscoring the performative dimension of imperial control.

## Downfall and Legacy

After Nero's fall, individuals associated with his court were vulnerable to reprisals and political cleansing, and Locusta is reported to have been executed in 69 CE.

In later cultural memory, she is often depicted as an archetype of the court poisoner, illustrating fears around hidden violence in elite households.
