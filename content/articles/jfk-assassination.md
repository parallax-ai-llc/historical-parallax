## Summary

Assassination of President John F. Kennedy in Dallas, Texas on November 22, 1963 by Lee Harvey Oswald. The Warren Commission concluded Oswald acted alone, though conspiracy theories persist.

## Background

Kennedy's 1963 trip to Texas occurred in a politically tense period marked by Cold War rivalry and domestic disputes over civil rights and foreign policy. The Dallas motorcade route and open-air limousine created an environment later scrutinized for security planning and risk.

## The Event (High-Level Sequence)

Shots were fired as the presidential motorcade passed Dealey Plaza. Kennedy was mortally wounded and pronounced dead later that day. Texas Governor John Connally, riding in the same vehicle, was also wounded but survived.

## Investigations and Public Records

The Warren Commission gathered witness testimony and reviewed physical evidence, producing a detailed report that shaped official understanding for decades. Later inquiries, including congressional reviews, examined forensic questions, intelligence-agency context, and the reliability of early evidence handling.

## Cultural and Political Legacy

The assassination became a defining moment in modern U.S. history, influencing public trust in government and media. It also spawned extensive research, documentary work, and persistent alternative theories about responsibility and motive.

## Declassification and Ongoing Review

Over time, U.S. authorities have released large collections of related records, with periodic announcements about additional declassifications. The continued release of documents has sustained public interest and scholarly re-evaluation even when core conclusions remain contested.

Because many records relate to intelligence and law-enforcement activities of the era, the pace and scope of disclosure has been politically sensitive.
