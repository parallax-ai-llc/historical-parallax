## Summary

William of Ockham (c. 1287-1347) was an English Franciscan friar, scholastic philosopher, and theologian who became one of the most influential figures of medieval thought.[^1] He is best known for the principle of parsimony called "Occam's Razor," which advocates for simplicity in explanations.[^2] His nominalist philosophy challenged the dominant realist tradition, arguing that universal concepts exist only in the mind rather than as metaphysical realities.[^3] Ockham's political writings defending apostolic poverty led to his excommunication and exile, where he became an early advocate for the separation of church and state.[^4] His work profoundly influenced the development of modern empiricism, scientific methodology, and political theory.[^5]

---

## Early Life and Background

William of Ockham was born around 1287 in the small village of Ockham in Surrey, England, though some sources place his birth as early as 1285 or within the broader range of 1280-1290.[^6] The exact date remains uncertain among historians, reflecting the limited documentary evidence from this period. At a remarkably young age—somewhere between seven and thirteen years old—Ockham was "given" to the Franciscan Order, known as the Greyfriars, a common practice for families dedicating their children to religious life.[^7]

He received his elementary education at the London House of the Greyfriars, where he would have begun his formation in the Franciscan tradition of poverty, humility, and service.[^8] This early education likely included basic literacy, Latin grammar, and introduction to religious texts. Around the age of fourteen, Ockham progressed to what would be considered secondary education, studying basic logic and natural philosophy (the medieval term for science).[^9] His ordination as a subdeacon by the Archbishop of Canterbury in Southwark, London, in 1306 confirms his deep integration into the London Franciscan community by his late teens.[^10]

The Franciscan order, founded by Saint Francis of Assisi in the early 13th century, emphasized radical poverty and humility in imitation of Christ. This commitment to apostolic poverty would later become central to Ockham's life and thought, ultimately leading to his dramatic conflict with papal authority. The order also valued learning and intellectual inquiry, which created a fertile environment for Ockham's philosophical development. Growing up in this tradition shaped his theological perspective and his later defense of Franciscan values against what he perceived as papal corruption.

The London Greyfriars served as one of the major intellectual centers for the Franciscan order in England, providing Ockham with access to an extensive library and community of learned friars. This environment exposed him to the major philosophical and theological debates of the late medieval period, including disputes over universals, the nature of knowledge, and the relationship between faith and reason. These formative years established the foundation for his later revolutionary contributions to philosophy and theology.

## Rise to Prominence

Around 1310, when Ockham was approximately twenty-three years old, he began his formal theological training, marking the beginning of his academic career.[^11] By 1318-19, Ockham was studying theology at the University of Oxford, one of Europe's premier centers of learning and intellectual innovation.[^12] During this period, he began the required two-year cycle of lectures commenting on Peter Lombard's *Sentences*, the standard theological textbook of the medieval university curriculum.[^13] This commentary work, completed between 1317 and 1319, was a prerequisite for obtaining the doctorate in theology.

However, despite completing these initial requirements, Ockham never received his doctorate in theology from Oxford—a fact that earned him the nickname "Venerabilis Inceptor" or "Venerable Beginner."[^14] The reasons for this remain somewhat mysterious, though it likely relates to the theological controversies that would soon engulf him. His failure to complete the degree did not diminish his intellectual output or influence; rather, it may have freed him from certain academic constraints.

During his Oxford years, Ockham produced an extraordinary body of work that would revolutionize medieval philosophy. His major philosophical and theological writings from this period demonstrate remarkable originality and logical rigor. He developed his nominalist position against the prevailing realism of thinkers like Thomas Aquinas and Duns Scotus, arguing that universals have no existence outside the human mind.[^15] This position challenged centuries of Aristotelian-inspired scholastic philosophy and would have far-reaching implications for epistemology and metaphysics.

Between 1323 and 1326, Ockham composed his *Summa Logicae* (Sum of Logic), described by scholars as "a manifesto masquerading as a textbook."[^16] While presented as a manual of logic, the work had the ambitious purpose of promoting a comprehensive philosophical program based on the principle that philosophical and theological errors could be resolved through correct understanding of logic and language.[^17] This work contained the main account of his nominalism and established him as one of the foremost logicians of the medieval period.

Ockham's rising prominence came with controversy. In 1324, his theological commentary was condemned as unorthodox, and he was summoned to Avignon, France, seat of the papacy, to defend himself before the papal court.[^18] This marked a turning point in his life, transforming him from an academic philosopher into a political exile and opponent of papal authority. The transition from respected scholar to condemned heretic would define the rest of his career.

## Major Achievements

### Philosophical Innovations

William of Ockham's most enduring contribution to philosophy is the principle known as Occam's Razor, though he did not invent the underlying idea, which can be found in Aristotle, Aquinas, and other earlier philosophers.[^19] However, Ockham employed this principle of parsimony in particularly striking and systematic ways, which is why it became associated with his name.[^20] In his *Summa Logicae*, he articulated the principle: "Frustra fit per plura quod potest fieri per pauciora" (It is futile to do with more things that which can be done with fewer).[^21] Another formulation states: "For nothing ought to be posited without a reason given, unless it is self-evident or known by experience or proved by the authority of Sacred Scripture."[^22]

This methodological principle advocates that when presented with competing hypotheses with equal explanatory power, one should prefer the hypothesis requiring the fewest assumptions.[^23] Occam's Razor remains a cornerstone of modern scientific methodology, guiding hypothesis formation and theory selection across disciplines from physics to biology to machine learning.[^24] Scientists regularly invoke the Razor when deciding between competing theories, preferring simpler explanations with fewer variables when other factors are equal.[^25]

### Nominalism and Metaphysics

Ockham is probably best known today for his espousal of metaphysical nominalism, making him among the most prominent figures in the history of Late Medieval philosophy.[^26] He maintained that only individual things exist, rather than supra-individual universals, essences, or forms.[^27] Universal concepts such as "humanity" or "whiteness" are merely concepts in the mind, products of abstraction from individual things, with no extra-mental existence.[^28] This represented a fundamental challenge to the realist position that had dominated medieval thought since the time of Plato and Aristotle.

Ockham argued that the realist position led to unnecessary metaphysical complexity and obscured the simplicity of God's creation.[^29] He contended that the notion of universals as real entities was incompatible with Christian doctrines of divine omnipotence and the contingency of creation.[^30] One of his main preoccupations was to purge Christian theology and philosophy of all traces of Greek necessitarianism, particularly the theory of essences, which he believed endangered the doctrines of divine liberty and omnipotence.[^31] His nominalism effectively divided theology from philosophy and faith from science, severing connections that scholasticism had developed over centuries.[^32]

### Logic and Language

William of Ockham's contributions to logic are considered among his most impressive achievements. He was certainly among the most imaginative, competent, and prolific of Medieval logicians, with the scope of apparently original concepts, problems, and results found in his works described as astounding.[^33] His *Summa Logicae* is recognized as a classic of analytical metaphysics, using fourteenth-century logic to defend a reductionist ontology.[^34]

The work is divided into multiple parts covering different aspects of logic and language. Part I deals extensively with terms—what they are and how they are divided into categorematic, abstract and concrete, absolute and connotative, and first and second intention.[^35] It also provides systematic treatment of Porphyry's five predicables and Aristotle's Categories. Part II offers a systematic exposition of Aristotle's *Topics*, while Part III deals with the definition and division of consequences. Part IV addresses the different species of fallacy enumerated in Aristotle's *Sophistical Refutations*.[^36]

Ockham's interest in terminist logic, linguistic theory, and semiotics has placed him at the forefront of medieval thinkers used as sources in contemporary philosophical discussion.[^37] His conceptualism and account of connotative concepts continue to challenge modern scholars and inform understanding of his contribution to empiricism.[^38] His work anticipated developments in fields ranging from computer science to linguistic analysis, with applications extending to data compression and model selection in information theory.[^39]

### Political Theory

In political theory, Ockham made groundbreaking contributions that helped shape modern constitutional thought. He advanced the notion of individual rights, separation of church and state, and freedom of speech—concepts that were revolutionary for the 14th century.[^40] His concept of the "two swords" held that there were two distinct sources of authority in the world: the spiritual authority of the church over matters of faith and morality, and the temporal authority of the state over matters of law and justice.[^41]

Ockham believed these two spheres should remain separate, with the church having no right to interfere in political matters and the state having no authority over religious matters.[^42] He argued that the pope and churchmen have no right or grounds for secular rule such as owning property, citing 2 Timothy 2:4.[^43] This doctrine of separation became foundational to modern democratic theory and influenced the framers of the United States Constitution.[^44]

Furthermore, Ockham was one of the first medieval authors to develop a sophisticated theory of rights. He argued that after the Fall, God had given humanity, including non-Christians, two fundamental powers: private ownership and the right to choose their rulers, who should serve the interest of the people rather than special interests.[^45] He posited the existence of certain natural, non-renounceable, and inalienable rights that all human beings possess.[^46] The Franciscan poverty debate in which Ockham played a significant role is considered an important watershed in the early development of property rights theory.[^47]

## Controversies and Criticisms

### The Papal Conflict

The central controversy of William of Ockham's life erupted in 1324 when his theological commentary was condemned as unorthodox, leading to his summons to Avignon to defend himself before Pope John XXII.[^48] While at Avignon, Ockham became embroiled in the Franciscan poverty controversy, which would transform him from academic philosopher to political exile and fierce critic of papal authority.[^49]

On November 12, 1323, Pope John XXII issued the bull *Quum inter nonnullos*, declaring "erroneous and heretical" the doctrine that Christ and his apostles had no possessions whatsoever.[^50] This directly attacked the Franciscan practice of apostolic poverty, which had been previously affirmed by Pope Nicholas III and was central to the Franciscan identity. As a devoted Franciscan, Ockham saw this as not merely a theological disagreement but an attack on the legitimate interpretation of Christianity and the Rule of Saint Francis.

In 1328, instructed by his superior general Michael of Cesena to study three papal bulls on poverty, Ockham concluded they contained numerous errors demonstrating that John XXII was a heretic who had forfeited his mandate.[^51] Ockham protested that John was a pseudo-pope, stubbornly and heretically wrong on matters of doctrine.[^52] This was an extraordinarily bold claim—arguing that a sitting pope had lost his legitimacy through heresy.

On May 26, 1328, Ockham, Michael of Cesena, and several Franciscan sympathizers fled Avignon, seeking protection at the court of Holy Roman Emperor Louis IV of Bavaria.[^53] On June 6, 1328, William was officially excommunicated for leaving Avignon without permission.[^54] He spent the remainder of his life in Munich writing voluminous political treatises defending his positions and attacking what he saw as papal corruption and overreach. According to legend, Ockham told Emperor Louis, "You defend me with the sword, and I will defend you with the pen."[^55]

### Theological Controversies

Ockham's theological positions generated significant controversy among his contemporaries and later scholars. He was a fideist, maintaining that belief in God is fundamentally a matter of faith rather than knowledge.[^56] He insisted that theology is not a science in the Aristotelian sense and rejected all alleged proofs of God's existence.[^57] His skepticism extended to the claim that human reason can prove neither the immortality of the soul nor the existence, unity, and infinity of God.[^58]

This separation of faith and reason troubled many orthodox theologians who saw it as undermining the rational foundations of Christian belief. The Thomist synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy and Christian theology, which had become influential in the 13th century, relied on the possibility of demonstrating certain theological truths through natural reason. Ockham's rejection of this approach appeared to some as a dangerous skepticism that could lead to the erosion of faith.

### Philosophical Criticisms

Within the medieval philosophical tradition, Ockham's nominalism faced vigorous opposition from proponents of realism. Critics argued that denying real universals undermines the objectivity of knowledge and the existence of a common human nature.[^59] If universals are merely names or mental constructs without corresponding reality, how can we have certain knowledge about categories of things?

Some opponents accused nominalism of making mathematics and general laws arbitrary.[^60] If concepts like "three-ness" or "equality" are mere names rather than real properties, how can arithmetic and geometry be certain? This criticism touched on fundamental questions about the nature of mathematical truth and scientific knowledge. Ockham's response relied on his sophisticated analysis of language and concepts, but not all found his answers satisfactory.

The via moderna (new way) represented by Ockham and his followers stood in tension with the via antiqua (old way) of earlier scholastics like Aquinas and Duns Scotus.[^61] This division created lasting splits in medieval universities, with different institutions and scholars aligning with different philosophical approaches. Some historians have portrayed Ockham as a destructive revolutionary who undermined traditional values, though more nuanced scholarship recognizes him as a product of medieval culture seeking to resolve problems generated by that culture.[^62]

### Impact on Scholasticism

Ockham's critiques of scholasticism, particularly his rejection of the existence of universals and his emphasis on simplicity, helped lay the intellectual groundwork for the Renaissance, the Scientific Revolution, and the Enlightenment.[^63] However, this also meant he contributed to the eventual decline of the scholastic synthesis that had unified medieval intellectual life. His philosophy separated theology from philosophy and faith from science, breaking down connections that scholasticism had carefully constructed.[^64]

Some Catholic thinkers have seen in Ockham's nominalism the roots of later theological problems, including those that contributed to the Protestant Reformation. His emphasis on divine will over divine reason, and faith over philosophical demonstration, resonated with later reformers like Martin Luther, though the precise lines of influence remain debated among historians.[^65]

## Personal Life

Very little is known about William of Ockham's personal life, as was common for medieval scholars whose biographies were rarely documented in detail. He never married, having taken vows as a Franciscan friar, which included commitments to poverty, chastity, and obedience.[^66] His entire adult life was lived within the Franciscan community, first in London and Oxford, then in Avignon, and finally in Munich.

The Franciscan order shaped not only his intellectual life but his daily existence. Franciscans lived communally, sharing meals, prayers, and work. They embraced voluntary poverty, owning no personal possessions and depending on charitable donations for their sustenance. This commitment to apostolic poverty was not merely an abstract ideal for Ockham but a lived reality that defined his identity and ultimately led to his conflict with the papacy.

When the court of Emperor Ludwig of Bavaria returned from Italy to Munich, Ockham accompanied them, living out the rest of his life in the Franciscan convent there.[^67] During these years in Munich, from 1328 until his death in 1347, Ockham devoted himself to writing extensive political treatises. He produced thousands of pages defending the independence of secular power from ecclesiastical power and stressing the autonomy of right reason in human affairs.[^68]

Ockham's personality emerges from his writings as intellectually rigorous, logically precise, and uncompromising in defense of principles he believed were true. His willingness to challenge papal authority at enormous personal cost—risking and receiving excommunication—demonstrates remarkable courage and conviction. He appears to have been deeply committed to what he saw as fundamental human rights and Christian values, which he believed were seriously threatened in his time and could only be preserved through radical measures.[^69]

His excommunication was a severe punishment that cut him off from the sacraments of the Church and the communion of the faithful. For a devout Franciscan friar, this must have been deeply painful, yet he never recanted his positions or sought reconciliation with the papacy on terms that would have required abandoning his principles. This steadfastness suggests a man of profound moral conviction, willing to sacrifice personal comfort and spiritual security for what he believed was truth.

## Legacy

### Death and Immediate Aftermath

William of Ockham died on April 9 or 10, 1347, in the Franciscan convent at Munich.[^70] Contrary to some popular accounts, he most likely did not die from the Black Death, as he passed away in April 1347, approximately two years before the plague reached Munich in late 1348.[^71] Some sources suggest his death year might have been 1349, which would make plague a possibility, but the more authoritative evidence supports 1347.[^72]

At the time of his death, Ockham remained under excommunication, separated from the institutional Church he had served and challenged. Some accounts suggest he may have sought reconciliation in his final days, but this remains uncertain. He was buried in Munich, and his grave became a site of respect despite his controversial status.

### Influence on Later Philosophy

Although Ockham scarcely had any acknowledged disciples and did not found a formal school of followers committed to defending his teachings (unlike Thomas Aquinas or Duns Scotus), the actual influence exerted by his thought was of a significance and breadth that may well have surpassed theirs.[^73] His influence extended from his own time into the seventeenth century and beyond, shaping the development of Western philosophy in profound ways.

Ockham's nominalism and empiricism are clearly discernible in the empiricist doctrines of John Locke and David Hume.[^74] His emphasis on experience and observation over abstract reasoning about universals helped establish the foundations for British empiricism, one of the major streams of modern philosophy. His insistence that we should not multiply entities beyond necessity became a guiding principle for philosophical and scientific inquiry.

Some scholars consider Ockham the father of modern epistemology because of his strongly argued position that only individuals exist and that universals are products of mental abstraction with no extra-mental existence.[^75] This view fundamentally reoriented questions about knowledge, moving focus from abstract essences to concrete particulars and the processes by which the mind forms general concepts.

### Impact on Science

Ockham's critiques of metaphysical speculation and his advocacy for a more empirical approach to knowledge set the stage for modern scientific inquiry, making him a bridge between medieval and modern thought.[^76] His emphasis on simplicity and his rejection of unnecessary explanatory entities anticipated the methodological approach that would characterize the Scientific Revolution.

Occam's Razor remains a key concept in modern scientific methodology, guiding how scientists form hypotheses and select among competing theories.[^77] When deciding between theories that explain the same phenomena, scientists typically prefer the simpler theory with fewer assumptions or variables. This principle has applications across all scientific disciplines, from physics and chemistry to biology and psychology.

In mathematics and logic, Ockham's contributions continue to be relevant. He anticipated aspects of the modern scientific renaissance while challenging Aristotelian prohibitions.[^78] His work on logic laid groundwork for developments in fields ranging from computer science to linguistic analysis.[^79] Occam's Razor is frequently invoked in discussions of data compression, model selection in machine learning, and information theory, demonstrating how his ideas continue to shape diverse fields of study.[^80]

### Political Legacy

Ockham's political writings had lasting influence on constitutional and democratic theory. His advocacy for separation of church and state, individual rights, and limitations on papal and imperial authority contributed to the development of modern liberal political thought.[^81] His arguments that rulers should serve the interest of the people rather than special interests, and that people have the right to choose their rulers, anticipated democratic principles that would not be fully realized for centuries.[^82]

His involvement in the conciliar movement—which argued that church councils have authority over popes—influenced debates about constitutional limits on authority that extended beyond ecclesiastical contexts to secular governance.[^83] The notion that even the highest authority is subject to law and can lose legitimacy through abuse of power became a cornerstone of constitutional theory.

### Scholarly Reassessment

With the revival of interest in late medieval thought in the second half of the twentieth century, Ockham has re-emerged as one of the major figures of scholastic thought, generally ranked alongside Thomas Aquinas and John Duns Scotus.[^84] Modern scholars have moved beyond earlier caricatures that portrayed him either as a destructive skeptic or as a proto-modern rationalist, developing more nuanced appreciation of his work within its medieval context.

His interest in terminist logic, linguistic theory, and semiotics has brought him to the forefront of medieval thinkers referenced in contemporary philosophical discussion.[^85] His sophisticated analysis of language and meaning resonates with modern concerns in philosophy of language and analytic philosophy. The projected completion of critical editions of his political works continues to deepen scholarly understanding of which of his ideas were traditional and which link him to modern liberalism.[^86]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Many scholars celebrate William of Ockham as one of the most brilliant and innovative thinkers of the medieval period. He is widely recognized as being among the most imaginative, competent, and prolific of Medieval logicians, with the scope of his original contributions described as impressive if not astounding.[^87] His systematic application of logical analysis to philosophical and theological problems set new standards for rigor and clarity in scholastic discourse.

Ockham's emphasis on simplicity and empiricism is praised for helping lay the intellectual groundwork for the Renaissance, the Scientific Revolution, and the Enlightenment.[^88] By challenging unnecessary metaphysical assumptions and advocating for explanations based on observation and experience, he helped shift Western thought toward the empirical methodology that would characterize modern science. His nominalism is seen as liberating philosophy from the constraints of excessive abstraction and returning attention to the concrete, observable world.

Supporters of his political thought view him as a champion of human freedom and dignity. He emerges as a man deeply committed to natural and Christian human rights, who found these fundamental values so seriously threatened in his time that their survival could be assured only by radical measures.[^89] His advocacy for separation of powers, individual rights, and constraints on arbitrary authority anticipated core principles of modern liberal democracy.

His willingness to challenge papal authority at enormous personal cost is admired as an example of intellectual courage and moral integrity. Scholars note that he was not merely a rebellious iconoclast but someone who believed he was defending authentic Christian values and the legitimate interpretation of Scripture and tradition against corruption and innovation. His extensive political writings demonstrate sophisticated engagement with questions of legitimacy, authority, and justice that remain relevant to contemporary political philosophy.

Contemporary philosophers appreciate how Ockham's work on language, logic, and concepts anticipates modern concerns. His analysis of meaning, reference, and the relationship between language and thought resonates with twentieth-century philosophy of language and analytic philosophy. His conceptualism and account of connotative concepts continue to inform understanding of empiricism and knowledge.[^90]

The principle of Occam's Razor is perhaps his most universally celebrated contribution, valued across disciplines for promoting clarity, simplicity, and intellectual economy. Scientists, mathematicians, and philosophers regularly invoke the principle as a guideline for theory construction and hypothesis selection, making Ockham's methodological insight one of the most enduring ideas in the history of thought.

### Negative Views

Critics of William of Ockham have raised serious concerns about both his philosophy and his conduct. Within the medieval tradition, realist philosophers argued that his nominalism undermines the objectivity of knowledge and scientific inquiry.[^91] If universals have no real existence but are merely mental constructs, critics contended, then knowledge of general truths becomes problematic. How can we have certain knowledge about categories of things if those categories are merely conventional names?

Some accused nominalism of making mathematics and general laws arbitrary.[^92] If mathematical concepts like "three-ness" or "equality" are mere names rather than real properties or relations, what guarantees the truth of mathematical propositions? This criticism suggested that Ockham's philosophy, taken to its logical conclusion, could lead to skepticism about the possibility of certain knowledge in mathematics and natural science.

Theological critics worried that Ockham's separation of faith and reason undermined rational theology and apologetics. By maintaining that God's existence, the soul's immortality, and other fundamental theological doctrines cannot be proven by reason but must be accepted on faith, Ockham appeared to some to be weakening Christianity's intellectual foundations. The Thomist synthesis had argued that faith and reason, properly understood, support and complement each other. Ockham's sharp division between them seemed to some critics to be a dangerous retreat from reason that could ultimately harm religious belief.

Some Catholic scholars have seen connections between Ockham's nominalism and later theological developments they view negatively, including aspects of Protestant Reformation thought.[^93] His emphasis on divine will over divine intellect and his voluntarism (the view that God's will rather than God's reason is primary) resonated with some Reformation thinkers, though the precise lines of influence remain contested. Critics suggest his philosophy contributed to fragmentation of the medieval Christian synthesis and the religious divisions that would tear apart Western Christendom.

Ockham's conduct during the poverty controversy has been questioned by some historians. While supporters see him as a courageous defender of truth, critics point out that he openly defied papal authority, fled from judgment, and spent the rest of his life in schism, under excommunication and protection of the emperor who was himself in conflict with the papacy. Some argue this demonstrates not principled resistance but rather insubordination and rebellion against legitimate authority.

His political writings, while praised by some as proto-democratic, have been criticized by others as tendentious and excessively polemical. He devoted thousands of pages to attacking Pope John XXII and defending Emperor Louis IV, sometimes making arguments that appear to subordinate careful analysis to political partisanship. Critics suggest his political theology was driven more by the contingencies of his exile and alliance with the emperor than by dispassionate philosophical reasoning.

Some scholars argue that while Ockham was brilliant as a logician, his contributions to metaphysics and theology were ultimately destructive rather than constructive. By breaking down the scholastic synthesis without building an adequate replacement, he may have contributed to intellectual fragmentation and the eventual decline of medieval philosophy. His razor, while useful for eliminating unnecessary complexity, may have been applied too zealously, cutting away not just excess but also necessary philosophical infrastructure.

## Famous Quotes

> "Frustra fit per plura quod potest fieri per pauciora" (It is vain to do with more what can be done with less) - This formulation of Occam's Razor from his *Summa Logicae* encapsulates his principle of parsimony.[^108]

> "Entities should not be multiplied unnecessarily" - Another expression of Occam's Razor, advocating for simplicity in explanations.[^109]

> "For nothing ought to be posited without a reason given, unless it is self-evident or known by experience or proved by the authority of Sacred Scripture" - This formulation emphasizes empiricism and scriptural authority.[^110]

> "Logic is the most useful tool of all the arts" - Reflects Ockham's deep appreciation for logical analysis as fundamental to knowledge.[^111]

> "Purely philosophical assertions which do not pertain to theology should not be solemnly condemned or forbidden by anyone, because in connection with such [assertions] anyone at all ought to be free to say freely what pleases him" - An early articulation of intellectual freedom and the autonomy of philosophy from theological control.[^112]

> "Of two equivalent theories or explanations, all other things being equal, the simpler one is to be preferred" - A clear statement of the methodological principle that bears his name.[^113]
