## Summary

British politician at the center of the 1963 Profumo Affair—a scandal involving his relationship with Christine Keeler, who was also involved with a Soviet naval attaché. He lied to Parliament. The scandal damaged the Macmillan government.

## Additional Context

- Profumo served as the UK **Secretary of State for War** when the affair became public, which amplified concerns about judgment and security during the Cold War.
- The scandal is often cited as a turning point in post-war British political culture because it combined private behavior, intense media attention, and national-security anxieties.
- After resigning, Profumo became known for **long-term charitable work**, particularly connected to **Toynbee Hall** in East London, and this later public service is frequently noted in biographies.

## Legacy / Discussion Points

- The case is commonly used in discussions of ministerial accountability: the decisive issue was not only the relationship itself but the **false statement to Parliament**.
- The "Profumo Affair" remains a reference point for how personal conduct can become a constitutional and political crisis when it intersects with public trust.
