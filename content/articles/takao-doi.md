## Summary

Takao Doi is a Japanese astronaut and aerospace engineer who made history as the first Japanese citizen to perform a spacewalk, during the STS-87 mission in November 1997. A veteran of two Space Shuttle flights, he represents a pioneering generation of Japanese space explorers who helped establish Japan's enduring presence in human spaceflight. Beyond his orbital achievements, Doi has contributed significantly to space science as an academic and researcher at Kyoto University.

His career bridged the worlds of engineering, astronautics, and academia, demonstrating a rare combination of hands-on spaceflight experience and scholarly rigor. Doi's work helped lay the groundwork for Japan's continued partnership with NASA and its growing role in International Space Station (ISS) operations.

---

## Early Life & Background

Takao Doi was born on September 18, 1954, in Minamitama, Tokyo, Japan. He pursued an exceptional academic path, earning a Bachelor of Engineering degree from the University of Tokyo in 1978, followed by a Master of Engineering in 1980 and a Doctor of Engineering in 1983, both from the same institution. He later obtained a Ph.D. in Aerospace Engineering from the University of Houston in 1992, underscoring his commitment to rigorous scientific training across two continents.

Before joining JAXA's predecessor organization NASDA (National Space Development Agency of Japan), Doi worked as a research engineer, developing expertise in fluid dynamics and space systems engineering.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Doi was selected as a NASDA astronaut candidate in 1985, part of Japan's first cohort of mission specialist candidates for NASA's Space Shuttle program. He completed NASA astronaut training at the Johnson Space Center in Houston, Texas, becoming a fully qualified mission specialist.

His first spaceflight came with STS-87 aboard Space Shuttle Columbia, which launched on November 19, 1997. During this mission, Doi conducted two spacewalks totaling approximately 12 hours and 43 minutes. On December 3, 1997, he became the first Japanese astronaut to walk in space, a landmark moment for Japan's space program. He returned to space on STS-123 in March 2008, a mission that delivered the first section of the Japanese Experiment Module Kibō to the ISS.

---

## Key Achievements

- **First Japanese spacewalker**: Performed the first extravehicular activity (EVA) by a Japanese citizen on December 3, 1997, during STS-87.
- **Two Space Shuttle missions**: Flew on STS-87 (1997) and STS-123 (2008), accumulating over 460 hours in space.
- **ISS construction**: Participated in the delivery and installation of the Kibō logistics module during STS-123, a key element of Japan's contribution to the ISS.
- **Academic career**: After retiring from active astronaut duties, Doi joined Kyoto University as a professor, contributing to space policy and aerospace research.

---

## Controversies & Criticism

Doi's career has been largely free of significant controversy. Some observers noted the extended gap between his two spaceflights — over a decade — as reflective of broader scheduling constraints within the international astronaut corps rather than any personal or professional shortcoming. No major ethical or professional criticisms have been publicly associated with him.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Doi's historic spacewalk opened a symbolic door for Japanese participation in human spaceflight, inspiring subsequent astronauts such as Koichi Wakata and Akihiko Hoshide. His academic work at Kyoto University continues to shape Japan's next generation of aerospace engineers and space policy thinkers. He remains a celebrated figure in Japan's scientific community, recognized for combining exploratory courage with scholarly depth.
