## Background

Thomas Lubanga Dyilo is a Congolese militia leader known internationally for being the first person convicted by the International Criminal Court (ICC).

He is associated with the Ituri conflict in the northeastern Democratic Republic of the Congo, where armed groups mobilized along political and ethnic lines amid broader regional warfare.

## Organizations and Role

Lubanga founded and led the **Union of Congolese Patriots (UPC)** and its armed wing, commonly described as the **Patriotic Force for the Liberation of the Congo (FPLC)**.

In reporting by international monitors and human-rights organizations, forces linked to these groups were accused of serious abuses, including violence against civilians and the recruitment and use of child soldiers.

## ICC Case (High-Level)

### Core charges

The ICC case that resulted in Lubanga’s conviction focused on the war crime of:

- **Conscripting and enlisting children under 15**, and
- **Using them to participate actively in hostilities**

This framing made the case a landmark for international criminal law because it centered on child-soldier recruitment as the primary criminal conduct at issue.

### Key procedural milestones

- **2006**: Surrendered/transfer to the ICC following an ICC warrant; he became the first person arrested under an ICC warrant.
- **2009**: Trial began at the ICC.
- **2012**: Convicted by the ICC (the court’s first conviction).

### Sentence and later developments

The ICC sentenced Lubanga to **14 years of imprisonment**, with time already spent in custody credited toward the term.

He was **released in 2020** after serving his sentence.

## Why the Case Matters

Lubanga’s proceedings are frequently cited in discussions of the ICC because they illustrate:

- The court’s early reliance on a limited set of charges to build precedent
- The evidentiary and witness-protection challenges in prosecuting crimes committed during active conflict
- The symbolic and practical importance of addressing child-soldier recruitment in international justice
