## Summary

Systematic extermination of 1.5 million Armenians by the Ottoman government during WWI. One of the first modern genocides.

## Historical Context

The Armenian Genocide occurred during the final years of the Ottoman Empire, amid the upheaval of World War I and longstanding tensions in eastern Anatolia and the Caucasus region. Armenian communities were a significant Christian minority within the empire and were subject to discrimination and periodic violence even before 1915.

## Key Features of the Genocide

High-level elements commonly described by historians include:

- **State-directed deportations**: Armenian civilians were forcibly removed from their homes under wartime security pretexts, including through the 1915 deportation law often called the *Tehcir Law*.
- **Mass killing and starvation**: Many deportees were killed outright or died during marches and in camps due to hunger, disease, and exposure.
- **Targeting of elites**: Early arrests and executions of Armenian intellectuals and leaders (often dated to April 1915) are frequently cited as a turning point.

## Aftermath and Accountability Efforts

In the postwar period, Ottoman military tribunals (1919–1920) tried some officials connected to wartime atrocities, though many perpetrators were not ultimately held accountable in a lasting way. Large-scale displacement created enduring Armenian diaspora communities, and the loss of life and property remained a central trauma in Armenian history.

## International Recognition and Debate

Many governments and institutions have formally recognized the events as genocide, while the Republic of Turkey has long disputed that characterization and the framing of intent. The topic remains politically sensitive and is frequently debated in diplomacy, education, and public memory.

## Legacy

The Armenian Genocide is often referenced in discussions of mass atrocity prevention and international criminal law. Raphael Lemkin, who later coined the term "genocide," cited the destruction of the Armenians as among the events that influenced his thinking.
