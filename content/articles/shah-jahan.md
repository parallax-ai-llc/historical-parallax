## Summary

Shah Jahan I (Shahab-ud-Din Muhammad Khurram; 5 January 1592 – 22 January 1666), also called Shah Jahan the Magnificent, was the fifth Mughal Emperor who reigned from 1628 until his deposition in 1658.[^1] His reign marked the zenith of Mughal architectural and cultural achievements, earning the period the designation "Golden Age of Mughal Architecture."[^2] He is best remembered for commissioning the Taj Mahal as a mausoleum for his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal, along with numerous other architectural masterpieces including the Red Fort and Jama Masjid.[^3] His reign ended in tragedy when his son Aurangzeb imprisoned him in the Agra Fort, where he spent his final eight years gazing at the Taj Mahal before his death in 1666.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Shah Jahan was born on 5 January 1592 in Lahore, present-day Pakistan, as the ninth child and third son of Prince Salim, who would later become Emperor Jahangir.[^5] His mother was Jagat Gosain, the Rajput princess of Marwar and Jahangir's chief consort.[^6] His birth brought great joy to the Mughal court, and his grandfather, the legendary Emperor Akbar, bestowed upon him the name Khurram, meaning "joyful" in Persian.[^7]

From an early age, Prince Khurram demonstrated exceptional abilities that set him apart from his siblings. In 1605, when Emperor Akbar lay dying, the young prince remained by his grandfather's bedside, witnessing the transfer of power to his father Jahangir.[^8] This early exposure to imperial succession would profoundly shape his understanding of Mughal politics and the precarious nature of dynastic power.

As a youth, Shah Jahan received the finest education available in the Mughal Empire, studying Persian literature, Arabic, history, and the art of war.[^9] He also developed an early talent for architecture and design. At age sixteen, he impressed his father by building quarters within Emperor Babur's Kabul fort and redesigning buildings within the Agra fort, demonstrating the aesthetic sensibility that would later define his reign.[^10]

In 1607, at the age of fifteen, Khurram became engaged to Arjumand Banu Begum, a Persian noblewoman who would later be known as Mumtaz Mahal.[^11] She was the daughter of Abu'l-Hasan Asaf Khan, a wealthy Persian noble who held high office in the Mughal Empire, and the niece of Empress Nur Jahan, who would later become Jahangir's most powerful wife.[^12] Due to astrological considerations and court politics, the prince would have to wait five years before marrying her in 1612.[^13]

The young prince's mother, Jagat Gosain, died in April 1619, an event that deeply affected him. According to Jahangir's own accounts, Khurram was inconsolable and mourned for 21 days, attending no public meetings and subsisting on simple vegetarian meals during this period of grief.[^14]

## Rise to Power

Shah Jahan's path to the imperial throne was marked by both military triumphs and political rebellion. His rise began with remarkable military successes that earned him his imperial title and established his reputation as a capable commander.[^15]

### Military Achievements Under Jahangir

In 1614, Prince Khurram participated in military campaigns against the Sisodia Rajputs of Mewar and the rebel Lodi nobles of the Deccan.[^16] His greatest early success came in 1617 when his military victories against the Lodi in the Deccan secured the southern border of the Mughal Empire.[^17] Impressed by these achievements, Emperor Jahangir bestowed upon him the prestigious title "Shah Jahan Bahadur" (Lord of the World), signaling his favor and effectively designating him as heir apparent.[^18]

### The Rebellion (1622-1626)

However, Shah Jahan's position as heir was not secure. After the marriage of Prince Shahryar (another of Jahangir's sons) to Ladli Begum, daughter of Empress Nur Jahan in 1620, there was a sudden fall in the fortunes of Prince Khurram.[^19] Nur Jahan, the most powerful woman at court, now backed the claims of her son-in-law Shahryar, creating a dangerous rival faction.[^20]

The immediate cause of rebellion came in 1622 when Shah Jahan refused to proceed to Kandahar, which had been besieged by the Persians.[^21] Prince Khurram feared that in his absence, Nur Jahan would poison his father against him and convince Jahangir to name Shahryar as heir in his place.[^22] This fear brought Prince Khurram to rebel against his father rather than fight against the Persians.

For approximately three to four years, he and his troops were chased around the states of the empire.[^23] He first proceeded to the Deccan, was then driven to Bengal, but unable to maintain his control, he returned to the Deccan and for a few years wandered about seeking the alliance of Malik Ambar and other regional powers.[^24] He was defeated at Bilochpur in March 1623 by Jahangir's trusted general Mahabat Khan.[^25]

In 1626, Khurram surrendered without conditions and was reconciled with his father.[^26] As part of the agreement, two of his sons—Dara Shikoh and Aurangzeb—were sent to Jahangir's court as hostages, and a tract in the Deccan was assigned for his expenses.[^27] Although forgiven, this rebellion had lasted approximately four years and caused significant disruption to the Mughal Empire.

### Succession Struggle and Coronation

When Emperor Jahangir died in October 1627, a succession struggle immediately erupted between Khurram and his half-brother Shahryar Mirza.[^28] Shah Jahan, supported by powerful nobles including Asaf Khan (his father-in-law), moved swiftly to secure the throne.[^29] He defeated Shahryar and crowned himself emperor in the Agra Fort in February 1628, four months after Jahangir's death.[^30]

To secure his position, Shah Jahan followed the brutal Mughal tradition of eliminating potential rivals. He ordered the execution of all male relatives who could pose a threat to his rule, including his brothers, cousins, and nephews.[^31] This ruthless consolidation of power, while shocking to modern sensibilities, was considered necessary statecraft in the context of Mughal succession politics.

## Major Achievements

Shah Jahan's thirty-year reign (1628-1658) represents the pinnacle of Mughal power and cultural achievement. His accomplishments span architecture, military expansion, administrative reform, and economic prosperity.[^32]

### Architectural Masterpieces

Shah Jahan's greatest legacy lies in architecture. His reign ushered in what historians call the "Golden Age of Mughal Architecture," characterized by the extensive use of white marble, intricate inlay work, and perfect proportions.[^33]

**The Taj Mahal**: Following the death of his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal in 1631, Shah Jahan commissioned the Taj Mahal as her mausoleum.[^34] Construction began in 1632 under the direction of chief architect Ustad Ahmad Lahori, employing over twenty thousand workers and utilizing exquisite materials including white marble and various gemstones.[^35] The mausoleum was completed in 1648, but work continued on other phases of the project for another five years, with the entire complex believed to have been completed in 1653.[^36] The Taj Mahal is considered the greatest architectural achievement in the whole range of Indo-Islamic architecture and remains one of the world's most recognizable monuments.[^37]

**Shahjahanabad and the Red Fort**: In 1638, Shah Jahan ordered his chief architect to construct a new imperial capital in Delhi called Shahjahanabad (now known as Old Delhi).[^38] At its core stood the Red Fort, which served as the seat of Mughal government and contained the famous Peacock Throne in its Diwan-i-Khas (Hall of Private Audiences).[^39] The Red Fort's architecture represented the sumptuous grandeur of an expanding empire.[^40]

**Jama Masjid**: Shah Jahan built the Jama Masjid (1650-56), the largest mosque in all of India at the time, as part of his new capital complex.[^41] This congregational mosque showcased his commitment to Islamic architecture on a monumental scale.[^42]

**Other Major Works**: His architectural patronage extended far beyond these famous monuments. Among his other constructions were the Wazir Khan Mosque, the Moti Masjid, the Shalimar Gardens, sections of the Lahore Fort, and the Mahabat Khan Mosque in Peshawar.[^43] Shah Jahan introduced the broad use of white marble instead of red sandstone in building construction, fundamentally changing Mughal architectural aesthetics.[^44]

**The Peacock Throne**: Commissioned in the early 17th century, the Peacock Throne took seven years to fabricate and became the focal point of the monarchy.[^45] Made of gold and covered with gems, it symbolized the unprecedented wealth and power of Shah Jahan's court.[^46]

### Military Campaigns and Territorial Expansion

Shah Jahan pursued an aggressive policy of territorial expansion, particularly in the Deccan and northwestern frontiers.[^47]

**Deccan Conquests**: Shah Jahan's Deccan policy continued the traditional expansion followed by Akbar and Jahangir.[^48] In 1636-37, his forces captured Ahmadnagar, bringing an end to its independence and incorporating it into the Mughal Empire, partly due to the death of the formidable Malik Ambar in 1626.[^49] By 1636, Bijapur and Golconda were obliged to accept a peace that endured for two decades and become tributaries.[^50] Shah Jahan established four Mughal provinces in the Deccan: Khandesh, Berar, Telangana, and Daulatabad, placing them under the command of his son Aurangzeb.[^51]

**Kandahar Campaigns**: In 1638, Shah Jahan's forces captured the city of Kandahar from the Safavids.[^52] However, the Persians led by Abbas II recaptured it in 1649.[^53] Shah Jahan made great attempts to recover Kandahar, sending three expeditions in 1649, 1652, and 1653, but all failed before the superior military preparations and skill of the Persians.[^54] The loss of Kandahar represented a significant strategic setback for the Mughal Empire.[^55]

**Central Asian Ambitions**: Between 1639 and 1647, Shah Jahan launched lengthy campaigns on the northwest frontier to reclaim ancestral lands. In 1646, Mughal forces occupied Badakhshan and Balkh, though these campaigns ultimately failed to secure permanent control.[^56]

### Administrative and Economic Reforms

Shah Jahan introduced significant administrative reforms that strengthened central control and improved governance.[^57]

**Administrative Restructuring**: He introduced a new administrative unit called "chakla," which was a cluster of parganas (districts), while the sarkar remained an established administrative division.[^58] The Faujdar served as the executive head of the sarkar, maintaining law and order and suppressing rebellions.[^59]

**Revenue System**: During his reign, the revenue system was further rationalized and streamlined. The Amil was responsible for assessment of revenue, while the Shiqdar was in charge of revenue collection and law and order.[^60] Due to his measures in financial and commercial fields, it was a period of general stability with centralized administration and systematized court affairs.[^61]

**Economic Prosperity**: Shah Jahan's economic strategies included significant taxation reforms and promotion of trade, which strengthened the empire's wealth.[^62] He encouraged the establishment of new trade routes and the construction of ports and warehouses to facilitate commerce.[^63] Under his rule, India became one of the world's largest economies, with some economic historians stating that India's wealth at that time even surpassed China.[^64] He strengthened trade ties with Persians, Ottomans, and Europeans, increasing the empire's commercial reach.[^65]

**Infrastructure Development**: Shah Jahan invested heavily in infrastructure, ensuring strong roads and bridges so traders could move goods efficiently.[^66] He also supported agricultural development, helping farmers with irrigation systems.[^67]

**Foreign Trade**: The English East India Company, which had established its first factory in India during Jahangir's reign, continued to expand during Shah Jahan's rule. He granted the company various privileges, including the right to trade freely and exemption from certain taxes and duties.[^68]

### Cultural and Artistic Patronage

Shah Jahan's reign was also a period of great literary activity, with the arts of painting and calligraphy flourishing at his court of great pomp and splendor.[^69]

**Manuscript Patronage**: While Shah Jahan maintained support for the arts, his primary focus was architecture rather than painting. The major manuscript commission of his reign was the Padshahnama, a lavishly illustrated history produced through the 1640s.[^70] Paintings from his reign were characterized by formal portraits and courtly scenes, replacing the more wide-ranging and personal subject matter favored under Jahangir.[^71]

**The Shah Jahan Album**: This spectacular album containing nature studies, portraits of the royal family and dignitaries, and fine examples of calligraphy offers a glimpse into courtly life and the diverse interests of its patrons.[^72]

**Literary Flourishing**: Poetry, literature, and music flourished under his patronage, with the emperor himself being an admirer and patron of the arts.[^73]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his architectural achievements, Shah Jahan's reign was marked by significant controversies that have shaped his historical reputation.[^74]

### Economic Burden and Wealth Concentration

The beauty of the Taj Mahal and the opulence of the Peacock Throne came at a severe price.[^75] His commitment to constant military expansion and lavish construction drained the imperial economy, with extravagant spending creating deep financial challenges.[^76] According to historical analysis, 62% of the total revenue of 220 million rupees of the empire was appropriated by only 665 individuals, making this period a golden age primarily for the king, princes, and wealthy elite rather than the common people.[^77]

### Military Failures

Despite some initial successes, Shah Jahan's major military ambitions all resulted in failure.[^78] The repeated unsuccessful campaigns to recapture Kandahar from the Persians (1649, 1652, 1653) wasted enormous resources and damaged imperial prestige.[^79] His Central Asian campaigns to occupy Badakhshan and Balkh ultimately failed to secure permanent territorial gains.[^80]

### Religious Policy Debates

Shah Jahan's religious policies remain contentious among historians. He is often characterized as an "orthodox Muslim" compared to his grandfather Akbar, who was liberal in his religious views.[^81] His reign saw a shift from liberal policies, with zeal for the revival of Islam witnessed in his preference for showing Islam as a dominant religion through grand mosques like Jama Masjid.[^82] This reflected the influence of Islamic revivalist movements like the Naqshbandi, particularly the teachings of Sheikh Ahmad Sirhindi.[^83]

However, recent scholarship has challenged this characterization. Historians argue that Shah Jahan's "orthodoxy" has been "largely misconstrued" due to inflated rhetoric of royal chronicles and colonial critics, as well as post-Partition prejudice.[^84] Despite his orthodox reputation, Shah Jahan continued tolerant policies toward his subjects in many respects.[^85] He never allowed orthodox elements to dominate completely, and the empire ran with support of heterogeneous nobility in which Hindus were given proper representation.[^86]

### Succession Brutality

To secure his throne in 1628, Shah Jahan ordered the execution of all male relatives who could pose a threat to his rule, including his brothers, cousins, and nephews.[^87] While such actions were common in Mughal succession struggles, they nevertheless represent a ruthless approach to power consolidation that has been criticized by historians.[^88]

### Allegations of Incest

Several accusations of an incestuous relationship between Shah Jahan and his daughter Jahanara were propagated by some contemporary European observers.[^89] François Bernier, a French physician, mentions rumors of such a relationship being propagated in the Mughal Court, though he did not claim to have witnessed it himself.[^90] However, Niccolao Manucci, a Venetian traveler, dismisses such accusations as gossip and "the talk of the Low People."[^91] Modern historians have dismissed these claims as unfounded gossip, as no credible witness or evidence has been produced.[^92] Historian K. S. Lal also dismisses such claims as rumors propagated by courtiers and mullahs.[^93]

### Rebellion Against His Father

Shah Jahan's four-year rebellion against his father Jahangir (1622-1626) caused significant disruption to the Mughal Empire and demonstrated his willingness to prioritize personal ambition over filial duty.[^94] This rebellion, though ultimately unsuccessful, revealed the ruthless calculation that would characterize his later political career.

## Personal Life

Shah Jahan's personal life was dominated by his profound love for Mumtaz Mahal and the devastating impact of her death, though he maintained a complex family structure typical of Mughal emperors.[^95]

### Marriage and Devotion to Mumtaz Mahal

Shah Jahan married Arjumand Banu Begum in 1612, and she became known as Mumtaz Mahal ("Chosen One of the Palace").[^96] According to the official court chronicler Qazwini, Shah Jahan's relationship with his other wives "had nothing more than the status of marriage," while "the intimacy, deep affection, attention and favor which His Majesty had for the Cradle of Excellence (Mumtaz) exceeded by a thousand times what he felt for any other."[^97]

Mumtaz Mahal was a cultured and educated woman, well-versed in Arabic and Persian, who could compose poems.[^98] She accompanied Shah Jahan on military campaigns, demonstrating the closeness of their relationship.[^99] Together they had fourteen children, though several died in infancy.[^100]

### The Tragedy of 1631

On 17 June 1631, while accompanying her husband during a campaign in the Deccan Plateau, Mumtaz Mahal died from postpartum hemorrhage in Burhanpur after a prolonged labor of around 30 hours while giving birth to her fourteenth child.[^101] Her body was temporarily buried at Burhanpur in a walled pleasure garden before being transported in a golden casket back to Agra in December 1631.[^102]

Contemporary historians noted that Shah Jahan was "paralyzed by grief" and experienced weeping fits.[^103] After her death, he went into secluded mourning for a year. When he appeared again, his hair had turned white, his back was bent, and his face worn.[^104] This profound grief inspired the construction of the Taj Mahal, taking 22 years to complete.[^105]

### Children and Family Dynamics

Shah Jahan had four sons who survived to adulthood, all born of Mumtaz Mahal: Dara Shikoh, Shah Shuja, Aurangzeb, and Murad Bakhsh.[^106] In 1633, Dara Shikoh was appointed Vali-ahad (heir-apparent) and was favored as successor by both his father and his elder sister, Princess Jahanara Begum.[^107]

Jahanara, Shah Jahan's eldest surviving daughter, held immense power and influence during her father's reign.[^108] After Mumtaz Mahal's death in 1631, the seventeen-year-old Jahanara was entrusted with the charge of the royal seal and conferred the title of Padshah Begum (First Lady) of the Mughal Empire.[^109] She has been described as "the most powerful woman in the empire" at the time.[^110]

### Personality and Character

Historical accounts of Shah Jahan's personality are contradictory. There is significant disagreement among sources, with some depicting him as a virtuous sovereign and others as cruel, treacherous, and unscrupulous.[^111] Contemporary evidence shows he had a deep capacity for emotional attachment, as demonstrated by his inconsolable grief at his mother's death in 1619 and his year-long mourning for Mumtaz Mahal.[^112]

Shah Jahan had a talent and taste for building, which manifested early in life.[^113] His court was characterized by extravagance and splendor due to his collection of jewels and love of luxury.[^114] Emperor Shah Jahan's court was unrivaled in its luxury, and he had a strong interest in the natural world and a taste for paintings, jewel-encrusted objects, textiles, and works of art.[^115]

### Imprisonment and Final Years

When Shah Jahan fell ill in September 1657, his sons began competing for the succession.[^116] After Aurangzeb emerged victorious from the war of succession (1658-1659), he declared his father incompetent to rule and imprisoned him in the Agra Fort from July 1658.[^117] Jahanara voluntarily shared her father's eight-year confinement and nursed him in his dotage.[^118]

During his imprisonment, Shah Jahan was stripped of power and had limited contact with the outside world.[^119] Some sources suggest he was imprisoned with a view of the Taj Mahal.[^120] He died on 22 January 1666 at age 74, after reciting the Kalima and verses from the Quran.[^121] He was laid to rest next to his wife in the Taj Mahal.[^122]

## Legacy

Shah Jahan's legacy remains complex and multifaceted, encompassing architectural genius, cultural patronage, and economic transformation, while also reflecting the costs of imperial ambition.[^123]

### Architectural Immortality

Shah Jahan left behind a grand legacy of structures and was one of the greatest patrons of Mughal architecture.[^124] His reign has been called the Golden Age of Mughals and represents a period when architectural achievement reached unprecedented heights.[^125] The Taj Mahal alone ensures his immortality, recognized universally as a masterpiece of human creativity and a UNESCO World Heritage Site.[^126]

His architectural innovations, particularly the introduction of white marble as the primary building material instead of red sandstone, fundamentally transformed Indo-Islamic architecture in ways still studied and admired today.[^127] The Red Fort, Jama Masjid, and numerous other monuments across the Indian subcontinent stand as testament to his aesthetic vision.[^128]

### Economic and Cultural Impact

Shah Jahan ruled over an empire that thrived economically and culturally.[^129] Under him, India became one of the world's largest economies, demonstrating the material success of his reign.[^130] His taste and patronage helped shape Indo-Islamic culture, influencing poetry, literature, music, painting, and calligraphy.[^131]

The period saw remarkable prosperity and cultural development, with artists enjoying royal patronage and creating works that continue to influence South Asian aesthetics.[^132] His court culture, characterized by unprecedented luxury and refinement, set standards for imperial magnificence that subsequent rulers attempted to emulate.[^133]

### Scholarly Reassessment

Modern scholarship has undertaken significant reassessment of Shah Jahan's reign. According to art historian Ebba Koch, when history and art history combine their findings, the reign of Shah Jahan emerges as a highly assertive and innovative phase when imperial authority assumed absolute and central power never before and never after reached in Mughal history.[^134] Shah Jahan claimed to be a renewer, a mujaddid whose rule ended an era of decline.[^135]

Koch emphasizes that the arts played a key role in the propagation of his imperial claims, and their aesthetic appeal should not induce us to underrate their historical significance.[^136] Imperial architecture was considered a reflection of the royal patron, and Shah Jahan's palaces, gardens, and tombs were physical visualizations of his imperial ideology.[^137]

### Complex Historical Memory

Shah Jahan's historical memory remains contested across different communities and scholarly traditions. In popular imagination, he is often remembered primarily as the romantic figure who built the Taj Mahal for his beloved wife.[^138] This narrative, while true, obscures the complexity of his political career, military campaigns, and the economic costs of his architectural ambitions.[^139]

The scholarly consensus recognizes Shah Jahan's unprecedented architectural achievements while also acknowledging the financial strain his projects placed on the empire and its people.[^140] His reign represents both the zenith of Mughal power and the beginning of fiscal challenges that would contribute to the empire's later decline.[^141]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Supporters and admirers of Shah Jahan emphasize his role as the greatest architectural patron in Indian history and a ruler who brought Mughal culture to its zenith.[^142]

**Architectural Genius**: Historians universally acknowledge that Shah Jahan's architectural patronage represents an unparalleled achievement in world history. Ebba Koch and other art historians have demonstrated how his buildings were not merely beautiful structures but sophisticated expressions of imperial ideology and political power.[^143] The technical and aesthetic accomplishment of monuments like the Taj Mahal continues to inspire scholars, artists, and visitors worldwide.[^144]

**Cultural Sophistication**: Shah Jahan's court represented the apex of Mughal cultural refinement. His patronage of poetry, literature, music, and the visual arts created an environment where creativity flourished.[^145] The Padshahnama and Shah Jahan Album demonstrate the high artistic standards maintained during his reign.[^146]

**Economic Prosperity**: Defenders note that under Shah Jahan, India achieved remarkable economic prosperity, becoming one of the world's wealthiest regions.[^147] His promotion of trade routes, support for infrastructure development, and rationalization of the revenue system contributed to this prosperity.[^148]

**Administrative Innovation**: Shah Jahan introduced important administrative reforms, including the chakla system and improvements to revenue collection, which strengthened central governance.[^149] His systematization of court affairs and centralized administration brought stability and efficiency.[^150]

**Reassessment of "Orthodoxy"**: Recent scholarship has challenged the traditional characterization of Shah Jahan as religiously intolerant. Scholars argue that his so-called orthodoxy has been misconstrued and that he maintained significant continuity with the tolerant policies of Akbar and Jahangir, ensuring Hindu representation in the nobility and avoiding complete domination by orthodox Islamic elements.[^151]

### Negative Views

Critics of Shah Jahan emphasize the costs of his reign, both human and economic, and question the sustainability of his policies.[^152]

**Economic Exploitation**: The most significant criticism concerns the enormous economic burden his architectural projects placed on the empire. The concentration of 62% of imperial revenue among only 665 individuals demonstrates that the "Golden Age" benefited primarily the elite while potentially impoverishing the broader population.[^153] The Taj Mahal and other monuments, while beautiful, required resources that some argue could have been better spent on public welfare.[^154]

**Military Incompetence**: Critics point to Shah Jahan's consistent military failures, particularly the repeated unsuccessful campaigns to recapture Kandahar and the failed Central Asian adventures.[^155] These campaigns wasted enormous resources without achieving strategic objectives, weakening the empire's position.[^156]

**Succession Brutality**: Shah Jahan's execution of numerous relatives to secure his throne, and his own eventual deposition and imprisonment by his son Aurangzeb, reveal the brutal and unstable nature of Mughal succession politics that his reign perpetuated rather than reformed.[^157]

**Religious Policy Controversies**: While recent scholarship has moderated earlier criticisms, some historians still maintain that Shah Jahan represented a turn toward greater Islamic orthodoxy compared to Akbar, citing his construction of grand mosques as symbols of Islamic dominance and the influence of conservative religious figures at his court.[^158]

**Sustainability Questions**: Some historians argue that Shah Jahan's extravagant spending and focus on monumental architecture rather than administrative or military modernization contributed to the Mughal Empire's later decline.[^159] The financial challenges created during his reign became more acute under his successors.[^160]

**Colonial and Moral Criticisms**: Some European contemporary observers and later colonial historians portrayed Shah Jahan as morally depraved, citing unsubstantiated rumors and emphasizing the harem system, though many of these accounts are now dismissed as biased or based on court gossip.[^161]

## Famous Quotes

> "If there is paradise on earth, it is this, it is this, it is this!" - Inscription above the inner corner arches of the Diwan-e-Khas (Hall of Private Audiences) in the Red Fort[^162]

This Persian couplet (Agar firdaus bar ru-ye zamin ast / Hamin ast-o hamin ast-o hamin ast) epitomizes Shah Jahan's vision of creating earthly paradise through architecture.

> "This is a good likeness of me in my fortieth year, the work of Bichitr." - Shah Jahan's inscription on his portrait by Bichitr, circa 1630[^163]

This rare personal inscription demonstrates Shah Jahan's attention to his royal image and his patronage of court painters.
