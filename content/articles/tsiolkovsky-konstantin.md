## Summary

Konstantin Eduardovich Tsiolkovsky was a Russian scientist and schoolteacher whose theoretical work helped lay the foundations of modern astronautics. He is best known for the rocket equation and for early proposals describing space travel using multi-stage rockets and liquid propellants.

## Early life and education

- Tsiolkovsky was born in 1857 in the Russian Empire.
- He experienced hearing loss in childhood, which affected his formal education and contributed to a largely self-directed approach to learning.
- He later worked as a teacher while pursuing scientific writing and research in his spare time.

## Contributions to rocketry and astronautics

- He developed a mathematical relationship between rocket velocity change, exhaust velocity, and mass ratio, widely known as the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation.
- His writings described the feasibility of spaceflight and emphasized that reaching orbit would require high-energy propulsion and careful mass management.
- He advocated concepts such as:
  - multi-stage rockets to improve performance,
  - liquid propellants as a practical high-energy option,
  - closed-cycle life support ideas for long-duration travel,
  - space stations and orbital habitats as stepping stones for exploration.

## Influence and legacy

- Although he did not build rockets himself, his theoretical framework influenced later Soviet and international work in rocketry.
- He is often grouped with other early spaceflight visionaries who helped shift space travel from speculation to engineering.
- Tsiolkovsky remains a prominent figure in the history of space science in Russia and worldwide.

## Notes

- In many histories of spaceflight, his role is characterized as primarily theoretical: developing equations and mission concepts that later engineers could implement.
