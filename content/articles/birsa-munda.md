## Summary

Indian tribal leader who led the Munda Rebellion against British colonial exploitation and missionary conversion in Bihar. He died in British custody at age 25 under suspicious circumstances.

## Additional Context

- Birsa Munda is remembered as a leader from the Munda Adivasi community in the Chotanagpur region.
- His movement emerged amid grievances over **land alienation**, exploitative labor arrangements, and cultural disruption under colonial administration and changing local power structures.

## Movement and Legacy (High Level)

- **Ulgulan (“The Great Tumult”):** the name often associated with the uprising linked to Birsa’s leadership.
- **Religious and social reform elements:** accounts describe a blend of political resistance with a reformist spiritual message.
- **Symbolic status:** in modern India he is frequently invoked as a symbol of tribal resistance and anti-colonial struggle.

## Why He Matters

- The rebellion is studied for how local communities responded to **economic extraction** and legal regimes that reshaped property and forest access.
- His death in custody helped cement a narrative of martyrdom and intensified later remembrance.

## Legacy in Public Memory

- Birsa Munda is commemorated in India through statues, memorials, and the naming of institutions.
- In scholarship and popular history, his uprising is framed as both a local land struggle and part of a wider anti-colonial arc.
- His story is also invoked in contemporary debates about indigenous rights, forests, and development.

- His remembrance also illustrates how anti-colonial figures can be reinterpreted in modern political and cultural contexts.
