## Summary

Iraqi politician who was convicted of bank fraud in Jordan for the collapse of the Petra Bank. He later provided false intelligence about Iraqi WMDs that was used to justify the 2003 US invasion of Iraq.

## Background

Ahmed Chalabi was an Iraqi politician and exile figure who became prominent in opposition politics during the era of Saddam Hussein. He later emerged as a controversial actor in the politics of Iraq’s post-2003 transition.

## Petra Bank Case

Chalabi was convicted in absentia in Jordan in connection with the collapse of Petra Bank. The case remained a longstanding part of his public reputation and was frequently referenced in reporting on his finances and political networks.

## Iraqi National Congress and U.S. Relations

Chalabi led the Iraqi National Congress (INC), an opposition group that sought to build support for regime change. In the run-up to the 2003 invasion, the INC was associated with the dissemination of claims about Iraqi weapons of mass destruction.

Multiple later investigations and journalistic accounts questioned the accuracy and sourcing of key intelligence claims that circulated at the time.

## Post-2003 Political Roles

After returning to Iraq, Chalabi held various political positions and was involved in major postwar policy debates, including de-Ba'athification and the restructuring of state institutions. His alliances shifted across Iraqi political blocs, reflecting the fragmentation of post-invasion politics.

## Legacy

Chalabi is often discussed as an example of the risks of politicized intelligence and exile-driven lobbying in major foreign policy decisions, as well as the complexities of legitimacy and governance in post-conflict state-building.
