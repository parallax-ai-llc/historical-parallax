## Summary

Kuroda Yoshitaka (黒田 孝高), better known as Kuroda Kanbei (黒田 官兵衛), was one of the most celebrated military strategists of Japan's turbulent Sengoku period.[^1] Born on December 22, 1546, in Himeji, he rose from a regional samurai family to become chief strategist to Toyotomi Hideyoshi, one of Japan's three great unifiers.[^2] Despite suffering a year of brutal imprisonment that left him with a permanent limp and blind in one eye, Kanbei's brilliant tactical mind helped shape the course of Japanese history.[^3] His genius was so feared that even his master Hideyoshi allegedly grew wary of his overwhelming intelligence and ambition.[^4] Kanbei died on March 20, 1604, after witnessing the dawn of the Edo period and the rise of Tokugawa power.[^5]

---

## Early Life and Background

Kuroda Yoshitaka was born on December 22, 1546, in Himeji as Mankichi (万吉), the eldest son of Kuroda Mototaka.[^6] The Kuroda clan originated from Ōmi Province, and Yoshitaka's grandfather Shigetaka had brought the family to Harima Province, establishing them at Gochaku Castle east of Himeji Castle.[^7] The family served as retainers to the more powerful Kodera clan, which controlled much of the strategically important Harima region during the mid-16th century.[^8]

Young Yoshitaka received an education befitting a warrior family, learning martial arts, military strategy, and the administrative skills necessary for governance.[^9] In 1567, at the age of 21, he assumed headship of the Kuroda family when his father Mototaka retired.[^10] That same year marked his military debut when he participated in the Siege of Inabayama Castle, supporting Oda Nobunaga's successful campaign against the Saitō clan of Mino Province.[^11]

The young clan leader demonstrated exceptional political acumen early in his career. In 1576, observing the meteoric rise of Oda Nobunaga and the inevitability of his expansion westward, Yoshitaka urged his overlord Kodera Masamoto to forge an alliance with the powerful Oda clan.[^12] This prescient strategic thinking would become Kanbei's hallmark throughout his career. When Toyotomi Hideyoshi, Nobunaga's brilliant general, led the Oda advance into the Chūgoku region in 1577, Yoshitaka persuaded the Kodera to surrender Himeji Castle to Hideyoshi, facilitating the Oda clan's campaign against the powerful Mōri clan.[^13]

This act of allegiance marked the beginning of one of the most significant strategic partnerships in Japanese history. Kanbei, together with the already renowned but sickly Takenaka Hanbei, became Hideyoshi's dual strategists.[^14] Folklore would later celebrate them as the two greatest tactical minds in Japan, with Hanbei known as "The All-knowing Hanbei" who traded wits with his friend Kanbei.[^15] The young Kuroda strategist had positioned himself at the center of Japan's unification, serving a master whose ambitions would reshape the nation.[^16]

## Rise to Power

Kuroda Kanbei's ascent to prominence was dramatically interrupted by a catastrophic event that would test his loyalty and nearly cost him his life. In 1577, Araki Murashige, a powerful vassal of Oda Nobunaga and lord of Arioka Castle in Settsu Province, formed a secret alliance with the Mōri clan and revolted against the Oda.[^17] This defection threatened the entire Oda campaign in the west, and Hideyoshi desperately needed to prevent other allies from following Araki's example.[^18]

In 1578, Yoshitaka volunteered to enter Arioka Castle to negotiate with Araki, leveraging their previous acquaintance to persuade him to abandon his rebellion.[^19] It was a fatal miscalculation. Araki, rather than listening to reason, imprisoned Yoshitaka in the castle's underground gaols.[^20] The conditions were horrific—Kanbei was confined in a space with insufficient room to properly sleep or sit, sustained only by the sight of a wisteria vine growing through his cell.[^21]

The consequences were immediate and severe. Oda Nobunaga, believing that Yoshitaka had defected to Araki's side, flew into a rage and ordered the execution of Kanbei's nine-year-old son, Shōjumaru (later known as Kuroda Nagamasa).[^22] The boy was only saved through the quick thinking and courage of Takenaka Hanbei, who secretly hid the child in the Takenaka household, falsely reporting that the execution had been carried out.[^23] This act of deception and loyalty would save the Kuroda lineage and cement an eternal debt between the two great strategists.

Kanbei suffered in darkness for nearly a year. When the Siege of Itami finally concluded in 1579, a weakened and broken Yoshitaka was rescued from the rubble of Arioka Castle.[^24] The imprisonment had devastated his body—he emerged with a permanent leg disorder that left him with a pronounced limp for the rest of his life, and he had lost the sight in one eye.[^25] Despite these profound disabilities, his mind remained as sharp as ever, perhaps even honed by the ordeal of isolation and suffering.[^26]

Following his rescue, Kanbei became Hideyoshi's indispensable strategist, especially after the death of Takenaka Hanbei, who finally succumbed to his long illness.[^27] Yoshitaka succeeded Hanbei as Hideyoshi's chief strategist and adviser, a position of immense influence and responsibility.[^28] In 1582, during the Siege of Bitchū-Takamatsu Castle against the Mōri clan, Kanbei devised one of the most ingenious tactics in Japanese military history—the mizuzeme or water attack.[^29]

The castle, protected by natural marshlands, seemed impregnable to conventional assault. Kanbei proposed diverting the rain-swollen waters of the Ashimori River to flood the castle's surroundings, turning its defensive advantage into a fatal vulnerability.[^30] In only twelve days, under Kanbei's direction, Hideyoshi's forces constructed a massive earthwork embankment some four kilometers in length.[^31] When the rainy season arrived in June, the torrential downpours swelled the river, and Takamatsu Castle became a solitary island surrounded by an artificial lake.[^32] The garrison's supply lines were severed, and relief forces could not reach them.[^33] This brilliant application of Chinese strategic principles to Japanese terrain demonstrated Kanbei's mastery of military theory and adaptation.[^34]

The siege was still ongoing when news arrived that would change everything: Oda Nobunaga had been assassinated at Honnōji by the traitor Akechi Mitsuhide.[^35] In this moment of crisis, Kanbei allegedly told Hideyoshi, "Now's your chance, don't hesitate and make your move."[^36] This counsel revealed not just Kanbei's strategic genius but his understanding that Nobunaga's death created a power vacuum that Hideyoshi could fill.[^37] According to later accounts, from that day forward, Hideyoshi never fully trusted Kanbei again, recognizing the dangerous ambition lurking behind the strategist's advice.[^38]

## Major Achievements

Kuroda Kanbei's military and political achievements fundamentally shaped the unification of Japan during one of its most turbulent periods. After the water siege of Takamatsu Castle, Kanbei participated in the Battle of Yamazaki in 1582, where Hideyoshi avenged Oda Nobunaga's death by defeating the traitor Akechi Mitsuhide.[^39] This victory established Hideyoshi as Nobunaga's legitimate successor and launched him on the path to supreme power.[^40]

Throughout the 1580s, Kanbei served as Hideyoshi's shadow strategist, helping him reign over the entire country through brilliant planning and tactical counsel.[^41] His involvement extended beyond military campaigns to major architectural and engineering projects that would define Japan's castle age. Kanbei contributed to the design and construction of several of Japan's most important fortifications, including Himeji Castle, Nagoya Castle, Osaka Castle, and Hiroshima Castle under the reign of the Toyotomi lords.[^42] At Himeji Castle specifically, piled stone walls built by Kuroda Kanbei remain among the oldest surviving structures, dating from the late 16th century.[^43] In 1580, Yoshitaka had presented Himeji Castle to Hideyoshi, and in 1581 Hideyoshi significantly expanded it, building a three-story keep.[^44] A tile decorated with a cross, discovered at Himeji, is believed connected to Kanbei's Christian period, a fascinating artifact of his spiritual journey.[^45]

In 1587, following Hideyoshi's successful Kyushu campaign, Yoshitaka received significant recognition for his distinguished service. He was granted lordship of six counties in Buzen Province with a domain valued at approximately 123,000 to 125,000 koku centered on Nakatsu.[^46] Kanbei built Nakatsu Castle at the strategic mouth of the Nakatsu River, creating the oldest modern water castle in Kyushu that utilized seawater drawn into its moats for defense.[^47] This period marked the height of Kanbei's temporal power as a daimyō, though his influence as a strategist remained even more valuable than his landholdings.[^48]

During this same Kyushu campaign, Kanbei encountered the thriving Christian communities of the region and, under the influence of the Christian daimyō Takayama Ukon, chose to convert to Christianity at age 38.[^49] He was baptized with the name Don Simeon (Dom Simeão), as recorded in letters by the Jesuit missionary Luis Frois.[^50] This spiritual transformation reflected Kanbei's intellectual openness and his complex engagement with the cultural currents transforming Japan through contact with European civilization.[^51]

However, the religious landscape shifted dramatically in 1587 when Hideyoshi issued his famous edict expelling foreign missionaries and ordering all Christian samurai to abandon their faith.[^52] Faced with this ultimatum, Yoshitaka gave up his adopted religion and assumed a monk's habit, calling himself Josui (如水)—a name that some scholars believe derived from "Josué," the Portuguese version of "Joshua," suggesting his Christian faith left a lasting mark even as he officially renounced it.[^53] In May 1589, Kanbei formally retired from active leadership, handing over headship of the Kuroda family to his eldest son Nagamasa, though he remained an influential adviser.[^54]

Kanbei's most ambitious campaign came in 1600 during the great crisis that would determine Japan's future following Hideyoshi's death in 1598.[^55] When conflict erupted between Ishida Mitsunari and Tokugawa Ieyasu, the Kuroda clan strategically divided their forces: Nagamasa joined Ieyasu's army for the main campaign, while Yoshitaka led forces in a stunning Kyushu campaign.[^56] Before the conflict began, Yoshitaka and Nagamasa had made contact with Ii Naomasa, a powerful Tokugawa general, forming a pact of alliance that helped sway other military commanders to support the Tokugawa cause.[^57]

In Kyushu, the elderly strategist recruited farmers and ronin from Buzen and surrounding territories to raise an army, then proceeded to conquer territory with lightning speed and skill.[^58] He defeated Ōtomo Yoshimune at the Battle of Ishigakibaru and united with Katō Kiyomasa to conquer several castles affiliated with the Western forces in Bungo and Chikuzen provinces.[^59] At the campaign's height, Yoshitaka had momentarily conquered seven of Kyushu's provinces and was preparing to invade the lands of the powerful Shimazu clan when news arrived of Tokugawa Ieyasu's decisive victory at the Battle of Sekigahara.[^60]

Some historians theorize that Yoshitaka's ultimate objective was not merely to support Tokugawa but to conquer all of Kyushu for himself during the confusion of the succession crisis, then challenge Ieyasu for supremacy by leading Kyushu's military might eastward.[^61] Whether this was genuine ambition or strategic posturing remains debated, but the fact that such theories emerged speaks to Kanbei's fearsome reputation as a man capable of seizing supreme power.[^62] In recognition of the Kuroda clan's crucial support, Ieyasu transferred Nagamasa to the much larger and more prestigious Fukuoka domain, dramatically increasing their holdings and influence.[^63]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his brilliant achievements, Kuroda Kanbei's career was marked by significant controversies that reveal the darker complexities of his character and the dangerous political environment he navigated. The most persistent criticism centers on the deep suspicion and fear he aroused in the very leaders he served, particularly Toyotomi Hideyoshi.[^64]

It is widely suggested that Yoshitaka was feared by Hideyoshi despite his attempts to hide his intelligence and influence.[^65] Hideyoshi's fear allegedly stemmed from his overwhelming debt to Yoshitaka, who had helped him reign over the entire country as his shadow strategist.[^66] The turning point came when Kanbei advised Hideyoshi to seize power immediately after Oda Nobunaga's assassination at Honnōji. While this counsel proved strategically brilliant, it also revealed to Hideyoshi the dangerous ambition and political acumen of his adviser.[^67] From that moment, Hideyoshi never fully trusted him again and gradually forced Kanbei away from the center of power, eventually relegating him to distant Kyushu.[^68]

This distrust extended beyond Hideyoshi. Although Yoshitaka supposedly did not harbor personal ambitions for supreme rule, the Three Unifiers—Oda Nobunaga, Toyotomi Hideyoshi, and Tokugawa Ieyasu—all feared Yoshitaka as the man who could easily take the land for his own if he chose.[^69] Such universal wariness from Japan's most powerful leaders suggests that Kanbei's intelligence and capability were so overwhelming that they became threatening rather than merely valuable.[^70]

The 1578 imprisonment incident, while demonstrating Kanbei's loyalty, also reveals a significant failure of judgment. His diplomatic mission to Araki Murashige failed catastrophically—not only did he fail to prevent the defection, but he fell into a trap that nearly destroyed his family and convinced Oda Nobunaga of his treachery.[^71] While Kanbei's eventual rescue vindicated him, the incident showed that even the brilliant strategist could miscalculate human nature and political intentions.[^72] His son's near-execution, though prevented by Takenaka Hanbei's deception, represented a terrible consequence of Kanbei's failed mission.[^73]

Kanbei's relationship with other military leaders was also fraught with conflict. During the Japanese invasions of Korea (1592–1598), Yoshitaka and his son Nagamasa bore a grudge toward Ishida Mitsunari due to personal conflicts over alleged mismanagement of the campaign.[^74] Ishida had accused Kobayakawa Hideaki of incompetence, a charge that saw Hideaki brought home and indirectly besmirched Yoshitaka's honor.[^75] Additionally, Mitsunari had made disparaging comments about both Yoshitaka and Nagamasa, creating lasting enmity.[^76] This personal animosity contributed to the Kuroda clan's decisive support for Tokugawa Ieyasu against Mitsunari at Sekigahara, suggesting that Kanbei's political choices were influenced by personal grievances as well as strategic calculation.[^77]

Critics have questioned Kanbei's loyalty and painted him as dark-hearted and overly ambitious.[^78] While defenders argue there is no concrete evidence for these accusations, the persistence of such characterizations indicates that Kanbei's reputation was ambiguous even among contemporaries.[^79] His religious conversions—first to Christianity and then his apparent renunciation under political pressure—have also drawn criticism as evidence of opportunism rather than genuine spiritual conviction.[^80] The fact that he adopted the Buddhist name "Josui" but possibly derived it from the Portuguese "Josué" (Joshua) suggests a complicated relationship with faith that prioritized political survival over religious principle.[^81]

Notably, Kanbei chose not to personally participate in Hideyoshi's Korean invasions from 1591 to 1598, remaining at home while his son Nagamasa commanded forces in Korea.[^82] Whether this was due to age, health considerations from his disabilities, political calculation, or disagreement with the campaign remains unclear, but his absence from this major military undertaking is conspicuous given his role as chief strategist.[^83]

## Personal Life

Kuroda Kanbei's personal life reveals a man of unusual fidelity and principled character despite the ruthless political environment he inhabited. His wife was Kushihashiteru (櫛橋光), whose name contained the character for "light."[^84] In a remarkable departure from the customs of his era, it is said that Yoshitaka, unusually for a daimyō of that period, did not have a side chamber or concubines, suggesting he remained faithful to his wife throughout his life.[^85] This fidelity was highly uncommon among the warrior elite of the Sengoku period, where maintaining multiple consorts was standard practice for both political alliances and personal pleasure.[^86]

The couple had several children according to family records, including sons named Nagamasa, Kumanoske (熊之助), and Kazushige (一成).[^87] Nagamasa, whose childhood name was Shojumaru (松寿丸), was the eldest son and eventual successor to the Kuroda clan.[^88] The near-execution of young Shojumaru when he was only nine years old, ordered by Oda Nobunaga during Kanbei's imprisonment, must have been a traumatic experience for the family.[^89] The boy's rescue by Takenaka Hanbei created a profound bond between the Kuroda and Takenaka families that transcended typical political alliances.[^90]

Kanbei's physical appearance in his later years was marked by his disabilities. The permanent limp from his year-long imprisonment in cramped conditions made him easily recognizable, as did his blindness in one eye.[^91] Yet these physical limitations never diminished his mental acuity or his reputation. Indeed, they may have enhanced his mystique as a man who had endured extraordinary suffering yet emerged with his strategic genius intact.[^92]

His personality was complex and multifaceted. Historical accounts describe him as possessing quick wit, bravery, and inspiring loyalty among his warriors.[^93] He was noted for his decency of character and unwavering loyalty to Hideyoshi, even as that loyalty was met with suspicion.[^94] At the same time, he was crafty and never hesitated to advance the cause of his family, though his frankness on that score made him all the more colorful and endearing to some observers.[^95] This combination of loyalty, cunning, and family devotion created a personality that contemporaries found both admirable and unsettling.[^96]

Kanbei possessed what sources describe as a coldly brilliant mind, making him one of the clearest thinkers of the Sengoku era.[^97] This intellectual capacity, however, became as much a burden as a gift, as it aroused fear and suspicion in those he served.[^98] His attempts to downplay his intelligence and influence were apparently unsuccessful, as Hideyoshi remained convinced that his strategist might eventually overthrow him.[^99]

In his final years, having officially retired in 1589 but remaining an influential adviser, Kanbei adopted a monk's appearance and the name Josui.[^100] His last words reportedly reflected his philosophical approach to life: "Do not try to curry favor with others and do not covet wealth."[^101] This counsel emphasized frugality and independence, values that Kanbei had evidently cultivated despite his position at the center of power politics.[^102] He died on March 20, 1604 (though some sources indicate April 19), having witnessed the rise of the Tokugawa shogunate and the peaceful transition his strategic efforts had helped create.[^103]

## Legacy

Kuroda Kanbei remains one of the most celebrated strategists in Japanese history, and his legacy extends far beyond his military victories.[^104] The Kuroda Clan is remembered in Japanese history for its military skill, remarkable loyalty, and strategic ability during the Sengoku period.[^105] Kanbei's involvement in pivotal events significantly influenced the course of Japanese history, helping to shape the unification process that ended centuries of civil war.[^106]

In Fukuoka, where the Kuroda clan established their major domain after Sekigahara, their influence is still felt in the history and culture of the region.[^107] Fukuoka Castle and other architectural legacies stand as monuments to Kuroda power and vision.[^108] The family's transformation from regional retainers in Harima to major daimyō controlling valuable territories in Kyushu represents one of the great success stories of the Sengoku period.[^109]

Kanbei's reputation as a brilliant strategist has inspired countless retellings in Japanese popular culture. In 2014, NHK produced "Gunshi Kanbee," a historical drama that brought his story to contemporary audiences.[^110] He appears as a character in numerous video games, novels, and films that explore the Sengoku period, usually portrayed as a genius tactician whose intelligence both served and threatened the powerful.[^111]

The mizuzeme water attack at Takamatsu Castle is studied in military academies as an example of innovative tactical thinking—adapting classical Chinese strategies to Japanese terrain and circumstances.[^112] This siege demonstrated that Kanbei was not merely an armchair theorist but a practical innovator capable of executing complex engineering projects under battlefield conditions.[^113] The four-kilometer embankment constructed in twelve days remains a testament to his organizational abilities and the loyalty he commanded from troops.[^114]

Career lessons have even been drawn from Kanbei's life for modern business leaders, emphasizing his strategic thinking, adaptability in the face of setbacks, and ability to build loyal teams.[^115] The fact that he transformed personal disaster—the imprisonment that left him disabled—into continued success resonates with contemporary narratives about resilience and perseverance.[^116]

Tourism focused on Kuroda Kanbei has developed in regions associated with his life, particularly around Himeji Castle and in the Fukuoka area.[^117] Historical sites connected to his activities draw visitors interested in the Sengoku period and the great personalities who shaped it.[^118] The stone walls he constructed at Himeji Castle are specifically marked as significant historical remnants, connecting modern visitors to his legacy.[^119]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholarly assessments consistently recognize Kuroda Kanbei as a gifted strategist and a man of considerable charisma, noted for his decency of character and loyalty to Hideyoshi.[^120] His strategic acumen is acknowledged as central to Toyotomi Hideyoshi's rise and the successful unification campaigns of the late 16th century.[^121] Military historians praise his innovative thinking, particularly the water attack at Takamatsu Castle, which demonstrated mastery of both classical military theory and practical application.[^122]

Kanbei's relationship with Takenaka Hanbei is celebrated as an ideal of friendly competition and mutual respect between great minds.[^123] Folklore states that he was paired with "The All-knowing Hanbei," and together they represented the intellectual foundation of Hideyoshi's military success.[^124] Their partnership is often held up as an example of how talented individuals can work together effectively despite potentially competing for the same position of influence.[^125]

His personal virtues receive particular praise—his unusual fidelity to his wife, his lack of concubines, and his devotion to family distinguish him from many contemporaries.[^126] His last words counseling against seeking favor and coveting wealth suggest a philosophical depth and self-awareness rare among the ambitious warriors of his era.[^127] These qualities make him a sympathetic figure despite his fearsome reputation as a strategist.[^128]

Defenders emphasize that accusations of disloyalty and dark ambition lack concrete evidence and represent more the fears of powerful men than actual betrayals.[^129] Kanbei's service to Hideyoshi was consistent and effective throughout his career, and he never actually rebelled or acted against his lord's interests.[^130] His advice, while sometimes politically astute to the point of discomfort, always served Hideyoshi's advancement.[^131]

The Kuroda family's lasting influence in Fukuoka and their successful navigation of the transition from Toyotomi to Tokugawa power demonstrate Kanbei's political wisdom benefited not just his immediate master but his family's long-term interests.[^132] This strategic foresight extended beyond individual battles to multi-generational planning, marking him as a truly exceptional leader.[^133]

### Negative Views

Critics point to the persistent fear and distrust that Kanbei aroused in multiple leaders as evidence that his ambition was genuinely threatening.[^134] The fact that all Three Unifiers—Nobunaga, Hideyoshi, and Ieyasu—allegedly feared him suggests that contemporaries recognized something dangerous in his character beyond mere competence.[^135] Hideyoshi's gradual distancing of Kanbei from central power and his relegation to Kyushu can be interpreted as a strategic containment of a potentially dangerous subordinate.[^136]

The theory that Kanbei attempted to conquer all of Kyushu for himself during the Sekigahara crisis, though debated, reflects historical suspicions about his ultimate ambitions.[^137] The speed and effectiveness of his campaign in 1600—conquering seven provinces before being forced to halt—suggests military capability that could indeed have challenged Tokugawa Ieyasu if circumstances had been different.[^138] Some historians see this episode as revealing Kanbei's true ambitions, momentarily unconstrained by the presence of a powerful overlord.[^139]

His religious conversions are viewed skeptically by some scholars as evidence of opportunism rather than genuine conviction.[^140] Converting to Christianity when advantageous among Kyushu's Christian daimyō, then abandoning the faith when Hideyoshi banned it, suggests that political calculation governed his spiritual choices.[^141] Even his Buddhist name "Josui," possibly derived from the Portuguese "Josué," indicates an ambiguous relationship with religious identity that prioritized survival and advancement.[^142]

The catastrophic failure of his diplomatic mission to Araki Murashige, resulting in year-long imprisonment and nearly causing his son's execution, demonstrates that Kanbei's judgment was not infallible.[^143] Critics argue that a truly wise strategist would have anticipated the possibility of betrayal and taken precautions, rather than walking into an obvious trap.[^144] The incident reveals potential blind spots in his assessment of human character and political loyalty.[^145]

His decision not to participate in the Korean invasions, while his son commanded forces there, raises questions about his commitment to Hideyoshi's vision.[^146] Whether this absence represented political disagreement, personal cowardice, or mere pragmatism about age and disability remains unclear, but it stands in contrast to his earlier active military involvement.[^147]

The grudges Kanbei harbored against rivals like Ishida Mitsunari, which influenced major political decisions including support for Tokugawa Ieyasu at Sekigahara, suggest that personal animosity sometimes trumped strategic objectivity in his decision-making.[^148] Critics see this as evidence that the supposedly rational strategist was as subject to human emotion and petty conflict as any other warrior.[^149]

Ultimately, negative assessments portray Kanbei as an example of how exceptional intelligence and capability can be as much a curse as a blessing in hierarchical feudal systems, creating suspicion and limiting advancement despite genuine loyalty and service.[^150]

## Famous Quotes

> "Now's your chance, don't hesitate and make your move." 

— Kuroda Kanbei advising Toyotomi Hideyoshi immediately after learning of Oda Nobunaga's assassination at Honnōji in 1582. This counsel, though strategically brilliant, reportedly caused Hideyoshi to distrust Kanbei's ambitions from that moment forward.[^166]

> "Do not try to curry favor with others and do not covet wealth."

— Kanbei's last words, reflecting his philosophical approach emphasizing frugality, independence, and integrity over political maneuvering and material gain.[^167]

> "People may get craftier, but their essential nature doesn't change."

— A reflection on human character that demonstrates Kanbei's understanding of psychology and his belief in fundamental human traits that persist despite superficial changes.[^168]

> "My life and my blade belong to the Toyotomi."

— Expressing his loyalty to Toyotomi Hideyoshi and the regime he served, despite the suspicions and distrust his intelligence aroused.[^169]
