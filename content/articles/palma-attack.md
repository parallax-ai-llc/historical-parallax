## Summary

ISIS-linked militants attacked the coastal town of Palma in Cabo Delgado killing dozens and disrupting a $20 billion gas project. The attack involved beheadings and destruction of infrastructure.

## Background

The attack occurred amid a wider insurgency in Mozambique’s Cabo Delgado province, a conflict that combined local grievances, recruitment networks, and the strategic importance of major natural-gas discoveries.

Palma was significant not only as a district town but also because of its proximity to large liquefied natural gas (LNG) projects that had attracted international investment.

## What Happened

High-level features reported in summaries of the event include:

- a coordinated assault on the town involving multiple entry points
- killings of civilians and security personnel, alongside reports of abductions
- destruction of buildings and disruption of transport and communications

The violence triggered chaotic evacuations, including by road and by sea, as residents and foreign workers attempted to flee.

## Impact on the LNG Project

The assault directly affected timelines and security assumptions for the Cabo Delgado LNG development. In the immediate aftermath, operators suspended activities and declared force majeure, reflecting how security risks can halt capital-intensive energy projects.

## Humanitarian and Security Aftermath

The Palma attack added to an already severe displacement crisis in the region. It also accelerated international attention to Mozambique’s counterinsurgency efforts and contributed to subsequent security assistance, including regional deployments.

## Significance

The event is often cited as a case study in how insurgent violence can intersect with extractive-industry megaprojects: the symbolic value of targeting a high-profile investment can be as consequential as the direct tactical gains.
