## Summary

Amelia Earhart (July 24, 1897 – disappeared July 2, 1937) was an American aviation pioneer and author who became one of the most celebrated figures in the history of flight. She was the first woman to fly solo across the Atlantic Ocean and set numerous other altitude and distance records throughout her career. Her daring exploits and outspoken advocacy for women in aviation made her a cultural icon during the 1920s and 1930s.

Beyond her flying achievements, Earhart was a vocal feminist who challenged gender barriers in a male-dominated field. She helped found The Ninety-Nines, an organization for female pilots, and worked as a visiting faculty member at Purdue University. Her mysterious disappearance over the central Pacific Ocean during an attempted circumnavigation of the globe in 1937 cemented her legendary status and has captivated the public imagination for nearly a century.

## Early Life & Background

Amelia Mary Earhart was born on July 24, 1897, in Atchison, Kansas, to Samuel and Amy Earhart. She spent much of her childhood with her maternal grandparents in Atchison while her father struggled with alcoholism and career instability. Despite family difficulties, she was an intellectually curious and adventurous child, keeping a scrapbook of newspaper clippings about women succeeding in male-dominated professions.

After the family reunited in Des Moines, Iowa, Earhart attended multiple high schools as the family relocated frequently. She graduated from Hyde Park High School in Chicago in 1915. During World War I, she trained as a nurse's aide with the Red Cross and served at a military hospital in Toronto, where she was first exposed to military aircraft. This experience sparked her interest in flying. She briefly attended Columbia University in 1919 but left to rejoin her parents in California, where her aviation journey truly began.

## Rise to Prominence

In December 1920, Earhart took her first airplane ride at an airfield in Long Beach, California, and was immediately hooked. She began taking flying lessons from aviator Anita "Neta" Snook and purchased her first aircraft, a Kinner Airster biplane, in 1921. By 1922 she had set her first women's altitude record at 14,000 feet.

Her international breakthrough came in 1928 when she became the first woman to cross the Atlantic by air — as a passenger aboard the Fokker trimotor Friendship. The flight brought her global fame, though she later expressed dissatisfaction at having been merely a passenger. Determined to cross under her own power, she trained extensively and on May 20–21, 1932, flew solo from Harbour Grace, Newfoundland, to Culmore, Northern Ireland — a journey of approximately 2,026 miles completed in roughly 14 hours and 56 minutes. For this feat she received the Distinguished Flying Cross from the U.S. Congress, becoming the first woman to do so.

## Key Achievements

- **1932**: First woman to fly solo across the Atlantic Ocean; set a new transatlantic speed record.
- **1935**: First person to fly solo from Hawaii to the U.S. mainland (Oakland, California), a distance of approximately 2,408 miles.
- **1935**: First person to fly solo from Los Angeles to Mexico City.
- **1929**: Helped found The Ninety-Nines, the first organization for licensed women pilots, and served as its first president.
- **Multiple altitude records**: Set women's altitude record of 14,000 feet in 1922 and later raised it to 18,415 feet.
- **Author**: Published two autobiographical books — *20 Hrs., 40 Min.* (1928) and *The Fun of It* (1932).

## Controversies & Criticism

Earhart's 1928 Atlantic crossing drew some criticism because she served only as a passenger and log-keeper, with pilots Wilmer Stultz and Louis Gordon doing the actual flying. Some contemporaries felt her celebrity was disproportionate to her contribution on that particular flight — a critique she herself acknowledged publicly.

Her final circumnavigation attempt in 1937 raised questions about preparation and judgment. The Lockheed Electra 10E she flew with navigator Fred Noonan lacked a functional trailing wire antenna, limiting radio communication. The decision to remove it for weight reduction was controversial. When Earhart and Noonan disappeared on July 2, 1937, near Howland Island in the Pacific, the inadequate radio coordination between the aircraft and the U.S. Coast Guard cutter *Itasca* drew scrutiny. The U.S. government spent approximately $4 million on the search — the most expensive in naval history at the time — but recovered no wreckage.

Various theories about her fate have circulated for decades, including that she landed on Gardner Island (now Nikumaroro), was captured by Japanese forces, or survived in some clandestine capacity, though none have been conclusively proven.

## Legacy & Influence

Earhart's influence on aviation and gender equality has been profound and lasting. She inspired generations of women pilots and astronauts, and her name remains synonymous with courage and pioneering spirit. The Amelia Earhart Memorial Airport in Atchison, Kansas, and numerous schools, scholarships, and awards bear her name.

The Zonta International Amelia Earhart Fellowship, established in 1938, has since awarded over $10 million to women pursuing advanced degrees in aerospace science. NASA astronauts have cited her as a formative inspiration, and she was posthumously awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1963. Her life has been the subject of numerous biographies, films, and documentaries, ensuring her story continues to reach new audiences.
