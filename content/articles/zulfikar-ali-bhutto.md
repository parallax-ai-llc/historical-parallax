## Summary

Zulfikar Ali Bhutto (January 5, 1928 – April 4, 1979) was a Pakistani barrister, politician, and statesman who served as the fourth President of Pakistan from 1971 to 1973 and the ninth Prime Minister from 1973 until his overthrow in 1977.[^1] Often referred to as Quaid-e-Awam ("the People's Leader"), Bhutto founded the Pakistan People's Party (PPP) in 1967 and is credited with drafting Pakistan's 1973 Constitution and initiating the country's nuclear weapons program.[^2] He was executed by hanging on April 4, 1979, following a controversial trial that has been widely described as a "judicial murder."[^3] Bhutto's legacy remains deeply divisive, with supporters praising his socialist agenda and work for the underprivileged, while critics condemn him for political repression, human rights abuses, and economic mismanagement.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Zulfikar Ali Bhutto was born on January 5, 1928, near Larkana, Sindh, to Shah Nawaz Bhutto and Khursheed Begum.[^5] He belonged to a prominent Sindhi family that traced its ancestry to a 9th-century Rajput prince of the Bhati clan, with the family converting to Islam around the 17th century.[^6] His father, Shah Nawaz Bhutto, served as the dewan (chief minister) of the princely state of Junagadh and maintained influential relationships with British Raj officials.[^7] His mother, Khursheed Begum, was born Lakhi Bai, a professional dance girl from a Hindu family who converted to Islam upon marrying Shah Nawaz.[^8]

Zulfikar was the third child in the family. His eldest brother, Sikandar Ali, had died from pneumonia at age seven in 1914, while his second brother, Imdad Ali, died of cirrhosis at age 39 in 1953.[^9] As a young boy, Bhutto moved to Worli Seaface in Bombay, where he studied at the Cathedral and John Connon School and later at St. Xavier's College, Mumbai.[^10] Although he came from a major landowning family in Larkana, he was raised in cosmopolitan Bombay, away from the feudal environment of his ancestral home, which shaped his worldview and political ideology.[^11]

In 1947, Bhutto was admitted to the University of Southern California to study political science.[^12] In 1949, as a sophomore, he transferred to the University of California, Berkeley, where he earned a B.A. (honours) degree in political science in 1950.[^13] He later studied at Christ Church, Oxford (1952), and Lincoln's Inn (1953), where he qualified as a barrister.[^14] This Western education exposed Bhutto to socialist and democratic ideals that would later influence his political philosophy. In 1943, his marriage was arranged with Shireen Amir Begum, but on September 8, 1951, he married Nusrat Ispahani, a woman of Iranian Kurdish origin, who became known as Begum Nusrat Bhutto.[^15]

## Rise to Power

Bhutto entered politics in 1957, when he was appointed leader of Pakistan's delegation to the United Nations General Assembly.[^16] The following year, he became Minister of Commerce under President Ayub Khan, marking the beginning of his political ascent.[^17] He served as head of the ministries of commerce, information, and industries, helping to negotiate the Indus Waters Treaty with India in 1960 and an oil exploration contract with the Soviet Union in 1961.[^18] These early accomplishments demonstrated his diplomatic acumen and established him as a rising star in Pakistani politics.

In 1963, Bhutto became Foreign Minister, a position from which he advocated for Operation Gibraltar in Kashmir, which led to the 1965 war with India.[^19] Following the Tashkent Declaration, which ended the 1965 war, Bhutto's opposition to the peace settlement with India caused friction with President Ayub Khan, ultimately leading to his dismissal from the government.[^20] His resignation was driven by his belief that Pakistan had not achieved a favorable outcome in the war, and he criticized the Tashkent Agreement as a betrayal of Pakistan's national interests.

On November 30, 1967, at the Lahore residence of Mubashir Hassan, Bhutto founded the Pakistan People's Party (PPP) alongside political activist Sufi Nazar Muhammad Khan, Bengali communist J.A. Rahim, and Basit Jehangir Sheikh.[^21] The PPP established a strong base in Punjab, Sindh, and among the Muhajirs, with a platform focused on Islamic socialism, democracy, and social justice.[^22] The party's slogan "Roti, Kapra aur Makan" (Bread, Clothing, and Shelter) resonated with Pakistan's poor and marginalized populations.

After the overthrow of the Ayub Khan regime by General Agha Muhammad Yahya Khan, national elections were held in 1970.[^23] Although Bhutto and the PPP won a sweeping electoral victory in West Pakistan, the biggest winner was the Awami League, an East Pakistan-based party that had campaigned for full autonomy for East Pakistan.[^24] Following West Pakistan's defeat by India in the 1971 war and the subsequent creation of Bangladesh, Yahya Khan turned the government over to Bhutto on December 20, 1971.[^25] Bhutto assumed the presidency in December 1971, imposing emergency rule and securing a ceasefire on the western front.[^26]

## Major Achievements

### Constitutional Framework and Democratic Governance

Bhutto is considered the main architect of the 1973 Constitution, which represented his vision to lead Pakistan toward parliamentary democracy.[^27] The drafting of Pakistan's first-ever consensus constitution stands as one of the major achievements of his political life.[^28] This constitution established a parliamentary federal system, delineated the powers of the provinces, and included provisions for fundamental rights. In August 1973, Bhutto transitioned from President to Prime Minister under this new constitutional framework, marking Pakistan's return to civilian rule.[^29]

### Economic Reforms and Nationalization

On January 2, 1972, Bhutto announced the nationalization of all major industries of Pakistan and introduced a new industrial policy that favored labor unions.[^30] The first stage of the nationalization program integrated approximately 31 major industrial megacorporations, industrial units, and enterprises under direct government management control across 10 different categories of basic industries.[^31] On January 1, 1974, Bhutto nationalized all banks, bringing the financial sector under state control.[^32]

The government justified nationalization with the aim that "wealth of the nation must be used for the benefit of the nation and cannot be allowed to be concentrated in the hands of a few individuals."[^33] By the end of Bhutto's government, the concentration of wealth had declined compared to the Ayub Khan era when 22 families owned 66% of industrial capital.[^34] The number of bank branches increased by 75% from December 1971 to November 1976, rising from 3,295 to 5,727.[^35] Banking reforms mandated that 70% of institutional lending should be for small landholders of 12.5 acres or less.[^36]

Bhutto instituted land reforms limiting the size of landholdings and distributed more than one million acres to landless farmers.[^37] These reforms aimed to break the power of feudal landlords and provide economic opportunities to rural populations. Inflation was brought down to 6% by 1976, demonstrating some success in economic stabilization.[^38]

### Education and Social Reforms

On March 15, 1972, Bhutto announced the New Education Reforms, which envisaged universal and free education up to class X throughout the country in three phases.[^39] Under this scheme, all educational institutions were to be nationalized to ensure equal access to education regardless of economic background.[^40] He also announced the separation of the judiciary from the executive, strengthening the rule of law and judicial independence.[^41]

### Infrastructure and Industrial Development

Bhutto established several major industrial and educational institutions that continue to serve Pakistan today, including Pakistan Steel Mills, Heavy Mechanical Complex Taxila, Port Qasim Authority, Quaid-e-Azam University, Allama Iqbal Open University, and Karachi Nuclear Power Plant.[^42] These institutions represented significant investments in Pakistan's industrial capacity and human capital development.

### Nuclear Program

Bhutto earned the title "Father of Nuclear Deterrence" due to his administration and aggressive leadership of Pakistan's atomic bomb program.[^43] In his 1969 book "The Myth of Independence," Bhutto argued for the necessity of Pakistan acquiring a fission weapon to stand up to industrialized states and a nuclear-armed India.[^44] Following India's nuclear test in May 1974, Bhutto's commitment intensified.[^45]

In 1972, Bhutto called a meeting in which he instructed top Pakistani scientists to build the bomb.[^46] He famously stated: "If India builds the (atom) bomb, Pakistan will eat grass or leaves, even go hungry, but we (Pakistan) will get one of our own (atom bomb).... We (Pakistan) have no other choice!"[^47] The program, codenamed Project-706 (also known as Project-786), was executed by the Pakistan Atomic Energy Commission and the Kahuta Research Laboratories, led by Munir Ahmed Khan and Abdul Qadeer Khan respectively.[^48] Though Bhutto was executed in 1979, the program he initiated eventually led to Pakistan becoming a nuclear-armed nation in 1998.[^49]

### Foreign Policy and the 1974 Islamic Summit

The highlights of Bhutto's tenure as foreign minister included the initiation of Pakistan's relationship with China and success in attracting investment from Soviet bloc countries.[^50] He made Pakistan a prominent member of the Non-Aligned Movement, built close diplomatic relationships with Arab Ba'ath parties, and extended support to movements for national liberation.[^51]

Bhutto's greatest diplomatic achievement was hosting the Second Islamic Summit Conference of the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC) in Lahore from February 22-24, 1974.[^52] This historic event marked the first gathering of Muslim leaders in the modern era, attended by 38 Muslim leaders including six kings, twelve presidents, six prime ministers, and eight foreign ministers.[^53] Notable attendees included King Faisal of Saudi Arabia, Muammar Gaddafi, Anwar Sadat, and Hafez al-Assad.[^54]

The summit resulted in Pakistan's recognition of Bangladesh on February 19, 1974, following pressure from other Muslim nations.[^55] The Islamic world also accepted the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO) under Yasir Arafat as the sole legitimate representative of the Palestinian people, with Arafat famously stating that "Palestine was born in Lahore."[^56]

### Simla Agreement and POW Release

In June 1972, Bhutto visited India to meet Prime Minister Indira Gandhi and negotiated the Simla Agreement, a formal peace treaty signed on July 2, 1972.[^57] This agreement included the release of 93,000 Pakistani prisoners of war, marking the largest POW repatriation since World War II.[^58] Through the agreement, Bhutto reclaimed five thousand square miles (13,000 km²) of Indian-held territory and converted the cease-fire line into the Line of Control (LOC) between India and Pakistan.[^59] The Delhi Agreement, a trilateral accord signed between India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh on August 28, 1973, formalized the return of Pakistani soldiers captured during the 1971 war.[^60]

## Controversies and Criticisms

### Political Repression and Human Rights Abuses

Bhutto's legacy is marred by accusations of political repression and human rights abuses.[^61] The Federal Security Force (FSF), Bhutto's paramilitary force armed with over fifteen thousand officers, was widely criticized as ineffective except when "terrorizing political opponents."[^62] Bhutto sacked two provincial governments within six months, arrested the chief ministers, two governors, and 44 legislators, demonstrating authoritarian tendencies.[^63]

### The Balochistan Insurgency

In 1973, Bhutto launched a military crackdown on Balochistan that caused thousands of deaths and led to massive human rights abuses.[^64] The conflict began when Bhutto dismissed the elected provincial government of Balochistan on the pretext that arms had been discovered in the Iraqi Embassy, ostensibly for Baloch rebels.[^65] The ensuing protest led to calls for Balochistan's secession, prompting Bhutto to order the Pakistan Army into the province.[^66]

The operation, led by General Tikka Khan, involved sporadic fighting between insurgents and the army from 1973 to 1977.[^67] By 1974, it was reported that as many as 55,000 Baluchis were fighting some 70,000 government troops.[^68] An estimated 100,000 deployed troops faced accusations of human rights abuses and large-scale civilian casualties, with over 5,000 insurgents and 3,000 government troops killed.[^69] The Iranian military, fearing a spread of Baloch resistance in Iran, aided the Pakistani military.[^70]

### Persecution of Ahmadis

Under Bhutto's leadership, Pakistan's parliament passed a law in 1974 declaring Ahmadis to be non-Muslims, institutionalizing religious persecution.[^71] This constitutional amendment has had lasting consequences for the Ahmadi community in Pakistan, subjecting them to ongoing discrimination and violence.

### Role in the Bangladesh Crisis

Bhutto has been accused of criminal complicity with the Pakistani military junta during the brutal events in East Pakistan in 1971.[^72] Following the 1970 elections, in which the Awami League won a landslide in East Pakistan while the PPP dominated West Pakistan, Bhutto refused to form a coalition government with the Awami League.[^73] Capitalizing on West Pakistani fears of East Pakistani separatism, Bhutto demanded power-sharing arrangements, with some accounts suggesting he proposed "idhar hum, udhar tum" (you there, we here), meaning he should govern the West and Mujib the East.[^74]

Historical debate continues about Bhutto's motivations, with many believing he wanted power in the West even at the expense of the separation of the East.[^75] Operation Searchlight was launched by the military on March 25, 1971, with both Yahya Khan and Bhutto still in Dhaka.[^76] Civil unrest in the East intensified, followed by civil war and a war with India, resulting in the creation of Bangladesh.[^77]

### Economic Mismanagement

While nationalization aimed to redistribute wealth, it caused significant damage to the private sector and crippled industrial development.[^78] Investment in the private sector nearly ceased, and reduced private and foreign direct investment had long-term negative consequences.[^79] The administration, lacking understanding and training, could not manage nationalized enterprises efficiently, turning the economic structure into a bureaucratic quagmire.[^80]

Many nationalized units were small businesses that didn't qualify as industrial units, leading to adverse consequences for numerous small businessmen and traders who were either ruined, displaced, or left unemployed.[^81] Top Pakistani industrialists not only lost industrial units but also lost the urge to invest in Pakistan.[^82] The Saigol family started businesses in Tanzania, Kenya, and the UAE; the Service House picked up enterprises in Saudi Arabia; the Habib family started business activities in New York and London; and a large number of bank staff fled to the Middle East.[^83]

### The Kasuri Murder Case and Execution

On the night of November 10-11, 1974, there was a murderous assault involving firing on Ahmad Raza Kasuri's car, which resulted in the murder of his father, Nawab Muhammad Ahmad Khan.[^84] When lodging the FIR at the Ichhra police station, Kasuri mentioned Bhutto's name as a possible culprit, despite police opposition.[^85] Ahmad Raza Kasuri had been a PPP member elected to the National Assembly in 1970 but had become so critical of Bhutto's policies that he had not signed the 1973 Constitution.[^86]

After General Zia-ul-Haq carried out a military coup in July 1977 to overthrow Bhutto's government, the deposed prime minister was arrested two months later on accusations of masterminding the murder.[^87] In a controversial trial that many observers and legal experts declared flawed, he was declared guilty and awarded a death sentence in March 1978.[^88] The death penalty was awarded 4-3 by a seven-member Supreme Court bench in February 1979.[^89]

Bhutto's trial faced numerous criticisms and remains the only case in Pakistan's judicial history to be conducted in the high court rather than a trial court, thereby denying him the right to appeal.[^90] Former judges, including a former Supreme Court chief justice, later admitted to imposing the death sentence under pressure from General Zia.[^91] Controversially tried and executed on April 4, 1979, at 2:00 am in Rawalpindi District Jail, his trial has been widely described both in Pakistan and internationally as a "judicial murder."[^92]

In March 2024, Pakistan's Supreme Court issued a landmark ruling on the case, with Chief Justice Qazi Faez Isa stating: "We didn't find that the fair trial and due process requirements were met."[^93] The court ruled that the judicial proceedings leading to Bhutto's death sentence and execution violated principles of due process and the right to a fair trial.[^94]

## Personal Life

Zulfikar Ali Bhutto married Nusrat Ispahani on September 8, 1951.[^95] Nusrat was of Iranian Kurdish origin, and this was Bhutto's second marriage, as he had previously had an arranged marriage with Shireen Amir Begum in 1943.[^96] Bhutto and Nusrat had four children: Benazir, Murtaza, Sanam, and Shahnawaz.[^97] Benazir was born on June 21, 1953, Murtaza in 1954, Sanam in 1957, and Shahnawaz in 1958.[^98]

Benazir Bhutto (1953-2007) followed in her father's footsteps, serving as Pakistan's first female Prime Minister (1988-90; 1993-96).[^99] After Benazir's assassination on December 27, 2007, her son Bilawal Bhutto Zardari became co-chairperson of the PPP in 2007 along with her widower Asif Ali Zardari.[^100] Tragically, two of Bhutto's sons, Shahnawaz and Mir Murtaza, were assassinated.[^101] Nusrat Bhutto outlived three of her children: Murtaza, Benazir, and Shahnawaz.[^102]

Bhutto was known for his charismatic personality, mesmerizing speeches, and strong socialist message that greatly influenced Pakistan's political landscape.[^103] He had a commanding presence and was fluent in multiple languages, reflecting his cosmopolitan upbringing and education. His ability to connect with the masses while maintaining relationships with global leaders demonstrated his political versatility.

## Legacy

Bhutto's legacy remains one of the most contentious in Pakistan's history, evoking strong opinions across the political spectrum.[^104] For his supporters, Zulfikar Ali Bhutto symbolizes the best elements in politics: struggle against poverty and inequality as well as sacrifices for democracy and civilian supremacy.[^105] As one journalist told British academic Anatol Lieven: "Zulfikar Ali Bhutto is the only Pakistani leader who has ever spoken to the poor as if they mattered, and made them feel that they mattered."[^106]

Scholar Owen Bennett-Jones notes that Zulfikar "deserves to be judged on the basis of his own performance," drawing attention to his work for the underprivileged and legislative victories, as well as his failures to build democratic institutions.[^107] He is often considered one of Pakistan's greatest leaders and referred to as the Quaid-e-Awam ("the People's Leader"), and was posthumously awarded the Nishan-e-Pakistan, the highest civilian award of the country.[^108]

The Pakistan People's Party that Bhutto founded continues to be a significant political force in Pakistan decades after his death.[^109] The party has been led by members of the Bhutto family, with his daughter Benazir serving twice as prime minister and his son-in-law, Asif Ali Zardari, becoming president.[^110] This political dynasty demonstrates the enduring influence of Bhutto's political vision and the loyalty he inspired among supporters.

Bhutto's execution "left a wound on Pakistan's body politic that is still raw forty years later," with lasting impact that cannot be minimized.[^111] His death by hanging affected the country immensely, making his memory one of the most controversial in the nation's history, and he was hailed by his supporters as a shaheed, a martyr.[^112] Today, Bhutto is remembered for his charismatic leadership and lasting impact on Pakistan's political landscape, with his life and work continuing to provoke strong opinions and debate within the country.[^113]

Bhutto returned Pakistan to civilian rule and reintroduced a parliamentary system of government, leading to a period of democracy.[^114] The 1973 Constitution he championed remains in effect today (with amendments), serving as the fundamental legal document of Pakistan. His vision of a more equitable Pakistan, though imperfectly realized, inspired generations of politicians and activists.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and supporters recognize Bhutto as a transformative figure in Pakistani politics who gave voice to the marginalized and challenged entrenched power structures. As one journalist observed, Bhutto was "the only Pakistani leader who has ever spoken to the poor as if they mattered, and made them feel that they mattered."[^115] This populist appeal and genuine concern for social justice distinguished him from Pakistan's traditional elite politicians.

The most vital and adequate achievements in Bhutto's five-year tenure were his economic policies, according to historian Hasan (1998).[^116] Bhutto founded the left-center Pakistan People's Party, concerned with the lives of women, the poor, and the dispossessed peoples of Pakistan.[^117] His nationalization program, while controversial, successfully reduced wealth concentration from the Ayub Khan era when 22 families owned 66% of industrial capital.[^118]

Bhutto's contribution to the reorientation of Pakistan's foreign policy has been considered immense.[^119] He strengthened Pakistan's relationships with China, the Soviet Union, and Muslim-majority nations, diversifying Pakistan's international partnerships beyond its traditional alliance with the United States. The 1974 Islamic Summit in Lahore showcased Pakistan's leadership role in the Muslim world and demonstrated Bhutto's diplomatic prowess.

The drafting of Pakistan's 1973 Constitution stands as a major democratic achievement, providing the country with its first consensus constitutional framework.[^120] This constitution established fundamental rights, federal structure, and democratic governance principles that continue to guide Pakistan's political system. Bhutto's vision of parliamentary democracy represented a break from military rule and authoritarian governance.

His initiation of Pakistan's nuclear program demonstrated strategic foresight in addressing the security challenges posed by a nuclear-armed India.[^121] Though he did not live to see the program's completion, Bhutto's commitment to nuclear deterrence addressed a fundamental national security concern that continues to define Pakistan's defense posture.

### Negative Views

Critics condemn Bhutto for Pakistan's economic ruin, catastrophic military defeats, and terrible human rights abuses.[^122] The nationalization program, while aimed at wealth redistribution, caused long-term damage to private enterprise, crippled industrial development, and led to capital flight as major industrialists relocated their businesses abroad.[^123] The bureaucratic inefficiency in managing nationalized enterprises turned productive industries into loss-making ventures that became a burden on the national treasury.[^124]

The Balochistan military operation of 1973-77 represents one of the darkest chapters of Bhutto's rule, with an estimated 100,000 troops deployed and thousands of civilian casualties.[^125] The dismissal of the elected provincial government and subsequent military crackdown violated democratic principles and constitutional norms, demonstrating Bhutto's authoritarian tendencies when his power was challenged.[^126]

Bhutto's role in the Bangladesh crisis remains controversial, with critics arguing that his refusal to form a coalition government with the Awami League after the 1970 elections contributed to the political crisis that led to civil war and the breakup of Pakistan.[^127] His presence in Dhaka when Operation Searchlight was launched and his subsequent failure to prevent or condemn the military action raises questions about his complicity in the events that led to Bangladesh's independence.[^128]

The persecution of Ahmadis through the 1974 constitutional amendment institutionalized religious discrimination and set a precedent for religious intolerance in Pakistan's legal framework.[^129] This decision, driven by political expediency to appease religious conservatives, had lasting negative consequences for religious minorities in Pakistan.

Bhutto's use of the Federal Security Force to intimidate and terrorize political opponents violated democratic norms and human rights.[^130] The dismissal of provincial governments, arrest of elected officials, and suppression of political dissent demonstrated an authoritarian approach to governance that contradicted his democratic rhetoric.[^131]

His trial and execution, while unjust, followed from the violent political culture he helped create, including the allegations surrounding the Kasuri murder case.[^132] Whether or not Bhutto was actually involved in ordering the assassination, the existence of such allegations reflected the atmosphere of political violence and intimidation that characterized his rule.

## Famous Quotes

> "If India builds the (atom) bomb, Pakistan will eat grass or leaves, even go hungry, but we (Pakistan) will get one of our own (atom bomb).... We (Pakistan) have no other choice!" - On Pakistan's nuclear program[^133]

> "If things do not change, there will be nothing left to change. Either power must pass to the people or everything will perish." - On democracy and power[^134]

> "The people have realized that Martial Law is not law. A regime not established by law is devoid of the attribute to dispense law. A regime which puts in a bunker the highest law in the land does not have the moral authority to say that nobody is above the law." - From his book "If I Am Assassinated"[^135]

> "They are going to kill me. It doesn't matter what evidence you or anyone comes up with. They are going to murder me for murder I didn't commit." - On his impending execution[^136]
