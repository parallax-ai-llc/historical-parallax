## Summary
On April 14, 2014, Boko Haram militants kidnapped 276 female students from the Government Secondary School in Chibok, northeastern Nigeria. The mass abduction sparked the global #BringBackOurGirls campaign. As of 2024, over 100 girls remain missing.

## Background
The events leading to Boko Haram Chibok Kidnapping 2014 were shaped by complex social, political, and criminal factors in Chibok, Nigeria. [^1]

## Search, Negotiations, and Releases
The abduction triggered large-scale search efforts by Nigerian security forces and drew international attention and assistance, including intelligence support from foreign partners. The fate of the abducted students became the subject of sustained public advocacy and repeated government statements.

Over subsequent years, some of the abducted girls escaped on their own or were released in groups following negotiations. However, many remained unaccounted for long after the initial kidnapping, keeping the case active in national and international human-rights reporting.

## Humanitarian and Social Impact
Beyond the immediate harm to the victims and families, the kidnapping deepened fear around schooling in parts of northeastern Nigeria. It affected school attendance, perceptions of safety for girls’ education, and community trust in the ability of authorities to secure vulnerable public institutions.

## Policy and Security Legacy
The event became a reference point in debates about counterinsurgency strategy, protection of soft targets such as schools, and the handling of mass-hostage crises. It also helped galvanize civil-society organizing, with #BringBackOurGirls remaining a notable example of sustained public advocacy linked to a specific security incident.
