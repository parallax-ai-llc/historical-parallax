## Summary

Irish diplomat who exposed atrocities in the Congo Free State and Peruvian Amazon before being hanged for treason.

## Early Life and Diplomatic Service

- Born in Dublin and raised partly in Ulster; entered British consular service as a young man.
- Served in Africa and South America during the height of European imperial expansion, which shaped his later human-rights reporting.

## Congo Free State Investigation (1903–1904)

- As a British consul in Africa, Casement collected testimony and observations about forced labor, mutilations, and killings linked to the rubber trade.
- His findings were compiled in the **Casement Report** (1904), which helped spur international pressure and reform efforts regarding the Congo Free State.
- The report is often cited as an early example of official documentation used to challenge colonial abuses.

## Putumayo (Peruvian Amazon) Inquiry (1910–1911)

- Investigated allegations against the Peruvian Amazon Company in the Putumayo region.
- Reported patterns of coercion, brutality, and exploitation affecting Indigenous communities in the rubber boom.
- His work contributed to public scrutiny and legal/political consequences for those implicated.

## Irish Nationalism and Political Turn

- Over time, Casement shifted from imperial civil servant to Irish nationalist.
- Sought foreign support for Irish independence during World War I; his actions led to British charges of treason.

## Trial, Execution, and Legacy

- Arrested in 1916 and tried in London; convicted of high treason.
- Executed by hanging in August 1916.
- Remembered both for pioneering human-rights advocacy through investigative reporting and for his controversial role in Irish revolutionary politics.

## Additional Career Milestones (Selected)

- **1903**: Commissioned to investigate conditions in the Congo Free State.
- **1904**: Casement Report published; becomes a focal document in the Congo reform movement.
- **1910–1911**: Conducted Putumayo investigation in the Amazon.
- **1916**: Arrested, convicted, and executed following the Easter Rising period.
