## Summary

Matthew Henson was an African American explorer who accompanied Robert Peary on the 1909 expedition that claimed to reach the North Pole. He was the first person to actually set foot on the Pole.

## Early Life and Skills

Henson was born in Maryland and went to sea as a teenager, gaining practical skills in navigation, sailing, and survival. These abilities later made him a valuable member of Robert Peary’s Arctic expeditions, where logistical competence often mattered as much as formal scientific training.

## Arctic Expeditions with Robert Peary

Henson participated in multiple journeys to Greenland and the high Arctic beginning in the 1890s. Accounts of the expeditions often emphasize that he:

- Became proficient in using dog teams and sledging techniques
- Learned languages and customs that helped cooperation with **Inuit communities**
- Took on roles involving hunting, camp management, and day-to-day travel decisions

## The 1909 North Pole Claim

The 1909 expedition is historically significant and also controversial. Peary’s team claimed to have reached the **geographic North Pole**, and Henson was among the small final party on the last push.

Many later discussions focus on how credit was distributed: Henson’s contributions were often minimized in his lifetime due to racial discrimination, while later scholarship and public recognition highlighted his central role.

## Recognition and Legacy

- **Explorers Club (1937)**: Henson became the first Black member.
- **Later honors**: Over time, Henson received broader recognition in American historical memory, including commemorations of his Arctic achievements.

Henson’s story is frequently cited in discussions about the hidden labor behind famous expeditions and the ways race shaped whose achievements were celebrated.
