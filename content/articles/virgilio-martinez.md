## Summary

Virgilio Martínez Véliz is a Peruvian chef and restaurateur best known as the founder of Central, a Lima-based restaurant that reached the No. 1 spot on The World's 50 Best Restaurants list in 2023. His cooking is defined by a deep commitment to exploring Peru's extraordinary biodiversity, sourcing ingredients from ecosystems spanning sea level to altitudes above 4,000 meters in the Andes and deep into the Amazon rainforest.

Martínez has become one of the most influential figures in contemporary Latin American gastronomy. Alongside his wife and partner Pía León, and his sister Malena Martínez — who leads the research arm Mater Iniciativa — he has built a culinary project that bridges science, anthropology, and cuisine. His work has brought global attention to indigenous Peruvian ingredients and traditional foodways that had been largely overlooked by fine dining.

## Early Life & Background

Virgilio Martínez was born on August 31, 1977, in Lima, Peru. He initially studied law before deciding to pursue a career in cooking. He trained at Le Cordon Bleu in London and subsequently worked in kitchens across Europe, North America, and Asia, gaining experience under established chefs in cities including London, New York, and Singapore. These formative years abroad exposed him to diverse culinary traditions but ultimately deepened his desire to return to Peru and explore its own vast culinary heritage.

## Rise to Prominence

Martínez opened Central in Lima in 2008. The restaurant's concept evolved into a tasting menu organized by altitude — each course representing a distinct Peruvian ecosystem, from the Pacific Ocean to the high Andes and the Amazon basin. This framework, which he calls "Mater Elevations," became Central's defining identity.

Central first appeared on The World's 50 Best Restaurants list in 2013, entering at No. 50. It climbed steadily over the following decade. In 2023, Central was named the No. 1 restaurant in the world, a landmark moment for Peruvian and South American gastronomy. Martínez also opened Mil, a restaurant and research center located at the archaeological site of Moray in the Sacred Valley near Cusco, at approximately 3,500 meters above sea level.

## Key Achievements

- **2023**: Central named No. 1 on The World's 50 Best Restaurants list.
- **2022**: Central ranked No. 2 on the same list.
- **2014–2023**: Central appeared consecutively in the top 10 of The World's 50 Best Restaurants on multiple occasions.
- **Mater Iniciativa**: Co-founded this research project with his sister Malena Martínez to catalog and study Peruvian ingredients, working directly with indigenous communities across the country.
- **Mil (Moray)**: Opened this high-altitude restaurant and research kitchen in 2018, integrating archaeological context with culinary exploration.
- **Pía León**, his wife and co-chef, was named The World's Best Female Chef in 2021, further elevating their shared culinary project.

## Controversies & Criticism

Some critics have questioned whether the high-concept, research-driven approach at Central risks aestheticizing indigenous food traditions for an elite international audience. The cost of dining at Central places it out of reach for most Peruvians, raising familiar debates about accessibility in fine dining. Martínez has responded by emphasizing that Mater Iniciativa's work supports local communities and aims to preserve endangered food knowledge rather than merely extract it.

## Legacy & Influence

Martínez has played a central role in positioning Peru as a global gastronomic destination, building on the foundation laid by chefs like Gastón Acurio. His altitude-based approach to menu design introduced a genuinely novel framework to fine dining, one rooted in ecology and geography rather than classical European technique. Through Mater Iniciativa, he has championed the documentation of hundreds of Peruvian ingredients — many virtually unknown outside their native communities — creating a body of research that extends well beyond the restaurant. His influence can be seen in a growing generation of Latin American chefs who foreground biodiversity and indigenous knowledge in their work.
