## Summary

The Nazis stole an estimated 600,000 artworks from Jewish collectors and museums. Many remain unreturned; the 2013 Gurlitt hoard discovery of 1,400 works reignited the restitution debate.

## Scope of the Looting

Nazi looting of art during the Third Reich and World War II involved systematic seizure, forced sales, and confiscation from Jewish collectors, dealers, and institutions. The scale is commonly described in the hundreds of thousands of objects across Europe.

Looting was carried out through a mix of state policy, party-linked agencies, and opportunistic theft, and it often relied on discriminatory laws that stripped targeted groups of property rights.

## Mechanisms and Institutions (High-Level)

Frequently discussed elements include:

- cataloging and appropriation programs that treated cultural property as assets of the regime
- “degenerate art” campaigns that removed modern works from German museums
- networks of dealers, storage depots, and transport routes that moved works across borders

## Restitution and Provenance Research

After 1945, restitution became a long-running legal and ethical challenge. Many works changed hands multiple times, leaving incomplete ownership chains. Modern restitution efforts typically involve:

- provenance research to reconstruct gaps in ownership history
- negotiation or litigation between heirs, museums, dealers, and states
- national and international guidelines encouraging transparent claims processes

## The Gurlitt Hoard and Renewed Debate

The discovery of the Gurlitt collection in 2013 renewed public attention to the persistence of unresolved claims and the need for well-resourced provenance teams. It also highlighted how private holdings can contain works with contested histories decades after the war.
