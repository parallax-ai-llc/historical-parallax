## Summary

Britain's longest prison riot lasted 25 days at HMP Manchester. Prisoners seized control of the roof and caused £60 million in damage. Led to major prison reforms.

## Context

The riot took place at Strangeways Prison in Manchester (later renamed HMP Manchester).
It occurred against a backdrop of longstanding concerns about overcrowding, poor living conditions, and prisoner grievances.
The disturbance became a nationally prominent event because of its duration, visibility, and the scale of the damage.

## What Happened (High-Level)

The riot began on 1 April 1990 and quickly escalated beyond a localized protest.
Prisoners gained control of parts of the facility and used the prison roof as a platform to communicate demands and draw public attention.
Negotiations and tactical responses continued for weeks as authorities sought to regain control while limiting casualties.
The incident ended after 25 days, leaving extensive destruction inside the prison.

## Human Impact

The event involved significant risk to both staff and inmates.
A prisoner was killed during the disturbance, and many others (including prison officers) were injured.
The riot also created a broader public debate about the responsibilities of the state in custodial settings.

## Aftermath and Reforms

One of the most consequential outcomes was the official inquiry led by Lord Justice Woolf.
The resulting Woolf Report analyzed underlying causes and argued that decent, safe prison conditions were essential for security and order.
Reform themes commonly associated with the post-riot period include:

- improving sanitation and basic living conditions
- reducing overcrowding pressures
- strengthening accountability and complaint mechanisms
- clarifying operational standards for prison management and staff safety

## Why It Matters

The Strangeways riot is frequently cited in UK penal history as a turning point that connected prison conditions to public safety and institutional stability.
It remains a reference point for discussions about riots, custodial governance, and reform following systemic failure.
