## Summary

Japanese officer responsible for the Sook Ching massacre of Chinese civilians in Singapore and accused of cannibalism of Allied POWs. Evaded prosecution and became a politician.

## Background

Tsuji was a Japanese Army officer whose career is commonly associated with Imperial Japan's expansion in Asia.
He is frequently described as an aggressive planner and an ideologue within the wartime military system.
Accounts of his wartime role appear across postwar memoirs, investigative works, and official histories.

## Wartime conduct and allegations

The Sook Ching operation in Singapore is generally understood as a mass purge targeting Chinese civilians.
In popular and historical narratives, Tsuji is portrayed as a key instigator or organizer.
Some sources also repeat allegations of atrocities against prisoners of war, including cannibalism.
Those allegations have circulated in memoir and journalistic literature, but their evidentiary basis is disputed in parts of the historiography.

## Postwar evasion and politics

After Japan's surrender, many suspected perpetrators faced Allied investigations and war-crimes tribunals.
Tsuji is noted for avoiding early postwar prosecution and remaining at large for a period.
In later years he re-entered public life and served as an elected politician.
This transition from fugitive to public figure is part of what makes his case notable in postwar Japanese memory.

## Historical significance

Tsuji's story is often used to illustrate gaps between wartime responsibility and postwar accountability.
It also shows how narratives of atrocity can persist with varying degrees of documentation.
In contemporary discussions, he is typically framed as emblematic of the radical wing of Japan's wartime military culture.
