## Scientific Work

- Taylor was a key member of the SLAC–MIT collaboration that used high-energy electron beams to probe the internal structure of protons and neutrons.
- The experiments measured “deep inelastic scattering,” where electrons scatter off point-like constituents inside nucleons, producing results inconsistent with nucleons being smooth, indivisible objects.

## Connection to Quarks and the Parton Picture

- The observed scattering patterns supported the idea that nucleons contain smaller, quasi-free constituents.
- These results aligned with theoretical developments such as the parton model and, later, quantum chromodynamics (QCD), the gauge theory describing quarks and gluons.

## Experimental Approach (High Level)

- The work relied on precise control of beam energy and detector systems to map how scattering varied with momentum transfer and energy loss.
- By comparing measured cross-sections to predictions, the collaboration inferred the presence of point-like scatterers, helping establish a new empirical foundation for particle physics.

## Recognition and Legacy

- The 1990 Nobel Prize in Physics recognized the experimental discovery of the nucleon’s internal constituents, shared with collaborators for the overall body of work.
- Taylor’s career is often cited as an example of how large-scale accelerator facilities and collaborative experimental teams drive progress in fundamental physics.
- The SLAC deep inelastic scattering results remain a cornerstone dataset in the history of high-energy physics.
