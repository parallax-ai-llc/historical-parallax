## Summary

Nagasaki Mayor Iccho Itoh was shot dead by Yamaguchi-gumi member Tetsuya Shiroo over a dispute about car damage. One of the most shocking political assassinations in modern Japan.

## Background

- **Victim**: Iccho Itoh (伊藤一長), the sitting mayor of Nagasaki at the time.[^1]
- **Context**: Contemporary coverage framed the killing as a rare and highly visible attack on an elected local leader in postwar Japan.[^1]

## Perpetrator and Motive (High-Level)

- The shooter is widely reported as **Tetsuya Shiroo**, identified as a member of the **Yamaguchi-gumi** (a major yakuza syndicate).[^1]
- Public summaries commonly describe the motive as a **personal grievance** tied to a dispute over **compensation for vehicle damage**, not an ideological policy conflict.[^1]

## Attack and Immediate Aftermath

- The mayor was **shot** in **2007** and later **died from his wounds**.[^1]
- The case drew national attention because it involved a sitting municipal executive and an alleged perpetrator linked to organized crime.[^1]

## Legal Proceedings (Overview)

- High-level accounts describe the matter as resulting in **arrest**, **prosecution**, and **conviction** under Japan’s criminal justice system.[^1]
- For exact sentencing details and appeal outcomes, rely on primary reporting and court coverage referenced by encyclopedic sources.[^1]

## Why This Event Matters

- It is frequently referenced in discussions about **organized crime (yakuza)**, **security for public officials**, and the boundaries between civic life and criminal networks.[^1]
- The assassination also became a touchstone example of how a seemingly local, non-ideological dispute can escalate into a nationally significant political-security incident.[^1]
