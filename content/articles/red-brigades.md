## Summary

Italian far-left militant organization that carried out kidnappings, robberies, and murders during the 'Years of Lead.' Their most notorious act was the 1978 kidnapping and murder of former PM Aldo Moro.

## "Years of Lead" context

Italy’s **Anni di piombo** ("Years of Lead") refers to a period—especially prominent in the 1970s—marked by intense political polarization and political violence from both far-left and far-right groups.

Within that landscape, the Red Brigades (Brigate Rosse) are commonly treated as the most prominent far-left armed group, and their activity is often discussed alongside:

- labor unrest and student movements,
- clashes between militant factions and the state,
- a broader European context of extremist political violence.

## Ideology and strategy (high-level)

The Red Brigades described themselves in revolutionary terms and framed their violence as part of an anti-capitalist, anti-imperialist struggle. Analyses of the group typically emphasize:

- attempts to build clandestine networks,
- targeting of state officials, business figures, and security forces,
- kidnappings intended to extract concessions and dramatize political goals.

## Aldo Moro kidnapping (1978)

The 1978 kidnapping and killing of **Aldo Moro** is widely considered the group’s defining act.

It is frequently cited because it:

- attacked the highest levels of the Italian political system,
- triggered a major national crisis and an extensive security response,
- left lasting debate about negotiation versus non-negotiation with militants.

## State response and decline

The organization’s decline is generally attributed to sustained policing and judicial pressure, intelligence work, and the cumulative impact of arrests, defections, and internal fragmentation.

Even after the group was largely dismantled, "Red Brigades" remained a reference point in Italian discussions of political extremism, security policy, and the vulnerabilities of democratic institutions under violent pressure.
