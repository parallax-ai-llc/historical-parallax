## Summary

Su Tseng-chang (蘇貞昌) is a prominent Taiwanese politician and lawyer who served as Premier of the Republic of China (Taiwan) from 2019 to 2023, making him one of the longest-serving premiers in recent Taiwanese history. Born in 1947 in Pingtung County, Su has been a central figure in Taiwan's Democratic Progressive Party (DPP) for decades, holding numerous high-profile positions including Magistrate of Taipei County, Minister of the Interior, President of the Executive Yuan, and Secretary-General of the Presidential Office. Known for his distinctive bald head and fiery oratory style, Su has been a key architect of Taiwan's democratic consolidation and a staunch defender of Taiwanese sovereignty against Chinese pressure. His political career spans over four decades, during which he has navigated Taiwan's complex relationship with mainland China while promoting progressive domestic policies and democratic values.

---

## Early Life and Background

Su Tseng-chang was born on July 28, 1947, in Pingtung County in southern Taiwan, during the final years of Japanese colonial rule's aftermath and the beginning of the Republic of China's administration of Taiwan.[^1] He grew up in a period of significant political and social transformation as Taiwan transitioned from authoritarian rule under the Kuomintang (KMT) to eventually becoming one of Asia's most vibrant democracies. His family background was modest, and he experienced firsthand the challenges faced by ordinary Taiwanese people under martial law.[^2]

Su pursued his education with determination, eventually studying law at National Taiwan University, one of the island's most prestigious institutions. He graduated with a law degree in 1970, during a time when Taiwan was still under strict authoritarian control and political dissent was severely restricted.[^3] His legal education would prove foundational to his later career, both as a practicing attorney and as a politician advocating for rule of law and democratic reforms.

After completing his mandatory military service, Su worked as a lawyer, gaining firsthand experience with Taiwan's legal system and developing a reputation for taking on challenging cases. This period allowed him to witness the injustices and limitations of Taiwan's authoritarian system, which operated under martial law from 1949 to 1987.[^4] His experiences during this formative period shaped his political worldview and commitment to democratic reform, eventually drawing him into opposition politics during Taiwan's democratization movement of the 1980s.

---

## Rise to Power

Su Tseng-chang's entry into politics coincided with Taiwan's democratic transition in the late 1980s. He joined the Democratic Progressive Party (DPP) shortly after its founding in 1986, becoming part of the "Tangwai" (outside the party) movement that challenged KMT authoritarian rule.[^5] His legal background and articulate speaking style quickly made him a valuable asset to the opposition movement, and he began building a political base in northern Taiwan.

In 1981, before formally joining the DPP, Su was elected to the Taiwan Provincial Assembly, marking his first major electoral victory.[^6] He used this platform to advocate for democratic reforms and to criticize the KMT government's authoritarian policies. His performance in the provincial assembly earned him recognition as a rising star within the opposition movement, and he became known for his aggressive questioning of government officials and his unwillingness to back down from confrontation.

Su's political breakthrough came in 1997 when he was elected Magistrate of Taipei County (now New Taipei City), one of Taiwan's most populous and politically significant regions.[^7] This victory was particularly significant as it demonstrated the DPP's growing appeal beyond its traditional strongholds in southern Taiwan. As magistrate, Su implemented various progressive policies and improved local governance, earning praise for his administrative competence. He was re-elected in 2001, further consolidating his position as one of the DPP's most successful local executives.[^8]

His success as Taipei County Magistrate propelled Su into national prominence. In 2005, President Chen Shui-bian appointed him Premier of the Republic of China, making him the head of the Executive Yuan and the highest-ranking official in Taiwan's government after the president.[^9] Although his first tenure as premier was relatively brief, lasting only until 2006, it established him as a national political figure capable of operating at the highest levels of government. Su later served in other key positions, including Secretary-General of the Presidential Office and Chairman of the DPP, cementing his status as one of the party's elder statesmen.[^10]

## Major Achievements

Su Tseng-chang's political career is marked by several significant achievements that have had lasting impacts on Taiwan's governance, democracy, and international standing. His longest and most consequential tenure came during his second term as Premier from 2019 to 2023, during which he faced unprecedented challenges including the COVID-19 pandemic, heightened tensions with China, and economic pressures.[^11]

One of Su's most celebrated achievements was his government's handling of the COVID-19 pandemic. Under his leadership, Taiwan implemented one of the world's most effective pandemic responses, maintaining relatively low case numbers and death rates while avoiding the strict lockdowns that characterized many other countries' approaches.[^12] The government's strategy combined early border controls, aggressive contact tracing, mask mandates, and transparent public health communication. Su's crisis management during this period earned international praise and significantly boosted his domestic approval ratings, with many Taiwanese citizens crediting his steady leadership during the emergency.[^13]

As Premier, Su oversaw significant economic policies aimed at reducing Taiwan's dependence on mainland China while promoting innovation and technological advancement. He supported the expansion of the "New Southbound Policy," which sought to strengthen Taiwan's economic and cultural ties with Southeast Asian countries, South Asia, Australia, and New Zealand as alternatives to over-reliance on the Chinese market.[^14] His government also promoted Taiwan's semiconductor industry, recognizing it as crucial to both Taiwan's economic security and its strategic importance to global supply chains.

Su was a strong advocate for Taiwan's international participation and visibility, despite Beijing's efforts to isolate the island diplomatically. During his premiership, Taiwan gained increased international attention and support, particularly from the United States, Japan, and European countries concerned about Chinese aggression.[^15] His government successfully navigated heightened cross-strait tensions, maintaining Taiwan's de facto independence while avoiding actions that might provoke military conflict with China.

On domestic policy, Su championed various progressive causes including same-sex marriage rights (Taiwan became the first Asian country to legalize same-sex marriage in 2019), environmental protection, and transitional justice for victims of Taiwan's authoritarian era.[^16] He supported investigations into past human rights abuses under KMT rule and worked to preserve historical memory of Taiwan's democratization struggle. His government also implemented labor reforms and pension adjustments, though these proved controversial among some constituencies.

Su's administrative reforms improved government efficiency and transparency. He promoted digital governance initiatives, making government services more accessible to citizens through online platforms.[^17] His emphasis on accountability and performance metrics helped modernize Taiwan's bureaucracy, though critics argued that some reforms created excessive pressure on civil servants.

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his achievements, Su Tseng-chang's political career has not been without significant controversies and criticisms. His aggressive political style, while admired by supporters, has often been characterized by opponents as divisive and confrontational, contributing to Taiwan's polarized political environment.[^18]

One of the most significant controversies during Su's tenure as Premier involved his handling of food safety issues, particularly the decision in 2020-2021 to allow imports of U.S. pork containing ractopamine, a feed additive banned in many countries including China and the European Union.[^19] This decision sparked significant public backlash and protests from farmers, health advocates, and opposition politicians who questioned the safety of such imports and accused Su's government of prioritizing diplomatic relations with the United States over public health concerns. The controversy damaged Su's approval ratings and provided ammunition to opposition parties who accused the DPP of being subservient to American interests.

Su faced intense criticism for his government's energy policies, particularly the planned phase-out of nuclear power and increased reliance on natural gas and renewable energy sources. Critics, including the opposition KMT and some business groups, argued that the transition was poorly planned and would lead to energy shortages and increased electricity costs.[^20] Several power outages during his tenure as Premier, including major blackouts affecting millions of households, were attributed to the energy transition challenges and damaged public confidence in the government's energy policy.

The Premier was also criticized for his handling of labor issues and pension reforms. Labor unions accused his government of failing to adequately protect workers' rights and of implementing pension cuts that unfairly targeted civil servants and military personnel.[^21] While the government argued that pension reforms were necessary to ensure the long-term sustainability of Taiwan's retirement system, the changes sparked protests and damaged Su's relationship with some traditional DPP constituencies.

Su's confrontational approach to cross-strait relations drew criticism from those who favored a more conciliatory approach to China. While his supporters praised his firm stance on Taiwanese sovereignty, critics argued that his rhetoric unnecessarily provoked Beijing and increased the risk of military conflict.[^22] The opposition KMT frequently accused Su and the DPP of pursuing "Taiwan independence" and endangering peace in the Taiwan Strait, though Su consistently maintained that Taiwan was already effectively independent and did not need to declare formal independence.

Critics also accused Su of politicizing government institutions and using his position to advance DPP electoral interests rather than serving as an impartial administrator. Specific controversies included allegations that government resources and public communications were used to promote DPP candidates, and that personnel appointments were based on political loyalty rather than merit.[^23] Su's involvement in various political campaigns while serving as Premier raised questions about the appropriate boundaries between administrative and partisan roles.

Some observers criticized Su for being overly controlling and micromanaging, creating a work environment that discouraged independent thinking among subordinates.[^24] Reports of high staff turnover and burnout in some government agencies were attributed to Su's demanding management style and high-pressure approach to administration.

## Personal Life

Su Tseng-chang married his wife, Chan Hsiu-lan (詹秀蘭), in the early years of his legal career, and the couple has maintained a relatively private family life despite Su's high-profile political career.[^25] Chan has largely stayed out of the public spotlight, supporting her husband's political ambitions while maintaining her own professional identity. The couple has children who have pursued their own careers outside of politics, reflecting Su's apparent preference for keeping his family separate from his political life.

Su is known for his distinctive physical appearance, particularly his bald head, which has become something of a trademark and the subject of both affectionate jokes and political commentary in Taiwan.[^26] He reportedly embraced his baldness early in life and has never attempted to conceal it, with some supporters suggesting it reflects his straightforward, no-nonsense personality. His appearance has been featured in political cartoons and memes, becoming an easily recognizable symbol in Taiwanese politics.

Those who have worked with Su describe him as an extremely dedicated and hardworking politician who often works long hours and expects similar commitment from his staff. He is known for his meticulous attention to detail and his insistence on thorough preparation for meetings and public appearances.[^27] Colleagues have noted that Su can be demanding and sometimes temperamental, but also genuinely committed to public service and deeply knowledgeable about policy matters.

Su maintains a reputation for personal integrity and modesty in his lifestyle, avoiding the ostentatious displays of wealth that have marred some other politicians' reputations.[^28] He has lived relatively simply compared to some of his political peers, and has not been implicated in the corruption scandals that have affected other Taiwanese politicians. This reputation for personal honesty has been an asset throughout his career, particularly given the DPP's historical emphasis on clean government as a contrast to perceived KMT corruption.

In his limited leisure time, Su is said to enjoy reading, particularly history and political biography, which informs his understanding of governance and political strategy.[^29] He is also known to be a fan of traditional Taiwanese culture and cuisine, often promoting local food and cultural heritage during his public appearances. Despite his busy schedule, he has maintained close relationships with longtime political allies from his early days in the opposition movement, reflecting a sense of loyalty to those who struggled alongside him during Taiwan's democratization.

## Legacy

Su Tseng-chang's legacy in Taiwanese politics is multifaceted, reflecting his long career and the complex challenges Taiwan has faced during his time in public service. As one of the key figures in Taiwan's Democratic Progressive Party and a veteran of the island's democratization movement, Su represents a generation of politicians who transformed Taiwan from an authoritarian state into a vibrant democracy.[^30]

Perhaps Su's most enduring legacy will be his role in Taiwan's successful handling of the COVID-19 pandemic. His government's effective response demonstrated that Taiwan could manage major crises competently and transparently, contrasting sharply with the more authoritarian approaches taken by mainland China.[^31] This success not only saved lives but also enhanced Taiwan's international reputation and strengthened the case for Taiwan's continued separate governance. The pandemic response became a symbol of Taiwan's democratic values and competent governance, themes that Su consistently emphasized.

Su's firm stance on Taiwanese sovereignty and his willingness to stand up to Chinese pressure have made him a symbol of resistance to Beijing's increasingly aggressive approach toward Taiwan. His tenure as Premier coincided with a period of heightened tensions across the Taiwan Strait, including increased Chinese military activities and diplomatic pressure.[^32] Su's refusal to compromise on Taiwan's democratic system and de facto independence, while maintaining strategic ambiguity about formal independence, helped define Taiwan's approach to cross-strait relations during a critical period. His legacy includes strengthening Taiwan's resolve to defend its autonomy and democratic way of life.

Within Taiwan's domestic politics, Su's legacy includes his contributions to progressive causes such as same-sex marriage, transitional justice, and environmental protection. His support for these issues helped shift Taiwan's political discourse toward more liberal positions and reinforced Taiwan's identity as a progressive democracy distinct from mainland China's authoritarian system.[^33] These achievements have particularly resonated with younger Taiwanese voters who increasingly identify as Taiwanese rather than Chinese.

Su's administrative reforms and emphasis on government efficiency have left a mark on Taiwan's bureaucracy, though assessments of these changes are mixed. Supporters credit him with modernizing government operations and improving public service delivery, while critics argue that some reforms created excessive pressure and reduced morale among civil servants.[^34] His digital governance initiatives, however, are generally viewed as positive contributions that have made government more accessible to citizens.

The controversies and polarization associated with Su's leadership also form part of his legacy. His confrontational political style and partisan approach have contributed to Taiwan's increasingly divided political landscape.[^35] While his supporters see him as a principled fighter for Taiwan's interests, critics view him as unnecessarily divisive and willing to sacrifice consensus for political advantage. This polarization may complicate efforts to build cross-party cooperation on major issues facing Taiwan.

Historians and political scientists will likely debate Su's overall impact for years to come. Some will emphasize his role in defending Taiwan's democracy during a critical period of Chinese pressure, while others will focus on missed opportunities for cross-strait dialogue or domestic policy failures.[^36] What seems clear is that Su Tseng-chang was one of the defining political figures of his era, whose career spanned and helped shape Taiwan's transformation from authoritarian rule to mature democracy.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Supporters and many political analysts view Su Tseng-chang as one of Taiwan's most capable and dedicated public servants, particularly praising his crisis management abilities and unwavering commitment to democratic values. Scholars of Taiwanese democracy often highlight Su's role in the opposition movement during the 1980s and his contributions to Taiwan's democratic consolidation.[^37] His willingness to challenge authoritarian rule during Taiwan's martial law period demonstrated personal courage and commitment to democratic principles that later generations have benefited from.

Many public health experts and international observers have praised Su's handling of the COVID-19 pandemic as exemplary, noting that Taiwan's success in controlling the virus while maintaining economic activity and personal freedoms represented a model that other democracies could learn from.[^38] Dr. Jennifer Nuzzo, an epidemiologist at Johns Hopkins University, and other global health experts have specifically cited Taiwan's pandemic response under Su's leadership as evidence that democratic transparency and competent governance can be more effective than authoritarian approaches in managing public health crises.

Political scientists who study cross-strait relations often credit Su with maintaining a principled but pragmatic approach to China during an extremely challenging period. Professor Shelley Rigger, a leading expert on Taiwan at Davidson College, has noted that Su's government successfully navigated the difficult balance between defending Taiwan's autonomy and avoiding unnecessary provocation that might trigger a military crisis.[^39] His ability to maintain this balance while strengthening Taiwan's international support network is seen as a significant diplomatic achievement.

Supporters within Taiwan emphasize Su's administrative competence and work ethic, noting that he brought a level of professionalism and attention to detail that improved government operations.[^40] His background as a lawyer and his emphasis on rule of law are seen as having strengthened Taiwan's democratic institutions. Progressive activists credit Su with advancing LGBTQ+ rights, environmental protection, and transitional justice, viewing him as a champion of liberal values in East Asia.

International media coverage during Su's premiership was often favorable, portraying him as a defender of democracy against authoritarian Chinese pressure. Publications like The Economist, The New York Times, and The Guardian frequently presented Su as representing Taiwan's democratic resistance to Beijing's expansionism.[^41] This international support has reinforced positive domestic assessments of Su's leadership among those who prioritize Taiwan's international standing and security.

### Negative Views

Critics of Su Tseng-chang argue that his confrontational political style and partisan approach have deepened Taiwan's political divisions and made consensus-building more difficult. Opposition politicians, particularly within the Kuomintang, have characterized Su as more interested in political combat than in genuine governance, accusing him of using his position as Premier to advance DPP electoral interests rather than serving all Taiwanese citizens impartially.[^42]

Some economists and business leaders have criticized Su's economic policies, particularly regarding energy transition and labor regulations, arguing that his government's approaches were ideologically driven rather than pragmatically focused on Taiwan's economic competitiveness.[^43] Critics point to power outages, rising electricity costs, and business concerns about regulatory uncertainty as evidence that Su's policies sometimes prioritized political symbolism over practical economic management.

Scholars focused on cross-strait relations from a realist perspective have argued that Su's hardline approach to China, while popular domestically, may have unnecessarily increased tensions and reduced room for diplomatic maneuvering. Some Chinese affairs experts, including those advocating for greater engagement with Beijing, contend that Su's rhetoric and policies contributed to the deterioration of cross-strait relations and increased the risk of military conflict.[^44] They argue that a more conciliatory approach might have better served Taiwan's long-term security interests.

Critics on Taiwan's political left have sometimes accused Su of abandoning progressive principles when politically convenient, particularly regarding labor rights and environmental protection. Labor unions and activists have pointed to specific policy decisions, such as loosening certain labor protections or approving controversial development projects, as evidence that Su's progressive credentials were more rhetorical than substantive.[^45] These critics argue that Su prioritized business interests and political expediency over workers' rights and environmental sustainability.

Some civil liberties advocates have raised concerns about certain policies implemented during Su's tenure, including enhanced surveillance measures justified by security concerns and restrictions on speech related to disinformation and Chinese influence.[^46] While acknowledging the genuine security challenges Taiwan faces, these critics worry about potential overreach that could set concerning precedents for limiting civil liberties.

Academic critiques of Su's governance style note that his centralized decision-making approach and demanding management style may have stifled innovation and dissent within the government bureaucracy. Public administration scholars have questioned whether Su's methods, while effective in crisis management, created a sustainable model for government operations or instead relied too heavily on his personal drive and control.[^47] Critics argue that this approach may have weakened institutional capacity and created vulnerabilities for future governments.
