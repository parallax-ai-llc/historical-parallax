## Summary

Assyrian king who besieged and destroyed the Kingdom of Israel, deporting the ten lost tribes.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| -727 | Acceded to the Assyrian throne after Tiglath-Pileser III |
| -725 | Began (or continued) the Assyrian siege of Samaria in the Kingdom of Israel |
| -722 | Samaria fell; deportations from the region became part of Assyrian imperial policy in the Levant |
| -722 | Died after a short reign; succeeded by Sargon II |

## Historical context

- Shalmaneser V ruled during a period of rapid Assyrian expansion and consolidation in the Levant.
- Much of what is popularly attributed to him—especially regarding the fall of Samaria—is discussed alongside the early reign of **Sargon II**, because the sources are limited and later Assyrian records may frame events for political legitimacy.

## Sources and legacy (high level)

- Compared with other Assyrian kings, relatively few royal inscriptions are securely attributed to Shalmaneser V, which contributes to uncertainty about details of his campaigns.
- In biblical history, the Assyrian conquest of the northern Kingdom of Israel and the subsequent deportations became associated with the idea of the "ten lost tribes," a theme that remained influential in later religious and historical writing.

## Key facts

- Reign: 727–722 BCE (approximate, using conventional chronology).
- Dynasty: Neo-Assyrian Empire; generally described as a son and successor of Tiglath-Pileser III.
- Major association in later tradition: the siege and fall of Samaria and Assyrian deportations from the northern Levant.
- Surviving contemporary documentation for his reign is comparatively limited.

## Name

- His name is commonly rendered in English as **Shalmaneser V**; scholarly works may also use Akkadian transliterations.

## The Siege of Samaria (High-Level Context)

In many summaries, Shalmaneser V is linked to the multi-year siege of **Samaria**, the capital of the northern Kingdom of Israel. Because the surviving sources are fragmentary and later royal inscriptions can serve political narratives, some accounts attribute parts of the conquest to Shalmaneser, while others emphasize Sargon II’s role in claiming credit for the outcome.

## Assyrian Imperial Policy: Deportation and Control

Assyrian administration is frequently characterized by:

- the use of deportations and resettlement to disrupt local resistance
- installation of loyal officials and incorporation of territories into provincial systems
- extraction of tribute and integration into imperial trade and logistics

The association of Shalmaneser V with deportations from the northern Levant connects his reign to long-running historical and religious interpretations, even though the precise administrative details are often reconstructed from broader Neo-Assyrian practice.

## Evidence and Uncertainty

Shalmaneser V is less well attested than several other Assyrian kings. High-level treatments typically note that:

- contemporaneous inscriptions are fewer, limiting direct campaign narratives
- later sources (including biblical texts and later Assyrian royal accounts) shape the traditional picture

As a result, modern reconstructions often focus on the likely sequence of events and institutional patterns rather than detailed year-by-year documentation.
