## Summary

Indian serial killer named K.D. Kempamma who murdered 6 women by offering them cyanide-laced prasad (holy offering). She targeted women at temples and stole their jewelry. Known as 'Cyanide Mallika.' Sentenced to death.

## Key Details

### Modus operandi

According to widely circulated case summaries, Kempamma approached women at or near Hindu temples, built trust through conversation, and offered prasad that had been adulterated with cyanide. After the victim collapsed or died, she stole jewelry and other valuables. The use of a religious offering as the delivery method is frequently cited as a reason the crimes initially drew limited suspicion.

### Victim selection and location

The murders were associated with temple areas in and around Bengaluru (Bangalore), with targets described as women visiting alone or with limited immediate protection. Reports emphasize opportunism rather than a personal connection to specific victims.

### Investigation and arrest

Public accounts describe investigators linking multiple unexplained deaths and robberies through similarities in circumstance and missing jewelry. The case became known in Indian media partly because cyanide is an uncommon poison in street-level crimes, raising questions about sourcing and handling.

### Trial and sentencing

Kempamma was convicted for multiple murders and robbery-related charges in Indian courts, and she was sentenced to death in at least one major judgment reported in public sources. Legal reporting on later appeals and final sentence status varies across outlets; the most reliable, consolidated summary is typically the referenced encyclopedia entry.

## Public Impact

The "Cyanide Mallika" case is often discussed in the context of women’s safety, the vulnerability of public religious spaces to predatory crime, and the importance of pattern recognition in serial investigations.
