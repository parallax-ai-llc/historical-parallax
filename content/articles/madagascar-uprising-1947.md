## Summary

French colonial forces suppressed a Malagasy independence uprising, killing an estimated 11,000-100,000 people. The violence included summary executions and dropping suspects from planes.

## Background

- Madagascar was a French colony; nationalist/independence sentiment grew during and after World War II.
- Political organization and expectations of postwar self-determination contributed to rising tensions between Malagasy activists and the colonial administration.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- The uprising began on **29 March 1947** and spread across multiple regions.
- French authorities treated the revolt as a major security threat and launched large-scale counterinsurgency operations.

## Perpetrators / Actors

- **French colonial military and security forces** led the suppression.
- **Malagasy insurgents and nationalist networks** carried out attacks and organized resistance.

## Reported Methods of Repression

- Contemporary and later accounts describe **summary executions**, mass reprisals, and collective punishment.
- Allegations also include **torture** and the reported practice of **throwing detainees from aircraft**.

## Casualty Estimates

- Estimates vary widely in published summaries, commonly ranging from **~11,000 to 100,000 deaths**.
- The wide range reflects differences in documentation, definitions (combatants vs. civilians), and the difficulty of verifying losses across dispersed rural areas.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- The suppression strengthened Malagasy nationalist memory and became a reference point for later independence politics.
- Madagascar eventually achieved **independence in 1960**, with the 1947 events often cited as an early, costly milestone in the decolonization struggle.

## Notes on Classification

- This entry is categorized under **Colonial Crime** due to large-scale civilian harm and documented/claimed extrajudicial practices during repression.
