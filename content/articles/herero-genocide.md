## Summary

German colonial forces exterminated 80% of the Herero people through a deliberate extermination order, concentration camps, and forced marches into the desert. Germany acknowledged it as genocide in 2021.

## Historical context

The Herero and Nama genocide occurred in **German South West Africa** (present-day Namibia) during Germany’s colonial rule. It followed uprisings and harsh counterinsurgency operations that escalated into mass killing, expulsion, and confinement.

## Methods of destruction (high-level)

Accounts of the genocide typically emphasize a combination of:

- **forced displacement** into arid regions where access to water was limited
- **mass death from starvation and thirst** during expulsions and pursuits
- **detention and forced labor** in camps, with high mortality from disease, malnutrition, and abuse

## Extermination order and command responsibility

The phrase “extermination order” is commonly associated with German General Lothar von Trotha’s policy toward the Herero in 1904. Historians discuss the degree of central coordination, but most summaries treat the order and subsequent practices as key evidence of genocidal intent.

## Memory, reparations, and diplomacy

In the 21st century, public debate has focused on:

- formal recognition and apology
- the relationship between recognition and **reparations**
- how colonial-era remains and artifacts should be returned or treated

Germany’s 2021 acknowledgment is often framed as part of a wider European reckoning with colonial violence.

## Why it matters

The Herero and Nama genocide is frequently cited as one of the major genocides of the 20th century’s opening years and as a case study in how colonial rule, racial ideology, and military policy can combine into systematic mass atrocity.
