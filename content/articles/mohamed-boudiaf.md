## Background

- Mohamed Boudiaf (محمد بوضياف) was a leading figure in the Algerian nationalist movement and is commonly described as one of the founders of the National Liberation Front (FLN).
- He was active in the political organizations that preceded the Algerian War of Independence, and later became part of the revolutionary leadership during the anti-colonial struggle against French rule.

## Role in Algerian Independence

- Boudiaf helped organize nationalist networks and was involved in the establishment of the FLN, which launched the armed struggle in 1954.
- In 1956, he was among the FLN leaders captured after a plane carrying several senior figures was intercepted by French authorities; the episode became a major symbol of the conflict.
- After independence in 1962, he fell out with the dominant post-independence leadership and spent long periods outside Algeria.

## Return to Power (1992)

- In early 1992, amid a severe political crisis and the onset of what became known as the Algerian Civil War, Boudiaf was brought back from exile.
- He was appointed chairman of the High Council of State, a collective presidency created after the electoral process was interrupted.
- Contemporary reporting and later accounts often emphasize that he publicly framed his mission in terms of state reform, restoring legitimacy, and combating corruption.

## Assassination

- On 29 June 1992, Boudiaf was shot during a public appearance in Annaba while delivering a speech; the killing was broadcast on Algerian television.
- The assailant was reported to be a member of his security detail (often identified as Lambarek Boumaarafi), and the circumstances and alleged motives have remained politically contentious.

## Legacy

- Boudiaf is frequently remembered in Algeria as a figure associated with integrity and reformist intent, though interpretations vary across political camps.
- His death is widely viewed as a pivotal early moment in the country’s long period of internal conflict in the 1990s.
