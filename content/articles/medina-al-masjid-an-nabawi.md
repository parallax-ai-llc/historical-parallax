## Summary

Al-Masjid an-Nabawi, the Prophet's Mosque, is the second holiest site in Islam after Masjid al-Haram in Mecca. Located in the city of Medina in the Hejaz region of western Saudi Arabia, it was originally built by the Prophet Muhammad himself in 622 CE upon his arrival in Medina following the Hijra — his migration from Mecca. The mosque contains the tomb of the Prophet Muhammad, making it a site of immense spiritual significance and a destination for millions of Muslim pilgrims who visit Medina each year.

The mosque's distinctive green dome, which marks the location of the Prophet's tomb, is one of the most recognized symbols in the Islamic world. Today the mosque is one of the largest in the world, capable of accommodating hundreds of thousands of worshippers at a time. The Saudi government has undertaken successive expansions over the past century, transforming what was once a modest structure into a vast complex that sprawls across much of central Medina.

Unlike Masjid al-Haram, visiting Al-Masjid an-Nabawi is not an obligatory part of the Hajj pilgrimage, but it is considered a highly recommended act of devotion. The Prophet Muhammad reportedly said that "a prayer in my mosque is better than a thousand prayers elsewhere, except the Masjid al-Haram" — a hadith that has drawn Muslims to the site for over fourteen centuries.

## History & Origins

The founding of Al-Masjid an-Nabawi is directly linked to one of the most pivotal events in Islamic history: the Hijra of 622 CE. When the Prophet Muhammad and his followers migrated from Mecca to Yathrib — the city that would subsequently be renamed Madinat al-Nabi (City of the Prophet), shortened to Medina — Muhammad oversaw the construction of a mosque as the first public building of the new Muslim community.

According to Islamic tradition, the site of the mosque was chosen by Muhammad's camel, which sat down at a particular location and refused to move. The site was purchased from two orphans whose grandfather had owned it, and the construction was carried out by the Muslim community collectively, with the Prophet himself participating in the building work. The original mosque was a simple rectangular enclosure approximately 30 by 35 meters, with walls of mud brick, a palm-trunk colonnade, and a roof of palm fronds and mud. It contained a covered hall (suffah) where the poorest of the Muslim community — the Ahl al-Suffah — lived.

The mosque served not only as a place of worship but as the administrative, judicial, and social center of the early Islamic polity. The Prophet received delegations of tribal leaders here, adjudicated disputes, organized military expeditions, and delivered sermons. Several of his wives lived in small apartments attached to the mosque's eastern wall. When the Prophet Muhammad died in 632 CE, he was buried in the apartment of his favorite wife, Aisha, which was located adjacent to the mosque.

The first expansion of the mosque was undertaken by the Prophet himself in 628 CE, after the conquest of Khaybar brought new wealth to the Muslim community. Under Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab, the mosque was expanded again in 638 CE to accommodate the rapidly growing Muslim community. Umar's expansion was modest but significant, nearly doubling the size of the structure.

The most elaborate early expansion came under Caliph Uthman ibn Affan in 649 CE. Uthman purchased surrounding houses and demolished them to enlarge the mosque, constructing walls of cut stone and a teak roof. Under the Umayyad Caliph al-Walid I (705–715 CE), the mosque was rebuilt on an even grander scale, incorporating the apartments of the Prophet's wives — and thus the Prophet's tomb — into the mosque proper. Al-Walid's architect introduced the first domed structure over the tomb and included elaborate mosaics and gilded columns.

The Abbasid caliphs also made contributions to the mosque's development. Al-Mahdi's expansion in 778 CE extended the building to the north. Several subsequent caliphs added minarets, restored damaged sections, and beautified the structure with new materials. The Mamluk Sultan Qaitbay undertook extensive restorations in the 15th century, rebuilding the dome over the Prophet's tomb in its distinctive form.

Under Ottoman rule from the 16th century onward, the sultans invested heavily in maintaining and beautifying the mosque. Sultan Suleiman the Magnificent contributed to its upkeep, and Sultan Abdulmejid I oversaw a major reconstruction between 1848 and 1860 that largely established the form of the mosque before the Saudi era. The distinctive green dome over the Prophet's tomb — so central to its modern visual identity — was painted green during the Ottoman period.

In the Saudi era, the mosque has undergone its most dramatic expansions. King Abdulaziz began construction in 1951 that more than doubled the mosque's capacity. King Fahd's expansion, completed in 1992, was the most ambitious in history, extending the mosque to accommodate over one million worshippers simultaneously. Additional expansions under subsequent kings have continued, with the ongoing Saudi Bin Ladin Group project expected to further increase the mosque's capacity in the coming decades.

## Religious Significance

For Muslims, visiting Al-Masjid an-Nabawi carries immense spiritual weight. The Prophet Muhammad taught that praying in his mosque is equivalent to one thousand prayers elsewhere (excepting the Grand Mosque of Mecca). Many Muslims combine their Hajj pilgrimage with a visit to Medina to pray at the Prophet's Mosque, though this combination is known as a recommended practice rather than an obligation.

The central object of devotion at the mosque — and the subject of much theological controversy — is the Prophet's tomb. The structure containing the tomb is called the "Rawda" or "Garden," and a section of the mosque between the tomb and the Prophet's old pulpit (minbar) is said in hadith to be "a garden from the gardens of Paradise." This area is considered especially blessed for prayer and is perpetually crowded with worshippers.

Muslim theological opinion on visiting and praying near the Prophet's tomb has been divided over the centuries. The majority Sunni position holds that visiting the Prophet's mosque and offering salutations at his tomb is permissible and meritorious. However, the Wahhabi/Salafi interpretation — which now dominates official religious discourse in Saudi Arabia — has long viewed the veneration of tombs with suspicion, fearing it may lead to shirk (associating partners with God). This tension has had practical consequences: the Saudi government has at various times restricted pilgrims' access to the tomb area and demolished adjacent historical structures.

The mosque also contains the original pulpit area where the Prophet delivered his Friday sermons, the first prayer direction (qibla) marker indicating the original direction toward Jerusalem before the qibla was changed to Mecca, and the well of Uthman — an ancient well associated with the third Caliph.

## Architecture & Description

The current Al-Masjid an-Nabawi is a vast complex of modern construction surrounding a historic core. The building covers approximately 400,000 square meters under its roofed sections and can hold up to 1.5 million worshippers during the Hajj season.

The most distinctive architectural feature is the Green Dome (al-Qubbah al-Khadra), which rises approximately 51 meters above the southeastern corner of the mosque over the Prophet's tomb. The dome visible today dates primarily from the Ottoman period but has been rebuilt and repainted multiple times. Beneath it lies the chamber containing the tomb of the Prophet Muhammad and, adjacent to it, the graves of his companions Abu Bakr and Umar — the first two caliphs of Islam.

The modern mosque extension, designed primarily in the 1980s–1990s, features a remarkable series of retractable umbrella-like structures — 250 giant parasols made of fiberglass and steel — that provide shade over the outer courtyards. These folding structures open during the day to shield worshippers from the intense heat and close at night or during rain. They are among the most technically sophisticated architectural features of any mosque in the world.

The mosque has ten minarets in the modern complex, the tallest rising to 105 meters. The prayer halls are equipped with air conditioning, elaborate marble flooring, and acoustic systems. The library within the complex holds thousands of volumes of Islamic scholarship.

## Pilgrimage & Rituals

While visiting Medina is not an obligatory component of the Hajj pilgrimage, approximately 95% of Hajj pilgrims visit the Prophet's Mosque in conjunction with their pilgrimage. Many pilgrims also make separate journeys to Medina outside of the Hajj season.

Upon entering the mosque, pilgrims typically offer prayers in the Rawda — the "Garden" between the tomb and the minbar — which is considered particularly auspicious for prayer. Worshippers then proceed to the Prophet's tomb to offer salutations (salawat), saying "Peace be upon you, O Messenger of God." The adjacent grave of Abu Bakr and Umar receive similar salutations.

The visit to the Prophet's Mosque (ziyara) is typically accompanied by prayers in the mosque itself, reading the Quran, and visiting associated historical sites in Medina. These include Masjid Quba — the first mosque ever built in Islam, located in the southern outskirts of Medina — and the Masjid al-Qiblatayn (Mosque of the Two Qiblas), where the Prophet received the command to redirect prayers from Jerusalem to Mecca. The Uhud battlefield and the associated martyrs' cemetery are also important sites nearby.

## Modern Significance

Medina and its Prophet's Mosque are governed by the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia under arrangements giving the Saudi monarchy the title "Custodian of the Two Holy Mosques" — a title adopted by King Fahd in 1986 that underscores the religious legitimacy the Saudi state derives from its stewardship of Islam's two holiest cities.

The expansion of Al-Masjid an-Nabawi has been, like that of the Grand Mosque in Mecca, a priority for successive Saudi rulers who see the accommodation of pilgrims as both a religious duty and a source of political legitimacy. The economic impact of religious tourism to Medina is enormous, supporting a vast hospitality and service industry.

Controversies have accompanied the development of both Medina and the mosque. Critics have documented the demolition of numerous historically significant sites from the early Islamic period, including buildings associated with the Prophet's companions, early mosques, and even the site of Muhammad's birthplace. The Ottoman-era Ajyad Fortress near Mecca was demolished in 2002 to make way for the Abraj al-Bait hotel complex, sparking international controversy. Scholars and preservation advocates argue that these demolitions represent a permanent loss of irreplaceable Islamic heritage.

Non-Muslims are not permitted to enter Medina's central sacred zone (al-Haram) surrounding the mosque, making it one of the most restricted major religious sites in the world.

## Timeline

- **622 CE**: The Prophet Muhammad arrives in Medina following the Hijra; construction of the original mosque begins.
- **628 CE**: Muhammad oversees the first expansion of the mosque.
- **632 CE**: Muhammad dies; he is buried in the apartment of Aisha, adjacent to the mosque.
- **638 CE**: Caliph Umar expands the mosque; constructs a roof.
- **649 CE**: Caliph Uthman undertakes major reconstruction using cut stone and teak.
- **707–709 CE**: Umayyad Caliph al-Walid I rebuilds the mosque, incorporating the tomb into the structure; first dome over the tomb.
- **778 CE**: Abbasid Caliph al-Mahdi extends the mosque to the north.
- **1481 CE**: Mamluk Sultan Qaitbay reconstructs the dome over the Prophet's tomb.
- **1817 CE**: Ottoman Sultan Mahmud II rebuilds the mosque; the dome is painted green for the first time.
- **1848–1860**: Major Ottoman reconstruction under Sultan Abdulmejid I.
- **1951**: King Abdulaziz of Saudi Arabia begins the first major Saudi-era expansion.
- **1986**: King Fahd adopts the title "Custodian of the Two Holy Mosques."
- **1992**: The King Fahd Expansion is completed; the mosque can now hold one million worshippers.
- **2012**: The King Abdullah Expansion project begins, aiming to triple the mosque's capacity.
- **2020**: COVID-19 restricts access to the mosque; Hajj held with dramatically reduced numbers.
