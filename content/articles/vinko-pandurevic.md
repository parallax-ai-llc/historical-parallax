## Summary

Bosnian Serb brigade commander convicted of aiding and abetting murder for his role during the Srebrenica genocide. He allowed some Bosniaks to escape through his lines but participated in executions.

## Additional Context

## Bosnian War and Srebrenica

Pandurević’s role is evaluated within the Bosnian War (1992–1995), during which mass atrocities were committed by multiple armed forces and political authorities.

The Srebrenica genocide (July 1995) is recognized internationally as one of the most severe crimes in Europe since World War II.

## Command Responsibility

As a brigade commander, the central questions in international criminal law typically include:

- What a commander knew or should have known about crimes committed by subordinates.
- What steps were taken (or not taken) to prevent crimes or punish perpetrators.
- Whether actions materially assisted the commission of crimes.

The public record also notes claims that he allowed some people to pass through his lines, while still being held criminally responsible for other actions connected to the killings.

## ICTY Proceedings

The International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY) prosecuted many senior and mid-level figures from the conflict.

Pandurević’s case illustrates how the ICTY:

- Constructed factual narratives from military orders, communications, and witness testimony.
- Distinguished between direct perpetration and aiding-and-abetting liability.

## Sentence and Broader Context

A prison sentence in an international tribunal context is commonly discussed alongside:

- Appeals and sentence reductions or adjustments.
- Post-conviction release after serving a portion of the sentence.
- Ongoing regional disputes over historical memory and responsibility.
