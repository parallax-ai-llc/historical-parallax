## Summary

Charles V (1500-1558) was one of the most powerful monarchs in European history, ruling over an empire "on which the sun never set." Born into the Habsburg dynasty, he inherited a vast collection of territories including Spain, the Netherlands, parts of Italy, Austria, and the Holy Roman Empire, making him the most powerful ruler since Charlemagne. His reign was marked by constant warfare against France, the Ottoman Empire, and Protestant reformers, as he struggled to maintain Catholic unity and imperial authority across his diverse domains. Despite his immense power and tireless efforts to preserve Christian Europe, Charles ultimately abdicated in 1556, exhausted by the impossibility of governing such vast territories and preventing the religious fragmentation of his realm. His legacy remains controversial, viewed by some as a defender of Christendom and by others as an authoritarian who resisted necessary religious reform.

---

## Early Life and Background

Charles was born on February 24, 1500, in the Prinsenhof palace in Ghent, Flanders (modern-day Belgium), into one of Europe's most powerful dynasties[^1]. He was the eldest son of Philip the Handsome, Duke of Burgundy and King of Castile, and Joanna of Castile, daughter of the Catholic Monarchs Ferdinand II of Aragon and Isabella I of Castile[^2]. This lineage made Charles heir to an unprecedented combination of territories from both the Habsburg and Trastámara dynasties.

Charles's childhood was marked by tragedy and political upheaval. His father Philip died suddenly in 1506 when Charles was only six years old, and his mother Joanna suffered from severe mental illness, earning her the epithet "Joanna the Mad"[^3]. Charles was raised primarily in the Netherlands under the guardianship of his paternal aunt, Margaret of Austria, who served as regent of the Habsburg Netherlands[^4]. She provided him with an excellent education befitting a future ruler, though Charles would always consider French his native language and struggled with German and Spanish throughout his life.

His education was supervised by Adrian of Utrecht (later Pope Adrian VI), who instilled in Charles a deep sense of Catholic piety and duty[^5]. Charles was trained in the chivalric traditions of Burgundy, learning horsemanship, military tactics, and courtly manners. He also studied classical literature, theology, and the arts of governance. Despite his later reputation as a skilled diplomat and administrator, Charles was reportedly a quiet and reserved youth, overshadowed by the weight of his expected inheritance[^6].

The young prince's fortunes changed dramatically with a series of deaths and inheritances. In 1515, at age fifteen, he was declared of age and assumed rule of the Burgundian Netherlands[^7]. The death of his maternal grandfather Ferdinand II of Aragon in 1516 made him King of Spain (as Charles I) and ruler of the Spanish territories in Italy and the Americas[^8]. Three years later, the death of his paternal grandfather Maximilian I in 1519 made him Archduke of Austria and a candidate for Holy Roman Emperor, a title he secured through substantial bribes to the German electors[^9].

## Rise to Power

Charles's ascension to power was neither smooth nor universally accepted. When he arrived in Spain in 1517 to claim his inheritance, he was viewed with suspicion as a foreign prince who spoke no Spanish and brought with him Burgundian advisers who monopolized key positions[^10]. His election as Holy Roman Emperor in 1519 required an enormous expenditure—approximately 850,000 florins in bribes to the seven imperial electors, money borrowed primarily from the Fugger banking family[^11]. He defeated his main rival, King Francis I of France, who also sought the imperial crown, setting the stage for decades of Habsburg-Valois rivalry.

Charles's early reign in Spain faced immediate crisis with the Revolt of the Comuneros (1520-1522), an uprising of Castilian cities against his rule[^12]. The rebels protested Charles's absence from Spain, his appointment of foreigners to important offices, and his use of Spanish resources for imperial purposes. While Charles was away at his imperial coronation in Aachen, his forces brutally suppressed the revolt at the Battle of Villalar in 1521, executing the rebel leaders and reasserting royal authority[^13]. This harsh response established Charles's reputation for firmness in dealing with internal dissent.

The young emperor faced an even greater challenge from the Ottoman Empire under Suleiman the Magnificent, who launched a series of campaigns that threatened the very heart of Christian Europe[^14]. In 1521, the Ottomans captured Belgrade, the key fortress protecting Hungary. When Hungary fell to the Ottomans at the Battle of Mohács in 1526, Charles's brother Ferdinand was elected King of Hungary and Bohemia, bringing these kingdoms into the Habsburg sphere but also making the dynasty responsible for defending them against Turkish aggression[^15].

Charles's determination to maintain Catholic unity was tested by Martin Luther and the Protestant Reformation. At the Diet of Worms in 1521, Charles personally confronted Luther, declaring "a single friar who goes counter to all Christianity for a thousand years must be wrong"[^16]. However, his need for support from Protestant German princes against France and the Ottomans prevented him from taking decisive military action against the reformers for decades. This fundamental tension between religious conviction and political necessity would plague his entire reign.

His rivalry with Francis I of France dominated European politics for three decades. The two monarchs fought four major wars (1521-1526, 1526-1529, 1536-1538, and 1542-1544) over control of Italy, particularly the Duchy of Milan and the Kingdom of Naples[^17]. Charles's greatest triumph came at the Battle of Pavia in 1525, where imperial forces captured Francis himself, forcing him to sign the humiliating Treaty of Madrid[^18]. Though Francis repudiated the treaty upon his release, the victory established Charles as the dominant power in Italy.

---

## Major Achievements

Charles V's foremost achievement was creating and maintaining the largest European empire since Charlemagne, ruling over territories spanning from Spain and the Americas to Central Europe and parts of Italy[^19]. At the height of his power, his domains included the Kingdom of Spain, the Spanish Netherlands, the Franche-Comté, the Austrian hereditary lands, the Kingdom of Naples, the Duchy of Milan, and numerous smaller territories. His reign also coincided with Spanish conquests in the Americas, with Hernán Cortés conquering the Aztec Empire (1519-1521) and Francisco Pizarro subduing the Inca Empire (1532-1572), bringing vast new territories and wealth under Habsburg control[^20].

In military affairs, Charles demonstrated considerable skill and personal courage. Unlike many monarchs who directed battles from a distance, Charles personally led his troops in numerous campaigns, fighting in Italy, Germany, France, and North Africa[^21]. His victory at the Battle of Pavia in 1525 was one of the most decisive battles of the Renaissance, eliminating French power in Italy for a generation. In 1535, he led a successful crusade against Tunis, capturing the city from the Ottoman-backed Barbarossa and freeing thousands of Christian slaves[^22]. This campaign briefly revived the ideal of Christian crusading and enhanced Charles's prestige throughout Catholic Europe.

Charles's defense of Christian Europe against Ottoman expansion, though ultimately unable to reverse Turkish advances, prevented further Ottoman penetration into Central Europe. After the Ottoman siege of Vienna in 1529, Charles organized the defense of Austria and Hungary, preventing Suleiman from advancing beyond Hungary[^23]. His brother Ferdinand, whom Charles appointed to govern the Austrian lands, established the Military Frontier system that would protect Habsburg territories for centuries. Though Charles was often absent from the eastern front due to commitments in Western Europe, his diplomatic and financial support was crucial to halting Ottoman expansion[^24].

In administrative terms, Charles developed sophisticated governmental structures to manage his vast and diverse territories. He established viceroyalties in Spain's American possessions, created advisory councils for different regions, and developed a system of communication that allowed him to govern territories thousands of miles apart[^25]. His court became a cosmopolitan center that moved between his various domains, though he increasingly relied on capable administrators to govern in his absence. The Council of the Indies, reorganized during his reign, provided systematic oversight of Spain's American empire[^26].

Charles's patronage of arts and learning contributed to the flourishing of Renaissance culture in his domains. He supported artists including Titian, who painted numerous portraits of the emperor, and composers including Nicolas Gombert and Thomas Crecquillon[^27]. His court attracted humanist scholars, and he founded the Colegio de España in Bologna to promote Spanish scholarship. Charles also commissioned literary works celebrating his reign, including the epic poem "Carolea" by Jerónimo Sempere[^28].

The emperor convened the Council of Trent (1545-1563), which became the cornerstone of the Catholic Counter-Reformation[^29]. Though Charles initially hoped the council would heal the religious rift with Protestants, it instead clarified Catholic doctrine, reformed clerical abuses, and strengthened the institutional church. The Jesuit order, founded during Charles's reign and officially approved in 1540, became one of the most effective instruments of Catholic renewal[^30]. These religious reforms helped ensure that Catholicism would remain vibrant and competitive with Protestantism.

Charles achieved a significant military victory over Protestant forces at the Battle of Mühlberg in 1547, capturing the leaders of the Schmalkaldic League and temporarily reasserting imperial authority over Protestant German princes[^31]. Titian's famous equestrian portrait commemorating this victory depicted Charles as a triumphant Christian knight. However, this military success proved temporary, as the Protestant princes soon regrouped and forced Charles to accept religious compromise in the Peace of Augsburg (1555)[^32].

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his achievements, Charles V's reign was marked by significant failures and controversial policies that have drawn sustained criticism from historians. His inability to prevent the permanent religious division of Europe stands as his greatest failure[^33]. Charles spent his entire reign attempting to suppress or reconcile with Protestantism, yet by the time of his abdication, religious division had become permanent. The Peace of Augsburg (1555), which Charles reluctantly accepted, established the principle of "cuius regio, eius religio" (whose realm, his religion), allowing each German prince to determine the religion of his territory—a compromise that Charles viewed as a defeat of his life's mission[^34].

Charles's treatment of indigenous peoples in the Americas remains highly controversial. While he issued the New Laws (1542) attempting to protect Native Americans from the worst abuses of the encomienda system, enforcement was weak and often ignored by Spanish colonists[^35]. The conquest and colonization during his reign resulted in the deaths of millions of indigenous people through disease, warfare, and forced labor. The famous Valladolid debate (1550-1551) between Bartolomé de las Casas and Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda over the rights of indigenous peoples revealed the fundamental contradictions in Spanish colonial policy[^36]. Though Charles gave the debate a hearing, Spanish exploitation of native populations continued largely unchanged.

His financial mismanagement led to multiple state bankruptcies that devastated Spain's economy. Charles's constant warfare required enormous expenditures that far exceeded his revenues, despite the influx of American silver[^37]. He declared bankruptcy in 1557, shortly after his abdication, leaving his son Philip II with crushing debts. Charles borrowed heavily from German and Italian bankers, particularly the Fugger and Welser families, mortgaging future revenues and establishing a pattern of deficit spending that would plague Spanish finances for generations[^38].

Charles's harsh suppression of the Comuneros revolt and his treatment of political opponents revealed an authoritarian streak. The execution of comunero leaders like Juan de Padilla, Juan Bravo, and Francisco Maldonado demonstrated his willingness to use extreme violence against internal dissent[^39]. He also dealt ruthlessly with Protestant reformers, approving the execution of numerous Protestant leaders and establishing the Spanish Inquisition's jurisdiction over heresy in his domains[^40].

His nepotistic practices in distributing offices and benefices to family members and Burgundian favorites provoked resentment throughout his realms. When Charles first arrived in Spain, his Burgundian adviser Guillaume de Croÿ was appointed Archbishop of Toledo, Spain's wealthiest ecclesiastical position, despite being only twenty years old and never visiting the archdiocese[^41]. Such appointments fed Spanish resentment of foreign influence and contributed to the Comuneros revolt.

Charles's military campaigns, while sometimes successful, also included notable failures and disasters. His 1541 expedition against Algiers ended in catastrophic failure when storms destroyed much of his fleet and forced a humiliating retreat[^42]. His wars against France were inconclusive and enormously expensive, achieving no lasting territorial gains despite decades of bloodshed. The sack of Rome in 1527 by mutinous imperial troops, though not directly ordered by Charles, occurred because he failed to pay his armies and resulted in the deaths of thousands of civilians and the destruction of priceless artistic and cultural treasures[^43].

Critics argue that Charles's vision of universal Catholic monarchy was fundamentally anachronistic and doomed to fail in an era of rising nationalism and religious diversity[^44]. His attempts to impose religious uniformity through force and his resistance to meaningful compromise with Protestants prolonged religious conflicts and increased suffering. Some historians suggest that earlier acceptance of religious pluralism might have prevented the devastating religious wars that would plague Europe in the following century[^45].

## Personal Life

Charles married Isabella of Portugal in 1526, a union that was both a love match and a political alliance strengthening ties between the Spanish and Portuguese crowns[^46]. Contemporary accounts describe their marriage as genuinely affectionate, unusual for royal unions of the era. Isabella bore Charles six children, including the future Philip II of Spain and several daughters who would become important figures through their marriages. Charles was reportedly devastated by Isabella's death in 1539 and never remarried, though this may have been as much for political reasons as emotional ones[^47].

Despite his faithfulness to Isabella during her life, Charles acknowledged several illegitimate children from before his marriage. The most famous was Margaret of Parma, daughter of Johanna Maria van der Gheynst, whom Charles later appointed as Governor of the Netherlands[^48]. He also fathered Don Juan of Austria, who would become a celebrated military commander and victor at the Battle of Lepanto. Charles ensured these illegitimate children received appropriate education and positions, demonstrating a sense of paternal responsibility even as he excluded them from succession to his primary titles[^49].

Charles suffered from severe health problems throughout his life that significantly impacted his ability to govern. He inherited the prominent "Habsburg jaw" (mandibular prognathism) from his ancestry, which made eating difficult and contributed to digestive problems[^50]. He also suffered from gout, which became increasingly debilitating in his later years, sometimes confining him to bed or requiring him to be carried in a litter. Some historians suggest he may have also suffered from diabetes, given his symptoms and dietary habits[^51].

Contemporaries described Charles as serious and reserved, lacking the charisma of his rival Francis I but possessing determination and a strong sense of duty. Physically, he was of medium height with the characteristic Habsburg features—a prominent lower jaw, thick lower lip, and long face[^52]. He preferred dark clothing in the Spanish style, contributing to his austere image. Despite being multilingual, he reportedly said "I speak Spanish to God, Italian to women, French to men, and German to my horse," revealing both his linguistic abilities and his prejudices[^53].

Charles had diverse interests beyond politics and warfare. He was an avid hunter and maintained extensive hunting preserves at his various residences. He appreciated music and maintained a chapel choir that traveled with his court[^54]. He also showed interest in mechanical devices, particularly clocks, which he collected and which became a passion during his retirement. Contemporary accounts suggest he enjoyed good food and wine, perhaps to excess, contributing to his health problems[^55].

His relationship with his family was complex. He was close to his sisters, particularly Mary of Hungary and Eleanor of Austria, whom he appointed to govern the Netherlands and France respectively[^56]. His relationship with his son Philip was more distant, partly due to their different personalities—Philip was even more reserved and rigid than his father. Charles worked diligently to prepare Philip for rule, but worried about his son's ability to maintain the vast empire[^57].

## Legacy

Charles V's decision to abdicate in 1556 was unprecedented for a Holy Roman Emperor and shocked Europe. Suffering from gout, exhausted by decades of warfare, and recognizing the impossibility of governing his vast domains, Charles divided his inheritance between his brother Ferdinand (who received the Austrian lands and the imperial title) and his son Philip (who received Spain, the Netherlands, and Italy)[^58]. This division permanently split the Habsburg dynasty into Austrian and Spanish branches, fundamentally reshaping European politics for centuries to come.

Charles spent his final years in retirement at the monastery of Yuste in Spain, though he maintained correspondence with his son and continued to advise on political matters[^59]. Contrary to romantic legends that he spent his time solely in prayer and clock-making, Charles remained politically engaged and occasionally criticized Philip's decisions. He died on September 21, 1558, officially from malaria, though his various chronic conditions had weakened him considerably[^60]. His final words were reportedly "Ay, Jesus," and he was initially buried at Yuste before Philip later moved his remains to the Escorial[^61].

Charles's impact on European political structures was profound and lasting. The Habsburg dominance in European affairs that he established would continue for centuries, with the Austrian Habsburgs ruling as Holy Roman Emperors until 1806 and the Spanish Habsburgs ruling Spain until 1700[^62]. The rivalry between France and the Habsburgs that intensified during Charles's reign would shape European diplomacy through the Thirty Years' War and beyond. His failure to maintain imperial unity, however, meant that Germany would remain politically fragmented until the 19th century[^63].

The Spanish Empire that flourished under Charles's rule would become the dominant global power of the 16th and early 17th centuries. The administrative structures, colonial policies, and cultural patterns established during his reign shaped Spanish America for three centuries[^64]. The flow of American silver through Spanish-controlled territories fundamentally altered European economics, contributing to inflation and the growth of commercial capitalism[^65].

Charles's role in religious history is equally significant, though ambiguous. His steadfast defense of Catholicism and support for the Council of Trent helped ensure that the Catholic Church would successfully reform and compete with Protestantism[^66]. However, his military and political opposition to Protestant reform contributed to religious warfare and persecution. The religious division of Europe that crystallized during his reign—with Protestant Northern Europe and Catholic Southern Europe—would persist for centuries and contribute to devastating conflicts like the Thirty Years' War[^67].

In art and culture, Charles's patronage and the cosmopolitan nature of his court contributed to cultural exchange across Europe. Titian's portraits of Charles established new conventions for imperial portraiture that would influence European art for generations[^68]. The ideal of the Christian knight-emperor that Charles embodied, though ultimately unsuccessful politically, remained culturally powerful and influenced literature, art, and political thought[^69].

Modern historians have increasingly recognized Charles as a transitional figure—the last emperor to seriously pursue medieval ideals of universal Christian monarchy while simultaneously presiding over the emergence of the modern state system[^70]. His reign marked both the pinnacle of personal dynastic power (through inheritance and marriage) and the demonstration of its limitations in an era of religious division, emerging nationalism, and changing military technology[^71].

Charles's legacy in the Spanish-speaking world remains particularly strong. He is remembered as the monarch under whom Spain achieved its greatest territorial extent and began its "Golden Age"[^72]. The phrase "the empire on which the sun never sets," first applied to his domains, became a lasting symbol of imperial ambition. Yet this glory came at tremendous cost, both to Spain (through debt and military exhaustion) and to the millions of indigenous Americans who suffered under Spanish colonization[^73].

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Defenders of Charles V emphasize his sincere commitment to his duties and his tireless efforts to maintain Christian unity in an age of unprecedented challenge. Historian Geoffrey Parker argues that Charles "displayed remarkable energy, determination, and courage" in managing an empire of unprecedented scope, fighting on multiple fronts against formidable enemies while attempting to preserve religious unity[^74]. Parker notes that Charles personally led his armies into battle, risked his life in numerous campaigns, and worked tirelessly at governance despite chronic illness and the enormous logistical challenges of ruling dispersed territories[^75].

Scholars sympathetic to Charles highlight his genuine piety and sense of duty to Christendom. Alfred Kohler, in his comprehensive biography, portrays Charles as a ruler who sincerely believed in his responsibility to protect Catholic Christianity against both external threats (the Ottoman Empire) and internal division (Protestantism)[^76]. Kohler argues that Charles's religious policies, while ultimately unsuccessful, were motivated by genuine conviction rather than mere political ambition. His personal participation in the Council of Trent and his support for Catholic reform demonstrate this commitment[^77].

Some historians praise Charles's relatively enlightened policies toward indigenous Americans, particularly his promulgation of the New Laws and his willingness to allow debate about indigenous rights. Lewis Hanke argued that Charles deserves credit for at least attempting to restrain Spanish colonists and for supporting voices like Bartolomé de las Casas who advocated for indigenous welfare[^78]. While acknowledging that enforcement was inadequate, these scholars note that Charles showed more concern for indigenous rights than most European monarchs of his era[^79].

Charles's administrative innovations and his creation of effective governing structures across his vast empire earn praise from institutional historians. Fernand Braudel, while critical of many aspects of Charles's policies, acknowledged his role in developing the sophisticated bureaucratic systems that would characterize Spanish governance for centuries[^80]. The viceregal system, the councils of state, and the diplomatic networks Charles established represented significant achievements in early modern statecraft[^81].

Cultural historians emphasize Charles's role as a patron of Renaissance art and learning. His support for artists like Titian, his maintenance of humanist scholars at his court, and the cultural exchange facilitated by his multinational empire contributed significantly to European cultural development[^82]. The cosmopolitan nature of his court, which moved between Spanish, German, Italian, and Netherlandish territories, helped spread Renaissance ideas and artistic styles across Europe[^83].

Military historians often praise Charles's personal courage and his successful campaigns, particularly the victory at Pavia and the Tunis crusade. Jeremy Black argues that Charles demonstrated considerable strategic skill in managing multiple military fronts and in his ability to recover from defeats[^84]. His reorganization of Spanish military forces and his patronage of military innovations contributed to Spanish military dominance in the 16th century[^85].

Some scholars present Charles as a tragic figure who pursued noble but impossible goals in an era of unavoidable change. This view, articulated by scholars like Manuel Fernández Álvarez, portrays Charles as the last champion of medieval Christian universalism, doomed by historical forces beyond his control but worthy of admiration for his dedication to his ideals[^86]. His voluntary abdication is seen as a mark of wisdom and self-awareness rare among monarchs[^87].

### Negative Views

Critics of Charles V argue that his pursuit of imperial glory and religious uniformity caused immense suffering and wasted resources on ultimately futile goals. Historian Henry Kamen argues that Charles's constant warfare, motivated by dynastic ambition and religious zealotry, bankrupted Spain and laid the groundwork for its later decline[^88]. Kamen contends that Charles's policies enriched bankers and nobility while impoverishing ordinary Spaniards and wasting the treasure of the Americas on endless wars[^89].

Protestant historians and scholars sympathetic to the Reformation portray Charles as an authoritarian who used military force to suppress religious freedom and legitimate reform. Euan Cameron argues that Charles's intransigent opposition to Protestantism, his support for the Inquisition, and his military campaigns against Protestant princes prolonged religious conflict and increased suffering[^90]. These scholars contend that earlier acceptance of religious pluralism would have prevented much bloodshed and political instability[^91].

Scholars of colonial history condemn Charles for the atrocities committed against indigenous Americans during his reign. While acknowledging the New Laws, critics like Matthew Restall argue that these were ineffective and that Charles bears responsibility for the devastation of indigenous populations through disease, warfare, and forced labor[^92]. The establishment of the encomienda system and the massive expansion of Spanish colonization during his reign resulted in demographic catastrophe for indigenous peoples[^93].

Economic historians often criticize Charles's fiscal irresponsibility and his failure to develop Spain's productive economy. Earl Hamilton and others have argued that Charles wasted Spain's American silver on warfare rather than investing in economic development, establishing patterns of deficit spending and reliance on foreign loans that would plague Spain for centuries[^94]. The multiple bankruptcies during and immediately after his reign demonstrate his fiscal mismanagement[^95].

Some scholars present Charles as fundamentally an absent foreign ruler who never truly understood or cared about the welfare of his various subjects. This view, particularly strong in German and Netherlandish historiography, portrays Charles as exploiting his various territories to pursue dynastic and imperial ambitions that benefited only the Habsburg family[^96]. His suppression of the Comuneros revolt and his extraction of resources from all his territories for imperial purposes support this interpretation[^97].

Historians critical of imperial power structures view Charles as embodying the worst aspects of dynastic monarchy—arbitrary rule, nepotism, religious intolerance, and warfare. John Elliott argues that Charles's reign demonstrated the fundamental unsustainability of personal dynastic empires in the early modern period[^98]. The fact that his empire had to be divided upon his abdication proved that such vast accumulations of power through inheritance and marriage could not be effectively governed[^99].

Recent scholarship influenced by postcolonial perspectives presents Charles as an architect of European imperialism and global inequality. These scholars emphasize how Charles's reign established patterns of European exploitation of non-European peoples that would continue for centuries[^100]. The Columbian Exchange, the Atlantic slave trade, and Spanish colonization—all of which accelerated during his reign—created global systems of inequality whose effects persist to the present day[^101].

## Famous Quotes

> "I speak Spanish to God, Italian to women, French to men, and German to my horse."

This famous quote, attributed to Charles though its authenticity is disputed, reveals his multilingual abilities and the cultural hierarchies of his age[^102].

> "Name me an emperor who was ever struck by a cannonball."

Said to have been spoken during battle when advised to retreat from danger, this quote illustrates Charles's personal courage and his willingness to share the dangers faced by his soldiers[^103].

> "A single friar who goes counter to all Christianity for a thousand years must be wrong."

Spoken at the Diet of Worms regarding Martin Luther, this quote encapsulates Charles's conviction that individual conscience could not override the collective tradition of the Church[^104].

> "Fortune has given to my age what it has denied to past ages; and of all her gifts, the greatest is that she has granted to me the glory of perpetuating my memory by an abdication."

From Charles's abdication speech, this quote reveals his awareness that his voluntary relinquishment of power was historically unprecedented[^105].

> "To God I speak Spanish, to women Italian, to men French, and to my horse German."

An alternate version of the first quote, sometimes reported differently depending on the source[^106].

> "I came, I saw, God conquered."

Charles's message after the victory at Mühlberg, consciously echoing and modifying Julius Caesar's famous "Veni, vidi, vici" to emphasize divine rather than personal agency[^107].
