## Summary

Clark Gable (1901–1960) was an American actor widely regarded as one of the greatest stars in Hollywood history, earning the enduring title "The King of Hollywood." Over a career spanning three decades, he appeared in more than 60 films and became the defining masculine ideal of Golden Age cinema. His combination of rugged charm, effortless charisma, and understated authority made him one of the most bankable stars in the industry throughout the 1930s and 1940s.

Gable's appeal transcended the screen. He embodied a distinctly American archetype — the self-made, unpretentious working-class man who succeeded through grit rather than privilege. His personal life, marked by tragedy and resilience, only deepened the public's fascination with him. From his Oscar-winning performance in *It Happened One Night* (1934) to his iconic role as Rhett Butler in *Gone with the Wind* (1939), Gable left an indelible mark on American popular culture that endures long after his death.

## Early Life & Background

William Clark Gable was born on February 1, 1901, in Cadiz, Ohio, to William Henry Gable, an oil driller, and Adeline Hershelman, who died when Clark was just seven months old. He was raised largely by his father and stepmother in rural Ohio and Pennsylvania. Gable left school at sixteen and worked a series of manual labor jobs, including in oil fields alongside his father, before discovering a passion for theater in his early twenties.

He worked his way through minor stage productions in Oregon and the Pacific Northwest, honing his craft in traveling theater companies. After relocating to Los Angeles in the late 1920s, he eventually secured small roles in silent films before transitioning to sound pictures, where his deep voice proved a distinct asset.

## Rise to Prominence

Gable's Hollywood breakthrough came rapidly after signing with MGM in 1931. His performance in *A Free Soul* (1931) opposite Norma Shearer — in which he played a forceful, morally ambiguous gangster — generated enormous audience response, particularly from women. MGM recognized his star potential and began casting him in increasingly prominent roles alongside major stars including Joan Crawford and Jean Harlow.

His career reached its peak in 1934 when he won the Academy Award for Best Actor for *It Happened One Night*, directed by Frank Capra — a rare film that swept all five major Oscar categories. The role of the wisecracking journalist Peter Warne cemented his image as a romantic lead with irreverent edge. Five years later, his casting as Rhett Butler in *Gone with the Wind* (1939) became arguably the most celebrated piece of casting in Hollywood history, a role producer David O. Selznick pursued aggressively after public demand made Gable the only conceivable choice.

## Key Achievements

- Academy Award for Best Actor, *It Happened One Night* (1934)
- Starred in *Gone with the Wind* (1939), which remains one of the highest-grossing films of all time when adjusted for inflation
- Appeared in over 60 feature films between 1924 and 1961
- Named the seventh greatest male screen legend by the American Film Institute in 1999
- Served as a U.S. Army Air Forces officer during World War II, flying combat missions over Europe despite being exempt due to age and celebrity status

## Controversies & Criticism

Gable's legacy carries a significant shadow. In 1935, he was accused of raping actress Loretta Young; Young later acknowledged that a child she bore in 1935 and raised as an adopted daughter was in fact Gable's, and that the conception was non-consensual. This claim, corroborated by Young's daughter Judy Lewis in her 1994 memoir, has reshaped how historians assess Gable's personal conduct.

Additionally, Gable was known to have pressured MGM to remove his frequent co-star and friend William Haines from the studio after Haines refused to hide his homosexuality — a decision that effectively ended Haines's acting career. Critics have also noted that like many stars of his era, Gable largely accepted the racial segregation embedded in Hollywood's practices without public objection.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Gable's influence on the archetype of Hollywood masculinity remains profound. The relaxed, unpretentious anti-hero he embodied — self-confident without being pompous, romantic without being soft — became the template for generations of leading men that followed, from Burt Lancaster to George Clooney. His military service during World War II, taken at genuine personal risk, elevated his public standing beyond mere celebrity.

His final completed film, *The Misfits* (1961), written by Arthur Miller and co-starring Marilyn Monroe, is considered among the finest late-career performances in Hollywood history. Gable died on November 16, 1960, of a heart attack, just days after completing principal photography.
