## Summary

US Army psychiatrist who killed 13 people and wounded 30 in a mass shooting at Fort Hood, Texas in 2009. He was motivated by radical Islamist ideology and had communicated with Anwar al-Awlaki.

## Background

Hasan was a U.S. Army officer and psychiatrist whose work brought him into contact with military personnel experiencing trauma and stress. Public reporting after the attack focused on warning signs, internal communications, and how institutions interpret potential indicators of extremist violence.

## 2009 Fort Hood Shooting

The 2009 attack at Fort Hood was one of the deadliest shootings on a U.S. military base. The incident raised immediate questions about base security, threat assessment, and how to categorize violence that involves ideological motivation but occurs within domestic institutions.

## Contacts and Radicalization Concerns

Hasan’s communications with Anwar al-Awlaki (an extremist cleric later killed in a U.S. drone strike) became a central element in public accounts of the case. Subsequent investigations examined what was known prior to the shooting and how that information was handled within military and federal channels.

## Trial and Sentence

He was tried by court-martial, convicted in 2013 of multiple counts of premeditated murder and attempted murder, and sentenced to death. The case drew attention to issues such as legal process in military courts and the treatment of high-profile mass-violence defendants.

## Aftermath and Impact

Fort Hood became a reference point in U.S. discussions about insider threats, prevention frameworks, and the intersection of mental health, ideology, and violence. It also influenced policy debates about information sharing and the balance between civil liberties and security screening.
