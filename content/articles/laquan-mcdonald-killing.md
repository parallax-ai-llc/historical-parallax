## Summary

Chicago police officer Jason Van Dyke shot 17-year-old Laquan McDonald 16 times. Dashboard camera video was withheld for 13 months; its release led to Van Dyke's murder conviction.

## Incident overview

Laquan McDonald, a 17-year-old Black teenager, was shot and killed by Chicago police officer Jason Van Dyke on October 20, 2014. The encounter became nationally prominent because a police dashboard-camera video appeared to contradict initial public accounts, and because the video’s release was delayed for more than a year.

## Video release and public response

The dashcam footage was not made public until 2015, after legal action and public pressure. When released, the video prompted large protests and intensified scrutiny of the Chicago Police Department (CPD) and city leadership. The delayed release became part of the broader controversy, raising questions about transparency, accountability, and the handling of police-involved shootings.

## Criminal proceedings

Van Dyke was charged and later convicted (in 2018) in connection with McDonald’s death, a rare outcome in cases involving on-duty police shootings in major U.S. cities. Coverage of the trial often focused on the number of shots fired, the timing of the encounter, and how the video evidence shaped juror understanding.

## Political and institutional fallout

The case had significant institutional consequences in Chicago, including leadership changes and ongoing debate over police oversight. It also became intertwined with wider national discussions about:

- The role of video evidence in police accountability.
- Prosecutorial decision-making and charging standards.
- Public-records law, settlements, and the timing of disclosures.

## Broader significance

The Laquan McDonald case is often cited in analyses of policing and governance as an example of how recorded evidence, public pressure, and legal processes can interact to produce accountability outcomes. It is also referenced in discussions of structural reforms, such as independent review mechanisms and federal or state-level oversight of police departments.
