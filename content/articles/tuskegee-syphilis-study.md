## Summary

Unethical US government study from 1932-1972 where 600 Black men were studied for syphilis without informed consent and denied treatment even after penicillin became available.

## Additional Context

The Tuskegee Study refers to a long-running U.S. Public Health Service study (begun in 1932 and publicly exposed in 1972) that observed the natural course of untreated syphilis in Black men in Alabama.

### Ethical violations

- Participants were **not given informed consent** for the true purpose of the study.
- Even after penicillin became the standard effective treatment for syphilis, treatment was withheld or obstructed.
- The study is widely used as a case example in bioethics and research ethics training.

### Policy and legacy

- The public outcry contributed to reforms in U.S. human-subject research oversight.
- Institutional review boards (IRBs) and clearer informed-consent standards became central to later research governance.
