## Summary

Lee Joon-seok, captain of the Sewol ferry that sank off South Korea's coast in 2014 killing 304 people, mostly high school students. He abandoned ship while passengers were told to stay in their cabins.

## The Sewol disaster (context)

- The **MV Sewol** sank on 16 April 2014 during a voyage from Incheon to Jeju, becoming one of South Korea's deadliest peacetime disasters.
- Many of the victims were students on a school trip, and early reporting highlighted that passengers were instructed to **remain in place**, limiting their ability to evacuate.
- Subsequent inquiries and public debate focused on vessel modifications, cargo loading practices, operational decision-making, and failures in emergency response.

## Conduct and criminal liability (high-level)

- Captain **Lee Joon-seok** became the central figure in public and legal scrutiny, particularly due to accounts that he and some crew left the vessel while passengers were still aboard.
- Prosecutors pursued charges tied to duty of care, abandonment, and responsibility during a maritime emergency.
- Court outcomes reported in mainstream summaries emphasized **gross negligence** and failures to properly order evacuation.

## Trials, appeals, and sentencing

- The captain and members of the crew faced criminal trials in the years following the sinking.
- Sentencing outcomes changed across proceedings in some accounts, reflecting appeals and differing legal interpretations of intent versus negligence.
- Regardless of legal nuances, the case remained symbolically central to broader accountability debates about the disaster.

## Societal and political impact

- The Sewol tragedy catalyzed sustained public mourning and activism by victims' families.
- It contributed to wide-ranging discussions about regulation, safety culture, government crisis management, and transparency.
- The captain's case is often referenced as a focal point for how individual accountability intersects with systemic failure.
