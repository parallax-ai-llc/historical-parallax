## Summary

Welsh pirate captain who used deception rather than force, often disguising himself as a legitimate trader. Killed in an ambush on the island of Príncipe; succeeded by Bartholomew Roberts.

## Background

- Howell Davis is commonly described as a Welsh pirate active during the early 18th-century “Golden Age of Piracy.”
- His notoriety is closely tied to his brief period as a captain and to the succession of Bartholomew Roberts after his death.

## Noted Methods and Reputation

Accounts frequently emphasize tactics based on misdirection:

- presenting himself as a legitimate merchant or privateer,
- approaching targets under a pretense of negotiation or trade, and
- relying on surprise and intimidation rather than prolonged combat.

These approaches are often contrasted with pirates who preferred direct violence or large-scale engagements.

## Death and Succession

- Davis was killed in 1719 on Príncipe (in the Gulf of Guinea) in an ambush.
- After his death, Bartholomew Roberts (also known as “Black Bart”) is widely reported to have been elected or assumed command.
- The transition is notable because Roberts would go on to become one of the most successful pirate captains of the era.

## Historical Notes (High Level)

- Many pirate biographies from this period mix documentation with later retellings; details can vary by source.
- Even so, Davis is consistently remembered as an example of how social engineering and credible disguise could be weaponized at sea.

## Related Topics

- **Golden Age of Piracy:** a period (roughly the late 1600s to early 1700s) associated with heightened piracy in the Atlantic and beyond.
- **Príncipe:** an island off Central Africa that appears in multiple piracy-era narratives.
