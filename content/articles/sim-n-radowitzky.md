## Summary

Ukrainian-Argentine anarchist who assassinated Buenos Aires police chief Ramón Falcón in 1909 with a bomb. He was sentenced to life in the brutal Ushuaia prison. Later pardoned and fought in the Spanish Civil War.

## Background and Context

Radowitzky was born in the Russian Empire (in what is now Ukraine) and migrated as a teenager to Argentina, where he entered the world of immigrant labor and radical politics. Early-20th-century Buenos Aires saw intense conflict between the state and working-class movements, including strikes, repression, and ideological battles among socialists, anarchists, and nationalists.

In 1909, police chief Ramón Falcón became a symbol of state violence after overseeing a harsh crackdown on demonstrations. Radowitzky’s attack was framed by supporters as retaliation for repression and by authorities as a terrorist act.

## Assassination (1909)

- **Method**: a bomb thrown at Falcón’s carriage.
- **Immediate outcome**: Falcón and his aide were fatally wounded.
- **Political meaning**: the killing intensified debates over public order, political violence, and the treatment of labor organizers.

## Trial and Imprisonment

Radowitzky was sentenced to life imprisonment and sent to **Ushuaia**, a remote penal colony often described as one of Argentina’s harshest prisons. His incarceration became a cause célèbre for parts of the Argentine left, and campaigns for clemency periodically resurfaced.

- **Conditions**: cold climate, isolation, and strict discipline were central to the prison’s reputation.
- **Public campaigns**: anarchist and labor circles used his case to highlight the penal system and state repression.

## Later Life

After receiving a pardon in 1930, Radowitzky was deported to Uruguay. He later participated in the **Spanish Civil War**, reflecting the transnational nature of leftist activism in the 1930s. In his final years, he lived in Mexico, where he died in 1956.

## Legacy

Radowitzky’s story is often discussed as a case study in:

- anarchist militancy in the early 1900s,
- the Argentine state’s response to radical labor politics,
- the symbolism of political prisoners for social movements.

Accounts differ sharply on moral evaluation: some emphasize the assassination as political terror, while others treat it as insurgent retaliation within a period of state violence.
