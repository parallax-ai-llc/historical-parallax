## Summary

Milton Friedman (1912–2006) was an American economist whose ideas reshaped the foundations of modern economic thought. A leading figure of the Chicago School of Economics, he championed free markets, limited government, and individual liberty at a time when Keynesian demand management dominated mainstream policy. His work spanned monetary theory, consumption analysis, and political philosophy, earning him the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences in 1976.

Friedman's influence extended far beyond academia. His advocacy for monetarism — the idea that controlling the money supply is the primary lever for managing inflation and economic stability — directly shaped the policy responses of central banks worldwide during the inflationary crises of the 1970s and 1980s. His popular writing and television series made him one of the most publicly visible economists of the twentieth century.

## Early Life & Background

Milton Friedman was born on July 31, 1912, in Brooklyn, New York, to Ashkenazi Jewish immigrants from Beregszász (in present-day Ukraine). He grew up in Rahway, New Jersey, where his father died when Milton was fifteen, leaving the family in modest financial circumstances. Despite this, he won a competitive scholarship to Rutgers University, graduating in 1932. He earned his MA from the University of Chicago in 1933 and his PhD from Columbia University in 1946. His time at Chicago — studying under Frank Knight and Jacob Viner — proved formative, instilling a rigorous commitment to empirical economics and market liberalism.

## Rise to Prominence

After working at the National Bureau of Economic Research and serving as a statistical analyst for the U.S. Treasury during World War II, Friedman joined the University of Chicago faculty in 1946, where he would remain for thirty years. His 1957 book *A Theory of the Consumption Function* challenged Keynesian models with his permanent income hypothesis. His 1963 collaboration with Anna Schwartz, *A Monetary History of the United States, 1867–1960*, argued controversially — and influentially — that the Federal Reserve's contraction of the money supply caused the Great Depression. His 1968 presidential address to the American Economic Association introduced the concept of the natural rate of unemployment, anticipating the stagflation of the 1970s.

## Key Achievements

- Awarded the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences in 1976 for contributions to consumption analysis, monetary history, and monetary theory.
- Authored *Capitalism and Freedom* (1962), a foundational text arguing for school vouchers, a negative income tax, and abolition of occupational licensing.
- Co-wrote *A Monetary History of the United States* (1963), which redefined understanding of the Great Depression's causes.
- Hosted *Free to Choose* (1980), a ten-part PBS television series (and companion book) that reached millions of viewers globally.
- Advised the Reagan administration and influenced Thatcher-era economic policy in the UK.

## Controversies & Criticism

Friedman's association with Chilean economists trained at Chicago — the "Chicago Boys" — who advised Augusto Pinochet's military dictatorship drew sustained criticism. Critics argued that free-market reforms implemented under authoritarian rule caused severe social harm. His acceptance of a lecture invitation to Chile in 1975 provoked protests. More broadly, post-2008 critics pointed to deregulatory frameworks inspired by his ideas as contributing to financial instability. His doctrine that corporations owe obligations solely to shareholders — articulated in a 1970 *New York Times* essay — remains contested in debates over corporate social responsibility.

## Legacy & Influence

Friedman's monetarist framework became the intellectual basis for inflation-targeting regimes adopted by central banks globally. His policy proposals — school choice, drug legalization, volunteer military service — moved from fringe to mainstream debate. Ben Bernanke, speaking at Friedman's 90th birthday in 2002, acknowledged on behalf of the Federal Reserve: "You're right, we did it. We're very sorry." Friedman died on November 16, 2006, in San Francisco, leaving a body of work that continues to anchor debates in economics and public policy.
