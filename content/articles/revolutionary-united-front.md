## Summary

Sierra Leonean rebel group infamous for amputating limbs of civilians, using child soldiers, and committing mass rape during the 1991-2002 civil war.

## Civil war context (1991–2002)

The Revolutionary United Front (RUF) is most commonly discussed in the context of the **Sierra Leone Civil War**, a conflict characterized by armed rebellion, state collapse in parts of the country, and severe violence against civilians.

Overviews of the war often emphasize:

- weak state institutions and contested political legitimacy,
- competition over resources (including diamonds),
- the regional spillover of conflict dynamics in West Africa.

## Patterns of abuse

The summary highlights the abuses most strongly associated with the RUF in major human-rights reporting:

- **amputations** and other forms of terror inflicted on civilians,
- **forced recruitment** and use of **child soldiers**,
- widespread **sexual violence**, including mass rape.

These practices are frequently described as deliberate strategies to intimidate communities, punish perceived opponents, and control territory.

## 1999 Freetown attacks

The 1999 assault on **Freetown** is repeatedly cited as a major episode in the conflict because it brought large-scale violence into the capital and intensified international attention, humanitarian response, and pressure for a negotiated settlement.

## International response and post-war accountability

The end of the war is commonly associated with a combination of:

- peace agreements and disarmament, demobilization, and reintegration (DDR) efforts,
- international peacekeeping involvement,
- post-conflict justice mechanisms.

In the Sierra Leone context, the **Special Court for Sierra Leone** is often referenced in discussions of accountability for wartime atrocities by multiple actors.

## Legacy

The RUF remains a central case study in discussions of:

- “conflict diamonds” and resource-financed insurgency,
- the long-term impact of child soldiering,
- rebuilding institutions after mass violence.
