## Summary

Ben Feringa is a Dutch chemist who shared the 2016 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for designing the first molecular motor.

## Background and Education

Feringa studied chemistry in the Netherlands and later became a professor at the University of Groningen. His career is closely associated with research programs that connect organic synthesis, stereochemistry, and nanoscale function.

## Work on Molecular Motors and Machines

High-level themes in his research include:

- **Unidirectional molecular rotation**: designing molecules that can rotate in one direction under external stimuli (for example, light).
- **Molecular switches**: systems that reversibly change state and can be controlled by chemical or physical inputs.
- **Asymmetric catalysis and stereochemistry**: methods that control molecular handedness, a core issue in making selective reactions.

In popular accounts of the field, the first widely recognized light-driven molecular motor associated with his group dates to the late 1990s, helping establish “molecular machines” as an experimental rather than purely conceptual area.

## Nobel Prize Context

The 2016 Nobel Prize in Chemistry recognized foundational work on **molecular machines**. Feringa shared the award with Jean-Pierre Sauvage and J. Fraser Stoddart.

## Legacy and Impact

Molecular-machine research is often discussed as a stepping stone toward:

- smart materials with controllable properties
- nanoscale actuators and sensors
- improved understanding of how motion can be programmed at the molecular level

These applications remain an active research area; the Nobel-recognized work is generally viewed as enabling core design principles.
