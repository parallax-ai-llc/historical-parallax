## Summary

Rwandan prefect of Kigali who organized and directed genocide in the capital city. He distributed weapons to militia and ordered massacres at roadblocks.

## Role of a Prefect in 1990s Rwanda

In Rwanda’s administrative structure at the time, a prefect was a senior territorial authority with influence over local officials, policing, and coordination across communes. During the 1994 genocide, prefectures were critical nodes in how state direction, local networks, and coercive capacity interacted.

## Allegations During the 1994 Genocide

Accounts of the case commonly describe allegations that Renzaho used the authority of his office in Kigali to facilitate violence, including by supporting militia activity and enabling the operation of checkpoints. These allegations were part of broader international efforts to attribute responsibility not only to national leaders but also to regional administrators.

## ICTR Proceedings

Renzaho was prosecuted at the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR). ICTR cases typically involved extensive witness testimony, documentary evidence, and legal analysis of how administrative authority translated into effective control or contribution to crimes.

Because ICTR judgments and appeals can significantly refine or alter findings, readers generally consult the final appellate decisions and official tribunal summaries for the authoritative procedural history.

## Why the Case Matters

Renzaho’s case is often discussed in the context of “mid-level” governance in mass-atrocity settings: how local executives, not just military commanders, can shape outcomes by directing resources, issuing orders, or signaling impunity.
