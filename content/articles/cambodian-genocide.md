## Summary

Genocide by the Khmer Rouge from 1975-1979 that killed 1.5-2 million people through execution, forced labor, and starvation under Pol Pots regime.

## Historical Context (High Level)

The Cambodian genocide is most often described as occurring under the **Khmer Rouge** government (Democratic Kampuchea) after it seized Phnom Penh in 1975. High-level accounts frame the period as a combination of ideological revolutionary policies, internal purges, and widespread coercion that resulted in mass death.

## Mechanisms of Mass Death

Summaries typically emphasize that deaths were caused by multiple, reinforcing mechanisms:

- **Executions and political purges:** targeting perceived enemies, including former officials, intellectuals, and alleged "traitors" within the movement.
- **Forced labor and collectivization:** large-scale work projects carried out under extreme conditions.
- **Starvation and disease:** food shortages, harsh rationing, and collapse of normal health systems.
- **Targeting of specific groups:** including ethnic and religious minorities in various accounts.

## Social and Political Features

High-level descriptions frequently note:

- **Mass displacement:** evacuation of cities and reorganization of populations into rural collectives.
- **Surveillance and coercion:** tight control over movement, association, and expression.
- **Security apparatus:** detention and interrogation sites, with some locations becoming emblematic in public memory.

## Aftermath and Accountability

After the Khmer Rouge regime ended in 1979, Cambodia experienced prolonged conflict and political instability. Accountability efforts have included domestic and internationalized legal processes, most notably the **Extraordinary Chambers in the Courts of Cambodia (ECCC)**, created to prosecute senior leaders and those most responsible.

## Memory and Documentation

The genocide has been documented through survivor testimony, archival records, forensic work, and historical research. Museums and memorial sites in Cambodia play a central role in public remembrance and education.

## Notes on Estimates

Death toll estimates vary by methodology and source. Figures cited in overviews generally refer to excess deaths from executions, starvation, and disease during the period.
