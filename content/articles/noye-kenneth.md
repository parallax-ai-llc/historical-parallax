## Summary

British criminal Kenneth Noye who was involved in laundering gold from the 1983 Brink's-Mat robbery and murdered Stephen Cameron in a road rage incident. He was one of Britain's most notorious criminals.

## Brink's-Mat Connection

The Brink's-Mat robbery became one of the most famous crimes in modern British history, in part because stolen gold and cash were gradually converted into other assets. Noye’s notoriety is closely tied to the downstream laundering and handling of proceeds rather than to the initial warehouse robbery itself.

## Road-Rage Murder Case

Noye later became widely known to the public through a fatal confrontation on a roadway that prosecutors characterized as a road-rage incident. The case highlighted how quickly routine disputes can escalate into serious violence, and it resulted in a life sentence.

## Manhunt and Public Profile

After leaving the UK, he was eventually located and arrested abroad, which intensified media coverage and reinforced his image as a high-profile fugitive. His name is often referenced in discussions of organized crime, proceeds-of-crime investigations, and long-running efforts to recover and trace stolen assets.

## Cultural Impact

Because the Brink's-Mat story involved long-term laundering and high-value assets, it has remained a recurring topic in British crime reporting. Noye’s role in that broader narrative, plus the later murder conviction, helped cement his status as a symbol of “old-school” organized crime intersecting with modern financial investigation.

The case is also often mentioned when discussing how proceeds-of-crime enforcement evolved: attention shifted from only catching perpetrators to tracing assets, using financial intelligence, and pursuing confiscation.

### Why the Case Endures

- Long time horizon between the robbery and downstream prosecutions
- Visibility of asset-laundering pathways
- A separate violent crime that kept attention on the same figure
