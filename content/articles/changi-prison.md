## Summary

Japanese-run POW camp in Singapore where Allied prisoners were subjected to brutal treatment, forced labor, and starvation. Thousands died from disease and malnutrition.

## Background and Context

- Changi Prison was originally built by the British in Singapore and later repurposed by the Japanese military after the 1942 fall of Singapore.
- The "Changi" name is often used broadly to refer to a wider system of POW and internment sites in Singapore, not only the original prison buildings.
- Conditions for prisoners varied over time, but the overall system was characterized by overcrowding, inadequate food, disease, and coercive discipline.

## Life for Prisoners

- Prisoners faced malnutrition and illnesses such as dysentery and malaria; limited medical supplies amplified mortality.
- Forced labor and transfers to other worksites were common, including movement to projects outside Singapore.
- Despite extreme conditions, many POWs created informal educational and cultural activities to maintain morale.

## End of War and Aftermath

- The camp system effectively ended with Japan’s surrender in 1945 and the return of Allied control.
- Survivor testimony, wartime records, and later historical research form the core of what is known about the site.
- Changi became a major symbol in Singapore’s World War II memory and in Allied POW histories of the Pacific War.

## Why It Matters

- Changi Prison is frequently cited in discussions of wartime incarceration, military occupation, and the treatment of prisoners under international norms.
- The site is also used as a case study for how societies commemorate traumatic events through museums, memorials, and public history.
