## Summary

The Third Crusade (1189–1192), known also as the Kings' Crusade, was launched in response to one of the most shocking events in medieval Christendom: the capture of Jerusalem by Saladin, Sultan of Egypt and Syria, on October 2, 1187.

Saladin's victory followed his decisive destruction of the combined forces of the Crusader states at the Battle of Hattin in July 1187 — a catastrophe that left the Kingdom of Jerusalem militarily helpless.

The loss of the Holy City — held by Latin Christians for 88 years since the First Crusade — triggered grief, panic, and eventually determination across Western Europe, drawing the three most powerful monarchs of the age into the largest crusading campaign since 1099.

King Richard I of England, King Philip II of France, and Holy Roman Emperor Frederick I Barbarossa all took the cross, though only Richard would ultimately make his presence felt on the battlefield.

The campaign that unfolded over the next three years was defined by extraordinary military prowess, painful political division, and ultimately a negotiated compromise that satisfied no one completely but reflected the genuine limits of what either side could achieve.

Richard I — the Lionheart — was without question the dominant military figure of the crusade, combining tactical genius with personal courage in a manner that left his contemporaries, friends and enemies alike, in awe.

His recapture of Acre, his victory at the Battle of Arsuf, his methodical advance down the coast of Palestine, and his diplomacy with Saladin produced the Treaty of Jaffa in September 1192.

That agreement preserved Muslim control of Jerusalem while guaranteeing Christian pilgrims safe and unmolested access to the Holy City's shrines — a compromise that neither side fully wanted but both could accept.

The Third Crusade stands as perhaps the most richly documented of the major crusades, with detailed accounts from both sides of the conflict.

It produced the most famous personal encounter between Christian and Muslim leaders in crusading history — the mutual admiration between Richard and Saladin became legendary in both Western and Eastern traditions.

Yet the crusade also produced atrocities — most notably Richard's massacre of 2,700 Muslim prisoners at Ayyadieh — that complicate any romantic narrative.

And for the Jews of England, the crusade's departure was accompanied by devastating violence at home, a reminder that the religious enthusiasm of crusading had dark and local consequences as well as distant ones.

## Historical Context

The fall of Jerusalem in 1187 was the culmination of a long strategic deterioration in the Crusader states' position that had its roots in the failures of the Second Crusade and the subsequent unification of Muslim Syria and Egypt under Saladin.

Saladin — Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub — was a Kurdish military commander who had taken control of Fatimid Egypt in 1169 while ostensibly serving Nur ad-Din of Syria.

After Nur ad-Din's death in 1174, Saladin gradually unified Egypt and Syria under his own Ayyubid dynasty, creating the strategic encirclement of the Crusader states that the Second Crusade had inadvertently made possible.

Throughout the 1170s and early 1180s, Saladin and the Crusader Kingdom of Jerusalem maintained an uneasy peace interrupted by periodic raids and skirmishes.

The decisive break came through the recklessness of Reynald of Chatillon, lord of the Transjordan, who repeatedly violated truces with Saladin — most catastrophically by attacking a large Muslim caravan in 1187, including pilgrims traveling from Cairo to Damascus.

Saladin had sworn personally to kill Reynald, and the caravan attack gave him both a casus belli and a genuine grievance. He assembled the largest Muslim army yet seen in the Levant and marched into Crusader territory.

The Kingdom of Jerusalem's response was fatally compromised by internal divisions — particularly between the "court party" around the regent Raymond III of Tripoli, which favored caution, and the "war party" around Reynald and the new king, Guy of Lusignan.

At the Battle of Hattin on July 4, 1187, Saladin lured the Crusader army into a waterless march across the plateau above the Sea of Galilee, then encircled and destroyed it.

The True Cross — Christendom's most sacred relic — was captured. King Guy was captured. Reynald of Chatillon was personally executed by Saladin, who fulfilled his oath.

The military orders were almost entirely killed — Saladin had the captured Templars and Hospitallers executed as irredeemable enemies.

In the weeks that followed, Crusader cities across the Levant fell in rapid succession: Acre, Sidon, Beirut, Ascalon. On October 2, 1187, Jerusalem surrendered.

Saladin, in pointed contrast to the Crusaders' massacre of 1099, allowed the population — Christian and Jewish alike — to depart for ransom, though thousands too poor to pay their ransom were enslaved.

## The Event

The news of Jerusalem's fall reached Europe in the autumn of 1187 and provoked a response of extraordinary scale. Pope Gregory VIII issued the bull *Audita tremendi*, attributing the disaster to the sins of Christendom and calling for a new crusade.

Within months, the three greatest monarchs of Western Europe — Frederick I Barbarossa of the Holy Roman Empire, Philip II Augustus of France, and Henry II of England — had taken the cross. Henry II died before departing; his son Richard I took up his vow with even greater determination.

Frederick Barbarossa set out first, in May 1190, leading a German army of enormous size. The German crusade began promisingly, defeating two Seljuk armies in Anatolia and negotiating passage from the Sultanate of Rum.

But on June 10, 1190, Frederick drowned while crossing the Saleph River in Cilicia — some accounts say he tried to ford the river on horseback, others that he fell from his horse into the water. The German army, deprived of its charismatic leader, largely disintegrated. Only a fraction eventually reached the Holy Land.

Richard I and Philip II departed together from France in 1190, traveling by sea through the Mediterranean. Richard's fleet was scattered by storms and he spent much of 1190 in Sicily, where he clashed with King Tancred over inheritance disputes involving his sister Joan's dowry.

In 1191, Richard conquered Cyprus from its Byzantine ruler, Isaac Komnenos — a campaign of considerable strategic importance that gave the Crusaders a permanent naval base in the eastern Mediterranean. Cyprus would remain in Crusader hands for over three centuries.

Philip II arrived at Acre in April 1191; Richard arrived in June. His fleet's arrival immediately transformed the military situation. The combined Franco-English fleet blockaded Acre from the sea while the land army maintained the siege.

On July 12, 1191, Acre surrendered. It was the Third Crusade's greatest military achievement.

What followed was one of the crusade's most controversial episodes. Saladin had agreed to pay a ransom for approximately 2,700 Muslim prisoners taken at Acre, release Christian prisoners, and return the True Cross.

When negotiations over the payment and timing broke down, Richard ordered the massacre of all the Muslim prisoners on August 20, 1191, at Ayyadieh, in full view of Saladin's army.

The decision was militarily motivated — Richard needed to march south and could not leave behind a garrison to guard thousands of prisoners — but it horrified Muslim observers and was recorded by both sides. Saladin reportedly wept.

Richard's subsequent advance down the coast toward Jaffa was a masterpiece of medieval logistics and tactical discipline: the army marched in tight formation, maintaining contact with the supply fleet, enduring constant harassment from Saladin's cavalry without breaking ranks.

At the Battle of Arsuf on September 7, 1191, Richard's controlled deployment of his cavalry charge shattered a Muslim attack and demonstrated his superiority as a battlefield commander.

Richard advanced to within sight of Jerusalem twice — in January 1192 and in June 1192 — but twice turned back without attacking.

His reasoning was coldly strategic: even if Jerusalem could be taken, the city lay inland, dependent on long supply lines through hostile territory, and could not be held without a permanent garrison that no European monarch intended to provide.

He famously refused to look upon Jerusalem from a hilltop, reportedly covering his face and saying he was not worthy to look upon the city he could not recover for God.

After Philip II's early departure for France in August 1191 — he was ill, and eager to exploit Richard's absence for political gain in France — Richard concluded peace negotiations with Saladin directly.

The Treaty of Jaffa, signed on September 2, 1192, established a three-year truce: Crusaders retained the coastal strip from Tyre to Jaffa, Ascalon was demolished, and unarmed Christian pilgrims were guaranteed free access to Jerusalem.

Richard left Palestine on October 9, 1192, never to return. He was captured by Duke Leopold of Austria on his way home and spent over a year as a prisoner before being ransomed for 150,000 marks.

## Multiple Perspectives

### Christian and Richard I View

For the Christians of the Third Crusade and for subsequent Western tradition, Richard I of England stands as the supreme exemplar of the crusading ideal: physically imposing, militarily brilliant, personally fearless, and driven by genuine religious conviction as well as martial pride.

Richard's victories at Acre and Arsuf, his disciplined coastal march, and his negotiations with Saladin were celebrated by contemporary chroniclers — particularly the anonymous author of the *Itinerarium Peregrinorum et Gesta Regis Ricardi*.

The Western narrative of the Third Crusade acknowledged failure — Jerusalem was not recovered — but framed it as an honorable failure, the result of political betrayal (Philip II's early departure), inadequate resources, and the impossible strategic situation.

Richard's decision not to attack Jerusalem, though criticized by some contemporaries as timid or faithless, was defended by those who understood the military realities: a successful assault was possible, but holding the city afterward was not.

The Treaty of Jaffa was presented as the best available outcome — a guarantee of Christian pilgrimage rights that was actually honored, with Saladin permitting pilgrims to visit the Holy Sepulchre and other shrines.

The chivalric mythology that grew up around Richard and the Third Crusade was enormous. Stories of Richard's feats of personal valor — killing enemy soldiers with his own hands, sending horses to Saladin when the sultan's mount was killed — filled the romances and chronicles of the thirteenth century.

The mutual respect between Richard and Saladin, expressed in exchanges of gifts and messages and in the treatment of prisoners, became the template for the crusading ideal of noble warfare between worthy enemies.

This powerful narrative shaped European ideas of chivalry for generations. Richard was celebrated as a Christian hero even by those who recognized that he had failed in the crusade's primary objective.

The crusade also revealed deep fractures within the Crusader enterprise. The quarrel between Richard and Philip II — rooted in the long-standing rivalry between England and France and inflamed by personal animosity — significantly hampered operations at Acre and contributed to Philip's premature departure.

The dispute over the kingship of Jerusalem, pitting Guy of Lusignan against Conrad of Montferrat (who was assassinated just days after being recognized as king), divided the Crusader leadership at critical moments.

Richard's high-handed treatment of his allies — he essentially sold Cyprus to the Templars and then took it back for his own use — made him enemies even among those nominally on his side.

The Third Crusade exposed the structural weakness of crusading as a cooperative enterprise dependent on the voluntary collaboration of proud, independent-minded monarchs.

### Islamic and Saladin View

From Saladin's perspective and from the viewpoint of Muslim contemporaries, the Third Crusade was a severe test of his achievements that he ultimately survived.

Saladin's reconquest of Jerusalem in 1187 had been the crowning achievement of his career, the fulfillment of decades of jihad ideology and strategic preparation. The arrival of an enormous Crusader force led by Richard I threatened everything he had built.

The fall of Acre in July 1191 was a genuine defeat — Saladin had failed to break the Crusader siege despite repeated attempts, and the garrison's surrender was a significant blow to Muslim morale and strategic position.

Saladin's response to Richard's Ayyadieh massacre — reportedly ordering the execution of Christian prisoners in his custody — illustrated the brutal reciprocity of medieval warfare.

But Muslim sources also emphasize that Saladin's overall conduct of the war was considerably more humane than his opponents'. His sparing of Jerusalem's population in 1187, his treatment of captured Crusader nobles, and his personal acts of generosity toward Richard were preserved in both Muslim and Western sources as evidence of his chivalric character.

Baha ad-Din ibn Shaddad, his biographer and secretary, presents Saladin as a genuinely pious man who experienced the war's hardships deeply and who sought peace with honor rather than war without end.

The Treaty of Jaffa represented, from Saladin's perspective, a diplomatic triumph. He had entered the negotiations exhausted, short of resources, and facing a military opponent he had been unable to defeat in open battle.

Richard had been unable to take Jerusalem; Saladin had been unable to drive the Crusaders into the sea. The treaty preserved what mattered most to Saladin: Muslim sovereignty over Jerusalem and the sacred sites of Al-Quds.

By permitting unarmed Christian pilgrimage, he demonstrated magnanimity without conceding the political point. Within a year of Richard's departure, the immediate threat had passed, and Saladin could turn to internal consolidation.

Saladin died in Damascus on March 4, 1193, just five months after the treaty was signed, reportedly having given away most of his treasury in charity. He was mourned across the Muslim world.

The memory of Saladin in Islamic tradition is complex. He was certainly celebrated as the liberator of Jerusalem and a model of Islamic kingship, but his legacy was also contested.

His Ayyubid successors would prove considerably less committed to the anti-Crusader struggle, at times making accommodations with the Franks that Saladin would likely have rejected.

The Ayyubid Sultan al-Kamil famously handed Jerusalem back to Holy Roman Emperor Frederick II in 1229 as part of a peace treaty — a decision condemned by many Muslims as a betrayal of everything Saladin had fought for.

### Jewish Perspective

The Third Crusade's departure from England was preceded by one of the most violent episodes in the history of medieval English Jewry.

Richard I's coronation on September 3, 1189, was accompanied by the first anti-Jewish riots in England since the Norman Conquest. Jewish leaders had come to present gifts at the coronation but were barred from the ceremony; a misunderstanding or deliberate incitement outside Westminster Abbey sparked violence that quickly spread through London.

Jewish homes were attacked and burned; Jewish men, women, and children were killed or injured. Richard, outraged, punished some of the perpetrators and issued writs of protection for the Jewish community — but the genie was out of the bottle.

Far worse followed in 1190, as crusade preparations in provincial England created an atmosphere of religious excitement and financial grievance.

Jewish communities had long functioned as moneylenders — one of the few economic roles available to them under medieval English law — and crusaders who owed money to Jewish creditors had both religious and financial incentives to attack them.

The worst episode occurred in York on March 16, 1190, at Clifford's Tower. The Jewish community of York, facing attack from a crusader mob led by local nobles who owed debts to Jewish creditors, took refuge in the royal castle.

When it became clear they could not hold out, the community's leader Rabbi Yomtov of Joigny apparently led a mass suicide: the men killed their wives and children and then themselves, rather than submit to forced baptism or death at the hands of the mob.

Approximately 150 Jews died. The mob then gathered in York Minster and burned the records of their debts to Jewish creditors.

The York massacre was the worst anti-Jewish violence in medieval England's history. The ruins of Clifford's Tower still stand, charged with historical significance for English Jews.

Richard I's genuine policy toward Jews was, by the standards of the time, relatively protective: he issued formal charters of protection and privileges to Jewish communities, and he punished some of the perpetrators of the London riots.

But his crusading enterprise had unleashed forces he could not control, and the wave of anti-Jewish violence that accompanied crusade recruitment across England demonstrated once again the lethal connection between crusading enthusiasm and Jewish vulnerability.

The Third Crusade, for all its chivalric romance about Richard and Saladin, was bounded by this domestic atrocity that the crusade's own energy had produced.

Jews also maintained a complex and precarious status in the Crusader states themselves. They were generally barred from Jerusalem under the Kingdom of Jerusalem, though they maintained communities in Acre and other cities.

Neither Muslim nor Christian rule in the Levant offered Jews full civic equality, and the Crusading era's intensification of religious identity made their position throughout the region more precarious, not less.

## Legacy & Consequences

The Third Crusade established a strategic status quo that would define the remaining history of the Crusader states. The Treaty of Jaffa left the Kingdom of Jerusalem as a coastal rump state, with no access to its namesake city except by pilgrimage.

Jerusalem remained under Muslim control until the anomalous interlude of 1229–1244, when the Holy Roman Emperor Frederick II negotiated its return diplomatically — a bizarre episode condemned by the papacy and the military orders with almost as much passion as it was welcomed by ordinary Christians eager to see the Holy City in Christian hands.

The Crusader states continued to exist, under increasing pressure, until the fall of Acre in 1291 ended the Latin presence in the Levant entirely.

The Third Crusade's failure to recover Jerusalem reinforced the theological imperative for future crusading while simultaneously demonstrating the enormous practical obstacles that made success so elusive.

The lesson that Western kings, however capable, could not sustain a prolonged commitment to the eastern Mediterranean was absorbed by subsequent crusade planners, who developed new strategic theories — targeting Egypt, building alliances with the Mongols — that would shape the Fourth, Fifth, and later crusades.

Richard I's legend, shaped in large part by the Third Crusade, became one of the most powerful in medieval European culture. The chivalric image of the Christian knight — brave, generous to worthy opponents, ferocious in battle but merciful in victory — owed much to the stories that accumulated around Richard and his interactions with Saladin.

Saladin, for his part, became a figure of fascination in Western literature — presented as a noble pagan or virtual Christian by admiring European authors who could not quite reconcile his qualities with his religion.

Both figures were transformed by the Third Crusade into cultural myths that long outlasted the historical realities they were built upon. These myths continue to shape popular culture's understanding of the Crusades to the present day.

## Key Figures

**Richard I of England (1157–1199)** — "The Lionheart," the dominant military figure of the Third Crusade. A skilled battlefield commander, brilliant logistician, and charismatic leader, Richard won victories at Acre and Arsuf and negotiated the Treaty of Jaffa. He spent only six months of his ten-year reign actually in England, dying from a crossbow wound in France in 1199.

**Saladin (1137–1193)** — Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub, the Kurdish Sultan of Egypt and Syria. His capture of Jerusalem in 1187 triggered the crusade; his military resistance and diplomatic skill preserved Muslim control of the Holy City through the Treaty of Jaffa. He died just five months after signing the treaty, mourned across the Muslim world.

**Philip II of France (1165–1223)** — "Philip Augustus," the calculating King of France who participated in the crusade partly to forestall Richard's dominance. He departed from Acre in August 1191, pleading illness, but also eager to pursue territorial gains in France at Richard's expense. His early departure significantly hampered the crusade's final push toward Jerusalem.

**Frederick I Barbarossa (1122–1190)** — The Holy Roman Emperor whose participation promised to make the Third Crusade the mightiest military expedition in crusading history. His drowning in the Saleph River in June 1190 was one of the great catastrophes of the campaign, effectively ending the German crusade before it reached the Holy Land.

**Conrad of Montferrat (c. 1145–1192)** — The defender of Tyre, the one Crusader city that had held against Saladin in 1187. Conrad was recognized as King of Jerusalem in April 1192, but was assassinated by Assassins just days later before he could be crowned. His murder — possibly instigated by Richard, according to some sources — deepened the factional divisions among the crusading leadership.

**Baha ad-Din ibn Shaddad (1145–1234)** — Saladin's secretary and biographer, one of the most important Muslim sources for the Third Crusade. His chronicle *The Rare and Excellent History of Saladin* provides a detailed Muslim perspective on the campaign and preserves accounts of Saladin's character and conduct.

**Rabbi Yomtov of Joigny (d. 1190)** — The leader of the York Jewish community who reportedly led the community in mass suicide at Clifford's Tower rather than submit to forced baptism at the hands of a crusading mob. His death and the deaths of approximately 150 members of his community made the York Massacre one of the most traumatic events in medieval Anglo-Jewish history.

**Isaac Komnenos of Cyprus (c. 1155–1195)** — The self-proclaimed Byzantine ruler of Cyprus who mistreated shipwrecked crusaders from Richard's fleet and refused to cooperate with Richard. His defeat and capture by Richard in 1191 resulted in Cyprus becoming a permanent Crusader possession — arguably the Third Crusade's most durable strategic achievement.

**Guy of Lusignan (c. 1150–1194)** — King of Jerusalem through his marriage to Queen Sibylla, Guy bore direct responsibility for the disaster at Hattin, where he marched his army into Saladin's trap. Despite this catastrophe, he clung to his claim to Jerusalem's throne throughout the Third Crusade, contesting the crown with Conrad of Montferrat. After Conrad's assassination and the eventual resolution of the succession dispute, he became the first ruler of Cyprus, founding a dynasty that would last until 1489.

**Reynald of Chatillon (c. 1125–1187)** — Lord of Kerak and Oultrejourdain, Reynald was the most reckless and destructive figure in the late Crusader Kingdom. His attacks on Muslim caravans and his raid into the Red Sea (aimed at attacking Mecca itself) enraged Saladin and made war inevitable. Saladin personally executed him after the Battle of Hattin, fulfilling a personal oath. His provocation of Saladin is often cited as the most proximate cause of Jerusalem's fall and thus of the Third Crusade itself.

**Berengaria of Navarre (c. 1165–1230)** — Richard I's queen, married in Cyprus in May 1191 during the campaign. She was the only English queen never to set foot in England. She spent most of the Third Crusade aboard ships or in Acre, as Richard's military focus left little room for domestic life. Her marriage to Richard produced no heirs, contributing to the succession crisis that followed Richard's death.

**Hubert Walter (c. 1160–1205)** — The Bishop of Salisbury who accompanied the Third Crusade and proved one of its most effective administrators. After Richard's capture on the return journey, Hubert Walter played a key role in raising the enormous ransom of 150,000 marks. He later became Archbishop of Canterbury and one of the most powerful men in England — a career built in part on the connections and reputation he gained in the Holy Land.

**Duke Leopold V of Austria (1157–1194)** — The Austrian duke who captured Richard I on his return journey from the Holy Land in December 1192, initiating the king's prolonged captivity. Leopold had a personal grievance against Richard dating from Acre, where Richard had reportedly had Leopold's banner torn down and thrown in a ditch — an insult to his honor that the Austrian nursed until the opportunity for revenge arose.

**Geoffrey of Lusignan (c. 1145–1216)** — Brother of King Guy of Lusignan, Geoffrey was among the crusading knights whose continued presence at Acre after the main armies' departure helped garrison the surviving Crusader coastal strip. His family's establishment on Cyprus — where his brother Guy became the first king — represented one of the Third Crusade's lasting dynastic consequences.

**Imad ad-Din al-Isfahani (1125–1201)** — Saladin's secretary and one of the primary Muslim chroniclers of the Third Crusade. His florid account of Saladin's campaigns, including the capture of Jerusalem and the resistance to Richard's crusade, provides invaluable detail on the Muslim military and diplomatic perspective. He was present at many of the key events he describes, making his work — despite its rhetorical excesses — an essential primary source.
