## Summary

Indian serial killer who murdered at least 18 people in Delhi and left dismembered body parts outside Tihar Jail as taunts to police. He killed over personal grudges and to assert dominance.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |

## Modus operandi and taunts

Jha’s case is often highlighted for the way victims’ remains were displayed as a deliberate provocation. Investigators reported that dismembered body parts were left near **Tihar Jail**, apparently to draw attention and to demonstrate contempt for police efforts.[^1] In many summaries, the killings are described as motivated by personal disputes and a desire for control rather than financial gain.[^1]

## Investigation and arrest

The discovery of remains near the jail contributed to the public profile of the case and intensified investigation.[^1] Police ultimately arrested Jha in 2007 and linked him to a series of murders in Delhi through evidence and confessions described in reporting.[^1]

## Trial and sentencing

Jha was tried in Indian courts across multiple cases. High-level accounts note convictions and the imposition of severe sentences, including a death sentence in at least one case.[^1] As with many serial-killer prosecutions, different counts and proceedings can lead to multiple judgments over time.

## Broader significance

The case is frequently cited in discussions of:

- urban policing and investigative capacity in large cities,
- the role of media coverage in high-profile criminal investigations,
- how offenders may use symbolic acts to influence public fear and police response.[^1]

## Notes on numbers

Published estimates of the number of victims can vary depending on whether they refer to suspected killings, charged cases, or convictions.[^1]
