## Summary

Mozambican rebel leader who led RENAMO in a devastating civil war that killed one million people. He later became opposition leader but periodically returned to armed conflict.

## Context and Background

RENAMO (Resistência Nacional Moçambicana) emerged during Mozambique’s post-independence period and became the main armed opponent of the ruling FRELIMO government. Dhlakama became the organization’s leader in 1979 and remained its central figure for decades, shaping both its military strategy and later its transformation into an electoral opposition party.

## Civil War and Its Aftermath

During Mozambique’s civil war (1977–1992), RENAMO fought a long insurgency that involved widespread violence and displacement. The conflict ended with the Rome General Peace Accords in 1992, after which RENAMO entered formal politics. Dhlakama repeatedly navigated the tension between maintaining an armed movement’s cohesion and competing within Mozambique’s evolving multiparty system.

## Electoral Politics

After the introduction of multiparty elections, Dhlakama became a recurrent presidential candidate and a prominent opposition leader. RENAMO remained an important parliamentary force and, in some regions, a key local political actor. Disputes over election outcomes and the integration of former combatants periodically heightened political तनाव and contributed to renewed insecurity.

## Renewed Tensions and Negotiations

In the 2010s, armed clashes again occurred between RENAMO elements and government forces, alongside intermittent ceasefires and negotiations. These episodes underscored unresolved issues around decentralization, security arrangements, and the long-term reintegration of RENAMO’s military structures.

## Death and Legacy

Dhlakama died in 2018. His leadership is often discussed in relation to Mozambique’s transition from civil war to peace, the durability of RENAMO as an opposition movement, and the recurring cycles of negotiation and confrontation that marked the country’s postwar politics.
