## Summary

Irish dissident republican paramilitary group responsible for the 1998 Omagh bombing that killed 29 people—the deadliest single attack in the Troubles. The bombing occurred after the Good Friday Agreement and was widely condemned.

## Origins and political context

The Real IRA is usually described as part of the **dissident republican** current that rejected the direction taken by mainstream republican politics in the late 1990s.

- The **Good Friday Agreement (1998)** created a framework for power-sharing and set out pathways for decommissioning of weapons and constitutional change by consent.
- Dissident groups opposed compromises implied by the settlement and argued for continuing an armed campaign.

## Omagh bombing (1998)

The Omagh bombing is central to the Real IRA’s historical reputation because it:

- caused a large number of civilian deaths (29 people are commonly cited),
- generated widespread political condemnation across Ireland and the UK,
- strengthened public support for the peace process by highlighting the costs of renewed violence.

Omagh is often treated as a turning point that isolated dissident militants socially and politically, even among communities that had previously been sympathetic to republican aims.

## Classification and security response (high-level)

Paramilitary organizations active during and after the Troubles were typically addressed through a mix of:

- criminal prosecution and intelligence-led policing,
- disruption of weapons procurement and financing,
- political measures supporting ceasefires and demobilization.

In many jurisdictions, groups associated with bombings and armed attacks have also been subject to proscription/terrorist-designation regimes, which can criminalize membership, fundraising, and public support.

## Aftermath and legacy

Even after ceasefire declarations, the legacy of the Real IRA is frequently discussed in relation to:

- the persistence of small dissident networks,
- debates over how peace agreements handle spoilers,
- the long-term impact of single mass-casualty events on public opinion.
