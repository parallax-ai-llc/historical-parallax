## Summary

Massive insurance fraud where executives created 64,000 fake insurance policies worth $2 billion. Used computer fraud to generate fictitious policyholders, one of the first computer-assisted frauds.

## Background

Equity Funding Corporation of America marketed itself as a fast-growing financial services firm, combining insurance products with investment-style offerings. In the late 1960s and early 1970s, growth expectations and sales incentives helped create pressure to report ever-higher volumes of policies and revenues.

## How the Fraud Worked (High Level)

The core scheme involved creating large numbers of **fictitious insurance policies** and policyholders. Records for these fake accounts were used to make the business appear larger and more profitable than it really was.

Because policy administration and reporting were increasingly computerized, the organization was able to scale the fabrication of records. This case is often cited as an early example of **computer-assisted financial fraud**.

## Detection and Exposure

The fraud ultimately came to light after internal concerns escalated and information reached regulators, auditors, and law enforcement. Once the existence of tens of thousands of false policies was established, the company’s reported performance rapidly lost credibility, triggering investigations and collapse.

## Aftermath

- The company failed and became a prominent corporate scandal of the era.
- Executives faced criminal proceedings tied to fraud and false financial reporting.
- The case influenced how investors, insurers, and auditors thought about controls around computerized records.

## Why It Matters

Equity Funding is remembered less for its legitimate business lines than for demonstrating that:

- **Scale can hide fabrication** when incentives reward growth over verification.
- **Systems and data** can become an attack surface when controls are weak.
- **Audit and compliance** must adapt as recordkeeping becomes more automated.
