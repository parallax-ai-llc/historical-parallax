## Summary

Bhagwan Shree Rajneesh (later widely known as Osho) was an Indian spiritual teacher whose movement attracted an international following and significant controversy. In the 1980s his community established a large commune in Oregon, where serious criminal acts by members of the organization—including a major salmonella contamination attack—became part of the public record. Rajneesh was later deported from the United States.

## Teachings and Movement (High Level)

Rajneesh blended elements of meditation, discourse-style teaching, and critiques of conventional religion and social norms. His movement emphasized personal transformation and included practices that supporters described as liberating and critics described as destabilizing or manipulative.

## Rajneeshpuram and Conflict

The Oregon commune (often called Rajneeshpuram) grew rapidly, developed substantial infrastructure, and became a focal point for conflict with local residents and authorities. Much commentary on the episode focuses on the intersection of religious freedom, local governance, immigration issues, and the practical realities of building a new city-like community in a rural area.

## Criminal Acts by Organization Members

Investigations and prosecutions established that some members of the movement committed significant crimes, including the 1984 salmonella attack intended to influence local political outcomes. In historical accounts, this incident is frequently cited as a landmark case in U.S. domestic bioterrorism.

## Deportation and Later Years

Rajneesh’s U.S. legal troubles culminated in deportation, after which he returned to India. The movement continued to evolve after the Oregon period, and his lectures and writings remained widely distributed internationally.

## Legacy

- **Cultural impact:** Osho’s talks and meditation branding remained influential in parts of global wellness culture.
- **Controversy:** The Oregon episode and related criminal cases remain central to his public image.
- **Case study:** Scholars and journalists often treat the movement as a case study in charismatic leadership, community formation, and the risks of insular governance.
