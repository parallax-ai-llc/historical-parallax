## Summary

US government-backed practice of denying mortgages and services to Black neighborhoods. Created by the FHA in the 1930s, redlining's effects on wealth inequality persist today.

## How redlining worked

The term “redlining” is commonly associated with government and private-sector risk maps that marked neighborhoods—often with high Black populations or other marginalized groups—as unsafe for mortgage lending or investment. These maps and underwriting guidelines influenced:

- Access to federally backed mortgages
- Bank lending terms and availability of credit
- Insurance pricing and coverage decisions
- Commercial investment and municipal services

A closely referenced historical artifact is the Home Owners’ Loan Corporation (HOLC) “Residential Security” mapping project in the 1930s, though scholars debate the precise causal role of HOLC maps versus broader discriminatory lending and housing practices.

## Relationship to segregation and housing policy

Redlining interacted with other systems that produced residential segregation, including restrictive covenants, exclusionary zoning, and discriminatory real-estate practices. The result was a durable pattern: many white households accumulated home equity through subsidized mortgage access, while many Black households were excluded from similar wealth-building channels.

## Long-term impacts

The effects of redlining are discussed in research on racial wealth gaps, neighborhood disinvestment, health outcomes, environmental exposure, and educational inequality. Because housing markets compound over time, the downstream consequences can persist even after formal bans on discrimination.

## Legal changes and modern debates

The Fair Housing Act (1968) and subsequent enforcement efforts sought to curb housing discrimination. In the modern era, debates focus on the legacy of historical redlining, how it can be measured (e.g., correlations with historical maps), and what policy interventions—such as fair lending enforcement, community reinvestment, or targeted public investment—can address entrenched disparities.
