## Summary

ISIS attacked the Yazidi community in Sinjar killing thousands of men, enslaving thousands of women and children as sex slaves, and displacing hundreds of thousands. It was recognized as genocide.

## Background

The Yazidis are a religious minority with deep historical roots in northern Iraq and surrounding regions.

In 2014, ISIS (ISIL) targeted Yazidis in and around the Sinjar area as part of its broader campaign of mass violence and territorial control.

## Nature of the Crimes

Public reporting and investigations summarized in sources such as Wikipedia describe multiple categories of abuse, including:

- Mass killings of men and older boys.
- Forced conversion and abduction.
- Enslavement and systematic sexual violence against women and girls.
- Forced recruitment and indoctrination of children.

## Displacement and Humanitarian Impact

The assault triggered large-scale displacement, with many Yazidis fleeing to Mount Sinjar and other areas under extreme conditions.

The consequences included long-term displacement, family separation, and ongoing trauma for survivors.

## International Recognition and Accountability

The events have been recognized by multiple bodies and governments as genocide.

A significant part of the aftermath has involved documenting crimes, locating missing persons, and pursuing prosecutions under domestic and international legal frameworks.

Wikipedia notes later prosecutions, including a landmark German conviction connected to genocide against Yazidis.

## Ongoing Issues

Even after ISIS lost territorial control, key issues remained:

- Recovery and identification of victims from mass graves.
- Support for survivors, including medical and psychosocial care.
- Safe return, reconstruction, and security guarantees in Sinjar.
- Legal recognition and reparations.
