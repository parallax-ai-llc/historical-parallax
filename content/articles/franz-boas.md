## Summary

Franz Boas was a German-born American anthropologist widely regarded as the founding figure of modern American anthropology. Often called the "Father of American Anthropology," he transformed the discipline by championing the concept of cultural relativism—the principle that every culture must be understood on its own terms rather than judged by the standards of another. He was a fierce opponent of scientific racism, using empirical evidence to challenge prevailing theories that linked race, biology, and intelligence. Over a long career centred at Columbia University, Boas trained a generation of influential anthropologists, including Margaret Mead, Ruth Benedict, Edward Sapir, and Zora Neale Hurston, leaving an indelible mark on the social sciences.

## Historical Significance

Boas's most enduring contribution to intellectual history was his rigorous, evidence-based dismantling of scientific racism. In the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, many Western scientists and academics maintained that human races were biologically distinct categories with fixed intellectual and moral hierarchies. Boas directly contested these claims. His 1912 study of immigrant families demonstrated that skull shapes—then considered a stable racial marker—changed significantly within a single generation in a new environment, showing that what had been attributed to heredity was in fact responsive to environmental conditions. *The Mind of Primitive Man* synthesised these findings into a systematic argument that no race could be shown to be intellectually inferior or superior to another, and that differences between human groups were primarily cultural, not biological.

The concept of cultural relativism, which Boas developed and promoted throughout his career, reshaped the humanities and social sciences. By insisting that cultures could only be properly understood from within their own frameworks, he challenged the evolutionary hierarchies that had dominated nineteenth-century anthropology—frameworks that arranged societies on a unilinear scale from "savage" to "civilised," invariably placing Western European societies at the top. Boas replaced this schema with a particularist approach: each culture had its own history and its own internal logic, and the anthropologist's task was to document and understand that logic, not to rank it.

His influence on American anthropology was institutional as well as intellectual. Through his teaching at Columbia, Boas built what became known as the "Four-Field Approach"—uniting cultural anthropology, physical anthropology, archaeology, and linguistic anthropology as complementary aspects of a single discipline. The scholars he trained went on to define American cultural anthropology for much of the twentieth century. Margaret Mead brought anthropological perspectives to popular audiences; Ruth Benedict's *Patterns of Culture* (1934) disseminated cultural relativism to a wide readership; Edward Sapir developed foundational theories in linguistic anthropology; and Zora Neale Hurston brought ethnographic sensibility to African American literature and folklore.

Boas was also notable for his public engagement. He consistently used his scholarly authority to oppose racism, nationalism, and eugenics in the public sphere, often at personal and professional cost. His outspoken criticism of German nationalism led to the revocation of his honorary doctorate by the University of Kiel in 1933, after the Nazis came to power. He continued to speak out against fascism and racism until his death, embodying a vision of scholarship as inseparable from ethical responsibility.
