## Summary

Al-Qaeda-inspired terrorists detonated bombs on four commuter trains in Madrid killing 193 people and wounding 2,000. The attack changed the outcome of Spain's general election.

## Context

The attacks occurred during Spain's involvement in the Iraq War and amid broader concerns in Europe about jihadist terrorism. The targets were commuter rail lines carrying morning rush-hour passengers into Madrid.

## Attack Overview (High Level)

- On 11 March 2004, multiple improvised explosive devices detonated on packed commuter trains.
- The blasts occurred at or near several stations on the Cercanías network during peak travel time.
- The scale and coordination created mass casualties and significant disruption to the capital.

## Perpetrators and Investigation (High Level)

Reporting and court findings commonly describe:

- A cell inspired by al-Qaeda’s ideology, with operational links attributed to extremist networks.
- Use of commercially available explosives and detonators assembled into backpack-borne devices.
- A large-scale forensic and intelligence investigation leading to arrests, trials, and convictions in Spain.

## Political and Social Impact

- The attack took place days before Spain’s general election, intensifying public debate over security and foreign policy.
- It prompted major demonstrations and sustained scrutiny of official communications during the immediate aftermath.
- Spain’s counterterrorism posture and public preparedness discussions evolved in the years following.

## Security and Emergency Response Lessons

- Coordinated emergency medical response and triage became a key case study for mass-casualty planning.
- Rail security practices and surveillance measures were reassessed, balancing public safety with civil liberties.
- The incident is often referenced in European counterterrorism cooperation and information-sharing frameworks.

## Legacy

The 2004 Madrid train bombings remain one of the deadliest terrorist attacks in modern Spanish history and are frequently cited in analyses of terrorism’s strategic aims, including influencing public opinion and political decision-making.
