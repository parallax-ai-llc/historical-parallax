## Summary

CIA officer who spied for the Soviet Union and Russia from 1985 to 1994, compromising over 100 operations and leading to the execution of at least 10 agents. He was paid over $4.6 million. Serving life without parole.

## Background

Ames worked as an operations officer in the U.S. Central Intelligence Agency (CIA), with assignments related to Soviet and later Russian targets during the Cold War and its aftermath. Because he had access to sensitive counterintelligence information, his betrayal affected both human sources and operational methods.

## Espionage and impact

Open-source summaries describe Ames as providing the Soviet Union—and later the Russian Federation—with identities of covert assets and details of intelligence operations. The damage is frequently characterized as severe, including compromised networks and the arrest and execution of multiple individuals who had provided information to U.S. intelligence.

## Detection and arrest

Ames’s case is often cited in counterintelligence discussions for the way investigators pieced together operational failures, financial anomalies, and behavioral indicators. His arrest in 1994 became a major public event and triggered internal reviews and reforms focused on security practices and the monitoring of insider threats.

## Sentencing and aftermath

He pleaded guilty and received a life sentence without the possibility of parole. The case remains a prominent reference point in U.S. intelligence history when discussing institutional vulnerability, trust, and the long-term consequences of compromised human sources.

## Notes

Public estimates of the number of compromised assets vary, but the case is consistently treated as among the most damaging U.S. intelligence penetrations of the late Cold War period.
