## Summary

Randy Schekman is an American cell biologist who shared the 2013 Nobel Prize for vesicle traffic discoveries.

## Scientific Contributions (High Level)

- Schekman is known for using yeast genetics to map the cellular machinery that moves proteins and membranes through the secretory pathway.
- This work helped establish how vesicles form, bud, and fuse, and how trafficking is organized inside cells.
- The discoveries provided a framework that connects basic cell biology to disease mechanisms where transport is disrupted.

## Roles and Recognition

- He has been associated with the University of California, Berkeley, where he led a laboratory and trained students in cell biology and genetics.
- In 2013 he shared the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine with other researchers recognized for complementary work on vesicle trafficking.
- Beyond research, he has held editorial and leadership roles, including advocacy for research publishing models and critiques of incentives tied to prestige journals.

## Influence on the Field

- Vesicle trafficking research underpins modern understanding of how cells organize secretion, endocytosis, and organelle maintenance.
- The genetic and biochemical approaches developed in this area influenced later work on membrane dynamics across many organisms.
- The topic remains relevant in areas such as neurobiology, immunology, and metabolism, where transport defects can have system-level effects.

## Notes

- In biographical coverage, Schekman is often described as a scientist who combined genetic screens with mechanistic cell biology.
- His public comments about publishing incentives are frequently referenced in conversations about research culture and reproducibility.
