## Summary

American crime boss who was the most powerful mob figure in Harlem from the 1940s through the 1960s. He fought against Dutch Schultz's encroachment into Harlem and later worked with the Italian Mafia.

## Background and Rise in Harlem

Ellsworth "Bumpy" Johnson became a well-known figure in Harlem’s underworld during a period when gambling, extortion, and the illegal alcohol trade created opportunities for organized crime.
He was associated with influential Harlem numbers operators and was frequently described as a power broker between local networks and larger criminal organizations.

## Relationship to Wider Organized Crime

Accounts of Johnson’s career often emphasize his ability to negotiate with outside groups seeking influence in Harlem.
He is commonly linked in popular histories to interactions with Italian-American Mafia figures, reflecting the broader pattern of alliances and rivalries in mid-20th-century New York.

## Public Image and Mythmaking

Johnson’s reputation has been shaped by both criminal cases and later retellings in books, film, and television.
Some portrayals emphasize a "Harlem protector" narrative, but the historical record also centers on coercive rackets and violence typical of organized crime.

## Death and Aftermath

Johnson died in 1968.
His death is often treated as the end of an era in Harlem’s organized-crime landscape, as law-enforcement pressure and changing markets altered the structure of illegal enterprises.
