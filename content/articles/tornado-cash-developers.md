## Summary

Developers of Tornado Cash crypto mixer charged with laundering $1 billion including funds stolen by North Korea.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2019 | Tornado Cash is described publicly as an open-source, non-custodial cryptocurrency "mixer" deployed on Ethereum-compatible networks.[^2] |

## What "developer" means in this context

Tornado Cash is generally described as an open-source set of smart contracts plus associated web interfaces and tooling.[^2] In public debate, the term "developers" can refer to:

- people who authored or maintained the smart-contract code,
- people who operated or maintained user-facing infrastructure (web apps, relayers, documentation),
- or DAO / governance participants associated with protocol changes.

This distinction matters because legal and policy arguments often turn on whether the relevant conduct is best characterized as **publishing code** (speech / engineering activity) versus **running a service** (operational activity).

## Legal and policy significance (high level)

The Tornado Cash developer cases are frequently cited in broader discussions about:[^2]

- **Financial privacy vs. enforcement:** privacy tools can be used for legitimate purposes, but can also be exploited to obscure criminal proceeds.
- **Sanctions and open-source software:** whether and how governments can sanction decentralized protocols and their maintainers.
- **Accountability in decentralized systems:** the challenge of assigning responsibility when a protocol is non-custodial and deployed as smart contracts.

## Commonly cited public-source details (non-exhaustive)

Public sources (including Wikipedia summaries) commonly associate the project with named individuals and with allegations that the protocol was used to launder large sums, including funds linked to the Lazarus Group (North Korea-associated hacking group).[^2] This repository entry keeps the description high-level and defers details to the cited references.
