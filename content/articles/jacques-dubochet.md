## Summary

Jacques Dubochet is a Swiss biophysicist who shared the 2017 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for developing cryo-electron microscopy.

## Cryo-EM and Vitrification

Dubochet’s Nobel-recognized contribution is closely tied to a practical obstacle in electron microscopy: biological samples are damaged in a vacuum and by the electron beam. The approach of rapidly freezing specimens so that water forms **vitreous (non-crystalline) ice** helps preserve structures close to their native state.

This “vitrification” technique became a key enabling step for modern cryo-electron microscopy, which can be used to determine structures of large biomolecules and complexes.

## Career and Recognition

He worked in academic and research settings in Europe and is associated with the broader cryo-EM revolution that accelerated in the 2000s and 2010s with improvements in detectors, computation, and sample preparation.

The 2017 Nobel Prize in Chemistry was awarded jointly to Dubochet and other pioneers of cryo-EM for methods that made high-resolution structural analysis of biomolecules more feasible.

## Public Engagement

Dubochet has also been noted for public-facing engagement and advocacy on scientific and social issues, reflecting a view that scientists can have responsibilities beyond the laboratory.
