## Summary

Caribbean-American crime boss known as Queenie who ran a numbers racket in Harlem during the 1920s-1940s. She battled Dutch Schultz for control of Harlem's illegal lottery and was one of the first Black women to lead a criminal enterprise.

## Harlem and the numbers game

St. Clair rose to prominence during a period when the “numbers” lottery was a major underground business in Harlem, intertwined with local politics, neighborhood employment, and informal credit networks. Operators collected small bets, paid out winnings, and relied on trusted runners and bookkeepers to manage cash flow.

## Conflict with rival gangs

As outside criminal groups sought to take control of Harlem’s numbers market, St. Clair became known for resisting encroachment and defending her organization’s territory. Her rivalry with Dutch Schultz is frequently cited as part of the broader struggle between established Harlem operators and outside racketeers in the 1930s.

## Public stance and legal pressure

Accounts of St. Clair often emphasize that she publicly criticized police corruption and harassment, including by speaking to journalists and participating in court proceedings. These actions were unusual for a major underworld figure and contributed to her later reputation as both a racketeer and a community power broker.

## Legacy

St. Clair’s story has been revisited in studies of Harlem history, women in organized crime, and the economics of illegal lotteries. She is also referenced in discussions of how marginalized communities built parallel institutions when excluded from formal banking and political influence.

## Key points

- Prominent Harlem numbers operator during the early-to-mid 20th century.
- Known for conflict with rival racketeers and for challenging police corruption.
- Remembered as an influential Black woman in an era dominated by male criminal leadership.
