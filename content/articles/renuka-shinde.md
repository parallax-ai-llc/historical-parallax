## Summary

Indian serial killer who, with her sister Seema Gavit, kidnapped and murdered 13 children to use as cover for their pickpocketing operations. When children became burdensome, they killed them. Both sentenced to death.

## Background and Context

- Shinde and Gavit were sisters from Maharashtra and were reported to have lived on the margins of urban life, relying on petty theft and pickpocketing.
- The pair targeted very young children, using them both as a diversion while stealing and as a means of eliciting sympathy from passersby.

## Crimes and Modus Operandi

- Accounts describe the abductions as opportunistic: children were taken from public places such as markets, stations, or crowded streets.
- When a child drew attention, became difficult to control, or reduced the effectiveness of theft operations, the sisters killed the child and moved on.
- The pattern of repeated kidnappings across multiple years made the case notable within Indian criminal justice reporting.

## Investigation, Trial, and Sentencing

- The sisters were arrested in 1996; the prosecution case focused on a series of abductions and murders attributed to them over the early-to-mid 1990s.
- A trial court convicted them in 2001 and imposed the death penalty.
- In 2014, the Supreme Court of India confirmed the death sentence, a decision often cited in discussions of capital punishment standards and the "rarest of rare" doctrine in India.

## Status and Legacy

- Public sources have periodically reported that Shinde and Gavit remained on death row for years after the Supreme Court judgment, amid India’s lengthy clemency and review processes.
- The case is frequently referenced in Indian media as an example of serial child abduction linked to organized petty crime.
