## Summary

Data breach at Marriott International discovered in 2018 that exposed personal information of 500 million guests. Chinese intelligence hackers were suspected.

## What was affected

The incident was linked to systems associated with Starwood (acquired by Marriott), illustrating how technical debt and legacy infrastructure can carry forward after a merger. Public reporting described a wide range of guest-related records being exposed, including contact details and travel-related information.

In many large breaches, the most serious downstream risk comes from data reuse: a leaked identifier (email, phone number, passport number) can be combined with other sources to enable identity fraud or targeted phishing.

## Discovery and disclosure

The breach was publicly disclosed after being detected in 2018, with a timeline suggesting unauthorized access began years earlier. Long dwell times are common in advanced intrusions, particularly when attackers prioritize stealth and repeated access over immediate disruption.

## Organizational and regulatory consequences

Large hospitality datasets are attractive targets because they include high volumes of frequent travelers and high-value individuals. The Marriott case is often cited in discussions of acquisition due diligence, incident response readiness, and how to manage security integration after corporate consolidation.

Regulatory scrutiny and civil litigation are common outcomes of breaches of this scale. Even when specific attribution is disputed, companies typically face mandatory notifications, remediation costs, and long-term investments in monitoring and security controls.

## Lessons frequently drawn

Common takeaways include the need for network segmentation, centralized logging, and continuous monitoring across inherited systems, as well as rigorous asset inventories so that older platforms do not become blind spots.

## Longer-term impact

Beyond immediate incident response, breaches like this often drive multi-year programs: migrating off legacy platforms, standardizing identity and access management, and improving vendor risk management across large franchise ecosystems.
