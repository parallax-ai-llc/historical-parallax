## Summary

Grünenthal's thalidomide drug prescribed to pregnant women caused 10,000+ children to be born with severe birth defects. The company suppressed evidence of the drug's dangers.

## What Happened (High Level)

- Thalidomide was marketed in the late 1950s for indications including nausea and as a sedative.
- When taken during pregnancy, it caused severe congenital malformations (teratogenic effects), leading to a large number of affected infants across multiple countries.
- The scale of harm turned the drug into a defining example of the risks of inadequate pre-market testing and post-market surveillance.

## Scientific and Medical Lessons

- The disaster reinforced the importance of evaluating developmental toxicity and the timing of exposure during pregnancy.
- It also highlighted the need for robust adverse-event reporting systems and clearer warning labels for medications.

## Regulatory Impact

- Thalidomide is often cited as a catalyst for stronger drug-approval standards and regulatory reforms in several jurisdictions.
- In the United States, it is commonly associated with the FDA's cautious review process during that era, shaping later expectations for clinical evidence.

## Corporate and Legal Aftermath

- The scandal led to lawsuits, criminal investigations in some contexts, and long-running compensation and support mechanisms for survivors.
- Public discussions frequently focus on what company leadership knew, when warnings became available, and how quickly risk information was communicated.

## Long-Term Legacy

- Survivors and advocacy organizations have remained active for decades, seeking medical support, recognition, and adequate compensation.
- Thalidomide later returned to medical use for limited indications under strict controls, often cited as an example of risk-management programs in pharmacovigilance.
