## Summary

Japan forced an estimated 780,000 Koreans into labor in mines, factories, and construction during WWII. Many died from brutal working conditions, including at the Hashima Island coal mine.

## Historical context

- Korea was under Japanese colonial rule (19106945), which shaped recruitment, coercion, and administrative control.
- Wartime industrial expansion increased demand for labor across Japan and Japanese-controlled territories.
- Mobilization policies tightened over time, moving from recruitment and incentives to more coercive forms.

## Forms of labor and conditions (high level)

- Work included mining, heavy industry, shipbuilding, and construction.
- Conditions often involved long hours, dangerous environments, inadequate food, and restricted movement.
- Mortality and injury risks were especially high in mines and other hazardous workplaces.

## Postwar legacy

- Survivors and families have sought recognition, compensation, and formal apologies.
- The issue has remained a recurring point of dispute in JapanSouth Korea relations.
- Legal cases and settlements have varied by jurisdiction and period, and the topic continues to be politically sensitive.

## Memory and documentation

- Documentation includes company records, government archives, survivor testimony, and investigative scholarship.
- Estimates can differ depending on definitions (e.g., location, time period, and degree of coercion).
