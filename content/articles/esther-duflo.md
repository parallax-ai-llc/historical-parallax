## Summary

Esther Duflo is a French-American development economist and co-founder of the Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab (J-PAL) at MIT, where she holds the Abdul Latif Jameel Professorship of Poverty Alleviation and Development Economics. She is best known for pioneering the application of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) to development economics, transforming how researchers and policymakers evaluate interventions aimed at reducing global poverty. In 2019, she was awarded the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences alongside Abhijit Banerjee and Michael Kremer for their experimental approach to alleviating global poverty.

Duflo's work spans education, health, microfinance, and governance in developing countries, with fieldwork concentrated in India, Kenya, Indonesia, and West Africa. Her research has directly influenced policy decisions affecting tens of millions of people. At 46, she became the second woman and youngest person ever to receive the Nobel Prize in Economics, cementing her status as one of the most consequential economists of her generation.

## Early Life & Background

Born on October 25, 1972, in Paris, France, Duflo grew up in an intellectually stimulating household — her mother was a pediatrician and her father a mathematics professor. She studied history and economics at the École Normale Supérieure in Paris, initially drawn to historical research. A formative experience came during a year spent in Russia in 1993–1994, where she worked as a research assistant to economist Jeffrey Sachs during the post-Soviet transition. Witnessing the effects of economic upheaval on ordinary people sharpened her focus on poverty and applied economics. She earned her PhD in Economics from MIT in 1999, where she was mentored by Abhijit Banerjee, who would later become her research partner and husband.

## Rise to Prominence

Duflo joined the MIT faculty immediately after completing her doctorate and, alongside Banerjee, founded J-PAL in 2003. The lab became the institutional home for a network of researchers conducting RCTs to rigorously test development interventions. Her early landmark studies — including work on teacher incentives in India and the impact of deworming programs in Kenya — demonstrated that careful experimental design could cut through anecdotal evidence and ideology to show what actually works. By the mid-2000s, J-PAL had grown into a global network with regional offices across six continents, influencing billions of dollars in policy spending.

## Key Achievements

- **Nobel Prize in Economics (2019):** Shared with Banerjee and Kremer for experimental approaches to poverty research.
- **J-PAL co-founder:** The lab has evaluated over 1,000 RCTs and influenced policies reaching more than 400 million people worldwide.
- **MacArthur Fellowship (2009):** Awarded a "genius grant" at age 36.
- **John Bates Clark Medal (2010):** Awarded to the top American economist under 40, making her the first woman to receive the prize.
- **Authored *Poor Economics* (2011):** Co-written with Banerjee, the book became an international bestseller and translated into over 17 languages.
- **Presidential appointment (2013):** Served on the President's Global Development Council under the Obama administration.

## Controversies & Criticism

Duflo's RCT-centered approach has attracted substantial methodological criticism. Economists including Angus Deaton and Martin Ravallion have argued that RCTs, while rigorous in isolation, struggle to generalize beyond specific local contexts and cannot address structural or macroeconomic causes of poverty. Critics contend that the emphasis on small-scale experiments may crowd out attention to systemic policy reform, trade, and governance. Others have questioned whether the "randomista" movement oversimplifies the complex political economy of development. Duflo has engaged these critiques directly, acknowledging the limits of RCTs while defending their role as one essential tool among many.

## Legacy & Influence

Duflo has fundamentally reshaped development economics by embedding empirical rigor at its core. J-PAL's policy outreach wing has translated findings into concrete government programs in India, Kenya, Morocco, and elsewhere. Her work has influenced the design of conditional cash transfer programs, school meal initiatives, and vaccination campaigns. Beyond academia, she has demonstrated that economics can be a vehicle for practical humanitarianism, inspiring a generation of researchers to treat poverty as a solvable empirical problem rather than an intractable structural condition.
