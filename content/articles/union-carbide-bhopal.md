## Summary

Union Carbide's Bhopal plant leaked 40 tons of methyl isocyanate killing 3,787 immediately and up to 16,000 over time. CEO Warren Anderson avoided extradition; victims received minimal compensation.

## Background

The disaster occurred at a pesticide plant in Bhopal operated by **Union Carbide India Limited (UCIL)**, in which **Union Carbide Corporation (UCC)** held a controlling stake. The plant used and stored **methyl isocyanate (MIC)**, a highly toxic intermediate used in pesticide production.

## What happened (high level)

In the early hours of 3 December 1984, a large release of MIC and other gases formed a toxic cloud over nearby densely populated areas. The incident is widely described as one of the worst industrial disasters in history, with long-term health impacts reported among exposed residents.

## Immediate aftermath and public health impact

Reported consequences include:

- **Acute deaths and injuries** in the days following exposure.
- **Chronic respiratory, ocular, neurological, and reproductive harms** described in later medical and public-health reporting.
- **Intergenerational concerns**, including ongoing disputes about attributable effects and the quality of available epidemiological data.

## Legal and corporate aftermath

Key themes in the legal history commonly cited in overviews include:

- **Jurisdiction and accountability disputes** between Indian and US proceedings.
- A major **civil settlement** between UCC and the Government of India, criticized by many victim advocates as inadequate relative to harms.
- Continued controversy over the role and liability of senior executives, including **Warren Anderson**, and the practical limits of extradition.

## Environmental contamination and legacy

Beyond the initial gas release, observers have highlighted:

- **Persistent site contamination** (soil and groundwater) in and around the former facility.
- The slow pace and contested scope of **cleanup and remediation**.
- The way Bhopal became a global reference point for **process safety**, **corporate governance**, and **industrial disaster regulation**.
