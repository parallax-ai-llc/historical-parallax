## Summary

Chinese banker and former chairman of Huarong Asset Management who was executed for bribery and corruption involving $277 million—the largest amount in a Chinese corruption case. Police found 3 tons of cash in his home.

## Background

Lai Xiaomin rose through China’s financial regulatory and state-owned banking ecosystem and later became a senior executive at China Huarong Asset Management, a major state-owned “bad bank” created to manage distressed assets.

## Role at Huarong

As chairman, Lai was associated with the company’s expansion beyond its original non-performing loan resolution mandate into broader financial activities.
In subsequent years, Huarong faced heightened scrutiny over governance, risk controls, and balance sheet complexity.

## Investigation and Trial (High-Level)

Lai’s case was pursued amid China’s broader anti-corruption campaign targeting senior officials across government and state-owned enterprises.
Chinese courts reported multiple charges, including accepting bribes and abusing authority.
The sentencing and execution were widely interpreted as a signal of strict enforcement, particularly in the financial sector.

## Broader Significance

The case became a prominent example in discussions about corporate governance at state-owned financial institutions, regulatory oversight, and the political importance of stability in China’s banking and shadow-finance systems.
