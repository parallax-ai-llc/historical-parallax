## Summary

Elizabeth Holmes claimed Theranos could run 200+ tests from a single drop of blood. The technology never worked; patients received inaccurate results that endangered their health.

## What Was Claimed

- Theranos asserted it could perform a large menu of laboratory tests from a very small blood sample (often described as a finger-prick).
- The company presented proprietary devices (often associated with the names “Edison” and later “miniLab”) as the basis for the breakthrough.

## What Investigations Found

- Reporting and subsequent investigations indicated that many tests were run on conventional analyzers rather than Theranos’s proprietary machines.
- Some Theranos methods and quality-control practices were alleged to be unreliable, contributing to inaccurate or inconsistent patient results.

## Timeline Highlights (expanded context)

- The Wall Street Journal reporting in 2015 triggered broader regulatory and legal scrutiny.
- U.S. regulators and prosecutors pursued actions that ultimately described the episode as a prolonged deception of investors, partners, and patients.

## Legal Outcome (high level)

- Elizabeth Holmes and other executives faced criminal charges related to fraud.
- Holmes was convicted on multiple counts connected to investor fraud (with the case becoming a major reference point for accountability in startup culture).

## Why It Matters

- The Theranos case is commonly used as a cautionary example of:
  - hype-driven fundraising and weak technical diligence
  - opacity in health-tech where claims can affect patient safety
  - governance failures (boards and partners failing to verify core technical assertions)
