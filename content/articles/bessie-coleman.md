## Summary

Bessie Coleman was an American civil aviator who became the first African American woman — and the first person of Native American descent — to hold a pilot's license. Born in 1892 in Atlanta, Texas, she overcame racial and gender barriers that barred her from flight schools in the United States by traveling to France, where she earned her international pilot's license from the Fédération Aéronautique Internationale in 1921.

Coleman dedicated her flying career to inspiring Black Americans and advocating for equal access to aviation. She performed as a barnstormer across the United States, drawing large crowds and refusing to perform at venues that excluded Black audiences. Her life was cut short at 34, but her pioneering example helped open the door to future generations of Black aviators.

## Early Life & Background

Bessie Coleman was born on January 26, 1892, in Atlanta, Texas, the tenth of thirteen children in a family of sharecroppers. Her father, George Coleman, was of mixed Cherokee and African American descent; her mother, Susan, was African American. When Bessie was two, the family relocated to Waxahachie, Texas, where she grew up picking cotton and walking four miles each day to a segregated schoolhouse.

Despite limited resources, Coleman developed a passion for reading and mathematics. She attended the Colored Agricultural and Normal University (now Langston University) in Oklahoma in 1910 but had to leave after one term due to lack of funds. In 1915, she moved to Chicago, where she worked as a manicurist and became inspired by pilots returning from World War I.

## Rise to Prominence

Determined to fly, Coleman applied to U.S. flight schools but was rejected on the basis of both her race and her sex. With encouragement from Robert Abbott, founder of the Chicago Defender, she enrolled in the Caudron Brothers' School of Aviation in Le Crotoy, France. On June 15, 1921, she received her pilot's license from the Fédération Aéronautique Internationale — the first issued to a Black woman anywhere in the world.

Coleman returned to France in 1922 for advanced training in stunt flying, then launched her American barnstorming career later that year. She performed her first U.S. air show on September 3, 1922, at Curtiss Field in Garden City, New York, attracting a large crowd. She became known as "Brave Bessie" and toured the South and Midwest, insisting that all her shows be open to Black and white audiences alike through integrated gates.

## Key Achievements

- **June 15, 1921:** Became the first Black woman and first person of Native American descent to earn an international pilot's license.
- Performed aerial exhibitions across the United States from 1922 to 1926, popularizing aviation among Black communities.
- Publicly refused to perform in venues enforcing racial segregation, leveraging her celebrity for civil rights advocacy.
- Announced plans to open a flight school for Black Americans, a goal she pursued until her death.

## Controversies & Criticism

Coleman's barnstorming career involved genuine physical danger, and she suffered a serious plane crash in Santa Monica, California, in February 1923, breaking her leg and several ribs. Critics within some circles questioned her decision to return to flying after the accident given the risks, but Coleman was unyielding. Some contemporary accounts also questioned the sustainability of her ambitions given the financial difficulties she faced in securing aircraft and sponsorship.

## Legacy & Influence

Coleman died on April 30, 1926, in Jacksonville, Florida, when she was thrown from an open-cockpit biplane during a rehearsal flight after the aircraft went into an uncontrolled dive. Her mechanic and pilot, William D. Wills, was also killed in the crash.

Her legacy endured powerfully. In 1929, the all-Black aviators' club the Bessie Coleman Aero Club was founded in Los Angeles. The Tuskegee Airmen credited her as an inspiration. In 1995, the U.S. Postal Service issued a commemorative stamp in her honor. Chicago's city government designated May 2 as Bessie Coleman Day, and in 1990 a street near O'Hare International Airport was renamed in her honor. She remains a central figure in the history of both aviation and the African American civil rights struggle.
