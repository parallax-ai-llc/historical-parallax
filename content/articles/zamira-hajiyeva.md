## Summary

Wife of jailed Azerbaijani banker subject to UK's first Unexplained Wealth Order. Spent £16 million at Harrods alone despite having no known independent source of income.

## Background

Zamira Hajiyeva became widely known in the UK through a legal process designed to scrutinize the origins of assets held by politically exposed persons (PEPs) or individuals linked to serious crime. Her case is frequently cited as the first high-profile test of the UK’s **Unexplained Wealth Order (UWO)** regime.

She is associated in reporting with the former chair of the International Bank of Azerbaijan, **Jahangir Hajiyev**, who was convicted in Azerbaijan on fraud and embezzlement-related charges. UK proceedings discussed her spending and property holdings in the context of whether her declared wealth could plausibly be explained by known lawful income.

## Unexplained Wealth Order (UWO) Context

A UWO is a civil investigative tool that can require a person to explain the nature and extent of their interest in certain property, and how it was obtained. In simplified terms:

- The court can order an explanation for assets above a value threshold.
- The focus is on whether the respondent can provide a credible lawful source of funds.
- Separate asset-recovery steps may follow if explanations are not accepted or are contradicted by evidence.

Because the measure is civil (not criminal), the standard and structure of proceedings differ from a criminal prosecution, and the case has been used in commentary about how the UK attempts to deter the use of London property and luxury markets for illicit finance.

## Public Significance

The case is often discussed in relation to:

- **Anti-money-laundering enforcement** and the use of financial intelligence in court.
- The role of luxury retailers and high-end services in detecting suspicious spending patterns.
- Cross-border challenges when allegations, convictions, or evidence originate in one jurisdiction while assets are held in another.

## Notes

Public coverage sometimes frames Hajiyeva primarily through the UWO and the “Harrods spending” headline, but the core legal question in UK proceedings concerns asset provenance and the credibility of explanations offered to the court.
