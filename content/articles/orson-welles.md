## Summary

George Orson Welles (May 6, 1915 – October 10, 1985) was an American actor, director, producer, and writer considered among the greatest and most influential filmmakers of all time.[^1] At age 25, he co-wrote, produced, directed, and starred in *Citizen Kane* (1941), often called the greatest film ever made.[^2] His innovative narrative techniques, use of photography, dramatic lighting, and music revolutionized cinema and influenced generations of filmmakers.[^3] Before Hollywood, Welles achieved fame through his Mercury Theatre productions and his 1938 radio broadcast of *The War of the Worlds*, which caused widespread panic among listeners.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Orson Welles was born George Orson Welles on May 6, 1915, in Kenosha, Wisconsin, the second son of Richard Head Welles, an inventor and businessman, and Beatrice Ives Welles, a concert pianist and suffragist.[^5] From birth, he was surrounded by artistic influence and intellectual stimulation. His mother, an accomplished musician, recognized her son's prodigious talents early and encouraged his artistic development.[^6]

Welles was a child prodigy skilled in music, acting, and writing. His extraordinary gifts manifested at an astonishingly young age—he could read before age three and was performing Shakespeare by age seven.[^7] However, his childhood was marked by tragedy. His parents separated when he was four years old, and his mother died when he was nine, leaving a profound impact on the young artist.[^8] His father died in 1930 when Welles was fifteen, and he became the ward of Chicago doctor Maurice Bernstein, a family friend who had been a significant presence in his life.[^9]

In 1926, Welles entered the exclusive Todd School for Boys in Woodstock, Illinois, where his extraordinary talents found fertile ground.[^10] Under the mentorship of Roger Hill, the school's headmaster, Welles dazzled teachers and students with stagings of both modern and classical plays. The Todd School became a laboratory for his theatrical experiments, allowing him to direct, act in, and design productions of Shakespeare and other classical works.[^11] In 1931, he graduated from Todd at age sixteen, but instead of attending college, he studied briefly at the Art Institute of Chicago before embarking on a transformative journey to Dublin, Ireland.[^12]

In Dublin, the sixteen-year-old Welles audaciously presented himself at the Gate Theatre, claiming to be a star of the New York Theatre Guild. Impressed by his confidence and talent, the theatre's directors gave him roles, and he made his stage debut in 1931.[^13] This early success in Ireland gave Welles invaluable professional experience and confirmed his decision to pursue a theatrical career rather than formal education.

---

## Rise to Power

Welles returned to the United States in 1933 and began working in radio and theatre, quickly establishing himself as a force in American cultural life.[^14] By 1935, he was working as a radio actor in Manhattan with many actors who would later form the core of his Mercury Theatre.[^15] His powerful, distinctive voice and dramatic flair made him a natural for the medium, and he soon became one of radio's most sought-after performers.

In 1935, John Houseman, director of the Negro Theatre Unit of the Federal Theatre Project in New York, invited the twenty-year-old Welles to join the project.[^16] Their first collaboration was an adaptation of William Shakespeare's *Macbeth* with an entirely African-American cast, set in Haiti and featuring voodoo elements.[^17] When the "Voodoo Macbeth" opened at the Lafayette Theatre in Harlem on April 14, 1936, 10,000 people filled the streets in anticipation, and every one of the theatre's 1,223 seats was filled.[^18] The production was a sensation, launching Welles's meteoric directing career before he had even turned twenty-one.[^19]

Welles and Houseman continued their collaboration with the Federal Theatre Project, staging *The Tragical History of Doctor Faustus* in January 1937, with Welles playing the title role while also directing and designing costumes.[^20] In July 1937, the Mutual Network gave Welles a seven-week series to adapt *Les Misérables*, marking his first job as a writer-director for radio and the debut of the Mercury Theatre name.[^21]

In August 1937, Welles and Houseman broke with the Federal Theatre Project and founded their own repertory company, the Mercury Theatre.[^22] Their first production, Shakespeare's *Julius Caesar* adapted by Welles and set in fascist Italy, opened on Broadway on November 11, 1937, to critical acclaim.[^23] The production's modern, politically charged interpretation resonated with audiences concerned about rising fascism in Europe.

On July 11, 1938, *The Mercury Theatre on the Air* debuted on the CBS Radio network, airing Mondays at 9 pm.[^24] The series presented adaptations of classic literature with innovative sound design and dramatic techniques. On October 30, 1938, the series broadcast Welles's adaptation of H.G. Wells's *The War of the Worlds*, formatting it as a series of breaking news bulletins describing a Martian invasion of New Jersey.[^25] Some listeners mistook the bulletins for reality, and their anxious phone calls to police and newspapers convinced many journalists that the show had caused nationwide panic.[^26] Though the scale of the panic was later disputed, the broadcast made Welles a household name overnight and demonstrated radio's power as a medium.[^27]

The notoriety from *The War of the Worlds* helped Welles secure an unprecedented contract with RKO Pictures. On July 22, 1939, Welles signed a summary agreement with RKO, followed by a full 63-page contract on August 21, 1939.[^28] The contract, signed when Welles was only twenty-four and had never made a feature film, gave him a degree of creative control—including final cut—that was unprecedented during the studio era.[^29] RKO agreed to pay Mercury Theatre $100,000 for the first film by January 1, 1940, plus 20% of profits after the studio recouped $500,000.[^30]

## Major Achievements

At age twenty-five, Orson Welles co-wrote, produced, directed, and starred in *Citizen Kane* (1941), his first feature film.[^31] The film tells the story of Charles Foster Kane, a powerful newspaper publisher, through a non-linear narrative structure that was revolutionary for its time. Welles developed the screenplay with Herman J. Mankiewicz, though the authorship credit would later become controversial.[^32]

*Citizen Kane* introduced or popularized numerous filmmaking techniques that would influence cinema for generations. Welles employed deep focus photography, allowing foreground and background elements to remain in sharp focus simultaneously, creating complex visual compositions.[^33] He used innovative camera angles, including low-angle shots that required cinematographer Gregg Toland to film through floors with removable sections.[^34] The film's narrative structure, told through flashbacks from multiple perspectives, challenged conventional Hollywood storytelling.[^35] Welles also innovated in sound design, using overlapping dialogue and sophisticated audio techniques learned from his radio work.[^36]

The film was voted the best picture of 1941 by the National Board of Review and New York Film Critics Circle.[^37] It garnered nine Academy Award nominations, though it won only Best Original Screenplay, shared by Mankiewicz and Welles.[^38] Despite its initial commercial failure, *Citizen Kane* was later re-evaluated by critics and consistently ranked as the greatest film ever made, topping the prestigious Sight and Sound poll from 1962 to 2012.[^39] The American Film Institute ranked it as the greatest American film ever made in both 1998 and 2007.[^40]

Following *Citizen Kane*, Welles directed *The Magnificent Ambersons* (1942), adapting Booth Tarkington's Pulitzer Prize-winning novel about a wealthy Midwestern family's decline during the automobile age.[^41] However, while Welles was in South America making a documentary for the government, RKO cut the film from his intended 131-minute version to 88 minutes and reshot the ending.[^42] Despite this studio interference, the film was selected for preservation in the National Film Registry in 1991 as "culturally, historically, or aesthetically significant."[^43]

Throughout the 1940s and 1950s, Welles continued to create groundbreaking work despite increasing conflicts with studios. *The Lady from Shanghai* (1947), starring Welles and his then-wife Rita Hayworth, featured a famous climactic shootout in a hall of mirrors.[^44] *Touch of Evil* (1958), written and directed by Welles, is now widely regarded as one of his best motion pictures and one of the last great films of the classic noir era.[^45] The film opens with one of cinema's most famous shots: a continuous three-minute tracking shot that establishes the border-town setting.[^46] Like *The Magnificent Ambersons*, *Touch of Evil* was re-cut by the studio, though a restored version closer to Welles's vision was released in 1998.[^47]

During his European period, Welles made *Othello* (1952), which won the Palme d'Or at the Cannes Film Festival, though its production was plagued by financial difficulties.[^48] *The Trial* (1962), adapting Franz Kafka's novel of existential dread, was considered by Welles himself to be one of his best films.[^49] *Chimes at Midnight* (1965) featured Welles as Shakespeare's Sir John Falstaff, borrowing elements from multiple Shakespeare plays to create a meditation on friendship, aging, and the loss of innocence.[^50] Many critics consider it one of the finest Shakespeare adaptations ever filmed.

*F for Fake* (1973), Welles's final completed feature, pioneered the essay film format.[^51] Focusing on art forger Elmyr de Hory, the film investigates themes of authorship, authenticity, and deception through innovative editing techniques.[^52] When asked if he was making a documentary, Welles replied: "No, not a documentary—a new kind of film."[^53]

In 2002, British Film Institute polls among directors and critics voted Welles the greatest film director of all time.[^54] Andrew Sarris included him in the "pantheon" of the fourteen greatest film directors who had worked in the United States in his influential book *The American Cinema: Directors and Directions 1929–1968*.[^55] Welles received an Academy Award, three Grammy Awards, the Golden Lion in 1947, the Palme d'Or in 1952, the Academy Honorary Award in 1970, the AFI Life Achievement Award in 1975, and the British Film Institute Fellowship in 1983.[^56]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his genius, Orson Welles's career was marked by significant controversies and conflicts. *Citizen Kane* created bitter enemies before it was even released. The film's plot, which followed a powerful newspaper publisher's rise and fall, bore striking similarities to the life of William Randolph Hearst.[^57] The film so angered Hearst that he banned press coverage in his publications and, at one point, accused Welles of being a communist—a charge that could have destroyed his career during the anti-communist fervor of the era.[^58]

At the 1942 Academy Awards ceremony, the audience at the Biltmore Hotel jeered at every *Citizen Kane* mention.[^59] Despite nine Oscar nominations, the film received only one award. There was considerable controversy over the screenplay credit between Mankiewicz and Welles, with some arguing that Mankiewicz deserved sole credit.[^60] The authorship dispute has been debated by scholars for decades, with recent films like *Mank* (2020) reigniting the controversy.

Welles's unprecedented RKO contract created instant enmity in Hollywood. The Saturday Evening Post reported that "No other newcomer's arrival in Hollywood ever caused so much indignation as Welles'."[^61] The agreement was bitterly resented by Hollywood studios and persistently mocked in the trade press.[^62] His co-writer Herman Mankiewicz reportedly quipped, "There but for the grace of God, goes God!"—a remark that captured the industry's perception of Welles's arrogance.[^63]

Throughout his career, Welles struggled with studio interference and financial problems. The studio famously butchered *The Magnificent Ambersons* in editing, cutting over forty minutes from Welles's version and reshooting the ending while he was in South America.[^64] A stage production of *Around the World* failed due to poor box office in 1946, leaving Welles personally bankrupt and unable to claim the losses on his taxes.[^65]

In Welles's last decades and following his death, the dominant narrative portrayed him as a tragic figure who had failed to realize his potential. The *New York Times Magazine* ran a cover story titled "Orson Welles: The Unfulfilled Promise," and his obituary described him as a "boy wonder" who had failed "to realize his dreams."[^66] Broadway critic Walter Kerr called him "possibly the world's youngest has-been."[^67]

Welles developed a notorious reputation for being hostile toward fellow filmmakers. He harshly criticized other directors, describing Alfred Hitchcock's later work as characterized by "egotism and laziness" and dismissing Woody Allen, saying "His arrogance is unlimited."[^68] These attacks, while reflecting Welles's strong artistic convictions, damaged his professional relationships and contributed to his reputation as difficult and egotistical.

Many of his later projects remained unfinished due to chronic funding problems. From 1970 to 1976, Welles shot and partially edited *The Other Side of the Wind*, a satire about the movie business, but money ran out before postproduction was completed.[^69] The film remained caught in a legal battle for decades, finally being released in 2018, thirty-three years after Welles's death.[^70]

## Personal Life

Orson Welles married three times, though none of his marriages lasted. He married his first wife, Virginia Nicholson, on December 23, 1934, when he was just nineteen years old.[^71] They had a daughter named Christopher, born on March 27, 1938.[^72] However, the marriage was marked by Welles's infidelities and frequent absences due to his theatrical work, ending on February 1, 1940.[^73]

His second marriage was to Hollywood star Rita Hayworth on September 7, 1943, when she was twenty-four and he was twenty-eight.[^74] Rita gave birth to their daughter, Rebecca Welles, on December 17, 1944.[^75] Despite Hayworth being described as "a wonderful wife," the marriage didn't last long.[^76] In March 1946, Hayworth separated from Welles, moving with their young daughter into a home of her own.[^77] The couple's relationship was strained by Welles's controlling nature and his obsessive dedication to his work at the expense of family life.

Welles's third and final wife was Countess Paola Mori di Girfalco, whom he met in the summer of 1952 while filming in Italy.[^78] They were married in 1955, two days before his fortieth birthday, and remained married until his death in 1985.[^79] They had a daughter, Beatrice, together.[^80] Despite remaining married to Paola Mori, Welles began a twenty-plus-year relationship with Croatian actress Oja Kodar after they met on the set of *The Trial* in 1962.[^81] Kodar became his artistic collaborator and companion for the remainder of his life, appearing in several of his later films.

Associates noted that "He wasn't going to be a family man" and "He just wasn't a person that a wedding ring meant that much to."[^82] Welles's 'womanizer' reputation was well documented, with numerous affairs throughout his marriages.[^83] His daughter Christopher later wrote about the emotional distance she felt from her father, who was often absent pursuing his artistic ambitions.

Beyond his romantic relationships, Welles was known for his larger-than-life personality, extraordinary conversational skills, and prodigious appetites. In his later years, he struggled with obesity and health problems related to his weight.[^84] He was a gourmet cook and wine connoisseur, talents he sometimes showcased in television commercials to finance his film projects.[^85]

Welles maintained friendships with numerous artists and intellectuals, including writer Herman Mankiewicz, actor Joseph Cotten, and filmmaker Peter Bogdanovich, who became a close friend and collaborator in Welles's final years.[^86] Their conversations formed the basis of the book *This Is Orson Welles*, published posthumously.[^87]

## Legacy

Orson Welles left behind a legacy as one of the most influential filmmakers in cinema history. His innovative narrative techniques and use of photography, dramatic lighting, and music made *Citizen Kane* one of the most influential films in the history of the art.[^88] François Truffaut declared, "Everything that matters in cinema since 1940 has been influenced by *Citizen Kane*."[^89]

Welles's directing style, characterized by deep focus photography, long takes, and experimental sound design, influenced generations of filmmakers.[^90] His use of deep shadows, oblique camera angles, and morally ambiguous characters helped define the visual and thematic elements of film noir.[^91] Directors such as Martin Scorsese, David Lynch, Christopher Nolan, Paul Thomas Anderson, and Wes Anderson have all acknowledged Welles's profound impact on their filmmaking.[^92]

Jean-Luc Godard remarked: "Everyone will always owe him everything" when describing Welles's influence on world cinema.[^93] The French New Wave directors, particularly those associated with *Cahiers du cinéma*, championed Welles as the ultimate auteur—a filmmaker with complete artistic vision and control.[^94]

Beyond his technical innovations, Welles expanded the possibilities of what film could express. His films explored themes of power, corruption, the nature of truth, the passage of time, and the impossibility of truly knowing another person.[^95] His work demonstrated that cinema could be as intellectually and artistically ambitious as literature or theatre while remaining viscerally entertaining.[^96]

Welles's influence extended beyond cinema to radio and theatre. His 1938 *War of the Worlds* broadcast was selected as one of the first fifty recordings added to the National Recording Registry of the Library of Congress in 2003.[^97] His theatrical innovations, particularly his politically engaged Shakespeare productions, influenced generations of stage directors.[^98]

The restoration and preservation of Welles's work continues decades after his death. A restored version of *Touch of Evil* conforming to Welles's notes was released in 1998.[^99] *The Other Side of the Wind* was finally completed and released in 2018.[^100] These restorations have allowed new generations to appreciate Welles's artistic vision without studio interference.

When he died on October 10, 1985, Welles left behind a legacy as a consummate artist and true auteur whose influence was profoundly felt by several generations of filmmakers.[^101] His work continues to be studied in film schools worldwide, and his techniques remain fundamental to cinema language.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

André Bazin, one of the most influential film critics in history, championed Welles in his book *Orson Welles: A Critical View*, discussing him as an artist whose use of deep focus and long takes helped to realize film's potential to re-present reality to the audience.[^102] Bazin's advocacy was instrumental in establishing Welles's critical reputation, particularly in France, where he was revered by the *Cahiers du cinéma* critics who would become New Wave filmmakers.[^103]

James Naremore's *The Magic World of Orson Welles* provides a comprehensive study from an auteurist standpoint, tracing themes and imagery that recur across Welles's career with detailed attention to individual scenes and shots.[^104] Naremore argues that Welles's entire body of work demonstrates a consistent artistic vision exploring power, performance, and authenticity.

Jonathan Rosenbaum's *Discovering Orson Welles*, collecting writings spanning thirty-five years, makes a persuasive case for the seriousness of Welles's work and illuminates both Welles the artist and Welles the man.[^105] Rosenbaum has been instrumental in recovering Welles's reputation from the "failed genius" narrative, arguing that Welles's later works are as artistically significant as *Citizen Kane*.

Film scholar Joseph McBride's books about Welles, including *What Ever Happened to Orson Welles?*, provide meticulously researched examinations of his career.[^106] McBride argues that the narrative of Welles as a "failure" stems from Hollywood's inability to accommodate his uncompromising artistic vision rather than any deficiency in Welles himself.

The career of Orson Welles and the critical consensus that developed around his films is inseparable from the history of auteur theory.[^107] Welles has been praised as "the ultimate auteur," a filmmaker who maintained artistic control and personal vision despite studio pressures.[^108] His example inspired generations of directors to fight for creative autonomy.

In 2002, a wide survey of critical consensus, best-of lists, and historical retrospectives identified Welles as the most acclaimed director of all time.[^109] This recognition vindicated those critics who had championed his work during his lifetime when he often struggled for funding and recognition in Hollywood.

### Negative Views

During his later career and immediately following his death, many critics characterized Welles as a tragic figure who squandered his potential. The "boy wonder" who created *Citizen Kane* at twenty-five was seen as unable to match that early achievement, leading to the "failed promise" narrative.[^110] This assessment focused on his unfinished projects and troubled production histories rather than the artistic merit of his completed works.

Some scholars have argued that Welles's ego and difficult personality contributed to his professional problems. His harsh criticisms of fellow directors, including Alfred Hitchcock and Woody Allen, suggested arrogance and a lack of professional generosity.[^111] These attacks alienated potential allies in the film industry and reinforced perceptions of Welles as impossible to work with.

The screenplay controversy surrounding *Citizen Kane* has led some critics to question whether Welles deserved sole authorship credit for his films' achievements. Pauline Kael's essay "Raising Kane" argued that Herman Mankiewicz deserved primary credit for the screenplay, suggesting that Welles exploited collaborators while taking credit for their work.[^112] Though Kael's argument has been widely criticized and largely debunked, it influenced perceptions of Welles's authorship.

Critics of the auteur theory have questioned the elevation of directors like Welles above other collaborators. They argue that the emphasis on Welles's singular genius obscures the contributions of cinematographers like Gregg Toland, composers like Bernard Herrmann, and editors like Robert Wise.[^113] This perspective sees the auteur narrative as perpetuating a romantic myth of the isolated genius rather than acknowledging film as a collaborative medium.

Some have criticized Welles's later work as self-indulgent and inaccessible. Films like *F for Fake* and *The Immortal Story* have been characterized as hermetic experiments of interest primarily to film scholars rather than general audiences.[^114] This criticism suggests that Welles became increasingly disconnected from mainstream cinema and audience expectations as his career progressed.

Hollywood executives and producers often found Welles financially irresponsible, going over budget and failing to deliver projects on schedule.[^115] This reputation, whether fair or not, made securing funding increasingly difficult and contributed to the string of unfinished projects that marked his later career.

## Famous Quotes

> "We're born alone, we live alone, we die alone. Only through our love and friendship can we create the illusion for the moment that we're not alone."[^116]

> "The enemy of art is the absence of limitations."[^117]

> "If you want a happy ending, that depends, of course, on where you stop your story."[^118]

> "Create your own visual style... let it be unique for yourself and yet identifiable for others."[^119]

> "I passionately hate the idea of being with it, I think an artist has always to be out of step with his time."[^120]

> "Nobody gets justice. People only get good luck or bad luck."[^121]

> "Everything about me is a contradiction, and so is everything about everybody else. We are made out of oppositions; we live between two poles."[^122]

> "If there hadn't been women we'd still be squatting in a cave eating raw meat, because we made civilization in order to impress our girlfriends."[^123]
