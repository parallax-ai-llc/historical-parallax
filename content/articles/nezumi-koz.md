## Summary

Famous Edo period thief who burglarized samurai estates and became a folk hero in kabuki theater. Eventually captured and executed.

## Historical Context

Nezumi Kozō ("Rat Boy") operated in late-Edo Japan, a period marked by rigid social hierarchy and strict controls over movement and commerce. High-profile thefts from samurai residences carried particular symbolic weight because they targeted the elite class.

## Folklore vs. Record

Later popular portrayals often emphasize a "Robin Hood" image—redistributing stolen wealth or humiliating the powerful. Historical accounts, however, tend to be cautious about claims of systematic redistribution, noting that legends grew through theater, gossip, and later print culture.

## Capture and Punishment

Edo authorities treated repeat burglary as a serious public-order threat. His execution and the handling of his remains became part of the lore, reflecting how public punishment was used to deter crime and reaffirm status order.

## Cultural Legacy

The figure endured in **kabuki**, storytelling, and modern media as an archetype of the clever outlaw. The durability of the story is often cited as an example of how criminal figures can be reimagined as folk heroes when they appear to challenge a disliked social order.

## Common Misconceptions

Some modern retellings exaggerate the scale of his thefts or portray him as an organized political rebel. Most historians treat him instead as a prolific burglar whose later myth grew because the story resonated with popular frustrations.
