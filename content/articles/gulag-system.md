## Summary

Soviet forced labor camp system that imprisoned 18 million people between 1930 and 1953. An estimated 1.5-1.8 million died from exhaustion, starvation, and disease in the camps.

## Administration and Purpose

- "Gulag" commonly refers to the Soviet state’s network of forced-labor camps administered through security and internal-affairs organs during the Stalin era and beyond.
- The system served multiple functions: punishment, political repression, social control, and the extraction of labor for state projects.

## Who Was Imprisoned

- Prisoners included people convicted of ordinary crimes as well as large numbers sentenced under political charges, often through harsh legal provisions.
- Terms and conditions varied by camp and period, but mortality was strongly influenced by food, climate, labor intensity, and medical access.

## Economic Role

- Forced labor contributed to mining, logging, construction, and infrastructure development, particularly in remote regions.
- Historians debate the economic efficiency of the camps, noting the high human cost and administrative burden alongside the output produced.

## Decline and Memory

- After Stalin’s death, amnesties and policy shifts reduced the camp population and changed the penal system’s structure.
- The Gulag remains a central symbol of Soviet repression, shaping modern discussions about totalitarian governance, political terror, and historical accountability.

## Notes

This article is a high-level overview; estimates of camp populations and deaths vary across historical studies.
