## Summary

Abdul Sattar Edhi was a Pakistani humanitarian and philanthropist widely regarded as one of the greatest humanitarians of the twentieth century. He founded the Edhi Foundation, which operates the world's largest volunteer ambulance network, along with homeless shelters, orphanages, rehabilitation centers, and free hospitals across Pakistan and internationally. Born in British India, Edhi dedicated his life to serving the poor, the sick, and the abandoned, often working in the most dangerous and neglected parts of society. He was nominated multiple times for the Nobel Peace Prize and received Pakistan's highest civilian honor, Nishan-e-Pakistan.

## Historical Significance

Edhi built one of the most comprehensive private welfare systems in the world from virtually nothing, relying on public donations and rejecting government funds to maintain independence. His ambulance fleet, at its peak numbering over 1,500 vehicles, responded to accidents, disasters, and medical emergencies around the clock. The Foundation's cradles placed outside its centers became a symbol of his ethos — a refuge for abandoned infants that saved thousands of lives. Edhi's work challenged the failures of state institutions in Pakistan and demonstrated that grassroots charity, sustained by personal sacrifice and public trust, could fill critical gaps in healthcare and social welfare. He is remembered as "Pakistan's Father Teresa" and remains a symbol of selfless service across religious and national boundaries.
