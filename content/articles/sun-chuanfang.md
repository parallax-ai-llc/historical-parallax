## Summary

Chinese warlord who controlled five provinces in eastern China during the warlord era. He was assassinated by the daughter of a man he had executed, who shot him in a Buddhist temple.

## Background and Rise

Sun Chuanfang emerged as a major military figure during the fragmentation of power in the early Republic of China, when regional commanders built personal armies and competed for control of tax bases, rail lines, and port cities.

## Political and Military Significance

His authority is often summarized as control over "five provinces," reflecting a period in the mid-1920s when shifting alliances and battlefield outcomes briefly consolidated his influence in the lower Yangtze region.

## Assassination

In 1935 Sun was killed in a Buddhist temple. Contemporary retellings commonly emphasize revenge as the motive: the attacker was the daughter of a man Sun had executed, and the killing became a widely cited example of personal vendetta intersecting with the violence of the warlord era.

## Legacy

Sun is remembered less for formal state-building than for illustrating the instability of the warlord period, where legitimacy was contested, governance was militarized, and political transitions were often decided through coercion and assassination.
