## Summary

French WWI hero who became head of the Vichy regime during WWII, collaborating with Nazi Germany. His government deported 77,000 Jews to death camps. Sentenced to death, commuted to life imprisonment due to age.

## Background

Pétain was a career French Army officer whose reputation was shaped by the First World War. He was associated with defensive tactics and with efforts to stabilize morale after the heavy losses of 1914–1915.

## World War I: Verdun and Reputation

During the 1916 Battle of Verdun, Pétain was credited with organizing logistics and rotation of units, contributing to France’s ability to hold the line. His status as a national hero in WWI later gave him political credibility during France’s crisis in 1940.

## Vichy France (1940–1944)

After France’s defeat by Germany in 1940, Pétain became Chief of State of the authoritarian Vichy regime. Key characteristics of the regime included:

- **Collaboration** with Nazi Germany in political and administrative matters.
- **Anti-democratic governance**, including suppression of political opposition and civil liberties.
- **Persecution policies**, including implementation of antisemitic statutes and participation in deportations.

Although the degree of autonomy and responsibility is debated in historical scholarship, Pétain’s government is widely held responsible for actions taken by the Vichy state, including deportations carried out by French authorities.

## Trial, Sentence, and Legacy

After the war, Pétain was tried and convicted of treason and related charges. His death sentence was commuted to life imprisonment, and he died in custody.

Pétain remains a highly controversial figure in France: remembered both for his WWI role and for leading a regime that collaborated with Nazi Germany and participated in crimes against humanity.
