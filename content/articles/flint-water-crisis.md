## Summary

See Rick Snyder. Michigan switched Flint's water source to save money, exposing 100,000 residents to lead contamination. The crisis disproportionately affected Black residents. Former governor Rick Snyder was charged but charges were later dropped.

## Additional Context

### What changed (water system mechanics)

A central technical point in the crisis was corrosion control. When Flint's water source was changed, water chemistry interacted with aging pipes and service lines. Without adequate anti-corrosion treatment, lead from plumbing materials could leach into drinking water.

### Public health and equity

The crisis is frequently discussed as a case study in environmental injustice because:

- Many residents affected were from low-income communities.
- The population of Flint includes a large share of Black residents.
- Residents reported discolored, foul-smelling water before broader government acknowledgment.

### Government response and aftermath

The episode led to multiple investigations, prosecutions, and civil litigation. It also triggered broader policy debates about:

- Oversight of municipal water systems.
- Emergency management and local democratic control.
- Lead service line replacement and long-term infrastructure funding.

### Long-term significance

Beyond Flint, the crisis became a national reference point for how administrative cost-cutting, fragmented accountability, and technical failures can converge into a large-scale public health emergency.
