## Summary

Former Rwandan mayor convicted of genocide by a German court for ordering the massacre of Tutsi refugees at a church. His was one of the first universal jurisdiction genocide convictions.

## Background

Onesphore Rwabukombe was a local official (bourgmestre/mayor) in Rwanda during the 1994 genocide against the Tutsi. The case is discussed within the broader history of mass violence, state collapse, and targeted killings that took place during that period.[^1]

## Case Significance

His conviction in Germany is often cited as an example of **universal jurisdiction**, where national courts prosecute certain grave international crimes even when they were committed abroad.[^1]

Key themes commonly highlighted include:

- accountability for local-level leadership and orders,
- the challenges of evidence collection years after the events,
- survivor testimony and documentation in international-crimes trials,
- coordination between domestic courts and international justice mechanisms.[^1]

## Legal Proceedings

The proceedings involved assessment of his role and responsibility for killings at a church where refugees had gathered. German courts evaluated both factual participation and command responsibility questions in arriving at judgment and sentencing.[^1]

## Broader Context

Cases like Rwabukombe’s are often referenced in discussions of how the international community pursues accountability when suspects relocate and years pass after mass atrocities. They also illustrate the reliance on survivor communities, archival records, and transnational cooperation to build prosecutable cases.[^1]
