## Summary

India's sand mafia illegally extracts billions of tonnes of sand for construction, destroying river ecosystems. Sand miners have murdered journalists, activists, and officials investigating the trade.

## What the Term Refers To

"Sand mafia" is a widely used label for criminal networks involved in **illegal sand mining** and related corruption. While the phrase is often associated with India, similar illegal extraction and trafficking of sand occurs in many countries where construction demand is high.

## Why Sand Is Targeted

Concrete and other construction materials require large volumes of sand and aggregates. In many regions, legal supply is constrained by environmental rules, permitting limits, or depletion of easily accessible deposits, creating incentives for:

- unlicensed extraction from rivers, beaches, and floodplains
- laundering of illegally mined sand into legal supply chains
- bribery and intimidation to avoid enforcement

## Environmental and Social Impacts

Illegal sand mining is linked to significant harms, especially in river systems:

- **Erosion and habitat loss**: removal of sediment can destabilize riverbanks and degrade aquatic habitats.
- **Lowering of riverbeds and groundwater changes**: altering sediment balance can affect water tables and local wells.
- **Infrastructure damage**: bridges and embankments can be undermined when sediment is removed aggressively.
- **Community conflict**: disputes arise when extraction affects farming, fishing, or drinking-water access.

## Enforcement Challenges

Investigations frequently describe a mix of economic incentives and coercion that makes enforcement difficult:

- operations occur at night or in remote areas
- use of heavy machinery and rapid transport networks
- local-level corruption and political patronage (alleged in many reports)
- threats and violence against journalists, activists, and officials

## Public Attention and Notable Incidents

Media reporting and public-interest litigation have drawn attention to deaths and attacks associated with the illegal trade. The issue is commonly discussed alongside the broader risks faced by people investigating organized crime and corruption in resource extraction.

## Responses

Responses in India have included a combination of regulatory and enforcement measures, such as tighter licensing, seizure of equipment, and court scrutiny of mining practices. Outcomes vary by state and locality, and the problem is often described as persistent because demand remains high.
