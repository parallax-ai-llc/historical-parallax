## Summary

Irish immigrant to Edinburgh who murdered at least 16 people to sell their bodies for medical dissection.

## Historical Context

In early 19th-century Britain, medical schools and private anatomy lecturers depended on cadavers for teaching. Legal supply was limited (largely to executed criminals), which encouraged a black market in bodies. So-called “resurrection men” commonly stole corpses from graveyards; Burke and his accomplices took a different route by killing to create supply.

## The Burke and Hare Murders

Burke is most often discussed alongside **William Hare**, his partner in the killings. High-level features of the case that are consistently described in historical accounts include:

- Victims were typically vulnerable people with limited social protection.
- Bodies were sold soon after death to maximize “freshness,” which increased their value to anatomists.
- The pair used a method of killing that left minimal external injury, which helped the bodies appear suitable for dissection.

The murders became notorious enough that the term **“burking”** entered usage to describe a suffocation-based killing associated with the case.

## Connection to Anatomy and Robert Knox

The bodies were sold to **Dr. Robert Knox**, a well-known Edinburgh anatomist. While Knox’s level of knowledge or suspicion remains a point of historical debate, the scandal contributed to public outrage over anatomy practices and corpse procurement.

## Investigation and Trial

The crimes came to light after suspicious circumstances surrounding a body led to discovery and reporting. In the subsequent prosecution:

- Hare negotiated a deal to testify (often summarized as becoming a state witness), which directed primary legal blame onto Burke.
- Burke was convicted and sentenced to death.

## Execution and Aftermath

Burke was executed in 1829. In a grim reversal that was widely publicized at the time, his body was **publicly dissected**, reflecting contemporary penal practices and serving as a moral warning in popular retellings.

The Burke and Hare case is often cited as part of the social pressure that led to reforms in the legal supply of cadavers for medical education (notably the UK Anatomy Act of 1832).
