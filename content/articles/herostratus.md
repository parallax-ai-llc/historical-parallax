## Summary

Ancient Greek arsonist who burned down the Temple of Artemis at Ephesus, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, in 356 BC to achieve lasting fame. The Ephesians banned mention of his name, but it was recorded by historians.

## “Herostratic fame”

Herostratus is the namesake of the idea of **“Herostratic fame”**: notoriety sought through shocking destruction so that the perpetrator’s name will be remembered.

## The Temple of Artemis at Ephesus

The Artemision at Ephesus was famed in antiquity and later tradition as one of the **Seven Wonders**. Accounts commonly note that the temple was rebuilt after the fire, demonstrating both the city’s wealth and the site’s religious importance.

## Damnatio memoriae (attempted)

The Ephesian response is often summarized as a civic ban on mentioning the arsonist’s name—an early example of a memory-sanction. The episode illustrates a paradox: prohibitions can fail when later writers preserve the story precisely because it is a cautionary example.

## Why the story persists

The Herostratus narrative is repeatedly used to discuss:

- incentive structures around attention and publicity
- the ethics of reporting on perpetrators of spectacular crimes
- the difference between commemoration of victims/heritage and amplification of attackers

## Modern parallels (careful framing)

Commentators sometimes draw parallels to contemporary acts of “attention-seeking” violence or vandalism. Any one-to-one comparison is limited by differences in context, but the underlying theme—destruction as a route to recognition—remains a durable lens.
