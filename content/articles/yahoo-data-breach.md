## Summary

All 3 billion Yahoo user accounts were compromised in the largest data breach in history. Russian intelligence agents were indicted for the hack.

## What Happened (high level)

- **Scope:** Yahoo later reported that **all of its user accounts** at the time—eventually stated as about **3 billion**—were affected.
- **Timeframe:** Public reporting and investigations describe intrusions associated with **2013** (and a separate incident often discussed for 2014).
- **Data types:** Commonly cited exposed data includes account identifiers and related profile/credential information; the exact mix depends on incident and disclosure.

## Disclosure and Aftermath

- **Public disclosure:** Yahoo disclosed major breaches in **2016**, with additional updates as internal review continued.
- **Business impact:** The breach disclosures affected Yahoo's negotiations during its acquisition by Verizon, including adjustments to deal terms reported in coverage.

## Legal / Law-Enforcement Notes

- **Attribution:** U.S. authorities publicly alleged involvement by actors linked to Russian intelligence services; indictments were reported.
- **Ongoing relevance:** Large-scale breaches are used as case studies in incident response, authentication, and credential hygiene.

## Lessons Commonly Cited

- **Defense-in-depth:** Strong internal monitoring, segmentation, and logging improve detection and containment.
- **Account security:** Multi-factor authentication and secure password storage practices reduce downstream harm.
- **Transparent reporting:** Clear user notification and accurate scope updates matter for trust and remediation.

## Notes

In many summaries, the Yahoo incidents are discussed alongside a separate 2014 breach; readers should distinguish between incidents, disclosures, and later scope revisions.
