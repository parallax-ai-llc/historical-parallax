## Summary

Pakistan's first military dictator who seized power in 1958. He imposed martial law and ruled for 11 years. While he modernized the economy, he suppressed political opposition and his policies led to the breakup of Pakistan.

## Rise to Power

Ayub Khan was a senior army officer who took control of the state during a period of political instability. His 1958 coup established a pattern of military dominance in Pakistani politics that would recur in later decades.

## Governance and Policy (High Level)

His administration emphasized centralized authority, technocratic planning, and state-led modernization. The period is often associated with rapid growth in some sectors alongside deepening inequality and political exclusion.

Key themes commonly discussed by historians include:

- **Martial law and political control:** Restrictions on parties, press, and opposition activity.
- **Institutional redesign:** Efforts to legitimize rule through managed electoral mechanisms and constitutional changes.
- **Economic modernization:** Industrial and infrastructure development, sometimes framed as “developmental authoritarianism.”

## Foreign Policy and Conflict

Ayub’s rule was shaped by Cold War alliances and regional rivalry. The 1965 war with India became a pivotal moment, affecting domestic legitimacy and long-term strategic trajectories.

## End of Rule and Legacy

Mass protests and political pressure culminated in his resignation in 1969. Assessments of his legacy split between views that credit modernization and those that highlight democratic backsliding and the long-term costs of military intervention in civilian governance.
