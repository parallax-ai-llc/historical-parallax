## Summary

William Withering introduced digitalis (from foxglove) into medical practice as a treatment for heart failure and dropsy. His 1785 publication documented ten years of careful clinical observations on the use of digitalis. This work established one of the first examples of systematic drug testing and dose optimization.

## Work on Digitalis (Foxglove)

Withering’s most enduring contribution is the clinical introduction of *Digitalis purpurea* (foxglove) preparations. In the 18th century, "dropsy" described fluid retention that is now often associated with heart failure and other conditions. Withering observed that certain patients improved with foxglove-based remedies and sought to identify effective doses and indications.

His 1785 book is frequently cited in the history of medicine as an early model of systematic clinical reporting: describing patient cases, outcomes, and adverse effects, and emphasizing careful titration rather than a one-dose-fits-all approach.

## Botany and Science

Beyond medicine, Withering was active as a botanist and contributed to the classification and study of plants. His work sits within a period when natural history, chemistry, and medical practice were closely intertwined.

## Legacy

Digitalis derivatives remained medically important for centuries, and Withering’s approach is often highlighted as a precursor to evidence-oriented pharmacology. His name is commonly associated with both the therapeutic breakthrough and the methodological emphasis on observation and dose optimization.

## Further Notes

Withering’s careful attention to side effects and dosing is often emphasized as much as the discovery itself, because it demonstrated a disciplined approach to therapeutic risk.

Later clinical practice built on this kind of case-based reporting to formalize drug evaluation and safety monitoring.
