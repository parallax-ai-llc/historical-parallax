## Summary

Military dictator of Mali for 23 years who was overthrown and sentenced to death for economic crimes and ordering the killing of pro-democracy protesters. Later pardoned.

## Background

Moussa Traoré was a Malian military officer who became head of state after a 1968 coup. His rule lasted more than two decades, making him one of the longest-serving leaders in Mali’s post-independence history.

## Rule and Governance

Accounts of Traoré’s period in power commonly emphasize a mix of military-led governance, restrictions on political opposition, and economic difficulty. Mali faced significant challenges during these decades, including drought and wider Sahel-region instability, which shaped public expectations and state capacity.

## 1991 Uprising and Overthrow

In 1991, mass protests and civil unrest culminated in Traoré’s removal from power. The crackdown on demonstrators, including reports of lethal force, became a defining episode in the transition away from military rule and toward multiparty politics.

## Trials, Sentencing, and Pardon

After his overthrow, Traoré was prosecuted in Mali. He was sentenced for crimes associated with his time in office, including allegations of economic wrongdoing and responsibility for violence against protesters. The death sentence was later commuted, and he was eventually pardoned and released, remaining a notable figure in debates about accountability and reconciliation.

## Legacy

Traoré’s legacy is often discussed in relation to Mali’s democratization in the 1990s and the tension between transitional justice and political stability. His long tenure and the circumstances of his removal continue to serve as reference points in Mali’s modern political narrative.
