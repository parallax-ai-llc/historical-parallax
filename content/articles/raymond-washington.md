## Summary

Co-founder of the Crips gang who was shot and killed at age 25 in a drive-by shooting.

## Context and Background

- Raymond Washington grew up in South Los Angeles during a period of rapid demographic change, deindustrialization, and rising street-level violence in the late 1960s and 1970s.[^1]
- Many accounts describe the early Crips as beginning as a loosely organized street group that later evolved into a broader network of affiliated sets.[^1]

## Formation of the Crips (High-level)

The Wikipedia summary describes Washington as a co-founder associated with the early formation of the Crips in 1969 in Los Angeles.[^1] Subsequent reporting and scholarship often emphasize that the group’s growth was shaped by local neighborhood dynamics, rivalries with other street groups, and the fragmentation of authority across different areas of the city.[^1]

Key points commonly noted in overviews:

- Early identity: a youth street group that became more formalized through reputation and conflict over time
- Structure: decentralized, with "sets" that could share a name while operating independently
- Geography: primarily associated with areas of South Los Angeles, with later diffusion beyond the city

## Death

- Washington was shot and killed on August 9, 1979, in Los Angeles.[^1]
- Public sources typically describe the killing as a drive-by shooting and note that details and attribution remain uncertain in many summaries.[^1]

## Legacy and Historical Significance

Washington is frequently referenced in mainstream accounts as an early figure in the origin story of the Crips, a gang that became one of the most widely recognized and studied street-gang networks in the United States.[^1] In historical writing, the broader significance is usually framed in terms of:

- The long-term impact of gang proliferation on urban public safety and community life
- The role of local social conditions (housing, policing, employment, and schooling) in shaping youth street formations
- The way decentralized networks can persist and spread even without centralized leadership
