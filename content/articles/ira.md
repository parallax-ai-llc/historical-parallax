## Summary

Irish Republican Army that fought for a united Ireland during the Troubles from 1969-1998. The conflict killed over 3,500 people through bombings and shootings.

## Terminology and Organizations

- “IRA” can refer to multiple organizations across different historical periods; in the context of the Troubles, discussions commonly focus on the Provisional Irish Republican Army (Provisional IRA).
- The term is often used as a shorthand in media, but careful writing distinguishes between factions, political movements, and splinter groups.

## Goals and Strategy (High Level)

- The organization is associated with Irish republican aims, including ending British rule in Northern Ireland and achieving Irish reunification.
- Tactics during the Troubles included armed attacks, bombings, and assassinations, alongside efforts to develop political leverage through allied or associated political actors.

## Ceasefires and Peace Process

- The late 20th century saw a shift toward ceasefires and negotiations, culminating in the 1998 Good Friday Agreement (also called the Belfast Agreement).
- Decommissioning of weapons and confidence-building measures were key components in the post-agreement security landscape.

## Impact and Controversy

- The conflict produced extensive civilian harm, displacement, and long-term social trauma, alongside contested narratives of legitimacy and resistance.
- Legacy issues include debates over truth recovery, commemorations, and accountability for violence committed by multiple parties.
