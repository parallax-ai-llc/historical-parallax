## Summary

Garrincha won the 1958 and 1962 World Cups and never lost with Pelé. Born with bent legs, he was one of football's greatest dribblers.

## Playing Style and Reputation

Garrincha is widely remembered for exceptional one-on-one dribbling, acceleration over short distances, and an ability to beat defenders repeatedly with feints and changes of pace. His style is often described as improvisational and joyful, contributing to a mythic status in Brazilian football culture.

Despite physical challenges from birth (including significant leg deformities), he developed balance and movement patterns that many observers consider part of what made his dribbling so difficult to predict.

## Club Career (High-Level)

Most accounts associate his peak club years with Botafogo, where he became a star in domestic competitions and in the Rio de Janeiro state championship context. As with many players of his era, statistics and match footage are less comprehensive than for modern football, so evaluations rely heavily on contemporary reporting, testimonials, and landmark tournament performances.

## 1962 World Cup Significance

The 1962 World Cup is frequently highlighted as the tournament where Garrincha carried Brazil through key matches, especially when Pelé was unavailable. His performances in that campaign are commonly cited among the most influential individual tournament runs in World Cup history.

## Later Life

Biographical summaries often note a difficult post-peak period, including health issues and personal struggles. His life story is sometimes used in discussions about player welfare, support structures after retirement, and the gap between sporting fame and long-term stability.

## Legacy

In Brazilian memory, Garrincha is often positioned alongside Pelé as a defining figure of the national team’s rise to global prominence. He remains an enduring reference point for the art of dribbling and for the idea that unconventional physical traits can coexist with athletic brilliance.
