## Summary

French bishop who presided over the rigged trial and burning of Joan of Arc.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|

## Context

- Pierre Cauchon was a French cleric and administrator whose career unfolded during the Hundred Years' War, when France was divided between factions aligned with the English and those supporting the Valois crown.
- He became bishop of Beauvais, a strategically important diocese that sat near contested territories in northern France.

## Role in the Trial of Joan of Arc (1431)

- Cauchon acted as the leading ecclesiastical authority for the proceedings against Joan of Arc, convened at Rouen while the city was under English control.
- The trial was conducted as a church court, but it was deeply entangled with wartime politics: Joan's legitimacy threatened the English claim and the Burgundian alliance.
- Historical accounts commonly describe the process as procedurally biased, with pressure from English authorities and a prosecution framed to produce a conviction.
- The formal charges centered on heresy-related accusations (including the claim that she relied on “voices” and refused submission to church authority), culminating in condemnation and execution by burning.

## Aftermath and Later Reputation

- In 1456, an ecclesiastical retrial (often called the “rehabilitation trial”) reviewed the earlier proceedings and annulled the 1431 verdict, contributing to Cauchon's lasting reputation as a symbol of judicial corruption.
- Cauchon's name is frequently invoked in popular history as the archetype of an official who uses institutional power to serve factional interests.

## Additional Career Notes

- Beyond the Joan of Arc case, Cauchon held senior clerical roles and moved within the political-religious institutions of the period, navigating between French and English spheres of influence.
- He later became bishop of Lisieux, reflecting continued advancement despite the controversy attached to the Rouen proceedings.

## Why He Is Remembered

- Presiding figure in one of the most famous political-religious trials in European history.
- Associated with the use of legal procedure as a tool of wartime propaganda and statecraft.
- Central antagonist in many later narratives about Joan of Arc, especially after her canonization and the rehabilitation of her trial record.
