## Summary

Georg Ohm formulated Ohm law relating voltage, current, and resistance. The SI unit of electrical resistance (ohm) is named after him.

## Ohm’s Law (High-Level)

Ohm’s law is commonly written as **V = I·R**, expressing a proportional relationship between voltage (V), current (I), and resistance (R) for many conductive materials under steady conditions. In practical terms, it provides a foundational rule for analyzing circuits: for a given resistance, increasing voltage increases current linearly.

Modern physics recognizes that this relationship holds well for “ohmic” materials over certain ranges, while other components (for example, diodes or some temperature-dependent conductors) can show non-linear behavior.

## Experiments and Publication

Ohm’s work is associated with careful measurement and the attempt to express electrical behavior quantitatively at a time when instrumentation and theory were still developing. His key publication in 1827 (“Die galvanische Kette, mathematisch bearbeitet”) is often referenced as the formal presentation of the law and its experimental grounding.

## Reception and Later Recognition

Historical accounts note that Ohm’s ideas were not universally celebrated immediately; like many scientific contributions, acceptance grew as the framework proved useful and reproducible. Over time, the law became central to electrical engineering and physics education, and the unit **ohm (Ω)** was named in his honor.

## Legacy

- **Engineering practice:** Circuit design, power calculations, and fault analysis often begin with relationships that descend from Ohm’s law.
- **Pedagogy:** It serves as one of the first mathematical laws taught in introductory electricity and electronics.
- **Standardization:** Naming SI units after scientists reflects institutional recognition of foundational contributions to measurement and technology.

Biographical sketches also describe Ohm as an educator, with appointments in secondary and higher education that shaped how he conducted and communicated his experimental work.
