## Summary

After 1,134 garment workers died, the Accord on Fire and Building Safety was created. However, exploitation in Bangladesh's garment industry — producing for global brands — continues.

## What Happened Next (High-Level)

- **International pressure intensified** on brands, factory owners, and the Bangladeshi government to improve building safety and labor conditions.
- **Factory inspections and remediation** became a central focus, emphasizing structural integrity, fire safety, and electrical systems.
- **Worker organizing and rights debates** continued, including concerns about wages, freedom of association, and retaliation.

## Major Safety Initiatives

### Accord on Fire and Building Safety in Bangladesh

- Created after Rana Plaza as a **legally binding** agreement involving global brands and labor organizations.
- Focused on **independent inspections**, public reporting, and **corrective action plans** for supplier factories.
- Became one of the most cited examples of a brand-driven, cross-border safety program in the garment sector.

### Alliance for Bangladesh Worker Safety

- A separate, brand-led initiative launched by a group of primarily North American companies.
- Also emphasized **inspections and remediation**, though it was structured differently from the Accord.

## Compensation, Accountability, and Legal Aftermath

- **Compensation discussions** addressed medical care, lost income, and long-term support for survivors and families.
- **Criminal and civil accountability** efforts continued over time, but outcomes and timelines have been contested and politically sensitive.
- The broader aftermath contributed to ongoing scrutiny of **supply-chain responsibility** and the limits of voluntary corporate social responsibility programs.

## Ongoing Issues

- Bangladesh’s garment sector remains a major global manufacturing hub, and **labor-rights and safety enforcement** continues to be debated.
- Safety programs improved some practices, but critics argue that **structural incentives** (price pressure, tight lead times, subcontracting) still drive risk.
- The issue is often framed as a continuing challenge of **governance, enforcement capacity, and buyer behavior** across the global apparel supply chain.
