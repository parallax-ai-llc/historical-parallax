## Summary

Japan's biggest postwar corruption scandal involved Recruit Cosmos pre-listing shares given to politicians including PM Takeshita. Led to the resignation of the Prime Minister and multiple officials.

## What happened

The Recruit Scandal centered on Recruit Cosmos, a real-estate subsidiary of the Recruit Company. Before Recruit Cosmos shares were publicly listed, shares were reportedly distributed to politically connected individuals. Because the recipients could later sell after listing at a higher price, the arrangement was treated as a de facto benefit linked to influence and access.

## Political impact

The scandal implicated figures across Japanese politics and bureaucracy and intensified public scrutiny of money politics in the late 1980s. Prime Minister Noboru Takeshita resigned amid the fallout, and multiple officials faced career-ending consequences.

## Legal and administrative aftermath

Following exposure, investigations and court proceedings addressed questions of bribery, ethics violations, and whether the benefits constituted improper inducements. The scandal is often discussed as a case study in how complex financial mechanisms can be used to conceal political patronage.

## Longer-term significance

Recruit is frequently cited in discussions of political reform in Japan, including debates about campaign finance, transparency in ties between business and government, and public trust in institutions. It also contributed to a broader narrative of late-Shōwa era political scandals and their role in reshaping party dynamics.
