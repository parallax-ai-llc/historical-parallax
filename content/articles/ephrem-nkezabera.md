## Summary

Rwandan businessman convicted of genocide in Belgium for financing and facilitating the killing of Tutsis. He used his business resources to transport militia members and weapons.

## Additional Context

### 1994 genocide in Rwanda

- The 1994 genocide against the Tutsi in Rwanda involved mass killings and persecution carried out over roughly 100 days, with extensive participation by state-linked structures and militia groups.[^2]
- Many cases have emphasized that logistical support—transport, funding, communications, and access to weapons—was a key enabler of large-scale violence in addition to direct participation in killings.[^2]

### Prosecution in Belgium

- Nkezabera’s conviction in Belgium is commonly discussed in the context of “universal jurisdiction” and related legal doctrines that allow national courts to prosecute certain international crimes committed abroad.[^1][^3]
- Belgian courts pursued multiple cases connected to the Rwandan genocide, reflecting both the presence of suspects in Belgium and broader international efforts to combat impunity through domestic prosecutions.[^3]

### Business resources as facilitation

- Accounts of the case describe the alleged use of business assets to support perpetrators, for example by providing transportation or financial backing, illustrating how civilian and commercial infrastructure can be repurposed for coercion during political violence.[^1]
- This framing aligns with wider scholarship and legal practice that treats aiding and abetting, financing, and facilitating as serious forms of criminal responsibility in mass-atrocity contexts.[^2][^3]

### Legacy

- The case is often cited as an example of diaspora-linked accountability: individuals who leave a conflict zone may still face prosecution years later if evidence is assembled and a competent court asserts jurisdiction.[^3]
