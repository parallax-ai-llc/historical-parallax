## Summary

RuPaul Charles is an American drag queen, television host, singer, and model who has become the most commercially successful drag performer in history. Rising to prominence in the early 1990s with his debut single "Supermodel (You Better Work)," he broke into mainstream entertainment at a time when drag remained largely underground. His influence reshaped how American popular culture perceives gender performance and LGBTQ+ identity.

Since 2009, RuPaul has hosted and produced *RuPaul's Drag Race*, a reality competition series that transformed drag from a subcultural art form into a global phenomenon. The show has aired across more than 200 countries and spawned dozens of international franchises, making RuPaul one of the most influential figures in contemporary television. He has won 12 Primetime Emmy Awards for Outstanding Host for a Reality or Reality-Competition Program, more than any other individual in that category.

## Early Life & Background

RuPaul Andre Charles was born on November 17, 1960, in San Diego, California. He was the youngest of four children raised by Irving and Ernestine Charles. His parents divorced when he was seven, and he moved with his mother and sisters to Atlanta, Georgia, where he attended Glynn Academy before eventually relocating to pursue performing arts. He later attended the North Atlanta School of Performing Arts, dropping out at 15 when he moved to Atlanta to live with his older sister and pursue entertainment full-time.

---

## Rise to Prominence

RuPaul moved to New York City in the late 1980s, working the downtown club scene and building a presence in the East Village drag world. His breakthrough came in 1993 with the release of "Supermodel (You Better Work)" from his debut album *Supermodel of the World*. The single reached No. 45 on the Billboard Hot 100 and became an anthem within LGBTQ+ communities. That same year he appeared in the music video for Elton John's "Don't Let the Sun Go Down on Me" and signed a cosmetics contract with MAC — one of the first drag performers to secure such mainstream brand representation.

Throughout the 1990s and 2000s, RuPaul hosted his own talk show on VH1, released additional albums, and appeared in numerous films and television series. In 2009, *RuPaul's Drag Race* premiered on Logo TV, marking the beginning of his most enduring cultural contribution.

## Controversies & Criticism

RuPaul has faced criticism from within the LGBTQ+ community on several occasions. In 2018, comments he made suggesting transgender women who had undergone surgery should not compete on *Drag Race* generated significant backlash; he subsequently clarified his position and the show has since featured transgender contestants. He has also been criticized by some drag artists for commercializing an art form historically tied to queer resistance, with detractors arguing the show's mainstream popularity has sanitized drag's subversive roots.

---

## Legacy & Influence

RuPaul's influence extends well beyond entertainment. *Drag Race* alumni have performed on Broadway, headlined world tours, and built multi-million dollar businesses. The show is widely credited with accelerating mainstream acceptance of LGBTQ+ identities in the 2010s. His catchphrases — most notably "We're all born naked and the rest is drag" — have entered popular lexicon as expressions of identity fluidity. By building an institutional platform around drag performance, RuPaul fundamentally altered the economics and visibility of queer art.
