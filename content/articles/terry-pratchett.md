## Summary

Sir Terry Pratchett (28 April 1948 – 12 March 2015) was a British author best known for the Discworld series, a sprawling fantasy universe that blended sharp satire, philosophical depth, and comedic brilliance across 41 novels. He became one of the UK's best-selling authors of the 20th century, with over 100 million books sold worldwide in 37 languages.

Pratchett's work transcended genre fiction. Using the scaffolding of fantasy tropes — wizards, witches, trolls, and a flat world carried on the backs of four elephants atop a giant turtle — he dissected real-world institutions: religion, politics, capitalism, war, and death itself. His satirical voice was both humane and unsparing, earning him a devoted global readership and critical respect often denied to comic fantasy writers.

His public battle with early-onset Alzheimer's disease, diagnosed in 2007, became its own form of legacy. He became an outspoken advocate for dementia research and assisted dying, continuing to write and campaign until near the end of his life. He was knighted in 2009 for services to literature.

## Early Life & Background

Terry Pratchett was born in Beaconsfield, Buckinghamshire, England. He grew up in the village of Forty Green and attended Holtspur Primary School and later High Wycombe Technical High School. He was a self-described average student who discovered a passion for reading through the local library. His first published story appeared in his school magazine at age 13, and he sold his first professional short story, "The Hades Business," at 15, published in *Science Fantasy* magazine in 1963.

He left school at 17 and worked as a journalist for the *Bucks Free Press*, then as a press officer for the Central Electricity Generating Board — a role he held until 1987, writing novels in his spare time.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Pratchett's debut novel, *The Carpet People*, appeared in 1971. His first Discworld novel, *The Colour of Magic*, was published in 1983. The series gained momentum rapidly; by the late 1980s, Pratchett was appearing regularly on bestseller lists. *Guards! Guards!* (1989) and *Reaper Man* (1991) are widely credited as the point where his writing reached its mature satirical power.

In 1987, after the success of his fourth Discworld novel, *Mort*, he left his day job to write full-time. Co-authoring *Good Omens* (1990) with Neil Gaiman expanded his international profile considerably.

## Key Achievements

- 41 Discworld novels published between 1983 and 2015
- Over 100 million books sold worldwide
- Knighted (KBE) in 2009 for services to literature
- Carnegie Medal winner for *The Amazing Maurice and His Educated Rodents* (2001)
- *Good Omens* (1990, with Neil Gaiman) adapted into a hit Amazon Prime series in 2019
- Donated £500,000 to Alzheimer's Research UK following his diagnosis
- Several Discworld novels adapted for stage, television, and film

---

## Controversies & Criticism

Pratchett was largely free of serious personal controversy. Some literary critics dismissed his work as "mere" genre comedy, a characterisation he contested publicly and vigorously. His advocacy for assisted dying generated debate; he filmed a 2011 BBC documentary, *Choosing to Die*, which won a BAFTA but was criticised by some religious and disability groups. He remained outspoken on the topic regardless.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Pratchett reshaped what comic fantasy could do, proving that humour and moral seriousness were not incompatible. His influence is visible across contemporary fantasy — authors including Neil Gaiman, Rhianna Pratchett (his daughter, a game writer), and many others cite him as formative. The Discworld series continues in print, stage productions, and screen adaptations. The *Good Omens* television series introduced his work to a new generation after his death. He remains one of the most-quoted British authors of his era.
