## Summary

Barry Marshall is an Australian physician who shared the 2005 Nobel Prize for discovering H. pylori causes ulcers.

## Background and Significance

Marshall’s work challenged the long-held belief that stress and spicy food were the primary causes of peptic ulcer disease. By showing that a bacterium could survive in the acidic stomach environment, the research helped reframe ulcers as, in many cases, an infectious disease that could be treated with antibiotics.

## Key Contributions

- **Helicobacter pylori hypothesis:** Alongside Robin Warren, Marshall linked *H. pylori* infection to chronic gastritis and peptic ulcers.
- **Self-experimentation (1984):** Marshall famously ingested *H. pylori* to demonstrate that infection could cause gastritis, drawing attention to the causal claim.
- **Clinical impact:** The work contributed to widespread adoption of antibiotic-based eradication therapy for many ulcer patients.

## Broader Impact

The discovery influenced clinical guidelines and public health by reducing recurrence rates for many ulcer cases when *H. pylori* is eradicated. It also encouraged further research into the role of microbes in chronic diseases previously considered non-infectious.

## Legacy

Marshall and Warren received the **2005 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine** for their discovery of *Helicobacter pylori* and its role in gastritis and peptic ulcer disease.
