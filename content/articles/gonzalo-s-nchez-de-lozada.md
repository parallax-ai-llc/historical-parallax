## Summary

Bolivian president who ordered military operations against protesters that killed 67 people in the 2003 Gas War. Fled to the US and was convicted of genocide in absentia.

## Political Career (High Level)

Gonzalo Sánchez de Lozada (often nicknamed "Goni") served as President of Bolivia in two non-consecutive terms.
He was associated with market-oriented economic policies and privatization programs that were influential and controversial.
His political career was closely tied to debates over natural-gas exports, state revenue, and indigenous and labor movements.

## The 2003 Gas War Crisis

In 2003, protests intensified over proposed gas export plans and broader economic grievances.
Security forces used lethal force in multiple incidents, and the period became known as the "Gas War."
The killings and injuries during the crackdown became a central element of later legal and political disputes.

## Exile and Legal Controversies

After resigning in 2003, Sánchez de Lozada left Bolivia and lived in the United States.
Bolivian authorities and victims' groups pursued accountability through domestic proceedings and international extradition requests.
Public descriptions of his legal status vary; some sources refer to severe criminal accusations (including terms like "genocide" in Bolivian political and legal discourse), while other reporting emphasizes the complexity and contested nature of jurisdiction and evidence.

## Legacy

His presidency remains a flashpoint in Bolivian politics.
Supporters highlight economic modernization efforts, while critics focus on the human cost of the 2003 crackdown and the unresolved accountability debates.
