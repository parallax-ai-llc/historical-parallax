## Summary

Chaz Bono is an American activist, author, and musician best known as the child of entertainers Sonny and Cher. Born Chastity Bono on March 4, 1969, he publicly completed his gender transition in 2010, becoming one of the most visible transgender men in the United States. His openness about the process helped shift mainstream conversation around transgender identity at a pivotal moment in cultural history.

Beyond his celebrity lineage, Bono has built a distinct public identity through advocacy, memoir writing, and entertainment. His willingness to document his personal journey in real time — through a documentary film and public appearances — made him a touchstone figure for transgender visibility long before widespread media representation existed.

## Early Life & Background

Chaz Bono was born in Los Angeles, California, to Sonny Bono and Cher. He grew up in the public eye, appearing on his parents' variety television program *The Sonny & Cher Comedy Hour* as a child. Despite the glare of celebrity, Bono struggled privately with his gender identity from an early age. He later described feeling from childhood that he was male. He attended the University of Southern California briefly before leaving to pursue music and activism.

During his twenties, Bono publicly came out as a lesbian in 1995 in an interview with *The Advocate*, at a time when such disclosure by a celebrity child carried significant cultural weight. He worked as a music journalist and recorded with the band Ceremony in the mid-1990s.

## Rise to Prominence

Bono announced he had begun gender transition in June 2009, and legally changed his name and gender in 2010. The process was documented in the 2011 documentary *Becoming Chaz*, which premiered at the Sundance Film Festival and later aired on OWN (Oprah Winfrey Network). The film offered an intimate look at transition — including its effects on his relationship with then-partner Jennifer Elia — and reached a broad audience unfamiliar with transgender experiences.

His appearance as a contestant on *Dancing with the Stars* in 2011 brought further mainstream exposure, generating both support and controversy. The booking drew protests from some viewers but was widely credited with humanizing transgender identity for a large television audience.

## Key Achievements

- Published *Family Outing* (1998), an early guide for families navigating a loved one's coming out as LGBTQ+.
- Released the memoir *Transition: The Story of How I Became a Man* (2011), which became a bestseller and a key text in transgender literature.
- *Becoming Chaz* (2011) won the GLAAD Media Award for Outstanding Documentary.
- Served as a media spokesperson for GLAAD and participated in numerous advocacy campaigns throughout the 2010s.

## Controversies & Criticism

Bono's *Dancing with the Stars* appearance in 2011 drew organized protests and complaints to ABC from viewers who objected to a transgender person appearing on prime-time television. Some within the transgender community have also raised questions about the degree to which Bono's celebrity platform shaped mainstream narratives in ways that may not reflect broader transgender experiences. His high-profile visibility was a double-edged sword: invaluable for representation, yet filtered through a lens of privilege unavailable to most trans people.

## Legacy & Influence

Chaz Bono's public transition arrived before Laverne Cox's *Time* magazine cover (2014) and the broader surge in transgender media representation. In that earlier moment, his willingness to be visible — and to document his experience in a feature documentary — helped lay cultural groundwork for subsequent progress. His memoirs remain reference points in LGBTQ+ literature, and his advocacy through GLAAD contributed to policy and media standards that shaped how transgender people are covered in American journalism.
