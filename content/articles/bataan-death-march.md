## Summary

Forced march of 75,000 American and Filipino POWs by Japanese forces in April 1942 from Bataan to prison camps. Between 5,000 and 18,000 died from abuse, disease, and starvation.

## Context

After the fall of Bataan during World War II, large numbers of American and Filipino troops became prisoners of war under Japanese control. The subsequent forced movement of prisoners to detention facilities became known as the Bataan Death March.

## Conditions During the March

Survivors described severe deprivation and violence, including:

- **Lack of water and food** over long distances in extreme heat.
- **Physical abuse** and summary killings by guards.
- **Disease and exhaustion** compounded by overcrowding and inadequate medical care.

## Aftermath and Accountability

The episode became one of the most notorious wartime atrocities in the Pacific theater. After the war, some Japanese officers and officials were prosecuted and convicted for war crimes connected to the treatment of POWs.

## Historical Memory

The march remains a major part of wartime remembrance in both the Philippines and the United States, often commemorated as an example of the human cost of captivity and the importance of the laws of war.
