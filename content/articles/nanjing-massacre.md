## Summary

Mass murder and war rape by Japanese troops in Nanjing in 1937-1938. Between 200,000 and 300,000 Chinese civilians and POWs were killed over six weeks.

## Historical Context

The Nanjing Massacre occurred during the Second Sino-Japanese War, after Japanese forces advanced into China’s then-capital, Nanjing (Nanking). The fall of the city followed major fighting in Shanghai and broader military campaigns that produced large civilian displacement.

## What Happened

Accounts of the atrocity describe widespread crimes committed by elements of the occupying Japanese army, including:

- **Mass executions:** Large-scale killings of civilians and captured soldiers.
- **Sexual violence:** Extensive rape and other forms of sexual assault.
- **Looting and arson:** Destruction of homes, shops, and public buildings.

The precise casualty figures are debated among historians and institutions, but high estimates commonly cited in modern scholarship and public memorialization range in the hundreds of thousands.

## International Safety Zone

A group of foreign residents and Chinese staff organized what is often referred to as the **Nanjing Safety Zone** to shelter civilians. Documentation from this zone—diaries, reports, and correspondence—has been frequently used as contemporary evidence about conditions in the city.

## Aftermath

The massacre had long-term consequences for Chinese society and for wartime propaganda and mobilization. Survivors’ testimonies and local records became part of later historical investigations.

## War Crimes Trials and Accountability

After World War II, various tribunals and national courts addressed atrocities in China, including crimes connected to Nanjing. Proceedings contributed to the postwar record, though debates over responsibility, documentation, and political framing continued.

## Memory and Controversy

The Nanjing Massacre remains a sensitive subject in East Asian historical memory and diplomacy. Disputes have focused on:

- interpretations of documentation and casualty estimates,
- public commemoration and museum narratives,
- how wartime conduct is represented in education and official statements.
