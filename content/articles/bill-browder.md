## Summary

American-born British financier who became Putin's 'enemy number one' after championing the Magnitsky Act. Russia has issued multiple Interpol red notices against him which have been rejected.

## Additional Context

- Browder ran **Hermitage Capital Management**, once a major foreign portfolio investor in Russia.
- After disputes with Russian authorities and business interests, his activities shifted from investing to **advocacy focused on corruption, asset tracing, and sanctions**.

## Magnitsky Case (High Level)

- Sergei Magnitsky, a lawyer connected to Browder’s firm, became the namesake of “Magnitsky” sanctions after his death in Russian custody.
- The case is frequently cited in debates about **rule of law, corruption, and human-rights accountability**.

## Impact and Controversy

- **Sanctions policy:** Browder is closely associated with lobbying for targeted sanctions against individuals accused of serious wrongdoing.
- **Disinformation/legal pressure:** he has described sustained campaigns to discredit him, while Russian authorities have pursued cases against him; multiple international bodies have declined to advance certain requests.
- **Polarizing figure:** supporters see a whistleblower-style campaign; critics argue his narrative serves political aims. The dispute is part of broader Russia–West tensions.

## Practical Significance

- The “Magnitsky” framework has been adopted or adapted in multiple countries as a way to sanction individuals without broad trade embargoes.
- Browder’s story is often used as an example of how finance, law, and geopolitics intersect through asset freezes and cross-border investigations.
- The ongoing dispute also shows limits of international policing tools when major powers contest the underlying facts.

- For researchers, the episode is a case study in how narratives compete across jurisdictions, courts, and media ecosystems.
