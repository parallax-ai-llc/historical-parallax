## Summary

Meriwether Lewis and William Clark led the Corps of Discovery expedition across western North America to the Pacific Ocean (1804-1806), mapping vast territories and making contact with numerous Native American nations.

## Background

The Lewis and Clark Expedition—also called the Corps of Discovery—was a U.S. government expedition initiated after the Louisiana Purchase. It aimed to explore and document the newly acquired territory and to seek a practical route to the Pacific.

## Expedition highlights

- **Scientific and geographic documentation:** The expedition recorded plants, animals, and geography, and produced influential maps and journals.
- **Indigenous diplomacy:** Lewis and Clark met many Native nations; these encounters were shaped by the expedition’s diplomatic goals and by existing Indigenous political landscapes.
- **Sacagawea and interpreters:** The expedition relied on interpreters and guides; Sacagawea is often highlighted for her role in communication and navigation in parts of the journey.

## Historical significance

The expedition became a foundational narrative in U.S. westward expansion history, while also being analyzed for its role in setting the stage for later settler colonialism and changing power relations across the region.
