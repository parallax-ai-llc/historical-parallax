## Background

Khalid Ahmed Shawky al-Islambouli was an officer in the Egyptian Army at a time when Egypt was undergoing major political realignment. President Anwar Sadat pursued a peace treaty with Israel and a closer relationship with the United States, while also carrying out arrests of opponents across the political spectrum.

Islamist militants portrayed Sadat's policies as betrayal and argued that violence against the state was justified. Islambouli became linked to this broader current of militant Islamist opposition.

## Assassination of Anwar Sadat (1981)

On October 6, 1981, during Egypt's annual Armed Forces Day parade in Cairo, Islambouli and accomplices attacked the reviewing stand and shot Sadat at close range. Sadat died from his wounds, and the assault also killed and injured other attendees.

The assassination triggered an immediate state crackdown, including mass arrests and trials of suspected militants and sympathizers.

## Trial and execution

Islambouli was tried by a military court and sentenced to death. He was executed by firing squad in April 1982.

## Legacy and political symbolism

In Egypt, the assassination is widely treated as a landmark act of political violence that reinforced the state's security posture and shaped how successive governments addressed Islamist militancy.

Outside Egypt, Islambouli's name has been used symbolically in some regional propaganda and commemorations. Because those uses are political, perceptions of him vary sharply by country and community.
