## Summary

Archbishop Chrysostomos I of Athens served as Archbishop of Athens during World War I and the Greek-Turkish population exchange. He played a crucial role in maintaining Greek national unity during turbulent times. He was a significant figure in the relationship between the Greek Church and the Greek state during the early 20th century.

## Background

Chrysostomos I (born as Chrysostomos Papadopoulos) was a senior cleric of the Church of Greece who rose through metropolitan posts before becoming Archbishop of Athens. His tenure coincided with a period of repeated national crisis for Greece, including the aftermath of World War I, the Greco–Turkish War (1919–1922), and the large-scale refugee movements and population exchange that followed.

## Historical Context

In the early 20th century, the Greek Church was closely intertwined with the state and public life. Church leaders were expected to provide pastoral leadership while also serving as prominent national figures during moments of political division and social disruption.

## Role as Archbishop of Athens (1923–1938)

As Archbishop, Chrysostomos was associated with efforts to stabilize church administration and maintain social cohesion in Athens and other urban centers that absorbed refugees from Asia Minor and Eastern Thrace. He also operated within ongoing debates about the institutional relationship between the Church of Greece and the Greek government.

## Legacy

Chrysostomos is generally remembered as a high-profile church leader of the interwar period whose public role reflected the broader influence of the Orthodox Church in Greek society. Later assessments often place his archiepiscopate within the challenges of reconstruction, refugee integration, and national reconciliation.
