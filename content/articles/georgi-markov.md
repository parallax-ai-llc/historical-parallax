## Summary

Bulgarian dissident writer assassinated in London by Bulgarian secret service using a poison-tipped umbrella. A tiny pellet containing ricin was injected into his leg on Waterloo Bridge.

## Work and Dissidence

Before leaving Bulgaria, Markov wrote fiction and worked in media.
After defecting, he became known for critical commentary on the Bulgarian communist leadership, including essays and broadcasts aimed at audiences behind the Iron Curtain.

## Assassination and Method

The “umbrella” attack became one of the most famous Cold War assassination stories because it combined an everyday object with a covert delivery mechanism.
Accounts describe a small pellet containing toxin being introduced through the skin, followed by a rapid decline over the next few days.

## Investigation and Attribution

British authorities investigated the case as a politically motivated killing linked to state security services.
The episode has often been discussed alongside other alleged Bulgarian intelligence operations abroad and the broader context of Soviet-aligned security cooperation.

## Legacy

Markov’s death is frequently cited in studies of political repression, exile journalism, and clandestine tradecraft.
It also helped cement his posthumous reputation as a prominent Bulgarian dissident voice of the period.

The case remains a reference point in public discussion of targeted killings and state-sponsored assassination allegations in Europe.
