## Summary

Spanish conquistador Hernan Cortes ordered the massacre of thousands of unarmed Cholulans in 1519 during the conquest of Mexico. Estimates range from 3,000 to 30,000 killed.

## Context

Cholula was a major city and religious center in central Mexico. The event occurred during Cortés’s march inland after landing on the Gulf Coast, in a period marked by shifting alliances among Indigenous polities and rapid Spanish advances.

## Accounts and Interpretations

Narratives of the massacre vary across sources, including Spanish chroniclers and later historians. Some accounts describe Cortés and his allies acting on intelligence that an ambush was planned; others emphasize the role of intimidation and preemptive violence as a tactic to deter resistance.

## Role of Indigenous Allies

Cortés’s expedition relied heavily on Indigenous allies, particularly Tlaxcalans, who were rivals of the Mexica (Aztec) Empire and other regional powers. Many accounts describe allied forces participating in or enabling the attack, highlighting the complex political landscape of the conquest.

## Consequences

The massacre contributed to the Spanish reputation for brutality and served as a warning to other communities as Cortés continued toward Tenochtitlan. It also intensified fear and uncertainty, affecting diplomacy, defections, and local decision-making as the conflict expanded.

## Historical Significance

The event is often discussed in scholarship on colonial violence, propaganda, and the mechanics of conquest. It illustrates how military force, psychological shock, and alliance politics interacted in the early stages of Spanish rule in Mesoamerica.
