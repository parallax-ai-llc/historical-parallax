## Summary

Hungarian art forger who created over 1,000 forgeries of works by Picasso, Matisse, and other modern masters. His fakes fooled major galleries and collectors worldwide. His story was told in Orson Welles' film 'F for Fake.'

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1946 | Began forging artworks in Paris |
| 1968 | Exposed by art dealer Fernand Legros |
| 1974 | Subject of 'F for Fake' by Orson Welles |
| 1976 | Committed suicide in Ibiza |

## Additional Context

- **Type of forgery**: De Hory specialized in imitating the styles of modern masters rather than creating literal copies of specific paintings.
- **Art-market vulnerability**: His career is commonly used as an example of how provenance gaps, expert disagreement, and market incentives can allow forged works to circulate.

## Cultural Impact

- Orson Welles’s *F for Fake* (1973/1974) blended documentary and essay film techniques to explore authorship, authenticity, and deception, using de Hory as a central figure.
- The story remains a frequent reference point in discussions of authentication methods and the economics of the art trade.

## Key Takeaways

- De Hory’s notoriety highlights a recurring tension in art: value is tied not only to aesthetics but also to attribution, documentation, and institutional trust.
