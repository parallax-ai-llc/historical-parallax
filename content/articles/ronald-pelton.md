## Expanded Overview

Ronald William Pelton was a U.S. National Security Agency (NSA) employee who was convicted in 1986 for espionage-related offenses after providing sensitive U.S. signals intelligence information to the Soviet Union.[^1]

### Background (high level)

- Pelton served in the U.S. Air Force, where he received Russian-language training and worked in signals intelligence roles before later working at NSA.[^1]
- Public reporting describes long-running financial stress (including bankruptcy) as a key factor preceding his contact with Soviet officials.[^1]

### What he compromised (high level)

- One of the most notable programs associated with the case is **Operation Ivy Bells**, an NSA/U.S. Navy effort to clandestinely tap undersea communications cables to collect Soviet military communications.[^1]
- Accounts emphasize that Pelton was able to provide detailed program knowledge largely from memory rather than by supplying physical classified documents.[^1]

### Contact with the USSR / KGB (summary)

- Pelton contacted the Soviet Embassy in Washington, D.C. (1980) and later met Soviet intelligence officers abroad (including meetings reported in Vienna in the early 1980s).[^1]
- Open sources describe payments from Soviet handlers totaling on the order of **tens of thousands of dollars**.[^1]

### Identification, investigation, and arrest (high level)

- The case is often linked to the 1985 defection of former KGB officer **Vitaly Yurchenko**, whose debriefing reportedly helped narrow investigative focus to a former NSA employee matching certain descriptors.[^1]
- After surveillance and investigative steps, Pelton was arrested and prosecuted in federal court, leading to conviction in 1986.[^1]

### Sentence and later life (public record)

- Wikipedia summarizes the criminal penalty as **three life sentences plus 10 years**.[^1]
