## Operating Model (High-Level)

LockBit is commonly described as a ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) operation, where core developers provide malware and infrastructure while affiliated operators conduct intrusions.

This division of labor is a major scaling mechanism in the ransomware ecosystem, enabling many parallel campaigns under a single brand.

## Typical Attack Lifecycle

Victimization patterns attributed to large ransomware groups often include initial access (e.g., stolen credentials or exploitation), lateral movement, privilege escalation, and deployment at scale.

Many modern campaigns also pair encryption with data theft and extortion pressure ("double extortion"), increasing leverage even when backups exist.

## Targeting and Impact

Summaries of LockBit activity cite a high volume of incidents across multiple sectors, consistent with financially motivated cybercrime rather than a single-issue political campaign.

The reported number of attacks and aggregate ransom amounts made LockBit a reference point in discussions of the broader ransomware economy.

## Law Enforcement Disruption

The group faced international law enforcement actions that disrupted infrastructure and attempted to degrade operational capacity.

Such disruptions are typically significant but not always definitive, as brands can reconstitute through affiliates, forks, or rebranding.

## Significance

LockBit is often used as a case study for the industrialization of cyber extortion: mature branding, affiliate onboarding, and operational scale.

It also illustrates the persistent challenge of cross-border enforcement when operators and infrastructure span multiple jurisdictions.
