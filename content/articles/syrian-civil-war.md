## Summary

Assad's response to protests created a civil war killing over 500,000. Chemical weapons were used on civilians; millions of refugees fled, creating Europe's worst refugee crisis.

## Additional Context

### Origins and early phase

The Syrian Civil War grew out of political unrest and protests in 2011, followed by escalation between state security forces and a widening set of armed opponents. Over time, the conflict fragmented into multiple fronts, with shifting alliances and competing political and territorial objectives.

### Major actors (high-level)

- **Syrian government forces** and affiliated groups.
- **Opposition armed groups** with varying ideologies and external support.
- **Extremist organizations** that exploited the conflict’s instability.
- **Kurdish-led forces** and local administrations in parts of the country.
- **External states** that provided military, financial, or diplomatic support to different sides.

### Humanitarian and regional consequences

The war produced one of the largest displacement crises of the 21st century, including millions of refugees and internally displaced people. Civilian harm, siege warfare, and the destruction of infrastructure contributed to long-term public-health, economic, and governance challenges.

International attention also focused on allegations and findings related to chemical weapons use, as well as on efforts to document war crimes and other violations of international humanitarian law.

### Diplomacy and attempts at settlement

Multiple diplomatic tracks—ceasefire negotiations, UN-sponsored talks, and regional arrangements—sought to reduce violence or reach a political settlement. These efforts faced persistent obstacles, including battlefield dynamics, external involvement, and disagreements over governance and accountability.
