## Summary

Forced transportation of 12.5 million Africans to the Americas between the 16th and 19th centuries. An estimated 1.8 million died during the Middle Passage alone.

## How the System Worked (High Level)

- **Commercial infrastructure:** The trade depended on ships, financing, insurance, ports, and colonial legal systems that treated enslaved people as property.
- **Routes:** It is often described through a “triangular” pattern linking Europe, Africa, and the Americas, though routes varied over time and by empire.
- **Middle Passage:** The ocean crossing from Africa to the Americas involved extreme overcrowding, violence, disease, and high mortality.

## Scale and Economic Role

- **Labor extraction:** Enslaved labor was central to plantation economies producing commodities such as sugar, tobacco, cotton, and coffee.
- **Wealth and state power:** Profits and tax revenues from slave-based economies contributed to the growth of Atlantic empires and commercial institutions.

## Abolition and Suppression

- **Legal abolition vs. practice:** Different states abolished the slave trade and later slavery at different times, and illegal trafficking continued after formal bans.
- **Enforcement:** Naval patrols, treaties, and court actions were used to suppress trafficking, with uneven effectiveness.

## Legacy

- **Demographic and cultural impact:** The trade reshaped populations, cultures, and languages across the Americas and the Atlantic world.
- **Long-term inequality:** It is widely discussed as a root cause of enduring racial hierarchies and economic disparities.
