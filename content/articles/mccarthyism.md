## Summary

Senator Joseph McCarthy led a campaign of political persecution accusing hundreds of Americans of communist sympathies without evidence. Ruined careers and violated civil liberties.

## What the term means

- “McCarthyism” is commonly used to describe a period and style of anti-communist political repression in the United States, particularly in the early Cold War.
- The term often refers not only to Senator Joseph McCarthy’s actions but to broader investigations, loyalty programs, and blacklisting practices.
- Accusations frequently relied on association or suspicion rather than proof of espionage.

## Mechanisms of persecution (high level)

- Congressional investigations (including Senate and House committees) held hearings that could expose witnesses to public accusation.
- Loyalty and security programs required screenings of government employees and sometimes compelled affirmations of political loyalty.
- Hollywood and other industries used informal blacklists, with employment consequences for those accused or for those who refused to cooperate.

## Key moments frequently cited

- The 1950 “Wheeling speech” is widely described as a catalyst for McCarthy’s rise in national prominence.
- Televised hearings, including the Army–McCarthy hearings, increased public scrutiny of investigative tactics.
- The US Senate’s censure of McCarthy in 1954 is commonly treated as a turning point in his political decline.

## Civil liberties and long-term impact

- Histories of the period emphasize chilling effects on speech, association, and political participation.
- The term remains a shorthand for demagoguery and guilt-by-association tactics.
- Scholarship distinguishes between legitimate counterintelligence concerns and abusive or politically motivated accusations.
