## Summary
On February 18, 2013, armed robbers dressed as police officers stole $50 million worth of diamonds from a Helvetic Airways plane at Brussels Airport. The heist took only 11 minutes. The stolen diamonds were being transported from Antwerp to Zurich.

## Background
The events leading to Brussels Airport Diamond Heist 2013 were shaped by complex social, political, and criminal factors in Brussels, Belgium. [^1]

## The Event
Brussels Airport Diamond Heist 2013 unfolded as a significant criminal event that drew widespread attention. On February 18, 2013, armed robbers dressed as police officers stole $50 million worth of diamonds from a Helvetic Airways plane at Brussels Airport. The heist took only 11 minutes. The stolen diamonds were being transported from Antwerp to Zurich. [^2]

## Tactics and Vulnerabilities
Reporting on the heist highlighted how quickly a small team, using police-style clothing and vehicles, could exploit airport-side access routes and timing around cargo handling. The operation’s speed and apparent familiarity with procedures fueled discussion about whether the perpetrators had detailed logistical knowledge of airport operations and shipment schedules.

## Investigation
Belgian authorities pursued leads related to organized-crime networks and the fencing of high-value gemstones, which can be difficult to trace once broken into smaller lots and moved across borders. The case underscored the investigative importance of surveillance footage, access-control logs, and coordination with international partners in crimes involving transnational supply chains.

## Security and Industry Impact
The incident was widely cited in debates about perimeter security for airside areas and the protection of high-value cargo. It contributed to increased attention on layered security controls—physical barriers, patrol patterns, authentication of uniforms/vehicles, and stricter procedures for escorting valuable shipments from secure facilities to aircraft.

## Additional Notes
High-value diamond shipments are typically managed through specialized logistics and insurance arrangements; the heist illustrated how adversaries can target narrow handoff moments where operational tempo is high and verification steps may be compressed.
