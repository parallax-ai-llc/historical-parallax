## Summary

Masatoshi Koshiba shared the 2002 Nobel Prize for detecting cosmic neutrinos using the Kamiokande detector.

## Key Contributions

- **Neutrino astronomy**: Koshiba helped establish experimental methods for observing neutrinos from astrophysical sources, a foundation for neutrino astronomy.
- **Kamiokande**: He led efforts at the University of Tokyo connected with the Kamioka Nucleon Decay Experiment (Kamiokande), a large underground water Cherenkov detector.
- **Supernova 1987A neutrinos**: Kamiokande recorded a burst of neutrinos associated with **Supernova 1987A**, providing rare direct evidence about core-collapse supernova processes.

## Context and Impact

Koshiba’s work linked **particle physics** and **astrophysics** by showing that neutrino detectors could act as observatories. The approach of shielding detectors deep underground to reduce background radiation became standard for later experiments.

Later, larger detectors such as **Super-Kamiokande** built on Kamiokande’s techniques and scientific goals, enabling higher-statistics measurements and broad neutrino physics programs.

## Recognition

- **Nobel Prize in Physics (2002)**: Shared for pioneering contributions to astrophysics, in particular for the detection of cosmic neutrinos.

## Legacy

Koshiba is widely cited in the history of modern neutrino research for demonstrating that elusive particles can be detected at scale and used to study violent astrophysical events.

His research groups also helped train a generation of experimental physicists who contributed to subsequent underground detector programs.
