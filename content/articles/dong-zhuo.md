## Summary

Eastern Han dynasty warlord who seized control of the imperial court and became a byword for tyranny in Chinese history. He burned the capital Luoyang and was assassinated by his adoptive son Lü Bu.

## Additional Context

### Rise to Power

- Dong Zhuo emerged as a military commander during the late **Eastern Han** period, when court factionalism and regional militarization weakened central authority.
- In **189**, after the death of Emperor Ling, he entered the capital and became the dominant power behind the throne.
- He **deposed Emperor Shao** and installed **Emperor Xian**, consolidating control through intimidation and strategic appointments.

### Rule and Policies

- Historical sources portray his regime as marked by:
  - **Violence and political purges** against perceived rivals.
  - Heavy-handed control of the imperial court.
  - Practices that accelerated the breakdown of imperial legitimacy.

### Relocation and Destruction of the Capital

- Facing opposition from a coalition of regional leaders, Dong Zhuo ordered the **evacuation of Luoyang** and moved the court to **Chang’an**.
- Accounts describe widespread **destruction and looting** associated with the move, contributing to his reputation for brutality.

### Assassination and Aftermath

- In **192**, Dong Zhuo was assassinated by **Lü Bu**, his adoptive son, in a plot linked to court officials.
- His death did not restore stability; instead, the resulting power vacuum furthered the era’s fragmentation into competing warlord states.

### Cultural Legacy

- Dong Zhuo became a canonical villain figure in later literature and popular culture, especially in the **"Romance of the Three Kingdoms"** tradition, where he symbolizes tyranny and corruption.
