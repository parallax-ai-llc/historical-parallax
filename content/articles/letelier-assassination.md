## Summary

Chilean dictator Pinochet ordered the car bombing of former ambassador Orlando Letelier in Washington DC, also killing his American colleague. The boldest act of state-sponsored terrorism on US soil.

## Context and Targets

Orlando Letelier was a former Chilean ambassador to the United States and a prominent critic of Augusto Pinochet’s military dictatorship after the 1973 coup.[^1]

- **Location**: Sheridan Circle area, Washington, D.C.
- **Method**: Car bomb placed on or under Letelier’s vehicle
- **Victims**:
  - Orlando Letelier (killed)
  - Ronni Karpen Moffitt, an American colleague at the Institute for Policy Studies (killed)
  - Michael Moffitt (injured)

## Responsibility and Planning

The attack is widely attributed to Chile’s secret police, the **Dirección de Inteligencia Nacional (DINA)**, which used intermediaries and collaborators to operate abroad.[^1]

Key points often highlighted in historical accounts include:

- The operation targeted an exiled political opponent rather than a military objective.
- It involved coordination across borders, including travel, logistics, and recruitment.
- It became one of the most notorious examples of state-linked political violence carried out in the United States.

## Investigation and Prosecutions

U.S. law enforcement investigated the bombing as a major terrorism and assassination case.[^1]

Commonly reported outcomes include:

- **Michael Townley** (a DINA operative) pleaded guilty in U.S. court and cooperated with investigators.
- Several anti-Castro Cuban exiles were charged and convicted in U.S. proceedings for roles connected to the bombing.
- In later years, Chilean courts convicted senior former DINA officials (including **Manuel Contreras** and **Pedro Espinoza**) in connection with the case.

## Diplomatic and Historical Significance

The Letelier assassination had lasting political consequences beyond the immediate loss of life:

- It severely strained relations between the United States and the Pinochet government.
- It drew international attention to cross-border repression associated with Chile’s security services.
- It remains a frequently cited case in discussions of **state-sponsored terrorism**, accountability, and extraterritorial political violence.
