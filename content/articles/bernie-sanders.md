## Summary

Bernie Sanders is an American politician from Vermont who has served as a U.S. Senator since 2007 and previously as the state's sole U.S. Representative from 1991 to 2007. As one of the longest-serving independent members of Congress in American history, Sanders built a career around democratic socialist policies, consistently advocating for universal healthcare, campaign finance reform, and reducing economic inequality.

Sanders became a nationally transformative figure through his 2016 and 2020 presidential campaigns, which galvanized millions of younger voters and shifted mainstream Democratic politics leftward. Though he did not secure the Democratic nomination in either race, his campaigns popularized ideas — Medicare for All, tuition-free public college, a $15 federal minimum wage — that moved from the political fringe to the center of national debate.

## Early Life & Background

Bernard Sanders was born on September 8, 1941, in Brooklyn, New York, to Eli Sanders, a Jewish immigrant from Poland, and Dorothy Glassberg. He grew up in a working-class household in Flatbush, Brooklyn, an experience he credits with shaping his awareness of economic hardship. He attended Brooklyn College briefly before transferring to the University of Chicago, where he graduated with a degree in political science in 1964. At Chicago, Sanders was active in the civil rights movement, participating in sit-ins and marching with the Congress of Racial Equality (CORE).

## Rise to Prominence

After college, Sanders moved to Vermont in 1968. He ran unsuccessful campaigns for governor and U.S. Senate through the 1970s as a third-party candidate. His breakthrough came in 1981 when he was elected mayor of Burlington, Vermont's largest city, by just ten votes, defeating a six-term incumbent. He served four terms as mayor (1981–1989), earning a reputation for pragmatic governance despite his socialist label. In 1990 he won Vermont's single U.S. House seat as an independent, a position he held for eight terms. He was elected to the U.S. Senate in 2006, defeating Republican incumbent Richard Tarrant.

## Key Achievements

Sanders chaired the Senate Veterans' Affairs Committee from 2013 to 2015, shepherding the Veterans' Access to Care Act of 2014 — a $16.3 billion bipartisan reform bill — into law following the VA wait-time scandal. His 2016 presidential primary campaign raised over $228 million, largely from small-dollar donors averaging around $27, demonstrating a viable alternative fundraising model to corporate PAC money. The 2016 campaign drew roughly 13 million primary votes. He has been one of the most consistent Senate voices on income inequality, introducing the Workplace Democracy Act and the Stop BEZOS Act, and he played a central role in expanding the Child Tax Credit provisions in the 2021 American Rescue Plan.

## Controversies & Criticism

Sanders has faced criticism from within the Democratic Party for running as an independent while competing in Democratic primaries, with some arguing his 2016 campaign damaged Hillary Clinton's general election prospects. His embrace of the "democratic socialist" label has drawn persistent Republican attacks equating his policies with authoritarian regimes. Critics on the left have pointed to his inconsistent record on gun control prior to 2016, including votes against the Brady Bill. His 2020 campaign was also scrutinized for tensions with women and minority candidates, including a disputed private conversation with Elizabeth Warren in which she alleged he told her a woman could not win the presidency — a claim Sanders denied.

## Legacy & Influence

Sanders's most durable legacy may be ideological: his campaigns normalized democratic socialist rhetoric in American mainstream politics and helped elect a new generation of progressive lawmakers, including Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez and other members of "The Squad." His insistence on Medicare for All put single-payer healthcare on the national agenda in a way it had not been since the 1990s. Now in his eighth decade, he remains a prominent Senate voice and a benchmark figure for the American left's ongoing tension between incrementalism and structural reform.
