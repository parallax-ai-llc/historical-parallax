## Background

Arif Naqvi is best known as the founder of the Abraaj Group, a private equity firm that positioned itself as a major investor across emerging markets.[^1] The firm’s brand was closely tied to “impact” narratives—health, infrastructure, and development themes—aimed at attracting large institutional and philanthropic capital.[^1]

## Abraaj Group and Emerging-Markets Private Equity

At its peak, Abraaj was frequently described as one of the largest private equity firms focused on emerging markets.[^1] The firm raised and managed funds targeting regions such as the Middle East, Africa, and South Asia, marketing local-market expertise and access as differentiators.

## Misappropriation Allegations (High-Level)

Reporting around the case (as summarized in commonly cited public references) centers on the allegation that investor money—particularly funds earmarked for specific mandates—was used in ways inconsistent with those mandates, including to address liquidity shortfalls elsewhere in the business.[^1] In large fund structures, this kind of misuse is often framed as a custody and controls failure: investors assume ring-fencing and strict use-of-proceeds, while the manager retains operational discretion that can be abused if governance breaks down.

## Legal Proceedings and Enforcement

Naqvi’s legal issues involved arrest and subsequent proceedings connected to U.S. fraud charges, and the wider collapse and restructuring of the Abraaj Group.[^1] The episode became a prominent case study in cross-border financial enforcement, because key actors, investors, and assets spanned multiple jurisdictions.

## Impact and Significance

The Abraaj collapse is often discussed for its implications in:

- **Trust in impact/ESG branding:** High-profile “impact” positioning increases reputational stakes when governance fails.
- **LP due diligence:** Institutional limited partners tend to scrutinize custody arrangements, valuation practices, and conflict-of-interest controls more heavily after such cases.
- **Emerging-market governance risk:** Complex local operating environments can magnify oversight gaps and reduce transparency.
