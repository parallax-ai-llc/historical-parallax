## Summary

Medellín Cartel co-founder who turned Norman's Cay into a cocaine transshipment hub.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1987 | Convicted and sentenced to life plus 135 years |

## Background

- **Full name used in sources:** Carlos Enrique Lehder Rivas.[^1]
- **Notability:** Known primarily for his role in early large-scale cocaine trafficking and for pushing the development of air routes that linked South America to the United States via the Caribbean.[^1]
- **Public profile:** Unlike many traffickers who tried to stay obscure, he became publicly recognizable and was associated with overt political messaging in Colombia during the 1980s.[^1]

## Operations and Significance (High-Level)

- **Norman's Cay (Bahamas):** Reported in public accounts as a major logistics node where aircraft could refuel, stage, and move shipments onward—an example of how geographic choke points were exploited for transshipment.[^1]
- **Medellín Cartel association:** Commonly described as a co-founder/early partner connected to the broader Medellín network, alongside other well-known figures of that era.[^1]
- **Aviation logistics:** His story is often cited to illustrate a shift from smaller, ad-hoc smuggling to more systematic, aviation-centered supply chains.[^1]

## Legal Proceedings and Aftermath

- **Extradition era context:** His conviction is frequently discussed in the context of Colombia–U.S. cooperation and the contentious Colombian debate over extradition in the 1980s.[^1]
- **Sentence note:** The widely quoted "life plus 135 years" sentence reflects U.S. federal sentencing in a major narcotics case; later developments about incarceration status are sometimes updated by news sources, but the baseline historical fact remains the 1987 conviction.[^1]

## Why This Figure Matters (Reference Notes)

- **Logistics over ideology:** Many summaries emphasize how his case demonstrates that trafficking organizations relied on infrastructure (airstrips, islands, aircraft, protection networks) as much as on leadership personalities.
- **Internationalization of enforcement:** His prosecution is often used as an example of how U.S. courts and extradition became central tools in high-profile transnational narcotics cases.
