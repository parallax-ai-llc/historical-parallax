## Summary

Ansaru terrorist group kidnapped and murdered seven Setraco construction workers including foreign nationals in Bauchi State. The group split from Boko Haram claiming it targeted foreigners, not Muslims.

## Context

Ansaru (also described as the “Vanguard for the Protection of Muslims in Black Africa”) emerged as a Boko Haram faction and publicly announced its independence in 2012, portraying itself as more internationally oriented and more closely aligned with al-Qaeda’s worldview than Boko Haram’s Nigeria-centered insurgency.[^2]

In public messaging, Ansaru leaders and spokespeople have argued that the group’s violence is aimed at foreign interests and state targets, and they have criticized Boko Haram’s indiscriminate killing of civilians (even as Ansaru itself has been linked to lethal hostage-taking and attacks).[^2]

## Why kidnappings mattered

Kidnappings served several overlapping purposes for jihadist groups active in northern Nigeria during this period:

- **Leverage and bargaining power**: hostages could be used for prisoner exchanges or political concessions.
- **Funding and logistics**: ransoms and associated criminal networks could help sustain operations.
- **Strategic signaling**: high-profile abductions communicated reach and raised international attention.

## Aftermath and significance

This episode is often discussed as part of a broader escalation in the targeting of foreign workers and international-linked projects in northern Nigeria, and as a signal that splinter factions could sustain operations independent of Boko Haram’s main command structures.
