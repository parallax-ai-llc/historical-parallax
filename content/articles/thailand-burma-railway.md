## Summary

Japanese forces used 180,000 Asian laborers and 60,000 Allied POWs to build the 'Death Railway' through Thailand and Burma. An estimated 90,000 Asian laborers and 12,000 POWs died from abuse.

## Strategic Purpose (World War II)

- The railway was built by Imperial Japan to connect Thailand and Burma (Myanmar) and support military logistics in the Burma campaign.
- Construction prioritized speed over safety, leading to extreme mortality among forced laborers and prisoners of war.

## Forced Labor and Conditions

- Workers included large numbers of Asian civilian laborers (often referred to as r%C5%8Dmusha) and Allied POWs.
- Commonly reported causes of death include malnutrition, disease (e.g., malaria, cholera), overwork, and physical abuse.
- Seasonal monsoon conditions and limited medical supplies worsened illness and injury.

## Notable Locations and Public Memory

- Sections associated with particularly severe conditions are commemorated today, including areas known as Hellfire Pass.
- The "Bridge on the River Kwai" became internationally famous through postwar literature and film, shaping popular awareness of the railway.

## Postwar Accountability and Legacy

- After the war, aspects of forced labor and mistreatment of POWs were addressed in war-crimes proceedings and historical investigations.
- The railway remains a major case study in the history of wartime forced labor, civilian suffering, and the responsibilities of occupying powers.

## Notes

Numbers for laborers and deaths are commonly cited estimates; historians may differ in methodology and totals depending on sources and definitions.
