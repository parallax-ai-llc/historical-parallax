## Summary

Sampoong Department Store in Seoul collapsed killing 502 people and injuring 937. The building's owner had ignored engineers' warnings and added illegal floors. The worst building disaster in Korean history.

## Background

Sampoong Department Store (삼풍백화점) was a large retail complex in Seoul’s Seocho District. It opened in 1989 and became one of the busiest department stores in the city. The collapse on 29 June 1995 is widely described as the deadliest peacetime building failure in South Korea.

## What happened (high level)

On the day of the disaster, structural problems escalated into a sudden progressive collapse of the building. The failure occurred during business hours, which contributed to the very high casualty count.

## Contributing factors (reported)

Public reporting and later investigations commonly cite a combination of factors:

- **Unauthorized design/usage changes**: the building’s configuration and loads were reportedly altered from earlier plans.
- **Overloading**: heavy equipment and/or additional floors increased structural stress beyond intended limits.
- **Ignored warnings**: engineers and internal staff reportedly raised concerns prior to the collapse.
- **Weak oversight and enforcement**: critics noted shortcomings in permitting, inspection, and accountability.

## Rescue and immediate aftermath

The disaster triggered a large-scale emergency response and extended rescue operations. Survivors were pulled from the rubble in the days following the collapse, and the incident prompted nationwide mourning.

## Legal and regulatory aftermath

The collapse led to criminal investigations and prosecutions related to negligence and illegal construction/alterations. It also intensified public scrutiny of building safety, construction practices, and regulatory enforcement in South Korea.

## Legacy

In South Korea, the Sampoong collapse remains a reference point for discussions about:

- structural safety and professional ethics in construction
- the consequences of cost-cutting and corruption in permitting and inspections
- disaster preparedness and emergency response capacity
