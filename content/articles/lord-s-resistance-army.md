## Background and context

The Lord's Resistance Army (LRA) emerged in northern Uganda in the late 1980s amid political upheaval following the rise of Yoweri Museveni's National Resistance Movement government and a series of insurgencies in the Acholi region. The group is most closely associated with Joseph Kony, who claimed spiritual authority and led the movement for decades.

## Organization and methods

The LRA became notorious for systematic abductions, including large-scale kidnapping of children who were forced into combat roles, portering, or sexual slavery. The organization relied on coercion, fear, and the fragmentation of small mobile units operating across remote areas rather than holding territory in a conventional way.

## Regional operations

Over time, LRA activity extended beyond Uganda into South Sudan, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, and the Central African Republic. Shifting bases and cross-border movement complicated military operations against the group and contributed to protracted insecurity affecting civilians across the region.

## International response

The LRA was designated a terrorist organization by multiple governments, and the International Criminal Court issued arrest warrants for senior LRA leaders in 2005. Efforts to end the conflict included intermittent peace talks, military campaigns by regional forces, and international support for tracking and protection initiatives.

## Humanitarian impact and legacy

The conflict associated with the LRA contributed to mass displacement, the disruption of schooling and livelihoods, and long-term trauma among affected communities. The LRA is frequently cited in discussions of atrocities committed by non-state armed groups in Africa, particularly regarding forced recruitment and the use of child soldiers.
