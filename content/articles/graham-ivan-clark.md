## Summary

American teenager who masterminded the 2020 Twitter hack that hijacked accounts of Obama, Biden, and Musk for a bitcoin scam. He was 17 at the time.

## Background

The 2020 Twitter incident drew attention because it involved access to internal tools used to manage high-profile accounts.
The attack relied more on social engineering and credential abuse than on a purely technical exploit of the platform.

## 2020 Twitter Account Takeover

In July 2020, a group gained control of multiple prominent Twitter accounts and posted messages soliciting bitcoin transfers.
The compromised accounts included major public figures and companies, increasing the credibility of the scam and its visibility.
Investigators described the method as involving the manipulation of employees or contractors to obtain access to administrative capabilities.

## Criminal Case and Sentencing

Clark was identified as a central figure in the operation while still a minor.
He faced criminal charges in Florida state court, and the case became a notable example of how cybercrime investigations can progress quickly when major platforms and law enforcement coordinate.
Public reporting describes a sentence that included a period of incarceration and supervision/probation, reflecting his juvenile status at the time.

## Broader Impact

The incident triggered security reviews focused on internal access controls, employee authentication, and protections for high-profile accounts.
It also highlighted the financial and reputational risk of account-takeover events, even when the direct monetary loss is relatively limited.

## Additional Notes

The incident is often summarized as a "Twitter hack," but many analyses classify it as an insider-access compromise enabled by social engineering.
This distinction matters because it shifts remediation toward employee security, internal tool auditing, and strict privileged-access management.
