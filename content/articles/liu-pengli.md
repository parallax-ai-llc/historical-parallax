## Summary

Chinese prince and possibly history's earliest documented serial killer, who murdered over 100 people for pleasure.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| -116 | Deposed and exiled for mass murder |

## Background

- **Title**: Prince of Jidong (濟東王) during the Western Han period.[^1]
- **Family**: Reported as a grandson of **Emperor Wen** and a nephew of **Emperor Jing**; his father was **Liu Wu**, Prince of Liang.[^1]
- **Appointment**: Later accounts place his enfeoffment as Prince of Jidong around **144 BCE** (during Emperor Jing's reign).[^1]

## Allegations and Sources

- Later summaries often describe Liu Pengli as an early (and sometimes "first") **documented serial killer**, based on imperial-era historical writing rather than modern criminal records.[^1]
- The core narrative is traced to **Sima Qian's _Records of the Grand Historian_ (Shiji)**, which portrays him as arrogant and violent, allegedly leading raids with retainers and committing killings and robberies.[^1]
- Because the story comes through court historiography, details may reflect a mix of factual reporting, moral judgment, and political framing typical of the genre.[^1]

## Deposition and Aftermath

- According to the received account, accusations of large-scale murder led to **deposition** and **exile**, ending his status as a ruling prince.[^1]
- The episode is also cited as an example of how the Han court could curb powerful regional princes by using alleged offenses as justification.[^1]

## Legacy

- In popular retellings, Liu Pengli is frequently referenced in lists of historical figures associated with extreme violence.
- In academic terms, he is more safely treated as a **case study in Han political control and historiography** than as a directly comparable figure to modern serial-crime typologies.[^1]
