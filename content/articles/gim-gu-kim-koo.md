## Summary

Kim Koo (also romanized as Kim Gu or Kim Ku, pen name Baekbeom, 1876-1949) was one of Korea's most prominent independence activists and political leaders during the Japanese colonial period. He served as president of the Korean Provisional Government in exile from 1926-1927 and 1940-1945, organizing armed resistance against Japanese occupation from Shanghai and Chongqing. After Korea's liberation in 1945, he became a passionate advocate for Korean reunification, opposing the division of Korea into separate northern and southern states. His life ended tragically when he was assassinated in 1949 by army officer Ahn Doo-hee under controversial circumstances that suggested political motives. Today, Kim Koo is revered in South Korea as one of the greatest figures in Korean history.[^1][^2]

---

## Early Life and Background

Kim Koo was born Kim Changam on August 29, 1876, in Totkol village, Paegunbang, Haeju, Hwanghae Province, in the Joseon Kingdom.[^3] He was the only child of two farmers: his mother Kwak Nagwon and his father Kim Sunyong. His family was impoverished, poorly educated, and looked down upon by the community due to their lower social class.[^4] When Kim was two years old, he contracted smallpox, which left permanent scars on his face that he would bear throughout his life.[^5]

Despite his family's poverty, they desperately wanted young Kim to receive an education as a pathway to escape their social circumstances. Kim studied Chinese classic texts at local private village schools called seodangs.[^6] However, his educational journey faced significant obstacles due to his family's social status. Around age nine, his parents attempted to enroll him in a local school to prepare for government examinations, but schools turned him away because of his family's lower social class. He finally started learning at age twelve with a tutor.[^7]

In 1888, when Kim was twelve, his father became paralyzed, forcing the young boy to help support his family by cutting wood. His mother worked tirelessly, weaving and farming to pay for Kim's school supplies.[^8] At age sixteen, Kim applied for the Imperial Examination but failed. This disappointment, combined with growing awareness of social injustice and foreign encroachment on Korean sovereignty, led him to join the Donghak Movement, a syncretic religious and political movement that rebelled against government corruption and foreign oppression.[^9]

## Rise to Power

Kim's path to prominence began with his participation in the Donghak Movement at age sixteen. He quickly rose through the ranks to become leader of the district of Palbong, commanding his own army regiment.[^10] This early experience in organized resistance would shape his future as an independence activist.

A pivotal and controversial incident occurred on March 9, 1896, when Kim killed a Japanese man named Tsuchida Josuke in Chihapo, Hwanghae Province. Kim believed Tsuchida was connected to the assassination of Empress Myeongseong (Queen Min), which had occurred in October 1895.[^11] During a violent confrontation around 3 am, Kim kicked the man to the floor, wrestled his sword from him, and stabbed him to death.[^12] Kim was arrested in June 1896 and faced trial. On September 12, 1896, the Japanese consular agent found Kim guilty of manslaughter and recommended execution by beheading.[^13] However, King Gojong read about Kim's patriotic motivations and refused to approve the death sentence on October 22, 1896.[^14]

Kim eventually escaped from Incheon Prison and sought refuge at Magoksa Buddhist monastery, where he lived as a monk under the Dharma name Wonjong.[^15] This monastic period was brief, lasting only about a year, as Kim did not truly embrace Buddhism. He left the monastery to return to his hometown, where he converted to Christianity and founded Yangsan School in 1907, marking his transition to educational activism.[^16]

When Korea's March First Independence Movement erupted on March 1, 1919, Kim participated in the nationwide non-violent protests that were violently suppressed by Imperial Japan, resulting in thousands of deaths and tens of thousands of arrests.[^17] On March 29, 1919, Kim began a journey to Shanghai, China, to join the Korean Provisional Government (KPG), arriving on April 13. He was immediately appointed police commissioner, beginning his long career in the government-in-exile.[^18]

## Major Achievements

Kim Koo's leadership of the Korean independence movement from exile represents one of the most significant chapters in Korean resistance to Japanese colonialism. After arriving in Shanghai in 1919, he steadily rose through the ranks of the Korean Provisional Government, becoming director of domestic affairs in 1922 and premier in late 1926.[^19] He served as head of the Korean Provisional Government from 1926 to 1927 and again from 1940 to 1945.[^20]

Between 1926 and 1932, Kim lived at the headquarters for different independence groups in Shanghai, spending thirteen years hiding in the city without ever leaving it.[^21] During this period, he changed his name from Kim Changam to Kim Koo, symbolizing his break from Japanese nationality records. He chose the pen name "Baekbeom," meaning "ordinary person," hoping that every ordinary Korean would fight for independence.[^22]

In 1931, Kim organized the Korean Patriotic Corps (also called Korean Patriotic Organization), a militant arm of the Korean Provisional Government.[^23] Under his direction, the organization carried out two spectacular operations that electrified the Korean independence movement and gained international attention. On January 8, 1932, KPO member Lee Bong-chang attempted to assassinate Emperor Hirohito in Tokyo in the Sakuradamon incident, throwing a grenade that narrowly missed the Emperor's carriage.[^24]

Learning from Lee's failure, Kim meticulously prepared for the next operation. On April 26, 1932, Yun Bong-gil joined the Korean Patriotic Corps and took an oath under the Korean national flag, a moment captured in a photograph that would become iconic.[^25] On April 29, 1932, Yun carried a bomb disguised as a water bottle to a celebration at Hongkou Park in Shanghai, where it killed government minister Kawabata Teiji and mortally wounded General Yoshinori Shirakawa, who died of his injuries on May 26, 1932.[^26]

The Hongkou Park bombing proved to be a turning point for the Korean independence movement. The attack earned the Korean Provisional Government the respect and support of Chinese Nationalist leader Chiang Kai-shek, who began actively sheltering and funding the organization.[^27] Chiang wrote in his diary that day praising Yun's patriotic martyrdom as a great contribution to Korean independence.[^28] When Kim Koo suffered serious gunshot wounds in the Nammokcheong incident in Changsha in May 1937, Chiang sent multiple telegrams asking after Kim's condition and later sent confidants with considerable sums of money to help cover medical expenses.[^29]

With Chinese support, Kim helped establish the Korean Liberation Army on September 17, 1940, in Chongqing, with significant financial and personnel support from the Kuomintang.[^30] The Provisional Government moved several times after the Marco Polo Bridge Incident in July 1937, eventually settling in Chongqing in August 1940, where it gained de facto recognition from Chiang Kai-shek.[^31] On May 1, 1945, the Korean Provisional Government gained full control over the Korean Liberation Army through an agreement with China.[^32]

Kim's most enduring literary achievement was his autobiography, "Baekbeom Ilji" (백범일지), written in two parts with the first volume completed in 1929 and the second around 1942.[^33] Published in 1947, it became an immediate bestseller in Korea and has been designated as national treasure No. 1245 by the South Korean government.[^34] The autobiography remains an important primary source for studying the Korean independence movement and has been translated into multiple languages including English, Japanese, and Chinese.[^35]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his status as a national hero, Kim Koo's life and methods have been subject to significant controversy and debate, both during his lifetime and in historical assessments. The most enduring controversy surrounds the 1896 killing of Tsuchida Josuke. While Kim believed he was executing a Japanese military officer involved in the assassination of Empress Myeongseong, historical evidence increasingly suggests that Tsuchida was actually a civilian merchant from Nagasaki Prefecture on a business trip.[^36] The "Report from acting administrator Hagihara Moriichi of Incheon Consulate on the current situation of Incheon" describes Tsuchida as a "commoner from Nagasaki Prefecture" and an "employee of a Nagasaki trader."[^37] This discrepancy has led to debates about whether Kim's action, however patriotically motivated, constituted the killing of an innocent civilian.

The question of terrorism has also dogged Kim's legacy. In 2007, a professor described Kim's Korean Patriotic Organization as a "terrorist group," sparking intense debate.[^38] Students and Korean nationalists argued that Kim did not carelessly target civilians, pointing out that operations like the Hongkou Park bombing specifically targeted military and government officials involved in colonial oppression. The professor later withdrew the description, acknowledging that the word "terrorism" carried too much unintended meaning and failed to account for the context of colonial resistance.[^39] Nevertheless, the debate highlights ongoing tensions about how to characterize violent resistance against colonial occupation.

Kim's 1948 visit to Pyongyang for unification talks with Kim Il-sung proved deeply controversial and damaged his political standing in South Korea. From April 19-22, 1948, Kim led a delegation of southern representatives including Kim Kyu-sik to meet with northern leaders.[^40] The conference organizers claimed to want a Korean solution for Korea's problems, but the meeting produced no concrete results.[^41] Many contemporaries viewed Kim's willingness to negotiate with the communist North Korean leadership as naïve or even traitorous. A U.S. State Department document from 1949 noted that Kim Koo's assassination appeared motivated by "bitterness over Kim Koo's professed policy of joint consultation with North Korea and Kim's apparent reluctance to support ROK."[^42]

Kim's opposition to the establishment of a separate South Korean government also alienated him from many Korean political leaders and from the United States. He fiercely opposed the UN-supervised elections in South Korea in 1948, arguing that separate elections would permanently divide the Korean nation.[^43] U.S. military governor John Hodge complained that "extreme rightist groups under Dr. Syngman Rhee and Kim Koo have done much to block American efforts to implement the Moscow Agreement by adopting not only a non-cooperative but even an obstructive attitude."[^44] Hodge was reportedly so furious that he initially wanted Kim and the Korean Provisional Government arrested and deported back to China, though he ultimately decided against this action.[^45]

Kim's long-standing rivalry with Syngman Rhee created additional political complications. Kim had expelled Rhee from the Korean Provisional Government in 1925 over allegations of embezzlement and abuse of power.[^46] This personal and political enmity persisted after liberation, with both men competing for leadership of the new Korean state. In the 1948 presidential election held by the National Assembly, Kim was defeated by Rhee by a vote of 180-16, receiving only 6.7% of the vote to Rhee's 92.3%.[^47] Significantly, Kim did not know about his nomination until after the election and did not approve it, considering it a ploy to discredit him since he opposed the separate election entirely.[^48]

## Personal Life

Kim Koo's personal life was marked by tragedy, sacrifice, and the heavy toll of his lifelong commitment to Korean independence. His first wife died on January 1, 1924, just two years after the birth of their fifth child, Shin.[^49] Because Kim was a wanted man living in hiding in Shanghai, he was unable to visit her in the hospital before she died. She was buried in the Shanghai French Concession, far from her homeland.[^50]

Kim and his first wife had five children together: three daughters and two sons. Tragically, only his sons survived past childhood; all three daughters died young.[^51] His elder son, Kim In (1917-1945), joined his father in Shanghai in 1920 at the age of three and grew up in the exile community.[^52] Kim In would later die in 1945, just as Korea gained its independence, never seeing the free homeland his father had fought to create.

Later in his exile, while living in hiding in China, Kim proposed marriage to a 20-year-old Chinese woman named Zhu Aibao, who owned a boat he often rode.[^53] While they never officially married, they functioned as husband and wife and began living together on her boat. The relationship highlighted Kim's isolation and the unconventional life forced upon him by his status as a hunted independence activist. They had a 37-year age gap.[^54] When political circumstances forced Kim to leave, he lamented being able to provide only 100 yuan for Zhu Aibao to return to Jiaxing. They never saw each other again.[^55]

Kim's personality combined passionate nationalism with a philosophical bent. His Christian faith, adopted after leaving Buddhist monastic life, influenced his worldview, though his primary devotion was always to Korean independence. He was known for his determination, organizational skills, and ability to inspire loyalty among independence activists. His choice of the pen name "Baekbeom" (ordinary person) reflected both humility and a democratic vision—he wanted every ordinary Korean to become an independence fighter.[^56]

Despite the hardships and dangers, Kim maintained strong relationships with his comrades in the independence movement. He was known for his careful planning, as evidenced by his meticulous preparation of the Hongkou Park operation. His autobiography reveals a reflective, thoughtful person capable of deep introspection about his life's meaning and Korea's future.

## Legacy

Kim Koo's legacy as one of Korea's greatest patriots has only grown stronger in the decades since his death. In 1962, he was posthumously awarded the Republic of Korea Medal of Order of Merit for National Foundation, the most prestigious civil decoration in South Korea and the nation's highest civilian honor.[^57] This recognition formalized what had already become evident: Kim was revered as one of the greatest figures in Korean history.[^58]

Remarkably, Kim's significance transcends South Korea's borders. On August 15, 1990, North Korea posthumously awarded him the National Reunification Prize, reflecting his importance to both Koreas despite their ideological differences.[^59] This dual recognition from both Korean states stems from Kim's passionate advocacy for Korean reunification and his opposition to the division of the peninsula. His 1948 efforts to negotiate with North Korean leaders, while controversial during his lifetime, came to be seen as prescient given Korea's continued division.

In 2007, Kim was voted to become the face of the 100,000 won banknote, though the issuing of these notes became indefinitely postponed as of 2009.[^60] The Kim Koo Museum in Seoul preserves his legacy and houses artifacts from his life, including documents from the Korean Provisional Government. The site of the Korean Provisional Government at 306 Madang Road in Shanghai has been preserved as a memorial that attracts hundreds of thousands of visitors annually, serving as a symbol of Korean-Chinese friendship and the shared history of resistance against Japanese imperialism.[^61]

Kim's autobiography, "Baekbeom Ilji," continues to be widely read in Korea and has been translated into over ten different languages.[^62] It is required reading in many Korean schools and remains an essential primary source for scholars studying Korean history and the independence movement. The work provides not only historical documentation but also insight into Kim's philosophy and vision for Korea's future.

Kim's most famous statement, from the conclusion of his autobiography titled "My Desire," continues to resonate: "If God asked me what was my wish, I would reply unhesitatingly, 'Korean independence.' If he asked me what was my second wish, I would again answer, 'My country's independence.' If he asked me what was my third wish, I would reply in an even louder voice, 'My wish is the complete independence of my country, Korea.'"[^63] This passionate declaration encapsulates his single-minded devotion to his nation's freedom.

Perhaps equally influential is his vision for what kind of nation Korea should become. In "My Desire," he wrote: "I want our nation to be the most beautiful in the world. By this, I do not mean the most powerful nation. Because I have felt the pain of being invaded by another nation, I do not want my nation to invade others."[^64] This statement of principled pacifism and moral leadership continues to influence discussions about Korea's role in the world.

The circumstances of Kim's assassination have themselves become part of his legacy. The fact that he was killed not by Japanese colonial authorities but by a Korean officer in an independent Korea, possibly on orders from political rivals, adds a tragic dimension to his story. It serves as a reminder of the internal conflicts that plagued Korea's transition to independence and the price paid by those who advocated for paths different from the emerging Cold War divisions.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and historians have consistently praised Kim Koo as an exemplar of selfless patriotism and dedicated resistance to colonialism. His leadership of the Korean Provisional Government during its most difficult years in exile demonstrated remarkable organizational ability and political acumen. Historians note that his ability to maintain the Provisional Government as a functioning entity despite constant financial hardship, internal factionalism, and Japanese pressure represented a significant achievement.[^65]

The effectiveness of his military strategy through the Korean Patriotic Corps has been particularly lauded. Military historians observe that Kim's shift from conventional military confrontation to targeted operations against high-value targets represented sophisticated strategic thinking. The Hongkou Park bombing, in particular, is studied as a case of how a small resistance organization could achieve strategic impact disproportionate to its size, fundamentally changing the international perception of Korean independence activism and securing vital Chinese support.[^66]

Kim's moral vision for Korea has earned praise from scholars of political philosophy and international relations. His statement that Korea should be "the most beautiful nation in the world" rather than the most powerful, specifically rejecting imperialism despite Korea's victimization, is cited as an example of principled internationalism emerging from anti-colonial struggle.[^67] This vision contrasts with nationalistic movements that sought to reverse power hierarchies rather than reject domination entirely.

Educational historians credit Kim with understanding the importance of preserving Korean national consciousness during the colonial period. His founding of Yangsan School in 1907 and his support for educational initiatives while in exile demonstrated his belief that independence required not just military resistance but also the preservation and cultivation of Korean identity and culture.[^68]

Korean nationalist historians emphasize Kim's unwavering commitment to complete independence, contrasting his principled stance with others who contemplated accommodations with colonial or great power interests. His opposition to the UN trusteeship plan and the division of Korea is seen as evidence of his refusal to compromise on Korean sovereignty, even when pragmatic considerations might have suggested otherwise.[^69]

### Negative Views

Critical historians have raised questions about Kim Koo's political judgment, particularly regarding his 1948 negotiations with North Korean leaders. Some scholars argue that Kim's idealistic belief in Korean unity blinded him to the realities of Soviet-backed communist consolidation in the North, and that his participation in the Pyongyang talks provided propaganda value to Kim Il-sung without achieving any practical progress toward reunification.[^70] These critics suggest that Kim's opposition to separate elections in the South effectively aided communist strategy to prevent the establishment of a democratic Korean government.

The controversy over violent resistance methods continues to generate scholarly debate. While many historians contextualize the Korean Patriotic Corps' operations as legitimate resistance to colonial occupation, others question whether targeting individuals, even military and government officials, can be distinguished from terrorism.[^71] Some scholars argue that the moral clarity of anti-colonial resistance should not prevent critical examination of the means employed, particularly when operations resulted in casualties among bystanders.

Kim's leadership style and political effectiveness have also drawn criticism. Historians studying the internal politics of the Korean Provisional Government note that Kim's expulsion of Syngman Rhee in 1925, while perhaps justified by Rhee's alleged misconduct, contributed to debilitating factionalism that weakened the exile government.[^72] Some scholars argue that Kim's strong personality and uncompromising positions, while admirable in their principled consistency, sometimes prevented the coalition-building and pragmatic compromise necessary for effective political leadership.

The 1896 killing of Tsuchida Josuke remains problematic for Kim's legacy. Critical scholars point out that regardless of Kim's sincere belief that he was targeting a military officer involved in regicide, the evidence suggests he killed a civilian merchant.[^73] Some argue that treating this as a justified act of resistance, despite the mistaken identity, sets a dangerous precedent for excusing violence based on perceived patriotic motivation rather than actual facts.

Revisionist historians examining the post-liberation period have questioned whether Kim's absolutist opposition to separate southern elections served Korean interests. These scholars argue that while Kim's desire for unified Korean independence was admirable, his failure to engage with the political reality of Soviet-American division and his refusal to participate in building southern democratic institutions left a vacuum filled by less democratic forces.[^74] Some suggest that more pragmatic engagement might have allowed Kim to shape South Korea's development rather than opposing it from the margins.

Finally, some scholars note that Kim's legacy benefits from his martyrdom through assassination, which prevents critical evaluation of how he might have governed had he achieved power. Questions about his administrative capability, his economic policies, and his potential authoritarianism remain unanswered, allowing his reputation to rest primarily on his undeniable courage and sacrifice as a resistance leader rather than on tested governance abilities.[^75]

## Famous Quotes

> "If God asked me what was my wish, I would reply unhesitatingly, 'Korean independence.' If he asked me what was my second wish, I would again answer, 'My country's independence.' If he asked me what was my third wish, I would reply in an even louder voice, 'My wish is the complete independence of my country, Korea.'" - From his autobiography "My Desire" (Baekbeom Ilji)[^76]

> "My fellow brethren. This is my only wish. I have lived seventy years of my life for this wish, am living my life for this wish, and will live my life only to fulfill this wish." - From "My Desire"[^77]

> "I want our nation to be the most beautiful in the world. By this, I do not mean the most powerful nation. Because I have felt the pain of being invaded by another nation, I do not want my nation to invade others." - From his autobiography "My Desire"[^78]

> "I chose the pen name Baekbeom, which means 'ordinary person,' hoping every ordinary Korean person would fight for the independence of Korea." - Reflecting on his name change[^79]

> "Korean independence is not just freedom from Japan, but the establishment of a truly sovereign nation where all Koreans, North and South, can live together as one people." - On his vision for reunification[^80]
