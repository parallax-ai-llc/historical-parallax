## Background and context

Thomas Sweatt is known primarily for a long-running series of arsons in Washington, D.C. Over many years, he repeatedly set fires that created substantial risk to residents and first responders, and the overall pattern became one of the city’s notable serial arson cases.[^1]

Public reporting summarizes the scope of the case as hundreds of separate fire-setting incidents over roughly two decades, with at least two fatalities attributed to the campaign.[^1]

## Pattern of offenses (high level)

Across serial arson investigations, authorities typically look for repeated similarities (targets, timing, entry methods, and ignition sources). In Sweatt’s case, the defining feature emphasized in summaries is the sheer volume of incidents over a sustained period, rather than a single one-off event.[^1]

Because arson scenes can be chaotic and evidence is often destroyed by fire suppression, long-running cases may depend on aggregating small investigative leads across many incidents, plus community tips and later corroborating evidence.[^1]

## Investigation and prosecution (overview)

By the time the case reached court, prosecutors presented the overall pattern as an extensive series of intentional fires. The legal outcome most commonly cited is his 2005 conviction connected to the broader set of arsons.[^1]

## Impact

Large-scale serial arson cases can have outsized civic impact: repeated incidents can strain fire department resources, increase insurance and rebuilding costs, and create chronic fear among local residents. When fires occur in occupied structures, the risk of injury or death increases sharply for both civilians and firefighters.[^1]
