## Summary

John O'Keefe is a neuroscientist who shared the 2014 Nobel Prize for discovering place cells in the hippocampus.

## Additional Context

- O'Keefe's best-known contribution is the identification of **place cells**—hippocampal neurons whose firing correlates with an animal's location in an environment.
- The finding helped establish the hippocampus as a key structure for **spatial memory** and **navigation**, linking neural activity to cognitive mapping.
- Place-cell work is often discussed alongside **grid cells** identified later in the entorhinal cortex, together describing a neural basis for representing space.
- The 2014 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine is commonly summarized as recognizing discoveries of a neural positioning system; O'Keefe shared the prize with **May-Britt Moser** and **Edvard I. Moser**.

## Why It Matters

- Place cells became foundational for research on how experiences are encoded and retrieved, because spatial context is tightly interwoven with episodic memory.
- The place-cell paradigm also influenced computational models of learning where an internal map supports planning and goal-directed behavior.
- Beyond neuroscience, the idea of a cognitive map is used as a cross-disciplinary metaphor for how agents build internal representations of the world.
