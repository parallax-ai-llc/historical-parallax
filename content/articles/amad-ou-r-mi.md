## Summary

Burkinabè-Ivorian warlord convicted of leading the Duékoué massacre that killed over 800 people during the 2010-2011 Ivorian crisis. He received a life sentence.

## Background

Amadé Ouérémi is described in public reporting as a **Burkinabè–Ivorian** militia leader/warlord linked to armed activity in western Côte d’Ivoire, an area that saw intense communal and political violence during the 2010–2011 crisis.[^1]

## The Duékoué Massacre (High-Level)

According to the account summarized in Wikipedia, Ouérémi was convicted for leading a mass killing in **Duékoué**, a town in western Côte d’Ivoire, during the unrest around the 2010–2011 post-election conflict.[^1]

Key high-level points often emphasized in summaries of the event:

- The reported death toll exceeded **800** people, making it one of the deadliest single episodes of the crisis.[^1]
- The violence is frequently discussed in the context of reprisals, inter-communal tensions, and the breakdown of state control in contested areas.[^1]

## Legal Proceedings and Sentence

Ouérémi’s case is notable because it reflects a domestic prosecution in Côte d’Ivoire connected to major atrocity crimes from the crisis period.[^1]

- He was tried and convicted by Ivorian authorities (as summarized in the reference) and received a **life sentence**.[^1]
- The conviction is commonly cited as part of the broader post-crisis effort—uneven and politically contested in public debate—to address serious crimes committed during the conflict.[^1]

## Historical Significance

The Duékoué violence remains a reference point in discussions of Côte d’Ivoire’s 2010–2011 crisis because it illustrates:

- how quickly political disputes escalated into mass violence,
- the role of armed groups and local power-brokers in shaping outcomes on the ground,
- and the long-term challenges of accountability, reconciliation, and restoring trust in affected communities.[^1]
