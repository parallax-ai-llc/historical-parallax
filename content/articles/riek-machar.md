## Summary

South Sudanese rebel leader and vice president whose rivalry with President Kiir triggered a civil war that killed 400,000 people. He was accused of directing ethnic massacres during the conflict.

## Political and Military Role

- Machar emerged as a senior figure in the Sudan People’s Liberation Movement/Army (SPLM/A) during the long conflict that preceded South Sudan’s independence.
- In 1991 he led a major split from the SPLA, a turning point that contributed to intra-southern fighting and has remained politically salient in South Sudanese narratives.

## Relationship with the Government of South Sudan

- After independence, Machar served as vice president under President Salva Kiir, but tensions within the ruling party escalated into open conflict in 2013.
- Multiple ceasefires and peace arrangements sought to reintegrate opposition forces and establish power-sharing, with Machar periodically returning to government positions and then leaving amid renewed violence.

## Peace Processes and Power-Sharing

- Regional mediation efforts (often associated with IGAD and neighboring states) produced agreements aimed at forming transitional governments and unifying armed forces.
- The 2020 return to the vice presidency was linked to an updated peace framework intended to end large-scale hostilities, though implementation has faced recurring delays and disputes.

## Allegations and Accountability

- International reporting and human-rights organizations have documented atrocities during the civil war, including ethnically targeted violence.
- Accusations against Machar and forces aligned with him have been part of broader debates about command responsibility and the difficulty of pursuing accountability in an active, fragmented conflict environment.

## Notes

- South Sudan’s civil war is often described as having both elite political roots and community-level dynamics, which complicates single-cause explanations focused only on individual leaders.
