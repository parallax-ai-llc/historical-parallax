## Summary

Choe Nam-seon (최남선, 1890-1957) was a pioneering Korean historian, poet, publisher, and political activist who played a dual role in modern Korean history. He authored Korea's first modern poem, coined the term "Hangul," founded the nation's first modern magazine, and drafted the Korean Declaration of Independence in 1919, becoming a leading figure in the independence movement.[^1][^2] However, his legacy remains deeply controversial: after joining the Japanese colonial government's Korean History Compilation Committee in 1928 and delivering pro-Japanese speeches during the 1940s, he became condemned as a collaborator while simultaneously being respected for his contributions to Korean language modernization and historical scholarship.[^3][^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Choe Nam-seon was born on April 26, 1890, in Seoul, Joseon Dynasty, into a chungin (중인) family—a social class positioned between the aristocratic yangban and commoners.[^5] This middle-class background provided him access to education while positioning him outside the traditional elite power structures. He received his early education in Seoul during the twilight years of the Joseon Dynasty, a period of profound upheaval as Korea faced increasing foreign pressures and modernization challenges.[^6]

In 1904, at age fourteen, Choe received a government scholarship to study in Japan, where he enrolled at the First Tokyo Middle School (now Hibiya High School).[^7] His time in Japan profoundly influenced his worldview, as he witnessed firsthand the transformative effects of the Meiji Restoration on Japanese society.[^8] However, his education was interrupted in 1905 when he organized a protest among Korean students against the signing of the Eulsa Treaty, which effectively made Korea a protectorate of Imperial Japan and stripped it of diplomatic sovereignty.[^9] The protest led to his expulsion from the school, and he returned temporarily to Korea.[^10]

Undeterred, Choe returned to Tokyo in 1906 and matriculated at Waseda University's School of Secondary Education, majoring in geography and history.[^11] At Waseda, he was exposed to Western educational methodologies and modern historical scholarship, which would profoundly shape his later work. During this period, he absorbed ideas from Chinese historian Liang Qichao's version of Social Darwinism, which influenced nationalist journalist-historians of his generation.[^12] This intellectual foundation would inform his lifelong mission to modernize Korean culture and raise national consciousness through literature, publishing, and historical research.[^13]

## Rise to Prominence

Upon returning from Japan, Choe Nam-seon immediately set about transforming Korean intellectual culture. In November 1908, at just eighteen years old, he founded Sonyeon (소년, "Youth"), Korea's first successful modern magazine.[^14] Published by his newly established Sinmunkwan (新文館) publishing house, Sonyeon represented a revolutionary departure from traditional Korean publications, seeking to bring modern knowledge about the world to Korea's youth through accessible vernacular Korean rather than classical Chinese.[^15][^16]

The inaugural issue of Sonyeon contained Choe's poem "Hae egeso Sonyeon ege" (해에게서 소년에게, "From the Ocean to the Youth"), widely recognized as the first "new-style" poem in Korean literature.[^17] This groundbreaking work pioneered modern Korean poetry by abandoning traditional forms in favor of free verse that captured modern sensibilities. Through Sonyeon and its successor publication Cheongchun (靑春, "Youth," 1914-1918), Choe translated Western classics such as Gulliver's Travels and Robinson Crusoe, often working from Japanese translations of the original texts.[^18][^19]

Choe's literary innovations extended beyond poetry and translation. He sought to create a new style of literary Korean that would be accessible to ordinary people, democratizing knowledge that had previously been confined to the educated elite.[^20] Most significantly, he coined the term "Hangul" (한글) for the Korean alphabet and vigorously promoted it as a literary medium through his magazines, giving the Korean writing system a distinct identity separate from Chinese characters.[^21] In 1910, he founded the Association for Korea's Glorious Literature, which encouraged ordinary Koreans to read the classics of Korean literature.[^22] He also revitalized the traditional Korean poetic form sijo and initiated compilation of the first dictionary of the Korean language.[^23]

By the late 1910s, Choe had established himself as one of the three great geniuses of his generation, alongside Yi Kwang-su and Hong Myung-hee.[^24] He became a central figure in the Patriotic Enlightenment Movement, which sought to modernize Korea and raise national consciousness as a foundation for eventual independence.[^25] This activism would culminate in his most consequential political act: drafting the Korean Declaration of Independence in 1919.[^26]

## Major Achievements

### The March First Movement and Korean Declaration of Independence

Choe Nam-seon's most celebrated achievement came in 1919 when he authored the Korean Declaration of Independence, the foundational document of the March First Movement.[^27] Working with Choe Rin, who coordinated with Presbyterian elder Lee Seung-hoon and religious leaders, Choe Nam-seon agreed to draft the text at Son Byong-hi's request.[^28] He completed the declaration in just a couple of days at age twenty-nine.[^29]

The declaration professed the right of the Korean people to determine their own national existence and called for independence from Japanese colonial rule.[^30] Critically, Choe believed the document needed to be moderate in content and peaceful in its appeal to gain sympathy from Japan and the Western powers meeting at the Paris Peace Conference in Versailles.[^31] This strategic moderation contrasted with the more provocative February 8 Declaration drafted by Korean students in Tokyo.[^32]

Though Choe did not actually sign the declaration himself, his authorship made him a target of Japanese authorities.[^33] On March 1, 1919, the declaration was publicly read, sparking nationwide demonstrations involving millions of Koreans in a peaceful protest for independence. For his role in drafting this historic document, Japanese authorities arrested Choe and sentenced him to imprisonment for two and a half years, from March 1919 to October 1921.[^34][^35]

### Literary and Cultural Contributions

Choe's writings spanned poetry, song lyrics, travelogues, literary criticism, social criticism, and an extensive body of historical works.[^36] As the pioneer of modern Korean poetry, his influence on Korean literature cannot be overstated. He was the only publisher of Korean literary magazines of his time and a pioneering publisher of daily newspapers and magazines for both young people and adults.[^37]

His publishing house Sinmunkwan served as a crucial vehicle for modernization, featuring numerous translations of Japanese publications and, through them, Western works.[^38] These translations were not merely linguistic exercises but active attempts to enlighten the Korean people and preserve Korean culture during the colonial period.[^39] Through these efforts, Choe made modern ideas, literature, and knowledge accessible to Koreans who would otherwise have remained isolated from global intellectual currents.

### Historical Scholarship

Choe devoted his entire life to compiling, revising, and annotating classical Korean works, particularly historical texts but also classics of Korean literature.[^40] His approach to Korean history was innovative and nationalist. Believing the historical record Samguk Sagi was too Confucian and Sino-centric, he chose to draw his history from Samguk Yusa, a collection of folktales, stories, and legends previously disregarded by historians.[^41][^42]

Choe developed what he called the "Purham culture theory," analyzing the mythological story of Dangun's birth from a she-bear to argue that the legend originated from an ancient culture with shamanistic roots.[^43] According to this theory, Dangun should be placed even above the Chinese and Japanese emperors, as those rulers were supposedly shamanistic rulers descended from the ancient Korean "Părk" tradition.[^44] When Japanese scholars Shiratori Kurakichi and Imanishi Ryū dismissed Dangun as a fabrication, Choe attacked Japanese mythology as being built upon fabrications itself.[^45]

After his release from prison in 1950 following a suspended collaboration trial, Choe published many historical works, including Korean People's History, Korea's Culture, and Korea's Mountains and Rivers.[^46] In 1954, he published a new edition of Samguk Yusa with revised scholarly interpretations,[^47] and in 1955 he edited the Korean Historical Dictionary.[^48] During the Korean War, he served on the Naval History Committee, and after the war, he served on the Seoul City History Committee until his death.[^49]

## Controversies and Criticisms

### Collaboration with Japanese Colonial Authorities

The most damaging aspect of Choe Nam-seon's legacy stems from his collaboration with Japanese colonial authorities, particularly during the 1940s. In 1928, lulled by promises of a house and research funds from the Governor-General of Korea, Choe joined the Korean History Compilation Committee, established by the Japanese colonial government in June 1925.[^50][^51] This committee was commissioned to compile the history of Korea under Japanese supervision, and Choe's participation in it represented a profound betrayal to many Korean nationalists.

The decision had immediate social consequences. Former friends and fellow independence activists Yi Kwang-su, Han Yong-un, and Hong Myung-hee cut ties with him after he joined the committee.[^52] While Choe argued he could use his position to refute Japanese imperialist interpretations of ancient Korean history by demonstrating that ancient Korea was not an impoverished backwater but rather the center of a vibrant civilization,[^53] his critics saw his participation as lending legitimacy to the colonial apparatus.

Choe's collaboration intensified during the 1940s as Japan prosecuted total war in Asia and the Pacific. From 1937 until Korea's liberation in 1945, he wrote many pro-Japanese speeches and articles.[^54] Most damningly, during the 1940s he delivered a speech to Korean students studying in Japan calling Britain and America "the most deadly enemies of Asians," urged students to support the war against the "Anglo-Saxons," and praised Japanese imperialism, stating that Koreans were fortunate to be colonized by Japan.[^55] He showed particular enthusiasm by personally visiting Japan to encourage Korean students living there to become soldiers for the Imperial Japanese Army.[^56]

South Korean historian Hwang Kyung Moon noted the striking contrast between Choe the passionate patriot who penned the Declaration of Independence in 1919 and Choe the collaborator of 1943 urging Korean university students to enlist in the Imperial Japanese Army and die for the Emperor of Japan.[^57] Hwang suggested this transformation was caused by the assumed "permanence" of Japanese rule by the early 1940s, as every attempt to win independence had ended in failure.[^58] Given this situation, Koreans like Choe had lost their youthful idealism and abandoned their dreams of freedom, simply hoping to reach an accommodation with the Japanese that might preserve some Korean cultural identity.[^59]

### Post-Liberation Trial and Controversy

After Korea's liberation in 1945, the question of how to handle collaborators became contentious. In 1949, Syngman Rhee's government arrested Choe for collaboration with the Japanese during the colonial period.[^60] However, the trial was suspended and he was released, reportedly due to illness.[^61] This incomplete reckoning with his wartime activities allowed Choe to resume historical work, but the stain on his reputation remained permanent.

### Scholarly Criticisms

Beyond political controversies, Choe faced scholarly criticism for his historical methodology. Historian Hong Isŏp (1914-1974) delivered a particularly harsh assessment, stating that "there was nothing unique or original in Yuktang's [Choe Nam-seon's] historical works because he merely borrowed from Japanese works."[^62] The resemblances between Choe's work and that of Japanese scholars like Torii Ryūzō and Shiratori Kurakichi were so pronounced that they raised questions about his originality.[^63]

However, defenders note that Choe was not the only Korean intellectual who referred to or was influenced by Japanese academia, as engaging with Japanese scholarship was inevitably necessary to offer resistance to the colonizer's stance.[^64] Choe's reputation as a Korean historian remains controversial due to his early nationalist studies on Dangun and his later contributions heavily influenced by Japanese views.[^65]

## Personal Life

Choe Nam-seon came from a chungin family background, which shaped his perspective as someone positioned between Korea's traditional aristocracy and common people.[^66] His eldest son was Choi Hak-joo, who later wrote a scholarly book about his grandfather titled "Yuktang Ch'oe Nam-son and Korean Modernity."[^67]

As a historian and intellectual, Choe held distinctive views on the nature of historical scholarship itself. He believed that "History cannot and should not be regarded as a science with no heart because it is learning for the sake of the people. Historical studies should have purpose and emotion behind them."[^68] This methodological stance reflected his nationalist commitment but also drew criticism from those who advocated for more objective historical research.

In 1906, Choe demonstrated his passionate nationalism when he wrote: "How long will it take us to accomplish the goal of flying our sacred national flag above the world and having people of the five continents kneeling down before it? Exert yourselves, our youth!"[^69] This youthful idealism would later give way to a more complex and compromised relationship with Korean independence.

Despite his later collaboration, Choe maintained his commitment to Korean cultural preservation throughout his life. After the 1910 Japanese annexation, he honestly acknowledged Korea's intellectual challenges, writing: "Our conscience is not numb enough to claim that the 'Ch'unhyangjŏn and Simch'ǒngjŏn are as great as Faust, Hamlet, Les Miserables, and The Divine Comedy."[^70] This self-critical assessment revealed his understanding of the cultural work needed to elevate Korean literature to world standards.

Choe struggled with diabetes and hypertension in his later years.[^71] He died of a cerebral hemorrhage on October 10, 1957, in Seoul, at the age of 67.[^72][^73] His death came just a few years after publishing his revised edition of Samguk Yusa in 1954 and editing the Korean Historical Dictionary in 1955, demonstrating his continued scholarly productivity until the end of his life.[^74]

## Legacy

Choe Nam-seon's legacy remains profoundly divided, embodying the complex moral compromises that characterized Korea's colonial period. He is simultaneously celebrated as a pioneer of Korean modernization and condemned as a collaborator who betrayed the cause he once championed.[^75]

### Cultural and Linguistic Impact

Choe's contributions to Korean language and literature remain undeniable. By coining the term "Hangul" and promoting the Korean alphabet as a literary medium, he helped establish a distinct identity for Korean writing separate from Chinese characters.[^76] His pioneering work in modern Korean poetry influenced generations of writers, and his efforts to create an accessible literary Korean democratized knowledge that had previously been confined to elites.[^77]

His publishing ventures, particularly Sonyeon magazine, introduced Korean youth to modern ideas and global knowledge at a critical juncture in Korean history.[^78] Through translations of Western classics and promotion of Korean literary heritage, he helped bridge traditional Korean culture and modern global intellectual currents.[^79] These contributions to Korean cultural modernization represent an enduring positive legacy.

### Historical Scholarship

Choe's historical work on Dangun and ancient Korean civilization contributed to nationalist historiography that emerged among early twentieth-century Korean intellectuals seeking to foster national consciousness.[^80] His choice to elevate Samguk Yusa, with its folktales and legends, over more conventional historical records represented an innovative approach that valued cultural memory alongside documentary evidence.[^81] During his time on the Korean History Compilation Committee, he attempted—with limited success—to refute Japanese imperialist interpretations by arguing that ancient Korea was the center of a vibrant civilization rather than a backward state.[^82]

However, his historical methodology faced substantial criticism. His heavy reliance on Japanese scholarship and the resemblances between his work and that of Japanese scholars undermined claims to originality.[^83] His participation in the Japanese colonial government's history project, regardless of his intentions, lent legitimacy to the colonial apparatus and damaged the credibility of his historical arguments.

### The Collaboration Question

The most contentious aspect of Choe's legacy remains his wartime collaboration. The transformation from independence movement leader to someone urging Korean students to die for the Japanese Emperor represents one of the most dramatic falls from nationalist grace in modern Korean history.[^84] While some historians contextualize this shift within the assumed permanence of Japanese rule and the loss of hope for independence by the 1940s,[^85] others view it as an unforgivable betrayal that cannot be excused by historical circumstances.

The incomplete resolution of collaboration through the suspended 1949 trial meant that Korea never fully reckoned with Choe's wartime activities.[^86] This unfinished business contributes to ongoing debates about how to evaluate figures who made both significant contributions and serious moral compromises during the colonial period.

### Contemporary Assessment

Today, Choe Nam-seon remains a deeply controversial figure in Korea, respected for his historical work and efforts to create the modern Korean language while condemned for his wartime statements supporting Japan.[^87] He represents the moral ambiguities that characterized Korea's colonial experience, where intellectuals faced impossible choices between resistance, accommodation, and collaboration. His life demonstrates how nationalist idealism could give way to pragmatic compromise, and how individuals who contributed significantly to national culture could also betray national aspirations.

The ongoing controversy surrounding Choe reflects broader questions about how to evaluate historical figures who defy simple categorization as heroes or villains. His legacy forces Koreans to grapple with uncomfortable truths about collaboration, cultural preservation under colonialism, and the personal costs of prolonged foreign domination.[^88]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars who emphasize Choe's contributions focus on his pioneering role in Korean modernization and cultural development. His creation of Korea's first modern magazine, Sonyeon, introduced an entire generation to modern ideas and helped forge a new Korean literary language accessible to ordinary people.[^89] By coining the term "Hangul" and promoting vernacular Korean literature, he contributed fundamentally to establishing Korean linguistic and cultural identity in the modern era.[^90]

Supporters note that his historical work on Dangun and ancient Korean civilization, despite methodological criticisms, contributed to nationalist consciousness during a critical period when Korean identity faced erasure under Japanese colonialism.[^91] His efforts to elevate Samguk Yusa and Korean cultural traditions represented important cultural preservation work that might otherwise have been lost.[^92]

Some scholars contextualize his later collaboration within the impossible circumstances Korean intellectuals faced under total colonial domination. They argue that by the 1940s, after decades of failed independence attempts, many Koreans believed Japanese rule was permanent and sought to preserve what Korean cultural identity they could within that framework.[^93] From this perspective, Choe's participation in the Korean History Compilation Committee represented an attempt, however compromised, to maintain some Korean historical narrative against complete Japanese revisionism.[^94]

His grandson Choi Hak-joo's scholarly work on Choe's life and contributions to Korean modernity represents an effort to rehabilitate his reputation by emphasizing his role in Korea's transition to modernity rather than focusing exclusively on wartime collaboration.[^95] Defenders point to his lifelong devotion to compiling, revising, and annotating classical Korean works as evidence of sustained commitment to Korean cultural preservation.[^96]

### Negative Views

Critics emphasize that Choe's collaboration cannot be excused by historical circumstances or offset by earlier contributions. Historian Hong Isŏp's assessment that "there was nothing unique or original in Yuktang's historical works because he merely borrowed from Japanese works" represents a fundamental scholarly repudiation of his historical methodology.[^97] This criticism suggests that even Choe's ostensibly nationalist historical work was derivative and overly dependent on Japanese scholarship.[^98]

The transformation from drafting the Korean Declaration of Independence in 1919 to urging Korean students to die for the Japanese Emperor in 1943 represents, for many critics, an unforgivable betrayal that nullifies earlier contributions.[^99] South Korean historian Hwang Kyung Moon's emphasis on this "striking contrast" highlights the moral inconsistency that critics find indefensible.[^100] The fact that Choe personally visited Japan to encourage Korean students to enlist demonstrates active collaboration rather than passive accommodation.[^101]

Critics also note that his decision to join the Korean History Compilation Committee in 1928 was motivated by material incentives—promises of a house and research funds—rather than noble intentions.[^102] This mercenary aspect of his collaboration undermines claims that he sought to resist Japanese interpretations from within the system. The immediate social consequences—Yi Kwang-su, Han Yong-un, and Hong Myung-hee cutting ties with him—demonstrate that his contemporaries recognized the betrayal his decision represented.[^103]

The incomplete reckoning with collaboration through the suspended 1949 trial means Choe never faced full accountability for his wartime activities.[^104] Critics argue this reflects a broader failure to adequately address collaboration and suggests that Choe's rehabilitation has been too generous given the severity of his betrayal of Korean independence.

> "How long will it take us to accomplish the goal of flying our sacred national flag above the world and having people of the five continents kneeling down before it? Exert yourselves, our youth!" - From a 1906 essay, expressing his early nationalist idealism and hopes for Korea's future independence and global recognition[^105]

> "Our conscience is not numb enough to claim that the 'Ch'unhyangjŏn and Simch'ǒngjŏn are as great as Faust, Hamlet, Les Miserables, and The Divine Comedy." - After the 1910 Japanese annexation, honestly assessing Korea's intellectual standing and the need for cultural development[^106]
