## Summary

Han So-hee (born November 18, 1994) is a South Korean actress who rose from minor television roles to become one of her generation's most compelling dramatic performers. Known for her intense screen presence and willingness to take on psychologically complex characters, she has earned recognition across both mainstream commercial productions and critically acclaimed series.

Her breakthrough came with the 2020 JTBC drama *The World of the Married*, in which she played a home-wrecker opposite Kim Hee-ae. The role demanded emotional range far beyond her experience at the time, and she delivered it with a raw authenticity that drew widespread attention. Since then, she has become one of the most sought-after actresses in Korean television and film.

Han So-hee represents a broader shift in Korean entertainment toward female leads who are morally ambiguous, emotionally volatile, and narratively dominant — characters that earlier industry conventions would have reserved for male actors.

## Early Life & Background

Han So-hee was born on November 18, 1994, in Ulsan, South Korea, and later moved to Seoul. She has spoken publicly about a difficult upbringing marked by financial instability; her mother accumulated significant gambling debts, a situation Han So-hee has addressed in interviews with candor. She worked part-time jobs throughout her teenage years to support herself, including stints in cafes and retail.

She pursued modeling before transitioning to acting, appearing in music videos and commercials in her late teens. She signed with 9ato Entertainment and made her acting debut in 2017 with a minor role in the MBC drama *Money Flower*.

## Rise to Prominence

Following her 2017 debut, Han So-hee took on small supporting roles in *100 Days My Prince* (2018) and *Abyss* (2019), steadily building experience. The watershed moment came in 2020 when she was cast as Yeo Da-kyung in *The World of the Married* (JTBC), the highest-rated drama in Korean cable television history at the time of its broadcast, peaking at 28.37% viewership. Her portrayal of a young woman who becomes entangled in an extramarital affair was nuanced enough to attract sympathy despite the character's role as antagonist.

She followed this with the lead role in *Nevertheless* (2021, JTBC), a romance drama in which she played a fine arts student navigating an emotionally ambiguous relationship. The show generated substantial discussion about contemporary dating dynamics in South Korea.

In 2022, she starred in *My Name* (Netflix), an action thriller in which she played a woman who infiltrates the police to avenge her father's murder. The physically demanding role required months of martial arts and stunt training, and the series performed strongly on Netflix's global charts upon release.

## Key Achievements

- *The World of the Married* (2020) set a record as the highest-rated Korean cable drama at the time, peaking at 28.37% nationally.
- *My Name* (2022, Netflix) debuted in Netflix's global top 10 non-English series in multiple regions.
- Named one of Forbes Korea's Power Celebrity list in 2021 and 2022.
- Won the Top Excellence Award at the 2020 JTBC Drama Awards for *The World of the Married*.

## Controversies & Criticism

In early 2024, Han So-hee became the subject of intense media scrutiny following a public exchange with actor Ryu Jun-yeol and actress Hyeri surrounding a reported romantic relationship. Her handling of the situation on social media — including direct posts and subsequent deletions — drew criticism for its tone and transparency. She later acknowledged that her responses had been poorly managed and expressed regret over the public nature of the dispute.

She has also faced occasional criticism for typecasting concerns, with some observers noting that many of her roles share similar traits: young women in chaotic, emotionally destabilizing situations.

## Legacy & Influence

Han So-hee has contributed to a redefinition of what commercially successful female-led Korean drama can look like. Her roles consistently foreground female interiority — desire, grief, rage — rather than subordinating those emotions to male-centered plots. *My Name* in particular helped establish a template for action-forward female protagonist stories on Korean streaming platforms.

Her career arc — from supporting villain to Netflix action lead in under five years — is frequently cited in Korean entertainment journalism as evidence of how rapidly the streaming era has accelerated actress career trajectories.
