## 개요

Francis 콰메 은크루마 was 가나's founding father and the first Prime Minister and President of 가나, leading the nation from independence in 1957 until his overthrow in 1966.[^1] A passionate advocate of Pan-아프리카nism, he was instrumental in establishing the Organization of 아프리카n Unity and championed the cause of 아프리카n liberation across the continent.[^2] His government pursued ambitious industrialization projects and promoted 아프리카n unity, but also became increasingly authoritarian, employing preventive detention laws and establishing a one-party state.[^3] Nkrumah died in exile in Bucharest, 루마니아 in 1972, but remains one of the most influential 아프리카n leaders of the twentieth century.[^4]

## 초기 생애와 배경

Francis 콰메 은크루마 was born on September 21, 1909, in the small coastal village of Nkroful in the British colony of the Gold Coast, though his mother, Elizabeth Nyanibah (1877-1979), later stated his year of birth as 1912.[^5] His father, Kofi Ngonloma, was a goldsmith from the Akan tribe who maintained a polygamous household with many wives and children.[^6] Despite being his mother's only child, Nkrumah grew up in a large extended family of fourteen people, including children of his father by other wives.[^7] His mother, Elizabeth Nyaniba, hailed from Nsuaem and belonged to the Agona family, working as a fishmonger and petty trader.[^8]

According to historian Ebenezer Obiri Addo, the name "Nkrumah"—a name traditionally given to a ninth child—indicates that Kwame probably held that place in the house of his father.[^9] Although he was christened Francis Nwia Kofi, his 아프리카n name Kwame is indicative of the day on which he was born, as was the local custom.[^10] He was baptized a Roman Catholic, and at his mother's insistence attended the nearby Roman Catholic mission schools at Esima and Sekondi-Takoradi.[^11]

Nkrumah excelled academically and was sent to the Government Training School in Accra, subsequently graduating from Achimota College in 1930, where he was trained as a teacher.[^12] After graduation, he taught at Catholic junior schools and a seminary until 1935.[^13] While at school, Nkrumah met Kwegyir Aggrey, the institution's first 아프리카n staff member, who profoundly influenced the young student and guided his mind toward the issues of 아프리카n liberation to which he would later devote his life.[^14]

In 1935, Nkrumah traveled to the 미국 under a small scholarship to attend Lincoln University in Pennsylvania, marking the beginning of a transformative period in his intellectual and political development.[^15] These were financially challenging times for Nkrumah, and he had to take on menial and uncomfortable jobs to support himself and supplement his grant.[^16] He graduated with a Bachelor of Arts in economics and sociology from Lincoln in 1939, and gained a Bachelor of Theology degree from the same institution in 1942, finishing as the top student in the course.[^17] He subsequently earned a Master of Arts degree in philosophy and a Master of Science in education from the University of Pennsylvania in 1943.[^18]

During his time in the 미국, Nkrumah spent his summers in Harlem, then a vibrant center of Black life, thought, and culture, where he found housing and employment with difficulty and deeply involved himself in the community.[^19] This experience in Harlem, combined with his formal education, shaped his understanding of racial oppression, colonialism, and the potential for Pan-아프리카n solidarity that would become central to his political philosophy.[^20]

## 권력의 부상

After twelve formative years abroad pursuing higher education, developing his political philosophy, and organizing with other diasporic Pan-아프리카nists, Nkrumah returned to the Gold Coast in 1947 to begin his political career as an advocate of national independence.[^21] In 1945, before his return to 아프리카, Nkrumah had played a central role in organizing the Fifth Pan-아프리카n 의회 in Manchester, alongside George Padmore, establishing himself as a significant figure in the Pan-아프리카n movement.[^22]

Nkrumah's activism and organizational abilities attracted the attention of 가나ian politician J.B. Danquah, who hired him to serve as general secretary of the United Gold Coast Convention (UGCC), an organization pursuing independence for the British colony.[^23] However, ideological differences between Nkrumah and the conservative UGCC leadership soon emerged.[^24] The UGCC leaders favored a gradual approach to independence, while Nkrumah advocated for immediate self-government.[^25]

These differences led Nkrumah to break away and found his own party, the Convention People's Party (CPP), in 1949.[^26] The Convention People's Party achieved rapid success through its unprecedented appeal to the common voter, employing populist slogans and mass mobilization techniques that contrasted sharply with the elite-focused approach of the UGCC.[^27] Nkrumah and the CPP sought self-government through the nonviolent strategy of "positive action," which employed the tactics of protest and strikes against the colonial administration.[^28]

In 1950, Nkrumah was imprisoned after supporting a general strike that turned violent, with colonial authorities viewing him as a dangerous agitator.[^29] However, his imprisonment only enhanced his popularity among the 가나ian masses.[^30] In the 1951 general election, held while Nkrumah was still in prison, the CPP won a stunning victory, capturing 34 out of 38 seats.[^31] The British colonial governor, faced with this overwhelming mandate, had little choice but to release Nkrumah from prison and invite him to form a government.[^32]

Nkrumah served as Leader of Government Business and then Prime Minister of the Gold Coast from 1952 until 1957.[^33] During this period, he negotiated with British authorities for full independence, while simultaneously building up the CPP's organization and preparing the country for self-rule.[^34] On March 6, 1957, 가나 finally secured its independence from 영국, becoming the first sub-사하라n 아프리카n colony to achieve independence.[^35] Nkrumah became the new nation's first 총리, declaring in his independence speech that "the independence of 가나 is meaningless unless it is linked up with the total liberation of the 아프리카n continent."[^36]

In 1960, 가나 became a republic, and Nkrumah transitioned from 총리 to president, consolidating his power and embarking on an ambitious program of economic development and Pan-아프리카n leadership.[^37]

## 주요 업적

Nkrumah's most significant achievement was leading 가나 to independence, making it the first sub-사하라n 아프리카n nation to break free from colonial rule and inspiring independence movements across the continent.[^38] His success demonstrated that 아프리카n self-rule was viable and provided a template for other independence struggles.[^39] As 가나's leader, he pursued an ambitious program of modernization and industrialization designed to transform the country's colonial economy into a self-sufficient, industrialized state.[^40]

Between 1961 and 1966, Nkrumah oversaw the construction of an ambitious and costly hydroelectric project on the Volta River, which was highly successful and provided electricity for industrial development and improved living standards.[^41] The Akosombo Dam created Lake Volta, the world's largest artificial reservoir by surface area, and generated hydroelectric power that became the foundation for 가나's aluminum industry and broader industrial development.[^42]

In 1964, Nkrumah brought forth the Seven Year Development Plan for National Reconstruction and Development, which identified education as a key source of development and called for the expansion of secondary technical schools.[^43] His government invested heavily in education, establishing new schools and universities and dramatically expanding access to education for ordinary 가나ians.[^44] In 1961, Nkrumah laid the first stones in the foundation of the 콰메 은크루마 Ideological Institute, created to train 가나ian civil servants and promote Pan-아프리카nism.[^45]

Nkrumah's greatest passion and perhaps his most enduring legacy was his advocacy for Pan-아프리카nism and 아프리카n unity.[^46] He was an influential advocate of Pan-아프리카nism and a founding member of the Organization of 아프리카n Unity (OAU), established in 1963.[^47] Nkrumah won the Lenin Peace Prize from the 소련 in 1962 in recognition of his anti-colonial and peace advocacy.[^48] He was the driving force at the 1945 Pan-아프리카n Conference in Manchester, and after 가나's independence, he spearheaded the Pan-아프리카n movement with two major Accra Conferences in 1958: the Conference of Independent 아프리카n States in April and the All-아프리카n People's Conference in December.[^49]

Nkrumah championed the end of colonial rule not just in 가나 but across the continent, offering generous assistance to other 아프리카n nationalist movements and providing haven for freedom fighters from other 아프리카n countries.[^50] President Sekou Toure of Guinea accepted Nkrumah's proposal for a 가나-Guinea Union in November 1958, and they made a joint declaration in May 1959 that the union of their two countries was to be the nucleus of a future "Union of Independent 아프리카n States" open to any 아프리카n state.[^51]

Although he became disenchanted with the Organisation for 아프리카n Unity, which adopted a more gradualist approach than he preferred, Nkrumah advocated for the creation of an 아프리카n common market, a common currency, and a central continental bank.[^52] He pursued a policy of nonalignment with both the 미국 and the USSR, famously declaring that 가나 looked "neither East nor West" but forward.[^53]

Nkrumah was also a significant political theorist who contributed important concepts to anti-colonial thought.[^54] He has a substantial legacy as a political theorist and is credited with being the first to apply the term "neo-colonialism" to the experience of 아프리카 in the twentieth century.[^55] In his influential work "Neo-colonialism: The Last Stage of Imperialism," Nkrumah argued that neo-colonialism manifested as an unequal economic system where the economic interests of 아프리카ns were subservient to those of international finance, extraction industries, and geopolitics.[^56] He became a passionate advocate of the "아프리카n Personality," embodied in the slogan "아프리카 for the 아프리카ns," and viewed political independence as a prerequisite for economic independence.[^57]

## 논란과 비판

Despite his achievements, Nkrumah's government became increasingly authoritarian, implementing policies that severely restricted civil liberties and political opposition.[^58] The most controversial measure was the Preventive Detention Act of 1958, which legalized the imprisonment without trial of those the government regarded as security risks.[^59] More specifically, the act allowed for incarceration for up to five years without charge or trial, with only Nkrumah empowered to release prisoners early.[^60] According to Nkrumah's biographer David Birmingham, "no single measure did more to bring down Nkrumah's reputation than his adoption of internment without trial."[^61]

His detractors deeply resent the fact that he imposed a one-party state and passed laws that landed his opponents in jail.[^62] In 1964, a constitutional amendment made 가나 a one-party state, with Nkrumah as president for life of both the nation and its party.[^63] He authorized the indefinite preventive detention of dissidents, took control of the judiciary, and promoted ideological indoctrination.[^64] These authoritarian measures were justified by Nkrumah as necessary to protect the revolution and prevent neo-colonial interference, but they fundamentally undermined democratic governance and 인권.[^65]

Critics accuse him of progressively running down 가나's economic gains at independence, gagging the press, curtailing freedom of speech, and being authoritarian.[^66] 콰메 은크루마's ambitious industrialization projects strained 가나's economy, with chronic food shortages and corruption scandals inspiring general strikes and several assassination attempts on Nkrumah.[^67] Historians consider 가나 under Nkrumah and the CPP the exemplar narrative of the folly of rapidly industrializing economies in post-independence 아프리카.[^68]

These historians agree that Nkrumah's economic program was over-reaching given the severe lack of 가나's infrastructure and the paucity of foreign capital investment.[^69] In the latter stages of his rule and in the early years after his overthrow, Nkrumah was decried for his government's growing authoritarianism and for the failure of his excessively ambitious domestic policies.[^70] Critics denounced what they saw as his improvisational and chaotic adoption of socialism, along with the distraction and expense of his precipitous drive for 아프리카n unity.[^71]

Nkrumah's personality cult also drew criticism, with statues erected in his honor, his image appearing on currency and stamps, and elaborate celebrations of his birthday.[^72] Schools and institutions were named after him, and criticism of the president became increasingly dangerous.[^73] After alleged assassination plots against him, coupled with increasingly difficult local economic conditions, Nkrumah's government became even more authoritarian in the 1960s, as he repressed political opposition and conducted elections that were neither free nor fair.[^74]

The corruption within his government became notorious, with members of the CPP enriching themselves while ordinary 가나ians faced economic hardship.[^75] The combination of economic mismanagement, authoritarianism, corruption, and the suppression of dissent ultimately created the conditions for his overthrow in 1966.[^76]

## 개인 생활

콰메 은크루마 married Fathia Ritzk, an 이집트ian Coptic bank worker and former teacher, on New Year's Eve, 1957-1958, the evening of her arrival in 가나.[^77] Fathia Nkrumah, born on February 23, 1931, served as the First Lady of 가나 during Nkrumah's presidency.[^78] The marriage was reportedly arranged to strengthen ties between 가나 and Arab 아프리카, and Fathia had to navigate the complex role of being first lady in a newly independent 아프리카n nation.[^79]

As a married couple, Fathia and Nkrumah had three children: Gamal (born 1958), Samia (born 1960), and Sekou (born 1964).[^80] Gamal became a newspaper journalist, while Samia and Sekou pursued careers in politics.[^81] Samia Nkrumah notably served as chairperson of the Convention People's Party and as a member of 가나's parliament, continuing her father's political legacy.[^82]

Nkrumah also had another son, Francis, a pediatrician born in 1935.[^83] Professor Francis Nkrumah was the only child of his relationship with a Euro-아프리카n woman, Fanny Miller, whom he briefly married before furthering his studies in the 영국.[^84] This earlier relationship and child were generally kept out of public discussion during Nkrumah's political career.[^85]

Nkrumah was known for his intellectual intensity and dedication to his political vision.[^86] He was an avid reader and writer who produced numerous books and pamphlets on political theory, 아프리카n liberation, and neo-colonialism.[^87] His works include "가나: The Autobiography of 콰메 은크루마" (1957), "I Speak of Freedom" (1961), "아프리카 Must Unite" (1963), "Consciencism" (1964), and "Neo-Colonialism: The Last Stage of Imperialism" (1965).[^88]

Despite his public persona as a confident and charismatic leader, Nkrumah faced multiple assassination attempts that reportedly made him increasingly paranoid and isolated in his later years in power.[^89] His workaholic tendencies and the immense pressures of leading a new nation while championing Pan-아프리카nism across the continent took a personal toll.[^90] After his overthrow, his family joined him in exile in Guinea, where they lived under the protection of President Sekou Toure until Nkrumah's death.[^91]

## 유산

콰메 은크루마's legacy remains profoundly influential and contested across 아프리카 and the 아프리카n diaspora.[^92] In 1999, he was voted BBC 아프리카n of the millennium, a recognition of his towering importance in twentieth-century 아프리카n history.[^93] Pan-아프리카nism was what Nkrumah passionately and consistently worked for throughout his life, and this remains his most important political and intellectual legacy.[^94]

Nkrumah's most important political legacy lies in his vision of Continental Union Government of 아프리카 or Pan-아프리카nism, which is not only a political legacy seeking to empower 아프리카ns and the 아프리카n continent but an intellectual one.[^95] As arguably the most important individual behind the creation of the OAU, Nkrumah's influence helped pave the way for increasing acceptance of colonial-era borders and shaped the legal framework for 아프리카n interstate relations.[^96] The transformation of the OAU into the 아프리카n Union appears to have re-ignited Nkrumah's vision of a long-term transformation of the inter-아프리카n system into a confederated supranational unit.[^97]

Nkrumah shaped the law of self-determination in 아프리카 by actively campaigning against "tribalism" in 가나, enlisting the UN to prevent the secession of Katanga in 1960, and playing a leading role in establishing the OAU in 1963, which endorsed the legal validity of colonial frontiers.[^98] This created an unintended legacy that continues to shape the legal and political contours of the continent to the present, prioritizing territorial integrity over ethnic self-determination.[^99]

Nkrumah's incisive critique of neo-colonialism remains central to a decolonial critique of 아프리카n external relations.[^100] He correctly warned that foreign powers—especially 유럽an states—would utilize aid, trade, finance, and security interventions to keep 아프리카n former colonies economically and politically subordinate to external interests.[^101] His analysis of neo-colonialism continues to resonate in contemporary debates about foreign aid, debt, and economic dependency.[^102]

His intellectual contributions extended beyond Pan-아프리카nism and neo-colonialism.[^103] A sixth intellectual legacy is Nkrumah's principled opposition to nuclear weapons, as he believed that nuclear weapons and the hostile climate they engendered were inimical to security and peace.[^104] Nkrumah's aphorism "we are looking neither East nor West, we are looking forward" remains applicable today, similar to how 아프리카n countries in the 냉전 period looked beyond the West to advance their own development path.[^105]

가나 rehabilitated Nkrumah's memory in the decades after his death, with his remains returned from Guinea and reburied in Accra in a memorial park that has become a site of pilgrimage for Pan-아프리카nists from around the world.[^106] Streets, universities, and institutions across 아프리카 bear his name, and his image appears on 가나ian currency.[^107] However, his legacy in 가나 itself remains contested, with debates continuing about whether he was primarily a liberator and visionary or an authoritarian who squandered the country's promise.[^108]

## 역사적 평가

### 긍정적 견해

Many scholars and admirers celebrate Nkrumah as one of the greatest 아프리카n leaders of the twentieth century and a pioneering figure in the anti-colonial struggle.[^109] In 1974, U.S. historian John Henrik Clarke described Nkrumah as "the first universal 아프리카n hero of this century."[^110] C.L.R. James, his decades-long mentor from Trinidad, called him in 1972 "one of the greatest political leaders of our century" and described him as the sort of figure who only appears on the world stage "at long intervals."[^111]

콰메 은크루마 remains a towering figure in 아프리카n history, and perhaps no other 아프리카n leader of the 1950s and 1960s personified the dreams, principles, and aspirations of this era more completely.[^112] His vision of 아프리카n unity continues to inspire contemporary Pan-아프리카nists and those seeking greater 아프리카n integration and cooperation.[^113] Supporters argue that his authoritarian measures were necessary responses to real threats of neo-colonial interference and that his economic policies, while flawed in execution, represented a genuine attempt to break free from colonial economic structures.[^114]

Scholars particularly praise Nkrumah's intellectual contributions, with his concept of neo-colonialism providing an analytical framework that remains relevant for understanding contemporary 아프리카n political economy.[^115] His emphasis on the "아프리카n Personality" and cultural decolonization anticipated later movements for 아프리카n cultural renaissance and psychological liberation from colonial mentalities.[^116] Admirers point to his generous support for other 아프리카n liberation movements and his willingness to provide haven for freedom fighters as evidence of his genuine commitment to Pan-아프리카n solidarity rather than narrow national interest.[^117]

The fact that he was overthrown while on a peace mission to 베트남, attempting to mediate in that conflict, adds to his image as a martyr for peace and 아프리카n dignity.[^118] His supporters argue that external forces, particularly Western intelligence agencies uncomfortable with his socialist leanings and Pan-아프리카n activism, played a role in destabilizing his government and facilitating the 1966 coup.[^119]

### 부정적 견해

Critics, however, argue that Nkrumah squandered 가나's considerable advantages at independence through economic mismanagement, corruption, and authoritarianism.[^120] Historians consider 가나 under Nkrumah and the CPP the exemplar narrative of the folly of rapidly industrializing economies in post-independence 아프리카.[^121] These historians agree that Nkrumah's economic program was over-reaching given the severe lack of 가나's infrastructure and the paucity of foreign capital investment.[^122]

His detractors emphasize that the Preventive Detention Act and the establishment of a one-party state violated fundamental 인권 and set a dangerous precedent for authoritarian governance in post-colonial 아프리카.[^123] They argue that his cult of personality and intolerance of criticism created a political culture that undermined democratic development and good governance.[^124] The corruption within his government, combined with the economic hardships faced by ordinary 가나ians in the 1960s, contradicted his socialist rhetoric about serving the masses.[^125]

Some scholars argue that Nkrumah's obsession with Pan-아프리카n unity led him to neglect 가나's domestic needs and waste resources on grandiose continental projects.[^126] His critics contend that a more modest, pragmatic approach to economic development would have served 가나 better than his ambitious industrialization schemes.[^127] The fact that 가나's economy declined significantly during the later years of his rule, despite starting independence as one of 아프리카's wealthiest countries, supports this critical assessment.[^128]

For students of 가나ian history and politics, 콰메 은크루마 remains an enigmatic figure at once lionized as a hero of 아프리카n liberation and Pan-아프리카nism, and dismissed as an authoritarian strongman.[^129] Critics argue that his romantic vision of Pan-아프리카n unity was unrealistic and that his attempts to impose it through the OAU failed to account for the diverse interests and circumstances of different 아프리카n nations.[^130] The general relief in 가나 following the 1966 coup, with crowds celebrating in the streets, suggests that by that point his government had lost popular support.[^131]
