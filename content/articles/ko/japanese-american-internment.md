## 개요

US government forcibly relocated 120,000 Japanese-Americans to internment camps during WWII. Two-thirds were US citizens; they lost homes, businesses, and civil rights.

## 경력 연표

| 연도 | 사건 |
|------|-------|
| 1942 | Executive Order 9066 signed |
| 1988 | US apologized and paid reparations |

## Origins and Policy Mechanism

- The mass removal followed Japan's attack on Pearl Harbor and wartime fear of espionage and sabotage on the U.S. West Coast.
- Executive Order 9066 enabled the military to designate exclusion zones, leading to forced relocation and confinement under the War Relocation Authority.

## Conditions and Social Impact

- Families were moved first to temporary "assembly centers" and then to remote camps.
- Confinement produced major economic losses (property, farms, small businesses) and long-term trauma.
- Despite incarceration, many Japanese Americans served in the U.S. military; the 442nd Regimental Combat Team is frequently cited in overviews for its combat record.

## Legal and Political Aftermath

- Several Supreme Court-era cases (commonly discussed in summaries) challenged aspects of exclusion and detention, including **Korematsu v. United States**.
- Decades later, a federal commission concluded the policy was driven by racism, war hysteria, and failed political leadership.
- The Civil Liberties Act of 1988 issued an official apology and authorized redress payments to surviving internees.

## 유산

The internment is widely referenced in U.S. civil liberties debates as a cautionary example of emergency powers overriding constitutional protections.
