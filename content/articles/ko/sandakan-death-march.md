## 요약

Japanese forces marched 2,434 Allied POWs from Sandakan prison camp to Ranau, Borneo. Only 6 survived. One of the worst atrocities against POWs in the Pacific War.

## Background and Context

- **Location:** Sandakan, British North Borneo (now Sabah, Malaysia), with the march route ending near **Ranau** on the slopes of Mount Kinabalu.
- **Prisoners:** Mostly **Australian** and **British** POWs captured in the Pacific theater.
- **Purpose of the camp:** POW labor was used for wartime construction projects (notably an airfield), under harsh conditions.

## The Marches (1945)

The term “Sandakan Death Marches” commonly refers to multiple forced marches in 1945 as the Japanese garrison tried to move prisoners inland while Allied forces advanced.

- **Conditions on the route:** severe malnutrition, disease, exposure, and brutality; many prisoners were unable to keep pace.
- **Guard treatment:** prisoners who fell behind were frequently beaten or killed.
- **Outcome:** the marches, combined with conditions at Sandakan and Ranau, produced an extremely low survival rate.

## 여파

- **Survivors:** the widely cited figure is **six survivors**, all of whom escaped; they later provided testimony about the camp and the marches.
- **Postwar accountability:** Sandakan and related atrocities were examined in **postwar war crimes investigations and trials**, contributing to historical documentation.

## Legacy and Memory

- The Sandakan marches are remembered as among the worst atrocities against Allied POWs in the Pacific War.
- Memorial efforts in Australia and Malaysia have included commemorations, historical work, and preservation of sites associated with Sandakan and the march route.

## Notes on Dates and Naming

- The event spans **early-to-mid 1945**, but the broader Sandakan POW story begins earlier with the establishment and operation of the Sandakan camp during the war.
- “Sandakan Death March” is often used as a singular label, but many sources treat it as **a series of marches**.
