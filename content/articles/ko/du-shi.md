## 개요

Du Shi was a Han Dynasty Chinese government official and 공학자 who in 31 AD invented the first water-powered bellows for iron smelting, using a water wheel to drive piston bellows. This innovation greatly improved iron production efficiency and quality, helping drive Chinese metallurgical advancement over a millennium ~ 전에 similar 기술 appeared in Europe.

## How Water-Powered Bellows Worked (Conceptually)

전통적인 iron-smelting bellows required continuous human labor to push air into a furnace. The innovation attributed to Du Shi replaced much of that manual effort by using a **water wheel** as a steady power source. In simplified terms:

- Flowing water turns a wheel.
- The wheel’s rotation is converted into a back-and-forth motion.
- That motion drives bellows (or piston-like mechanisms) that force air into the furnace.

This creates a more consistent blast of air, which can raise furnace temperatures and improve throughput.

## Why It Was Important

The broader significance of the device is less about one machine and more about what it represents: the application of mechanical power to industrial production. It is frequently mentioned in histories of 기술 as an 초기의 example of:

- Harnessing renewable energy (water power) for manufacturing
- Automating repetitive physical work
- Scaling metallurgical processes beyond the limits of human muscle

## 유산 in the History of Technology

Accounts of Du Shi’s bellows appear in discussions of the long arc of pre-현대의 engineering in China and the development of water-powered machinery. Even where details are debated by historians, the story functions as a canonical illustration of 초기의 mechanization in iron production.
