## 개요

- **Founded:** 1985 (via merger of Houston Natural Gas and InterNorth).
- **Headquarters:** Houston, Texas.
- **Business focus (1990s):** Natural gas and electricity trading, plus a push into broadband and other ventures.
- **Signature issue:** Use of complex financial structures to hide debt and inflate reported earnings.

## Key People (commonly cited)

- **Kenneth Lay:** Founder and long-time executive; served as CEO and chair ~ 동안 key periods.
- **Jeffrey Skilling:** CEO in 2001; promoted an aggressive “trading company” model.
- **Andrew Fastow:** CFO; central figure in creating and managing special purpose entities (SPEs).
- **Arthur Andersen:** External auditor whose role in the scandal 에 기여했다 the firm’s collapse.

## How the Fraud Worked (high-level)

- **Mark-to-market accounting:** Booking estimated future profits on long-term deals as current income, which increased pressure to keep “new” gains flowing.
- **Special purpose entities (SPEs):** Off-balance-sheet vehicles used to move liabilities away from Enron’s main statements.
- **Related-party conflicts:** Some SPE arrangements involved executives with personal financial interests.
- **Opaque disclosure:** Complexity and limited transparency made it difficult for investors to see leverage and risk.

## Collapse and Aftermath

- **2001:** Confidence eroded as accounting practices came under scrutiny and restatements occurred.
- **Bankruptcy (2001):** Enron filed for Chapter 11, then the largest U.S. bankruptcy at the time.
- **Market impact:** Thousands of employees lost jobs; many saw retirement savings harmed due to concentrated company-stock exposure.
- **Audit industry shock:** Arthur Andersen was effectively dismantled ~ 후에 criminal conviction (later overturned), reshaping the “Big Five” into the “Big Four.”

## Regulatory and Cultural Impact

- **Sarbanes–Oxley Act (2002):** Strengthened corporate governance, internal controls, and auditor 독립 requirements in the U.S.
- **Enduring shorthand:** “Enron” ~이 되었다 reference point for corporate malfeasance, weak controls, and incentives-driven accounting abuse.
- **Case-study staple:** Frequently taught in business, accounting, and ethics courses as an example of governance failure.

## Notes

- The Enron scandal is often discussed as a combination of **incentives**, **weak oversight**, **complexity**, and **conflicts of interest**, rather than a single accounting trick.
- The case also highlighted the risk of relying on **unaudited narratives** and **aggressive projections** when underlying cash flows are unstable.
