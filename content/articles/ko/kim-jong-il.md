## 개요

김정일 (February 16, 1941 – December 17, 2011) was the second Supreme Leader of 북한 who ruled from 1994 until his death in 2011[^1]. Born in the 소련 during 제2차 세계대전, he was groomed from the early 1970s to succeed his father, 김일성, creating the world's first communist hereditary succession[^2]. His 17-year rule was marked by the development of 북한's nuclear weapons program, the devastating famine of the 1990s that killed hundreds of thousands to millions, severe 인권 abuses, and a pervasive personality cult[^3][^4]. Known as the "Dear Leader," 김정일 implemented "military-first" politics (Songun) while maintaining one of the world's most repressive totalitarian regimes[^5].

---

## 초기 생애와 배경

김정일 was born on February 16, 1941, in Vyatskoye, near Khabarovsk in the 소련, where his father 김일성 was receiving military training with Soviet authorities[^6]. However, official 북한n propaganda claimed he was born on February 16, 1942, at a secret military camp on Mount Paektu, Korea's sacred mountain, accompanied by supernatural phenomena including a double rainbow and a new star in the heavens[^7]. According to scholar Lim Jae Cheon, Kim is generally agreed to have been born in Voroshilov, a Soviet military camp near Nikolsk[^8].

His mother, Kim Jong-suk, was 김일성's first wife and a former guerrilla fighter. In 1945, when Kim was four years old, 제2차 세계대전 ended and Korea regained independence from 일본[^9]. His father returned to 평양 that September, and in late November, young Jong-il returned to Korea via a Soviet ship, landing at Sonbong[^10]. The family moved into a former 일본ese officer's mansion in 평양, complete with a garden and pool, marking the beginning of the Kim family's privileged position in 북한n society[^11].

During the 한국전쟁 (1950-1953), 김정일 was moved to 중국 for safety[^12]. He received his education in 중국 during this period before returning to 북한. He later attended 김일성 University, graduating in 1963 with a degree in political economy[^13]. Some sources indicate he also studied in East 독일 for two years, where he trained to be a pilot[^14]. After graduating from university in 1964, 김정일 devoted himself to cultural, ideological, and propaganda work at the Department of Organization and Guidance of the Central Committee of the Workers' Party[^15].

From an early age, 김정일 was exposed to the machinery of totalitarian power and witnessed his father's consolidation of absolute control over 북한. This upbringing in the heart of 북한's political elite would profoundly shape his worldview and his later approach to leadership, emphasizing loyalty, control, and the centrality of the Kim family to the 북한n state.

---

## 권력의 부상

김정일's ascent to power was unprecedented in the communist world, representing the first hereditary succession in a communist state[^16]. According to East German documents, Kim had been groomed from as early as 1974 to take over the helm of the 북한n state[^17]. The succession process began so early out of fear that "something grave might happen to 김일성," and extended over two decades[^18].

In 1967, 김정일 was appointed to the state propaganda and information department, where he began to focus his energy on developing the veneration of his father[^19]. It was around this time that the title "Great Leader" came into habitual usage for 김일성. In 1973, 김정일 became party secretary of the propaganda department[^20]. At the February 1974 plenum, 김정일 was formally proclaimed as the successor and became a member of the Political Council[^21].

The first public confirmation of 김정일's position as successor came in 1977, when in a booklet he was designated as 김일성's only heir[^22]. In 1980, he was elevated to the Politburo and was granted the title "Dear Leader," distinguishing him from his father while maintaining the dynastic mystique[^23]. In 1991, he was named commander of the Democratic People's Republic of Korea (DPRK) armed forces[^24].

김정일's succession strategy included the early distribution of photographs of the successor, the institutionalization of the Kim-family cult, and the use of mass campaigns, including the Three Revolutions Movement, to build a base of support among cadres in the ruling Korean Workers' Party and among the masses[^25]. He personally guided the production of revolutionary films such as _Sea of Blood_ (1969), _The Fate of a Self-Defence Corps Man_ (1970), and _The Flower Girl_ (1972), using cinema as a tool for ideological indoctrination[^26].

On July 8, 1994, 김일성 died of a heart attack, and 김정일 inherited power[^27]. However, he did not immediately assume all of his father's titles. In October 1997, 김정일 was named General Secretary of the Workers' Party[^28]. In April 1993, he had been named Chairman of the National Defense Commission, a position that became increasingly important under his "military-first" ideology[^29]. This dynastic succession was unprecedented in a communist country, marking a unique development in communist political systems and setting a precedent that would later be followed when 김정일's own son, 김정은, succeeded him.

## 주요 업적

### Nuclear Weapons Program

Under 김정일's leadership, 북한 conducted its first nuclear test in October 2006, becoming the world's ninth nuclear-armed state[^30]. This represented a major shift in the regional and global security landscape. In 2009, a second nuclear test was conducted, further cementing 북한's status as a nuclear power[^31]. 김정일 leveraged 북한's nuclear program to help achieve diplomatic and economic objectives, at times offering limited nuclear concessions in return for economic aid and improved relations with the 미국[^32].

The nuclear program became a defining legacy that would be passed to his son 김정은 and remains central to 북한's national strategy[^33]. In 2002, Kim's government admitted to having produced nuclear weapons in violation of the 1994 Agreed Framework with the 미국[^34].

### Military-First Politics (Songun)

김정일 strengthened the role of the military through his Songun ("military-first") policies, making the army the central organizer of civil society[^35]. His regime adopted the basic guideline of "military-first politics" (sŏngun chŏngch'i) to safeguard it from adverse impacts such as the collapse of the 소련 and persistent economic hardships at home[^36]. This ideology prioritized military spending and placed the Korean People's Army at the center of political, economic, and social life.

### Diplomatic Achievements

The year 2000 marked a significant milestone in relations between North and 대한민국 when 김대중 and 김정일 came together for the 2000 inter-Korean summit, the first time leaders of each Korea had met since the end of the 한국전쟁[^37]. The June 15, 2000 summit has widely been regarded as the biggest achievement of 대한민국's 햇볕 정책[^38]. 김정일 had in-person meetings with both 대한민국n presidents 김대중 and Roh Moo-hyun — still to date, the only two 대한민국n presidents to actually meet the 북한n leadership in person[^39].

### Economic Initiatives

Kim's rule saw tentative economic reforms, including the opening of the Kaesong Industrial Park in 2003, just north of the 비무장지대, which represented unprecedented economic cooperation with 대한민국[^40]. 북한 adopted economic reforms during the 김정일 era, including enabling state-owned enterprises to make profits in 1997, reforming wages and prices in 2002, and legalizing markets in 2003[^41]. These reforms included the "July 1 economic measures" launched in 2002, though many were reversed by the end of 김정일's rule[^42].

In 1998, 김정일 revised the Constitution to consolidate power and address economic difficulties, expanding ownership rights to include social organizations alongside the state and cooperatives, reducing state control and fostering a more conducive environment for private ownership[^43].

### Cultural Legacy

김정일 was a passionate cinephile with a collection of approximately 20,000 films, including James Bond movies, action films, and all 아카데미상 winners[^44]. His treatise _On the Art of the Cinema_ from 1973 is considered the most authoritative work on 북한n filmmaking[^45]. In his 1987 essay "The Cinema and Directing," Kim wrote that cinema occupies an important place in art and literature development and "is a powerful ideological weapon for the revolution and construction"[^46].

## 논란과 비판

### The 1990s Famine

김정일 was responsible for the deaths of hundreds of thousands, and perhaps millions, of 북한ns through widespread preventable starvation during the "Arduous March" of the 1990s[^47]. Following the collapse of the 소련 and its support for 북한 in 1991, 김정일 presided over this disastrous period, which killed massive numbers of people through mismanagement of an already ill-equipped economy, combined with droughts and floods that severely hurt the harvest, and brutal policies that steered scarce food to the military and government elites[^48]. Estimates of deaths range from hundreds of thousands to more than 2.5 to 3 million between 1994 and 1998[^49]. Kim's government was accused of "crimes against humanity" for its alleged culpability in creating and prolonging the famine[^50].

### Political Prison Camps and Human Rights Abuses

김정일's legacy includes the fate of tens of thousands who have died in the kwanliso (political prison) camps for alleged enemies of the state, where an estimated 200,000 북한ns continued to work and die in conditions of near starvation and brutal abuse[^51]. Human Rights Watch characterized him as a dictator and accused him of 인권 violations[^52]. Amnesty International condemned him for leaving "millions of 북한ns mired in poverty" and detaining hundreds of thousands of people in prison camps[^53]. A Human Rights Watch official described "북한 under 김정일 has been a 인권 hell on earth"[^54].

### Kidnapping of 대한민국n Filmmakers

김정일 instructed the kidnapping of 대한민국n filmmaker Shin Sang-ok and actress Choi Eun-hee in the late 1970s, holding them captive for years and forcing them to make films for him to gain global recognition for 북한's film industry[^55]. This extraordinary crime demonstrated the regime's willingness to violate international law and human dignity to achieve its objectives.

### Nuclear Proliferation

김정일's development of nuclear weapons violated the 1994 Agreed Framework and created regional instability[^56]. His regime's pursuit of weapons of mass destruction came at enormous cost to the 북한n people, diverting resources from desperately needed economic development and humanitarian aid during the famine years.

### Personality Cult and Propaganda

The personality cult built around 김정일 was grandiose, extensive, pervasive, and enduring, with propaganda writers heralding him as "the greatest of great men produced by heaven" and touting him as the world's greatest leader, thinker, scientist, politician, guerrilla leader, and songwriter[^57]. State propaganda made absurd claims, such as that he could walk and talk before the age of six months, and that when he was born, a glacier on the lake on Mount Paektu cracked, a double rainbow appeared in the sky, and a new star appeared in the heavens[^58]. A year after 김정일's death, 북한 faced serious financial difficulties after spending US$100 million building a personality cult around the deceased leader[^59].

## 개인 생활

김정일 had four partners and at least six children with three of them[^60]. He married his first wife, Hong Il-chon, at the behest of 김일성 in 1966, and they had one daughter, Kim Hye-kyung (born 1968), before divorcing in 1969[^61]. He later fathered 김정남 (born 1971) with his first consort, film star Song Hye-rim, and due to Song being a divorcée, Kim concealed the relationship and son from his father[^62].

In 1974, 김정일 married his second wife, Kim Young-suk. They had two daughters, Kim Sol-song (born 1974) and Kim Chun-song (born 1976), and he divorced her in 1977[^63]. In 1980, 김정일 married his third wife, Ko Yong-hui, who was the de facto First Lady of 북한 from 김정일's becoming leader in 1994 until her death in 2004[^64]. The couple had two sons, Kim Jong-chul (born 1981) and 김정은 (born 1982 or 1983), and one daughter, Kim Yo-jong (born 1987)[^65].

His third and youngest son, 김정은, succeeded him as Supreme Leader[^66]. 김정은 came to power with the death of his father, who died from a heart attack in December 2011[^67]. 김정일's second son, Kim Jong-chul, was "deemed too effeminate" to rule, which left 김정은, the youngest of the three sons, to succeed his father as the head of 북한[^68].

김정일 was known for his eccentric lifestyle and tastes. He favored large-scale epics of 할리우드, including works of 존 포드 and James Cameron, plus James Bond movies of the 1960s and 1970s, and 일본ese monster films like Godzilla[^69]. Although Kim was known to have been privately interested in 할리우드 films, he forbade discussion on foreign film concepts at the Aesthetic Review Meeting, an important annual film conference[^70].

In August 2008, 김정일 reportedly suffered a stroke[^71]. He was treated by French doctors at 평양's 적십자 Hospital and suffered a debilitating stroke that raised questions about succession and the stability of his regime[^72]. Despite his health issues, Kim maintained control until his death in 2011.

## 유산

김정일's legacy remains deeply contested and multifaceted. International assessments of his legacy were generally negative, blaming him for the continued economic decline of the country[^73]. However, scholarly perspectives varied by region and approach. Western perspectives and DPRK defectors/refugees were particularly negative, with widespread claims that 김정일 was far less popular than 김일성 and that apparent outpourings of grief were all staged[^74]. Commentators in 중국 and 러시아 were somewhat more nuanced, noting the continued existence of the DPRK 17 years after 김정일 took power despite widespread predictions in 1994 that neither he nor the regime would last[^75].

김정일 is associated both with the trauma of famine and crisis in the late 1990s, and with limited steps toward economic reform in the early 2000s that have since been scaled back[^76]. Defectors' reports suggest the view of Kim within his country was more mixed, with 김정일 associated both with the trauma of famine and crisis in the late 1990s and with limited steps toward economic reform in the early 2000s[^77].

The most significant aspect of 김정일's legacy is the successful establishment of dynastic succession in 북한. He not only inherited power from his father but successfully passed it to his son, 김정은, creating a three-generation hereditary dictatorship unique in the modern world[^78]. This dynastic system has proven remarkably resilient despite predictions of collapse.

김정일's legacy of military-first politics still governs 북한[^79]. The nuclear weapons program he developed became a cornerstone of 북한n national strategy and continues to shape regional security dynamics. His emphasis on military power over economic development set a pattern that his son has largely followed, though with some modifications.

The personality cult 김정일 helped create around his father and then around himself has become an integral part of 북한n political culture[^80]. State propaganda dubbed 김일성 "Great Leader" and 김정일 "Dear Leader," with their portraits ubiquitous in the country, appearing in apartments, subway cars, offices, and almost every major facility[^81].

A more considered judgment will in large part depend on how the DPRK develops in the future. If 북한 survives and its circumstances improve, Kim may be remembered as a leader who guided the country through its worst post-war crisis and prepared the way for reform[^82]. However, for most observers, his legacy remains one of 인권 abuses, economic mismanagement, and the perpetuation of one of the world's most repressive regimes.

## 역사적 평가

### 긍정적 견해

Some scholars and commentators, particularly in 중국 and 러시아, have offered more nuanced assessments of 김정일's leadership[^83]. They note that despite widespread predictions in 1994 that neither 김정일 nor the 북한n regime would survive, the DPRK persisted for 17 years under his leadership and beyond[^84]. This survival through the collapse of the 소련, the devastating famine of the 1990s, and international isolation is seen by some as evidence of political skill and adaptability.

Within 북한's official historiography, 김정일 is celebrated as a revolutionary leader who upheld the principles of Juche (self-reliance) ideology during an extraordinarily difficult period[^85]. State propaganda credits him with maintaining 북한's independence and sovereignty against foreign pressure, particularly from the 미국. His military-first politics are portrayed as a necessary response to external threats and the key to national survival.

Some analysts acknowledge that 김정일 did implement certain economic reforms, including the July 2002 measures that reformed wages and prices, legalized markets in 2003, and enabled state-owned enterprises to make profits[^86]. The establishment of the Kaesong Industrial Complex in cooperation with 대한민국 represented an unprecedented opening, albeit limited[^87]. These tentative steps toward marketization laid groundwork that some scholars believe could have led to more significant reform under different circumstances.

김정일's diplomatic engagement, particularly the historic 2000 inter-Korean summit with 대한민국n President 김대중, demonstrated a willingness to engage in dialogue and reduced tensions on the 한반도, at least temporarily[^88]. For this achievement, some credit Kim with pragmatism and strategic flexibility, even if subsequent developments undermined these gains.

Defenders of 김정일's cultural legacy point to his genuine passion for cinema and his theoretical writings on film, particularly _On the Art of the Cinema_, which some film scholars consider a serious work of film theory, albeit one serving an authoritarian ideology[^89]. His emphasis on culture and propaganda as tools of governance showed sophistication in understanding the importance of narrative and symbolism in maintaining political power.

### 부정적 견해

The overwhelming consensus among international scholars, 인권 organizations, and defectors is that 김정일 was a brutal dictator responsible for crimes against humanity[^90]. Human Rights Watch's assessment that "북한 under 김정일 has been a 인권 hell on earth" reflects the severity of the regime's abuses[^91]. The political prison camp system, with an estimated 200,000 prisoners subjected to forced labor, torture, and starvation, represents one of the worst ongoing 인권 situations in the world[^92].

Critics emphasize that 김정일's policies were directly responsible for the famine that killed hundreds of thousands to millions of 북한ns in the 1990s[^93]. Rather than prioritizing the welfare of his people, Kim maintained military spending and continued to live in luxury while his citizens starved. The regime's refusal to accept international humanitarian aid without strict conditions and its diversion of aid to the military and party elites demonstrated a callous disregard for human life.

Scholars critical of 김정일 point out that his economic "reforms" were minimal, inconsistent, and ultimately reversed[^94]. Unlike 중국's Deng Xiaoping or 베트남's reformers, Kim never committed to genuine economic opening. The limited marketization that occurred was a survival response to state collapse rather than a planned reform strategy. His resistance to meaningful economic change perpetuated 북한's poverty and isolation.

The personality cult surrounding 김정일 is widely condemned as one of history's most extreme examples of totalitarian propaganda[^95]. The absurd claims about his birth, abilities, and achievements, combined with the mandatory worship of Kim family portraits and the severe punishment for any perceived disrespect, created a climate of fear and psychological control that stifled human dignity and freedom of thought.

International relations scholars criticize 김정일's development of nuclear weapons as a dangerous destabilization of regional security that triggered an arms race and increased the risk of catastrophic conflict[^96]. His brinksmanship diplomacy, threatening nuclear weapons development to extract economic concessions, is seen as irresponsible and ultimately counterproductive, leading to tighter sanctions and greater isolation.

Western scholars and defectors emphasize that any apparent popular support for 김정일 was manufactured through coercion and propaganda rather than genuine affection[^97]. The staged displays of grief at his death, while impressive in scale, reflected the totalitarian control over public expression rather than authentic emotion. Defector testimonies consistently describe a climate of fear, surveillance, and arbitrary punishment that made genuine political opposition impossible.

## 유명한 어록

> "Nothing is impossible for a man with a strong will." [^98]

This quote reflects 김정일's emphasis on determination and revolutionary spirit, central themes in 북한n ideology.

> "The revolution is carried out by means of one's thought, not through one's family background." [^99]

An ironic statement given that 김정일 himself came to power entirely through hereditary succession.

> "It is my greatest wish to enable our people to live with nothing to envy at the earliest possible date, and it is my greatest pleasure to work energetically, sharing my joys and sorrows with our people, on the road of translating my wish into reality." [^100]

This quote represents Kim's propagandistic self-portrayal as a devoted servant of the people, contradicted by the reality of famine and repression.

> "The core in the Juche outlook on the revolution is loyalty to the party and the leader. The cause of socialism and communism is started by the leader and is carried out under the guidance of the party and the leader." [^101]

This statement encapsulates the centrality of personal loyalty in 북한n political ideology.

> "We oppose the reactionary policies of the U.S. government but we do not oppose the American people. We want to have many good friends in the 미국." [^102]

김정일's attempt to distinguish between the U.S. government and its people, a common rhetorical device in 북한n propaganda.

> "I am the object of criticism around the world." [^103]

A rare moment of apparent self-awareness from 김정일 acknowledging international condemnation.

> "A man who dreads trials and difficulties cannot become a revolutionary. If he is to become a revolutionary with an indomitable fighting spirit, he must be tempered in the arduous struggle from his youth." [^104]

This quote emphasizes the 북한n ideological emphasis on hardship and struggle as character-building experiences.
