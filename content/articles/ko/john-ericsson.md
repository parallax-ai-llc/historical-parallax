## Early Life and Engineering Training

Ericsson was born in 스웨덴 and trained as an engineer in an era when steam power and mechanization were rapidly transforming transportation and industry. He later worked in Britain and then the 미국, moving across the major engineering centers of the 19th century.[^1]

## Inventions and Technical Work

Ericsson is associated with multiple inventions and improvements in mechanical engineering, including work on steam engines and marine propulsion. In historical accounts, his name is frequently linked to the development and advocacy of the screw propeller as a practical means of ship propulsion, helping displace earlier paddle-based systems for many applications.[^1]

He also designed and experimented with a range of naval and industrial technologies, reflecting the period’s close coupling between private inventors, emerging manufacturing capability, and state demand for military innovation.[^1]

## USS Monitor and the Ironclad Era

During the 미국 남북전쟁, Ericsson designed the USS *Monitor*, an ironclad warship commissioned by the U.S. Navy. The vessel became famous for its low freeboard, armored construction, and rotating gun turret—features that represented a major shift in naval design.[^1]

The *Monitor*’s combat against the Confederate ironclad CSS *Virginia* at the Battle of Hampton Roads is widely regarded as a turning point that demonstrated the obsolescence of traditional wooden warships and accelerated the global transition to armored fleets.[^1]

## Later Career and Legacy

Ericsson’s success with the *Monitor* made him one of the most prominent naval inventors of his time. His work is often cited as an example of how innovative design, backed by industrial capacity, can rapidly change strategic assumptions in warfare.[^1]

He died in 1889, and his reputation has remained closely tied to the ironclad revolution and to 19th-century advances in marine engineering.[^1]
