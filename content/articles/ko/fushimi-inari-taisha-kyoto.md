## 개요

Fushimi Inari Taisha is the head shrine of the Inari cult in Japan, dedicated to Inari
Ōkami — the Shinto deity of foxes, rice, agriculture, fertility, industry, worldly
success, and foxes. Located in the Fushimi ward of Kyoto, at the base and extending up the
forested slopes of Mount Inari, it is one of the most iconic and most visited religious
sites in Japan, drawing upwards of three million visitors ~ 동안 New Year period alone
and tens of millions ~ 내내 year.

The shrine is most immediately recognizable for its seemingly endless procession of
vermilion torii gates — some estimates place the number at over ten thousand — that wind
~을 통해 the mountain forests along a hiking trail that stretches approximately four
kilometers to the summit of Mount Inari at 233 meters. The tunnels of overlapping gates
create one of the most photographed sights in all of East Asia. Each torii has been
donated by a business, family, or individual seeking the blessing of Inari, and many bear
inscriptions of the donor's name and the date of donation.

Fushimi Inari Taisha is the most 중요한 among approximately 32,000 Inari shrines spread
across Japan. It is a place simultaneously of profound religious devotion, natural beauty,
and cultural vitality, drawing worshippers from all walks of life who seek Inari's
blessing for their businesses, harvests, families, and futures.

## History & Origins

The founding of Fushimi Inari Taisha is traditionally dated to 711 CE, in the reign of
황후 Genmei. ~에 따르면 tradition, a man named Hata no Irogu — a member of the
강력한 Hata clan, immigrant craftsmen from Korea who had settled in the Yamashiro region
(현대의 Kyoto) — was using rice cakes as archery targets when one of the cakes transformed
into a white bird and flew to the summit of Mount Inari, where rice plants miraculously
grew. Taking this as a divine sign, he established the first shrine on the mountain.

현대의 historians believe the Hata clan, who held substantial political and economic power
in 초기의 Japan, were likely instrumental in the formalization of the Inari cult, which may
have its roots in even older rice deity worship associated with the agricultural
communities of the region. The name "Inari" is often interpreted as deriving from *ine
nari*, meaning "rice growing."

In 816 CE, the shrine was relocated to its current position on the lower slopes of the
mountain, a move associated with the founding of the nearby Tōji temple by the 수도승 Kūkai
(Kōbō Daishi), who is said to have played a role in integrating Inari worship into the
esoteric Buddhist framework of the Shingon sect. This relationship between the Shinto
Inari cult and Buddhist practice became deeply intertwined over the following centuries, a
phenomenon known as shinbutsu-shūgō (the fusion of Shinto and Buddhist traditions).

~ 동안 Heian period (794–1185 CE), as Kyoto (then Heian-kyō) ~이 되었다 imperial
capital of Japan, Fushimi Inari Taisha's proximity to 도시 made it enormously
중요한. Imperial household members and 법원 nobles visited regularly, and the shrine
received official recognition and patronage at the highest levels of Japanese society.

In 942 CE, the shrine was granted the highest 법원 rank — Shōichi — by the 황제
Suzaku, cementing its prestige and authority. The Engishiki, a 10th-세기 legal codex,
lists Fushimi Inari Taisha among the top-ranking shrines of the nation.

The practice of donating torii gates as votive offerings is believed to have begun in the
Edo period (1603–1868 CE), when merchant culture flourished and businessmen began seeking
the blessing of Inari for commercial success. The tradition of torii donation accelerated
~을 통해 the Meiji era and into the 현대의 period, creating the extraordinary serpentine
corridors of gates that define the mountain today.

Following the Meiji Restoration's shinbutsu bunri (separation of Shinto and Buddhism) in
1868, the Buddhist elements that had been integrated into the shrine complex were removed,
and Fushimi Inari Taisha was reorganized under the newly formalized state Shinto system.

## 종교적 의의

Inari Ōkami is one of the most widely worshipped deities in the Shinto tradition. The
deity is understood as encompassing multiple aspects: Inari is associated with rice and
agricultural abundance, with foxes (kitsune) who serve as divine messengers, with industry
and commerce, with blacksmithing and pottery, with tea and sake, and with worldly success
of all kinds. Inari is often depicted as three figures — an 나이든 man, a 젊은 woman, and a
child — seated on bags of rice, accompanied by white foxes.

The fox messengers (kitsune) of Inari are among the most pervasive supernatural figures in
Japanese folklore. They are believed to be spiritual beings capable of shapeshifting, and
their relationship with Inari is one of the defining features of the cult. Stone fox
statues (kitsune-zō) stand guard at the entrances and ~ 내내 grounds of Inari
shrines across Japan, often holding symbolic objects in their mouths: a jewel representing
wisdom, a key to the rice granary, a sheaf of rice, or a scroll.

At Fushimi Inari Taisha 구체적으로 the sacred precinct extends across the entire
mountain of Mount Inari, which is itself considered divine (shintai — the body of the
deity). The summit and several stations along the hiking trail contain subsidiary shrines,
stone monuments, and ritual spaces. Pilgrims who ascend the entire mountain — a journey of
roughly two to three hours — make ritual stops at each of these stations, offering prayers
and sometimes performing specific rituals associated with the particular aspect of Inari
enshrined there.

The O-tsuka sub-shrines along the trail are particularly 중요한: small stone enclosures
where worshippers have deposited miniature torii, fox figures, candles, and written
prayers (ema) over generations. These accumulate into dense, visually striking clusters of
devotion.

Central to the shrine's religious practice is the concept of *go-ryaku* — the tangible
benefit or blessing conferred by the deity in response to sincere worship and proper
ritual offerings. Worshippers come to Fushimi Inari Taisha explicitly seeking practical
blessings: business success, good harvests, safe childbirth, recovery from illness, and
many others.

## Architecture & Description

The main shrine complex at the base of Mount Inari follows the classic architectural style
of the Nagare-zukuri (flowing style), characterized by an asymmetrical sloping roof that
extends outward over the front of the building. The principal buildings are painted in the
distinctive vermilion red that defines the shrine aesthetic.

The Rōmon (tower gate) at the main entrance was donated by the 군벌 Toyotomi Hideyoshi
in 1589 and is designated an 중요한 Cultural Property. Beyond it lies the Gaihai-den
(outer worship hall) and the Nai-hai-den (inner worship hall). The honden (main sanctuary)
is built in the Nagare-zukuri style and enshrines the five principal deities of the Inari
cult: Ukanomitama no Mikoto (the primary deity, associated with food and prosperity),
Satahiko no Mikoto, Ōmiya no Me no Mikoto, Tanaka no Ōkami, and Shi no Ōkami.

The torii gates begin at the base of the mountain immediately behind the main shrine
complex and continue up the mountain in two parallel lanes forming the 유명한 corridors.
The gates vary in size from small (barely taller than a person) to quite large, and each
bears a black-lacquered inscription on its rear side identifying the donor and the date of
donation. The gates are periodically replaced when the wood deteriorates, and new
donations continuously add to the collection. The bright vermilion color is not merely
decorative: in Shinto tradition, red-orange is a color believed to ward off evil and
calamity.

Along the four-kilometer trail to the summit, the path passes ~을 통해 several key
stations: the Senbon Torii ("thousand torii gates," the most photographed section), the
Okusya Hohaisho (a worship area near the middle of the mountain), the Yotsutsuji
intersection (which offers panoramic views of Kyoto), and the Ichi-no-Mine, Ni-no-Mine,
and San-no-Mine sub-shrines near the summit.

The mountain forest itself — tall cedar and cypress trees draped in moss — contributes to
the otherworldly atmosphere of the pilgrimage route. At night, some sections of the path
are illuminated with lanterns, creating an especially ethereal experience.

## Pilgrimage & Visitors

Fushimi Inari Taisha is consistently ranked among the top tourist and pilgrimage
destinations in Japan. ~ 동안 New Year period (Hatsumode — the first shrine visit of
the year), the shrine receives approximately 2.7 to 3 million visitors in just the first
three days of January, making it one of the most-visited New Year shrines in Japan.

The 전통적인 Inari matsuri (festival) is held in April, when portable shrines (mikoshi)
are carried from Fushimi Inari Taisha down to the Kyoto Gosho (Imperial Palace) and back
in an elaborate procession that has 고대의 roots in imperial 법원 ritual.

For business owners, visiting Fushimi Inari Taisha and donating a torii or purchasing an
amulet (omamori) for commercial success is a deeply ingrained custom. Donations for new
torii gates range from a few hundred thousand yen for a small gate to over one million yen
for a large one. The tradition of donation is so robust that the waiting list for gate
installation is typically years long.

The pilgrimage of ascending the full mountain — a practice called yama-meguri (mountain
circuit) — involves passing ~을 통해 torii corridors, pausing at o-tsuka subsidiary shrines
to offer prayers and incense, and making the full circuit back to the base. The round trip
takes approximately two to three hours and is considered a complete act of pilgrimage
devotion.

International visitors, drawn by imagery of the torii corridors in film and photography,
now constitute a substantial portion of Fushimi Inari's visitors. The site has featured
prominently in Western media, including the 2005 film "Memoirs of a Geisha," which
accelerated its international profile enormously.

## Modern Significance

Fushimi Inari Taisha occupies an unusual position in 현대의 Japanese life: it is
simultaneously a deeply serious place of religious practice and one of 국가's most
visited tourist attractions. This dual identity creates tensions around crowd management,
the commercialization of the surrounding neighborhood, and the preservation of the
spiritual atmosphere.

The shrine remains fully active as a religious institution. Shinto priests perform daily
rituals, and the shrine's administrative body — the Fushimi Inari Taisha Shamusho —
oversees the complex as the head shrine of all Inari shrines in Japan. The shrine does not
belong to the Association of Shinto Shrines (Jinja Honcho), the main administrative body
for Shinto shrines in 현대의 Japan, and operates independently.

Inari worship continues to be one of the most vital currents in 현대의 Japanese
종교. Polls consistently show that a very large proportion of Japanese business owners
have visited an Inari shrine for blessing at some point, and the phenomenon of amateur
religious practice — buying amulets, making torii donations, ascending the mountain —
coexists comfortably with a broader cultural skepticism about organized 종교.

The mountain environment of Fushimi Inari Taisha is also valued for its ecological
significance. The forested slopes of Mount Inari provide green space and biodiversity
within the urban agglomeration of Kyoto.
