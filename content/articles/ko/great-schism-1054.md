## 개요

The Great Schism of 1054, also known as the East-West Schism, was the formal break in communion between the Roman Catholic Church of the West and the Eastern Orthodox Church — a rupture that has endured for nearly a thousand years and remains one of the most consequential divisions in 역사 of Christianity. On July 16, 1054, 추기경 Humbert of Silva Candida, acting as a papal legate, placed a bull of excommunication on the high altar of the Hagia Sophia in Constantinople against Patriarch Michael I Cerularius and his allies. Several days later, Cerularius and the Holy Synod of Constantinople excommunicated the papal legates in return. Though the two churches had been drifting apart for centuries, this exchange of anathemas — conducted in the most sacred church in Christendom — crystallized the separation into a definitive institutional reality.

The causes of the schism were long in the making. They encompassed profound theological disagreements, especially the *filioque* controversy over whether the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone (the Eastern position) or from both the Father and the Son (the Western addition to the Nicene Creed). They included clashing theories of ecclesial authority — whether the 주교 of Rome held universal jurisdiction over all Christians, or whether he was first among equals among the five 고대의 patriarchates. And they involved a host of liturgical, canonical, and disciplinary differences that had accumulated over centuries as the Greek-speaking East and Latin-speaking West developed in increasing cultural isolation from one another, particularly ~ 후에 the political division of the Roman Empire in the 4th 세기 and the collapse of Roman imperial authority in the West in the 5th.

What happened in 1054 was in many ways the formal legal expression of an estrangement that had been building for two centuries and was arguably already a sociological reality ~ 전에 the bulls were exchanged. Yet it matters enormously as a date, a symbol, and a wound — ~ 때문에 it fixed in institutional form a division between two of 세계's most 고대의 Christian traditions that has never been fully healed, ~에도 불구하고 the lifting of the mutual anathemas by 교황 Paul VI and Patriarch Athenagoras I in 1964. Today approximately 1.3 billion Roman Catholics and 260 million Eastern Orthodox Christians trace their separate institutional identities back, in different ways, to the fracture of 1054.

## 역사적 맥락

The unity of the Christian church had always been more aspirational than actual. From its earliest centuries, Christianity had spread in multiple directions — west toward Rome, Gaul, and North Africa; east toward Syria, Mesopotamia, Persia, and 결국 India; south toward Egypt and Ethiopia; north toward the Balkans and 결국 the Slavic peoples. Different regional traditions developed different liturgical practices, theological emphases, and administrative structures. The five great patriarchates recognized by the Council of Nicaea and subsequent councils — Rome, Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, and Jerusalem — reflected both geography and theological prestige.

The political division of the Roman Empire in 395 CE into western and eastern halves deepened the cultural divergence between Latin-speaking western Christianity and Greek-speaking eastern Christianity. When the Western Roman Empire collapsed in 476 CE, the 주교 of Rome found himself in a unique position: the most prestigious religious authority in a political vacuum, surrounded by Germanic kingdoms and with no secular imperial counterpart. The papacy gradually developed theological and political claims to universal authority that reflected this unique circumstance. The Eastern church, by contrast, continued to exist within a functioning empire — the Byzantine Empire centered on Constantinople — and developed an ecclesiology in which the 황제 and the patriarch worked in a complex relationship of mutual authority and dependence (*symphonia*), with no single 주교 claiming universal jurisdiction over all.

The 8th and 9th centuries produced two major preliminary crises that foreshadowed 1054. The first was the *Iconoclast Controversy*, which erupted in 726 CE when 황제 Leo III ordered the destruction of religious images. 교황 Gregory II and his successor Gregory III condemned iconoclasm; the emperors responded by confiscating papal estates in southern Italy and removing several regions from Roman jurisdiction. The second was the *Photian Schism* of 863–867 CE, triggered when 교황 Nicholas I refused to recognize Patriarch Photius I of Constantinople and the two churches exchanged condemnations. 비록 ~이지만 the Photian Schism was resolved, it introduced into Eastern theology a formal critique of the *filioque* addition to the Nicene Creed — the theological trigger that would remain at the center of East-West dispute. By the mid-11th 세기, the question of Roman authority over newly converted Christian peoples in Bulgaria and other Slavic territories, combined with disputes over Latin churches in Constantinople and Greek churches in southern Italy, provided the immediate political kindling.

## The Event

The immediate trigger of the 1054 crisis was a letter written by Patriarch Michael Cerularius — a proud and contentious man whose personal style was more confrontational than diplomatic — to 주교 John of Trani in southern Italy. In this letter, written in 1053, Cerularius attacked Latin liturgical practices, particularly the use of unleavened bread (*azymes*) in the Eucharist rather than the leavened bread used in Eastern churches, and the Latin practice of clergy shaving their beards. He ordered the Latin churches in Constantinople closed when they refused to adopt Greek liturgical customs. Archbishop Leo of Ohrid forwarded the letter to a wider audience, and it reached Rome in a climate already inflamed by disputes over papal authority in southern Italy, where Norman armies were redrawing the political map.

교황 Leo IX, who was himself a prisoner of the Normans at the time (having been captured at 전투 of Civitate in June 1053), dispatched a delegation to Constantinople in January 1054 under the leadership of 추기경 Humbert of Silva Candida, a brilliant but rigidly combative ecclesiastic who was temperamentally ill-suited for delicate diplomacy. The delegation also included 추기경 Frederick of Lorraine and Archbishop Peter of Amalfi. The legates carried a letter from Leo IX that was sharply assertive of Roman primacy, citing the forged *Donation of Constantine* as evidence of the 교황's supreme authority. The Byzantines regarded the letter as unacceptable; the exchanges between Humbert and the 학자 Nicetas Stethatos (a 수도승 of the Stoudios monastery who had written a treatise attacking Latin practices) were acrimonious.

황제 Constantine IX Monomachos, who was conducting difficult diplomatic negotiations with Rome about a military alliance against the Normans, attempted to smooth things over and personally ensured that Nicetas' treatise was burned. But the fundamental impasse between Humbert and Cerularius was irresolvable: Cerularius refused to recognize the claims of Roman primacy that Humbert insisted upon, and Humbert refused to grant Cerularius the respect he believed his office deserved. The situation worsened when news arrived in July 1054 that 교황 Leo IX had died in April — which technically ended the legates' official mandate. Rather than return to Rome to await new instructions, Humbert pressed forward.

On Saturday, July 16, 1054, as the clergy of the Hagia Sophia were preparing for afternoon liturgy, 추기경 Humbert and the two other legates entered the great church, walked to the high altar, and deposited a bull of excommunication. The bull was addressed to Cerularius personally and charged him with simony, Valesianism (a heretical sect), Arianism, Donatism, Nicolaitism, Manichaeism, and numerous other heresies — a list so wild in its accusations as to be more a polemical broadside than a legal document. The legates then turned, shook the dust from their feet, and walked out. A deacon ran ~ 후에 them in the street, begging them to take back the bull; they refused. The bull fell to the ground. Patriarch Cerularius convened the Holy Synod, which formally excommunicated the three legates on July 24, 1054. The emperors of Rome and Constantinople, the two most 강력한 Christian rulers in 세계, were now each presiding over a church that had anathematized the other's senior clergy.

The immediate reaction in Constantinople was one of outrage: the populace sided strongly with Cerularius against what was perceived as arrogant Western overreach. The negotiations for a military alliance collapsed. In Rome, the response was more muted — most Latin Christians had little awareness of or interest in the arcane theological and jurisdictional disputes with Constantinople. For many years afterward, Christians on both sides continued to have complex, overlapping relationships; the formal schism of 1054 took time to filter down from the diplomatic and ecclesiastical level to the lived reality of ordinary believers. The true depth of the rupture became clear only with the catastrophic Fourth Crusade of 1204, when Western Crusaders sacked Constantinople — an act of 폭력 that Eastern Orthodox Christians still recall as the moment when the schism became irreparable in the consciousness of ordinary people.

## Multiple Perspectives

### Roman Catholic View

The Roman Catholic Church understands the Great Schism as a deeply regrettable rupture caused by the Eastern church's resistance to the legitimate authority of the Successor of Peter — the 주교 of Rome — who holds, by divine institution, the supreme and universal governance of the entire Church. The Catholic theology of the papacy rests on Jesus' words to Peter in Matthew 16:18–19 ("You are Peter, and on this rock I will build my church... I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven") and on the historical primacy that Rome exercised, as the see of the martyred apostles Peter and Paul, in the first centuries of Christianity. From this perspective, the Eastern church's refusal to acknowledge the full scope of papal authority is a theological error, not merely an institutional disagreement.

On the *filioque* question — whether the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone or from the Father and the Son — Catholic theology holds that the Western addition to the Nicene Creed reflects genuine revealed truth about the immanent Trinity. The *filioque* was introduced into the Creed by the Frankish church in the 8th 세기, spread to Rome by the 11th 세기, and has been defended by Western theologians from Anselm of Canterbury to Thomas Aquinas on the grounds that the eternal relation between Father and Son demands that the Spirit, proceeding from the Father, proceeds from the Father as the Father-of-the-Son — hence also from the Son. Catholic ecumenists have worked ~ 이래로 the Second Vatican Council (1962–1965) to recover a more collegial understanding of 교회 and to frame the primacy of Rome in terms of service rather than domination, but the fundamental claim of papal universal jurisdiction has not been abandoned.

For Catholic historians and ecumenists, the Great Schism is a scandal — a word used in its strong theological sense, a stumbling block to faith and unity — that resulted from the failures of human pride, cultural misunderstanding, and political rivalry on both sides. The Catholic Church officially regards the Eastern Orthodox as separated brethren who have preserved valid sacraments and apostolic succession, and the dialogue toward reunion — formalized ~을 통해 the Joint International Commission for Theological Dialogue between the Catholic Church and the Orthodox Church, established in 1979 — remains one of the most 중요한 ecumenical priorities of the 현대의 church. But the path to unity requires agreement on the papacy — and this remains the most difficult theological obstacle of all.

### Eastern Orthodox View

The Eastern Orthodox Church understands the Great Schism as the moment when the Roman papacy's centuries-long drift toward an unprecedented and illegitimate claim of universal monarchical authority finally broke communion with the rest of 교회. In Orthodox ecclesiology, 교회 is fundamentally conciliar: authority is held collegially by the bishops gathered in councils, and no single 주교 — not even the 주교 of Rome — can claim to govern the universal church by himself. The primacy of Rome that Orthodox theology accepts is a primacy of honor (*primus inter pares*, first among equals among the 고대의 patriarchates), not a primacy of jurisdiction. The papal claims asserted by 추기경 Humbert in 1054 — and vastly amplified in subsequent papal documents culminating in the First Vatican Council's declaration of papal infallibility in 1870 — represent, from the Orthodox perspective, a heretical ecclesiological innovation with no foundation in the first-millennium church.

The *filioque* is, from the Orthodox perspective, simultaneously a theological error and a canonical scandal. Theologically, Orthodox theology insists that the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone (following John 15:26: "the Spirit of truth who proceeds from the Father"). The monarchy of the Father — the principle that the Father alone is the unoriginate source, the *arche*, of divine life within the Trinity — is a cornerstone of Orthodox Trinitarian theology, developed by the Cappadocian Fathers. To say the Spirit proceeds from the Son as well is to introduce a second principle within the Trinity, potentially dividing the divine source. Canonically, the Council of Ephesus (431 CE) explicitly prohibited adding anything to the Nicene Creed, and the Western church's unilateral insertion of the *filioque* — without an ecumenical council — is itself a violation of conciliar authority, an instance of the very papal overreach that Orthodox theology condemns.

For Eastern Orthodox Christians, the wound of 1054 was deepened incalculably by the sack of Constantinople in 1204 by the Fourth Crusade — Western Christian knights who looted, burned, and desecrated the churches of the most Christian city in 세계. Three centuries of Latin rule over parts of Greece and the imposition of Catholic hierarchies on Orthodox populations left a scar in Eastern Orthodox memory that makes the mere exchange of anathemas in 1054 look like a minor diplomatic incident by comparison. The lifting of the 1054 excommunications by Paul VI and Patriarch Athenagoras in 1964 was a gesture of goodwill welcomed by Orthodox Christians, but the deeper theological and ecclesiological questions — above all, the question of the papacy — remain unresolved, and full communion between Rome and Constantinople is not imminent.

### Modern Ecumenical Scholars View

현대의 scholars of Christian history and ecumenical theology approach the Great Schism with a much more nuanced and in some ways more melancholy perspective than either the Catholic or the Orthodox tradition tends to provide. The work of historians such as Yves Congar (*After Nine Hundred Years*, 1959), Steven Runciman (*The Eastern Schism*, 1955), and more recently John Meyendorff and Alexander Schmemann has illuminated how the schism resulted from a complex convergence of long-term structural factors, cultural divergence, political accidents, and the personal failures of specific individuals — rather than from a clear-cut theological choice between truth and error.

The *filioque*, 예를 들어 looks to 현대의 scholars less like a cosmic theological battle and more like a tragedy of miscommunication. The Greek *ekporeusis* (the term used in the original Creed for the Spirit's procession from the Father) and the Latin *processio* do not map exactly onto one another; Western theologians who spoke of the Spirit "proceeding" from the Father and the Son were often saying something that Eastern theologians who understood the terminology might have found acceptable, or at least discussable. The 1995 clarification by the Pontifical Council for Promoting Christian Unity (*The Greek and Latin Traditions Regarding the Procession of the Holy Spirit*) represents the Catholic Church's acknowledgment that the Eastern formula "from the Father through the Son" may be compatible with Western orthodoxy — a remarkable concession that suggests the *filioque* controversy, ~하는 동안 real, may have been partly a terminological dispute magnified by cultural hostility.

The events of 1054 themselves, in the judgment of most 현대의 historians, were more a symptom than a cause. The actual exchange of bulls involved a dead 교황's legates, a bull whose legal validity was questionable, and a list of heresy charges so extravagant as to be absurd. The schism that "happened" in 1054 had been developing ~ 이래로 at least the 9th 세기, was temporarily papered over multiple times, and only became permanent in the consciousness of ordinary Christians ~ 후에 1204. The ecumenical movement of the 20th 세기 — culminating in the Second Vatican Council's new approach to the Orthodox as churches with valid sacraments and apostolic succession, and in the 1964 lifting of the anathemas — has produced genuine theological progress. But it has also revealed how deep the differences run: not just on the papacy and the *filioque*, but on the theology of created and uncreated grace (the Palamite controversy), on purgatory, on the Immaculate Conception, on the relationship between scripture and tradition, and on a dozen other issues that emerged in the centuries of separate development ~ 후에 1054.

## 유산 & Consequences

The immediate consequences of 1054 were less dramatic than the event's retrospective importance might suggest. Trade, diplomatic contact, and even some ecclesiastical interaction between East and West continued. Christians on both sides were probably more concerned with the threats posed by the Seljuk Turks advancing from the east and the Norman conquests reshaping southern Italy than with the finer points of a theological dispute between clerics in Rome and Constantinople. The schism became truly definitive only gradually — and was sealed in the popular consciousness by the catastrophe of 1204, when the Fourth Crusade's sack of Constantinople turned theological estrangement into visceral, lived hostility. The Latin Empire of Constantinople (1204–1261) imposed Catholic hierarchies on Orthodox populations, desecrated holy sites, and looted sacred objects — including the relics of saints — in ways that generated a level of bitterness not fully healed to this day.

Over the following centuries, the schism shaped the entire political and cultural geography of Christian Europe. The 선교사 expansion of the two churches proceeded along different axes: Catholicism spread to Western Europe, the Americas, sub-Saharan Africa, and parts of Asia, ~하는 동안 Orthodoxy spread to Russia, the Balkans, Ukraine, Georgia, and Ethiopia. The conversion of 왕자 Vladimir of Kiev in 988 CE to Eastern Christianity — predating the schism but made definitive by it — determined that the largest Slavic state would remain in the Orthodox orbit, a consequence whose political ramifications continue to resonate in the 21st 세기 in the form of tensions between Russia (which sees itself as the heir to Orthodox Byzantine civilization) and the Catholic-influenced cultures of Poland, Hungary, and Western Ukraine. The deep cultural and political fault line between Western and Eastern Europe, which traces its origins at least in part to the religious division of 1054, ~의 하나가 되었다 the defining axes of European history ~을 통해 the Cold 전쟁 and beyond.

The formal gestures of reconciliation that have been made ~ 이래로 the Second Vatican Council — the 1964 lifting of the anathemas, 교황 John Paul II's historic visit to Greece in 2001 (~ 동안 which he apologized for the sack of Constantinople), the ongoing theological dialogues ~을 통해 the Joint International Commission — represent genuine progress but have not resolved the fundamental ecclesiological question. The papacy remains the central stumbling block: whether Rome's primacy is one of honor and fraternal seniority (the Orthodox position) or of universal jurisdiction and teaching authority (the Catholic position) is not a technical question that can be resolved by careful drafting. It reflects two genuinely different visions of what 교회 is and how it exercises authority. The Great Schism of 1054 thus remains open — not as a dated historical curiosity, but as a living theological challenge to the unity that Christians profess as an article of their faith.
