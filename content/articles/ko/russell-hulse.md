## 요약

Russell Hulse shared the 1993 Nobel Prize for discovering the first binary pulsar, providing evidence for gravitational waves.

## 과학적 중요성

- The binary pulsar discovered by Hulse and Joseph H. Taylor Jr. (often referred to as PSR B1913+16) became a major test of Albert Einstein’s general theory of relativity.
- Long-term timing observations showed the orbital period shrinking at a rate consistent with energy loss from gravitational radiation, matching general relativity’s prediction.

## Discovery Context

- The discovery was made using radio astronomy techniques and pulsar timing, where extremely regular pulses allow precise measurement of orbital dynamics.
- The system’s relativistic effects (such as periastron advance and time dilation signatures) provided multiple, independent checks on gravitational theory.

## Recognition and Later Career

- The 1993 Nobel Prize in Physics recognized the discovery’s role in establishing strong indirect evidence for gravitational waves years before direct detections were achieved.
- Hulse continued work in astrophysics and related areas, and his contributions are often discussed in the broader history of experimental tests of relativity.
