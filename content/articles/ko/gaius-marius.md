Critics also challenge the notion that Marius's military reforms were entirely beneficial. ~하는 동안 they created a more effective army in the short term, they also created personal armies loyal to generals rather than to the state, providing the mechanism for military strongmen to overthrow constitutional government.[^116] The fact that these reforms contributed directly to the rise of figures like Sulla, Pompey, and Caesar—all of whom used their armies to pursue personal political objectives—suggests that Marius's military legacy was profoundly destructive to republican institutions.[^117]

게다가 some historians argue that Marius's military successes have been overstated. Sulla deserves 중요한 credit for the 성공적인 conclusion of the Jugurthine 전쟁, and Marius's victories over the Germanic tribes, ~하는 동안 중요한, were achieved against enemies who were brave but poorly organized and tactically unsophisticated compared to Roman forces.[^118] His military reputation rested heavily on propaganda and self-promotion as much as on genuine tactical genius.

The scholarly consensus among critics is perhaps best summarized in the judgment that Marius "died so hated by contemporaries," and this hatred was justified by his 후기의-career transformation into a paranoid, violent 폭군 whose personal ambitions took precedence over the Republic's welfare.[^119] ~하는 동안 he achieved genuine military successes earlier in his career, his ultimate legacy was teaching future Romans that personal ambition backed by military force could triumph over constitutional government—a lesson that would prove fatal to the Republic he had once defended.[^120]

## 유명한 인용문

> "The law speaks too softly to be heard amidst the din of arms."
>
> This quote, attributed to Marius ~ 동안 period of civil 분쟁, reflects his justification for using military force to achieve political objectives, essentially arguing that legal niceties become irrelevant when armies are involved—a dangerous principle that helped normalize the use of 폭력 in Roman politics.[^121]

> "I did not learn to read Greek; there seemed little point in learning a language whose teachers were slaves."
>
> This quote (reported by Plutarch) reveals Marius's attitude toward Greek culture and 교육, which the Roman aristocracy typically embraced. It demonstrates both his rough, military character and his appeal to Roman traditionalists who viewed Greek cultural influence with suspicion.[^122]

> "In war, events of importance are the result of trivial causes."
>
> Attributed to Marius, this quote reflects his military wisdom and understanding that small tactical decisions and seemingly minor factors could determine the outcome of battles. It demonstrates the strategic insight that made him a 성공적인 장군.[^123]

> "I carry my decorations on my body."
>
> When criticized by aristocrats for lacking 뛰어난 ancestry, Marius reportedly gestured to his battle scars, arguing that his military service and wounds represented honors superior to inherited family names. This quote encapsulates his self-made identity and challenge to aristocratic privilege.[^124]

> "Perhaps this is the man"
>
> ~하는 동안 not Marius's own words, but rather Scipio Aemilianus's comment about the 젊은 Marius at Numantia (~에 따르면 Plutarch), this quote proved prophetic. It represents the moment when Rome's established military elite first recognized Marius's exceptional potential, foreshadowing his future role as Rome's defender.[^125]
