## 개요

김일성 (1912-1994) was the founding leader of the Democratic People's Republic of Korea (북한), serving as its supreme leader from 1948 until his death in 1994.[^1] Born Kim Song-ju, he adopted the name "김일성" (meaning "Kim become the sun") in 1935 while fighting as an anti-일본ese guerrilla in Manchuria.[^2] Through a combination of Soviet backing, ruthless purges of political rivals, and the creation of an unprecedented personality cult, Kim transformed 북한 into one of the world's most totalitarian states.[^3] His legacy includes the 한국전쟁, the establishment of the Juche ideology of self-reliance, a hereditary dictatorship that continues through his descendants, and a catastrophic 인권 record that persists decades after his death.[^4]

---

## 초기 생애와 배경

김일성 was born Kim Song-ju on April 15, 1912, in the small village of Mangyongdae-guyok near 평양, Korea, during the 일본ese colonial occupation.[^5] His father, Kim Hyong-jik, was variously described as a liberal-minded teacher, an occasional practitioner of traditional Korean medicine, and an independence activist who opposed 일본ese rule.[^6] His mother, Kang Pan-sok, came from a Presbyterian Christian family, and young Kim was raised in a devoutly Christian household—his maternal grandfather served as a Protestant minister, and his father was an elder in the Presbyterian Church who had attended a missionary school.[^7]

The Kim family's life under 일본ese occupation was marked by hardship and resistance. In May 1919, when Kim was only seven years old, his father took the family into exile, fleeing to Manchuria to escape 일본ese persecution.[^8] They settled in Badaogou, 중국, where Kim's formative years were spent among Korean expatriate communities resisting 일본ese imperialism. This early experience of displacement and anti-colonial struggle would profoundly shape Kim's political consciousness and revolutionary identity.

Kim attended Whasung Military Academy in 1926 but left after only one year in 1927.[^9] He then enrolled at Yuwen Middle School in Jilin, 중국, where he studied until 1930. During his teenage years, Kim became increasingly involved in communist youth activities. In 1931, he joined the Chinese Communist Youth League, marking the beginning of his formal association with communist organizations.[^10] His political activism led to his first arrest—he was jailed for several months in 1929 when police discovered his underground Marxist organization.[^11]

The death of Kim's father in 1926, when Kim was just fourteen, left a lasting impression on the young revolutionary.[^12] This personal loss, combined with witnessing 일본ese oppression firsthand, fueled Kim's commitment to the anti-일본ese resistance movement. His mother, Kang Pan-sok, would later be incorporated into 북한's official mythology as the "mother of Korea," though she played a less direct role in Kim's political career.[^13] Kim also had two younger brothers, Kim Ch'ŏl-chu and Kim Yong-ju, the latter of whom would later become known as "the revolutionary fighter" within the Kim dynasty's personality cult.[^14]

## 권력의 부상

김일성's path to power began in the forests and mountains of Manchuria, where he joined various anti-일본ese guerrilla groups in northern 중국 during the early 1930s.[^15] In 1931, after joining the Chinese Communist Party, Kim became a member of the organization committee for Fusong and Antu of the Korean Farmers' General Union in Eastern Provinces.[^16] His guerrilla career took a significant step forward in February 1936 when he joined the Northeast Anti-일본ese United Army, a guerrilla force established by the Chinese Communist Party.[^17]

Kim rapidly rose through the guerrilla ranks, demonstrating both military capability and leadership skills. In 1937, at the remarkably young age of 24, he was appointed commander of the 6th division, controlling a few hundred men in what became known as "김일성's division."[^18] While historians generally accept that Kim's exploits were later exaggerated by the personality cult built around him, there is consensus that he was indeed a significant guerrilla leader who conducted effective operations against 일본ese forces.[^19]

The most famous of Kim's guerrilla actions occurred on June 4, 1937, when he led approximately 200 guerrillas in a daring raid on Poch'onbo, a town just inside the Korean border.[^20] During this attack, Kim's forces destroyed local government offices, set fire to a 일본ese police station and post office, and briefly occupied the town before withdrawing. The 일본ese military regarded Kim as one of the most effective and popular Korean guerrilla leaders of the era.[^21] By late 1939, the 일본ese had placed a 200,000 yen reward—an enormous sum—for any information leading to Kim's capture, making him the most wanted guerrilla in all of Manchuria.[^22]

However, the tide turned against the guerrillas by the late 1930s. After effective 일본ese counterinsurgency campaigns that reduced guerrilla numbers from well over 200,000 in the early 1930s to just a few thousand by the mid-1930s, Kim was forced to retreat.[^23] By 1939, 일본ese forces had gained the upper hand, and Kim fled from Manchuria to the Soviet Far East. He crossed into Soviet territory and settled in the area around Khabarovsk, where he received military and political training from Soviet authorities and served in the Red Army during 제2차 세계대전.[^24]

Kim's fortunes changed dramatically with 일본's defeat in 1945. Stalin, viewing 북한 as important to Soviet security interests in defending the 아시아n front, identified 김일성 as a potential communist leader who could be installed to ensure Soviet control over northern Korea.[^25] 라브렌티 베리야, Stalin's notorious security chief, personally selected Kim for this role.[^26] On September 19, 1945, Kim and 66 other Korean Red Army officers who had fought the 일본ese in Manchuria and subsequently lived and trained in the USSR arrived in Wonsan.[^27]

On October 14, 1945, Soviet authorities formally introduced Kim to the 북한n public, presenting him as a guerrilla hero and national liberator.[^28] The Soviets provided massive military support, shipping 17,000 rifles and carbines, 5,800 machine guns, 268 mortars, and 234 artillery guns in 1946 alone to arm the newly formed Korean People's Army.[^29] Using Soviet advisers and equipment, Kim constructed a large, well-equipped army trained in infiltration tactics and guerrilla warfare.[^30]

In February 1946, the 북한n Provisional People's Committee was established with 김일성 as chairman.[^31] Following elections held in the northern occupation zone on August 25, 1948, the Democratic People's Republic of Korea was officially proclaimed on September 9, 1948, with Kim installed as the Soviet-designated premier.[^32]

## 주요 업적

김일성's most significant policy achievement was the development and implementation of the Juche ideology, a philosophy of national self-reliance that became the defining principle of the 북한n state.[^33] Introduced in the mid-1950s and refined throughout his rule, Juche positioned 북한 as ideologically independent from both Soviet and Chinese communism. The ideology emphasized that "man is the master of all things and the decisive factor in everything," placing human agency and Korean national identity at the center of political, economic, and social organization.[^34] Juche became the foundation not only for 북한's domestic policies but also for the personality cult surrounding Kim and his family, effectively rendering communist ideology and Korean nationalism subservient to absolute submission to the leader.[^35]

In the economic sphere, Kim oversaw rapid industrialization and growth during the 1950s and 1960s, a period when 북한's economy actually outperformed 대한민국's.[^36] After the devastation of the 한국전쟁, Kim immediately launched an ambitious three-year reconstruction period followed by a five-year economic plan in 1956, modeled on Soviet five-year plans.[^37] The strategy focused heavily on heavy industry and arms production, pushing the economy forward at record growth rates.[^38] Rich in natural resources and benefiting from extensive Chinese and Soviet assistance, 북한's economy performed impressively during this period.[^39] By the 1960s, 북한 had achieved a slightly higher standard of living than 대한민국, which was then suffering from political chaos and economic crises.[^40]

Kim's land reform program, implemented between 1946 and 1958, fundamentally transformed 북한n agriculture.[^41] Land was confiscated from 일본ese colonizers and Korean landlords and redistributed, then subsequently collectivized into cooperative farms. While the collectivization process faced significant resistance—many farmers killed their livestock rather than surrender them to collective farms—the program succeeded in eliminating the traditional landlord class and establishing state control over agricultural production.[^42]

On the international stage, Kim achieved several diplomatic successes, particularly in positioning 북한 within the Non-Aligned Movement. Although the Democratic People's Republic of Korea initially faced skepticism from Third World nations, Kim successfully gained membership in the Non-Aligned Movement in August 1975.[^43] Between 1973 and 1979, Kim exchanged no fewer than 25 items of correspondence with Yugoslav leader 요시프 브로즈 티토, who served as Kim's mentor and advocate within the movement.[^44] This relationship was particularly remarkable given that Kim had spent over two decades denouncing Tito as ideologically backward and revisionist, demonstrating Kim's pragmatic flexibility in foreign policy when it served 북한's interests.[^45]

Kim's longevity itself represents a kind of achievement in the brutal world of 20th-century authoritarian politics. He served as 북한's supreme leader for more than 45 years, making him the third-longest serving non-royal head of state and government in the 20th century.[^46] Through systematic purges and the creation of an omnipresent personality cult, Kim successfully eliminated all potential rivals and established absolute personal control over every aspect of 북한n life—political, economic, and cultural.[^47]

Perhaps most significantly for the Kim family legacy, Kim established the world's first communist dynasty by successfully engineering the succession of his son, 김정일. At the 6th Workers' Party of Korea 의회 in 1980, Kim's oldest son was elevated to the Presidium and officially designated as successor, institutionalizing hereditary rule.[^48] By the time of 김일성's death in 1994, the transition mechanism was firmly in place, allowing 김정일 to assume power smoothly and ensuring the continuation of the Kim family's control over 북한 into the 21st century.[^49]

## 논란과 비판

The 한국전쟁 stands as 김일성's most catastrophic and controversial decision. After obtaining approval from 이오시프 스탈린, Kim ordered the invasion of 대한민국 on June 25, 1950, sparking a three-year conflict that killed approximately three million people, more than half of whom were 북한n.[^50] The war, intended to reunify the 한반도 under communist rule by force, ended in military stalemate and the continued division of Korea when an armistice was signed in July 1953.[^51] Kim's attempt at forcible reunification not only failed utterly but devastated the 한반도, leaving a legacy of division, militarization, and inter-Korean hostility that persists seven decades later.[^52]

Beyond the 한국전쟁, Kim's regime engaged in systematic terrorism and provocations that damaged 북한's international standing. The year 1968 proved particularly damaging. On January 21, Kim authorized the Blue House raid, sending 31 북한n commandos to assassinate 대한민국n President Park Chung-hee.[^53] The commandos were intercepted near the presidential residence, resulting in fierce fighting that left 26 대한민국ns and four Americans dead, along with 66 wounded, including 24 civilians.[^54] Just two days later, 북한n forces seized the USS Pueblo, a U.S. Navy intelligence ship, escalating tensions to the brink of another major war.[^55]

These 1968 incidents reflected Kim's strategic miscalculations. While Kim hoped to assist North 베트남 by opening a second front against the 미국 and to strengthen 북한's alliance with 중국, the aggressive tactics backfired.[^56] The Pueblo crisis harmed 평양's reputation in the Third World by demonstrating that Kim's guerrilla-style approach risked plunging the 한반도 into catastrophic conflict.[^57] The incidents particularly damaged Kim's relationship with 베트남ese Communists, who had not requested such provocations, and further deteriorated already declining relations with the 소련.[^58]

Kim's consolidation of absolute power required the systematic elimination of all potential rivals through brutal purges that consumed thousands of lives. Following the 한국전쟁, Kim orchestrated a series of purges targeting the four major factions within the Korean Workers' Party: the domestic faction, his own guerrilla faction's rivals, the Soviet faction, and the Yan'an (Chinese) faction.[^59] Pak Hon-yong, leader of the Korean Communist Party, was purged and executed in 1955.[^60] The August 1956 faction incident represented a pivotal moment when Kim successfully resisted Soviet and Chinese pressure to institute collective leadership, instead consolidating his personal dictatorship.[^61] By 1961, only two members of the Soviet faction, three members of the Yan'an faction, and three members of the domestic faction remained in a Central Committee of 68 members.[^62] The 1967 Kapsan faction incident eliminated even these remnants, leading to the introduction of the Monolithic Ideological System that codified Kim's absolute authority.[^63]

The personality cult Kim created surpassed even those of his models, 이오시프 스탈린 and Mao Zedong, in its pervasiveness and extremity.[^64] Kim designated himself "Supreme Leader" and cultivated public belief in his immortality, infallibility, and omnipresence.[^65] By 1960, approximately 10,000 statues, portraits, or murals of Kim decorated 평양 alone.[^66] Every 북한n home was required to display Kim's portrait, with severe penalties—including imprisonment—for any damage to or removal of these images.[^67] When Kim died in 1994, citizens found not mourning were sent to prison.[^68] During the de-Stalinization period in the USSR in 1956, 북한n students studying in the 소련 who criticized Kim's growing personality cult upon their return home were subjected to intensive interrogation, with those deemed suspicious killed in secret.[^69]

Kim's most enduring crime was the establishment of a comprehensive system of political repression and 인권 violations that continues to this day. In 1957, Kim created the songbun system, a rigid caste structure dividing 북한n society into three classes—"core," "wavering," or "hostile"—based on political, social, and economic background.[^70] The songbun system determined every aspect of existence: access to education, housing, employment, food rations, party membership, and residential permits.[^71] Those classified in the hostile class faced systematic discrimination, and many were forcibly relocated to 북한's isolated and impoverished northern provinces.[^72]

Kim established an authoritarian government that systematically eradicated independent media, free trade unions, and all independent organizations.[^73] He created a hidden gulag system of political prison camps (kwanliso), fenced and heavily guarded colonies located in remote mountainous areas where prisoners performed back-breaking labor such as logging, mining, and agricultural work.[^74] Hundreds of thousands of 북한ns disappeared into these camps, from which few ever returned.[^75] Entire extended families of perceived dissenters were punished collectively by reduction to the lowest songbun rank and often incarcerated in the prison camp system.[^76] Eyewitness testimony from defectors documents gruesome public executions for possessing or importing Bibles and for participating in clandestine worship.[^77]

## 개인 생활

김일성's family life was carefully constructed to support his political power and dynastic ambitions, with family members integrated into 북한's official mythology and personality cult. His mother, Kang Pan-sok, was posthumously elevated to the status of "mother of Korea," while his first wife, Kim Jong-suk, became known as the "mother of the revolution."[^78] This deliberate mythologizing of family members served to create a sacred revolutionary lineage that justified hereditary succession and enhanced the Kim family's claim to rule.

Kim Jong-suk (1917-1949) was 김일성's first wife and the mother of his heir, 김정일.[^79] She had been Kim's compatriot during the anti-일본ese guerrilla struggle in Manchuria, sharing the hardships and dangers of partisan warfare. Their relationship thus carried revolutionary legitimacy that 김일성 exploited for political purposes. After Kim Jong-suk's death in 1949 under circumstances that remain somewhat mysterious, Kim married Kim Song-ae, who had worked as a clerical worker in the Ministry of National Defense.[^80]

Kim Song-ae first met 김일성 in 1948 while working in his residence as an assistant to Kim Jong-suk.[^81] After the latter's death, Kim Song-ae assumed management of 김일성's household and domestic affairs. The couple married, and in 1953, Kim Song-ae gave birth to their first child, a daughter named Kim Kyong-jin.[^82] She subsequently had at least two sons with 김일성: Kim Pyong-il (born 1954) and Kim Yong-il (born 1955).[^83] However, 김일성's decision to designate 김정일—his son with his first wife—as successor created significant family tensions. Kim Song-ae reportedly advocated for her own sons to be considered for succession, but 김일성 remained committed to the son who symbolized his guerrilla revolutionary heritage.[^84]

김일성 had two younger brothers who played supporting roles in his regime. Kim Ch'ŏl-chu remains a shadowy figure in historical records, but Kim Yong-ju became more prominent, earning the designation "the revolutionary fighter" within the family cult.[^85] The extended Kim family formed an elite political dynasty, with relatives occupying important positions throughout the party, military, and government apparatus.

As a leader, 김일성 exhibited traits that psychological profiles suggest align with ISTJ personality characteristics: a strong sense of duty and responsibility, a logical and practical approach to problem-solving, and a preference for traditional values and established systems.[^86] However, these traits manifested in service of authoritarian control rather than democratic governance. Kim demonstrated remarkable political cunning in playing Chinese and Soviet patrons against each other, maintaining 북한n autonomy while extracting maximum economic and military aid from both.[^87]

Those who met Kim often commented on his charisma and leadership presence. Kim "emerged from the purge as a definitive leader, not only for the bold move but also for his compassion," according to some accounts, though this "compassion" was highly selective and did not extend to perceived enemies.[^88] His son 김정일 was later described as having "inherited his father's kingdom but none of his charisma or leadership skills," suggesting that 김일성 himself possessed significant personal magnetism that helped him maintain power.[^89]

Kim authored multi-volume autobiographical memoirs titled "With the Century," which covered his early life and revolutionary activities, though heavily filtered through the lens of his manufactured "glorious" image.[^90] In these memoirs, Kim systematically skipped details unfavorable to his legend and claimed credit for initiatives, such as the establishment of the 88th Special Brigade, that were actually Soviet in origin.[^91]

## 유산

김일성 died from a heart attack at his home in 평양 on July 7, 1994, just days before a scheduled unprecedented summit with 대한민국n President 김영삼 that might have eased inter-Korean tensions.[^92] His death came at a crucial moment, cutting short what could have been a historic diplomatic breakthrough. In 1997, the Democratic People's Republic of Korea formally adopted Kim's birth year, 1912, as the base for a new Juche calendar, making Kim's birth the zero point of 북한n time itself.[^93] Upon his death, Kim was declared the "Eternal President" of 북한, afforded near-godlike status that extends far beyond typical posthumous honors for deceased leaders.[^94]

The order 김일성 built rests on six pillars that scholars identify as fundamental to the system: cult, control, secrecy, hierarchy, exploitation, and terror—all of which remain present in 북한 today, more than three decades after his death.[^95] This enduring institutional structure represents Kim's most significant legacy. Kim ensured that no national identity could exist apart from absolute submission to the leader, a principle his successors have maintained and intensified.[^96]

What distinguishes 김일성's legacy from those of other 20th-century dictators is its persistence. Stalin and Mao's legacies were largely dismantled by their successors within three years of their deaths—Khrushchev's de-Stalinization and Deng Xiaoping's economic reforms fundamentally repudiated key elements of their predecessors' systems.[^97] In stark contrast, more than 30 years after 김일성's death, 북한 still swears eternal loyalty to him, remains ruled by his bloodline through his grandson 김정은, and continues as the hypermilitarized, impoverished, closed society with a terrorized population that he envisioned.[^98]

The legacy of 김일성's manufactured collective memory continues to shape 북한n nationalism. As Kim consolidated power throughout the 1950s, the centrality of the anti-일본ese guerrilla revolution was emphasized across politics, economics, society, and literature.[^99] In 북한 today, the legacy of the guerrilla movement against 일본 and the 한국전쟁 against the 미국 constitute essential elements of nationalism, with rulers using this carefully constructed historical narrative as rationale for national unification under 북한n rule.[^100]

Kim's economic legacy is decidedly mixed. While 북한's state-run economy grew rapidly in the 1950s and 1960s, it eventually stagnated, with critical food shortages emerging by the early 1990s.[^101] Kim's misguided policies created 북한n economic reliance on the 소련 and 중국, and his implementation of collective farming completely disregarded the expertise of individual farmers.[^102] Most problems that emerged during 김정일's era arose directly from policies established during his father's regime.[^103] The 북한n famine that began in the mid-1990s, coinciding with 김정일's assumption of power after 김일성's death, was attributed to the withdrawal of Soviet aid combined with the Stalinist economic system 김일성 had created, which provided little incentive for food production.[^104] Between 1995 and 1998, severe flooding combined with systemic economic failure resulted in a devastating famine that killed between 900,000 and 2.4 million people.[^105]

The Kim dynasty itself represents 김일성's most visible legacy. The Kim family, officially designated the "Mount Paektu Bloodline," encompasses three generations of 북한n leadership: 김일성 (1948-1994), 김정일 (1994-2011), and 김정은 (2011-present).[^106] This hereditary succession in a nominally communist state represents an unprecedented fusion of monarchy and totalitarianism, creating what analysts term a "personalist dictatorship" unlike any other in modern history.[^107]

## 역사적 평가

### 긍정적 견해

Within 북한, 김일성 remains the object of veneration approaching religious worship, presented as the greatest Korean who ever lived and the father of the nation. Official 북한n historiography portrays him as a military genius who single-handedly defeated 일본ese imperialism, a visionary who created an independent socialist paradise, and a philosopher who developed Juche thought as Korea's unique contribution to world revolutionary theory.[^108] This official narrative, while clearly propagandistic, represents how millions of 북한ns have been taught to understand their nation's founding.

Some scholars acknowledge that despite the exaggerations of the personality cult, Kim was indeed a significant anti-일본ese guerrilla fighter who demonstrated genuine military leadership and courage during the 1930s.[^109] The Poch'onbo raid and other guerrilla actions were real historical events that did strike blows against 일본ese colonial control, even if their strategic impact was limited.[^110] In this narrow context, Kim's anti-colonial resistance credentials have some historical foundation, distinguishing him from leaders whose revolutionary biographies were entirely fabricated.

Sympathetic analysts, particularly from nations that were part of the Non-Aligned Movement, sometimes credited Kim with skillfully navigating the challenging geopolitics of the 냉전, maintaining 북한n sovereignty while extracting economic and military support from both the 소련 and 중국.[^111] From this perspective, Juche ideology represented a genuine attempt at ideological independence rather than mere subordination to either 모스크바 or 베이징, even if the practical implementation fell far short of true self-reliance.[^112]

Kim's ability to maintain stability in 북한 for 46 years, avoiding the coups and internal conflicts that plagued many post-colonial states, is sometimes noted as evidence of political acumen, though critics would argue this "stability" came at the cost of systematic terror and totalitarian control.[^113] Some observers point to the relatively successful economic performance of the 1950s and 1960s as evidence that Kim's early economic policies achieved real industrialization and development, at least before the system's inherent contradictions led to stagnation.[^114]

Defenders of Kim's legacy sometimes argue that many of 북한's worst 인권 abuses and economic failures emerged or intensified under his successors, suggesting that 김정일 and 김정은 bear responsibility for the system's most catastrophic outcomes.[^115] However, this argument fails to account for the fact that 김일성 himself designed the institutional foundations—the songbun system, the prison camps, the personality cult, the command economy—that enabled these later atrocities.[^116]

### 부정적 견해

The overwhelming consensus among international scholars, 인권 organizations, and defector testimony presents 김일성 as one of the 20th century's most destructive dictators, responsible for millions of deaths and the creation of perhaps the world's most repressive state.[^117] Human Rights Watch has characterized 김일성's legacy as "catastrophic," establishing an authoritarian government that crushed dissent, abducted foreign nationals from 대한민국, 일본, and elsewhere, and disappeared hundreds of thousands into a hidden gulag system from which few ever returned.[^118]

The 유엔 Commission of Inquiry on 인권 in 북한 heard extensive testimony from defectors, producing a 372-page report that serves as a devastating indictment of the 북한n system Kim created.[^119] By 2010-2011, approximately 23,000 북한n refugees had reached 대한민국, including hundreds of persons formerly detained in 북한's variety of slave labor camps, penitentiaries, and detention facilities.[^120] Their consistent testimony provides overwhelming documentation of the systematic 인권 violations built into the system Kim designed.[^121]

Scholars emphasize that Kim directed all aspects of economic, political, and cultural life in 북한, providing the country with his personal ideology (Juche thought) and creating a cult of personality "without parallel in its excesses."[^122] The pervasiveness and extreme nature of 북한's personality cult surpasses that of both Stalin and Mao, its original inspirations, reaching levels of totalitarian control unprecedented in modern history.[^123] Academic analysis characterizes the 북한n system as based on "cult, control, secrecy, hierarchy, exploitation, and terror," all of which remain operative today.[^124]

The 한국전쟁, initiated by Kim's decision to invade 대한민국 in 1950, killed approximately three million people and devastated the 한반도, leaving a divided nation, massive militarization on both sides, and inter-Korean hatred that persists seventy years later.[^125] Kim's attempt at forcible reunification failed completely, achieving the opposite of his stated goal by hardening the division and making peaceful reunification infinitely more difficult.[^126]

Critics point to Kim's systematic elimination of all independent institutions and creation of absolute personal dictatorship as fundamentally anti-democratic and oppressive.[^127] The purges of the 1950s and 1960s that eliminated thousands of perceived rivals demonstrated ruthless consolidation of power that prioritized Kim's personal authority over any collective leadership or socialist principles.[^128] The songbun caste system created a hereditary discrimination structure that contradicts any genuine socialist or egalitarian principles.[^129]

Kim's economic legacy is viewed as fundamentally flawed. While achieving initial growth through massive Soviet and Chinese aid and by mobilizing 북한's natural resources, the centrally planned economy Kim created proved unable to sustain development.[^130] His policies of extreme militarization, collective agriculture that destroyed farmer initiative, and Juche self-reliance that actually meant isolation from global economic integration, created the conditions for the catastrophic 1990s famine.[^131] Analysts note that Kim's economic policies resulted in 북한n complete dependence on Soviet and Chinese aid, meaning that when this support ended with the 소련's collapse, the economy collapsed as well.[^132]

> "The basis of the Juche Idea is that man is the master of all things and the decisive factor in everything." - Defining the core principle of his signature ideological contribution[^138]

> "We are opposed to the line of compromise with imperialism. At the same time, we cannot tolerate the practice of only shouting against imperialism, but, in actual fact, being afraid to fight it." - Criticizing what he viewed as revisionism among communist allies[^139]
