## Criminal case: Trump Organization tax fraud (New York)

- The criminal matter focused on the Trump Organization and related entities, not only on individual executives.
- Prosecutors alleged a scheme involving compensation and benefits that were not properly reported for tax purposes.
- A jury convicted the company in 2022; reporting described the case as a significant corporate criminal conviction involving the former president’s business.
- Allen Weisselberg, a senior executive, served a prison sentence connected to the tax-fraud case.

## Civil fraud case: asset valuation and business practices

- A separate civil proceeding alleged that asset values were inflated in financial statements to obtain favorable terms.
- Civil findings and penalties differ from criminal convictions; the case centered on remedies such as monetary judgments and business restrictions.
- In 2024, reporting described a large civil judgment (often summarized around $454 million) tied to the fraud findings.

## Why the cases are often discussed together

- Media coverage frequently grouped the matters under a broad “Trump Organization fraud” label, even though they were distinct proceedings.
- The criminal case involved corporate criminal liability and tax-related charges, while the civil case involved state enforcement and financial penalties.
- Both matters became prominent examples in discussions of corporate compliance, executive accountability, and the boundary between aggressive business practices and unlawful conduct.

## Key concepts

- Corporate defendants can be prosecuted and convicted independently of individuals.
- Civil fraud cases typically apply a different burden of proof and provide different remedies than criminal cases.
- Appeals and post-judgment proceedings can affect timelines and final outcomes.
