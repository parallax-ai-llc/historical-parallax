## 요약

Spanish serial killer known as the Werewolf of Allariz who murdered at least 13 people in Galicia during the 1840s-1850s. He claimed to suffer from lycanthropy.

## 역사적 맥락

- The murders were associated with rural Galicia in the mid-19th century, a period when travel between villages could be dangerous and record-keeping was limited.
- Folkloric beliefs about witches and werewolves were part of local cultural background, shaping how communities interpreted unexplained violence.

## Crimes and Allegations (High-Level)

- Accounts describe a pattern of targeting travelers and vulnerable individuals, followed by efforts to conceal the crimes.
- The notoriety of the case grew from claims that victims were lured with promises of work or safe passage and later disappeared.

## Trial and Public Reaction

- Romasanta’s defense became famous for invoking “lycanthropy” as an explanation, positioning the defendant as afflicted rather than purely criminal.
- The case is often discussed as an early example of public interest in psychological or medical framing of violent crime, even if the legal system treated the acts as murder.

## Cultural Legacy

- The nickname “Werewolf of Allariz” persists in popular retellings, documentaries, and true-crime literature.
- The story is frequently cited in discussions of how mythic narratives can coexist with—and sometimes obscure—factual criminal investigations.

## Notes for Interpretation

- Reported victim counts and details vary by source, reflecting inconsistent contemporary documentation.
- Many later summaries emphasize the sensational aspects; careful reading separates folklore claims from what was established in court.
