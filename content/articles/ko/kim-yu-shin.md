## 개요

김유신 (595–673 CE) was a legendary Korean military general and politician who orchestrated the first unification of the 한반도 under the 신라 kingdom during the 7th century. Born into the royal lineage of the fallen Gaya kingdom, he became a Hwarang warrior at age 15 and rose to command all 신라 forces by age 34. Through strategic alliances with Tang 중국 and brilliant military leadership, he conquered both 백제 (660 CE) and 고구려 (668 CE), ending centuries of division among the Three Kingdoms. His legacy as Korea's greatest unifying general endures through countless cultural works, monuments, and the Gimhae Kim clan, which numbers over 4.4 million descendants today.[^1][^2][^3]

---

## 초기 생애와 배경

김유신 was born in 595 CE in Manno county (present-day Jincheon County) during a turbulent period when three kingdoms—신라, 백제, and 고구려—competed for dominance over the 한반도. He was the son of General Kim Seohyeon, the second son of General Kim Mu-ryeok, and Lady Manmyeong, who was the daughter of Kim Sukheuljong, the younger brother of King Jinheung.[^4][^5] This aristocratic lineage was further distinguished by his descent from King Guhae of Geumgwan Gaya, the last ruler of the Gaya state before its annexation by 신라 in 532 CE.[^6]

His unique ancestry placed him in a privileged yet complex position within 신라's rigid bone rank system (golpum), which governed political and military advancement. As a descendant of Gaya royalty integrated into 신라 society, 김유신 held "true bone" (jingol) status, the second-highest caste below "sacred bone" (seonggol).[^7][^8] This high rank would prove instrumental in his military career, though his Gaya heritage also meant his family had to prove their loyalty to the 신라 throne.

At the age of 14 or 15, 김유신 joined the Hwarang, an elite organization of young nobles who combined martial training with studies in 유교, 불교, and 도교.[^9][^10] His particular group was known as the "Band of the Dragon Flower Tree" (Yonghwa-Hyangdo), referencing the Bodhi tree under which Maitreya Buddha would teach his disciples. The Hwarang trained according to the Five Commandments established by the monk Wongwang: loyal service to the king, filial piety, faithfulness to friends, courage in battle, and the prohibition of indiscriminate killing.[^11][^12]

김유신 proved an exceptional student of the Hwarang code. He became an accomplished swordsman and was promoted to Gukseon (Hwarang leader) by the age of 18, demonstrating both martial prowess and leadership capabilities.[^13] The Hwarang's holistic education—which included poetry, music, dance, and martial arts alongside philosophical studies—shaped Kim into not merely a warrior but a cultured leader who embodied the ideals of courage, loyalty, and spirituality that would define his career.[^14][^15]

김유신 had two sisters, Kim Bo-hee and Kim Mun-hee. His sister Kim Mun-hee would later marry his close friend Kim Cheon-chu, cementing a political alliance that would prove crucial to the unification of Korea.[^16][^17] This marriage was reportedly orchestrated through a calculated incident during a polo game where 김유신 deliberately arranged circumstances for his sister to meet Kim Cheon-chu when her tassel was torn—a story that illustrates the strategic political thinking that would characterize his entire career.[^18]

## 권력의 부상

김유신's ascent through 신라's military hierarchy was remarkably rapid, driven by both his exceptional abilities and strategic family alliances. By the age of 34 in 629 CE, he had been given total command of the 신라 armed forces—an extraordinary achievement that reflected both his military competence and the political capital his family had accumulated.[^19][^20] His first military engagement in command is believed to have occurred around 629 CE, marking the beginning of a career that would span over four decades and fundamentally reshape the 한반도.[^21]

Three years later in 632 CE, a pivotal moment occurred when his cousin, Princess Deokman, ascended the throne as Queen Seondeok—신라's first female ruler. Queen Seondeok retained 김유신 as commander-in-chief of the royal army, demonstrating her trust in his abilities and their family connection.[^22][^23] During Queen Seondeok's reign (632–647 CE), 김유신's power grew considerably. He reportedly owned ten thousand private soldiers and won numerous battles against 백제, becoming one of the most powerful men in 신라.[^24][^25]

The strategic marriage between 김유신's sister, Kim Mun-hee (later known as Queen Munmyeong), and his close friend Kim Cheon-chu dramatically elevated 김유신's political standing. Kim Cheon-chu would later become 태종 Muyeol, making 김유신 the king's brother-in-law.[^26][^27] This alliance was further strengthened when 김유신 himself married Lady Jiso, the third daughter of 무열왕—making the general both the king's brother-in-law and son-in-law simultaneously.[^28] After his first wife died, he had also married another of Kim Cheon-chu's sisters, creating multiple layers of familial bonds with the royal family.[^29]

During 무열왕's reign (654–661 CE), 김유신 became the most powerful man in the court and eventually achieved the position of Sangdaedeung, one of the highest ministerial ranks in 신라.[^30][^31] This network of family ties created an unprecedented concentration of power that united military command with royal authority—a crucial foundation for the ambitious campaigns of conquest that would follow.

By 655 CE, when 고구려 and 백제 launched coordinated attacks against 신라, the strategic situation demanded bold action. Recognizing that 신라 could not defeat both rival kingdoms alone, 김유신 and 무열왕 made the fateful decision to form an alliance with Tang dynasty 중국.[^32] This diplomatic maneuver demonstrated 김유신's understanding that military victories required not just battlefield tactics but also strategic international relationships. The Tang-신라 alliance would provide the massive military resources necessary to challenge the powerful kingdoms of 백제 and 고구려, though it would also create complications that 김유신's successors would have to resolve.[^33]

김유신's rise to power was thus characterized by a sophisticated blend of martial excellence, political acumen, and strategic family alliances that positioned him as the indispensable military leader for 신라's unification ambitions.

## 주요 업적

### The Conquest of 백제 (660 CE)

김유신's most famous military triumph came in 660 CE with the conquest of 백제, one of the Three Kingdoms that had dominated Korean politics for centuries. The campaign was a massive joint operation: 김유신 led a 신라 army of 50,000 troops while Tang Emperor Gaozong dispatched a naval force of approximately 122,711 to 130,000 men.[^34][^35] This overwhelming combined force created a strategic pincer movement that would prove decisive.

The climactic engagement occurred at the Battle of Hwangsanbeol (in present-day Nonsan), where 김유신 faced General Gyebaek, one of 백제's most legendary commanders.[^36] Despite having only about 5,000 troops—outnumbered approximately ten to one—Gyebaek established three military camps and initially repelled the 신라 forces in five consecutive skirmishes, demonstrating remarkable tactical skill and the fighting spirit of his outnumbered defenders.[^37][^38]

Facing this setback, 김유신 employed a brilliant psychological tactic. General Kim Pumil sent his young son Gwanchang, a Hwarang warrior, to single-handedly charge the enemy lines.[^39] Gyebaek initially captured the youth but released him out of respect. When Gwanchang charged again, Gyebaek was forced to execute him and return his body to the 신라 forces. The martyrdom of the young Hwarang warrior dramatically renewed 신라 morale and inspired the troops to fight with renewed determination.[^40]

However, some historians argue that Gwanchang's charge served a more tactical purpose: while the 백제 forces focused on the dramatic individual combat, 김유신 divided his forces and executed a flanking maneuver, bypassing the hills along the Yeonsancheon River that flowed through Hwangsanbeol to surround Gyebaek's 5,000 troops.[^41] This use of deception—appearing to focus on one point while maneuvering elsewhere—exemplified 김유신's strategic brilliance. The 신라 forces ultimately achieved a decisive victory, and General Gyebaek died in battle.[^42]

Following the victory at Hwangsanbeol, 김유신's forces crossed the Sobaek mountains and, combined with the Tang naval forces, marched on Sabi (modern-day Buyeo), the capital of 백제.[^43] Caught in the overwhelming pincer movement and demoralized by the loss at Hwangsanbeol, 백제 surrendered in 660 CE, ending its 678-year history as an independent kingdom.[^44]

### The Fall of 고구려 (661-668 CE)

After conquering 백제, 김유신 turned his attention to the most formidable of the Three Kingdoms: 고구려. This northern kingdom controlled Manchuria and the northern half of the 한반도 and had successfully resisted multiple Chinese invasions over centuries, including massive campaigns by the Sui and early Tang dynasties.[^45]

In 661 CE, 김유신's 신라 forces and their Tang allies launched a coordinated attack on 고구려 from two directions.[^46] However, the seemingly impregnable kingdom repelled this initial assault, demonstrating why 고구려 had earned its reputation as one of 동아시아's strongest military powers. Though the attack failed to conquer 고구려, it significantly weakened the kingdom, which had already depleted its resources fighting two large-scale wars against Chinese dynasties.[^47]

김유신 and the 신라-Tang alliance maintained pressure on 고구려 over the following years. In 667 CE, they launched another major offensive, and after sustained campaigning, the allied forces finally captured 평양, 고구려's capital, in 668 CE.[^48][^49] The fall of 고구려 completed the conquest of the Three Kingdoms and represented the culmination of 김유신's military career—the 한반도 was unified under 신라 rule for the first time in history.

### Military Innovation and Leadership

Beyond specific battles, 김유신's achievements included significant innovations in military tactics and strategy. He skillfully employed flanking attacks, as demonstrated at Hwangsanbeol.[^50] He understood psychological warfare, using both martyrdom and deception to affect enemy morale. His strategic vision encompassed not just battlefield tactics but also international diplomacy, recognizing that alliance with Tang 중국—despite its risks—provided the overwhelming force necessary to achieve unification.

Throughout his career, 김유신 maintained a personal commitment to the Hwarang code of conduct, living by principles of loyalty, courage, and ethical warfare until his death.[^51] His leadership inspired intense loyalty among his troops and established a military tradition that would influence Korean martial culture for centuries.

### Honors and Rewards

In recognition of his extraordinary achievements, King Munmu bestowed numerous honors upon 김유신. In 668 CE, following the conquest of 고구려, he received the unprecedented title of Taedaegakgan (태대각간; 太大角干), approximately meaning "Supreme Herald of Defense"—a rank created specifically above the previously highest rank of Daegakgan.[^52][^53] He reportedly received a village of over 500 households as a personal reward.[^54] In 669 CE, when 174 new ranches were established throughout the kingdom, 김유신 was granted control of either 6 or 142 separate horse farms (sources vary), the largest allocation excluding royal palaces and government offices.[^55][^56]

## 논란과 비판

Despite his legendary status, 김유신's historical record and military campaigns have faced significant scholarly criticism, particularly regarding the reliability of historical sources and the consequences of his alliance with Tang 중국.

### Historiographical Problems and Source Reliability

The primary source for 김유신's life, the *Samguk Sagi* (History of the Three Kingdoms), contains what modern historians recognize as serious limitations. The section known as "The Chronicles of 김유신" presents a narrative notably devoid of defeats or setbacks, which historians view as a clear indication of bias and hagiographic embellishment rather than objective historical documentation.[^57][^58]

Danjae Shin Chae-ho, a prominent Korean historian during the 일본ese colonial period, was particularly critical of the 김유신 historical narrative. He characterized 김유신's entire biographical record as "full of hiding defeats and exaggerating small victories," arguing that the accounts were written from a non-objective viewpoint designed to glorify rather than accurately document.[^59] This criticism highlights a fundamental problem: Kim's descendants, who held significant power and influence, deliberately crafted historical records to showcase their ancestor as a flawless national hero, describing only his victories while omitting his struggles and failures.[^60]

A specific example of this selective editing concerns the Battle of Hwangsanbeol. The fact that General Gyebaek caused 김유신's forces significant difficulties—initially repelling five consecutive 신라 attacks—appears in the 신라 Bongi (main chronicles) but is conspicuously absent from 김유신's personal biography.[^61] This omission reveals how the biographical accounts systematically removed any information that might diminish Kim's reputation.

Furthermore, the *Samguk Sagi* was compiled during the mid-고려 period (12th century), approximately 500 years after 김유신's death, and was reorganized from a Confucian perspective to suit the historical circumstances of that later era.[^62] This temporal distance and ideological reframing introduced additional layers of distortion. The compiler, Kim Pusik, himself acknowledged concerns about the legendary embellishments, stating that he included certain miraculous stories only as sidenotes because "much of these legends are considered as an attempt of aggrandization on the basis of claiming favoritism from the heavens or being a descendent of a god."[^63]

There are also problematic chronological inconsistencies. For instance, records claim 김유신 invaded 백제 and captured over 20 castles during Queen Jindeok's reign, but these events are not recorded in the main chronicles (Bongi). Historians therefore argue that claims appearing only in personal biographies (Yeoljeon) but absent from main chronicles must be subjected to "thorough and critical inspection of related historical materials" before being accepted as factual.[^64]

Despite these legitimate concerns about source reliability, it's important to note that 김유신's reputation was widely recognized beyond 신라 during his lifetime, including in 중국 and 일본—even among his enemies. This contemporary recognition cannot be entirely dismissed as fabrication by later descendants.[^65]

### The Tang 중국 Alliance: Strategic Success or Dangerous Compromise?

김유신's decision to ally with Tang 중국, while strategically necessary for defeating 백제 and 고구려, introduced a dangerous new threat to Korean independence. After the conquest of 백제 in 660 CE, Tang 중국 attempted to exert direct control over the 한반도 by establishing administrative structures including the Ungjin Commandery in former 백제 territory, the Protectorate-General to Pacify the East in former 고구려 lands, and even the Gyerim Territory Area Command within 신라 itself.[^66]

This imperial overreach transformed former allies into adversaries. 신라 was forced to conduct "difficult conflicts" to expel Tang forces from the peninsula, essentially fighting a new war against their former ally to complete true unification.[^67] 김유신's second son, Kim Won-sul, would play a central role in this struggle to complete 신라's independence from Tang 중국, suggesting that managing the consequences of his father's alliance became a primary concern for the next generation.[^68]

Critics argue that 김유신's strategy of inviting a powerful foreign empire onto the 한반도—even if necessary to defeat 고구려—created dangers that nearly negated the very unification he sought to achieve. The fact that 신라 didn't fully secure the peninsula until 676 CE (three years after 김유신's death in 673 CE) through military action against Tang forces complicates the narrative of Kim as the sole architect of unification.[^69]

### Ethical Questions About Warfare

While 김유신 lived by the Hwarang principle against "indiscriminate killing," the unification wars inevitably involved massive casualties and suffering across all three kingdoms. The destruction of centuries-old kingdoms like 백제 and 고구려, with their distinct cultures and traditions, represented not just military conquest but cultural devastation. Some modern historians question whether the violent unification, regardless of its long-term benefits, can be celebrated without acknowledging its human costs.

### The Problem of Legendary Embellishments

Many stories about 김유신—including miraculous births, divine interventions, and superhuman feats—are recognized even by traditional historians as likely fabrications. Kim Pusik's own commentary suggests these were "attempts of aggrandization on the basis of claiming favoritism from the heavens," common practices in pre-modern historical writing but nonetheless distortions of historical truth.[^70] The challenge for modern historians is separating the actual military commander from the legendary figure created by later hagiographers.

## 개인 생활

김유신's personal life was deeply intertwined with 신라's royal family through multiple marriages and familial connections that served both political and personal purposes. He had at least three wives during his lifetime, reflecting both personal loss and strategic alliance-building.[^71]

His first wife's identity remains largely unknown in historical records, though we know she died before his subsequent marriages. After her death, 김유신 married one of Kim Cheon-chu's sisters (his close friend who would become 무열왕), bringing the two men even closer together and solidifying their blood brotherhood into actual family ties.[^72] This marriage represented not merely personal affection but a calculated political alliance that strengthened both men's positions within 신라's complex aristocratic hierarchy.

His third and most politically significant marriage was to Lady Jiso, the third daughter of 무열왕.[^73] This union created the remarkable situation where 김유신 was simultaneously 무열왕's brother-in-law (through his sister Kim Mun-hee's marriage to the king) and son-in-law (through his own marriage to the king's daughter). While such close intermarriage might seem unusual by modern standards, it was a deliberate strategy within the bone rank system to consolidate power among true bone aristocrats and bind military leadership directly to royal authority.

김유신 fathered ten children, creating a large family that would establish one of Korea's most influential lineages.[^74] His second son, Kim Won-sul, would prove particularly important, later playing a central role in completing 신라's independence from Tang 중국 after the unification wars.[^75] This suggests that 김유신 not only trained his sons in military affairs but also passed on his strategic thinking about Korea's relationship with 중국—a concern that would persist beyond his death.

### Personality and Character

Historical records describe 김유신 as "a model of good deeds and spirituality" during his time as a Hwarang warrior.[^76] His adherence to the Five Commandments of the Hwarang—loyal service to the king, filial piety, faithfulness to friends, courage in battle, and restraint in killing—apparently continued throughout his life, shaping both his personal conduct and military leadership.[^77]

One revealing incident demonstrates his courage and decisiveness. When facing a bad omen before a crucial battle—a swooping bird that his men interpreted as a sign of doom—김유신 unsheathed his sword, struck the bird dead mid-flight, and laid it at his general's feet, declaring: "A small grotesque bird cannot interfere with our great expedition against a bad king."[^78] This act showed not only his martial skill but also his willingness to confront superstition with direct action, boosting his troops' morale through decisive leadership.

Another recorded statement reveals his sense of duty and strategic thinking. Before a difficult battle, he said to his father: "They've defeated us. But throughout my life I was guided by loyalty and filial piety. In the face of battle one must be courageous. Now, I have heard that if you shake a coat by its collar, the fur will hang straight. And that, if you lift up the headrope, the whole fishing net will open and it can be thrown far and wide. Let me become the collar and the border rope."[^79] This metaphor suggests he saw himself as the critical element that, through personal example and leadership, could organize and direct larger forces toward victory.

### Philosophical Convictions

Throughout his life, 김유신 felt deeply that 백제, 고구려, and 신라 should not exist as separate kingdoms but should instead be united as one nation.[^80] This conviction—that Korean peoples shared a fundamental unity despite political division—drove his military campaigns and gave them an ideological purpose beyond mere conquest. He envisioned not 신라's domination over subjugated territories but rather the creation of a unified Korean nation, a vision that would profoundly shape Korean identity.

His personal life, characterized by strategic marriages, numerous children, adherence to Hwarang principles, and an unwavering conviction about Korean unification, reveals a complex figure who skillfully navigated between personal relationships, political necessities, and philosophical ideals throughout his 78-year life.

## 유산

김유신's ultimate legacy is the first unification of the Korean nation—a political achievement that fundamentally shaped Korean identity and culture for subsequent centuries.[^81] Without his military leadership and strategic vision, Korea would likely have remained divided among separate kingdoms, potentially developing distinct national identities rather than the unified Korean culture that emerged during the Unified 신라 period (668-935 CE).[^82]

### Cultural and National Significance

Within Korean culture, 김유신 is revered as a symbol of national unity, embodying the ideals of courage, loyalty, and patriotism that define Korean martial tradition.[^83] He is the focus of numerous stories and legends, familiar to most Koreans from childhood, occupying a place in Korean historical consciousness comparable to figures like El Cid in 스페인 or King Arthur in Britain.[^84] This cultural significance extends beyond historical education into popular identity—he represents the possibility of overcoming division and achieving unity through dedication and strategic thinking.

Korean historians characterize him as "the driving force in the unification of the 한반도" and "the most famous of all the generals in the unification wars of the Three Kingdoms."[^85][^86] This assessment acknowledges that while kings like Muyeol and Munmu provided political leadership and vision, 김유신 supplied the military genius and tactical execution that transformed political ambitions into historical reality.

### The Gimhae Kim Clan

김유신's biological legacy is equally remarkable. According to the *Samguk Sagi*, he was the 12th descendant of King Suro, the legendary founder of the Gimhae Kim clan, and he became the "middle progenitor" (mid-ancestor) of that clan—meaning he is the common ancestor of all subsequent Gimhae Kim family lines.[^87][^88] The Gimhae Kim clan has 142 branches, three of which are direct descendants of 김유신.[^89]

The scale of this legacy is staggering. According to the 2015 대한민국n census, there were 4,456,700 Gimhae Kim clan members in 대한민국, making it by far the most populous of all Korean clans.[^90] During the 고려 왕조 (918–1392), the Gimhae Kim produced numerous civil and military officials, including 15 individuals reaching 총리ial ranks and 11 serving as royal lecturers.[^91] This sustained influence across centuries demonstrates how 김유신's legacy extended far beyond his military achievements into sustained political and social prominence for his descendants.

### Physical Memorials and Tourist Sites

김유신's tomb, located in Gyeongju (the former 신라 capital), has become an important historical site and tourist destination. The burial mound, which measures roughly 30 meters in diameter, is surrounded by 12 stone slabs carved in relief with animals from the Oriental zodiac, providing eternal symbolic protection for the general within.[^92][^93] Although 김유신 was not a king, his tomb was built in a style similar to royal tombs—a recognition of his extraordinary contributions. On January 21, 1963, it was designated Historic Site of 대한민국 No. 21.[^94]

The twelve zodiac statues represent some of the finest stone carving from the Unified 신라 period. These figures have armed human bodies with animal heads, standing on rock-shaped pedestals while holding swords, and they face rightward in military costumes.[^95] Some of these statues are currently housed in the National Museum of Korea, where they serve as important examples of 신라 artistic achievement.[^96]

Beyond the tomb, there is an equestrian statue of 김유신 in Gyeongju, and another statue stands at Namsan Mountain in 서울 at the base of the staircase leading to the summit.[^97][^98] These monuments ensure his presence in Korea's physical landscape, constantly reminding visitors of his historical significance.

### Popular Culture and Media

김유신's story has inspired countless works of art and literature across Korean history, from epic poems and historical novels to modern dramas and films.[^99] His youth was depicted in the MBC drama "Queen Seondeok," which aired in 2009 and portrayed the turbulent life of 신라's first queen. The drama's success significantly increased tourism to 김유신's tomb and other 신라 historical sites in Gyeongju.[^100]

### Posthumous Honors

Following his death in 673 CE, King Munmu held a funeral with exceptional courtesy and erected a monument honoring 김유신's merits. His remains were buried in Geumsanwon, and a grave attendant was appointed to guard the tomb—honors typically reserved for royalty.[^101]

Most remarkably, King Heungdeok (r. 826–836 CE) awarded 김유신 the posthumous title of "Great King Heungmu" (Heungmu Taewang, "Great King Promoting the Warlike") approximately 162 years after his death.[^102][^103] This was an extraordinary honor—김유신 was the only person in thousands of years of Korean history from a family outside the royal bloodline (Gukseong) to be posthumously honored as a king.[^104] This unprecedented recognition acknowledged that his contributions to Korea's unification merited treatment equal to royalty, transcending the rigid bone rank system that had governed his life.

김유신 remains a towering figure in Korean history, a potent symbol of national identity and a source of inspiration for Koreans worldwide.[^105] His legacy encompasses military genius, political acumen, philosophical vision of unity, and cultural significance that continues to resonate fourteen centuries after his death.

## 역사적 평가

### 긍정적 견해

Modern and traditional scholars who praise 김유신 emphasize his role as the indispensable architect of Korean unification and a model of martial virtue. Korean historians regard him as "the driving force in the unification of the 한반도" and celebrate him as the military genius who transformed the political vision of unification into historical reality.[^106] His tactical innovations—including the use of flanking maneuvers, psychological warfare, and strategic deception at Hwangsanbeol—are studied as examples of brilliant battlefield leadership against numerically superior or strategically positioned opponents.[^107]

Defenders of 김유신's legacy argue that his alliance with Tang 중국, despite its complications, represented sophisticated strategic thinking rather than naive collaboration. They note that without Tang's overwhelming naval and military resources, 신라 could never have defeated both 백제 and 고구려—kingdoms that had successfully resisted conquest for centuries.[^108] The fact that 신라 ultimately expelled Tang forces and secured true independence (completed by 676 CE) demonstrates that the alliance was a calculated risk rather than submission to foreign domination.

Cultural historians emphasize 김유신's embodiment of Hwarang ideals—the synthesis of martial prowess with ethical conduct, philosophical learning, and cultural sophistication. As a model Hwarang who "lived by the Five Commandments until his death," he represented the warrior-scholar ideal that influenced Korean military culture for centuries.[^109][^110] His reported acts of courage, such as striking down the ominous bird before battle and declaring that "a small grotesque bird cannot interfere with our great expedition," exemplify the decisive leadership that inspired extraordinary loyalty among his troops.[^111]

Supporters also highlight that 김유신's reputation was widely recognized even by his enemies during his lifetime, including in 중국 and 일본. This contemporary international recognition cannot be entirely attributed to later hagiographic embellishment by his descendants, suggesting that his military achievements were genuinely extraordinary by the standards of 7th-century 동아시아.[^112]

Furthermore, 김유신's philosophical conviction that the Korean kingdoms should unite rather than remain perpetually divided is celebrated as visionary. His belief in Korean unity—that "백제, 고구려 and 신라 should not be separate countries, but should instead be united"—provided ideological purpose to his campaigns beyond mere 신라 expansionism and established a foundation for Korean national identity.[^113] Without this unification, Korea would not have developed its distinct culture and national consciousness.

The extraordinary posthumous honor of being titled "Great King Heungmu" 162 years after his death—unprecedented for someone outside the royal bloodline—is cited as evidence that even Koreans centuries removed from his era recognized his contributions as equal to those of kings.[^114][^115] His descendants' sustained influence through the Gimhae Kim clan, which produced 15 총리s during the 고려 왕조 and numbers over 4.4 million members today, demonstrates the lasting impact of his legacy.[^116][^117]

### 부정적 견해

Critical scholars challenge the heroic narrative surrounding 김유신 on multiple grounds, beginning with fundamental questions about source reliability. Historians like Danjae Shin Chae-ho have characterized the historical accounts of 김유신's life as "full of hiding defeats and exaggerating small victories," arguing that the records were deliberately crafted to create a flawless hero rather than document historical reality.[^118] The fact that "defeats cannot be found at all in The Chronicles of 김유신" is viewed not as evidence of military perfection but as "a clear limitation of the historical sources."[^119]

Critics point out that 김유신's biography was compiled approximately 500 years after his death during the mid-고려 period and "reorganized from a Confucian perspective to suit the historical situation" of that later era.[^120] His powerful descendants "deliberately chose to show off the abilities and roles of their ancestor 김유신, who was well-known as a national hero, and only described the fact that he had won many wars," systematically omitting information about struggles and setbacks.[^121] The absence of Gyebaek's initial successes at Hwangsanbeol from 김유신's biography—though present in other chronicles—exemplifies this selective editing.[^122]

The compiler of the *Samguk Sagi*, Kim Pusik, himself acknowledged concerns about legendary embellishments, noting that miraculous stories were "an attempt of aggrandization on the basis of claiming favoritism from the heavens or being a descendent of a god."[^123] Critics argue that separating the actual historical 김유신 from the legendary figure created by hagiographers is extremely difficult, if not impossible, given the systematically distorted sources.

Regarding the Tang alliance, critics argue that inviting a powerful foreign empire onto the 한반도 was a dangerous strategy that nearly resulted in Chinese colonization rather than Korean unification. Tang 중국's establishment of administrative structures throughout the peninsula—including within 신라 itself—represented an existential threat to Korean independence.[^124] The fact that 신라 had to fight "difficult conflicts" to expel former allies and didn't fully secure the peninsula until 676 CE (three years after 김유신's death) suggests that his strategy created nearly as many problems as it solved.[^125]

Some historians question whether 김유신's role has been exaggerated at the expense of other contributors to unification. Kings Muyeol and Munmu provided crucial political leadership and diplomatic vision; other generals and admirals contributed to military victories; and Kim Won-sul (김유신's son) played the essential role of expelling Tang forces to complete true independence.[^126] The tendency to attribute all aspects of unification to a single military hero may oversimplify a complex historical process involving many actors and factors.

There are also ethical criticisms of celebrating military conquest without acknowledging its human costs. The destruction of 백제 and 고구려 involved massive casualties, the devastation of centuries-old cultures and traditions, and enormous suffering across the peninsula. While unification may have produced long-term benefits, some modern historians argue that the violent methods and their immediate consequences deserve acknowledgment alongside celebration of the outcome.

Finally, problems with specific claims in 김유신's biography—such as conquests during Queen Jindeok's reign that appear in his biography but not in main chronicles—suggest that historians must approach all claims "with thorough and critical inspection of related historical materials" rather than accepting them at face value.[^127] This methodological concern applies to many aspects of the traditional narrative about 김유신's life and achievements.

## 유명한 어록

> "They've defeated us. But throughout my life I was guided by loyalty and filial piety. In the face of battle one must be courageous. Now, I have heard that if you shake a coat by its collar, the fur will hang straight. And that, if you lift up the headrope, the whole fishing net will open and it can be thrown far and wide. Let me become the collar and the border rope." — 김유신, speaking to his father before a crucial battle, emphasizing his role as the critical organizing force[^128]

> "A small grotesque bird cannot interfere with our great expedition against a bad king." — 김유신, after striking down an ominous bird that his troops interpreted as a bad omen, demonstrating decisive leadership[^129]

> "백제, 고구려 and 신라 should not be separate countries, but should instead be united." — 김유신's philosophical conviction about Korean unification, reflecting his vision beyond mere military conquest[^130]
