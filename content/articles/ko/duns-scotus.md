## 주요 업적

### Philosophical Innovations

John Duns Scotus's most enduring contributions lie in his 혁명가 philosophical concepts that challenged prevailing 중세의 thought and influenced 철학 for centuries.

**Univocity of Being**: Scotus's most 유명한 philosophical doctrine was the univocity of being—the claim that existence is the most abstract concept we have, applicable with exactly the same meaning to everything that exists, including both God and creatures.[^22] This directly opposed the dominant view, championed by Thomas Aquinas, that terms apply to God and creatures only analogically, with different meanings. Aquinas and his followers insisted that univocal predication would collapse the distinction between Creator and creation, bringing metaphysics and natural theology to ruin.[^23]

Scotus turned this argument on its head, contending that unless the concept of being is univocal, both 철학 and natural theology would actually come to ruin, ~ 이래로 we could have no genuine knowledge of God at all.[^24] If our concepts of existence, goodness, and wisdom mean something entirely different when applied to God than when applied to creatures, we would have no basis for reasoning about divine attributes. His defense of univocity represented a fundamental shift in 중세의 metaphysics that later influenced 현대의 philosophers including Martin Heidegger and Gilles Deleuze.[^25][^26]

**Haecceity and Individuation**: Scotus developed the concept of "haecceity" (from the Latin *haec*, meaning "this")—the principle of individuation that makes each individual thing uniquely itself.[^27] ~하는 동안 Scotus himself seldom used this exact term, he maintained that individuation requires a substantial difference added to the common nature, a "thisness" that distinguishes one individual from all others sharing the same nature.[^28] 예를 들어 humanity exists as a common nature in both Socrates and Plato, but Socrates's haecceity makes him Socrates rather than Plato, and vice versa.[^29] This novel account of universals and individuation inspired both brilliant followers and fierce counterarguments from William of Ockham and Thomist opponents.[^30]

**The Formal Distinction**: Scotus introduced the "formal distinction," a way of distinguishing between different formalities or aspects of the same thing that are not merely conceptual but exist in reality, though they are not fully separate entities.[^31] This subtle tool allowed him to navigate complex theological and philosophical problems, particularly regarding the divine attributes and the relationship between essence and existence.

### Theological Contributions

**Defense of the Immaculate Conception**: Perhaps Scotus's most 영향력 있는 theological contribution was his defense of the Immaculate Conception—the doctrine that the Virgin Mary was conceived without original sin.[^32] At the time, this was a highly contested question, with major theologians including Augustine, Bernard of Clairvaux, Anselm, Thomas Aquinas, and Bonaventure all hesitating or denying the doctrine.[^33]

The theological problem seemed intractable: if Mary was preserved from original sin from the first moment of her conception, how could she need Christ's redemption? Wouldn't this doctrine 분쟁 with the universality of Christ's salvific work?[^34] When the question arose in a solemn quodlibetal disputation at Paris, the secular master Jean de Pouilly declared the Scotist thesis not only improbable but potentially heretical, arguing that one should proceed against anyone asserting it "not with arguments but otherwise"—an implicit threat of ecclesiastical punishment.[^35]

Scotus offered two theological breakthroughs that resolved the dilemma. First, he 뛰어난 between the order of nature and the order of time, and second, he introduced the concept of "preventive redemption" or "preservative redemption."[^36] He argued that Mary's immaculate conception did not exempt her from redemption by Christ; rather, it was the result of a more perfect redemption granted her ~ 때문에 of her unique role in salvation history. Mary was totally redeemed by Christ, but in a superior way—preserved from original sin rather than cleansed from it ~ 후에 contracting it.[^37]

This argument appears explicitly in 교황 Pius IX's 1854 declaration of the dogma of the Immaculate Conception, which stated that "at the first moment of Her conception, Mary was preserved free from the stain of original sin, in view of the merits of Jesus Christ."[^38] Scotus's position was later hailed as "a correct expression of the faith of the Apostles."[^39] When 교황 John Paul II beatified Scotus on March 20, 1993, he described him as the "minstrel of the Incarnate Word and defender of Mary's Immaculate Conception."[^40]

## 논란과 비판

~에도 불구하고 his brilliance and influence, Scotus faced 중요한 controversies ~ 동안 his lifetime and posthumous criticisms that damaged his reputation for centuries.

### Contemporary Theological Disputes

The Immaculate Conception controversy represented Scotus's most heated theological battle. The secular master Jean de Pouilly's declaration that the doctrine was potentially heretical and that advocates should be dealt with "not with arguments but otherwise" reveals the intensity of opposition Scotus faced.[^46] The fact that giants like Aquinas and Bonaventure had rejected the doctrine meant Scotus was challenging established theological authority.

### The Origin of "Dunce"

Perhaps the cruelest legacy of later controversies is linguistic. In the sixteenth 세기, philosophers accused Scotus's followers—called "Dunses" or "Scotists"—of sophistry and hairsplitting. The term "dunce" developed from "Dunse," becoming a word for "somebody who is incapable of scholarship."[^49] This transformation from honored epithet to insult represents one of the more dramatic reversals in intellectual history, though 현대의 scholarship has thoroughly vindicated Scotus's philosophical rigor and creativity.

### Modern Philosophical Criticisms

In recent decades, Scotus has faced sophisticated philosophical and theological criticisms from various quarters. ~ 동안 1990s, scholars argued that Scotus was the first thinker to succumb to what Martin Heidegger termed "onto-theology"—the problematic reduction of God to a being among beings within a totalizing metaphysical system.[^50] This criticism has been disseminated ~을 통해 the writings of the "Radical Orthodoxy" movement of theologians who see Scotus as inaugurating a series of philosophical errors that culminated in modernity's problematic relationship with transcendence.[^51]

The list of accusations levied against Scotus by various critics includes "formalism, nominalism, skepticism, fatalism, pantheism, voluntarism, individualism, modernism, Spinozism, Kantianism and radical Islamism"—a remarkable and contradictory catalogue that reveals more about the diverse concerns of his critics than about Scotus himself.[^52]

### Voluntarism and Divine Arbitrariness

Scotus's voluntarism—his emphasis on the primacy of the will over the intellect in both God and humans—attracted sustained criticism.[^53] Critics worried that his position made morality arbitrary, subject merely to God's will rather than grounded in rational necessity or the divine nature. Scotus contended that God can do whatever is not logically impossible, and whatever God wills is by that very fact right, with God's 정의 not constraining his will to any single consistent system of laws.[^54]

His distinction between natural law in the "strict sense" (containing only necessary negative propositions like "God is not to be hated") and natural law in a "looser sense" (containing contingent positive precepts that are merely "highly consonant" with strict natural law but depend on God's will) troubled those who sought a more stable foundation for moral norms.[^55] Critics charged that this made the moral law dangerously dependent on divine voluntarism.

### The Univocity Controversy

Scotus's doctrine of univocity generated intense philosophical controversy. The prevailing 중세의 view, championed by Aquinas, held that 철학 and theology would come to ruin if being were univocal; Scotus's startling counter-claim was that they would come to ruin if being were not univocal.[^56] This fundamental disagreement persisted for centuries.
