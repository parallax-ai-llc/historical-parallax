## 개요

무령왕 (461-523 CE) was the 25th monarch of 백제, one of the Three Kingdoms of Korea, reigning from 501 to 523 CE.[^1] Born on the 일본ese island of Kakara during a diplomatic mission, he ascended to power following his father King Dongseong's assassination and immediately consolidated his authority by crushing aristocratic rebellions.[^2] His 22-year reign marked a golden age of 백제 culture, characterized by military victories against 고구려, diplomatic expansion with 중국's Liang Dynasty and 일본, territorial conquests including Tamna (Jeju Island), and unprecedented economic prosperity.[^3] The 1971 discovery of his intact tomb, containing over 4,600 artifacts including magnificent gold crown ornaments, revolutionized understanding of 백제 civilization and remains one of the most significant archaeological finds in Korean history.[^4]

---

## 초기 생애와 배경

According to the Nihon Shoki (Chronicles of 일본), 무령왕 was born under extraordinary circumstances in 461 CE when his father's brother, Buyeo Gonji, was traveling to 일본 as an envoy of King Gaero.[^5] Gonji's pregnant wife went into labor during the sea voyage, forcing them to anchor at Kakara Island (also called Kakashima) in what is now northern Saga Prefecture, 일본.[^6] The child was born on this small island, earning him the name "Semakishi" or "King Shima" in 일본ese records—both names deriving from the word for "island."[^7] The 백제 people called the birthplace "Nirimu Sema," meaning "main island."[^8]

무령왕's unusual birth story became part of the mythological narrative surrounding his legitimacy and connection to both Korea and 일본.[^9] He was the second son of King Dongseong, the 24th ruler of 백제.[^10] Little is recorded about his childhood or early education, though as a member of the Buyeo royal family, he would have received training in Confucian classics, military strategy, and statecraft.[^11]

The circumstances of his birth on 일본ese soil would later prove significant in establishing diplomatic relations between 백제 and the Yamato court.[^12] His early years coincided with a period of instability in 백제, as the kingdom struggled to recover from the devastating loss of its capital Hansong to 고구려 in 475 CE, an event that had forced the kingdom to relocate to Ungjin (modern-day Gongju).[^13] This traumatic relocation, which occurred when Muryeong was approximately 14 years old, shaped the political landscape he would inherit.[^14]

Growing up in the shadow of 백제's humiliation by 고구려 and the subsequent move to a less strategic capital, the young prince would have been acutely aware of the kingdom's vulnerability and the need for strong leadership.[^15] The factional politics among aristocratic clans—particularly the traditionally powerful Hae and Jin clans and the rising influence of local Ungjin nobility—created a volatile political environment that would explode into violence at the end of his father's reign.[^16]

## 권력의 부상

무령왕's ascension to the throne in 501 CE was precipitated by violence and political intrigue.[^17] His father, King Dongseong, was assassinated while hunting by forces loyal to Baekga, a powerful court official whom Dongseong had exiled to the remote Karim Fortress in an attempt to curtail the growing power of local aristocratic clans.[^18] The assassination reflected the deep tensions between the monarchy and powerful noble families who had gained influence following the kingdom's relocation to Ungjin.[^19]

The circumstances surrounding Dongseong's death remain somewhat controversial among historians.[^20] While the Samguk Sagi (History of the Three Kingdoms) attributes the assassination directly to Baekga, 일본's Nihon Shoki presents a different narrative, suggesting that "people of 백제 had got rid of him and had Muryeong to replace him because King Dongseong did not accomplish the way of King."[^21] This alternative account raises questions about whether Baekga acted alone or was part of a broader aristocratic conspiracy, possibly with Muryeong's knowledge or even involvement.[^22]

What is certain is that Muryeong moved swiftly and decisively upon assuming the throne.[^23] In 502 CE, just one year after becoming king, he crushed a planned rebellion by Baekga with overwhelming force.[^24] Historical records note that Baekga offered no resistance when the royal army came to execute him, leading some scholars to speculate that he may have been a scapegoat for broader political upheaval rather than the primary conspirator.[^25] Regardless of Baekga's true role, his elimination served Muryeong's purposes perfectly, allowing the new king to demonstrate strength while eliminating a potential rival.[^26]

Following the suppression of the revolt, Muryeong implemented comprehensive administrative reforms designed to prevent future aristocratic challenges to royal authority.[^27] He reorganized 백제's system of local rule to facilitate more effective central oversight, sending members of the royal family to 22 regional areas across the kingdom to ensure direct royal control over provincial territories.[^28] This restructuring strengthened governance at the provincial level, reducing vulnerabilities to local power brokers and enabling firmer royal control over the kingdom's territories.[^29]

These early decisive actions established Muryeong's reputation as a strong and capable ruler who would tolerate no challenges to his authority.[^30] By combining military force with administrative innovation, he successfully consolidated power in a way that would enable 백제 to enter its most prosperous period.[^31]

## 주요 업적

### Military Victories and Territorial Expansion

무령왕's reign was marked by sustained military success against 백제's primary rival, 고구려.[^32] In 501, immediately upon taking the throne, he dispatched an army to attack 고구려's Sugok-seong fortress, signaling his aggressive stance toward the northern kingdom.[^33] In 503, he successfully repelled an attack by the Mohe, a Tungusic people allied with 고구려 who inhabited ancient Manchuria.[^34] He repeated this defensive success in 507, again defeating combined 고구려 and Mohe forces.[^35]

The most dramatic military engagement occurred in 512 when 고구려 forces conquered two 백제 castles.[^36] Rather than accept this loss, 무령왕 personally led 3,000 men into battle and destroyed the 고구려 army, reclaiming the lost territories.[^37] This personal leadership in combat enhanced his prestige and demonstrated the martial valor expected of 백제 monarchs.[^38] In 523, recognizing the ongoing threat from the north, he ordered the construction of a fortified wall to defend 백제's northern border, a massive infrastructure project that would protect the kingdom for decades.[^39]

Beyond defensive operations, Muryeong successfully expanded 백제's territory by conquering the state of Tamna, based on Jeju Island, and annexing parts of the Gaya confederacy in southern Korea.[^40] The conquest of Tamna was particularly significant as it gave 백제 control over strategic maritime routes and expanded the kingdom's influence into island territories.[^41] These military achievements were facilitated by 백제's alliance with 신라, formed in 433 CE, which allowed both kingdoms to counter 고구려's power and created the relatively stable century during which 백제 enjoyed its greatest prosperity.[^42]

### Diplomatic Excellence

무령왕's diplomatic accomplishments rivaled his military successes.[^43] He established formal diplomatic ties with 중국's newly founded Liang Dynasty, sending his first mission in 512 CE.[^44] This was followed by a second mission in 521 to announce various victories over 고구려.[^45] In response, the Liang emperor bestowed prestigious titles on Muryeong, including "Great General Tranquilizing the East" (寧東大將軍) and "King of 백제"—titles that were later found engraved on tablets in his tomb, providing archaeological confirmation of these diplomatic exchanges.[^46]

These diplomatic relationships with 중국 brought substantial benefits to 백제.[^47] According to both historical and archaeological sources, contact and trade between 중국 and 백제 increased dramatically during Muryeong's reign.[^48] The exchange included not only commercial goods but also cultural and technological knowledge that enhanced 백제's civilization.[^49] The relationship ensured that 백제 was recognized not merely as a regional power but as a key player on the continental stage of Northeast 아시아n politics.[^50]

Muryeong's diplomatic engagement with 일본 was equally significant.[^51] In 503, he sent a bronze mirror to the Yamato court, and in 513 and 516, he dispatched Confucian scholars including Managuen and Sahaguen to 일본.[^52] These missions involved sending specialists such as monks and scholars, Buddhist goods, various books, and medicines, while 일본 regularly provided thousands of soldiers, hundreds of horses, and scores of ships in return.[^53] This mutually beneficial relationship saw 백제 provide economic, cultural, and technological aid in exchange for military and political support.[^54]

The cultural impact of these exchanges was profound and lasting.[^55] Muryeong's son, King Seong, would later send a mission to 일본 in 538 that brought an image of Shakyamuni Buddha and several sutras to the 일본ese court—an event traditionally considered the official introduction of 불교 to 일본.[^56] The connections established during Muryeong's reign laid the foundation for centuries of cultural transmission from Korea to 일본.[^57]

### Administrative and Economic Reforms

Muryeong's governance extended beyond military and diplomatic affairs to encompass significant domestic reforms.[^58] He implemented policies that allowed wanderers to return to their homelands and devote themselves to agriculture, contributing to increased agricultural production and stability in the lives of farming households.[^59] This focus on agricultural development strengthened the economic foundation of the kingdom and improved the welfare of common people.[^60]

His administrative restructuring involved promoting a system that emphasized local governance while maintaining strong central control.[^61] By engaging communities actively in decision-making processes while ensuring royal oversight through appointed family members, he created a balanced system that prevented both aristocratic dominance and excessive centralization.[^62] These reforms laid a solid foundation for the prosperity that characterized his reign and enabled the cultural flowering that would continue under his successors.[^63]

### Religious and Cultural Patronage

무령왕 gave unwavering support to 불교, endorsing Buddhist practices and the construction of temples, allowing the religion to take deep root within 백제 society.[^64] His efforts to construct temples and promote Buddhist teachings left an enduring spiritual legacy that remains evident in contemporary Korean culture.[^65] While it would not be until 528 that his son King Seong officially established 불교 as the state religion, Muryeong's reign created the conditions for this religious transformation.[^66]

The cultural achievements of his era are evidenced by artifacts such as the splendid gilt-bronze incense burner excavated from the ancient Buddhist temple site at Neungsan-ri, which exemplifies the artistic excellence of 백제 craftsmanship.[^67] 백제 visual arts during this period reveal technical maturity along with warm human qualities, sometimes attributed to the influence of southern Chinese art from the Six Dynasties period, particularly evident in softly modeled Buddha statues with their distinctive and expressive "백제 smile."[^68]

## 논란과 비판

### The Archaeological Disaster of 1971

The most significant controversy surrounding 무령왕 concerns not his reign but the 1971 excavation of his tomb, which is widely criticized as one of the worst archaeological disasters in Korean history.[^69] The tomb was accidentally discovered during water drainage work on nearby tombs, and what should have been a careful, methodical excavation lasting several years was instead completed in just 17 hours—essentially one night.[^70]

Dr. Kim Won-ryong of 서울 National University, who directed the excavation, later stated in his memoir: "The excavation of 무령왕's tomb was the biggest shame and mistake of my life."[^71] Testimonies indicate that relics were thrown into sacks indiscriminately, and field records documenting the arrangement of artifacts were completely destroyed.[^72] The rushed excavation report published in 1973 contained numerous errors and omitted crucial archaeological data that could never be recovered.[^73]

The excavation is thus considered both the best and worst in Korean archaeology—best because the intact tomb provided unprecedented insights into 백제 civilization, worst because the careless methodology destroyed irreplaceable archaeological context and data.[^74] The problems that arose during the excavation and the subsequent self-critical reflection within the archaeological community brought about key developments in Korean archaeology and 대한민국n cultural heritage policies, ultimately paving the way for the systematic excavations of 신라 and Gaya royal tombs that followed.[^75]

### Questions of Legitimacy and Succession

Historical controversies exist regarding Muryeong's rise to power.[^76] The discrepancy between Korean and 일본ese historical records about King Dongseong's death has led some scholars to question whether Muryeong was complicit in his father's assassination or whether he benefited from a broader aristocratic conspiracy.[^77] The fact that Baekga offered no resistance to execution has fueled speculation that he may have been a scapegoat rather than the actual mastermind of Dongseong's murder.[^78]

These questions are impossible to resolve definitively given the limited and politically motivated nature of surviving historical sources.[^79] The Samguk Sagi, which provides much of our information about Muryeong, was written from a 신라 perspective reflecting the violent conflicts between 신라 and 백제 in the 6th and 7th centuries, potentially biasing its portrayal of 백제 rulers.[^80]

### Scholarly Debates on Artifacts and Identity

The interpretation of artifacts from Muryeong's tomb has sparked ongoing scholarly debates.[^81] The design of the king's crown ornaments is particularly controversial, with disagreements about whether these items were worn during life or reserved exclusively for burial rites.[^82] Similar controversies involve artifact functionality and tomb symbolism more broadly.[^83]

Linguistic controversies surround Muryeong's name "Sama" (斯麻).[^84] The general scholarly consensus holds that this name originated from "Sema," meaning island in the 백제 language, referring to his birthplace.[^85] However, some have attempted to link it to the 일본ese honorific suffix "sama" (様), a hypothesis that most scholars reject as grammatically and historically weak.[^86]

### Military Limitations

Despite his military successes, Muryeong's reign did not fundamentally alter the strategic balance of power on the Korean peninsula.[^87] While he successfully defended 백제 against 고구려 and even scored significant victories, he was unable to recover the Han River valley that had been lost in 475 CE—a loss that continued to weaken 백제's strategic position.[^88] The kingdom remained vulnerable to 고구려 pressure, necessitating continued alliance with 신라, an arrangement that would ultimately prove fatal when 신라 betrayed 백제 in 550 CE, two decades after Muryeong's death.[^89]

## 개인 생활

무령왕 was buried alongside his queen in the tomb discovered in 1971 in Songsan-ri, Gongju, 대한민국.[^90] According to inscriptions found on stone epitaph plaques in the tomb, the queen died in December 526, three years after Muryeong's death, and was interred on February 12, 529.[^91] Remarkably, despite the wealth of artifacts discovered in their tomb, the queen's name was not recorded on any inscription, a mystery that has puzzled historians.[^92] The plaques identify her only as "King of King 백제," suggesting her elevated status but leaving her personal identity unknown.[^93]

The king and queen's tomb contained elaborate burial goods that speak to their elevated status and the sophistication of 백제 culture.[^94] Both were provided with gold crown ornaments, though the designs differed—the queen's crown featured symmetrically arranged lotus, palmette, and "dangling bottle" motifs, while the king's ornaments employed a dynamic flame motif.[^95] Analysis revealed these were made of 98-99.1% pure gold, created through hammering, cutting, and hanging golden spangles.[^96] Both royal personages were provided with gilded bronze shoes, elaborate jewelry including earrings, necklaces, and bracelets with inscribed characters, along with jade objects, glass beads, and other precious items.[^97]

무령왕 had at least two sons who left significant historical legacies.[^98] His first son became King Seong of 백제 (r. 523-554), the 26th monarch, who before ascending the throne was known as Buyeo Myeong or Buyeo Myeongnong.[^99] King Seong would continue and expand his father's policies, moving the capital to Sabi in 538 and officially establishing 불교 as 백제's state religion in 528.[^100] He would also send the mission to 일본 in 538 that formally introduced 불교 to the Yamato court.[^101]

Muryeong's second son, Prince Junda (known in 백제 as Buyeo Junta), took a different path.[^102] He settled in 일본 and became an ancestor of 일본ese noble lineages.[^103] According to the Shoku Nihongi, Emperor Kanmu's mother, Takano no Niigasa (720-790 CE), was a descendant of Prince Junda.[^104] This connection created a direct blood link between the 백제 royal family and the 일본ese imperial line that continues to the present day.[^105]

Historical records also mention a child named Saa-gun, though details about this individual remain scarce.[^106]

무령왕's personality can be inferred from his actions rather than direct descriptions.[^107] His decisive suppression of Baekga's rebellion, his personal leadership in the 512 campaign against 고구려, and his comprehensive administrative reforms all suggest a ruler who combined martial prowess with strategic vision and political acumen.[^108] His successful balancing of relationships with 중국, 일본, and 신라 demonstrates sophisticated diplomatic skill.[^109] His support for 불교 and cultural development indicates an appreciation for religion and the arts beyond mere military matters.[^110]

The tomb inscriptions record that 무령왕 died on June 6, 523 CE, at the age of 62 (by traditional 동아시아n counting methods).[^111] His burial in an elaborate brick chamber tomb modeled on Chinese architectural styles of the period reflected 백제's cultural connections to the continent.[^112] The tomb measured 4.2 meters north-south, 2.72 meters east-west, and 3.14 meters in height, constructed of black brick with a vaulted ceiling.[^113]

## 유산

### Archaeological and Historical Impact

The 1971 discovery of 무령왕's tomb revolutionized understanding of 백제 civilization and Korean history more broadly.[^114] As the only 백제 royal tomb discovered intact, it provided unprecedented insights into royal burial practices, artistic achievements, and international connections of the kingdom.[^115] The approximately 4,600 artifacts recovered from the tomb, with 17 designated as National Treasures of Korea, constitute the most important collection of 백제 material culture in existence.[^116]

The tomb's discovery unveiled crucial insights into 백제's past and rejuvenated academic research, revealing the kingdom's historical importance and craft excellence.[^117] Advanced science, technology, and ideology documented through tomb artifacts demonstrated that cultural exchange rather than warfare characterized relationships between 중국, the 한반도, and the 일본ese Archipelago, playing an important role in enhancing the overall cultural level of Northeast 아시아.[^118] The internationalism and creative adoption of foreign cultures evidenced in the metalwork and other artifacts revealed 백제's sophisticated engagement with continental and maritime networks.[^119]

In 2015, the Songsan-ri Burial Ground, where the Tomb of 무령왕 is located, was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the 백제 Historic Areas.[^120] The artifacts from the tomb played a key role in this successful designation, through which the historic importance, creativity, and cultural significance of 백제 sites came to be acknowledged internationally.[^121]

### Cultural and Religious Endurance

무령왕's support for 불교 created lasting spiritual legacies that remain evident in contemporary Korean culture.[^122] His efforts to construct temples and promote Buddhist teachings, continued and expanded by his son King Seong, established 불교 as a defining feature of Korean civilization.[^123] The transmission of Buddhist culture from 백제 to 일본, facilitated by relationships Muryeong established, profoundly influenced 일본ese religious and artistic development.[^124]

The sophisticated cultural production of Muryeong's reign, exemplified by the famous "백제 smile" in Buddhist sculpture and elaborate metalwork, set artistic standards that influenced Korean aesthetics for centuries.[^125] His fostering of an environment that encouraged trade, commerce, and cultural exchanges facilitated the spread of advanced civilization throughout Northeast 아시아.[^126]

### International Connections

무령왕's legacy extends directly into modern international relations through his documented connection to the 일본ese imperial family.[^127] On December 23, 2001, Emperor Akihito of 일본 publicly acknowledged this ancestry, stating: "I, on my part, feel a certain kinship with Korea, given that it is recorded in the Chronicles of 일본 that the mother of Emperor Kanmu was one of the descendant of King Muryong of 백제."[^128] This was the first time a 일본ese emperor publicly referred to Korean blood in the imperial line, and the statement was made during a period of tensions between 일본 and 대한민국.[^129]

Since Emperor Akihito is a direct descendant of Emperor Kanmu through the unbroken imperial line, and Kanmu's mother Takano no Niigasa descended from Muryeong's son Prince Junda, this creates a verifiable genealogical link between modern 일본's imperial family and the ancient 백제 kingdom.[^130] This connection serves as a powerful reminder of the deep historical ties between Korea and 일본, predating modern political divisions.[^131]

### Modern Korean Identity

Understanding 무령왕's contributions inspires modern Koreans to cherish their rich heritage while recognizing the importance of their interconnectedness with the broader world.[^132] Modern interpretations of his reign not only reflect evolving societal values but also serve to engage younger audiences with the rich history of the 백제 Dynasty.[^133] His successful balancing of military strength, diplomatic finesse, cultural patronage, and administrative innovation provides a historical model of effective leadership.[^134]

무령왕's reign represents a golden age in 백제's history—a period when the kingdom recovered from earlier disasters, expanded its power and influence, and created a cultural flowering that would influence all of Northeast 아시아.[^135] His legacy thus continues to influence Korean culture through 불교, international cultural exchange, archaeological practices, and national heritage preservation.[^136]

## 역사적 평가

### 긍정적 견해

Modern scholarship generally regards 무령왕 as one of 백제's most successful and important rulers.[^137] Historians praise his decisive handling of the political crisis that brought him to power, noting that his swift elimination of aristocratic threats prevented the kind of political fragmentation that had weakened the kingdom in previous decades.[^138] His administrative reforms, which strengthened central control while engaging local communities, are viewed as innovative solutions to the challenges of governing a kingdom recovering from military disaster and capital relocation.[^139]

Military historians commend Muryeong's strategic acumen, particularly his personal leadership in the 512 campaign that destroyed the 고구려 army.[^140] His construction of border fortifications and successful defense against multiple 고구려 attacks are seen as critical to maintaining 백제's independence during a period when 고구려 was at the height of its power.[^141] The conquest of Tamna and expansion into Gaya territories demonstrated an ability to seize offensive opportunities while maintaining defensive stability.[^142]

Scholars of international relations highlight Muryeong's sophisticated diplomacy as perhaps his greatest achievement.[^143] His establishment of formal ties with 중국's Liang Dynasty and his cultivation of productive relationships with 일본 created a strategic environment favorable to 백제's interests.[^144] The exchange of cultural, technological, and religious knowledge facilitated by these diplomatic connections enriched 백제 civilization and positioned the kingdom as a cultural bridge between 중국 and 일본.[^145]

Cultural historians emphasize Muryeong's support for 불교 and patronage of the arts as creating the foundation for 백제's cultural golden age.[^146] The artistic achievements evident in artifacts from his tomb, particularly the exquisite gold crown ornaments and refined metalwork, demonstrate the high level of craftsmanship achieved during his reign.[^147] His policies fostered an environment of cultural creativity and international exchange that would influence Korean and 일본ese culture for centuries.[^148]

The archaeological community, despite criticizing the 1971 excavation methods, acknowledges that the discovery of Muryeong's intact tomb has been invaluable for understanding 백제 civilization.[^149] The tomb provides concrete evidence of international connections documented in historical texts, confirming the accuracy of records about diplomatic exchanges with 중국 and 일본.[^150] The artifacts have enabled scholars to understand 백제 artistic styles, metalworking techniques, religious practices, and social organization in unprecedented detail.[^151]

### 부정적 견해

Critical historians point to several limitations and failures in Muryeong's reign.[^152] Most significantly, he was unable to recover the strategically vital Han River valley that 백제 had lost to 고구려 in 475 CE.[^153] This failure meant that 백제 remained in a fundamentally weakened strategic position throughout his reign, dependent on alliance with 신라 and unable to achieve the kind of territorial restoration that might have altered the balance of power on the Korean peninsula.[^154]

Some scholars question the long-term sustainability of Muryeong's diplomatic achievements.[^155] While his relationships with 중국 and 일본 brought immediate benefits, they also created dependencies that could become vulnerabilities if those relationships soured.[^156] The reliance on 신라 as an ally against 고구려, while necessary given the strategic situation, ultimately proved disastrous when 신라 betrayed 백제 in 550 CE—though this occurred after Muryeong's death, critics argue he should have anticipated the potential for such betrayal and developed alternative strategies.[^157]

Questions about the legitimacy of Muryeong's accession continue to trouble some historians.[^158] The discrepancies between Korean and 일본ese accounts of King Dongseong's death, and the suspiciously convenient elimination of Baekga who conveniently did not resist execution, suggest possible complicity or manipulation that the historical record obscures.[^159] If Muryeong was involved in or aware of a conspiracy to assassinate his father, this would cast a shadow over his otherwise successful reign.[^160]

Archaeological critics focus not on Muryeong himself but on the catastrophic handling of his tomb's excavation.[^161] While this is not a criticism of the historical figure, it represents a tragic loss of historical knowledge about his reign, burial practices, and the material culture of his era.[^162] The destruction of archaeological context and the careless handling of artifacts means that many questions about 백제 civilization that the tomb might have answered can never be resolved.[^163]

Some revisionist historians argue that Muryeong's reign, while stable and prosperous, was essentially defensive and conservative rather than transformative.[^164] He successfully maintained 백제's position but did not fundamentally alter the strategic situation or achieve the kind of dramatic expansion that characterized the reigns of 고구려's Gwanggaeto or 신라's later rulers.[^165] From this perspective, Muryeong was a competent manager of decline rather than a visionary who could reverse 백제's long-term trajectory toward eventual destruction in 660 CE.[^166]

## 유명한 어록

Unfortunately, no direct quotations from 무령왕 have survived in historical records.[^167] The primary sources for his reign—the Samguk Sagi (History of the Three Kingdoms) and the Nihon Shoki (Chronicles of 일본)—record his actions and policies but do not preserve his spoken or written words.[^168] Most knowledge about Muryeong comes from archaeological discoveries rather than textual records containing his statements.[^169]

However, the inscriptions found in his tomb, while not quotes from the king himself, provide valuable contemporaneous text:

> "Great General Tranquilizing the East, King of 백제" - Title bestowed by Liang Dynasty Emperor, inscribed on tomb tablet[^170]

This title reflects both Chinese recognition of Muryeong's authority and 백제's position within the Northeast 아시아n diplomatic order.[^171]

> "The King passed away in 523, at the age of sixty-two" - Stone epitaph plaque from tomb[^172]

This inscription provides precise dating that has been crucial for establishing the chronology of 백제 history.[^173]

While not direct quotes, later historical assessments capture scholarly views of his reign:

> "The excavation of 무령왕's tomb was the biggest shame and mistake of my life." - Dr. Kim Won-ryong, archaeological excavation director[^174]

This quote, while about the tomb rather than the king, speaks to the immense historical significance of Muryeong's burial site and the tragedy of its mishandled excavation.[^175]

> "I, on my part, feel a certain kinship with Korea, given that it is recorded in the Chronicles of 일본 that the mother of Emperor Kanmu was one of the descendant of King Muryong of 백제." - Emperor Akihito of 일본, December 23, 2001[^176]

This modern statement by Emperor Akihito acknowledges the enduring legacy of 무령왕's bloodline connection to the 일본ese imperial family.[^177]
