## 개요

알리야 이제트베고비치(1925-2003)는 보스니아의 변호사이자 정치 이론가, 정치가로, 1990년부터 1996년까지 보스니아 헤르체고비나 공화국의 초대 대통령을 역임했으며, 이후 보스니아 헤르체고비나 대통령직 위원회의 초대 의장을 지냈습니다. 복잡하고 논란의 여지가 있는 인물로, 그는 유고슬라비아로부터의 독립과 파괴적인 보스니아 전쟁(1992-1995)을 거치며 보스니아를 이끌었고, 보슈냐크족의 저항과 다민족 공존의 상징으로 부상했습니다. 지지자들에게는 "현명한 왕"으로 알려진 이제트베고비치는 공산주의 유고슬라비아 하에서 그의 수감으로 이어진 영향력 있는 이슬람 정치 저술을 저술했지만, 그는 일관되게 다민족 보스니아 국가를 옹호했습니다. 그의 유산은 여전히 깊이 논쟁의 여지가 있으며, 찬양하는 사람들에게는 보스니아의 주권 수호자로, 비판하는 사람들에게는 전쟁 범죄에 책임이 있는 이슬람 근본주의자로 여겨집니다.[^1][^2][^3]

## 어린 시절과 배경

알리야 이제트베고비치는 1925년 8월 8일, 당시 세르비아인, 크로아티아인, 슬로베니아인 왕국(이후 유고슬라비아)이었던 보스니아 남부의 보산스키 샤마츠라는 마을에서 태어났습니다. 그는 이 지역에 뿌리 깊은 명망 있고 독실한 무슬림 가정 출신이었습니다. 그의 친할아버지 역시 알리야라는 이름을 가졌으며, 오스만 제국 복무 중에 위스퀴다르에서 군인으로 복무하며 터키 여성 시디카 하님과 결혼했습니다. 부부는 결국 보산스키 샤마츠에 정착했고, 할아버지는 그 마을의 시장을 역임하며 다섯 자녀를 양육했습니다.[^4][^5]

이제트베고비치의 아버지는 회계사로 일했으며 제1차 세계 대전 중 오스트리아-헝가리 군대에서 복무하며 이탈리아 전선에서 싸웠습니다. 그는 전쟁 중 심각한 부상을 입어 최소 10년 동안 반신불수 상태로 지내 가족의 상황에 큰 영향을 미쳤습니다. 알리야는 처음에는 부유한 가정에서 태어난 그의 가족의 다섯 번째 자녀였습니다. 그러나 이제트베고비치가 겨우 세 살 때 재정적 어려움이 닥쳤습니다. 그의 아버지는 1927년에 파산을 선언했고, 가족은 이사를 가야 했습니다.[^6][^7]

1928년, 알리야가 약 세 살이었을 때, 이제트베고비치 가족은 보스니아의 수도인 사라예보로 이사했습니다. 이 이주는 결정적인 영향을 미쳤는데, 사라예보는 무슬림, 정교회 기독교, 가톨릭, 유대교 공동체가 수세기 동안 공존해 온 세계적인 도시였습니다. 어린 알리야는 사라예보에서 모든 교육을 받았으며, 제2차 세계 대전과 나치의 유고슬라비아 점령이라는 혼란스러운 시기인 1943년 그의 동시대 최고 명문인 사라예보 제1 리얼 고등 남학교를 졸업했습니다.[^8]

전쟁 시기는 이제트베고비치의 세계관과 정치 의식에 깊은 영향을 미쳤습니다. 전쟁 중 십대였던 그는 이후 그의 정치 경력을 정의할 복잡한 민족 및 종교적 긴장을 목격했습니다. 1944년, 18세의 나이에 그는 미래의 아내인 할리다 레포바츠를 만났는데, 그녀의 두 오빠는 추축국 점령에 맞선 저항 운동에 복무하던 중 사망했습니다. 전쟁 저항 운동과의 이러한 개인적인 연결은 투쟁 중에 치러진 희생에 대한 그의 인식을 심화시켰습니다.[^9]

고등학교 졸업 후, 이제트베고비치는 처음에 농업 학교에서 3년간 농업학을 공부했습니다. 그러나 유고슬라비아 군 복무 중 체포되면서 그의 교육 과정은 극적으로 중단되었습니다. 공산주의 유고슬라비아에서 이슬람 정체성을 보존하고자 했던 범이슬람 조직인 '청년 무슬림'(Mladi Muslimani) 운동의 일원이었던 이제트베고비치는 1946년부터 1949년까지 3년간의 징역형을 선고받았습니다. 21세의 어린 나이에 받은 이 첫 번째 수감은 그의 결의를 굳히고 이슬람 정치 사상에 대한 그의 헌신을 깊게 한 결정적인 경험이었습니다.[^10][^11]

1949년 감옥에서 석방된 후, 이제트베고비치는 처음에 농업학을 계속하며 교육을 재개했습니다. 그러나 3학년 때, 그는 법학으로 전공을 바꾸기로 결정하고 사라예보 대학교 법학부에 입학했습니다. 뛰어난 학업 능력을 보여 그는 단 2년 만에 법학 학위를 취득하여 1956년에 졸업했습니다. 그 후 그는 사라예보에서 법률 컨설턴트 겸 변호사로 일하며 성공적인 개업을 하여 재정적 안정과 지역 사회 내에서 전문적인 존경을 받았습니다.[^12]

## 권력 장악

이제트베고비치의 정치적 유명세에 대한 길은 수십 년간의 지적 활동, 박해, 그리고 이슬람 정치 철학에 대한 흔들림 없는 헌신으로 특징지어졌습니다. 1960년대와 1970년대 동안 그는 변호사로 일하면서 자신의 정치 및 종교 사상을 쓰고 발전시키는 데 전념했습니다. 이러한 지적 활발함의 시기는 1970년 사라예보에서 출판된 그의 가장 논란이 많은 저작인 "이슬람 선언"(Islamska deklaracija)으로 정점에 달했습니다.[^13]

이슬람 선언은 이제트베고비치가 서구식 발전과 이슬람 전통을 조화시키는 비전을 제시하고 무슬림 세계 전체에 걸친 "이슬람 갱신"을 촉구하는 포괄적인 에세이였습니다. 이 문서는 이슬람이 정치, 사회, 경제, 문화 생활을 포괄하는 전체 시스템을 구성하며, 서구 세속주의, 공산주의 또는 기타 비이슬람 이데올로기와 근본적으로 양립할 수 없다고 주장했습니다. 이제트베고비치는 이슬람 정부는 무슬림이 인구의 절대 다수를 차지하는 국가에서만 실현될 수 있으며, 그러한 국가는 "종교의 표현이어야 하고 그 도덕적 개념을 지지해야 한다"고 썼습니다.[^14][^15]

비판적으로, 이슬람 선언은 보스니아 헤르체고비나를 명시적으로 언급하지 않았고, 이제트베고비치는 나중에 이슬람 국가를 건설하는 것에 대한 그의 진술이 보스니아의 다민족 상황에 적용될 의도가 없는 가상적이고 이론적인 것이었다고 반복해서 주장했습니다. 그럼에도 불구하고 이 저작은 유고슬라비아의 공산주의 당국으로부터 적대적인 관심을 끌었고, 그들은 이를 반역죄이자 샤리아 법 시행을 촉구하는 것으로 간주했습니다.[^16]

10년 이상 동안 이제트베고비치는 더 이상의 주요 사고 없이 법률 실무와 지적 작업을 계속했습니다. 그러나 1983년, 유고슬라비아 정부는 그와 다른 12명의 보슈냐크 활동가들을 대상으로 대규모 재판을 시작했으며, 그들에게 무슬림 민족주의, 반역죄, 그리고 "무슬림 민족주의에서 파생된 적대적이고 반혁명적인 행위" 혐의를 적용했습니다. 이슬람 선언은 그에 대한 주요 증거로 사용되었습니다. 광범위하게 정치적 쇼 재판으로 여겨졌던 이 재판은 보슈냐크 민족 의식을 억압하기 위해 설계되었으며, 이제트베고비치는 14년의 징역형을 선고받았습니다.[^17][^18]

항소심에서 형량은 나중에 처음에는 12년, 그 다음에는 9년으로 감축되었습니다. 이제트베고비치는 여러 유고슬라비아 감옥에서 복역하며 보스니아 무슬림 공동체의 저항의 상징이 되었습니다. 그의 투옥은 국제적인 관심을 끌었고, 인권 단체들은 유고슬라비아 정부가 그의 종교적, 정치적 신념을 박해한 것에 대해 비판했습니다. 약 5년간 복역한 후, 이제트베고비치는 1988년 11월 25일, 동유럽 전역에서 공산주의 체제가 붕괴되기 시작하면서 정치적 자유화가 진행되는 가운데 감옥에서 석방되었습니다.[^19]

그의 석방은 유고슬라비아 역사에서 결정적인 순간에 이루어졌습니다. 1980년대 말 유고슬라비아에 다당제 정치 시스템이 도입되면서 정치 조직을 위한 새로운 기회가 생겼습니다. 1990년 5월, 이제트베고비치와 다른 보슈냐크 활동가들은 이 기회를 포착하여 보스니아 무슬림 인구를 주로 대표하지만 공식적으로는 다민족 협력을 약속하는 정당인 민주행동당(Stranka Demokratske Akcije, SDA)을 설립했습니다. 이 당의 창당은 세르비아와 크로아티아의 민족주의 고조에 대한 보스니아 무슬림의 정치적 동원을 나타냈습니다.[^20][^21]

보스니아 헤르체고비나 최초의 다당제 선거는 1990년 11월에 실시되었습니다. 결과는 주로 민족적 선을 따라 나타났으며, SDA는 무슬림 표를, 세르비아 민주당(SDS)은 세르비아인들 사이에서, 크로아티아 민주연합(HDZ)은 크로아티아인들 사이에서 승리했습니다. 세 주요 민족 집단을 모두 대표하는 삼국 연립 정부가 구성되었습니다. 1990년 12월 20일, 65세의 나이로 이제트베고비치는 새로 선출된 집단 대통령단으로부터 만장일치로 의장으로 선출되어 보스니아 헤르체고비나의 실질적인 국가 원수가 되었습니다.[^22]

## 주요 업적

이제트베고비치의 가장 중요한 업적은 보스니아 헤르체고비나를 독립으로 이끌고 참혹한 전쟁 동안 그 주권을 수호한 것입니다. 1991년 슬로베니아와 크로아티아의 독립 선언으로 유고슬라비아가 해체되면서 보스니아는 불가능한 선택에 직면했습니다. 세르비아 중심의 유고슬라비아에 남을 것인가, 아니면 독립을 추구하고 전쟁을 감수할 것인가. 이제트베고비치는 처음에는 중립을 유지하려 했고, 1991년 10월 텔레비전 연설에서 "이것은 우리의 전쟁이 아니다"라는 유명한 말을 했습니다. 이는 슬로베니아와 크로아티아의 분쟁을 가리키는 말이었습니다.[^23]

그러나 정치적 상황은 더 이상 지속될 수 없었습니다. 독립을 반대했던 보스니아 세르비아 대표들이 불참한 보스니아 의회는 1992년 2월 29일 유고슬라비아로부터의 공식 독립을 선언했습니다. 이 선언은 1992년 2월 29일과 3월 1일에 실시된 국민 투표에서 지지를 받았으며, 투표자의 99.7%가 독립을 지지했지만, 보스니아 세르비아인 대다수는 이 투표를 보이콧했습니다. 이제트베고비치는 1992년 3월 3일 공식적으로 국가의 독립을 발표했으며, 국제 사회는 1992년 초 보스니아 헤르체고비나를 독립 국가로 인정했습니다.[^24][^25]

인정은 합법성을 가져왔지만 안전은 가져오지 못했습니다. 이제트베고비치는 국제적 인정에 평화 유지군이 동반되어 전쟁을 막을 것이라고 도박했을 것으로 보이나, 이는 실현되지 않았습니다. 1992년 4월 6일, 유고슬라비아 인민군과 준군사 부대의 지원을 받은 세르비아군이 보스니아 전역에서 공격을 개시하면서 전쟁이 발발했습니다. 전쟁 발발 당시 이제트베고비치는 사실상 군대가 없는 국가의 대통령이었습니다. 그는 나중에 "정규전이 두 군대 간의 충돌을 의미한다면, 보스니아의 전쟁은 초기 단계에서 정규전이 아니었다. 그것은 강력한 전쟁 기계가 무장하지 않은 민족을 공격한 것이었다. 목표는 대세르비아를 건설하는 것이었다."라고 회고했습니다.[^26]

## Summary

Alija Izetbegović (1925-2003) was a Bosnian lawyer, political theorist, and statesman who served as the first president of the Republic of Bosnia and Herzegovina from 1990 to 1996, and later as the first chairman of the presidency of Bosnia and Herzegovina. A complex and controversial figure, he led Bosnia through its independence from Yugoslavia and the devastating Bosnian War (1992-1995), emerging as a symbol of Bosniak resistance and multi-ethnic coexistence. Known to supporters as the "Wise King," Izetbegović authored influential Islamic political works that led to his imprisonment under communist Yugoslavia, yet he consistently advocated for a multi-ethnic Bosnian state. His legacy remains deeply contested, viewed by admirers as a defender of Bosnia's sovereignty and by critics as an Islamic fundamentalist responsible for war crimes.[^1][^2][^3]

## Early Life and Background

Alija Izetbegović was born on August 8, 1925, in the town of Bosanski Šamac, in what was then the Kingdom of Serbs, Croats, and Slovenes (later Yugoslavia). He came from a distinguished and devout Muslim family with deep roots in the region. His paternal grandfather, also named Alija, had served as a soldier in Üsküdar while in service of the Ottoman Empire, where he married a Turkish woman named Sıdıka Hanım. The couple eventually settled in Bosanski Šamac, where the grandfather became the town's mayor and raised five children.[^4][^5]

Izetbegović's father worked as an accountant and had served in the Austro-Hungarian Army during World War I, fighting on the Italian Front. He sustained serious injuries during the war that left him in a semi-paralyzed state for at least a decade, profoundly affecting the family's circumstances. Alija was the fifth child in his family, born into what was initially a well-to-do household. However, financial difficulties struck when Izetbegović was just a toddler—his father declared bankruptcy in 1927, forcing the family to relocate.[^6][^7]

In 1928, when Alija was approximately three years old, the Izetbegović family moved to Sarajevo, the capital of Bosnia. This move would prove formative, as Sarajevo was a cosmopolitan city where Muslim, Orthodox Christian, Catholic, and Jewish communities had coexisted for centuries. Young Alija received all of his education in Sarajevo, attending the city's prestigious First Real Gymnasium for Boys, from which he graduated in 1943 during the tumultuous years of World War II and the Nazi occupation of Yugoslavia.[^8]

The wartime period profoundly shaped Izetbegović's worldview and political consciousness. As a teenager during the war, he witnessed the complex ethnic and religious tensions that would later define his political career. In 1944, at the age of 18, he met Halida Repovac, his future wife, whose two brothers had been killed while serving in the resistance movement against the Axis occupation. This personal connection to the wartime resistance movement deepened his awareness of the sacrifices made during the struggle.[^9]

Following his graduation from gymnasium, Izetbegović initially pursued agricultural studies for three years at an agronomy school. However, his educational trajectory was dramatically interrupted when he was arrested during his military service in the Yugoslav Army. As a member of the 'Young Muslims' (Mladi Muslimani) movement—a pan-Islamic organization that sought to preserve Islamic identity in communist Yugoslavia—Izetbegović was sentenced to three years in prison from 1946 to 1949. This first imprisonment, at the young age of 21, would be a formative experience that hardened his resolve and deepened his commitment to Islamic political thought.[^10][^11]

After his release from prison in 1949, Izetbegović resumed his education, initially continuing with agronomy studies. However, in his third year, he made the decisive choice to switch to law, enrolling at the University of Sarajevo's Faculty of Law. Demonstrating exceptional academic ability, he completed his law degree within just two years, graduating in 1956. He subsequently worked as a legal consultant and lawyer in Sarajevo, establishing a successful practice that would provide him with financial stability and professional respect within the community.[^12]

## Rise to Power

Izetbegović's path to political prominence was marked by decades of intellectual activism, persecution, and unwavering commitment to Islamic political philosophy. Throughout the 1960s and 1970s, while working as a lawyer, he dedicated himself to writing and developing his political and religious ideas. This period of intellectual ferment culminated in the publication of his most controversial work, the "Islamic Declaration" (Islamska deklaracija), published in 1970 in Sarajevo.[^13]

The Islamic Declaration was a comprehensive essay that presented Izetbegović's vision for reconciling Western-style progress with Islamic tradition and issued a call for "Islamic renewal" throughout the Muslim world. The document argued that Islam constitutes a total system encompassing political, social, economic, and cultural life, fundamentally incompatible with Western secularism, communism, or other non-Islamic ideologies. Izetbegović wrote that an Islamic government could only be realized in nations where Muslims represented the absolute majority of the population, and that such a state should be "an expression of religion and should support its moral concepts."[^14][^15]

Critically, the Islamic Declaration never explicitly mentioned Bosnia and Herzegovina, and Izetbegović later insisted repeatedly that the statements about creating an Islamic state were hypothetical and theoretical, not intended to be applied to Bosnia's multi-ethnic context. Nevertheless, the work attracted the hostile attention of Yugoslavia's communist authorities, who viewed it as seditious and a call for the imposition of Sharia law.[^16]

For more than a decade, Izetbegović continued his legal practice and intellectual work without further major incident. However, in 1983, the Yugoslav government launched a major trial against him and twelve other Bosniak activists, charging them with Muslim nationalism, sedition, and "hostile and counterrevolutionary acts derived from Muslim nationalism." The Islamic Declaration served as primary evidence against him. In what was widely seen as a political show trial designed to suppress Bosniak national consciousness, Izetbegović was sentenced to fourteen years of imprisonment.[^17][^18]

The sentence was later reduced on appeal, first to twelve years and then to nine years. Izetbegović served his time in various Yugoslav prisons, becoming a symbol of resistance for Bosnia's Muslim community. His imprisonment drew international attention, and human rights organizations criticized the Yugoslav government for persecuting him for his religious and political beliefs. After serving approximately five years, Izetbegović was released from prison on November 25, 1988, amid the broader political liberalization occurring throughout Eastern Europe as communist systems began to collapse.[^19]

His release came at a pivotal moment in Yugoslav history. The introduction of a multi-party political system in Yugoslavia at the end of the 1980s created new opportunities for political organization. In May 1990, Izetbegović and other Bosniak activists seized this opportunity to establish the Party of Democratic Action (Stranka Demokratske Akcije, or SDA), a political party primarily representing Bosnia's Muslim population but officially committed to multi-ethnic cooperation. The party's founding represented the political mobilization of Bosnia's Muslims in response to rising Serbian and Croatian nationalism.[^20][^21]

The first multi-party elections in Bosnia and Herzegovina were held in November 1990. The results largely followed ethnic lines, with the SDA winning the Muslim vote, the Serbian Democratic Party (SDS) winning among Serbs, and the Croatian Democratic Union (HDZ) winning among Croats. A tripartite coalition government was formed representing all three major ethnic groups. On December 20, 1990, at the age of sixty-five, Izetbegović was unanimously selected by the newly elected collective presidency to serve as its president, making him the de facto head of state of Bosnia and Herzegovina.[^22]

## Major Achievements

Izetbegović's most significant achievement was navigating Bosnia and Herzegovina to independence and defending its sovereignty during the catastrophic war that followed. As Yugoslavia disintegrated in 1991 with the declarations of independence by Slovenia and Croatia, Bosnia faced an impossible choice: remain in a Serb-dominated Yugoslavia or seek independence and risk war. Izetbegović initially attempted to maintain neutrality, giving a televised proclamation in October 1991 that included the famous statement "it is not our war," referring to the conflicts in Slovenia and Croatia.[^23]

However, the political situation became untenable. The Bosnian parliament, which had been vacated by Bosnian Serb representatives who opposed independence, formally declared independence from Yugoslavia on February 29, 1992. This declaration was supported by a referendum held on February 29 and March 1, 1992, in which 99.7% of voters supported independence, though the referendum was boycotted by the majority of Bosnian Serbs. Izetbegović officially announced the country's independence on March 3, 1992, and the international community recognized Bosnia and Herzegovina as an independent state in early April 1992.[^24][^25]

Recognition brought legitimacy but not security. Izetbegović appears to have gambled that international recognition would be accompanied by a peacekeeping force to prevent war, but this did not materialize. On April 6, 1992, war erupted as Serbian forces, supported by the Yugoslav People's Army and paramilitary units, launched attacks throughout Bosnia. At the outbreak of war, Izetbegović was president of a country that had virtually no army. As he later reflected, "If a conventional war means a conflict between two armies, then the war in Bosnia was not conventional in its early stages. It was the assault of a powerful war machine on an unarmed people. The goal was to create Greater Serbia."[^26]

Despite the desperate military situation, Izetbegović achieved remarkable success as an international advocate for Bosnia's cause. Throughout the war from 1992 to 1995, he was extraordinarily effective in representing Bosnia in international politics, traveling to the United Nations, meeting with world leaders, and consistently articulating the case for Bosnia as a multi-ethnic state under attack from Serbian and Croatian nationalist forces seeking to partition the country. His advocacy helped maintain international support for Bosnia, though it never translated into the robust military intervention he sought.[^27]

Militarily, Izetbegović oversaw the creation of the Army of the Republic of Bosnia and Herzegovina (ARBiH), which began as a poorly equipped and disorganized force but gradually developed into a more effective fighting force. Initially, Bosnian government forces cooperated with Croatian forces against the Serbs, but this alliance collapsed in 1993 when a separate war erupted between Bosniaks and Croats over territorial control. Izetbegović's political and military leadership during this period, when Bosnia faced enemies on multiple fronts, demonstrated considerable strategic acumen.[^28]

One of Izetbegović's key achievements was his ability to successfully balance and manipulate relationships with Serbian leader Slobodan Milošević and Croatian leader Franjo Tuđman. Both neighboring countries harbored territorial ambitions toward Bosnia, yet Izetbegović managed to prevent the complete partition of Bosnia between Serbia and Croatia—an outcome that many international observers had predicted as inevitable. His diplomatic maneuvering ensured that Bosnia would survive as an independent state, albeit in a radically transformed configuration.[^29]

The culmination of Izetbegović's wartime leadership came with the Dayton Peace Accords, negotiated at Wright-Patterson Air Force Base in Ohio from November 1-21, 1995. U.S. diplomat Richard Holbrooke had pressured Izetbegović, along with Milošević and Tuđman, to attend the intensive three-week negotiation process. For Izetbegović, the negotiations were extraordinarily difficult. The Bosnian Muslims considered themselves victims who had suffered tremendous loss of life, yet Izetbegović recognized that peace was the greater imperative despite the painful compromises it would require.[^30][^31]

The agreement was deeply unsatisfying to Izetbegović because it required sharing power with Bosnian Serb leaders whom he considered war criminals and legitimized the territorial gains made through ethnic cleansing. According to those present at Dayton, Izetbegović feared he would not be able to carry his people with him in accepting peace, that many would prefer to continue fighting. When he signed the agreement, he stated: "My government is taking part in this agreement without any enthusiasm, but as someone taking a bitter yet useful potion or medication."[^32]

Nevertheless, recent historical assessments suggest the Dayton Agreement stands as a significant diplomatic achievement for Izetbegović, who secured a comparatively more favorable outcome for Bosnia than many had thought possible given the military situation. The agreement preserved Bosnia and Herzegovina as a single, internationally recognized state, prevented its partition between Serbia and Croatia, and established a framework, however imperfect, for multi-ethnic governance.[^33]

Following Dayton, Izetbegović served as a member of the three-person collective presidency of Bosnia and Herzegovina established by the agreement. He was elected as the Bosniak member and served as the chairman of the presidency in rotating terms. He continued in this role until stepping down in October 2000 at the age of 74, citing poor health. His peaceful transfer of power represented an important moment for Bosnia's democratic development.[^34]

Beyond politics, Izetbegović was a prolific author whose intellectual contributions extended far beyond the Islamic Declaration. His most significant work was "Islam Between East and West" (Islam između Istoka i Zapada), a philosophical treatise that explored the relationship between Islamic civilization and Western modernity. The book examined Islam's unique position between Eastern spirituality and Western materialism, arguing for Islam's capacity to synthesize the best elements of both traditions. This work gained international recognition and has been translated into numerous languages, establishing Izetbegović as an important Muslim intellectual of the twentieth century.[^35]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Izetbegović remains one of the most controversial figures in Balkan history, and accusations against him range from Islamic fundamentalism to responsibility for war crimes. The most serious allegations concern war crimes committed by forces under his nominal command during the Bosnian War. Bosnian Serb authorities and some international observers claimed that Izetbegović bore responsibility for atrocities committed by Bosniak forces against Serb and Croat civilians, and they provided documentation to the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY) attempting to have him formally indicted.[^36]

During various war crimes trials at The Hague, Bosnian Serbs and Croats made accusations of genocide and crimes against humanity against Izetbegović. However, the ICTY never issued an indictment against him, citing insufficient evidence to support the allegations. Former ICTY Chief Prosecutor Carla del Ponte reportedly stated that Izetbegović's death in 2003 prevented him from potentially standing trial, though this remains disputed and the tribunal never formally pursued charges against him during his lifetime.[^37]

A particular source of controversy was the role of foreign mujahideen fighters in Bosnia. During the war, several thousand foreign Islamic fighters from various countries, many with connections to extremist networks including Al-Qaeda, traveled to Bosnia to fight alongside Bosniak forces. These fighters were incorporated into units such as the Third Corps of the Seventh Muslim Mountain Brigade and the Tenth Muslim Brigade. These units are suspected of committing serious war crimes and crimes against humanity against Croatian and Serbian civilians and prisoners of war.[^38]

The most notorious incident involved the presence of Osama bin Laden in Bosnia. Multiple sources indicate that bin Laden visited Izetbegović in Sarajevo during the war, and Al-Qaeda provided financial and material support to Bosniak forces. Many secular Bosnian Muslim officers had grave reservations about these foreign fighters and their extremist ideology, which was alien to Bosnia's traditionally moderate Islamic culture. Critics argue that Izetbegović's willingness to accept support from radical Islamist networks demonstrated either poor judgment or genuine sympathy for Islamic fundamentalism.[^39]

The Islamic Declaration continues to haunt Izetbegović's legacy. Critics, particularly among Bosnian Serbs and Croats, cite the document as definitive proof that Izetbegović harbored fundamentalist intentions to create an Islamic state in Bosnia. The declaration's statement that "there can be no peace or coexistence between the Islamic faith and non-Islamic social and political institutions" has been repeatedly invoked as evidence of his religious extremism and incompatibility with multi-ethnic coexistence.[^40]

Izetbegović's defenders argue that the Islamic Declaration was a theoretical work about general Islamic political philosophy, not a blueprint for Bosnia, which was never mentioned in the text. They point out that his actual policies as president consistently supported multi-ethnic governance and that he never attempted to implement Sharia law or create an Islamic state. Nevertheless, the document's existence and its uncompromising language have made it impossible for Izetbegović to escape accusations of fundamentalism.[^41]

Governance failures also marked Izetbegović's presidency. The international community and domestic critics accused his party, the SDA, of endemic corruption, nepotism, and attempting to dominate the supposedly multi-ethnic state apparatus. Izetbegović was criticized for failing to remove corrupt individuals from Muslim-dominated sections of the administration and for allowing the SDA to function more as an instrument of Bosniak nationalism than as a genuinely multi-ethnic party. The parallel institutions created by the SDA in areas under Bosniak control often mirrored the nationalist structures established by Serbian and Croatian parties, undermining the ideal of a unified Bosnian state.[^42]

Some critics within the Bosniak community itself have questioned Izetbegović's strategic decisions during the war. They argue that his refusal to accept various peace proposals in the early stages of the conflict—when Bosnia might have secured more favorable terms—unnecessarily prolonged the war and resulted in tens of thousands of additional deaths. The counterargument is that early peace proposals would have legitimized Serbian territorial conquests and resulted in the de facto partition of Bosnia, which Izetbegović was determined to prevent.[^43]

The Dayton Agreement itself, while ending the war, created a dysfunctional state structure that has plagued Bosnia for decades. Critics argue that Izetbegović accepted an agreement that institutionalized ethnic division, created an unwieldy bureaucracy with multiple layers of government, and made Bosnia virtually ungovernable. The country's subsequent political paralysis and economic stagnation are sometimes attributed to the compromises Izetbegović made at Dayton, though defenders note he had little negotiating leverage given the military situation.[^44]

## Personal Life

Alija Izetbegović married Halida Repovac, whom he had met in 1944 when both were young adults during World War II. Halida came from a family deeply affected by the war—her two brothers had been killed fighting in the resistance movement against the Axis occupation. The couple's shared experiences during the tumultuous war years and the subsequent communist period created a strong bond that lasted throughout their lives. Halida remained a constant presence throughout Izetbegović's long political career, including during his imprisonments and the Bosnian War.[^45]

The couple had three children: two daughters and a son named Bakir. The family maintained a relatively private life despite Izetbegović's increasingly prominent public role. Bakir Izetbegović would later follow his father into politics, entering the political arena after his father's death and eventually serving as a member of the tripartite presidency of Bosnia and Herzegovina from 2010 to 2018, representing the Bosniak community just as his father had done. Alija also had two granddaughters, Jasmina and Mirzeta Izetbegović.[^46]

Those who knew Izetbegović personally often described him as a man of deep religious conviction, intellectual curiosity, and unwavering determination. He was known for his ascetic lifestyle and personal modesty, maintaining relatively simple living arrangements even during his presidency. Unlike many leaders in the former Yugoslavia who accumulated wealth and lived lavishly, Izetbegović reportedly remained personally honest and uninterested in material enrichment, though this did not prevent corruption among his associates and party members.[^47]

Izetbegović was described as soft-spoken and thoughtful in personal interactions, quite different from his sometimes fiery public rhetoric. He was an avid reader and writer who continued his intellectual work throughout his political career, producing essays, speeches, and books even during the chaos of wartime. His philosophical and religious writings reveal a complex thinker grappling with profound questions about Islam's place in the modern world, the nature of civilization, and the relationship between faith and politics.[^48]

Health problems increasingly plagued Izetbegović in his later years. He suffered from advanced heart disease that limited his activities and contributed to his decision to step down from the presidency in October 2000. In October 2003, he suffered a fall in his home that resulted in serious injuries. Given his advanced age of 78 and compromised health, he never recovered from these injuries. Alija Izetbegović died on October 19, 2003, in Sarajevo, from complications related to the fall and his underlying heart condition.[^49]

His funeral was an enormous event in Sarajevo, with tens of thousands of mourners filling the streets to pay their respects. He was buried in the Kovači Cemetery, the historic graveyard that overlooks Sarajevo and contains the graves of numerous important Bosnian figures. The massive public outpouring at his funeral demonstrated his enduring significance to Bosnia's Muslim community, who viewed him as the defender of their existence during the country's darkest hour.[^50]

## Legacy

Alija Izetbegović's legacy is profoundly contested and varies dramatically depending on ethnic, national, and political perspectives. Among Bosniaks, he is predominantly remembered as a hero—the leader who secured Bosnia's independence, defended the country during an existential war, and ensured the survival of the Bosniak people when their very existence was threatened. Supporters often refer to him as the "Wise King" (Mudri Kralj), a title that reflects the reverence in which many Bosniaks hold him.[^51]

His defenders argue that Izetbegović's most enduring legacy is that Bosnia managed to pull a fragile peace from the wreckage of the 1990s and that the multi-ethnic society, despite its wounds and dysfunction, continues to exist. They credit him with maintaining the vision of Bosnia as a multi-ethnic, multi-religious state even when such an ideal seemed impossible to sustain amid the nationalist fervor and ethnic violence of the war. His genius, they argue, was his capacity to unite people under a common idea: that Bosnia deserved to survive.[^52]

Within the broader Muslim world, Izetbegović is remembered as one of the most important Islamic thinkers of the late twentieth century. His books, particularly "Islam Between East and West," continue to be widely read and discussed in Muslim intellectual circles. He is seen as a philosopher who grappled seriously with the challenge of reconciling Islamic values with modernity, democracy, and European identity—questions that remain highly relevant for Muslim-majority societies and Muslim minorities in the West.[^53]

International perspectives on Izetbegović's legacy are more mixed. Some Western observers admire him as a defender of multi-ethnic democracy against nationalist aggression and ethnic cleansing. They view his wartime leadership as courageous and his commitment to a unified Bosnia as principled. Academic studies have increasingly recognized his role in preventing the complete partition of Bosnia between Serbia and Croatia, an outcome that seemed likely and that would have rewarded ethnic cleansing.[^54]

However, other international observers are more critical, viewing Izetbegović as a flawed leader whose Islamic political ideology, tolerance of mujahideen fighters, and occasional nationalist rhetoric contributed to Bosnia's ethnic polarization. Some argue that his unwillingness to accept early peace proposals unnecessarily prolonged the war. The dysfunctional state structure created by the Dayton Agreement, which Izetbegović accepted, is sometimes cited as evidence of failed leadership.[^55]

Among Bosnian Serbs, Izetbegović is generally viewed very negatively, often characterized as an Islamic fundamentalist and war criminal responsible for crimes against Serbian civilians. Serbian nationalist narratives portray him as someone who deliberately destroyed multi-ethnic Yugoslavia in pursuit of an Islamic state, using the Islamic Declaration as primary evidence. Monuments, streets, and institutions named after Izetbegović in Bosniak-controlled areas are sources of deep resentment among many Serbs.[^56]

Bosnian Croats hold similarly negative views, though perhaps less intensely than Serbs. Croatian nationalists criticize Izetbegović for attempting to dominate a Bosniak-controlled unitary state rather than accepting the division of Bosnia into ethnic cantons. The 1993-1994 Bosniak-Croat war left lasting bitterness, and many Croats view Izetbegović as equally responsible for that conflict as Croatian leaders.[^57]

The institutional legacy of Izetbegović is visible throughout Bosnia. The Alija Izetbegović International Airport in Sarajevo bears his name, though this naming was controversial among non-Bosniak communities. Museums, foundations, streets, and schools throughout Bosnia's Federation entity (the Bosniak-Croat half of the country) are named in his honor. An Alija Izetbegović Museum was established in Sarajevo to preserve his writings, personal effects, and document his role in Bosnian history.[^58]

Perhaps the most significant aspect of Izetbegović's legacy is the question he poses to contemporary Bosnia and the broader Balkans: Is multi-ethnic coexistence possible in deeply divided societies? Izetbegović consistently promoted this ideal even as the war seemed to make it impossible. Nearly three decades after Dayton, Bosnia remains a fragile state, ethnically divided and politically dysfunctional, yet it has not collapsed into renewed war. Whether this represents the success or failure of Izetbegović's vision remains a matter of intense debate.[^59]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholarly defenders of Izetbegović emphasize his role as a philosopher-statesman who attempted to navigate impossible circumstances with principle and pragmatism. Historians sympathetic to the Bosnian cause argue that he faced an unprecedented challenge: defending a multi-ethnic state against two nationalist neighbors with vastly superior military power, while the international community watched but declined to intervene decisively. In this context, they argue, Izetbegović's achievement was simply that Bosnia survived at all.[^60]

Academic supporters point to his consistent advocacy for multi-ethnic coexistence despite the pressures of war. Unlike some nationalist leaders who exploited conflict for personal power, Izetbegović maintained throughout the war that Serbs and Croats were equal citizens of Bosnia. His governments, even during the darkest periods of the war, included Serbian and Croatian members, however symbolic their participation might have been. This commitment to inclusive rhetoric, even when it seemed politically disadvantageous, demonstrates genuine principle according to his defenders.[^61]

Scholars of Islamic political thought regard Izetbegović as a sophisticated thinker who made important contributions to debates about Islam and modernity. His works, particularly "Islam Between East and West," are credited with offering a nuanced perspective that rejects both blind Westernization and reactionary fundamentalism. Experts argue that the Islamic Declaration, read in full rather than through selective quotations, reveals a complex political philosophy rather than a fundamentalist manifesto, and that using it to condemn Izetbegović is intellectually dishonest.[^62]

Some international relations scholars credit Izetbegović with skillful diplomatic maneuvering that kept Bosnia's cause alive in international forums even when military circumstances were desperate. His speeches at the United Nations, meetings with world leaders, and media appearances effectively communicated the horror of ethnic cleansing and maintained pressure on Western governments to act. While this pressure never resulted in the robust military intervention he sought, it did contribute to the eventual NATO bombing campaign that forced Serb forces to negotiate.[^63]

Admirers within the Muslim world celebrate Izetbegović as a martyr-figure who endured imprisonment for his faith, led his people through an existential crisis, and demonstrated that Islamic values are compatible with democratic governance and European identity. Commemoration events marking anniversaries of his birth and death are held throughout the Muslim world, particularly in Turkey, where he is widely admired. Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan has frequently praised Izetbegović as a model Islamic leader and statesman.[^64]

### Negative Views

Critics, particularly among Serbian academics and politicians, characterize Izetbegović as a religious extremist whose Islamic fundamentalism made multi-ethnic coexistence impossible. They argue that the Islamic Declaration reveals his true intentions and that his wartime rhetoric and actions, including cooperation with mujahideen fighters and appeals to Islamic solidarity, demonstrate that he pursued a sectarian agenda disguised by democratic language. Serbian scholars often cite the declaration's statement about the incompatibility of Islam with non-Islamic institutions as proof of his fundamentalism.[^65]

Some Western academics and journalists have criticized Izetbegović for naiveté and strategic blunders that contributed to the war's outbreak and prolongation. Critics argue that his declaration of independence in spring 1992 without adequate preparation or international security guarantees was reckless and predictably led to catastrophic violence. They suggest that accepting one of the early peace proposals, such as the Vance-Owen Plan, might have prevented tens of thousands of deaths, even if it meant compromising on the ideal of a unified state.[^66]

International critics have highlighted the presence of mujahideen fighters and Izetbegović's acceptance of support from radical Islamic networks as deeply problematic. They argue that these foreign fighters committed atrocities, brought extremist ideology to Bosnia's traditionally moderate Muslim community, and some remained in Bosnia after the war, creating security concerns. The alleged visit by Osama bin Laden to Sarajevo during Izetbegović's presidency, if verified, would represent an association with terrorism that severely compromises his legacy.[^67]

Analysts critical of the Dayton Agreement argue that Izetbegović bears significant responsibility for accepting a peace settlement that institutionalized ethnic division and created an ungovernable state. They contend that his lack of flexibility during negotiations and insistence on unattainable goals resulted in a worse outcome than might have been achieved through more pragmatic diplomacy. The current dysfunction of Bosnia's political system is sometimes attributed to the compromises made at Dayton.[^68]

Some critics within Bosnia's secular and multi-ethnic community argue that Izetbegović's religious politics and the SDA's increasing identification with Bosniak nationalism rather than civic citizenship contributed to the ethnic polarization that persists decades after the war. They argue that true multi-ethnic democracy required secular political parties based on ideology rather than ethnicity, and that Izetbegović's leadership of an explicitly Muslim party undermined this possibility.[^69]

Human rights organizations have criticized the lack of accountability for crimes committed by forces under Izetbegović's command. While he was never indicted by the ICTY, critics argue this reflects political considerations rather than the absence of command responsibility for atrocities. They point to documented cases of abuses against Serb and Croat civilians in areas controlled by Bosniak forces as evidence that all sides, including Izetbegović's government, committed serious violations of international humanitarian law.[^70]

## Famous Quotes

> "보스니아는 발칸반도의 작은 땅덩어리가 아니라, 우리 중 많은 사람에게 보스니아는 하나의 이념입니다."

이 인용문은 이이제트베고비치의 보스니아를 단순한 영토 이상의 것으로 보는 비전을 담고 있으며, 민족주의로 분열된 지역에서 다민족, 다종교 공존이라는 이상을 상징합니다.[^71]

> "보스니아의 주권을 위해 평화를 희생할 용의가 있지만, 보스니아에서의 평화를 위해 주권을 희생하지는 않을 것입니다."

평화를 위해서라도 타협할 수 없는 특정 원칙에 대한 그의 흔들림 없는 헌신과 신념을 반영하는 진술입니다.[^72]

> "내 정부는 이 협정에 열의 없이 참여하지만, 마치 쓰고도 유용한 약이나 치료제를 복용하는 사람처럼 말입니다."

1995년 11월 데이턴 협정에 서명하면서 한 이 인용문은 보스니아를 보존했지만 민족 분할과 침략자로 간주되는 자들과의 권력 공유를 합법화해야 하는 평화 협정에 대한 이제트베고비치의 깊은 양면성을 보여줍니다.[^73]

> "정규전이 두 군대 간의 충돌을 의미한다면, 보스니아의 전쟁은 초기 단계에서 정규전이 아니었습니다. 그것은 강력한 전쟁 기계가 무장하지 않은 민족을 공격한 것이었습니다."

이제트베고비치가 보스니아 전쟁 초기 단계를 묘사하며, 충돌의 비대칭적인 성격과 전쟁 발발 시 보스니아의 취약성을 강조했습니다.[^74]

> "나는 사람들이 보기 좋은 거짓말 대신, 아무리 추악하더라도 진실을 들어야 한다고 믿습니다."

시민들과의 정직한 소통에 대한 그의 철학을 반영하는 진술로, 진실이 고통스럽거나 정치적으로 불리하더라도 마찬가지입니다.[^75]

> "삶의 모든 의미는 악과 싸우는 것입니다."

이제트베고비치의 세계관을 보여주는 철학적 진술로, 수십 년간의 억압에 맞선 투쟁으로 형성되었으며, 그의 이슬람 신앙과 투옥 및 전쟁의 개인적인 경험 모두를 반영합니다.[^76]
