## 개요

레이디스미스 블랙 맘바조 is a 남아프리카 공화국n male choral group founded in 1960 by 조지프 샤발랄라 in Ladysmith, KwaZulu-Natal. Rooted in isicathamiya and mbube traditions — a cappella styles developed by Zulu migrant workers — the group became one of 아프리카's most recognized musical ensembles. Their sound, characterized by soft, precise harmonies and rhythmic footwork, stands apart from the louder mbube style through its deliberate restraint and spiritual warmth.

The group achieved global prominence in 1986 when Paul Simon featured them prominently on his landmark album *Graceland*, introducing their sound to Western audiences and sparking renewed international interest in 남아프리카 공화국n music. Over six decades, 레이디스미스 블랙 맘바조 has released more than 70 albums, won five 그래미상s, and performed for heads of state, at the Olympics, and before the British royal family. They remain active cultural ambassadors for 남아프리카 공화국 and the Zulu people.

## Early Life & Background

조지프 샤발랄라 founded the group in Ladysmith, a town in what was then Natal Province, 남아프리카 공화국, in 1960. Shabalala had been singing in various choral groups since childhood and drew on the isicathamiya tradition — a performance style shaped by Zulu migrant laborers who competed in all-night singing competitions called *chadula* in the hostels of Durban and Johannesburg.

The group's name reflects its origins: "Ladysmith" is their hometown; "Black" refers to the black ox, symbolizing strength; and "Mambazo" means axe in Zulu, representing their ability to cut down the competition. Early membership consisted primarily of Shabalala's brothers and cousins, grounding the ensemble in family and communal identity from the outset.

## 두각

레이디스미스 블랙 맘바조 first rose to national prominence in 남아프리카 공화국 through Gallo Records in the early 1970s. Their debut album, *Amabutho* (1973), became the first 남아프리카 공화국n album by a Black artist to go gold. Through the 1970s and early 1980s, they were dominant figures in isicathamiya competitions and prolific recording artists, though largely unknown outside the continent.

Their international breakthrough came with Paul Simon's *Graceland* (1986), on which they contributed to tracks including "Homeless" and "Diamonds on the Soles of Her Shoes." The album sold over 16 million copies worldwide. Simon subsequently produced their album *Shaka Zulu* (1987), which won the first of their 그래미상s — Best Traditional Folk Recording. The group toured globally throughout the late 1980s and 1990s, solidifying their international reputation.

## Key Achievements

## Controversies & Criticism

The group has not been without difficulty. Their participation in *Graceland* drew criticism from some anti-apartheid activists who argued that cultural exchanges with 남아프리카 공화국 during the apartheid era — even by Black artists — undermined the cultural boycott. Shabalala and the group maintained that their work was a form of resistance and cultural pride rather than collaboration with the apartheid state.

조지프 샤발랄라 stepped back from performing after the 2002 murder of his wife, Nellie, shot at a police roadblock. Leadership of the group transitioned gradually to Shabalala's sons and nephews. 조지프 샤발랄라 died on February 11, 2020, at age 78, prompting reflection on the group's future continuity.

## Legacy & Influence

레이디스미스 블랙 맘바조 is credited with preserving and globalizing isicathamiya, a tradition that might otherwise have remained confined to regional 남아프리카 공화국n competitions. Their collaboration with Paul Simon helped open Western markets to 아프리카n popular music more broadly, predating and informing later waves of world music popularity.

Their music was used in the 남아프리카 공화국n anti-apartheid movement as a vehicle for cultural identity and dignity, and after 1994 they became symbols of the post-apartheid "Rainbow Nation." 남아프리카 공화국n schools use their recordings as educational material in Zulu music curricula. The group continues to perform and record under the Shabalala family's stewardship, carrying 조지프 샤발랄라's vision into a seventh decade.
