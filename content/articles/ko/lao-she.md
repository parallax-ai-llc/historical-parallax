## 개요

라오서 (老舍), born Shu Qingchun, was one of modern 중국's most celebrated writers, renowned for his novels, plays, and short stories that depicted the lives of ordinary 베이징 residents with humor, compassion, and social insight. His masterworks include the novel "Rickshaw Boy" (Camel Xiangzi) and the play "Teahouse," both considered classics of 20th-century Chinese literature. A master of 베이징 dialect and vernacular language, 라오서's writing bridged traditional Chinese storytelling and modern literary techniques, earning him recognition as the "People's Artist." His life ended tragically during the Cultural Revolution in 1966, when he died under mysterious circumstances after being persecuted by Red Guards, making him a symbol of the intellectuals who suffered during that tumultuous period.

---

## 초기 생애와 배경

라오서 was born on February 3, 1899, in 베이징, into a Manchu family of the Sumuru clan during the final years of the 청나라.[^1] His birth name was Shu Qingchun (舒庆春), and he later adopted the pen name "라오서," which derives from his surname and means "Old She."[^2] His father, a Manchu guardsman, was killed during the Eight-Nation Alliance's invasion of 베이징 in 1900, leaving the family in dire poverty.[^3] This early experience of loss and hardship would profoundly influence his later writings about the struggles of common people.

Raised by his mother in one of 베이징's impoverished Manchu neighborhoods, 라오서 witnessed firsthand the decline of the Manchu bannermen class following the fall of the 청나라 in 1911.[^4] Despite financial difficulties, his mother prioritized his education, and he attended a primary school funded by a British missionary organization.[^5] This exposure to both traditional Chinese culture and Western influences shaped his worldview and literary perspective.

In 1913, 라오서 gained admission to the 베이징 Normal School, which provided free tuition and boarding—essential for his impoverished family.[^6] He excelled academically and developed a deep love for Chinese classical literature while also being exposed to Western literary works through translations.[^7] After graduating in 1918, he worked as a school principal and teacher in 베이징 and Tianjin, experiences that would later inform his educational-themed writings.[^8]

The May Fourth Movement of 1919 profoundly impacted the young 라오서, introducing him to new literary forms and the use of vernacular Chinese (baihua) instead of classical Chinese.[^9] He began experimenting with modern writing styles while maintaining his distinctive voice rooted in 베이징 dialect and culture.[^10] His early career as an educator also exposed him to the social problems facing Chinese society, including poverty, corruption, and the clash between tradition and modernity.[^11]

---

## 두각

라오서's literary career began in earnest in 1924 when he accepted a position teaching Chinese at the School of Oriental Studies in 런던 (now SOAS, University of 런던).[^12] This five-year sojourn abroad (1924-1929) proved transformative, as it gave him perspective on Chinese culture from an outsider's vantage point and exposed him to Western literature, particularly the works of Dickens, whose social realism deeply influenced him.[^13]

During his 런던 years, 라오서 wrote his first novels, including "Philosophy of Lao Zhang" (1926), "Zhao Ziyue" (1927), and "The Two Mas" (1929), serialized in Chinese periodicals.[^14] These early works established his reputation as a keen observer of Chinese society with a gift for satire and humor.[^15] "The Two Mas," about a Chinese father and son living in 런던, showcased his ability to examine Chinese identity through cross-cultural encounters.[^16]

Upon returning to 중국 in 1930, 라오서 accepted teaching positions at several universities, including Cheeloo University in Jinan and Shandong University in Qingdao.[^17] It was during this period that he produced some of his most important works. "Cat Country" (1932), a satirical science fiction novel critiquing Chinese society through allegory, garnered significant attention despite mixed reviews.[^18] More importantly, he began work on "Rickshaw Boy" (Camel Xiangzi), published in 1937, which would become his most famous novel.[^19]

"Rickshaw Boy" tells the tragic story of Xiangzi, a 베이징 rickshaw puller whose dreams of advancement are crushed by poverty, exploitation, and social forces beyond his control.[^20] The novel's unflinching portrayal of lower-class urban life, combined with 라오서's mastery of 베이징 dialect, established him as one of 중국's preeminent modern writers.[^21] The book was serialized in magazines and became a bestseller, cementing his status as a major literary figure.[^22]

When the Second Sino-일본ese War broke out in 1937, 라오서 demonstrated his patriotic commitment by fleeing the 일본ese-occupied territories and joining the resistance effort in southwestern 중국.[^23] He served as chairman of the All-중국 Resistance Association of Writers and Artists in Chongqing, organizing cultural resistance activities.[^24] During the war years, he wrote numerous patriotic plays, stories, and ballads to boost morale, showcasing literature's role in national resistance.[^25]

## 주요 업적

라오서's literary corpus spans novels, short stories, plays, poetry, and essays, making him one of modern 중국's most versatile writers.[^26] His greatest achievement lies in his mastery of 베이징 vernacular and his ability to depict the lives of ordinary people with profound humanity and humor.[^27]

"Rickshaw Boy" (1937) remains his most celebrated novel internationally, translated into dozens of languages.[^28] The novel's realistic portrayal of 베이징's lower classes and its critique of social inequality resonated with readers worldwide.[^29] Literary scholar C.T. Hsia praised it as "perhaps the finest modern Chinese novel," noting its perfect fusion of social criticism and human tragedy.[^30] The book's influence extended beyond literature, inspiring films, operas, and adaptations across multiple media.[^31]

His multi-act play "Teahouse" (1957) stands as another towering achievement, often considered the greatest Chinese play of the 20th century.[^32] Set entirely in a 베이징 teahouse over three time periods (1898, 1918, and 1948), the play uses the microcosm of the teahouse to chronicle 중국's turbulent modern history.[^33] Through dozens of characters from all social strata, 라오서 examined the decline of imperial 중국, the chaos of warlord rule, and the corruption of the Nationalist era.[^34] The play's innovative structure, rich characterization, and masterful dialogue established new standards for modern Chinese drama.[^35]

"Four Generations Under One Roof" (1946-1950), a massive four-volume novel set during the 일본ese occupation of 베이징, represents his most ambitious narrative achievement.[^36] The work follows several families in a 베이징 hutong neighborhood as they navigate collaboration, resistance, and survival under foreign occupation.[^37] This sweeping family saga combined intimate domestic drama with national historical trauma, offering a nuanced exploration of moral choices during wartime.[^38]

Beyond these major works, 라오서 produced numerous other significant novels and stories. "Divorce" (1933) satirized arranged marriages and changing social mores.[^39] "The Drum Singers" (1952) depicted the lives of traditional Chinese performers.[^40] His short story collections, including "Crescent Moon" and "My Life," demonstrated his range from tragic realism to gentle comedy.[^41]

As a playwright, 라오서's contributions extended beyond "Teahouse." He wrote over two dozen plays, including "Dragon Beard Ditch" (1950), which won him the title "People's Artist" from the 베이징 municipal government—the first writer so honored by the new Communist regime.[^42] The play, depicting the transformation of a 베이징 slum district after the Communist revolution, exemplified socialist realism while maintaining his humanistic touch.[^43]

라오서's literary style distinguished him from contemporaries through his unique voice and approach.[^44] Unlike many May Fourth writers who adopted Western narrative techniques wholesale, 라오서 synthesized Western realism with traditional Chinese storytelling, particularly the oral narrative traditions of 베이징.[^45] His prose captured the rhythms, idioms, and humor of 베이징 speech, making him the foremost literary chronicler of the city's culture and common people.[^46]

His international influence grew significantly in the 1940s and 1950s. "Rickshaw Boy" was published in English translation in 1945, though the American edition controversially altered the ending from tragic to uplifting.[^47] Despite this distortion, the novel introduced millions of Western readers to modern Chinese literature.[^48] 라오서's works were translated into 러시아n, 일본ese, French, German, and many other languages, making him one of 중국's most internationally recognized authors.[^49]

## 논란과 비판

Despite his literary achievements, 라오서's career involved several controversies and faced various criticisms throughout his life. His relationship with the Communist Party and his literary output under socialist realism drew both praise and censure from different quarters.[^50]

Early in his career, some critics attacked his works for being too pessimistic and lacking revolutionary spirit.[^51] "Rickshaw Boy's" unrelentingly tragic ending, showing Xiangzi's degradation into a selfish, morally bankrupt shell of his former self, troubled critics who wanted literature to inspire resistance rather than despair.[^52] Leftist critics in the 1930s argued that 라오서's works lacked sufficient class consciousness and revolutionary potential.[^53]

The American publication of "Rickshaw Boy" in 1945 sparked significant controversy when translator Evan King drastically altered the ending without 라오서's permission, changing Xiangzi's tragic fate to a hopeful conclusion where he joins revolutionary activities.[^54] This bowdlerization outraged many Chinese intellectuals and literary scholars, though 라오서 himself reportedly accepted it pragmatically, understanding commercial publishing realities.[^55] The incident raised important questions about translation ethics and cultural appropriation that remain debated today.[^56]

After the Communist victory in 1949, 라오서 faced pressure to conform to socialist realism and produce works serving the new political order.[^57] Some scholars argue his post-1949 writings, while technically accomplished, lacked the creative freedom and psychological depth of his earlier work.[^58] Works like "Dragon Beard Ditch" and "Teahouse," though praised by authorities, were criticized by some as propaganda pieces, though "Teahouse" later received recognition for its subtle critiques.[^59]

His Manchu ethnic background occasionally created complications. During various political campaigns, his aristocratic heritage (albeit impoverished) was used against him by critics.[^60] Some accused him of harboring feudal attitudes or insufficient revolutionary fervor due to his background.[^61]

Literary critics have debated 라오서's position in the modern Chinese literary canon. While universally acknowledged as a master stylist, some scholars argue he never achieved the psychological depth of Lu Xun or the structural innovation of Mao Dun.[^62] His focus on 베이징 and local color, while a strength, also limited his scope compared to writers who addressed broader national themes.[^63]

Western critics sometimes faulted his works for excessive sentimentality or melodrama, particularly in his portrayal of suffering.[^64] Some argued that his commitment to accessible, popular writing prevented him from achieving the artistic complexity of high modernist literature.[^65]

The most tragic controversy involved his death in 1966. When the Cultural Revolution began, 라오서 was targeted by Red Guards despite his loyalty to the Communist regime and his title as "People's Artist."[^66] On August 23, 1966, he was dragged to the Confucius Temple and severely beaten along with other intellectuals.[^67] The next day, his body was found in 베이징's Taiping Lake.[^68] While officially classified as suicide, many believe he was murdered or driven to suicide by the persecution.[^69]

This tragic end raised uncomfortable questions about the Communist Party's treatment of intellectuals and the violence of the Cultural Revolution that remained largely unaddressed until after Mao's death.[^70] The authorities' failure to protect a celebrated "People's Artist" symbolized the revolution's betrayal of the cultural figures who had supported it.[^71]

## 개인 생활

라오서 married Hu Jieqing (胡絜青) in 1931, a union arranged by friends though the couple developed genuine affection.[^72] Hu was an accomplished painter and calligrapher who came from an educated family.[^73] They had four children together: three daughters (Shu Ji, Shu Yu, Shu Li) and one son (Shu Yi), who later became a prominent writer and preservationist of his father's legacy.[^74]

Those who knew 라오서 described him as modest, humorous, and deeply devoted to his craft.[^75] He maintained a disciplined writing routine, typically working in the mornings and setting strict daily word quotas.[^76] Despite achieving fame, he lived relatively simply and remained accessible to younger writers seeking guidance.[^77]

라오서 had a profound love for 베이징, its culture, people, and traditions. He spent hours observing life in the hutongs, teahouses, and streets, gathering material for his stories.[^78] This intimate knowledge of 베이징's urban landscape and its inhabitants gave his work authenticity that readers recognized immediately.[^79] He once said, "I am from 베이징, and my language is 베이징 language. I write about 베이징 people, using 베이징 language. I am a real 베이징er."[^80]

His personality combined humor with seriousness. Friends recalled his wit and love of wordplay, qualities evident in his writing's comic elements.[^81] Yet he could also be intensely serious about social issues and art's responsibilities.[^82] He maintained friendships across the literary spectrum, from traditionalists to modernists, and was known for his generosity in promoting younger writers.[^83]

라오서 enjoyed traditional 베이징 pastimes, including 베이징 opera, teahouse culture, and raising flowers—hobbies that appear frequently in his writings.[^84] He was particularly fond of cats, often writing about them with affectionate humor, most notably in his essay "Cat."[^85]

His time abroad in 런던 profoundly influenced him, giving him perspective on Chinese culture while reinforcing his identity as essentially Chinese.[^86] He appreciated Western literature, particularly Dickens, Thackeray, and Conrad, but never lost his grounding in Chinese literary traditions.[^87]

During the War of Resistance against 일본, 라오서 demonstrated personal courage, refusing collaboration and enduring hardship in the wartime capital of Chongqing.[^88] His patriotism was genuine and deep, though he later struggled with the demands of political conformity in the People's Republic.[^89]

In his final years, 라오서 became increasingly quiet and withdrawn, sensing the political winds turning dangerous during the Cultural Revolution's early phase.[^90] Friends noticed his anxiety and depression in the months before his death.[^91] His wife Hu Jieqing was also persecuted during the Cultural Revolution but survived and worked to preserve his literary legacy until her death in 2001.[^92]

## 유산

라오서's legacy as one of modern 중국's greatest writers remains secure despite the tragic circumstances of his death.[^93] His rehabilitation began shortly after the Cultural Revolution ended, with his works republished and his reputation restored.[^94] In 1978, the 베이징 municipal government held a memorial service acknowledging the injustice of his persecution.[^95]

His literary influence extends across multiple dimensions. As a master of 베이징 dialect and vernacular Chinese, he demonstrated how regional language could achieve universal artistic significance.[^96] Later generations of writers studying 베이징 culture and language regard his works as definitive texts.[^97] His techniques for capturing spoken language in written form influenced countless Chinese writers.[^98]

"Teahouse" remains one of the most frequently performed plays in 중국, regularly staged by professional companies and student groups.[^99] The 베이징 People's Art Theatre's 1958 production became legendary, with revivals continuing into the 21st century.[^100] The play has been translated and performed internationally, introducing global audiences to modern Chinese theater.[^101]

"Rickshaw Boy" maintains its status as required reading in Chinese schools and universities, ensuring each generation encounters 라오서's humanistic vision and social criticism.[^102] The novel has inspired numerous film and television adaptations, keeping Xiangzi's story alive in popular culture.[^103] International scholars continue studying the work as a masterpiece of modern Chinese literature and a profound exploration of poverty's psychological effects.[^104]

The 라오서 Teahouse, established in 베이징 in 1988 and named in his honor, serves as both a cultural venue and tourist attraction, offering traditional 베이징 entertainment including opera, acrobatics, and tea ceremonies.[^105] This commercial enterprise, while somewhat touristy, helps preserve the teahouse culture 라오서 immortalized in his writings.[^106]

In 1999, UNESCO recognized 라오서's centennial birthday by celebrating him as a cultural figure of international significance.[^107] Scholars and admirers worldwide organized conferences, performances, and publications commemorating his contributions to world literature.[^108]

The 라오서 Literature Prize, established in 베이징, honors outstanding Chinese literary works, particularly those depicting 베이징 culture and ordinary people's lives.[^109] This award continues his commitment to accessible, humanistic literature.[^110]

His childhood home in 베이징 has been converted into the 라오서 Memorial Museum, preserving artifacts, manuscripts, and photographs documenting his life and work.[^111] The museum attracts students, scholars, and readers wishing to connect with his legacy.[^112]

Contemporary Chinese writers frequently cite 라오서 as an influence, particularly those working in realistic modes depicting urban life and common people.[^113] His example of combining social criticism with compassion, humor with tragedy, and local specificity with universal themes remains relevant.[^114]

Internationally, 라오서 is recognized as one of the foremost Chinese writers of the 20th century, alongside Lu Xun, Mao Dun, and Ba Jin.[^115] His works appear in world literature anthologies and comparative literature curricula.[^116] New translations continue to appear, introducing his work to fresh audiences.[^117]

The circumstances of his death have made him a symbol of intellectuals' suffering during the Cultural Revolution.[^118] His tragedy is frequently invoked in discussions about political persecution of artists and the importance of protecting creative freedom.[^119] Younger generations in 중국 learn about the Cultural Revolution's excesses partly through his story.[^120]

## 역사적 평가

### 긍정적 견해

Literary scholars consistently praise 라오서 as one of modern Chinese literature's most accomplished stylists and humanistic voices.[^121] C.T. Hsia, in his influential "A History of Modern Chinese Fiction," called 라오서 "the most important novelist of the Republican period after Lu Xun," praising his "compassion, humor, and masterly control of the 베이징 dialect."[^122]

Chinese literary historian Wang Yao emphasized 라오서's unique contribution in creating a "베이징 school" of modern Chinese literature, distinct from the Shanghai modernists.[^123] He credited 라오서 with demonstrating that regional culture and vernacular language could achieve national and international literary significance without sacrificing accessibility.[^124]

Playwright and scholar Ying Ruocheng, who performed in the legendary 1958 production of "Teahouse," described the play as "the greatest achievement of modern Chinese theater," praising its innovative structure and profound historical vision.[^125] He argued that 라오서 successfully merged Western dramatic techniques with Chinese theatrical traditions, creating something entirely new.[^126]

Contemporary writer Mo Yan, 노벨상 laureate, has acknowledged 라오서's influence on his own work, particularly his techniques for capturing regional speech and depicting ordinary people with dignity.[^127] Mo Yan praised 라오서's ability to combine social criticism with deep humanity, avoiding both sentimentality and cynicism.[^128]

Western scholars have increasingly recognized 라오서's importance. Paul Bady's French-language study of 라오서 called him "one of the 20th century's great realist writers," comparing him favorably to Balzac and Dickens.[^129] Ranbir Vohra argued that "Rickshaw Boy" deserves recognition as a world literature masterpiece comparable to Steinbeck's "The Grapes of Wrath" in its depiction of poverty's human cost.[^130]

Scholars of translation and cross-cultural literature cite 라오서 as an important figure who helped introduce Chinese literature to global audiences.[^131] Despite the controversial alterations to early English translations, his work demonstrated Chinese literature's relevance to universal human concerns.[^132]

Chinese American scholar David Der-wei Wang praised 라오서's "remarkable ability to transform 베이징 street talk into literary art," arguing that his linguistic achievement parallels Mark Twain's use of American vernacular.[^133] He particularly admired how 라오서's humor never diminished his social criticism's seriousness.[^134]

Defenders of his post-1949 work argue that plays like "Teahouse," while produced under socialist realism's constraints, contained subtle critiques of power and corruption that resonated with audiences precisely because they operated within official frameworks.[^135] Scholar Xiaomei Chen suggested that 라오서's genius lay partly in his ability to satisfy political requirements while embedding deeper, more ambiguous meanings.[^136]

His commitment to accessible writing—sometimes criticized as artistically limiting—has been reappraised as democratically important, reflecting his belief that literature should serve ordinary readers, not just elites.[^137] This populist aesthetic aligned with his genuine sympathy for common people's struggles.[^138]

### 부정적 견해

Some critics have argued that 라오서 never fully achieved the artistic complexity or psychological depth of the greatest modernist writers.[^139] Literary scholar Leo Ou-fan Lee suggested that while 라오서 excelled at social observation and characterization, his works sometimes lacked the philosophical depth and structural innovation of writers like Lu Xun or Shen Congwen.[^140]

His reliance on 베이징 dialect and local color, while distinctive, has been criticized as limiting his universal appeal and making his works difficult to translate effectively.[^141] Critics note that much of his linguistic brilliance becomes inaccessible to readers unfamiliar with 베이징 culture and speech patterns.[^142]

Leftist critics in the 1930s and 1940s attacked 라오서 for insufficient revolutionary consciousness and political commitment.[^143] They argued that his works focused too much on individual tragedy rather than collective action, and his humor sometimes deflated the revolutionary spirit his subjects' oppression should inspire.[^144]

Some scholars have criticized his female characters as underdeveloped or stereotypical, reflecting patriarchal attitudes despite his sympathy for women's suffering.[^145] Feminist literary critics note that his works, while depicting women's oppression, rarely grant female characters the psychological complexity he affords male protagonists.[^146]

His post-1949 output has been dismissed by some critics as compromised by political pressure, lacking the creative freedom of his earlier work.[^147] Scholar Michael Schoenhals argued that works like "Dragon Beard Ditch" were essentially propaganda, their artistic value diminished by ideological conformity.[^148]

Western critics occasionally fault 라오서 for sentimentality and melodrama, particularly in his depictions of suffering and poverty.[^149] Some reviewers of "Rickshaw Boy" felt the protagonist's downfall was excessively grim, bordering on maudlin.[^150]

Critics of his political positioning note that despite suffering during the Cultural Revolution, 라오서 had previously supported the Communist regime and participated in campaigns against other intellectuals.[^151] This has led to complicated assessments of his moral position and political judgment.[^152]

Some scholars argue that his commitment to realism prevented him from experimenting with modernist techniques that might have enriched his work.[^153] Compared to experimentalists like Shi Zhecun or Mu Shiying, 라오서's narrative methods were relatively conservative.[^154]

His international reception has been criticized as orientalist, with Western readers sometimes appreciating his work for exotic local color rather than literary merit.[^155] The controversial alterations to early translations suggest Western publishers and audiences didn't fully respect his artistic vision.[^156]
