## 개요

King Faisal bin Abdulaziz Al Saud served as the third King of 사우디아라비아 from 1964 until his assassination in 1975, presiding over a transformative period in the kingdom's modern history. As a skilled diplomat and conservative 이슬람ic reformer, Faisal modernized 사우디아라비아's infrastructure and education system while maintaining strict adherence to Wahhabi religious principles[^1]. He is perhaps best remembered internationally for wielding the "oil weapon" during the 1973 Arab-이스라엘i War, orchestrating an oil embargo that quadrupled petroleum prices and demonstrated OPEC's geopolitical power[^2]. His reign balanced traditional 이슬람ic values with cautious modernization, earning him respect as a statesman while also drawing criticism for authoritarian governance and the kingdom's restrictive social policies[^3]. Faisal's life ended tragically when he was assassinated by his nephew in 1975, cutting short a reign that had significantly elevated 사우디아라비아's global standing[^4].

## 초기 생애와 배경

Faisal bin Abdulaziz was born on April 14, 1906, in Riyadh, the third son of Abdulaziz ibn Saud, who would later unify the Kingdom of 사우디아라비아[^5]. His mother, Tarfa bint Abdullah Al Sheikh, was a descendant of Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab, the founder of Wahhabism, giving Faisal impeccable religious credentials that would shape his worldview throughout his life[^6]. Unlike many of his brothers, Faisal received a traditional 이슬람ic education focused on Quranic studies and 이슬람ic jurisprudence, studying under prominent religious scholars in Riyadh[^7].

Faisal's childhood occurred during a turbulent period as his father fought to consolidate control over the Arabian Peninsula. This environment of constant warfare and political maneuvering profoundly influenced young Faisal's understanding of power and statecraft[^8]. His father recognized his intelligence and seriousness early on, often involving him in political discussions despite his youth[^9].

At age thirteen, Faisal accompanied his father on a military campaign, gaining firsthand experience in desert warfare and tribal politics[^10]. This early exposure to leadership responsibilities distinguished him from his half-brothers and prepared him for the diplomatic roles he would later assume[^11]. His mother's death when he was young also had a profound impact, making him more reserved and contemplative than many of his siblings[^12].

By his teenage years, Faisal had already developed the austere, pious demeanor that would characterize his entire life. He practiced strict religious observance, avoided the lavish lifestyle common among Arabian princes, and demonstrated an unusual capacity for patience and strategic thinking[^13]. These qualities would later serve him well in navigating the complex challenges of modernizing a deeply conservative 이슬람ic kingdom[^14].

## 권력의 부상

Faisal's ascent to power was gradual and built on decades of diplomatic service to the Saudi state. In 1919, at just thirteen years old, his father sent him on a diplomatic mission to 런던 to meet with British officials, marking the beginning of his career as 사우디아라비아's chief diplomat[^15]. This mission was remarkable not only for Faisal's youth but also for the trust it demonstrated from his father, who was carefully positioning his sons for future leadership roles[^16].

Throughout the 1920s and 1930s, Faisal served as Viceroy of Hejaz, the western region of 사우디아라비아 containing the holy cities of Mecca and Medina[^17]. This position required exceptional diplomatic skills, as he had to balance the expectations of conservative Wahhabi forces with the more cosmopolitan traditions of Hejaz's urban populations[^18]. His success in this role demonstrated his ability to navigate conflicting interests while maintaining his father's authority[^19].

In 1932, when 사우디아라비아 was formally unified as a kingdom, Faisal became the first Saudi Foreign Minister, a position he would hold for nearly four decades[^20]. In this capacity, he represented 사우디아라비아 at the founding of the 유엔 in 1945 and established the kingdom's presence on the international stage[^21]. His fluency in English and French, along with his dignified bearing, made him an effective advocate for Saudi interests in Western capitals[^22].

The critical turning point came in 1958 when Faisal was appointed Prime Minister by his brother King Saud, who was facing a financial crisis and political challenges[^23]. Faisal implemented sweeping fiscal reforms, eliminating the budget deficit and establishing financial discipline that had been absent under Saud's spendthrift reign[^24]. This success enhanced his reputation as a capable administrator and reformer[^25].

A power struggle between Faisal and King Saud dominated Saudi politics from 1958 to 1964, with the royal family and religious establishment increasingly backing Faisal due to his competence and Saud's erratic behavior[^26]. In 1964, senior princes and religious leaders formally deposed Saud and proclaimed Faisal as King, a transition accomplished without violence due to Faisal's careful coalition-building and respect for traditional sources of authority[^27].

## 주요 업적

King Faisal's reign from 1964 to 1975 witnessed profound transformations in 사우디아라비아's domestic development and international standing. His most significant achievement was modernizing the kingdom's infrastructure and educational system while maintaining 이슬람ic legitimacy[^28]. He dramatically expanded public education, including controversial support for girls' education despite fierce opposition from conservative religious scholars, arguing that the Prophet Muhammad's wife Aisha had been educated[^29]. By 1975, 사우디아라비아 had thousands of schools where previously there had been only a handful[^30].

Faisal established the kingdom's first television network in 1965, overcoming religious objections by ensuring that Quran recitation was the first broadcast and that programming adhered to 이슬람ic values[^31]. He similarly expanded telecommunications, healthcare facilities, and transportation infrastructure, transforming 사우디아라비아 from a largely medieval society into a modern state[^32]. His approach to modernization was always filtered through 이슬람ic principles, earning him the description as a "conservative modernizer"[^33].

In foreign policy, Faisal positioned 사우디아라비아 as the leader of the 이슬람ic world, hosting the 1969 이슬람ic Summit Conference in Rabat following the burning of Al-Aqsa Mosque and subsequently establishing the Organization of 이슬람ic Cooperation[^34]. He used 사우디아라비아's oil wealth to provide aid to Muslim countries and 이슬람ic causes worldwide, from 파키스탄 to 팔레스타인, building a network of influence that extended Saudi soft power globally[^35].

His most dramatic achievement was orchestrating the 1973 oil embargo following the October War between Arab states and 이스라엘[^36]. When the 미국 resupplied 이스라엘 during the conflict, Faisal—despite his traditionally pro-Western orientation—led OPEC's Arab members in cutting oil production and embargoing exports to the 미국 and other 이스라엘i supporters[^37]. This action quadrupled oil prices, caused economic disruption throughout the Western world, and demonstrated that oil-producing nations could use petroleum as a political weapon[^38]. The embargo established 사우디아라비아 as a crucial player in global economics and gave Faisal enormous leverage in international diplomacy[^39].

Faisal also strengthened 사우디아라비아's military capabilities, using growing oil revenues to purchase advanced weapons systems primarily from the 미국 and Britain[^40]. He established the Saudi National Guard as a tribal-based force loyal to the royal family, creating a counterbalance to the regular army and reducing the risk of military coups[^41].

Domestically, Faisal abolished slavery in 사우디아라비아 in 1962 while serving as Prime Minister, compensating slave owners to ease the transition and removing a practice that had increasingly embarrassed the kingdom internationally[^42]. He also initiated development projects that brought electricity, clean water, and healthcare to remote areas of the kingdom that had previously been untouched by modernization[^43].

## 논란과 비판

Despite his achievements, Faisal's reign was marked by significant controversies and authoritarian practices that drew substantial criticism. His government maintained absolute monarchy with no political participation, freedom of speech, or independent judiciary, ruling through royal decrees and religious edicts rather than representative institutions[^44]. Political dissidents faced imprisonment, torture, and execution, with no due process protections or meaningful legal recourse[^45].

The kingdom's treatment of women remained severely restrictive under Faisal despite his support for girls' education. Women were prohibited from driving, traveling without male permission, or participating in public life, restrictions justified by the government's interpretation of 이슬람ic law but condemned by 인권 organizations as discriminatory[^46]. While Faisal allowed girls' schools, the curriculum reinforced traditional gender roles and women's education remained segregated and limited in scope[^47].

Faisal's fierce anti-communism led him to support conservative and sometimes reactionary forces throughout the Muslim world, prioritizing opposition to Soviet influence over democratic governance or 인권[^48]. He funded religious schools that promoted Wahhabi ideology globally, an investment that critics later linked to the rise of 이슬람ic extremism, though this connection remained contentious[^49]. His support for 파키스탄 during its 1971 civil war, despite evidence of mass atrocities against Bengalis, exemplified his willingness to overlook 인권 violations when aligned with strategic interests[^50].

The 1973 oil embargo, while demonstrating Saudi power, imposed severe economic hardship on developing nations that had no involvement in the Arab-이스라엘i conflict, causing inflation, unemployment, and economic disruption that particularly harmed the poorest countries[^51]. Critics argued that Faisal's oil weapon was used indiscriminately, punishing innocent populations for their governments' foreign policies[^52].

Religious minorities faced systematic discrimination under Faisal's reign, with Shia Muslims subjected to educational restrictions, employment discrimination, and prohibition of public religious practice[^53]. Non-Muslim religious practice was completely banned, with foreign workers prohibited from public worship and Christian churches forbidden even in compounds housing Western expatriates[^54].

Faisal's concentration of wealth within the royal family while many Saudis remained poor drew criticism, particularly as oil revenues soared in the 1970s[^55]. The lack of transparency in government spending and the royal family's access to state resources without accountability created a system that critics described as kleptocratic[^56]. Labor rights were non-existent, with foreign workers facing exploitation and abuse with no legal protections[^57].

His suppression of the Arab nationalist movement within 사우디아라비아, including imprisonment of activists inspired by Nasser's pan-Arabism, reflected his fear of ideologies that could challenge monarchical rule[^58]. This repression extended to any form of political organization, including peaceful advocacy for reform, creating a climate of fear that stifled civil society development[^59].

## 개인 생활

Faisal's personal life was marked by piety, austerity, and dedication to duty that distinguished him from many other Arab monarchs of his era. He married multiple times as permitted under 이슬람ic law, with his most prominent wife being Iffat Al-Thunayan, whom he married in 1932 and who became an influential advocate for women's education[^60]. Iffat was of Turkish-Saudi heritage and had received education in Istanbul, making her more cosmopolitan than typical Saudi royal women[^61]. She played a crucial role in convincing Faisal to support girls' education and established the first schools for girls in the kingdom, often facing fierce opposition from conservative clergy[^62].

Faisal fathered nine children, including eight sons and one daughter, and maintained a relatively modest household by royal standards[^63]. He was known for his austere personal habits, avoiding the lavish parties and ostentatious displays of wealth common among other Gulf monarchs[^64]. His daily routine included rising for dawn prayers, reading the Quran, and maintaining a strict work schedule that often extended late into the evening[^65].

Those who knew Faisal described him as reserved, formal, and intensely serious, with little interest in leisure activities or entertainment[^66]. He rarely smiled in public and maintained a dignified, almost stern demeanor that reflected his deep religiosity and sense of responsibility[^67]. His speeches were typically delivered in classical Arabic with measured, careful phrasing that emphasized 이슬람ic values and traditional wisdom[^68].

Despite his conservative religious views, Faisal appreciated certain aspects of Western technology and efficiency, hosting foreign diplomats and businessmen with courtesy and engaging in detailed discussions about development projects[^69]. He traveled extensively during his years as Foreign Minister, visiting the 미국, 유럽, and 아시아n countries, where he observed governance systems and technological advances that he selectively adapted to Saudi circumstances[^70].

Faisal's relationship with his brothers was complex, marked by cooperation in governing the kingdom but also by the power struggle with King Saud that dominated the late 1950s and early 1960s[^71]. After becoming King, Faisal worked to maintain family unity and established the principle of collective decision-making among senior princes, though ultimate authority remained with him[^72].

His relationship with Iffat was reportedly one of genuine partnership, unusual in Saudi royal marriages, with Faisal consulting her on social policy and education matters[^73]. She accompanied him on foreign trips, breaking with tradition, and established charitable organizations that provided healthcare and education services[^74]. Their partnership demonstrated Faisal's willingness to grant women influential roles in private even as public restrictions remained severe[^75].

## 유산

King Faisal's legacy remains complex and contested, with assessments varying dramatically based on perspective and priorities. Within 사우디아라비아, he is revered as the architect of modern development, the king who transformed a poor desert kingdom into a wealthy, influential state while maintaining 이슬람ic authenticity[^76]. His image adorns currency, public buildings, and monuments, and the generation that lived through his reign often speaks of it as a golden age of wise leadership and rapid progress[^77].

Faisal's most enduring legacy is the model he established for combining 이슬람ic conservatism with selective modernization, a formula that subsequent Saudi rulers have largely followed[^78]. He demonstrated that a monarchy rooted in Wahhabi religious principles could adopt modern technology, infrastructure, and administrative systems without secularizing, providing a blueprint for development that rejected both Western liberal democracy and secular Arab nationalism[^79].

The educational infrastructure Faisal created transformed Saudi society, producing the first generation of university-educated Saudis who would staff the growing bureaucracy and oil industry[^80]. The inclusion of girls' education, despite fierce initial opposition, eventually became accepted and expanded, fundamentally changing women's opportunities even within the kingdom's restrictive framework[^81].

Internationally, Faisal elevated 사우디아라비아 from a marginal desert kingdom to a major player in global affairs, primarily through the strategic use of oil wealth[^82]. The 1973 embargo demonstrated that resource-rich developing nations could challenge Western economic dominance, inspiring other commodity producers and reshaping international economic relations[^83]. 사우디아라비아's position as the de facto leader of OPEC and a crucial energy supplier to the global economy was firmly established during Faisal's reign[^84].

His promotion of pan-이슬람ic solidarity created lasting networks of Saudi influence, with the kingdom funding mosques, schools, and 이슬람ic centers worldwide[^85]. This soft power strategy established 사우디아라비아 as the center of Sunni 이슬람ic world, though critics later connected this ideological export to the rise of extremist movements[^86].

However, Faisal's legacy also includes the entrenchment of authoritarian governance, gender discrimination, and religious intolerance that have persisted in 사우디아라비아[^87]. His modernization was always bounded by strict limits on personal freedom, political participation, and social liberalization, establishing patterns that his successors maintained for decades[^88].

The concentration of wealth and power in the royal family, which accelerated dramatically during the oil boom of Faisal's final years, created a system of governance that many critics view as fundamentally unjust and unsustainable[^89]. The absence of mechanisms for political accountability or peaceful power transition remains a vulnerability in the Saudi system that Faisal did nothing to address[^90].

Historians debate whether Faisal's conservative approach to modernization was necessary given 사우디아라비아's deeply traditional society or whether it represented a missed opportunity to pursue more comprehensive social and political reform[^91]. His assassination in 1975 left these questions unresolved, as his successors faced different challenges in subsequent decades[^92].

## 역사적 평가

### 긍정적 견해

Admirers of King Faisal emphasize his skillful navigation of extraordinary challenges, transforming a backward, impoverished kingdom into a modern state without provoking the social upheaval that plagued many developing nations during rapid modernization[^93]. Scholars like Alexei Vassiliev argue that Faisal's genius lay in understanding the limits of what Saudi society would accept, pushing reform as far as possible without triggering a conservative backlash that could have destabilized the kingdom[^94].

His supporters point to the abolition of slavery, expansion of education including for girls, and infrastructure development as genuine progressive achievements that required courage and political skill to accomplish against fierce conservative opposition[^95]. The fact that girls' education eventually became accepted, despite riots and death threats against its early advocates, demonstrates Faisal's ability to achieve social change through patient, incremental reform[^96].

Western diplomats who worked with Faisal consistently praised his integrity, intelligence, and reliability, describing him as a man of his word whose commitments could be trusted[^97]. Unlike many oil-rich autocrats, Faisal avoided personal corruption and maintained modest personal habits while investing oil revenues in national development rather than private enrichment[^98].

Supporters of Faisal's foreign policy argue that the 1973 oil embargo was a justified response to American support for 이스라엘i occupation of Arab lands, representing a rare moment when developing nations successfully leveraged their resources against Western dominance[^99]. The embargo forced American policymakers to take Arab concerns seriously and contributed to eventual peace negotiations[^100].

이슬람ic scholars sympathetic to Faisal's worldview praise his commitment to 이슬람ic principles in governance, his support for 이슬람ic causes worldwide, and his resistance to secularization pressures from Western-oriented reformers[^101]. They argue that his model of 이슬람ic modernization preserved religious and cultural authenticity while adopting beneficial technologies, offering an alternative to both secular dictatorship and Western liberal democracy[^102].

Economic historians note that Faisal inherited a bankrupt kingdom and left it with substantial foreign reserves, a balanced budget, and modern infrastructure, representing a remarkable fiscal and administrative achievement[^103]. The transformation from a state dependent on British subsidies to one wielding significant global economic influence occurred almost entirely during his leadership[^104].

---

### 부정적 견해

Critics of Faisal condemn his reign as establishing and legitimizing an authoritarian system that denied basic 인권 to Saudi citizens for decades. Human rights organizations have consistently ranked 사우디아라비아 among the world's most repressive states, citing restrictions on freedom of expression, assembly, and religion that Faisal actively enforced and institutionalized[^105].

Feminist scholars view Faisal's legacy with particular harshness, arguing that his support for girls' education was severely limited and ultimately served to reinforce gender segregation rather than promote genuine equality[^106]. The restrictions on women's mobility, autonomy, and public participation that characterized his reign have had lasting harmful effects on Saudi women's rights[^107].

Some historians argue that Faisal's anti-communist obsession led him to support reactionary forces throughout the Muslim world, backing military dictators, opposing democratic movements, and funding religious extremism as a counterweight to leftist ideologies[^108]. The long-term consequences of this strategy, including potential links to later terrorist movements, represent a deeply problematic legacy[^109].

Economic critics note that despite massive oil revenues, wealth distribution in 사우디아라비아 remained extremely unequal, with the royal family capturing disproportionate benefits while many ordinary Saudis lived in poverty[^110]. The lack of economic diversification during Faisal's reign, with overdependence on oil exports, created vulnerabilities that persist decades later[^111].

Arab nationalists view Faisal as a reactionary who opposed progressive movements for Arab unity and self-determination, using oil wealth to prop up conservative monarchies and undermine democratic aspirations throughout the Arab world[^112]. His alignment with Western powers, particularly the 미국, represented a betrayal of Arab independence and dignity in this perspective[^113].

Scholars of 중동ern politics argue that Faisal's use of the oil weapon, while demonstrating Saudi power, ultimately backfired by accelerating Western investment in alternative energy sources and non-OPEC oil production, reducing long-term Saudi leverage[^114]. The embargo also damaged relations with the West without achieving substantive changes in 이스라엘i policies toward Palestinians[^115].

Critics of Wahhabi ideology view Faisal's global promotion of this strict 이슬람ic interpretation as deeply harmful, funding schools and mosques that preached intolerance toward other religions and 이슬람ic traditions[^116]. Researchers have documented connections between Saudi-funded religious institutions and the radicalization of individuals who later engaged in terrorism, though the extent of responsibility remains debated[^117].
