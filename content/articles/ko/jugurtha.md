## 개요

Numidian king who murdered his co-rulers and bribed Roman senators to avoid prosecution.

## Context (Numidia and 로마)

- **Numidia** was a North 아프리카n kingdom whose rulers had long interacted with 로마 through alliance, diplomacy, and warfare.
- 유구르타’s rise occurred during a period when **Roman provincial influence** and **elite patronage networks** could shape outcomes far beyond 이탈리아.
- Ancient sources (and later historians) often used the Jugurthine War to illustrate themes of **corruption**, **factional politics**, and the limits of senatorial accountability.

## Rise to Power (High-Level)

- 유구르타 was raised within the **Numidian royal household** and became a prominent military figure before the civil conflict.
- After King Micipsa’s death, 유구르타 clashed with co-heirs **Adherbal** and **Hiempsal**, eventually eliminating rivals and consolidating power.
- Roman attempts to mediate the succession dispute did not end the conflict, and 유구르타 continued pursuing dominance over the kingdom.

## The Jugurthine War (112–105 BCE)

- The war began as a regional conflict but became a major Roman concern after violence touched **Roman/Italian interests** in North 아프리카.
- Roman operations shifted across commanders and strategies:
  - Early efforts were undermined by political controversy and allegations of bribery.
  - Under **Quintus Caecilius Metellus**, Roman forces applied sustained pressure and pursued a more disciplined campaign.
  - Under **Gaius Marius**, 로마 increased manpower and operational intensity, eventually tightening the net around 유구르타.
- 유구르타 relied on **mobility**, **local knowledge**, and **shifting alliances**, making him a difficult opponent for conventional Roman tactics.

## Capture and Death

- 유구르타’s final defeat came through diplomacy as much as battlefield outcomes.
- **Bocchus I of Mauretania**, 유구르타’s father-in-law and intermittent ally, negotiated with 로마 and ultimately handed 유구르타 over.
- 유구르타 was taken to 로마, displayed in a triumph, and then executed (traditionally associated with imprisonment in the **Tullianum**).

## Historical Significance

- The conflict is frequently cited as an example of how **external wars** intersected with **Roman domestic politics**.
- It contributed to the political prominence of leaders like **Marius** and to narratives about reform, patronage, and elite competition in the late Republic.
- 유구르타’s story became a durable case study in the vulnerability of institutions when **incentives favor bribery and factional advantage**.
