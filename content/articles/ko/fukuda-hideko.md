Recent scholarship has worked to address the historical neglect of Hideko's contributions, with researchers like Murata Shizuko dedicating substantial effort to documenting her life and activism.[^137] Academic conferences and edited volumes on rethinking Japanese feminisms increasingly include Hideko's work, reflecting growing recognition among scholars that her contributions deserve central placement in histories of Japanese feminism and 사회주의.[^138]

### Critical and Negative Views

~에도 불구하고 growing scholarly recognition, Fukuda Hideko has faced various forms of criticism and remains less visible in popular consciousness than other Meiji-era feminists. The most 중요한 criticism concerns her participation in the 1885 Osaka Incident, which involved planning an armed intervention in Korea with smuggled weapons.[^139] ~하는 동안 sympathetic scholars view this as anti-imperialist internationalism, critics—particularly at the time—saw it as dangerous criminal conspiracy that could have provoked wider 분쟁. The Meiji government treated it as serious sedition, and even some within the reform movement questioned the wisdom and ethics of the planned action.[^140]

Some critics have noted limitations in Hideko's approach to feminist organizing. ~하는 동안 *Seitō* was established and run exclusively by women, *Sekai Fujin* was published with substantial assistance from male socialists, particularly Ishikawa Sanshirō.[^141] This has led some feminist historians to argue that Hideko's publication may have been constrained by male perspectives and priorities within the socialist movement, potentially limiting its feminist autonomy and vision. The fact that two of the three major male partners in Hideko's life—Ōi Kentarō and Ishikawa Sanshirō—treated her poorly and 궁극적으로 abandoned her suggests that even progressive male activists failed to fully embrace gender 평등 in their personal lives.[^142]

The relative obscurity of Hideko compared to Hiratsuka Raichō and the *Seitō* circle has been noted by scholars who point out that standard historical texts mention *Seitō* as the first women's movement in Japan ~하는 동안 omitting Hideko's earlier organizing efforts entirely.[^143] ~하는 동안 this neglect likely reflects factors beyond Hideko's control—including her poverty, 정부 suppression of her publications, and the lack of institutional support for preserving her legacy—some might argue that her inability to create lasting organizational structures or to cultivate the kind of visibility that *Seitō* achieved represents a limitation in her activism's effectiveness and reach.

Critics of Hideko's ideological positions might point to her conflicts with various political factions. She broke with elements of the Liberal Party over what she perceived as their "insincerity" and disreputable behavior, and she maintained 비판적인 distance from mainstream political parties throughout her career.[^144] ~하는 동안 her principled 독립 can be viewed positively, it also meant she often lacked institutional support and organizational resources that more accommodating activists might have secured, potentially limiting her practical impact.

Some might also note that in her later years, Hideko became "much less involved in political affairs" and lived in "extreme poverty," suggesting that her activism may not have been sustainable or that she failed to build the networks of support that might have ensured a more dignified and 영향력 있는 final decades.[^145] 그러나 such criticism must be tempered by recognition of the extraordinary obstacles she faced, including systematic government 박해, economic exploitation, and the abandonment by male partners who had far more social and economic resources available to them.
