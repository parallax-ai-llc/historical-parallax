## 개요

Siddhartha Gautama (c. 563/480 – c. 483/400 BC), known as the Buddha ("The Awakened One" or "The Enlightened One"), was a wandering ascetic and spiritual 교사 who founded Buddhism in 고대의 India. Born as a 왕자 in the Shakya kingdom (in present-day Nepal), Siddhartha renounced his privileged life at age 29 to seek the solution to human suffering. ~ 후에 six years of extreme asceticism and meditation, he attained enlightenment (Bodhi) under the Bodhi tree at age 35, discovering the Middle Way and the Four Noble Truths. For the remaining 45 years of his life, the Buddha traveled throughout northeastern India, teaching the Dharma (his discovery of the path to liberation from suffering) and establishing the Sangha (community of monks). His teachings ~이 되었다 foundation of Buddhism, now practiced by over 500 million people worldwide, profoundly influencing Asian civilization and gaining increasing adherents in the West.

## The Great Renunciation

### Leaving the Palace

Deeply troubled by discovering suffering's reality, Siddhartha made the momentous decision to abandon his princely life. One night, he looked upon his sleeping wife and infant son, feeling both love and recognition that attachment would prevent his quest. In an event called the "Great Renunciation," Sid

dhartha left the palace on his horse Kanthaka, accompanied only by his charioteer Channa.

At the forest's edge, Siddhartha cut off his hair with his sword, exchanged his royal garments for simple robes, and sent Channa and Kanthaka back to the palace. He ~이 되었다 śramaṇa—a wandering ascetic seeking truth. This renunciation of worldly life at its peak (youth, family, power, wealth) ~이 되었다 central model for Buddhist monasticism.

### Study with Teachers

Siddhartha first sought instruction from 저명한 meditation masters:

**Ālāra Kālāma** taught him to achieve the formless absorption called "the sphere of nothingness," a high meditative state. Siddhartha mastered this quickly, and Ālāra offered to make him co-교사. But Siddhartha realized this state, though peaceful, did not end suffering permanently.

**Uddaka Rāmaputta** taught an even subtler absorption called "neither perception nor non-perception." Again, Siddhartha mastered the technique but recognized it did not provide ultimate liberation.

Concluding that meditative absorption alone could not end suffering, Siddhartha departed to pursue his own path.

### Extreme Asceticism

Siddhartha joined five ascetics practicing severe self-mortification near Bodh Gaya. For six years, he engaged in extreme austerities:

- Holding his breath for extended periods

These practices reduced him to skin and bones, bringing him near death. Buddhist texts describe his body becoming so emaciated that touching his stomach could feel his spine. Yet 이에도 불구하고 extreme discipline, Siddhartha still had not achieved enlightenment.

## Enlightenment

### The Middle Way

Near death from self-starvation, Siddhartha realized that extreme asceticism was no more effective than extreme indulgence. He remembered experiencing a spontaneous meditative absorption (jhāna) as a child sitting under a rose-apple tree, achieved without self-torment. This memory revealed a crucial insight: the path to liberation lay neither in sensual indulgence nor in self-mortification, but in a "Middle Way" between extremes.

Siddhartha accepted milk-rice from a village girl named Sujata, breaking his fast and beginning to restore his health. Witnessing this, his five ascetic companions were disgusted, believing he had abandoned the quest, and they left him.

### Bodhi Tree

Strengthened, Siddhartha sat down beneath a pipal tree (later called the Bodhi Tree or "Tree of Awakening") in Bodh Gaya, vowing not to rise ~까지 he had found the truth. Buddhist tradition describes him sitting in meditation ~을 통해 the night, progressing ~을 통해 increasingly deep states of consciousness.

**Mara's Attack**: ~ 동안 his meditation, Māra (the personification of delusion, death, and desire) attempted to prevent Siddhartha's enlightenment:
- Sending his daughters to tempt him with seduction
- Sending armies of demons to terrify him
- Challenging his right to sit beneath the tree
- Siddhartha touched the earth, calling it to witness his countless past lives of virtue; the earth goddess appeared, testifying on his behalf

**The Night of Enlightenment**: As Māra was defeated, Siddhartha achieved successively deeper insights ~을 통해 three "watches" of the night:

1. **First Watch**: Recalled all his previous lives in detail, understanding the cycle of rebirth (saṃsāra)
2. **Second Watch**: Developed divine eye (clairvoyance), seeing all beings dying and being reborn ~에 따르면 their karma
3. **Third Watch**: Directly realized the Four Noble Truths and comprehended dependent origination (pratītyasamutpāda)—the chain of causation binding beings to suffering

At dawn, at age 35, Siddhartha ~이 되었다 Buddha—"The Awakened One"—having achieved complete enlightenment (nirvāṇa/nibbāna). He had broken the cycle of death and rebirth and discovered the path for others to do likewise.

## Core Teachings

### Dependent Origination

The Buddha taught *pratītyasamutpāda* (dependent origination)—the principle that all phenomena arise in dependence upon conditions:

The 전통적인 twelve-link chain describes how:
1. Ignorance leads to formations
2. Formations lead to consciousness
3. Consciousness leads to name-and-form
4. Name-and-form leads to six sense bases
5. Six sense bases lead to contact
6. Contact leads to feeling
7. Feeling leads to craving
8. Craving leads to clinging
9. Clinging leads to becoming
10. Becoming leads to birth
11. Birth leads to aging and death

Understanding this chain reveals how to break the cycle of rebirth.

### Three Marks of Existence

The Buddha taught three characteristics of all conditioned phenomena:
1. **Anicca (Impermanence)**: All things are constantly changing
2. **Dukkha (Suffering/Unsatisfactoriness)**: Clinging to impermanent things causes suffering
3. **Anattā (Non-self)**: There is no permanent, unchanging self or soul

The doctrine of an

attā directly contradicted the Hindu concept of ātman (eternal soul), becoming a distinctive Buddhist teaching.

### Karma and Rebirth

The Buddha accepted the Indian concepts of karma (intentional action) and saṃsāra (cycle of rebirth) but reinterpreted them:
- **Karma**: Intentional actions create consequences shaping future experiences
- **Rebirths** occur in six realms (gods, demi-gods, humans, animals, hungry ghosts, hell beings)
- Liberation (nirvāṇa) ends the rebirth cycle
- Ethical conduct, meditation, and wisdom together lead to liberation

### Middle Way

The Buddha consistently advocated moderation between extremes:
- Between sensual indulgence and self-mortification
- Between eternalism (belief in permanent soul) and annihilationism (belief existence ends at death)
- Between absolute existence and non-existence

### Silence on Metaphysical Questions

The Buddha famously refused to answer certain "unanswerable questions" (*avyākṛta*):
- Is the universe eternal or not?
- Is the universe finite or infinite?
- Is the soul identical with the body or different?
- Does an enlightened being exist ~ 후에 death?

He compared asking such questions to a man shot with a poisoned arrow demanding to know who shot it, what wood the arrow was made from, etc., ~ 전에 allowing treatment. The urgent matter is removing the arrow (ending suffering), not metaphysical speculation.

## Final Days and Death

### Last Journey

At age 80, ~에도 불구하고 declining health, the Buddha continued traveling and teaching. ~ 동안 his final journey from Rajgir toward Kusinara, several 중요한 events occurred:

**Illness at Vesali**: The Buddha fell seriously ill but recovered ~을 통해 meditation, telling Ānanda he had postponed death ~을 통해 willpower.

**Relinquishing Life**: At the Cāpāla shrine, the Buddha three times hinted to Ānanda that he could extend his life if requested. Ānanda, influenced by Māra, failed to ask. The Buddha then formally relinquished his will to live, declaring he would achieve *parinirvāṇa* (final nirvāṇa) in three months.

**Last Meal**: The Buddha accepted a meal from Cunda, a blacksmith. The food (possibly pork or mushrooms—texts unclear) caused severe dysentery, which would 궁극적으로 kill him. The Buddha insisted Cunda not be blamed and that only he could digest that particular food.

### Parinirvāṇa

Arriving in Kusinara, the Buddha lay down between two sal trees, his head pointing north. Surrounded by disciples, he gave final instructions:

**Last Words**: "All compounded things are subject to decay. Strive with diligence!"

**Final Questions**: He asked three times if any disciple had doubts or questions. All remained silent, moved by faith in his teaching.

**Passing Away**: The Buddha entered progressively deeper meditative absorptions, finally passing into *parinirvāṇa*—death with no rebirth. The moment of his death was marked by earthquakes and supernatural signs. ~에 따르면 tradition, even the trees wept, shedding flowers.

### Cremation

The  Mallas of Kusinara cremated the Buddha's body with great ceremony. Kings and communities from throughout northeastern India sent representatives to claim relics. The remains were divided into eight portions and enshrined in stupas (memorial monuments) across the region, becoming pilgrimage sites.

## Historical Buddha vs. Legendary Buddha

### Historical Evidence

~하는 동안 no 현대의 written records of the Buddha exist, scholarly consensus affirms his historical existence based on:
- 초기의 Buddhist texts showing development of doctrine
- Archaeological evidence of 초기의 Buddhist sites
- Consistency of core teachings across traditions
- Edicts of Ashoka (3rd 세기 BC) mentioning Buddha

**Likely Historical Facts:**
- Lived in northeastern India (5th-4th 세기 BC)
- Born in Shakya kingdom
- Left home for spiritual quest
- Achieved enlightenment and taught for decades
- Founded monastic community
- Died at advanced age

### Legendary Elaborations

Over centuries, the Buddha's biography accumulated miraculous elements:
- **Jātaka Tales**: 547 previous lives of the Buddha-to-be
- **Supernatural Birth**: Described in increasingly elaborate terms
- **Superhuman Abilities**: Walking on water, flying, multiplying himself
- **Cosmic Scale**: Buddha's influence extending to heavenly realms, past and future
- **Ten Perfections**: Perfected over countless lifetimes

Different traditions emphasize different aspects:
- Theravāda maintains more austere biography
- Mahāyāna emphasizes cosmic, eternal Buddha
- Vajrayāna incorporates tantric elements
