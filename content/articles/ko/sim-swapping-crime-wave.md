## 요약

Growing cybercrime technique where criminals trick telecom providers into transferring victims phone numbers, enabling theft of cryptocurrency and bypass of two-factor authentication.

## 추가 맥락

### How SIM Swapping Works

SIM swapping (also called SIM hijacking) typically relies on social engineering rather than malware. Attackers persuade or trick a mobile carrier into porting a victim’s phone number to a SIM card controlled by the attacker, often by exploiting weak identity verification or compromised employee accounts.

### Why Phone Numbers Matter

Because many online services use SMS or phone calls for account recovery and two-factor authentication, control of a phone number can be enough to reset passwords, intercept verification codes, and take over accounts. This makes SIM swapping particularly damaging for victims who rely on SMS-based security.

### Typical Targets and Motives

High-value targets have included cryptocurrency holders, investors, executives, and people with publicly visible identities. The technique has also been used to access email accounts, financial services, and social-media profiles that can then be monetized or used for further fraud.

### Law Enforcement and Industry Response

As incidents increased, U.S. law enforcement agencies publicized arrests and prosecutions, and carriers faced pressure to strengthen customer verification and internal controls. The crime wave is often cited as evidence that telecommunications systems can be a weak link in modern digital identity.

### Security Lessons (High Level)

A common takeaway from SIM swapping cases is that SMS-based authentication can be vulnerable. More robust approaches include app-based authenticators, hardware security keys, and stronger account recovery policies that do not depend solely on phone-number control.
