## 개요

On November 17, 1973, a tank crashed ~을 통해 the gates of the Athens Polytechnic, crushing students who had occupied 대학교 for three days in defiance of Greece's military junta. The images of that gate—bent and broken—~이 되었다 symbol of resistance against dictatorship. The voices of students broadcasting from inside, calling "Bread, Education, Freedom," were silenced by automatic weapons and armored vehicles.[^1]

The Athens Polytechnic uprising was the climax of six years of simmering resistance to the military regime known as the "Regime of the Colonels." Students, workers, and ordinary Greeks gathered outside 대학교's walls to support those inside. When the junta decided to crush the revolt, they did so with overwhelming brutality—at least 24 people died (some estimates say 40+), and over 1,000 were injured.[^2]

The uprising failed in the short term. The junta remained in power for another seven months. But the regime was mortally wounded. The colonels had shown what they really were: rulers willing to kill their own youth. Within a year, a disastrous adventure in Cyprus and internal power struggles brought the dictatorship down. 민주주의 returned to Greece—and November 17 became its most sacred date, a national holiday commemorating those who died for 자유.[^3]

## Historical Background

### The Military Junta (1967-1974)

**April 21, 1967:**

- A group of colonels staged a coup
- 왕 Constantine II initially cooperated
- Parliamentary 민주주의 suspended
- Greece under military rule

**The "Colonels":**

- **Georgios Papadopoulos:** Leader ~까지 1973
- **Dimitrios Ioannidis:** Hardliner who later took over
- **Stylianos Pattakos, Nikolaos Makarezos:** Key conspirators

**Characteristics of the regime:**

- Anti-공산주의 as ideology
- Censorship and media control
- Political prisoners and 고문
- Thousands exiled
- Cultural conservatism (long hair banned, etc.)
- Economic growth but political repression[^4]

### Growing Opposition

By 1973, resistance was mounting:

**Domestic:**

- Underground resistance movements
- Student activism growing
- International pressure
- Economic problems emerging

**February-March 1973:**

- Athens Law School occupation
- Students protested forced conscription for union activists
- Brutally suppressed, but showed possibility of resistance[^5]

### The Markezinis Experiment

**October 1973:**

- Papadopoulos attempted "controlled liberalization"
- Spyros Markezinis appointed 총리
- Political parties to be gradually allowed
- Elections promised

**But the hardliners distrusted this opening—and students saw opportunity.**[^6]

## The Uprising

### November 14: The Occupation Begins

**Athens Polytechnic (National Technical University of Athens):**

- Students at Greece's most prestigious technical university
- Began occupying buildings
- Called themselves "Eleftheri Poliorkimenoi" (Free Besieged)—invoking Greece's 전쟁 of 독립

**Demands:**

- End to military dictatorship
- Free elections
- Civil liberties restored
- "Bread, Education, Freedom" (Ψωμί, Παιδεία, Ελευθερία)[^7]

### November 14-16: Building Momentum

**Inside the Polytechnic:**

- Students set up radio station (Radio Polytechneio)
- Broadcast to Athens: "Here is the Polytechnic! Greeks, the Polytechnic is the flag of our struggle and your struggle!"
- Calls for 장군 uprising
- Songs, speeches, organization

**Outside:**

- Thousands of Athenians gathered to support
- Workers joined students
- Barricades erected around 대학교
- Police attempts to disperse met with resistance

**The crowd grew to tens of thousands.**[^8]

### November 16: Escalation

**Police action:**

- Water cannons used
- Tear gas deployed
- Running battles in streets around Polytechnic
- Several injuries

**Inside:**

- Students refused to leave
- Radio continued broadcasting
- Leadership debated strategy
- Some called for more militant action

**Evening:**

- Police surrounded 대학교
- Electricity cut
- Confrontation inevitable[^9]

### November 17: The Assault

**2:00 AM:**

- Order given to clear the Polytechnic
- AMX-30 tanks deployed

**3:00 AM (approximately):**

- Tank crashed ~을 통해 main gate
- Students still inside, some clinging to gate
- Gunfire erupted
- Soldiers and police stormed the campus

**The gate image:**

- Bent, twisted metal
- Students crushed or scattered
- Symbol forever of the dictatorship's brutality

**The aftermath:**

- Mass arrests
- Students beaten
- Some shot in the streets
- Bodies removed quickly
- Hospitals forbidden to report casualties[^10]

## 죽음 Toll

**Official junta figure:** 15 killed

**Actual estimates:** 24-44 killed

| Category               | Estimate |
| ---------------------- | -------- |
| Inside Polytechnic     | ~10-15   |
| In surrounding streets | ~10-15   |
| Hospitals (unreported) | Unknown  |

**Known victims include:**

- Diomidis Komninos (high school student, shot outside gates)

**Injured:** Over 1,000-2,000

**The exact toll remains disputed because the junta destroyed records.**[^11]

## 여파

### Immediate

**November 17-25, 1973:**

- Martial law declared
- Mass arrests continued
- Universities closed
- Even stricter censorship

**Ioannidis Coup:**

- November 25, 1973: Brigadier Dimitrios Ioannidis staged internal coup
- Papadopoulos (건축가 of the suppression) was overthrown
- Hardliner faction took control
- Liberalization experiment ended[^12]

### The Fall of the Junta

**1974:**

- **July 15:** Junta attempted to overthrow Cyprus government
- **July 20:** Turkey invaded Cyprus
- Military disaster for Greece
- **July 23:** Junta collapsed
- **July 24:** Constantine Karamanlis returned from exile
- 민주주의 restored

**The Polytechnic uprising had mortally wounded the regime. The Cyprus disaster finished it.**[^13]

### 재판s

**1975:**

- Junta leaders tried for:
  - Treason
  - Insurrection
  - Polytechnic deaths
- Papadopoulos: Life 투옥
- Ioannidis: Life 투옥 (multiple sentences)
- Others: Various sentences

**Justice—partial but real—was served.**[^14]

## 역사적 평가

### Why Did Students Revolt?

**Immediate grievances:**

- Forced conscription for activists
- University autonomy violated
- Six years of dictatorship exhausting

**Deeper causes:**

- Generation growing up under dictatorship
- International examples (May '68, Prague Spring)
- Regime's partial opening created space
- Youth felt they had little to lose

### Why Did the Junta Respond So Violently?

- Fear of spreading revolt
- Internal hardliner pressure
- Miscalculation about consequences
- Dictatorship logic: force solves problems

### Long-Term Impact

**The uprising permanently delegitimized:**

- Military rule
- Political 폭력 against civilians
- Authoritarianism in Greece

**It established:**

- Students as democratic vanguard
- November 17 as sacred date
- Foundation myth for post-junta democracy[^15]

## 유산

### November 17 Holiday

**Every year:**

- National holiday in Greece (~ 이래로 1981)
- March from Polytechnic to US Embassy
- Wreath-laying at the gate
- Political speeches (often left-wing)
- School closures

**The gate remains:**

- Preserved as damaged
- Symbol of resistance
- Never fully repaired

### 정치적 영향

**In Greek politics:**

- Left claims Polytechnic legacy
- PASOK (socialist party) drew heavily on uprising
- Still referenced in 현대의 debates
- Some controversy over politicization

### The "17 November" Group

**Dark legacy:**

- Far-left terrorist organization "Revolutionary Organization 17 November"
- Operated 1975-2002
- Assassinated dozens
- Claimed Polytechnic legacy
- Dismantled in 2002 ~ 후에 murder of British military attaché

**The group distorted the uprising's meaning, though most Greeks distinguish between the students' peaceful resistance and later terrorism.**[^16]

### Global Context

The Athens Polytechnic uprising was part of a wave:

| Year | Event                                 |
| ---- | ------------------------------------- |
| 1968 | May '68 France, Prague Spring, Mexico |
| 1973 | Athens Polytechnic                    |
| 1974 | Carnation Revolution (Portugal)       |

**Mediterranean student movements in the early 1970s helped end dictatorships in Greece, Portugal, and later Spain.**[^17]
