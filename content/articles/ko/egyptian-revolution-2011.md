## 개요

The Egyptian Revolution of 2011 (ثورة 25 يناير / Thawrat 25 Yanāyir), also known as the January 25 Revolution, was an 18-day popular uprising that ended the nearly 30-year rule of 대통령 Hosni Mubarak. Part of the broader Arab Spring wave of pro-민주주의 protests across the Middle East and North Africa, the Egyptian revolution captured global attention as millions gathered in Cairo's Tahrir Square demanding "bread, freedom, and social justice."[^1]

Beginning on January 25, 2011—deliberately chosen as Egypt's "National Police Day" to protest police brutality—demonstrations quickly escalated from scattered protests to a mass movement that paralyzed 국가. ~에도 불구하고 violent crackdowns that killed over 840 people, protesters refused to leave Tahrir Square ~까지 Mubarak was gone. On February 11, 2011, Vice 대통령 Omar Suleiman announced that Mubarak had resigned and transferred power to the Supreme Council of the Armed Forces (SCAF).[^2]

혁명's aftermath proved tragically complex. ~ 후에 Egypt's first free presidential election brought Muslim Brotherhood candidate Mohamed Morsi to power in 2012, a military coup led by 장군 Abdel Fattah el-Sisi overthrew him in July 2013. The subsequent 학살 of pro-Morsi protesters at Rabaa al-Adawiya Square killed over 800 people. By 2014, Sisi had become 대통령, establishing a military-backed authoritarianism arguably more repressive than Mubarak's. 혁명's aspirations remain unfulfilled, but its memory endures as a moment when Egyptians dared to imagine a different future.[^3]

## Historical Background

### The Mubarak Era (1981-2011)

Hosni Mubarak came to power in October 1981 following the 암살 of 대통령 Anwar Sadat. For nearly three decades, he maintained authoritarian rule ~을 통해:

**Political Repression:**

- Continuous "state of emergency" (1981-2012) granting police unlimited powers
- Rigged elections maintaining National Democratic Party (NDP) dominance
- Suppression of opposition parties and civil society
- Extensive surveillance and censorship[^4]

**Economic Grievances:**

- High unemployment, especially among youth (estimated 25%+ for ages 15-29)
- Rising food prices and inflation
- Extreme inequality—~하는 동안 Mubarak's circle enriched themselves, 40% lived on under $2/day
- Crony 자본주의 benefiting regime-connected elites[^5]

**Police Brutality:**

- The security services (particularly the hated State Security Investigations) operated with impunity
- 고문 in detention was routine
- Extrajudicial killings, arbitrary arrests, and forced disappearances were common[^6]

### The Case of Khaled Said

A crucial catalyst for 혁명 was the June 2010 death of 28-year-나이든 Khaled Mohamed Said. Plainclothes police beat him to death in an Alexandria internet cafe, reportedly ~ 때문에 he had posted a video exposing police corruption. Photos of his battered corpse circulated widely, and a Facebook page "We Are All Khaled Said" (كلنا خالد سعيد), created by Google executive Wael Ghonim, attracted hundreds of thousands of followers and ~이 되었다 key organizing tool for 혁명.[^7]

### The Tunisian Spark

On December 17, 2010, Tunisian street vendor Mohamed Bouazizi set himself on fire to protest police harassment, triggering protests that forced 대통령 Zine El Abidine Ben Ali to flee on January 14, 2011. The Tunisian revolution proved that an Arab 독재자 could be overthrown by popular protest, inspiring activists across the region—including in Egypt.[^8]

## The Revolution

### January 25: Day of Rage

Egyptian activists chose January 25, 2011—National Police Day—to begin protests, deliberately challenging the security forces Egyptians most resented. Organizers used Facebook, Twitter, and other social media to coordinate demonstrations in Cairo, Alexandria, Suez, and other cities.[^9]

The initial demands focused on:

- End to police brutality
- End to the state of emergency
- Free and fair elections
- End to corruption

As crowds grew larger than expected—tens of thousands in Cairo alone—demands escalated to the fall of the regime itself.

### January 28: "Friday of Rage"

~ 후에 Friday prayers on January 28, millions joined protests across Egypt. 정부 responded with:

- Cutting internet and mobile phone services nationwide
- Deploying riot police with tear gas, rubber bullets, and live ammunition
- Curfew declarations (widely ignored)

~에도 불구하고 the crackdown, protesters overwhelmed police in Cairo and other cities. That night, the military deployed to the streets—but, crucially, soldiers did not fire on protesters. Many Egyptians embraced soldiers, chanting "The army and the people are one hand" (الجيش والشعب إيد واحدة).[^10]

### Tahrir Square: The Heart of the Revolution

Tahrir Square ("Liberation Square") in central Cairo ~이 되었다 revolution's symbolic center and physical headquarters:

- **Occupation:** Protesters established a continuous presence, with tents, field hospitals, and organized security
- **Diversity:** The crowds included Islamists and secularists, Muslims and Christians, workers and professionals, 젊은 and 나이든
- **Non-Violence:** ~에도 불구하고 provocations, protesters largely maintained non-violent discipline
- **Self-Organization:** 혁명가 committees coordinated logistics, security, media, and negotiations[^11]

### February 2: The Battle of the Camel

On February 2, pro-Mubarak thugs (many reportedly paid and later identified as police and NDP members) attacked Tahrir Square, some riding horses and camels into the crowd. The "Battle of the Camel" (موقعة الجمل) lasted hours, leaving at least 11 dead and hundreds wounded. Rather than demoralizing protesters, the attack strengthened their resolve and discredited the regime's claims of popular support.[^12]

### February 11: Mubarak Falls

As protests continued and the military signaled it would not 학살 protesters, the regime finally collapsed:

- February 10: Mubarak gave a televised address refusing to resign, delegating some powers to Vice 대통령 Omar Suleiman—the crowd responded with fury
- February 11, 6:00 PM: Suleiman announced that Mubarak had resigned and transferred power to the Supreme Council of the Armed Forces (SCAF)

Tahrir Square erupted in celebration. Egyptians across 국가 took to the streets in joy. The pharaoh had fallen.[^13]

## 여파 and Counter-Revolution

### SCAF Rule (February 2011 - June 2012)

The Supreme Council of the Armed Forces, led by Field 원수 Hussein Tantawi, ruled Egypt ~ 동안 transition:

- Suspended the 헌법
- Dissolved 의회
- Promised elections and civilian rule
- Faced continued protests over slow reforms and military trials of civilians[^16]

### Morsi Presidency (June 2012 - July 2013)

In June 2012, Muslim Brotherhood candidate Mohamed Morsi won Egypt's first free presidential election, narrowly defeating Ahmed Shafik (Mubarak's last 총리). Morsi's presidency was marked by:

- Constitutional crisis over Islamist-drafted 헌법
- Economic difficulties and continuing unrest
- Accusations of Brotherhood power grab
- Mass protests in June 2013 demanding his removal[^17]

### The 2013 Coup and Sisi's Rise

On July 3, 2013, Defense Minister 장군 Abdel Fattah el-Sisi announced that the military had removed Morsi from office:

- 헌법 suspended
- Morsi and Brotherhood leaders arrested
- Pro-Morsi protesters established sit-ins at Rabaa al-Adawiya and al-Nahda squares[^18]

### Rabaa Massacre (August 14, 2013)

On August 14, 2013, Egyptian security forces violently dispersed the Rabaa sit-in in what 인권 Watch called "the most serious incident of mass unlawful killings in modern Egyptian history":

- At least 817 killed (possibly over 1,000)
- Mostly unarmed protesters
- Security forces used live ammunition, bulldozers, and snipers
- The four-finger "Rabaa sign" ~이 되었다 symbol of the Brotherhood and anti-Sisi opposition[^19]

### Sisi's Egypt (2014-Present)

Sisi was elected 대통령 in 2014 and has maintained authoritarian control:

- Muslim Brotherhood declared a terrorist organization
- Tens of thousands of political prisoners (including many 2011 activists)
- Severe press censorship
- Civil society crushed
- Many 2011 revolutionaries jailed, exiled, or silenced[^20]

## 역사적 평가

### 긍정적 평가 (Revolutionary Perspective)

**Breaking the Fear Barrier:** For 18 days, Egyptians proved they could overcome the fear that had sustained decades of dictatorship. The experience of collective action, solidarity across religious and class lines, and the fall of Mubarak demonstrated that change was possible.[^21]

**Democratic Moment:** Egypt held genuinely competitive elections for the first time in its 현대의 history. Whatever followed, 혁명 proved that Egyptians wanted and could achieve democratic participation.[^22]

**Inspirational Legacy:** The Egyptian revolution inspired pro-민주주의 movements worldwide. The images from Tahrir Square—the massive crowds, the creativity, the courage—became globally iconic symbols of popular power.[^23]

### Critical Views (Counter-Revolutionary/Realist Perspective)

**Naive About Power:** Critics argued that revolutionaries were naive about the military's interests, failed to build institutions, and allowed Islamists and the military to outmaneuver them.[^24]

**Stability Argument:** Some argued (particularly the Sisi regime and its supporters) that 혁명 unleashed chaos that only military intervention could end, and that order was more 중요한 than democratic experimentation.[^25]

**Unfinished Business:** From a 혁명가 perspective, the tragedy is that the goals of 2011—bread, 자유, dignity—remain unachieved under a more repressive government than ~ 전에.[^26]
