The Prioress's Tale, recounting a blood libel legend in which Jews murder a Christian child, traffics in 중세의 anti-Semitism that 현대의 readers find repugnant. ~하는 동안 some scholars argue Chaucer ironically undercuts the Prioress's bigotry ~을 통해 subtle critique, others find this reading unconvincing. Steven Kruger's "Medieval Christian (Dis)identifications: Muslims and Jews in Guibert of Nogent" examines how Chaucer's works construct Christian identity against racialized religious others. The Man of Law's Tale and other works deploy Orientalist stereotypes that, 그러나 conventional in 중세의 문학, nonetheless perpetuate harmful representations.[^49]

Feminist critics, even those who find progressive elements in Chaucer, acknowledge deeply problematic content. The Miller's Tale, Merchant's Tale, Shipman's Tale, and others feature plots turning on female deception and sexual manipulation. The Clerk's Tale celebrates Griselda's submissive endurance of her husband's psychological 고문, including fake murder of her children, as wifely virtue. Louise O. Fradenburg argues that "however much Chaucer critiques patriarchy, his works remain products of a misogynist culture and frequently reproduce that misogyny, sometimes with disturbing enthusiasm." The question of whether Chaucer's irony sufficiently distances him from his characters' sexism remains contested.[^50]

Class-based critiques note that ~에도 불구하고 Chaucer's 유명한 range across social ranks, his perspective remains fundamentally bourgeois and conservative. The peasant uprising of 1381, which temporarily seized control of London and executed royal officials, receives no direct mention in Chaucer's works—a conspicuous silence given that he lived in London and served in government positions. His idealized Plowman, presented as a model of patient labor, contrasts sharply with the actual rebellious peasantry. David Aers argues in *Chaucer, Langland, and the Creative Imagination* that "Chaucer's social vision, for all its breadth, ultimately defends existing hierarchies and mocks those who challenge their subordination," making him an essentially conservative 작가 ~에도 불구하고 surface rebelliousness.[^51]

Some critics question whether Chaucer's reputation as "Father of English Literature" reflects literary merit or nationalist myth-making. Seth Lerer's *Chaucer and His Readers* (1993) demonstrates how Chaucer's canonical status resulted partly from historical accidents—초기의 printing, Tudor appropriation, institutional curricula—rather than purely aesthetic value. Lerer argues that "the Chaucer we revere is partly a construction of successive generations who needed a founding father for English literature," raising questions about whether 중세의 readers would recognize our exalted Chaucer.[^52]

Textual scholars note the profound instability of Chaucer's texts as a problem glossed over in celebrations of his genius. No holograph manuscripts survive; the earliest copies postdate his death; tale order varies wildly across manuscripts; connections between fragments remain unclear; many tales lack conclusions. Ralph Hanna argues that "we cannot know with certainty what Chaucer actually wrote, yet we confidently assert his greatness based on texts that may significantly differ from his original compositions." This textual uncertainty, Hanna contends, should introduce more humility into Chaucerian criticism.[^53]

Recent critiques challenge the assumption that Chaucer's works transcend their historical moment to speak to universal human experience. New historicist scholars argue that this claim of universality actually erases historical specificity and projects 현대의 values onto 중세의 texts. Lee Patterson contends that "the Chaucer who speaks to all ages and peoples is largely a fiction created by critics who find in him their own preoccupations while ignoring what made him distinctively medieval." This critique suggests that celebrating Chaucer's modernity may actually misrepresent his works and appropriate them for agendas alien to their original contexts.[^54]
