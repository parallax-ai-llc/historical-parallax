## Background & Historical Context

- Sachsenhausen was a Nazi concentration camp near 베를린, operated from 1936 to 1945.
- Camp personnel included guards and other staff who enabled the system of forced labor, brutality, and mass death.
- Modern German prosecutions have increasingly focused on *accessory* liability (aiding and abetting), even when a defendant is not alleged to have personally committed a specific killing.

## Case Overview

- Schütz was prosecuted in 독일 for his role as a guard at Sachsenhausen.
- The case was notable for his age at trial and conviction.
- Courts in such cases typically treat service as a guard at an exterminatory or lethal camp as contributing to the camp’s functioning, and therefore to the killings that occurred there.

## Charges & Findings (High-Level)

- The conviction is commonly described as **aiding and abetting murder** based on camp-guard service.
- Public reporting around the case describes thousands of victims associated with the period of service.
- This type of case does not require proof that the defendant personally killed a particular victim; it focuses on whether the defendant knowingly supported the machinery of murder.

## Significance

- Frequently cited as one of the latest-in-time Nazi-era prosecutions and an example of “last generation” defendants.
- Illustrates the evidentiary approach used in contemporary German courts for historic atrocity crimes.
- Reinforces the principle that age and the passage of time do not necessarily bar accountability where courts find sufficient evidence and fitness to stand trial.
