## Summary

Leontyne Price (February 10, 1927 – October 10, 2024) was an American soprano widely regarded as one of the greatest opera singers of the twentieth century. Born in Laurel, Mississippi, she became the first African American to achieve international superstar status in opera, breaking through racial barriers that had long restricted Black artists from major operatic stages. Her rich, powerful voice — described by critics as a "natural wonder" — became the standard against which dramatic sopranos were measured for decades.

Price's career coincided with the Civil Rights Movement, and her triumphs carried cultural weight far beyond music. Her 1961 debut at the Metropolitan Opera in New York received a 42-minute standing ovation, one of the longest in the institution's history. She remained the reigning soprano of the Met throughout the 1960s and into the 1970s, particularly celebrated for her interpretations of Verdi heroines.

## Early Life & Background

Mary Violet Leontyne Price was born on February 10, 1927, in Laurel, Mississippi, to James Price, a sawmill worker, and Katherine Baker Price, a midwife. She grew up in the segregated American South, where opportunities for Black artists were severely limited. Her family recognized her musical gift early, and despite modest means, her parents arranged piano lessons for her.

She attended Central State College in Wilberforce, Ohio, graduating in 1948, before earning a full scholarship to the Juilliard School of Music in New York City. At Juilliard, she studied with the celebrated teacher Florence Page Kimball, who helped develop her into a world-class vocalist. Composer Virgil Thomson and conductor Samuel Barber were among those who recognized her talent during these formative years.

## Rise to Prominence

Price's breakthrough came when composer Virgil Thomson cast her in the 1952 revival of his opera *Four Saints in Three Acts*, an all-Black production that toured the United States and played in Paris. This led to her casting in the 1952 Broadway production of *Porgy and Bess*, opposite baritone William Warfield — whom she later married — in a production that toured Europe and won widespread acclaim.

Television provided an unlikely platform for advancement in the racially tense 1950s. In 1955, NBC cast her as Tosca in a live television broadcast, making her the first Black singer to appear in opera on American television. Her 1961 Metropolitan Opera debut in Verdi's *Il Trovatore* cemented her status as a global superstar, and she went on to open six Met seasons — a record unmatched by any other soprano.

## Key Achievements

Price won 19 Grammy Awards over her career, more than any other classical singer at the time of her retirement. She was the recipient of the Presidential Medal of Freedom (1964), the National Medal of Arts (1985), and the Kennedy Center Honors (1980). Samuel Barber composed his *Antony and Cleopatra* specifically for her voice, and she premiered the work at the opening of the new Metropolitan Opera House at Lincoln Center in 1966. Her recordings of Verdi's *Aida* and *Il Trovatore* remain benchmark interpretations.

## Controversies & Criticism

Price's career was not without friction. Her 1966 debut in the Barber *Antony and Cleopatra* received mixed reviews, with some critics finding the production overwhelming and the new opera uneven, though Price's singing was generally praised. Navigating the racial politics of mid-century America, she occasionally faced criticism from within the Civil Rights community for operating within predominantly white cultural institutions, though she consistently maintained that excellence on the world stage was itself a form of activism.

## Legacy & Influence

Price's legacy is threefold: artistic, cultural, and personal. Artistically, she redefined the dramatic soprano, particularly in Verdi, setting interpretive standards that endure in recordings and teaching. Culturally, her success proved transformative, opening doors for subsequent generations of Black opera singers including Jessye Norman, Kathleen Battle, and Denyce Graves. Personally, she remained a role model and mentor, supporting young singers through institutions including the Metropolitan Opera. She passed away on October 10, 2024, at age 97 in New York City.
