## Summary

Billy Joel is one of the best-selling music artists of all time, with over 150 million records sold worldwide. Known as the "Piano Man," he built a career spanning five decades on melodic rock, pop, and classical-influenced songwriting that documented American working-class life with wit and emotional honesty. His ability to blend rock energy with classical training and sharp narrative lyricism set him apart from his contemporaries.

His catalog spans genres—from the saloon-stool balladry of "Piano Man" to the hard rock of "It's Still Rock and Roll to Me" to the orchestral sweep of "Goodnight Saigon." Joel was the rare artist who achieved both critical legitimacy and massive commercial success without significantly compromising his sound. He is the third-best-selling solo artist in U.S. history, behind Elvis Presley and Garth Brooks.

## Early Life & Background

William Martin Joel was born on May 9, 1949, in the Bronx, New York, and raised in Levittown, Long Island. His father Howard Joel was a classically trained pianist who had emigrated from Germany, and Billy began piano lessons at age four. The family was working-class, and Joel grew up in a culturally mixed suburb that would later color his lyrical themes of blue-collar identity and suburban restlessness.

Joel dropped out of Hicksville High School in 1967, just short of graduation credits, to join the local band the Hassles. He later claimed he was too busy performing to attend his graduation. He briefly trained as a professional boxer in his teens, a period he cited as teaching him discipline and toughness. He earned his high school diploma equivalency from Columbia University in 1992.

## Rise to Prominence

Joel's solo debut *Cold Spring Harbor* (1971) was marred by a mastering error that made his voice sound unnaturally fast. He relocated to Los Angeles in 1972, performing under the pseudonym Bill Martin at the Executive Room piano bar—an experience that directly inspired his breakthrough single "Piano Man" (1973). The song reached No. 25 on the Billboard Hot 100 and established his signature voice as a chronicler of ordinary lives.

His commercial ascent accelerated with *The Stranger* (1977), produced by Phil Ramone. It became Columbia Records' best-selling album at the time, containing hits including "Just the Way You Are," "Movin' Out (Anthony's Song)," "She's Always a Woman," and "Scenes from an Italian Restaurant." "Just the Way You Are" won Grammy Awards for Record of the Year and Song of the Year in 1979.

## Key Achievements

- *The Stranger* (1977) has sold over 10 million copies in the United States alone.
- *Glass Houses* (1980) reached No. 1 on the Billboard 200 and produced the chart-topping single "It's Still Rock and Roll to Me."
- Joel was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1999.
- His 1994 greatest hits compilation *Greatest Hits Vol. I & II* is one of the best-selling albums in U.S. history, certified 23× platinum.
- He became the first pop artist to perform at Yankee Stadium in 2008, and held a record-breaking residency at Madison Square Garden beginning in 2014 that ran for over 150 consecutive monthly shows.
- He received the Kennedy Center Honor in 2013 and the Library of Congress Gershwin Prize for Popular Song in 2014.

## Controversies & Criticism

Joel has been candid about struggles with alcohol, entering rehabilitation multiple times between the 1990s and 2000s. In 2002 and 2004 he was involved in separate car accidents attributed to alcohol use, drawing significant media criticism. He publicly acknowledged his dependency in multiple interviews.

Some critics have long characterized his work as commercial and sentimental, placing it outside serious rock credibility. Rolling Stone's dismissive early coverage of albums like *An Innocent Man* (1983) reflected a critical ambivalence that contrasted with his enormous popular support. His personal life—three divorces, including a highly publicized split from supermodel Christie Brinkley in 1994—was a recurring tabloid subject through the 1980s and 1990s.

## Legacy & Influence

Joel stopped releasing pop albums after *River of Dreams* (1993), focusing instead on classical composition and touring. Despite this, his catalog continued to generate revenue and cultural relevance. His Broadway musical *Movin' Out* (2002), choreographed by Twyla Tharp, ran for 1,303 performances and won two Tony Awards.

His influence is evident across multiple generations of piano-driven rock artists, from Elton John contemporaries to later acts like Ben Folds and John Legend, who have cited him as a primary influence. His songwriting's sociological focus—capturing Long Island diner culture, Vietnam veterans, immigrant ambition—gave pop music a documentary texture rarely seen at his commercial scale.
