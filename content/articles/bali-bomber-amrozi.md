## Summary

Indonesian terrorist known as the 'smiling assassin' for his gleeful demeanor. He was a member of Jemaah Islamiyah who helped plan the 2002 Bali bombings that killed 202 people. Executed by firing squad.

## Background

- Amrozi bin Nurhasyim was an Indonesian militant associated with networks that Indonesian authorities linked to Jemaah Islamiyah (JI).
- JI was a Southeast Asian Islamist militant organization implicated in multiple attacks in the early 2000s.

## The 2002 Bali bombings (context)

- The bombings targeted nightlife venues in Kuta, Bali, on 12 October 2002, resulting in mass casualties including many foreign tourists.
- The attack became one of Indonesia’s deadliest terrorist incidents and prompted major counterterrorism reforms and international cooperation.

## Investigation and trial

- Indonesian police investigations led to arrests of multiple suspects connected to the Bali bombings.
- Amrozi was tried in Indonesian courts, convicted, and sentenced to death; his case was closely followed domestically and internationally.
- The proceedings were part of a wider set of prosecutions that included other prominent defendants from the same attack.

## Execution and public reaction

- He was executed by firing squad in 2008.
- The case remained controversial in parts of public discourse, reflecting broader debates in Indonesia about terrorism, radicalization, and the death penalty.

## Legacy

- The Bali bombings are frequently cited as a turning point in Indonesia’s modern security policy.
- Subsequent years saw expanded counterterrorism capabilities, including specialist units and changes in investigative and intelligence coordination.
- The incident also influenced regional approaches to extremist financing, cross-border networks, and travel of militants.
