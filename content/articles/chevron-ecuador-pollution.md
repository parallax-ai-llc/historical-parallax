## Summary

Texaco (now Chevron) dumped 16 billion gallons of toxic waste into the Ecuadorian Amazon between 1964 and 1992, poisoning indigenous communities. An Ecuadorian court ordered Chevron to pay $9.5 billion but the company has refused to pay.

## Background

The dispute commonly referred to as the “Chevron Ecuador” case traces back to oil extraction in Ecuador’s Amazon region, where Texaco (later acquired by Chevron) operated as part of a consortium. Allegations centered on long-term contamination from produced water, waste pits, and spills, and on resulting harms to local ecosystems and nearby communities.

## Litigation and Enforcement Efforts

A major Ecuadorian court judgment against Chevron became one of the most prominent environmental liability cases involving a multinational oil company. Because Chevron did not have significant assets in Ecuador at the time enforcement was sought, plaintiffs pursued recognition and enforcement actions in other countries.

## Counterclaims, Fraud Allegations, and Arbitration

Chevron disputed liability and argued that earlier remediation and releases resolved its obligations. The company also pursued legal action in the United States alleging that the Ecuadorian judgment was procured through misconduct, including fraud and improper influence, and it sought relief that would block enforcement in U.S. courts. Parallel to domestic litigation, aspects of the dispute also intersected with international arbitration concerning state responsibility and treaty obligations.

## Why the Case Matters

The Chevron Ecuador controversy is often cited in debates about corporate accountability, transnational litigation strategy, environmental justice for Indigenous and rural communities, and the practical challenges of enforcing judgments across jurisdictions.

## Related Topics

Environmental remediation standards; class actions and mass torts; recognition and enforcement of foreign judgments; investor–state dispute settlement.

## Ongoing Status

Different court and arbitration proceedings have produced conflicting outcomes over time, and the dispute has continued to be discussed as an example of how complex environmental harm claims become when they span decades and multiple legal systems.
