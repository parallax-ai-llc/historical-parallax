## Summary

Diana Frances Spencer, known globally as Princess Diana, was the first wife of Charles, Prince of Wales, and mother to Princes William and Harry. Born in 1961, she became one of the most photographed and recognizable figures of the 20th century, transforming the public role of British royalty through her warmth, accessibility, and willingness to engage with causes considered taboo by the establishment.

Her charitable work — spanning HIV/AIDS awareness, landmine abolition, and homelessness — redefined what a royal could accomplish on the world stage. She challenged institutional distance by physically embracing AIDS patients at a time when the disease was deeply stigmatized, reshaping public understanding overnight. Her death in a Paris car crash on 31 August 1997 triggered an unprecedented global outpouring of grief and remains one of the defining moments of the late 20th century.

Diana's influence persists across fashion, philanthropy, mental health advocacy, and royal culture itself. Her sons have cited her example as foundational to their own public service work, and her legacy helped push the monarchy toward greater transparency and emotional openness.

## Early Life & Background

Diana was born on 1 July 1961 at Park House, Sandringham, Norfolk, the third daughter of Viscount Althorp (later the 8th Earl Spencer) and Frances Roche. Her parents divorced in 1969 — a custody battle that was contentious and formative. She attended Riddlesworth Hall preparatory school and later West Heath Girls' School in Kent, where she showed aptitude for music and dance but did not excel academically. She twice failed her O-level examinations. After finishing school she moved to London, working as a nursery assistant and kindergarten teacher at the Young England School in Pimlico.

## Rise to Prominence

Diana's relationship with Prince Charles became public in 1980. They were engaged on 6 February 1981 and married on 29 July 1981 at St Paul's Cathedral in a ceremony watched by an estimated 750 million television viewers worldwide. She became Princess of Wales instantly and was thrust into relentless media scrutiny. Despite suffering from postnatal depression and bulimia — conditions she later spoke about publicly — she cultivated a distinctive public persona marked by directness and compassion. Her 1987 handshake with an HIV-positive patient at Middlesex Hospital, at a time when many feared casual contact, was a pivotal moment that shifted public perception of the disease.

## Key Achievements

- **Landmine campaign**: Diana's 1997 visits to Angola and Bosnia, where she walked through partially cleared minefields, brought global attention to the humanitarian toll of landmines. The Ottawa Treaty banning anti-personnel mines was signed in December 1997, months after her death; advocates credited her campaign with accelerating political will.
- **HIV/AIDS advocacy**: From 1987 onward she regularly visited AIDS wards and hospices, helping destigmatize the disease at its peak social crisis.
- **Mental health openness**: In a 1995 BBC Panorama interview watched by 23 million viewers in the UK alone, she spoke candidly about her bulimia, self-harm, and depression — rare disclosures for a public figure of her stature.
- **Fashion influence**: She is consistently ranked among the most influential style icons of the 20th century; her fashion choices regularly set global trends and her dresses command record auction prices.

## Controversies & Criticism

Diana's 1995 Panorama interview, conducted without prior palace knowledge, strained relations with the royal family and was cited by Queen Elizabeth II as a factor in urging an early divorce. Critics within the establishment accused her of using the media strategically for personal advantage. Her relationship with Dodi Fayed following her divorce from Charles in 1996 drew tabloid controversy. Some royal commentators argued her approach undermined institutional decorum, though this view has become a minority position over time.

## Legacy & Influence

Diana's death at age 36 prompted a national reassessment of British stoicism and monarchy. The public demand that the royal family mourn visibly contributed to lasting changes in how the institution communicates emotionally. The Diana, Princess of Wales Memorial Fund distributed over £100 million to charitable causes before closing in 2012. Prince William and Prince Harry founded the Heads Together mental health campaign in 2016, directly citing their mother's example. The Diana Award, established in her memory in 1999, annually recognizes young humanitarians globally.
