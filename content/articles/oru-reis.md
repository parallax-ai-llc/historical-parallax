## Additional Context

Oruç Reis (Oruç Reis / Baba Oruç) was an Ottoman-era corsair active in the western Mediterranean during the early 16th century. He is closely associated with the rise of Ottoman influence in North Africa and is commonly referenced alongside his younger brother, Hayreddin Barbarossa.

### Mediterranean corsairing

In this period, “corsair” activity often blended private warfare, piracy, and state-sponsored raiding. Mediterranean powers—including Ottoman-aligned and Christian polities—used naval raiders to:

- disrupt enemy shipping and coastal supply lines,
- capture prizes and ransom captives,
- project influence without full-scale fleet deployments.

### North Africa and Ottoman expansion

Oruç Reis operated around the Maghreb at a time when several coastal cities and inland rulers navigated competing pressures from Spain, local dynasties, and Ottoman authority. Control of key ports could determine access to trade, fortifications, and naval bases.

### Legacy

Modern references to Oruç Reis appear in Ottoman naval history and popular narratives about the Barbarossa brothers, reflecting the larger geopolitical struggle for dominance in the Mediterranean.

### Historical note

Accounts of corsair careers often rely on a mix of Ottoman, European, and later historiography; details can differ across narratives.
