## Summary

China detained an estimated 1-3 million Uyghurs and other Turkic Muslims in internment camps in Xinjiang. The UN found evidence of crimes against humanity including torture and forced sterilization.

## Background

The camps are associated with a broader security and social-control campaign in China’s Xinjiang region that expanded in the late 2010s.
Chinese authorities have described elements of the program using terms such as "vocational education and training" and have linked policy to counterterrorism and "de-extremification" goals.

## Evidence and reporting

Information about the system has come from satellite imagery, leaked documents, survivor testimonies, investigative journalism, and academic research.
Reports have discussed detention without due process and a range of coercive practices, alongside broader surveillance and restrictions on religious and cultural expression.

## International response

The issue has prompted diplomatic disputes, UN-related statements, and policy responses including sanctions and import restrictions by some governments.
Other states have defended China’s policies or avoided condemnation, reflecting geopolitical alignments and economic ties.

## Human-rights framing

Observers have used different legal characterizations, including allegations of crimes against humanity and genocide, while Chinese officials have rejected those claims.
The debate has become a major reference point in contemporary discussions of human rights enforcement and great-power politics.

## Continuing significance

Uyghur detention and related policies remain central to the Uyghur diaspora’s activism and to wider scrutiny of supply chains, corporate due diligence, and transnational repression.

## Commonly discussed topics

- The role of mass surveillance technologies and policing practices
- Family separation and impacts on language, religion, and cultural transmission
- The use of international law and multilateral forums to address alleged abuses
