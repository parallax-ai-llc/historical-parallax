## Summary

Proposed Department of Defense plan to stage terrorist attacks on US soil and blame Cuba to justify an invasion. President Kennedy rejected the proposal and removed the Joint Chiefs chairman.

## Background and Context

- **Sponsoring environment:** Operation Northwoods was developed during heightened Cold War tensions, following the Cuban Revolution and amid U.S. policy debates on how to respond to Fidel Castro’s government.
- **Institutional origin:** The proposals were associated with the U.S. Department of Defense and the Joint Chiefs of Staff, and were part of broader planning around potential military action against Cuba.

## What the Proposal Contained (High-Level)

Publicly discussed summaries of the Northwoods concept describe a menu of *hypothetical* incidents intended to create political justification for escalation, including:

- **Staged or fabricated incidents** attributed to Cuba (i.e., designed to appear as Cuban aggression).
- **Provocations involving U.S. assets** (for example, creating the impression that U.S. forces or civilians had been attacked).
- **Information operations** intended to shape public and international perception in favor of intervention.

## Outcome

- **Not implemented:** The plan is widely described as having been rejected at the presidential level and never carried out.
- **Policy significance:** It is frequently cited as an example of aggressive contingency planning that did not translate into executed policy.

## Declassification and Public Knowledge

- **Declassified documentation:** Details about Northwoods became widely known after U.S. government records related to the Kennedy era were declassified and released.
- **Historical framing:** In scholarship and public discussion, it is often treated as evidence of how far some planners were willing to go in considering covert or deceptive options during the period.

## Legacy and Common References

- **In public discourse:** Operation Northwoods is regularly invoked in discussions about state deception, false-flag allegations, and skepticism toward official narratives.
- **Caution on overreach:** Many popular retellings expand beyond the documented proposals; careful summaries distinguish between *what was proposed in planning documents* and *what was actually done*.
