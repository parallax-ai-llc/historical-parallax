## Summary

Namyang Dairy was caught on tape forcing small-store owners to buy excessive product quantities. The call exposed widespread bullying by large Korean companies against small retailers.

## What the Recording Alleged

- The widely-circulated phone call was described as a supplier-side representative pressuring a small retailer.
- Allegations centered on **coercive ordering** (pushing more inventory than a shop could reasonably sell) and **verbal intimidation**.
- In South Korea, this kind of abuse of leverage is often discussed under the umbrella term **갑질** (gapjil).

## Why Small Retailers Are Vulnerable (High-Level)

- **Inventory risk**: unsold perishables or short-shelf-life items quickly become direct losses for the shop.
- **Asymmetric bargaining power**: a large brand or distributor can threaten reduced supply, worse terms, or loss of promotional support.
- **Information advantage**: suppliers typically know promotion schedules and pricing levers better than individual stores.

## Public Reaction and Reputational Impact

- The incident became a widely referenced example in debates about large-firm misconduct toward small businesses.
- Consumer anger in similar “gapjil” cases often manifests as boycotts, negative press cycles, and calls for executive accountability.
- Even when legal outcomes vary by case, reputational damage can persist because the story is easy to understand and share.

## Aftermath (Common Corporate Responses)

The following are typical post-scandal moves seen in comparable Korean corporate controversy cycles:

- Public apology statements and internal disciplinary actions.
- Compliance and ethics program updates (hotlines, vendor conduct policies, training).
- Commitments to “fair trade” practices with downstream retailers and franchisees.

## Patterns to Watch For in Supplier Pressure Cases

- **Forced purchase quotas** framed as “promotion participation.”
- **Retaliation threats** (explicit or implied) if a retailer refuses.
- **Channel stuffing** behavior (pushing product into the channel to meet targets), which shifts risk downstream.
