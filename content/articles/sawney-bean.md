## Summary

Legendary Scottish cannibal who allegedly led a 48-member clan that lived in a cave and murdered and ate over 1,000 people over 25 years. While his existence is debated, the story has endured for centuries.

## Status as Legend

- Sawney Bean is generally treated as a **folkloric figure** rather than a conclusively documented historical person.
- The story is widely retold, but contemporary primary-source evidence for a real "Bean clan" is disputed.

## Core Elements of the Tale

- The legend centers on a family/clan said to have lived in seclusion (often described as a coastal cave in Scotland).
- The clan allegedly ambushed travelers, murdered them, and practiced cannibalism over many years.
- A common version ends with royal intervention and mass execution.

## Cultural Impact

- The story has been used as a cautionary and sensational narrative in British and Scottish popular culture.
- It has influenced depictions of "cannibal clans" in later fiction and horror media, often as a template for remote, predatory communities.

## Why It Persists

- The tale blends anxieties about lawlessness, wilderness, and social breakdown with graphic imagery that makes it memorable.
- As with many enduring legends, retellings vary significantly by period and publisher.

## Notes

- Modern historians often treat the Sawney Bean narrative as part of a broader genre of early-modern sensational crime literature.
- The lack of contemporary corroboration is one reason the story is frequently classified as legend rather than verified history.
