## Summary

Nathan Leopold and Richard Loeb were wealthy Chicago teenagers who murdered 14-year-old Bobby Franks in 1924 as a thrill kill to commit the 'perfect crime.' Defended by Clarence Darrow, they received life sentences instead of death.

## Background

The Leopold and Loeb case became a landmark in American criminal justice and media history. The defendants were young men from affluent Chicago families, and the crime attracted intense national attention.

## Trial and legal significance

- **Defense strategy:** Clarence Darrow’s arguments at sentencing are frequently cited in discussions of opposition to capital punishment and the use of psychological and social explanations in mitigation.
- **Outcome:** The case is remembered in part because the defendants avoided the death penalty despite widespread public outrage.
- **Public discourse:** Contemporary coverage reflected early 20th-century debates about juvenile offenders, morality, and the influence of education and privilege.

## Cultural impact

The case inspired numerous later works in literature, theater, and film that reinterpreted the crime, the courtroom drama, and the relationship between the two defendants.
