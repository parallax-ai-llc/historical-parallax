## Summary

English sea captain who initiated the British slave trade, making three voyages that kidnapped Africans and sold them in the Caribbean. Knighted by Queen Elizabeth I.

## Additional Context

## Role in the Early English Atlantic World

Hawkins is frequently described as a key figure in the early expansion of English privateering and overseas commerce. He organized voyages that combined trading, coercion, and violence, and he became a prominent example of how state power and private profit intertwined in the early modern Atlantic.

## Slave Trading Voyages

He is associated with three major voyages (1560s) in which Africans were seized or purchased under coercive conditions and then sold in Spanish America and the Caribbean. These voyages are widely treated in scholarship and public history as foundational to England’s participation in the transatlantic slave trade.

## Naval Administration and War

Beyond privateering, Hawkins held senior roles in naval administration under Elizabeth I, including responsibilities for provisioning and reforming the Royal Navy. He later served in major naval campaigns, and is commonly listed among the commanders involved in the 1588 defense against the Spanish Armada.
