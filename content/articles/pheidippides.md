## Summary

Pheidippides was the legendary ancient Greek messenger who ran from Marathon to Athens to announce victory over the Persians in 490 BC. The modern marathon race is named after this legendary run.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 490 BC | Ran from Marathon to Athens to announce victory |
| 490 BC | Collapsed and died after delivering the message |

## Historicity and Sources

- **Legend vs. evidence:** Pheidippides is best known through later literary sources, and details of the famous “Marathon to Athens” run vary depending on which ancient author is referenced.
- **Alternate tradition (Athens–Sparta):** A commonly cited account describes a long-distance run from Athens to Sparta (and back) to request help before the Battle of Marathon, highlighting endurance-messenger roles in Greek warfare.

## Cultural Legacy

- **Modern marathon:** The modern marathon race is inspired by the Marathon story, though the standardized marathon distance (42.195 km) was set much later for modern sporting reasons.
- **Symbol of endurance:** The narrative has been used as a cultural shorthand for extreme effort, last-mile delivery, and perseverance.

## Notes

- Because the story is semi-legendary, it is best treated as a cultural and historical tradition rather than a fully verified biographical record.

## Key Takeaways

- **Function in narratives:** A messenger-runner who links battlefield outcomes to civic morale.
- **Modern adoption:** Sporting events and endurance culture reused the legend as an origin story.
- **Historical caution:** Ancient texts are not modern reportage; the same “fact” can appear in multiple, conflicting versions.
