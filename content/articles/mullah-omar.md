## Summary

Afghan cleric who founded the Taliban and served as its supreme leader. He sheltered Osama bin Laden and refused to hand him over after 9/11, leading to the US invasion of Afghanistan. His death was concealed for two years.

## Early Life and Rise

- **Background**: Omar was an Afghan cleric and former mujahideen fighter who emerged from the post-Soviet civil-war milieu.
- **Taliban formation**: The Taliban initially presented itself as a religiously motivated movement promising order and anti-corruption discipline amid factional violence.

## Taliban Rule (1996–2001)

- **Supreme leadership**: Omar became the movement’s symbolic and strategic leader; the title “Commander of the Faithful” was used to bolster religious legitimacy.
- **Governance style**: The Taliban government was marked by strict social rules and international isolation, with limited diplomatic recognition.
- **Hosting al-Qaeda**: The regime’s relationship with Osama bin Laden and al-Qaeda became central to international pressure and later war.

## Post-9/11 and Insurgency Phase

- **Refusal and invasion**: After 9/11, the Taliban’s refusal to hand over bin Laden contributed to the U.S.-led invasion and the fall of the Taliban government.
- **Underground leadership**: Omar remained a figurehead for the insurgency while staying largely out of public view, with communications attributed to him circulating intermittently.

## Death and Secrecy

- **Concealment**: His death was not announced for a significant period, which later raised questions about internal Taliban decision-making and messaging.
- **Succession**: The delayed disclosure complicated leadership transitions and factional cohesion.

## Why He Is Notable

Mullah Omar is a pivotal figure in modern Afghan history because his leadership connects:

- the Taliban’s rise to state power,
- the geopolitical consequences of hosting al-Qaeda,
- and the long insurgency that shaped Afghanistan for decades.
