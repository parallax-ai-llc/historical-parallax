## Context and Background (high level)

Public sources describe Tore Hedin as being connected to a highly publicized killing spree in Scania (Skåne County), Sweden, in the early 1950s, sometimes referred to in Swedish as the "Hurva murders".[^1] In those accounts, he was a police officer and is described as having died the same night as the killings (by suicide), which is why some summaries categorize the event as a mass murder/spree killing rather than a long-running series of separate murders.[^1]

## Why the case is notable

High-level points commonly emphasized in overviews include:[^1]

- **Scale and shock:** the event is described as one of the deadliest single-incident mass killings in Swedish criminal history up to much later decades.
- **Rural setting:** the victims and scenes are described as being in small communities in Scania, which amplified the sense of vulnerability in the area.
- **Perpetrator role:** the alleged perpetrator being connected to law enforcement is highlighted as a complicating factor for public trust and for how the case was discussed.

## Reported method and sequence (non-graphic)

Summaries typically mention a late-night spree across multiple locations, with attacks and associated arson used to cover tracks.[^1] This article intentionally avoids operational detail and focuses on the broad contours that are most relevant for historical overview.

## Legacy and interpretation

In later retellings, the case is often used as a reference point in Swedish discussions about:

- how rare mass-casualty events were historically in Sweden,
- the limits of small-community policing and investigation resources,
- and how sensational media coverage can shape collective memory of a crime.
