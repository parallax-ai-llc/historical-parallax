## Charges and central issues

The U.S. federal case centered on wire fraud and conspiracy allegations tied to statements made to investors and (in some accounts) to customers and business partners. Prosecutors argued that Theranos presented its blood-testing capabilities as proven and operational at scale when internal testing and deployment did not support those claims.

A key theme at trial was the gap between public marketing narratives and the technical reality of the company’s laboratory performance and validation.

## Evidence highlighted in public coverage

- **Investor presentations and emails:** Materials used to raise money were scrutinized for accuracy and for claims about technology readiness.
- **Third-party relationships:** Statements about partnerships with major companies and government entities were examined to determine whether they were overstated.
- **Laboratory operations:** Testimony and documents addressed how tests were actually run, including the use of conventional analyzers and quality-control issues.

## Defense themes

Holmes’ defense emphasized intent and reliance on employees and experts, and also argued that business setbacks and optimistic projections were being framed as criminal deception. The defense also raised personal and relational factors to explain decision-making during the company’s growth.

## Verdict, sentencing, and aftermath

Holmes was convicted on multiple counts related to investor fraud, and later sentenced to a significant prison term. The case also involved restitution proceedings for victims.

Theranos’ former president and COO, Ramesh "Sunny" Balwani, faced separate charges and was also convicted in a related federal case, reinforcing the legal view that misrepresentations were not limited to a single executive.

## Broader impact

The Theranos trials became a high-profile reference point for how courts evaluate deception claims in venture-backed technology companies, especially where products intersect with patient care. The proceedings also increased scrutiny of startup governance, due diligence, and the role of high-status boards in validating claims.
