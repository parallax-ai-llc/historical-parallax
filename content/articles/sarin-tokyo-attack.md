## Summary

The 1995 Tokyo subway sarin attack by Aum Shinrikyo killed 13 people and injured over 6,000. Five coordinated attacks released nerve gas on three subway lines during morning rush hour.

## Perpetrators and lead-up

The attack was carried out by members of Aum Shinrikyo, a Japanese religious movement that developed an internal structure capable of planning violence.
Prior to March 1995, the group had already been linked to other criminal acts, including earlier chemical incidents.
Investigations after the subway attack focused on how the organization acquired precursors, produced sarin, and coordinated operatives.

## Immediate response

Tokyo’s emergency services and hospitals faced a sudden influx of casualties with symptoms consistent with nerve agent exposure.
The event highlighted challenges in rapid identification of chemical agents and in protecting first responders from secondary exposure.
It also led to major review of medical preparedness and inter-agency coordination for mass-casualty incidents.

## Legal aftermath

Japanese authorities arrested numerous Aum members and pursued long-running prosecutions.
Senior figures, including founder Shoko Asahara (Chizuo Matsumoto), were ultimately sentenced, and several were executed in 2018.
The legal process became a landmark in Japan’s handling of domestic terrorism and organized criminal conspiracies.

## Broader impact

The Tokyo subway attack is widely cited in security studies as a defining case of chemical terrorism in a major urban transit system.
It influenced public awareness, counterterrorism policy, and planning for chemical, biological, radiological, and nuclear (CBRN) threats.
