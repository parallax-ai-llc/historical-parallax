## Summary

Military analyst who leaked the Pentagon Papers revealing US government deception about the Vietnam War. The leak contributed to ending the war and established whistleblower precedent.

## Additional Context

## Background

- Ellsberg worked as a military analyst and became a prominent figure in debates about secrecy, accountability, and democratic oversight in wartime decision-making.
- He is most closely associated with the release of the **Pentagon Papers**, a classified history of U.S. involvement in Vietnam.

## The Pentagon Papers (1971)

- The documents detailed internal assessments and decisions across multiple administrations.
- Their publication highlighted contradictions between public statements and internal government analysis, intensifying public scrutiny of the war.

## Legal Case and Precedent

- Ellsberg was charged under U.S. law, but the case was dismissed in 1973 due to government misconduct.
- The episode is often cited in discussions of:
  - press freedom and the public-interest defense
  - the limits of executive secrecy
  - the risks and ethics of whistleblowing

## Later Public Life

- Ellsberg remained active in public commentary and advocacy related to war powers, nuclear risk, and transparency.
- His name is frequently invoked as a reference point when later whistleblowers and leaks are debated.
