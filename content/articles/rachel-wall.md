## Summary

Last woman hanged in Massachusetts and one of the few known American female pirates. She and her husband lured ships by pretending to be in distress, then robbed them.

## Background and Legend

- Rachel Wall is often mentioned in lists of early American female pirates, though surviving documentation is limited and later retellings can blur fact and folklore.
- Accounts commonly describe coastal robbery schemes in which victims were lured toward shore under false pretenses, then attacked.
- Her story is part of a broader maritime world in which shipwreck salvage, opportunistic theft, and outright piracy sometimes overlapped.

## Arrest and Execution

- Historical summaries typically note that Wall was ultimately convicted of robbery (and associated crimes) rather than piracy in the strict legal sense.
- She was executed in Boston in 1789, and later narratives emphasize her as one of the last women executed in Massachusetts.

## Historical Context

- In the late 18th century, coastal communities in New England depended heavily on maritime trade, and crimes connected to shipping could draw severe punishment.
- Female participation in piracy was rare but not unheard of; the few documented cases often attract outsized attention in later popular history.

## Why She’s Remembered

- Wall’s notoriety persists because the “female pirate” archetype is culturally salient, while real records are scarce.
- The case illustrates how a small number of surviving references can become an enduring historical sketch through repetition in secondary sources.
- When writing about her, historians often flag uncertainty and distinguish primary records from later embellishment.
