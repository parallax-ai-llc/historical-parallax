## Summary

Bonded labor system in Nepal where Tharu girls as young as six were sold to wealthy families as domestic servants. An estimated 13,000 girls were enslaved before the practice was banned.

## What the Term Refers To

The term **Kamlari** has been used to describe a form of bonded labor affecting girls—often from the Tharu community—in parts of Nepal. In many descriptions, girls were placed into domestic work in exchange for payments to families, with the arrangement functioning in practice as coercive and exploitative labor.

## Social and Economic Drivers

Reporting and advocacy material commonly link the system to poverty, limited access to schooling, social marginalization, and debt dynamics. Domestic work placements were often framed as employment or apprenticeship, but accounts emphasize restricted freedom and vulnerability to abuse.

## Abolition Efforts

Campaigns against the Kamlari system involved local activists, NGOs, and international attention. Legal prohibitions were accompanied by efforts to identify affected children, remove them from households, and pressure authorities to enforce bans.

## Rehabilitation and Long-Term Challenges

Post-liberation efforts have included school enrollment support, transitional housing, and vocational programs. However, long-term challenges cited in summaries include documentation hurdles, economic insecurity, social stigma, and the need for sustained government services.

## Human Rights Framing

The system is frequently discussed within the broader context of child labor, trafficking, and modern slavery, highlighting the importance of education access and effective labor protections.
