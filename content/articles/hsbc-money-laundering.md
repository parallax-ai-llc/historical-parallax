## Summary

HSBC was fined $1.9 billion in 2012 for laundering money for Mexican drug cartels and violating sanctions. The bank processed $881 million for the Sinaloa and Norte del Valle cartels.

## Background (High Level)

- The term “money laundering” generally refers to processes used to conceal the origin of funds derived from criminal activity.
- Large financial institutions are typically required to maintain anti-money-laundering (AML) programs, including customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting.

## Core Allegations and Enforcement

Public accounts of the case emphasize:

- failures in AML controls and oversight,
- processing of transactions linked to criminal organizations, and
- compliance issues related to sanctions regimes.

In 2012, U.S. authorities announced major penalties and a settlement framework, widely reported as including a large monetary fine and compliance commitments.

## Why the Case Was Notable

- The scale of the penalty drew significant public attention.
- The case became a frequently cited example in debates about enforcement intensity for large banks.
- It contributed to broader discussion of “too big to jail” narratives and the limits of corporate accountability.

## Compliance and Risk Themes

- **Know Your Customer (KYC):** processes to verify identities and understand customer risk.
- **Transaction monitoring:** systems intended to detect unusual patterns or known typologies.
- **Sanctions compliance:** controls to prevent prohibited dealings with sanctioned entities or jurisdictions.

## Broader Impact

- The episode was referenced in later policy conversations about strengthening AML standards.
- It also reinforced the expectation that global banks maintain consistent controls across subsidiaries and regions.
