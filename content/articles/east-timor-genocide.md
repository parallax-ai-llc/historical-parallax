## Summary

Indonesian military occupation of East Timor from 1975-1999 that killed an estimated 100,000-180,000 people through military operations, famine, and disease. The 1991 Santa Cruz massacre drew international attention.

## Overview

The Indonesian occupation of East Timor followed a period of decolonization after Portugal’s withdrawal. The ensuing conflict involved Indonesian security forces and allied militias, as well as Timorese resistance movements.

## Scale of Harm

Estimates of deaths vary across studies and institutions. Many accounts emphasize that mortality was driven not only by direct violence, but also by:

- Forced displacement and disruption of food production.
- Famine conditions in certain periods.
- Disease and limited access to healthcare during the occupation.

## International Attention

The 1991 Santa Cruz massacre, captured by journalists and witnesses, significantly increased international visibility of the conflict and human-rights concerns.

## Transition to Independence

In 1999, a UN-sponsored referendum supported independence. The period around the vote saw extensive violence and destruction attributed to pro-integration militias and elements of the security apparatus.
