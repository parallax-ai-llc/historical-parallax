## Summary

Philippe Lenard won the 1906 Nobel Prize for cathode ray research. He showed cathode rays can penetrate thin metal foils.

## Key Contributions

- **Lenard window**: Developed thin metal-foil "windows" that allowed cathode rays to pass from an evacuated tube into air or other media, enabling measurements outside the tube.
- **Photoelectric and electron studies**: Contributed experimental results on electron emission and absorption that informed early electron and radiation physics.
- **Radiation penetration**: Investigated how charged particles and radiation interact with matter, including attenuation in foils and gases.

## Notes on the Nobel Prize (dating)

Some sources describe Lenard as receiving the **1905 Nobel Prize in Physics** (awarded for his work on cathode rays) during the **1906 ceremony**. The year in summaries can therefore vary depending on whether it refers to the prize year or the award presentation.

## Later Life and Historical Context

Lenard spent much of his career in German universities and was influential in experimental physics in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

In the 1930s he became associated with the "Deutsche Physik" movement, which opposed aspects of modern theoretical physics and was intertwined with the politics of Nazi Germany. This political involvement is frequently discussed alongside his scientific legacy.

## Related Concepts

- **Cathode rays** are streams of electrons observed in evacuated tubes, foundational to early atomic and particle physics.
- Lenard’s experimental techniques influenced later work on **electron beams**, **vacuum technology**, and radiation measurement.
