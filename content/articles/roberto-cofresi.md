## Historical Context

Cofresí operated during a period of intense maritime trade in the Caribbean, when merchant routes connected the Spanish Caribbean, North America, and Europe.
After the Napoleonic Wars, naval patrols expanded and piracy was increasingly framed as a threat to commercial stability and state authority.
Puerto Rico was a Spanish colony at the time, and coastal communities were closely tied to shipping and privateering traditions.

## Piracy and Reputation

Accounts of Cofresí describe small, fast attacks on shipping and a reliance on local geography to evade pursuit.
The “Robin Hood” image—sharing wealth with poor communities—reflects later folklore as much as documented practice.
His notoriety nonetheless made him a symbol in stories about resistance, smuggling, and the social tensions of colonial rule.

## Capture, Trial, and Execution

He was captured after a pursuit involving United States naval forces, an early example of cross-border anti-piracy cooperation in the region.
Cofresí was tried under Spanish colonial authority in Puerto Rico and executed in 1825.
His death is often marked as the end of an era in which piracy and quasi-legal privateering blurred together in the Caribbean.

## Cultural Memory

Cofresí remains prominent in Puerto Rican popular history, appearing in songs, literature, and local legends.
Modern retellings frequently emphasize themes of defiance and redistribution, even when specific details vary across sources.
