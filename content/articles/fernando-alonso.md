## Summary

Fernando Alonso won two F1 World Championships with Renault in 2005 and 2006, becoming the youngest champion at the time.

## Teams and Notable Periods

- **Renault (2003–2006)**: Broke through as a race winner and secured consecutive championships (2005, 2006).
- **McLaren (2007)**: High-profile season that remains one of the most scrutinized in modern Formula One.
- **Ferrari (2010–2014)**: Multiple title challenges and regular race-winning form, often finishing near the top in the championship standings.
- **Return to McLaren (2015–2018)**: Period marked by the sport’s hybrid era and reliability/performance struggles.
- **Alpine and Aston Martin era (2021– )**: Returned to full-time F1 racing after time away; continued to score podiums and points into his 40s.

## Racing Beyond Formula One

- **Endurance racing**: Competed in top-level endurance events and is strongly associated with the modern era of the FIA World Endurance Championship.
- **24 Hours of Le Mans**: Won overall at Le Mans, strengthening his reputation as a versatile driver across disciplines.
- **IndyCar / Indianapolis 500**: Attempted the Indianapolis 500 multiple times as part of a wider pursuit of major motorsport achievements.
- **Daytona**: Also entered major U.S. endurance races, adding to his cross-category résumé.

## Driving Style and Reputation

- **Racecraft**: Known for aggressive but controlled wheel-to-wheel defense and strategic decision-making under pressure.
- **Technical feedback**: Frequently credited for detailed feedback and an ability to maximize results even when the car is not the outright fastest.
- **Consistency**: Maintained elite-level competitiveness across multiple regulation eras and team environments.

## Legacy

- **Early benchmark**: His 2005 title made him the youngest world champion at the time, ending Michael Schumacher’s run of dominance.
- **Multi-discipline profile**: Considered one of the most prominent modern examples of an F1 champion successfully pursuing top-level wins outside F1.
- **Longevity**: Often cited in conversations about athletic longevity in motorsport due to his sustained performance over two decades.
