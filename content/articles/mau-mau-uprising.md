## Summary

British colonial forces detained 1.5 million Kenyans in concentration camps during the Mau Mau Uprising. Systematic torture, rape, and castration of detainees was documented.

## Additional Context

- The Mau Mau uprising is generally framed as an anti-colonial rebellion within British Kenya, with roots in land dispossession, labor controls, and political exclusion.
- Participation and support were not uniform across Kenyan communities; the conflict intersected with local rivalries and colonial “divide and rule” governance.

## Colonial Response (High Level)

- The declaration of a state of emergency expanded police and military powers, including detention without trial and extensive screening operations.
- Large-scale detention and forced resettlement were used to separate insurgents from civilian support networks.

## Memory and Interpretation

- In later decades, Mau Mau veterans and their descendants have sought recognition, pensions, and inclusion in national narratives of independence.
- Scholarship debates how to categorize the conflict (insurgency, civil conflict, liberation struggle) and how to weigh violence committed by all parties.

## Why It Matters

- The uprising is a major reference point for understanding decolonization in East Africa.
- It is also central to contemporary discussions of transitional justice, reparations, and the archival record of colonial security states.
