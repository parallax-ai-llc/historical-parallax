## Summary

Abhijit Vinayak Banerjee (born February 21, 1961) is an Indian-American development economist and Ford Foundation International Professor of Economics at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT). He is best known for co-pioneering the use of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) to study and combat global poverty, work for which he was awarded the 2019 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences alongside Esther Duflo and Michael Kremer. In 2003, he co-founded the Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab (J-PAL) at MIT with Duflo and Sendhil Mullainathan, which has become the world's leading center for experimental research in development economics.

Banerjee's research covers a wide range of topics including education, health, credit markets, political economy, and social norms in developing countries. His collaboration with Esther Duflo — his longtime research partner and wife — produced the internationally acclaimed book *Poor Economics* (2011), which brought rigorous poverty research to a broad audience. He is widely regarded as one of the most influential economists of his generation.

## Early Life & Background

Born in Kolkata, India, into an academic family, Banerjee grew up immersed in intellectual life. His mother, Nirmala Banerjee, was a professor of economics at Presidency College, Calcutta, and his father, Dipak Banerjee, chaired the economics department at the same institution. He completed his undergraduate degree at Presidency College, Calcutta, in 1981, before pursuing graduate studies at Jawaharlal Nehru University (JNU) in New Delhi, where he earned a master's degree in economics.

During his time at JNU in 1983, Banerjee was briefly jailed following student protests over the appointment of a new vice-chancellor — an episode he has described as formative. He subsequently moved to the United States and earned his PhD in Economics from Harvard University in 1988, writing his dissertation under Nobel laureate Eric Maskin.

## Rise to Prominence

After completing his doctorate, Banerjee joined the MIT faculty, where he has spent most of his career. His early research challenged conventional assumptions in development economics by focusing on the behavioral and informational constraints facing poor households, rather than treating poverty as a simple income problem. His work with Duflo beginning in the late 1990s introduced rigorous experimental methods — borrowed from medical research — to test development interventions in the field.

The founding of J-PAL in 2003 institutionalized this approach, creating a global network of affiliated researchers who design and evaluate policy-relevant RCTs in low- and middle-income countries. J-PAL's influence grew rapidly; by the mid-2010s, the lab had evaluated hundreds of interventions across health, education, agriculture, and social protection, shaping billions of dollars in policy spending worldwide.

## Key Achievements

- **Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences (2019):** Shared with Esther Duflo and Michael Kremer for pioneering experimental approaches to alleviating global poverty.
- **J-PAL co-founder:** The lab has overseen more than 1,000 randomized evaluations and influenced policies benefiting over 400 million people.
- **Authored *Poor Economics* (2011):** Co-written with Duflo, the book won the Financial Times and Goldman Sachs Business Book of the Year Award and was translated into more than 17 languages.
- **Authored *Good Economics for Hard Times* (2019):** A follow-up co-written with Duflo addressing migration, trade, inequality, and climate change for a general audience.
- **Infosys Prize (2009):** Awarded in Social Sciences — Economics.
- **Gerald Loeb Award:** Honored for financial journalism contributions.

## Controversies & Criticism

The RCT methodology that Banerjee helped popularize has generated significant academic debate. Economists including Angus Deaton and Lant Pritchett have argued that while RCTs produce locally valid results, they are poorly suited to informing broader policy at scale, and that the "randomista" movement has distorted funding priorities in development economics away from macroeconomic and structural questions. Deaton has also critiqued the ethical implications of withholding interventions from control groups. Banerjee has engaged these critiques, defending RCTs as indispensable tools while acknowledging their limitations alongside other methods.

Separately, Banerjee has been outspoken on Indian economic policy, including criticism of the Narendra Modi government's handling of demonetization in 2016 — a position that attracted political pushback in India.

## Legacy & Influence

Banerjee's legacy rests on transforming development economics from a field reliant on observational data and theoretical modeling into one grounded in field experiments. The J-PAL network has spread the RCT approach to researchers and policymakers across Africa, South Asia, Latin America, and beyond. His books have shaped public understanding of poverty as a complex, empirically tractable problem rather than an inevitable feature of underdevelopment. Together with Duflo, he has mentored a generation of development economists who continue to expand this research agenda globally.
