## Summary

Wan Hu is a legendary Chinese official from the Ming Dynasty who allegedly attempted to fly to space around 1500 CE by strapping 47 gunpowder rockets to a chair. According to legend, when the rockets were ignited, he disappeared in a massive explosion. While likely apocryphal, his story represents humanity's earliest recorded aspiration for rocketry and spaceflight. A lunar crater is named after him.

## Historical status of the story

Wan Hu is generally treated as a legend rather than a well-attested historical figure in the technical history of rocketry. Accounts are typically presented as anecdotal and appear in later retellings rather than in contemporaneous engineering or court records.

Because gunpowder rockets existed in China by earlier centuries, the story is often framed as a thought experiment about propulsion rather than as a plausible attempt at controlled flight. A chair with many rockets might generate thrust, but stability, ignition timing, structural integrity, and survivability would be overwhelming constraints.

## Cultural and scientific legacy

Despite its doubtful historicity, the Wan Hu legend has been influential as a symbolic “first” in the long arc of spaceflight imagination. It is frequently cited in popular histories to illustrate that dreams of leaving Earth predate modern physics and aerospace engineering.

The naming of a lunar crater after Wan Hu reflects this symbolic role: the crater name acknowledges the cultural narrative of early rocketry aspiration, even if the underlying event is not verified.

## What the legend highlights (at a high level)

The story is a compact way to discuss core aerospace problems that later became formal disciplines:

- Thrust vs. mass: rockets must provide sufficient impulse to overcome weight.
- Guidance and stability: thrust must be directed and controlled to avoid tumbling.
- Staging and ignition: coordinating multiple engines is non-trivial.
- Structural loads: acceleration and vibration can destroy a vehicle before it flies.

In that sense, Wan Hu endures less as a biography and more as an early narrative lens on the engineering challenges of rocketry.
