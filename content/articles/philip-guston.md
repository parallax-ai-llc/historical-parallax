## Summary

Abstract Expressionist who shocked the art world by returning to figuration with cartoonish paintings of hooded Klansmen, everyday objects, and self-portraits. His late work anticipated Neo-Expressionism.

## Early Work and Influences

- Guston’s early career included figurative and socially engaged painting, with influences from Renaissance art as well as modern muralism.
- Before becoming closely associated with Abstract Expressionism, he moved between representational and abstract approaches, reflecting shifting artistic and political climates.

## Shift from Abstraction to Figuration

- In the late 1960s and around 1970, he reintroduced recognizable imagery—hooded figures, shoes, cigarettes, clocks, and studio scenes—rendered in a blunt, cartoon-like style.
- The hooded figures are often interpreted as a critique of racism and complicity, with Guston frequently implicating the self rather than depicting an external “other.”

## Reception and Reassessment

- The initial critical response to the figurative turn was often hostile, especially among audiences expecting continued abstraction.
- Over time, his late work has been reassessed as influential for later figurative painting, including currents associated with Neo-Expressionism.

## Exhibitions and Controversy

- Guston’s use of Klan imagery has led to recurring debate about context, intent, and display, including institutional decisions about how to present the work to contemporary audiences.
