## Summary

Baseball legend banned for life for involvement in the 1919 Black Sox Scandal. Whether he actually participated in throwing the World Series remains debated to this day.

## Playing career (high-level)

- Jackson was one of the standout hitters of the early 20th century and played primarily for the Cleveland Naps/Indians and the Chicago White Sox.
- His reputation rests on elite batting averages and strong outfield defense for his era.
- "Shoeless" is a nickname associated with an early-career anecdote and became part of his popular image.

## The 1919 Black Sox scandal

- The scandal involved allegations that several White Sox players conspired with gamblers to deliberately lose games in the 1919 World Series.
- Jackson admitted in some statements to receiving money; the extent of his prior knowledge or intentional on-field underperformance has been a central dispute.
- Statistical arguments are frequently cited in Jackson's defense (e.g., that he recorded strong hitting numbers during the series), but they do not resolve questions about off-field agreements.

## Legal and league consequences

- In 1921, players were acquitted in court, but Major League Baseball's new commissioner, Kenesaw Mountain Landis, imposed lifetime bans under the sport's integrity policy.
- Jackson never returned to MLB play and later worked in semiprofessional baseball and business ventures.

## Posthumous debate and legacy

- Jackson has been the subject of repeated campaigns for reinstatement and Hall of Fame consideration.
- Popular culture (notably films and literature about the scandal) helped keep his case in public discussion and shaped perceptions of guilt and innocence.
