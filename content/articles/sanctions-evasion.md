## Summary

Following sanctions on Russia, elaborate networks emerged to help Russian oligarchs hide yachts, planes, and assets. Shell companies in UAE, Turkey, and Central Asia facilitate evasion.

More broadly, **sanctions evasion** refers to deliberate efforts to bypass legal restrictions imposed by states or international bodies. Evasion can involve disguising the true owner, origin, destination, or end-use of goods and money so that prohibited parties can still access finance, technology, or markets.

Common targets include:

- Financial restrictions (asset freezes, correspondent banking limits).
- Trade controls (export bans, import bans, dual-use technology restrictions).
- Transport limits (shipping, aviation leasing, port access, insurance).

Typical enablers include shell companies, nominee directors, complex ownership chains, intermediaries in third countries, and document fraud.

## Common Evasion Methods (High-Level)

Sanctions evasion techniques vary by sector, but recurring patterns include:

- **Asset concealment:** moving yachts, aircraft, real estate, or securities to entities with opaque ownership; using nominees and layered trusts.
- **Trade rerouting and transshipment:** routing goods through third countries, altering invoices, or misdeclaring the end destination.
- **Front companies and intermediaries:** using apparently legitimate firms to purchase controlled items and re-export them.
- **Ship-to-ship transfers and “flag hopping”:** changing vessel flags/registries, manipulating AIS tracking practices, or transferring cargo at sea (often discussed in the context of oil and commodities).
- **Financial workarounds:** using non-sanctioned banks, barter-like arrangements, alternative payment rails, or high-risk payment instruments.

## Compliance and Countermeasures

Governments and financial institutions typically respond with a mix of:

- Beneficial-ownership requirements and corporate registry reforms.
- “Know your customer” (KYC) and enhanced due diligence for high-risk jurisdictions and sectors.
- Export-control screening for dual-use goods, end-user checks, and diversion risk assessments.
- Targeted enforcement (designations, seizures/forfeitures where lawful, and penalties for facilitation).

## Why It Matters

Sanctions evasion can blunt the intended economic and political impact of sanctions, create parallel logistics and finance networks, and increase corruption and organized-crime risks. It also raises compliance costs for legitimate businesses operating across borders.
