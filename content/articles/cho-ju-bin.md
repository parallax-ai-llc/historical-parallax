## Summary

Operator of the 'Nth Room' digital sex crime ring who blackmailed 74 victims including 16 minors into producing sexual material distributed through Telegram. Sentenced to 42 years.

## The “Nth Room” case (context)

- The “Nth Room” scandal refers to a set of online sexual-exploitation rooms that operated on encrypted chat platforms, most prominently Telegram.
- Victims were coerced and blackmailed into providing sexual images and videos, which were then distributed to paying or invited participants.
- The case became a major social issue in South Korea because it involved organized digital abuse, large numbers of participants, and victims including minors.

## Modus operandi (as commonly reported)

- Cho Ju-bin was accused of using threats, deception, and doxxing-style intimidation to control victims.
- Payment mechanisms reported in the case included forms of digital transfer and indirect monetization, reflecting how online crime groups can scale through platform features.
- Investigators emphasized the role of anonymity and rapid redistribution in amplifying harm and making takedowns difficult.

## Investigation, trial, and sentencing

- The case involved coordinated police investigations, digital forensics, and public reporting by journalists and online communities who pressured authorities to act.
- Courts convicted Cho Ju-bin of multiple offenses related to the production, possession, and distribution of child sexual abuse material and coercion (as summarized in major secondary sources).
- He received a lengthy prison sentence (commonly reported as 42 years), reflecting the multi-victim and aggravated nature of the crimes.

## Social and policy impact

- The Nth Room scandal accelerated debates over platform responsibility, identity verification, stronger penalties, and victim-support systems.
- It also became a reference point in discussions about gender-based violence, privacy, and how law enforcement should handle digital evidence and encrypted services.
