## Summary

The crew of the slave ship Zong threw 132 enslaved Africans overboard to collect insurance money. The case became a landmark in the abolitionist movement when charges of murder were dismissed.

## Background and Context

The Zong was a British slave ship involved in the transatlantic slave trade. In late 1781, during a voyage from West Africa toward Jamaica, the ship’s crew made decisions that resulted in the killing of more than one hundred enslaved Africans.

A central feature of the case is that it was framed in commercial terms rather than as a homicide: the dispute that reached the courts focused on insurance and “cargo” loss claims, reflecting the legal and economic structures that underpinned slavery in Britain’s Atlantic empire.

## What Happened

High-level points commonly noted in historical accounts include:

- A large number of enslaved people were thrown overboard from the ship.
- The act was later argued to have been motivated by an attempt to claim insurance money.
- The legal proceedings centered on insurance liability rather than criminal accountability for murder.

## Legal Case and Public Impact

The Zong incident is often discussed alongside the growth of British abolitionist activism. The controversy contributed to public debate by highlighting how enslaved people could be treated as insurable property under prevailing law.

While the immediate litigation did not result in murder convictions, the wider political effect was significant: abolitionist writers and campaigners used the case as an example of the brutality of the slave trade, helping to build momentum for reform and eventual abolition.

## Legacy

Today, the Zong massacre is referenced in studies of:

- The history of slavery and the Atlantic slave trade
- The development of abolitionism in Britain
- The intersection of commerce, insurance law, and human rights
