## Background

Leggett is a condensed-matter theorist best known for explaining how quantum effects can appear at macroscopic scales in certain low-temperature systems. His work helped clarify how superfluidity and superconductivity can be described using microscopic theory while still matching experimentally observed bulk behavior.

## Key Contributions (High Level)

- **Helium-3 superfluidity:** Leggett developed a theoretical description of the superfluid phases of helium-3, a fermionic system that becomes superfluid only at extremely low temperatures.
- **Macroscopic quantum phenomena:** He contributed to the conceptual and mathematical frameworks used to discuss when and how quantum coherence can persist in larger, many-particle systems.
- **Interdisciplinary relevance:** Ideas from superfluid theory and macroscopic coherence have influenced neighboring areas such as superconductivity, quantum fluids, and aspects of quantum information discussions about decoherence.

## Recognition and Legacy

The 2003 Nobel Prize in Physics cited “pioneering contributions to the theory of superconductors and superfluids,” and Leggett was among the recipients recognized for the theoretical foundations of these phenomena.
