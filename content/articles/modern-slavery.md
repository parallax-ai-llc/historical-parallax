## Summary

An estimated 49.6 million people are trapped in modern slavery including forced labor, forced marriage, and trafficking. Every country in the world has victims of modern slavery.

## What “Modern Slavery” Includes

Modern slavery is an umbrella term commonly used to describe severe forms of exploitation where a person cannot refuse or leave due to threats, violence, coercion, deception, or abuse of power.[^2]

Commonly cited categories include:

- **Forced labor** (including debt bondage) in factories, agriculture, construction, domestic work, fishing, mining, and other sectors
- **Forced marriage** where a person is married without free and informed consent
- **Human trafficking** for labor exploitation or sexual exploitation (often a process that can lead to forced labor)
- **Child labor in its worst forms**, including hazardous work and commercial sexual exploitation

## Key Drivers and Vulnerabilities

Modern slavery is associated with structural and situational risk factors, including:

- **Poverty and debt**, including recruitment fees and predatory lending
- **Irregular migration and weak legal status**, increasing dependence on employers or intermediaries
- **Conflict and displacement**, which disrupt livelihoods and protection systems
- **Discrimination** (gender, caste, ethnicity, and other social hierarchies) that limits access to safe work
- **Weak enforcement** of labor protections and corruption that reduces accountability

## How Prevalence Is Estimated

Global estimates are commonly produced using combinations of:

- **Household surveys** measuring experiences of forced labor and forced marriage
- **Administrative data** (victim identification, prosecutions, labor inspections), which typically undercount total prevalence
- **Model-based estimation** that extrapolates survey findings across regions and populations

The 49.6 million figure is widely attributed to joint estimates by the ILO, Walk Free, and IOM for 2021 (published in 2022).[^3]

## Responses and Countermeasures

High-level responses often include:

- Strengthening **labor inspection**, grievance mechanisms, and worker protections
- Regulating or banning **worker-paid recruitment fees** and improving recruitment transparency
- Increasing **supply-chain due diligence** and corporate accountability for forced labor risks
- Expanding **victim protection** (shelter, legal aid, immigration relief) to reduce re-exploitation
- Improving **cross-border cooperation** on trafficking investigations and victim identification
