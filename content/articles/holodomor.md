## Summary

Man-made famine in Soviet Ukraine in 1932-1933 caused by Stalins forced collectivization policies. An estimated 3.5-7.5 million Ukrainians died of starvation.

## Background

The Holodomor took place amid Soviet agricultural collectivization, rapid state procurement targets, and political repression. Policies around grain requisitioning, movement restrictions, and punitive measures against perceived “hoarding” contributed to severe food shortages in many rural areas.

## Human Impact

Millions died from starvation and related causes, and the famine caused long-lasting demographic, social, and cultural damage. Survivors’ accounts and archival research have been central to documenting both day-to-day conditions and state decision-making.

## Historical Interpretation

How the Holodomor should be classified—particularly the question of intent and whether it constitutes genocide—has been the subject of extensive scholarly debate and differing legal/political recognitions by states and institutions. Regardless of classification, it is widely recognized as a catastrophic famine shaped by human policy choices.

## Memory and Commemoration

Ukraine and Ukrainian communities worldwide hold commemorations and memorial events, and the topic remains prominent in education, museum work, and public history. Archival openings after the Soviet period expanded the evidentiary base for research.
