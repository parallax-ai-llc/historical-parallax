## Summary

Norwegian far-right attacker who murdered his Chinese-born stepsister and attempted to attack the Al-Noor Islamic Centre mosque in Bærum. He was inspired by the Christchurch attack.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2019 | Murdered stepsister and attacked mosque in Bærum |
| 2020 | Sentenced to 21 years in preventive detention |

## Background

- The 2019 attack attempt in Bærum occurred in the context of heightened concern in Europe about far-right extremist violence.
- Investigators and prosecutors described the attacker as influenced by online extremist subcultures and prior mass-casualty attacks.
- Norway’s security debate after the incident focused on radicalization pathways and the monitoring of violent extremist threats.

## Attack Attempt and Immediate Response

- The attack targeted the Al-Noor Islamic Centre, where people were present for religious services.
- The attacker was confronted and subdued by individuals inside the mosque before larger loss of life could occur.
- The incident was closely linked in public reporting to the killing of the attacker’s stepsister earlier the same day.

## Investigation, Trial, and Sentencing

- Norwegian courts applied the country’s legal framework for serious violence, including the possibility of preventive detention when an offender is assessed as a continuing danger.
- The sentencing outcome is frequently cited in discussions of how Norway balances punishment, public safety, and rehabilitation.
- The case is also referenced in broader assessments of far-right terrorism risks and the role of online propaganda.

## Broader Significance

- The attempted mosque attack intensified discussions about protecting minority religious communities and countering hate-motivated violence.
- It contributed to continued scrutiny of transnational extremist narratives that circulate across national borders.
