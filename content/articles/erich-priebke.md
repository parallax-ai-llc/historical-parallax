## Summary

SS captain who participated in the Ardeatine massacre killing 335 Italian civilians in Rome. Lived openly in Argentina for 50 years before being extradited to Italy.

## Historical Context

The **Ardeatine massacre** (Fosse Ardeatine) occurred during the German occupation of Rome in World War II. It was carried out as a reprisal after a partisan attack killed German personnel, and it became one of the most infamous atrocities committed in Italy under occupation.

## Role and Responsibility (High Level)

Priebke served as an SS officer in Rome and was among those involved in the organization and execution of the killings. Postwar accountability for such events often depended on establishing both participation and command responsibility, particularly when perpetrators argued they were following orders.

## Flight and Postwar Life

After the war, Priebke avoided immediate prosecution and eventually settled in **Argentina**, where he lived openly for decades. His case later became emblematic of how some former Nazi officials escaped justice through postwar migration networks and weak enforcement.

## Extradition and Trial in Italy

When his identity and role became widely public, Italian authorities pursued legal proceedings. The trials examined documentary evidence, chain-of-command issues, and the legality of reprisal killings. He was ultimately convicted in Italy for his role in the massacre.

## Legacy

- The case reinforced public attention on delayed war-crimes prosecutions.
- It highlighted the role of archives, journalists, and survivor communities in reopening investigations.
- It remains a reference point in debates about accountability decades after mass atrocities.
