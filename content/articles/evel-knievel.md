## Summary

Evel Knievel was the most famous daredevil and motorcycle stunt performer in history. He attempted over 75 ramp-to-ramp motorcycle jumps and suffered 433 bone fractures.

## Additional Context

Robert Craig “Evel” Knievel became a major American pop-culture figure by turning motorcycle jumping into a televised spectacle. His career combined showmanship, sponsorship, and a willingness to attempt highly publicized stunts with significant personal risk.

### Showmanship and Media

- Knievel’s jumps were promoted as major events, often drawing large in-person crowds and national television audiences.
- His signature red-white-and-blue outfit and carefully branded persona helped make him recognizable beyond motorsports.
- A cycle of **attempt → crash or success → medical recovery → next stunt** became part of the public narrative around his career.

### Risk and Injury

The “433 bone fractures” figure is frequently repeated in summaries of his life as a shorthand for the cumulative physical cost of repeated crashes and high-impact landings.

### Cultural Impact

- Helped establish the template for later “extreme” sports celebrity: danger, spectacle, and mass media.
- His Snake River Canyon attempt, though not a standard motorcycle jump, is remembered as an example of the era’s large-scale televised stunts.

### Later-Life Note

In later years, documentaries and retrospectives treated his career as both entertainment history and a cautionary example about risk, injury, and promotion-driven escalation.
