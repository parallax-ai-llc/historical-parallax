## Summary

Italian noblewoman from the infamous Borgia family, rumored to have poisoned political enemies.

## Background and Family

- Daughter of Rodrigo Borgia (later Pope Alexander VI) and Vannozza dei Cattanei.[^1]
- Sister (or half-sister) of Cesare Borgia, a central figure in Borgia political and military campaigns.[^1]
- Her marriages were used to forge alliances with major Italian powers during the Italian Wars period.[^1]

## Marriages and Political Role

- Married Giovanni Sforza (Lord of Pesaro) in 1493; the marriage was later annulled amid political shifts.[^1]
- Married Alfonso of Aragon (a Neapolitan prince) in 1498; he was killed in Rome in 1500 (widely attributed in contemporary rumor to Borgia power politics).[^1]
- Married Alfonso I d'Este (Duke of Ferrara) in 1502, becoming Duchess of Ferrara and spending most of her later life there.[^1]

## Duchess of Ferrara: Governance and Patronage

- As duchess, she participated in court administration and is documented acting as a regent during absences of her husband.[^1]
- The Este court at Ferrara was a major Renaissance cultural center; Lucrezia is associated with patronage and courtly culture rather than the sensational poison legends.[^1]

## Reputation and Later Myth

- Early modern and later writers amplified stories portraying her as a poisoner; modern historians generally treat these as largely unsubstantiated compared to the surviving documentary record.[^1]
- Her historical image is often discussed as a case study in how propaganda, family politics, and later literature can reshape a person's reputation.[^1]

## Quick Facts

- Known for: Borgia/Este dynastic politics; later life in Ferrara.
- Common allegations: poisoning and sexual scandal (frequently repeated, weakly evidenced in primary sources).[^1]
- Burial: Ferrara, Italy.[^1]
