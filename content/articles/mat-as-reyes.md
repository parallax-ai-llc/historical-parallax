## Summary

American serial rapist and murderer who confessed in 2002 to the 1989 Central Park jogger attack, exonerating the Central Park Five who had been wrongfully convicted. He was already serving life for other crimes.

## Background

Matías Reyes is known primarily for his role in the later resolution of the Central Park jogger case. By the time he confessed to that assault, he had already been convicted of other violent crimes, including rape and murder, and was serving a life sentence.

## Criminal Pattern (High Level)

Reporting and court records describe Reyes as a serial offender who committed multiple sexual assaults and killings in New York City around the late 1980s. His crimes involved opportunistic attacks and, in some cases, lethal violence.

## Central Park Jogger Case Significance

Reyes’s 2002 confession and the related DNA evidence were central to the exoneration of the five teenagers who became known as the “Central Park Five.” The case is frequently cited in discussions of:

- False confessions and coercive interrogation practices.
- The reliability of eyewitness accounts and contemporaneous narratives.
- The importance of biological evidence and post-conviction review.

## Legal Outcome and Public Impact

Reyes’s confession did not erase the harm of the original assault, but it materially changed the historical and legal understanding of the case. The exonerations prompted broader public and institutional scrutiny of policing, prosecutorial decision-making, and the mechanisms available for correcting wrongful convictions.

## Further Notes

In many summaries of the case, Reyes is treated as the confirmed perpetrator of the 1989 assault based on his confession and the DNA match reported in official reviews.
