## Summary

Human trafficking network in the Sinai Peninsula where Eritrean and Ethiopian refugees were kidnapped, tortured, and held for ransom. Traffickers extracted ransoms through torture broadcast via phone.

## Background and context

The term "Sinai Trafficking" is commonly used for a wave of kidnapping-for-ransom and human trafficking that targeted migrants and asylum seekers transiting through Egypt's Sinai Peninsula, particularly people fleeing Eritrea and Ethiopia in the late 2000s and early 2010s. Many victims were attempting to reach Israel or onward routes in the Mediterranean basin.

Several conditions contributed to the growth of the phenomenon, including regional conflict and repression that drove migration, weak law enforcement capacity in some desert areas, and criminal networks able to move people across borders and hold captives in remote locations.

## Modus operandi

Trafficking groups were reported to:

- Abduct migrants during transit (including from border areas and along smuggling routes).
- Transport victims to holding sites in Sinai.
- Use violence and torture to coerce families into paying ransoms.
- Conduct ransom negotiations by telephone, sometimes forcing victims to call relatives while being abused.

Ransom demands varied across cases and over time, and the system often involved intermediaries who facilitated payments and communications.

## Geography and routes

Sinai's desert terrain and its proximity to multiple borders made it a nexus for both smuggling and trafficking. Victims were often moved through a chain of handlers before reaching a detention site, which complicated investigations and accountability.

## Responses and aftermath

Accounts describe an eventual reduction in large-scale trafficking activity following intensified Egyptian security operations and changes in migration routes. Human rights organizations also increased documentation of abuses, while governments and international bodies faced pressure to address both the immediate violence and the underlying drivers of irregular migration.

## Why it mattered

Sinai Trafficking became a widely cited example of how irregular migration corridors can evolve into markets for kidnapping and extortion when armed actors, corrupt facilitators, and transnational payment channels converge. It also shaped later discussions about protection for refugees in transit and responsibilities of states along migration routes.
