## Summary

Chinese-born American defense contractor who spied for China for over 20 years, passing sensitive US Navy technology including submarine propulsion data. He was one of the most damaging Chinese spies in US history.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1983 | Began working for US defense companies |
| 2005 | Arrested by FBI for espionage |
| 2008 | Sentenced to 24 years in federal prison |

## Additional Context

- Mak worked as an engineer for U.S. defense contractors, giving him access to sensitive technical information.
- The case was investigated by the FBI as part of counterintelligence efforts focused on technology transfer.
- According to public reporting about the prosecution, investigators alleged he used physical media (such as disks/CDs) and intermediaries to move information.

## Legal Case (High Level)

- Mak was convicted in U.S. federal court on espionage-related charges.
- The case also involved allegations and prosecutions of other participants connected to the transfer of documents.
- Sentencing reflected the court’s view that the materials involved could materially aid a foreign military.

## Why the Case Was Notable

- It is frequently cited in discussions of long-term, workplace-based espionage.
- The incident is used in security training as an example of insider risk and gradual exfiltration of technical data.

## Aftermath

- The case contributed to heightened attention on protecting export-controlled and classified-adjacent engineering information.
- It also became part of broader public debate about balancing open research, commercial collaboration, and national security.
