## Summary

Fire at Grenfell Tower in London on June 14, 2017 that killed 72 residents. Flammable cladding allowed the fire to spread rapidly up the 24-story building. An inquiry found systemic failures.

## Building Safety Context (High-level)

- Grenfell became a defining case in debates about high-rise fire safety, including the risks of certain exterior wall systems and the interaction between materials, design, and compartmentation.
- Large disasters of this kind often reveal multi-layered failure chains: procurement choices, compliance checks, contractor oversight, and regulatory enforcement.
- Residents’ prior safety concerns and the handling of complaints have been widely discussed in public reporting and inquiry materials.

## Inquiry and Accountability

- A public inquiry is intended to establish an official factual record, identify contributing factors, and recommend changes; it is distinct from criminal proceedings.
- Discussions of "systemic failures" typically encompass corporate decision-making, government oversight, and the adequacy of building control regimes.
- When expanding this article, it is important to attribute conclusions to specific inquiry phases or reports.

## Broader Impact

- The fire accelerated policy and regulatory attention on cladding remediation, inspection of high-rise residential buildings, and resident safety rights.
- It also became a social and political symbol of inequality, housing policy failures, and the consequences of cost-cutting in safety-critical contexts.
