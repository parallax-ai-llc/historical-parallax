## Summary

Communal violence in Gujarat, India in 2002 that killed over 1,000 people, mostly Muslims. The riots followed the Godhra train burning and were accused of being organized with state complicity.

## Background

- Gujarat has a long history of communal tensions, and the 2002 violence unfolded in a charged political environment.
- The immediate trigger widely cited was the burning of a train coach in Godhra on 27 February 2002, which killed Hindu pilgrims and sparked widespread unrest.

## Scale and Patterns of Violence

- Reports described riots, arson, and targeted attacks that disproportionately affected Muslim communities, alongside retaliatory incidents.
- The death toll and responsibility for specific incidents have been contested, but the episode is consistently treated as one of the most serious communal violence events in modern India.

## Government Response and Investigations

- The riots led to major political and legal fallout, including inquiries, commissions, and court-supervised investigations.
- Debates centered on questions of state capacity, alleged complicity or negligence, and accountability for organizers and perpetrators.

## Political and Social Aftermath

- The events influenced Indian political discourse for years, including debates over governance, majoritarian politics, and minority protection.
- Survivors and affected communities faced long-term challenges such as displacement, economic loss, and ongoing social polarization.
