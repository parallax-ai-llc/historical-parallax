## Summary

Martin Karplus is an Austrian-American theoretical chemist who shared the 2013 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for multiscale models of complex chemical systems.

## Scientific contributions (high-level)

Karplus is associated with foundational work in theoretical and computational chemistry, including methods that connect quantum mechanics and classical mechanics in practical simulations. This family of approaches is important because many chemically relevant systems are too large to treat fully quantum-mechanically, yet too subtle to model with classical approximations alone.

One widely cited concept in NMR spectroscopy is the "Karplus equation," relating certain NMR coupling constants to molecular dihedral angles. In practice, this helps chemists infer three-dimensional structure and conformational preferences from experimental spectra.

## Multiscale modeling and the Nobel Prize

The 2013 Nobel Prize in Chemistry recognized the development of multiscale models that allow accurate, computationally tractable study of complex chemical processes. The central idea is to treat a chemically active region with higher accuracy while representing the surrounding environment with a simpler model, enabling simulations of reactions in realistic settings.

## Impact on fields and tools

Computational methods developed and popularized by pioneers like Karplus underpin modern applications across chemistry, biochemistry, and materials science, including enzyme mechanism studies, drug design support, and interpretation of experimental data.

Beyond specific equations, the broader legacy is methodological: establishing standards for how simulations are validated, how approximations are justified, and how theory and experiment can be integrated to produce reliable scientific inference.

## Academic roles

Karplus held research and teaching positions at major institutions, and his work influenced generations of chemists who adopted computation as a core laboratory tool. In historical accounts of the field, he is often placed among the figures who helped move molecular simulation from a niche technique into mainstream chemical practice.

## Enduring relevance

Modern chemical software and workflows still reflect the multiscale thinking recognized by the 2013 Nobel Prize, keeping Karplus's contributions visible in everyday research practice.
