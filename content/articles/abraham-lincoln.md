## Summary

Abraham Lincoln (February 12, 1809 – April 15, 1865) was the 16th President of the United States, serving from March 1861 until his assassination in April 1865. He led the nation through the Civil War, preserving the Union and abolishing slavery through the Emancipation Proclamation of 1863 and the passage of the 13th Amendment.

Lincoln rose from poverty on the frontier to become one of the most consequential leaders in American history. A self-educated lawyer and former Illinois legislator, he entered the presidency at a moment of existential national crisis and guided the country through its bloodiest conflict. His moral clarity on the question of slavery and his political skill in holding together a fractious coalition defined his tenure.

His legacy extends far beyond the war itself. Lincoln redefined the meaning of American democracy, linking the nation's founding ideals to the promise of equality for all people. He remains a touchstone in American political culture and is consistently ranked by historians as the greatest U.S. president.

## Early Life & Background

Lincoln was born on February 12, 1809, in a log cabin in Hardin County, Kentucky. His family moved to Indiana in 1816 and to Illinois in 1830, seeking better land and escaping the slave economy. His mother, Nancy Hanks Lincoln, died in 1818; his stepmother, Sarah Bush Johnston, encouraged his intellectual curiosity.

Largely self-educated, Lincoln had fewer than 12 months of formal schooling. He read voraciously by firelight — the Bible, Aesop's Fables, and later Blackstone's *Commentaries on the Laws of England* — teaching himself law. He worked as a store clerk, rail-splitter, postmaster, and surveyor before being admitted to the Illinois bar in 1836.

## Rise to Prominence

Lincoln served four terms in the Illinois state legislature (1834–1842) as a Whig before winning a single term in the U.S. House of Representatives in 1846. He gained national attention in 1858 during his Senate race against Stephen Douglas. Though he lost that election, the Lincoln–Douglas debates, which centered on the morality and expansion of slavery, established him as a leading voice in the newly formed Republican Party.

At the Republican National Convention in Chicago in May 1860, Lincoln secured the presidential nomination on the third ballot. He won the general election in November 1860 with 39.8% of the popular vote — without carrying a single Southern state — triggering the secession of South Carolina within weeks.

## Key Achievements

- **Preserved the Union** by prosecuting the Civil War (1861–1865) to a successful conclusion.
- **Emancipation Proclamation** (January 1, 1863) declared enslaved people in Confederate states free, reframing the war as a moral crusade against slavery.
- **13th Amendment** (passed by Congress January 31, 1865) permanently abolished slavery throughout the United States.
- **Gettysburg Address** (November 19, 1863), delivered in 272 words, redefined the war's purpose and remains one of the most celebrated speeches in the English language.
- Established the **U.S. Department of Agriculture** (1862), signed the **Homestead Act** (1862), and chartered the **Transcontinental Railroad** (1862).

## Controversies & Criticism

Lincoln suspended the writ of habeas corpus during the war, allowing the military detention of civilians without trial — an act critics then and since have condemned as a violation of constitutional rights. He also authorized the Dakota War executions of December 1862, in which 38 Dakota men were hanged in the largest mass execution in U.S. history following a violent conflict in Minnesota.

Historians debate the sincerity and timing of his anti-slavery convictions. His early political positions tolerated slavery's existence where it already stood, and his primary stated war aim in 1861 was preserving the Union, not emancipation. Some scholars argue his evolution was driven as much by military necessity as moral conviction.

## Legacy & Influence

Lincoln's assassination at Ford's Theatre on April 14, 1865 — just five days after Confederate General Robert E. Lee's surrender — transformed him into a martyr. His image became central to the Republican Party's identity for decades and to American national mythology broadly.

He has appeared on the penny since 1909 and the $5 bill since 1914. The Lincoln Memorial, dedicated in 1922 in Washington, D.C., has served as a backdrop for defining moments in American civic life, including Martin Luther King Jr.'s "I Have a Dream" speech in 1963. Virtually every major poll of historians ranks Lincoln first among U.S. presidents.
