Co-founder of the Crips who was executed for four murders despite becoming an anti-gang activist on death row.

## Background

Stanley Tookie Williams (1953–2005) became nationally known both for his role in founding the Los Angeles street gang the Crips and for his later efforts to discourage youth from joining gangs.

He grew up in an environment marked by poverty and instability, and he moved to Los Angeles as a child. In the early 1970s, he and Raymond Washington helped form what became the Crips, which later fractured into numerous sets and played a major role in the growth of gang violence in Southern California.

## Criminal Case and Sentencing

Williams was convicted in California state court for four murders committed during armed robberies. The jury imposed a death sentence in 1981, and he spent decades on death row at San Quentin.

The case remained contested: supporters emphasized rehabilitation claims and argued about the fairness and evidence in the proceedings, while prosecutors and victims’ families emphasized the convictions and the severity of the crimes.

## Death Row Activism and Writing

While incarcerated, Williams published anti-gang messaging aimed at young readers and participated in public campaigns against gang violence. His supporters pointed to these efforts as evidence of personal transformation.

His name also became associated with broader debates about:

- the purpose of punishment (retribution vs. rehabilitation)
- the reliability and finality of capital convictions
- the role of public advocacy and celebrity attention in clemency cases

## Clemency and Execution

In the final years of his life, Williams sought executive clemency. The clemency process drew significant public attention, including support from some activists and public figures and opposition from others. California Governor Arnold Schwarzenegger ultimately denied clemency.

Williams was executed by lethal injection at San Quentin in December 2005.
