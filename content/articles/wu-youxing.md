## Summary

Wu Youxing was a Ming Dynasty physician who proposed that epidemic diseases are caused by invisible pathogenic particles transmitted through the air, anticipating germ theory by over 200 years. His 1642 treatise Wen Yi Lun (Treatise on Pestilence) was one of the earliest works to suggest that different diseases have different specific causes.

## Wen Yi Lun (Treatise on Pestilence)

Wu Youxing’s treatise is often highlighted for arguing that epidemic illnesses could arise from **specific external agents** rather than solely from generic imbalances. This framing encouraged careful differentiation between diseases and emphasized observation during outbreaks.

## Historical Importance

Although written centuries before modern microbiology, Wu’s core ideas are frequently described as anticipating later concepts:

- contagious agents that spread between people
- disease specificity (different epidemics can have different causes)
- the importance of environmental transmission (including air)

## Influence

In the history of Chinese medicine, Wu Youxing is cited as an example of practitioners who updated theory in response to crisis conditions, using epidemic waves as data for refining explanations and treatment approaches.

## Modern Interpretation

Modern historians of medicine often read Wu Youxing as part of a broader global pattern: epidemics pressure medical systems to become more empirical and more attentive to transmission. His work is therefore used less as a claim of "modern germ theory" and more as evidence of early reasoning about contagion and specificity.
