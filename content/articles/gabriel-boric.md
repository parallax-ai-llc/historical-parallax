## Summary

Gabriel Boric Font, born February 11, 1986, is a Chilean politician who has served as the 37th President of Chile since March 11, 2022, becoming the youngest president in Chilean history at age 35.[^1] Rising from student protest leader during the 2011 Chilean education movement to the nation's highest office, Boric represents a millennial left-wing political generation that sought to fundamentally transform Chile's neoliberal economic model.[^2] His presidency has been marked by ambitious progressive proposals including constitutional reform, expanded social services, and educational transformation, though many initiatives faced significant opposition and voter rejection.[^3] Despite achieving some economic gains including minimum wage increases and reduced working hours, his administration struggled with rising crime, immigration challenges, and failed constitutional referendums that left his approval ratings in persistent decline.[^4] His presidency represents both the promise and limitations of Latin America's new left, demonstrating the difficulties of implementing transformative change within established democratic and economic frameworks.[^5]

---

## Early Life and Background

Gabriel Borić Font was born on February 11, 1986, in Punta Arenas, the southernmost city of continental Chile, located in the remote Magallanes region.[^6] His family background reflects Chile's immigrant heritage: on his father's side, Boric descends from Croatian immigrants, with roots tracing back to Ugljan, an island off the Adriatic coast of Croatia.[^7] His great-grandfather, Juan Boric (originally Ive Borić Barešić), along with his brother Simón (Šime), arrived in Punta Arenas around 1885, being among the first ten Croats to settle in the Magallanes region.[^8] His mother, María Soledad Font Aguilera, is of Catalan descent, giving Boric a bicultural European heritage common among middle-class Chilean families.[^9]

Boric's father, Luis Javier Boric Scarpa, worked as a chemical engineer for Chile's state-owned oil company ENAP for over 40 years, providing the family with stable middle-class status.[^10] He grew up with two brothers, Simón and Tomás, in a household that his relatives describe as devout Catholic, with his mother actively involved in the Schoenstatt Apostolic Movement.[^11] However, Boric himself would later identify as agnostic, reflecting a generational shift in religious identification common among younger Chileans.[^12]

He completed his primary and secondary education at The British School in Punta Arenas, a bilingual institution that educated many of the region's elite families.[^13] During his school years, he demonstrated early political engagement by leading the re-founding of the Federación de Estudiantes Secundarios de Punta Arenas (Punta Arenas Secondary School Students' Federation) in 1999 and 2000.[^14] At some point during his secondary education, he served as president of his class, foreshadowing his later leadership roles.[^15]

In 2004, at age 18, Boric moved to Santiago to attend law school at the prestigious University of Chile, one of the nation's top public universities.[^16] This transition from Chile's remote south to the capital city exposed him to the political ferment and student activism that characterized Chilean universities. He completed his law coursework in 2009, though he never formally graduated with a law degree as he did not complete his thesis, instead devoting his energy to student politics and activism.[^17] His time at university would prove transformative, converting a young man from a conservative southern city into one of Chile's most prominent left-wing political figures.

---

## Rise to Power

Boric's political ascent began in the halls of the University of Chile, where he quickly became involved in student governance and activism. In 2008, he was elected as a Counsellor of the University of Chile's Student Federation (FECH), representing the Law Faculty.[^18] The following year, he became Chair of the Center of Law Students (CED), demonstrating his growing influence within the university's political structure.[^19] He also served as University Senator during 2010 and 2011, giving him experience in institutional governance and coalition-building.[^20]

The pivotal moment in Boric's political career came with his election as President of the FECH in 2011, winning with 30.52% of the votes in a highly competitive race.[^21] He defeated Camila Vallejo, the incumbent president seeking re-election as part of the Communist Youth of Chile list, marking a victory for more autonomous left-wing forces outside the traditional Communist Party structure.[^22] This victory positioned him at the forefront of what would become one of Chile's most significant social movements of the 21st century.

As president of the FECH in 2011, Boric emerged as one of the primary spokespersons for massive student protests demanding free education for all and fundamental reforms to Chile's privatized education system.[^23] The 2011 Chilean student movement brought hundreds of thousands of protesters into the streets, shaking Chile's political establishment and forcing a national conversation about inequality and the legacy of Augusto Pinochet's neoliberal economic model implemented during the dictatorship (1973-1990).[^24] Boric's role as a student leader during these historic protests established him as a national figure and gave him a platform that extended far beyond university politics.[^25]

Building on his national prominence, Boric successfully ran for Congress in 2013 as an independent candidate, winning election to the Chamber of Deputies for the Magallanes region.[^26] His victory was significant: he received 15,418 votes (26.2%), the highest number received by any candidate in the region, demonstrating his strong electoral appeal in his home territory.[^27] He was re-elected in 2017, this time as part of the newly formed Broad Front (Frente Amplio) coalition.[^28]

In January 2017, Boric's political movement joined with other new parties and collectives, including Giorgio Jackson's Democratic Revolution party, to launch the Chilean Broad Front, a coalition that sought to offer a left-wing alternative to both the traditional center-left Concertación and the right-wing Chile Vamos coalition.[^29] In 2018, Boric co-founded the Social Convergence party, which became a member of the Broad Front before the coalition later merged into a single political party.[^30]

The October 2019 social uprising in Chile, sparked by metro fare increases but fueled by deeper grievances about inequality, created the political opening for Boric's presidential bid. The protests led to an agreement to hold a referendum on drafting a new constitution, fundamentally reshaping Chile's political landscape.[^31] In July 2021, Boric won the Apruebo Dignidad (Approve Dignity) coalition's presidential primary with over 60% of the vote, defeating Communist Party candidate Daniel Jadue.[^32]

In the November 2021 first round of presidential voting, Boric finished second with 25.8% of votes, trailing far-right candidate José Antonio Kast who received 27.9%.[^33] However, in the December 19, 2021 runoff election, Boric achieved a stunning victory, defeating Kast with 55.9% of the vote—the highest vote percentage in Chilean history and the highest number of total votes ever received by a candidate.[^34] At age 35, he became the youngest president-elect in Chilean history, representing a generational shift in the country's politics.[^35]

## Major Achievements

Upon taking office on March 11, 2022, President Boric assembled the youngest and most gender-diverse cabinet in Chilean history, with women comprising a majority of ministerial positions.[^36] This symbolic gesture reflected his administration's commitment to generational change and gender equality in governance.

One of Boric's most significant economic achievements was increasing Chile's minimum wage by 51% over his term without triggering runaway inflation, a notable accomplishment during a period of global economic uncertainty.[^37] This represented a substantial improvement in living standards for Chile's lowest-paid workers and demonstrated that progressive wage policies could be implemented without destabilizing the economy. Additionally, his administration successfully reduced the standard working week from 45 to 40 hours, a reform long sought by labor movements and one that improved work-life balance for millions of Chilean workers.[^38]

On fiscal management, Boric's government succeeded in lowering public spending after the pandemic-era expansion, helping to control inflation and stabilize public finances.[^39] Chile's GDP grew 2.6% in 2024, exceeding expectations and marking the best result in three years, demonstrating that his administration could maintain economic stability despite political turbulence.[^40] Under his watch, inflation was brought under control after the post-pandemic spike, returning to more manageable levels.[^41]

In the realm of security policy, Boric took decisive action to address rising crime concerns. He increased funding for the Carabineros (national police force) after years of budget cuts following the 2019 protests, and created a new Ministry of Public Security to coordinate anti-crime efforts.[^42] The rate of homicide victims in the first half of 2024 fell by 9.4% year-over-year, suggesting that some of his security measures were beginning to show results.[^43] He also militarized Chile's northern border in an attempt to stem irregular migration and combat transnational criminal organizations operating in the region.[^44]

Boric's administration achieved a significant pension reform that, according to analysts, will have "long-term consequences that improve pensions" for Chilean retirees.[^45] Chile's privatized pension system, established during the Pinochet dictatorship, has been widely criticized for providing inadequate retirement benefits, and Boric's reforms represented an important step toward addressing this structural problem, even if they did not fully transform the system as progressives had hoped.

On the international stage, Boric established himself as a strong voice for progressive values and human rights. His September 2022 speech to the United Nations General Assembly received widespread acclaim, with the young president calling for greater social justice and sustainable growth globally.[^46] He stated: "I invite you to plan ahead in the search for greater social justice to better distribute wealth and power. This should go hand in hand with sustainable growth."[^47]

In a controversial but principled decision, Boric declined to receive the credentials of Israeli Ambassador Gil Artzyeli in September 2022, protesting child casualties during the 2022 Gaza-Israel clashes.[^48] While this triggered a diplomatic crisis between Chile and Israel, it demonstrated Boric's willingness to take moral stands on international issues even at the cost of diplomatic complications. His administration has consistently advocated for Palestinian rights and a two-state solution to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.

Boric also positioned Chile as a leader on climate change and environmental issues, hosting the APEC CEO Summit in 2023 where he emphasized the connection between economic prosperity and environmental sustainability.[^49] He stated: "When economies are doing well, people are doing well" and "Today, maybe more than ever, because of globalization, the destiny of each one of us is intertwined with the destiny of the other."[^50]

His administration made Chile a voice for democratic values in Latin America at a time when authoritarian tendencies were growing in some countries. Boric has been particularly critical of the Nicolás Maduro regime in Venezuela, breaking with some traditional Latin American left allies to take a strong stance against democratic backsliding in the region.[^51]

## Controversies and Criticisms

The most devastating failure of Boric's presidency was the rejection of his proposed new constitution. In September 2022, Chilean voters rejected by over 60% a draft constitution that would have been, according to supporters, the world's most progressive charter.[^52] Critics described the draft as excessively lengthy (388 articles), overtly left-leaning, and overly radical in its provisions, including extensive indigenous rights, environmental protections, and social guarantees that many voters found threatening to economic stability and property rights.[^53] The overwhelming rejection represented a major political defeat for Boric and the left, forcing a reset of the constitutional process.

A second attempt at constitutional reform, this time led by a more conservative constitutional convention, was also rejected by voters in December 2023, creating a political stalemate and effectively ending the constitutional reform process that had begun with such promise following the 2019 protests.[^54] This double failure on the constitution represented the death of Boric's most transformative ambition and marked a significant setback for the Chilean left.

Boric's administration struggled significantly with public security issues. Backlash against police after harsh crackdowns on protesters in 2019 led to funding cuts and officers opting to shirk duties, and crime rates promptly doubled during the early years of his administration.[^55] While Boric increased police funding and created a Ministry of Public Security, many voters blamed him personally for the crime wave that made Chilean cities feel less safe than they had in decades.[^56] Homicide rates, while still low by Latin American standards, soared about 50% since 2018, and organized crime groups—including Venezuela's Tren de Aragua, Colombia's Los Shottas, and the Trinitarios—established operations in Chile for the first time.[^57]

Immigration policy proved another political minefield for Boric. Chile's immigrant population doubled to over 1.6 million under recent governments, an enormous figure for a country of just under 20 million, and around 80% of Chileans wanted greater restrictions on immigration.[^58] Record low deportations under Boric provoked anger from working-class voters who blamed immigrants for job competition and increased crime, though the administration attempted to stem new arrivals by militarizing the country's northern border.[^59] His constitutional reform proposal to permanently assign the armed forces responsibility for border security came late in his term and was seen by critics as too little, too late.[^60]

Economic policy failures plagued the administration. Boric's ambitious tax reform, which sought to fund expanded social services by raising taxes on the wealthy and corporations, failed in Congress and was significantly watered down.[^61] Post-COVID fiscal constraints meant that promises to revitalize social services, education, and healthcare weren't wholly fulfilled, disappointing the social movements that had propelled him to power.[^62] The flagship "gas at a fair price" policy cost the state-owned oil company ten times more than the market price for each cylinder, representing a poorly designed subsidy that wasted public resources.[^63]

After remodeling the way poverty is measured in the country, the latest figures suggested that over 22% of Chileans were living below the poverty line, a statistic that undermined Boric's claims of social progress.[^64] More than two million patients awaited treatment by specialists for basics like eye care and gynecology, with wait times averaging nearly a year, demonstrating the healthcare system's continued failures.[^65]

Boric's popularity plummeted dramatically during his presidency. Just six weeks into office, his disapproval rating jumped more than 30 points, one of the fastest collapses in approval for any Chilean president.[^66] By May 2025, Boric's approval rating declined to its lowest level of his presidency, falling to just 27% approving, with 66% of Chileans disapproving of his governing according to polling from Chile's Center for Public Studies.[^67]

Critics on the left argued that Boric betrayed the transformative promise of his campaign. Jeannette Jara, a former minister in his government, stated: "We were a flash in the pan," suggesting that the Boric administration's progressive moment had passed without achieving fundamental change.[^68] Accusations emerged that Boric had accommodated himself too much to Chile's neoliberal establishment rather than challenging it as he had promised during the campaign.[^69]

His mental health struggles, while humanizing to some voters, raised questions about his fitness for the demanding role of president. Boric has been vocal about his obsessive-compulsive disorder, a condition he was diagnosed with as a child, and in 2018 he took a leave of absence from Congress after being hospitalized due to his condition.[^70] While his openness about mental health challenged stigma, critics questioned whether someone with such challenges should hold the nation's highest office during times of crisis.

## Personal Life

Gabriel Boric's personal life has been marked by intellectual curiosity, mental health advocacy, and evolving romantic relationships that have occasionally intersected with his public role. He identifies as agnostic, a departure from the devout Catholicism of his family upbringing, particularly his mother's involvement in the Schoenstatt Apostolic Movement.[^71] This religious evolution reflects broader secularization trends among younger generations in Chile.

Boric has been remarkably open about his struggles with obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), a mental health condition he was diagnosed with as a child.[^72] His willingness to discuss his OCD publicly, including his 2018 hospitalization that required a leave of absence from Congress, has been praised by mental health advocates as helping to reduce stigma around psychological conditions in Latin American political culture, where such admissions are traditionally seen as signs of weakness.[^73] His advocacy for mental health awareness has been consistent throughout his political career, making him an important role model for others struggling with similar conditions.

Between 2019 and 2023, Boric was in a relationship with Irina Karamanos, an anthropologist and sociologist who became a public figure herself during his presidency.[^74] Karamanos took on an unprecedented role as "First Lady," though she rejected traditional ceremonial functions associated with that position and instead focused on feminist and social issues. Their relationship represented a modern partnership that challenged traditional gender roles in Chilean politics. In November 2023, Boric and Karamanos announced the end of their relationship, conducting their separation with dignity and mutual respect.[^75]

Following his separation from Karamanos, Boric began a relationship with Paula Carrasco, an environmental chemist and former national team basketball player.[^76] Their relationship was made public in September 2024, and on June 25, 2025, their daughter Violeta Boric Carrasco was born.[^77] This birth was historic: Violeta became the first child ever born to a sitting Chilean president since 1928-1930, making Boric the first president to become a father while in office in nearly a century.[^78] The birth brought a humanizing dimension to Boric's public image during a politically difficult period of his presidency.

Boric's social media presence, particularly on Twitter, reflects his intellectual interests and political philosophy. He has quoted philosophers like Albert Camus, with his Twitter account featuring: "Doubt must follow conviction like a shadow," a sentiment that captures his self-reflective approach to politics.[^79] His informal communication style and willingness to engage directly with citizens online has been both praised as authentic and criticized as unpresidential.

Friends and colleagues describe Boric as an avid reader with interests spanning political theory, history, and literature. His speeches often reference literary and philosophical sources, demonstrating an intellectual depth uncommon among contemporary politicians. He has maintained friendships from his student activist days, and his inner circle includes many former student leaders who have transitioned into professional politics alongside him.

## Legacy

Gabriel Boric's legacy remains contested and will likely be debated for years as historians assess whether he represented a genuine transformative moment or merely a brief progressive interlude in Chile's political evolution. Academic assessments conducted near the end of his term have been sobering: "Boric is not leaving a remarkable legacy. Since Chile's re-democratisation in 1990, his government has arguably enacted the fewest reforms and his government has ended up preserving much of the status quo."[^80]

The most consequential aspect of Boric's presidency may be what it revealed about the limits of progressive change within Chile's institutional framework. His campaign promise to "bury neoliberalism" in Chile—the country where the neoliberal model was first extensively implemented under Pinochet—went largely unfulfilled.[^81] The failure of two constitutional reform processes marked the definitive end of the transformative moment that began with the 2019 social uprising, suggesting that Chilean society, while desiring change, was unwilling to embrace the radical restructuring proposed by the left.

However, Boric's presidency did achieve concrete improvements in workers' lives through the minimum wage increase and reduced working week, reforms that will benefit millions of Chileans for years to come.[^82] His pension reform, while not revolutionary, established a foundation for future improvements to Chile's inadequate retirement system.[^83] These incremental achievements, while disappointing to those who hoped for transformation, represent tangible progress on economic justice issues.

Boric's impact on Chilean political culture is significant. He demonstrated that millennial politicians from social movements could win power through democratic means, potentially inspiring future generations of progressive leaders.[^84] His openness about mental health struggles helped normalize conversations about psychological wellbeing in a political culture that traditionally valorized machismo and stoicism. His commitment to gender equality in cabinet appointments set new standards for representation in Chilean governance.

On the international stage, Boric positioned Chile as a voice for progressive values, human rights, and democratic governance in Latin America at a critical moment.[^85] His willingness to criticize authoritarian regimes regardless of their ideological orientation—including both far-right governments and left-wing autocracies like Venezuela—established a model of principled foreign policy that transcended traditional left-right divides. His advocacy for Palestinian rights and climate action gave Chile a higher profile on global issues than its size might otherwise warrant.

The 2025 Chilean elections, which saw right-wing candidate José Antonio Kast defeat the left-wing coalition, suggested that Boric's progressive movement had limited lasting political impact.[^86] However, analysts note that "Boric's coming of age is giving way to a likely presidential contest between moderates rather than the political extremes on the left and the right that his administration helped to discredit."[^87] In this interpretation, Boric's presidency, despite its failures, helped Chilean politics move away from polarization toward more centrist governance.

Boric's unlikely legacy may be that of a transitional figure who represented youthful aspirations for change while ultimately demonstrating the resilience of Chile's existing economic and political structures. His presidency marked both the high point of post-2019 progressive mobilization and its exhaustion, closing a chapter of Chilean history rather than opening a new one. Whether future generations will judge this as a necessary period of experimentation or a wasted opportunity remains an open question.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Supporters argue that Boric faced extraordinary challenges that would have overwhelmed any president, including post-pandemic economic disruption, a global inflation crisis, rising crime driven by transnational criminal organizations, and unprecedented migration flows.[^88] Given these headwinds, his achievements in maintaining economic growth, controlling inflation, and improving workers' wages and hours represent significant accomplishments that should be recognized.[^89]

Progressive scholars emphasize that Boric's commitment to democratic norms and institutions, even when they constrained his agenda, represents an important model for left-wing governance in Latin America.[^90] Unlike some leftist leaders who have undermined democratic institutions when faced with opposition, Boric consistently respected constitutional limits, congressional authority, and electoral verdicts, even when they went against him. This democratic temperament, supporters argue, is his most important legacy.

Boric's defenders note that his presidency fundamentally changed conversations about inequality, worker rights, and social justice in Chile, even if concrete policy changes were limited.[^91] The mere fact that a former student protest leader could become president and implement significant labor reforms demonstrates the expansion of political possibility in Chilean democracy. His administration showed that outsider candidates from social movements could govern responsibly, potentially opening doors for future progressive leaders.

Feminist scholars praise Boric's gender-balanced cabinet and his partnership with politically engaged women like Irina Karamanos as representing a more egalitarian model of political leadership.[^92] His willingness to challenge traditional machismo in Chilean politics, combined with his mental health advocacy, offered a different vision of leadership that prioritized vulnerability and equality over traditional masculine strength.

International relations experts commend Boric's principled foreign policy, particularly his willingness to criticize human rights abuses regardless of ideological alignment.[^93] His stance on Palestine, Venezuela, and climate change demonstrated that small countries can exercise moral leadership on global issues, and his speeches at international forums like the UN General Assembly articulated a compelling vision of social justice that resonated far beyond Chile's borders.

### Negative Views

Critics on the left view Boric as a tragic failure who betrayed the social movements that brought him to power. Jeannette Jara's characterization of the Boric government as "a flash in the pan" captures the disappointment of progressives who believed his election would fundamentally transform Chilean society.[^94] These critics argue that Boric too quickly accommodated the business elite, abandoned transformative economic policies, and governed as a moderate rather than the revolutionary change agent he had promised to be.[^95]

Conservative critics maintain that Boric's leftist ideology, even in its diluted form, damaged Chile's economy, encouraged crime, and undermined social cohesion.[^96] They argue that his initial support for the radically progressive 2022 constitutional draft demonstrated dangerous political judgment and a willingness to impose an ideological agenda that most Chileans rejected. His immigration policies, they contend, showed naive multiculturalism that ignored working-class Chileans' legitimate concerns about rapid demographic change and its economic impacts.[^97]

Security analysts fault Boric for his slow response to rising crime and his initial reluctance to support police forces following the 2019 protests.[^98] By the time he increased police funding and created the Ministry of Public Security, crime had already surged to levels that frightened Chilean citizens and dominated political discourse. His constitutional reform proposal giving the military permanent border security responsibilities came too late to prevent the establishment of transnational criminal organizations in Chile.[^99]

Economic critics argue that Boric's administration demonstrated the persistent failure of left-wing economic policies to deliver prosperity. Despite his promise to bury neoliberalism, his term is on track to have the lowest rate of GDP growth since Chile's return to democracy at 1.8%, and poverty affects over 22% of Chileans.[^100] His failed tax reform and costly subsidy programs like the "gas at a fair price" policy showed poor economic management that wasted public resources without achieving transformative goals.[^101]

Political scientists note that Boric's presidency may have actually strengthened the neoliberal order he sought to transform by demonstrating the electoral and institutional barriers to fundamental change.[^102] The two failed constitutional referendums, rather than being mere setbacks, may have permanently closed the window for constitutional transformation that opened in 2019. In this view, Boric's failures inoculated Chile against future progressive challenges to the existing order by exhausting the appetite for radical change.

Some observers criticize Boric's leadership style as insufficiently pragmatic and too focused on symbolic gestures rather than achievable reforms.[^103] His gender-balanced cabinet, while symbolically important, did not translate into effective governance, and his government was marked by ministerial instability and policy inconsistency. His rhetorical skills, which served him well as a student leader and opposition politician, proved insufficient for the executive function of actually running a complex modern state.
