## Summary

Iraqi terrorist who succeeded al-Baghdadi as leader of ISIS. He detonated a suicide vest during a US special forces raid in Idlib, Syria, killing himself and family members. He had overseen the Yazidi genocide.

## Overview

Abu Ibrahim al-Hashimi al-Qurayshi (also reported under the name Amir Muhammad Sa'id Abdal-Rahman al-Mawla) was the successor to Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi as leader of the Islamic State (ISIS). Public reporting around his identity relied on intelligence assessments and investigative journalism, and many details about his background remained opaque during his tenure.

## Leadership of ISIS (2019–2022)

As ISIS leader, he presided over a period in which the organization had lost most of its territorial holdings but continued to operate as an insurgent and terrorist network. ISIS-affiliated cells remained active across parts of Iraq and Syria, and the group continued to claim attacks and to direct or inspire violence through propaganda channels.

At the strategic level, his leadership is often described as focused on maintaining cohesion after the collapse of the so-called caliphate, sustaining recruitment and financing networks, and leveraging regional affiliates outside the core Iraq/Syria theater.

## Death and U.S. raid in Syria (2022)

He died during a U.S. special operations raid in the Idlib governorate of Syria. According to widely reported accounts, he detonated an explosive device during the operation, resulting in his own death and the deaths of others in the building.

The raid was publicly framed as part of U.S. efforts to target ISIS leadership and disrupt the group’s operational capability. Reporting also emphasized the difficulty of assessing civilian harm in such operations and the limited independent verification available from the site immediately afterward.

## Legacy and notes

- He is commonly listed among the most senior ISIS leaders after al-Baghdadi.
- Many biographical details (education, early organizational roles, and the precise sequence of his rise) have been debated in open sources.
- His tenure is typically characterized as a continuation of ISIS’s transition from territorial control to dispersed clandestine activity.
- Open-source accounts often note the gap between public claims about leadership successions and the limited documentation available to corroborate them.
