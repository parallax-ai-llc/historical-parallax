## Summary

French revolutionary who carried out the mass execution of 1,900 people during the Siege of Lyon using cannon fire ('mitraillades'). Later became Napoleon's feared chief of secret police.

## Early life and revolutionary politics

- Fouché entered public life during the French Revolution and aligned with radical republican currents at different points in the conflict.
- He served as a political representative and became associated with hardline measures used to suppress perceived counterrevolutionary threats.

## Lyon repression

- In 1793, after the revolt and siege of Lyon, revolutionary authorities undertook severe reprisals.
- Fouché is often cited in historical accounts for his role in punitive actions, including mass executions carried out as part of the broader “Terror” politics of the period.

## Minister of Police under Napoleon

- Under Napoleon Bonaparte, Fouché became Minister of Police and built a reputation for surveillance, intelligence gathering, and political maneuvering.
- His tenure is frequently discussed as an early example of a modern-style internal security apparatus operating to protect a regime from coups, conspiracies, and dissent.

## Reputation and historical assessments

- Historians have portrayed Fouché as a highly pragmatic operator who survived multiple regime changes by shifting alliances and prioritizing institutional power.
- His legacy is contested: he is remembered both for revolutionary-era repression and for shaping the tools and norms of state security in post-revolutionary France.

## Later life

- After Napoleon’s fall, Fouché’s political position deteriorated and he spent his final years in exile.
