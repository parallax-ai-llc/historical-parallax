## Summary

First President of Madagascar whose neo-colonial government enriched a small elite while the majority remained impoverished. Forced to resign amid social unrest.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1960 | Became first president |

## Background

- Tsiranana rose to prominence during the final years of French colonial rule.
- After independence, Madagascar retained strong political, military, and economic ties with France, shaping early state-building and elite networks.

## Governance and Political System

- His administration emphasized political stability and continuity with existing institutions.
- Critics described the system as overly centralized and dependent on patronage, with limited avenues for opposition.
- In practice, government posts and state-linked economic opportunities were often concentrated among a narrow circle close to power.

## Economy and Social Outcomes

- Madagascar remained heavily dependent on primary commodities and external financing.
- Growth and state investment did not translate evenly into improved living standards, contributing to inequality between urban elites and rural populations.
- Corruption allegations focused on the use of public office for private enrichment and the protection of connected business interests.

## Crisis and Resignation

- By the early 1970s, discontent grew over education policy, economic hardship, and perceptions of neo-colonial influence.
- Large demonstrations and unrest escalated into a national political crisis.
- Under pressure and amid a breakdown in public order, Tsiranana stepped down and transferred authority, marking the end of the First Republic.

## Legacy (High-Level)

- Supporters credit him with navigating an early post-independence transition without immediate state collapse.
- Detractors emphasize the entrenchment of elite privilege, the persistence of poverty, and the long-term political instability that followed.
