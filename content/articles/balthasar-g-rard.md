## Summary

French Catholic who assassinated William the Silent, Prince of Orange, on July 10, 1584—the first assassination of a head of state with a handgun. He was brutally tortured and executed in Delft.

## Additional Context

### Background: the Dutch Revolt

Gérard’s assassination of William the Silent took place during the Dutch Revolt (Eighty Years’ War), a prolonged conflict in which parts of the Low Countries rebelled against Habsburg Spanish rule. William of Orange had become a leading political and military figure for the revolt and a symbolic rallying point for the rebel provinces.

### Motives and incentives

Contemporary accounts portray Gérard as a committed Catholic who viewed William as a heretic and traitor. The Spanish crown had also issued proclamations treating William as an outlaw and offering rewards for his capture or killing, which contributed to an environment in which assassination could be framed as both religiously and politically justified.

### The attack

The killing is often noted for the use of a handheld firearm (a pistol) against a prominent political leader, illustrating the increasing accessibility and lethality of gunpowder weapons in targeted violence.

### Aftermath and punishment

Authorities captured Gérard shortly after the attack. Reports of his interrogation, torture, and execution are frequently cited in early modern European history as an extreme example of punitive spectacle intended to deter political violence.

### Historical significance

The event had major political consequences for the rebel cause and for succession and governance in the emerging Dutch polity. In broader historical writing, the assassination is discussed in relation to:

- the role of confessional conflict in political legitimacy
- the evolution of state security and protection of leaders
- the relationship between propaganda, martyr narratives, and political violence
