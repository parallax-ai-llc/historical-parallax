## Summary

London has become a global hub for money laundering through real estate, luxury goods, and professional services. An estimated £100 billion of dirty money flows through the UK annually.

## What the term refers to

- "London Laundromat" is a journalistic shorthand for mechanisms used to **move, disguise, and store illicit wealth** in the UK, especially via London-linked markets.
- The phrase is typically discussed alongside related ideas such as "dirty money" in property, shell companies, and professional enablers.

## Commonly cited channels

- **Real estate:** high-value property purchases through offshore companies and trusts can obscure beneficial ownership.
- **Corporate vehicles:** rapid company formation and limited verification can be exploited to create layers of ownership.
- **Professional services:** lawyers, accountants, and company service providers can be used to structure transactions (legally or illegally), making enforcement complex.
- **Luxury assets:** art, jewelry, and other portable stores of value are often cited as risk areas in anti-money-laundering (AML) discussions.

## Estimates and measurement limits

- The figure of **£100 billion** is often quoted in public debate as an estimate of the scale of money laundering affecting the UK, but estimates vary and are sensitive to methodology.
- Illicit finance is difficult to measure directly because it is intentionally hidden and frequently crosses borders.

## Policy and enforcement responses

- The **Criminal Finances Act 2017** (sometimes nicknamed the "McMafia" law) introduced tools such as **Unexplained Wealth Orders (UWOs)**.
- After Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine, scrutiny of sanctions compliance and ownership transparency increased.
- The UK established a **Register of Overseas Entities** for property ownership transparency through 2022 economic crime legislation.

## Why it matters

- Concerns include corruption proceeds being parked in stable jurisdictions, distortion of local property markets, and weakened trust in institutions.
- The issue is often framed as both a national security problem and a governance problem, not only a financial-crime problem.
