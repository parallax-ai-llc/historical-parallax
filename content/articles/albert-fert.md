## Summary

Albert Fert is a French physicist who shared the 2007 Nobel Prize in Physics for the discovery of giant magnetoresistance.

## Scientific contribution

Fert is best known for research that led to the discovery of giant magnetoresistance (GMR), a phenomenon in which the electrical resistance of certain layered materials changes significantly in response to a magnetic field. The effect arises in nanoscale structures (often thin films) where electron spin and scattering behavior can differ depending on magnetic alignment.

## Why GMR mattered

GMR became foundational for "spintronics"—electronics that leverages electron spin in addition to charge. In practical terms, GMR enabled far more sensitive magnetic read heads in hard disk drives, which helped increase data storage density and reduced cost per gigabyte over time.

## Nobel Prize context

The 2007 Nobel Prize in Physics recognized the discovery and characterization of GMR, shared by Fert and Peter Grünberg. Their work is frequently cited as a case where condensed matter physics translated into a widely deployed technology within a relatively short time.

## Legacy

Beyond a single discovery, Fert’s career is often discussed in the context of European research institutions and the broader shift toward nanoscience and materials engineering in late-20th-century physics.
