## Summary

Roman senator who organized a conspiracy to overthrow the Republic through armed insurrection. Plot exposed by Cicero; died in battle.

## Historical Context

- Catiline (Lucius Sergius Catilina) operated during the late Roman Republic, a period marked by intense elite competition, debt crises, and recurring political violence.
- Roman politics at the time mixed formal institutions (elections, Senate debate) with factional patronage and occasional extralegal force.

## The Catilinarian Conspiracy (High-Level)

- The alleged conspiracy is remembered as an attempted uprising involving recruitment of supporters, plans for coordinated action, and the prospect of armed confrontation.
- The plot was publicly denounced by **Cicero**, then consul, in a series of speeches that became famous as the "Catilinarian Orations."

## Aftermath

- Catiline left Rome and joined forces in the field; the confrontation culminated in a battle where he was killed.
- The episode became a lasting example in Roman political memory of how the Republic responded to perceived internal threats.

## Sources and Interpretation

- Much of what is known comes through hostile or politically interested sources, especially Cicero and later historians.
- Because of this, modern summaries often distinguish between (a) what is firmly attested and (b) what may reflect rhetorical framing or partisan exaggeration.

## Legacy

- Catiline’s name is frequently used as a symbol of sedition or revolutionary plotting in later political writing.
- The controversy around the case also feeds broader debates about emergency powers, due process, and political legitimacy.
