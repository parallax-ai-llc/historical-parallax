## Summary

State-organized forced labor system in Uzbekistans cotton industry that forced over a million citizens including children to pick cotton annually. International boycotts pressured reforms.

## Key Details

### How the system worked

Uzbek cotton production was long managed through state planning and procurement targets. Local officials, schools, and public-sector workplaces were pressured to supply pickers to meet quotas, with penalties for noncompliance. Reports described a cycle in which district authorities mobilized teachers, medical workers, students, and other citizens for seasonal field work, often under coercive conditions.

### International pressure and monitoring

Civil-society documentation and media coverage led to sustained international scrutiny, including campaigns urging apparel brands and retailers to avoid Uzbek cotton sourced with forced labor. The International Labour Organization (ILO) conducted monitoring in cooperation with the Uzbek government, tracking the prevalence of child labor and later broader forms of forced labor during harvest seasons.

### Reform trajectory

Reforms were reported as a multi-year process that included formal prohibitions, changes in recruitment practices, and greater space for monitoring. The ILO later reported that systematic forced labor in the cotton harvest had ended, while observers continued to emphasize the need for durable accountability, labor rights protections, and transparency in agricultural supply chains.

## Legacy and Significance

The Uzbek cotton case is frequently cited in discussions of state-linked forced labor, responsible sourcing, and how brand-level purchasing decisions can interact with international labor standards. It also illustrates how monitoring methodologies and repeated harvest-season assessments can be used to measure policy change over time.

### Ongoing considerations

Analyses of the post-reform period often focus on whether local quota pressure has been fully eliminated, how complaints mechanisms function for seasonal workers, and whether independent monitoring remains consistent over time.
