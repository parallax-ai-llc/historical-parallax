## Context

Antoine-Henri Becquerel (often referenced as Henri Becquerel) was part of a multigenerational French scientific family and worked in the late-19th-century transition from classical physics to modern atomic science.

## Notability

Becquerel is best known for discovering evidence of spontaneous radioactivity in uranium salts in 1896, providing a key experimental starting point for subsequent work by Marie and Pierre Curie and for early atomic physics.

## What He Discovered (High-Level)

- Certain uranium compounds could expose photographic plates even without sunlight or external excitation.
- The effect suggested a natural emission from matter itself, not merely an induced phenomenon.

## Recognition and Legacy

- Shared the 1903 Nobel Prize in Physics with Pierre Curie and Marie Curie for work connected to the discovery and investigation of radioactivity.
- Becquerel’s findings helped shift scientific attention toward the internal structure of atoms and the physics of radiation.
