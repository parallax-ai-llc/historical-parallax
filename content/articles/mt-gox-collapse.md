## Summary

Collapse of Mt. Gox, the worlds largest bitcoin exchange, in February 2014 after 850,000 bitcoins worth $450 million were stolen by hackers. It was the biggest crypto theft at the time.

## Background

Mt. Gox began as a site for trading game cards and later became a major Bitcoin exchange, handling a large share of global Bitcoin trading volume at its peak. Its rapid growth occurred during an early phase of cryptocurrency market infrastructure.

## Losses and Bankruptcy

In early 2014 Mt. Gox halted withdrawals and soon filed for bankruptcy protection in Japan. The company reported that a large number of bitcoins were missing, and the incident became a landmark failure for the crypto ecosystem.

## Recovery and Legal Proceedings

After the collapse, legal processes in Japan addressed both creditor claims and the handling of remaining assets. Over time, additional bitcoins were reported as found in old wallets, and creditors pursued repayment through court-supervised processes including civil rehabilitation.

## Industry Impact

The Mt. Gox collapse accelerated efforts to professionalize security, custody, auditing, and regulatory oversight for exchanges. It is frequently referenced in discussions about exchange risk, proof-of-reserves practices, and the dangers of concentrated custodial control.

## Lessons Commonly Cited

Observers often highlight operational security, internal controls, and transparent accounting as key weaknesses revealed by the case, shaping expectations for later crypto platforms.
