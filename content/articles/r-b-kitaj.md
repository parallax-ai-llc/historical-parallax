## Summary

American-British painter whose complex, literary figurative works explored Jewish identity, exile, and political engagement. He coined the term School of London for figurative painters like Freud and Bacon.

## Artistic Style

Kitaj is often placed within postwar figurative painting, but his approach was distinctive for how densely it layered references. His canvases frequently combine:

- Figurative scenes with deliberately fragmented composition
- Allusions to literature, philosophy, and art history
- Notes, quotations, and explanatory text attached to the work's meaning

This "essay-like" quality made his paintings feel closer to visual arguments than purely representational images.

## Themes

A recurring subject in Kitaj's work is diaspora and Jewish identity, treated not as a single narrative but as a set of historical pressures: exile, assimilation, persecution, and cultural memory. He also addressed contemporary politics and the moral responsibilities of intellectual life.

## The "School of London"

Kitaj promoted the label "School of London" to describe a loose network of artists committed to figurative painting in an era dominated by abstraction and conceptual art. In this framing, painters such as Lucian Freud and Francis Bacon represented a continuing seriousness about the human figure and psychological presence.

## Writing and Self-Interpretation

Unlike many painters, Kitaj produced substantial written commentary around his art. These texts helped audiences navigate the dense references, but they also contributed to debate about whether the works should be read through the artist's stated intentions or as autonomous images.

## Later Life and Reputation

Kitaj's reputation has been shaped by both admiration and controversy, including strong critical reactions to some exhibitions. Over time, scholarship has continued to re-evaluate his contribution to postwar figurative painting and the way his art connected personal identity to intellectual history.
