## Summary

Malcolm X was assassinated by Nation of Islam members while giving a speech in Manhattan. In 2021, two of the three convicted men were exonerated after evidence of FBI and NYPD misconduct emerged.

## Background

Malcolm X was a prominent Black nationalist and civil rights figure whose public role evolved significantly during the early 1960s. In the period leading up to his death, he had split from the Nation of Islam (NOI) and was building new organizations and political relationships.

## The assassination

The killing took place on 21 February 1965 at the Audubon Ballroom in Manhattan (New York City) during a public address. The event was widely reported and quickly became a focal point for debates about political violence, surveillance, and conflict within movements.

## Investigation and prosecutions

Several men were arrested and convicted in connection with the shooting. Over time, historians, journalists, and community advocates questioned aspects of the investigation and the handling of evidence.

## 2021 exonerations and institutional review

In 2021, a court vacated the convictions of two of the three men previously convicted, with public reporting emphasizing the late disclosure of information and criticism of investigative practices. The episode has been used as a case study in wrongful convictions, disclosure obligations, and the impact of state surveillance on political activism.

## Legacy

Malcolm X's assassination remains a defining moment in the history of the U.S. civil rights era. The continuing reassessment of the case has also shaped public understanding of the era's movement politics and law-enforcement activity.
