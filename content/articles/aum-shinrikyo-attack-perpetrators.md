## Background and Scope

The term "Aum Shinrikyo attack perpetrators" is commonly used to refer to individuals associated with Aum Shinrikyo who planned, supported, or directly carried out the group’s major acts of violence, especially the March 1995 Tokyo subway sarin attack.[^1]

Aum Shinrikyo (later known as Aleph) was a Japanese religious movement led by Shoko Asahara. By the early 1990s it had attracted followers in Japan and abroad and developed clandestine capabilities including chemical weapons production.[^2]

## Key Events and Aftermath

- **March 1995:** Coordinated release of sarin on multiple Tokyo subway lines during morning rush hour, killing 13 people and injuring thousands.[^2]
- **1995 onward:** Large-scale police raids and arrests targeting senior leadership and operational members.
- **2000s–2010s:** Trials and appeals continued for years, reflecting the scale of evidence and number of defendants.
- **2018:** Shoko Asahara and a group of senior members were executed after final appeals were exhausted.[^3]

## Legal Proceedings (High Level)

Japanese courts prosecuted Aum Shinrikyo members under a range of charges including murder, attempted murder, and violations related to the manufacture and use of chemical agents. The long judicial process resulted in multiple death sentences and long prison terms for other participants and supporters.[^3]

## Legacy

The Tokyo subway sarin attack became a defining case study in modern domestic terrorism and chemical weapons use. It prompted significant changes in Japanese security policy, emergency response planning, and monitoring of dangerous groups.
