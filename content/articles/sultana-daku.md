## Summary

Notorious colonial-era dacoit who terrorized the United Provinces. Captured and hanged by British authorities.

## Historical Context (High Level)

“Dacoity” refers to armed banditry/robbery carried out by gangs, a phenomenon that colonial administrators often treated as both a criminal and security problem. In parts of North India during the early 20th century, rural poverty, weak local policing capacity, and rugged terrain contributed to persistent bandit activity.

## Colonial Policing and Enforcement

British-era responses to banditry included specialized police operations, intelligence gathering through local informants, and high-visibility prosecutions meant to deter similar groups. Records from the period can be shaped by colonial narratives and administrative priorities, so later historians sometimes compare official accounts with oral histories and regional sources.

## Reputation and Cultural Memory

Figures labeled as “dacoits” in colonial archives sometimes became folk characters in later retellings, with portrayals ranging from ruthless criminal to romanticized outlaw. The specifics of Sultana’s life are often summarized through a small number of sources, which can contribute to mythologizing.

## Depictions

Colonial-era bandit figures have appeared in novels, films, and television, often emphasizing adventure and pursuit. Such depictions can blur the line between documented events and dramatization, so they are best treated as cultural artifacts rather than primary evidence.

## Terms (Quick Glossary)

- **Dacoit / dacoity**: A term used in South Asia for gang robbery, often involving weapons.
- **United Provinces**: A British administrative region broadly corresponding to parts of present-day Uttar Pradesh.
- **Colonial archive**: Official records created by a colonial administration, with inherent biases in perspective.

When available, cross-checking official accounts with local histories helps reduce single-source distortion.
