## Summary

Takata's defective airbag inflators could explode and shoot metal shrapnel at vehicle occupants. At least 27 deaths worldwide; the largest automotive recall in history affecting 100 million vehicles.

## Additional Context

### What failed (high level)

The scandal centered on airbag inflators that could rupture during deployment. In a rupture event, the metal inflator housing could break apart and project fragments into the passenger compartment. The problem was associated with certain inflator designs and propellant behavior over time.

### Scale and recall complexity

Takata-related recalls expanded over multiple years and across many automakers and models. The logistical challenge included identifying affected vehicle populations, sourcing replacement parts, prioritizing higher-risk inflators, and communicating recall actions to owners in different regulatory jurisdictions.

### Regulatory and legal outcomes

The crisis prompted investigations by regulators, civil litigation, and corporate restructuring. It is frequently cited in discussions of supply-chain risk, quality assurance, and the difficulty of managing safety-critical components that are produced at massive scale and embedded across many product lines.

## Broader lessons

- Safety-critical defects can propagate widely when a single supplier is deeply integrated across an industry.
- Long-tail failure modes (degradation over time, environmental exposure) can make detection and remediation harder.
- Transparent reporting and rapid corrective action are central to limiting harm once a systemic defect is identified.
