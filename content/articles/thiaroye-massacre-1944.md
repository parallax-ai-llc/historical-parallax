## Summary

French forces massacred West African colonial soldiers (tirailleurs sénégalais) at Thiaroye camp near Dakar who were demanding their unpaid wages. At least 35-300 were killed.

## Background

Thiaroye occurred in the context of French colonial rule in West Africa and the uneven treatment of colonial troops compared with metropolitan French soldiers. Many of the men involved had served in wartime units and expected wages, bonuses, and discharge payments to be honored upon return.

## What Triggered the Confrontation (High-Level)

Accounts commonly describe a dispute over demobilization pay, arrears, and conditions of repatriation. The soldiers pressed for what they believed they were owed; French authorities treated the protest as indiscipline and escalated the response rather than negotiating a settlement.

## Death Toll and Historical Dispute

The number of people killed is not settled in popular summaries, and figures reported in secondary sources can range from the officially acknowledged minimum to much higher estimates. The uncertainty reflects contested records, colonial-era reporting, and later political sensitivities.

## Legacy

Thiaroye is often referenced in scholarship and public memory as a case study in colonial military exploitation and postwar demobilization failures. It has also been represented in cultural works, including Ousmane Sembène’s film *Camp de Thiaroye* (1988), which helped bring wider attention to the event.
