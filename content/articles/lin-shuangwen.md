## Summary

Lin Shuangwen (1756-1788) was a Chinese immigrant from Fujian Province who led the largest armed rebellion in Taiwan during the Qing dynasty's rule. As leader of the Tiandihui (Heaven and Earth Society), he organized a massive uprising in 1787 that drew as many as 300,000 participants and occupied most of southern Taiwan for over a year. The rebellion, sparked by the Qing government's suppression of secret societies and arrest of Lin's relatives, became one of Emperor Qianlong's celebrated "Ten Great Campaigns" after its eventual suppression. Lin was captured in February 1788 and executed in Beijing that April, marking the end of the costliest rebellion in Qing-era Taiwan.[^1][^2]

---

## Early Life and Background

Lin Shuangwen was born in 1756 in Zhangping, Fujian Province (also referred to as Fukian), China, into a family that would later migrate to Taiwan seeking better economic opportunities.[^3][^4] His ancestral home was in the Zhangzhou region of Fujian, an area known for producing many emigrants to Taiwan during the Qing period. The Zhangzhou people formed one of the major Han Chinese subethnic groups in Taiwan, alongside the Quanzhou people and Hakka communities, and these divisions would later play a crucial role in Lin's rebellion.[^5]

In 1773, when Lin was seventeen years old, he immigrated to central Taiwan with his father, settling in what is now Taichung County.[^6][^7] This migration occurred during a complex period of Qing immigration policy toward Taiwan. The Qing government maintained strict restrictions on Chinese immigration to Taiwan, periodically banning families from crossing the Taiwan Strait. These policies were intended to prevent the island from becoming a refuge for undesirables and potential rebels, but they were largely unenforceable and created a population imbalance with far more men than women.[^8]

Taiwan during Lin's youth was a frontier society characterized by lawlessness, ethnic tensions, and weak government authority. The common saying "every three years an uprising, every five years a rebellion" (三年一反、五年一亂) captured the chronic instability that plagued the island under Qing rule.[^9][^10] Armed conflicts between different settler groups—Zhangzhou versus Quanzhou people, Han Chinese versus indigenous peoples, and various clan feuds—were regular occurrences. In this environment, secret societies and mutual aid organizations became essential for survival and protection.

Lin's early life in Taiwan remains poorly documented in historical records, but we know he worked as an incense maker, a humble occupation that provided him modest means.[^11] What transformed his trajectory was his involvement with the Tiandihui, also known as the Heaven and Earth Society. In 1784, Lin became the leader of the local chapter of this secret society in central Taiwan.[^12] The Tiandihui had allegedly been founded by Ming loyalists and was rumored to have connections to the legendary Zheng Chenggong (Koxinga), who had driven the Dutch from Taiwan in the 1660s.[^13]

However, by Lin's time, the Tiandihui had largely evolved from its anti-Qing political origins into primarily a mutual aid society. The organization drew members from the poorest social classes—people living in precarious conditions who sought economic mutual support and the sense of belonging to a protective group.[^14] In the lawless frontier society of Taiwan, where official government protection was minimal and blood feuds were common, such societies provided essential social networks for survival. Lin's rise to leadership within the Tiandihui demonstrated his organizational abilities and standing within the Zhangzhou immigrant community, setting the stage for the events that would make him a central figure in Taiwan's history.

## Rise to Power

Lin Shuangwen's transformation from a humble incense maker to the leader of Taiwan's largest rebellion began with escalating tensions between the Qing government and secret societies in 1786. The Tiandihui, despite having evolved into primarily a mutual aid organization, remained suspicious in the eyes of Qing officials who feared any organization that might harbor anti-Manchu sentiment or challenge imperial authority.[^15]

The immediate catalyst for Lin's uprising came when Sun Jingsui, the Qing-appointed Governor of Taiwan, discovered the existence of the Tiandihui and moved to suppress it in 1786.[^16] Sun ordered a crackdown on the society and arrested several of its members, including Lin Shuangwen's uncles and possibly his father.[^17][^18] This action struck at the heart of Lin's family and his social network, leaving him with a stark choice: submit to Qing authority and accept the destruction of his organization, or resist.

Lin chose resistance. He gathered other Tiandihui leaders, including Zhuang Datian, who would become his chief lieutenant throughout the rebellion.[^19] Together they organized the society members to launch a revolt, initially with the stated goal of freeing Lin's imprisoned relatives.[^20] The Tiandihui members mobilized quickly, drawing on their networks throughout central Taiwan and appealing to Ming loyalist sentiment that still lingered among some Han settlers who resented Manchu rule.

On January 16, 1787, Lin made his decisive move by murdering Governor Sun Jingsui and other Qing officials.[^21] This bold assassination of the highest Qing authority on the island demonstrated Lin's commitment to open rebellion and eliminated the government's leadership at a critical moment. The act also served as a rallying cry for disaffected elements throughout Taiwan.

The rebellion spread with remarkable speed. The number of insurgents quickly rose to 50,000 active fighters, with estimates suggesting that as many as 300,000 people participated in the rebellion in various capacities.[^22][^23] By February 1787, less than a month after the initial uprising, the rebels had occupied almost all of southern Taiwan except for Zhuluo County (later renamed Jiayi) and the port town of Lukang.[^24] Lin and Zhuang's forces successfully attacked multiple cities including Changhua, Fengshan, Tainan, and others, killing Qing officials and sympathizers.[^25]

The rebellion's rapid success stemmed from multiple factors. First, the Qing government maintained only a minimal military presence in Taiwan, viewing it as a peripheral possession of limited strategic value. Second, the rebellion tapped into genuine grievances: corrupt officials, heavy taxation, ethnic tensions, and the heavy-handed suppression of community organizations that provided essential social support. Third, the rebellion activated existing Zhangzhou networks throughout the island, as Lin's forces were predominantly composed of Zhangzhou people.[^26]

However, this ethnic dimension also created complications for the rebellion. The Zhangzhou and Quanzhou communities had been feuding with each other for years, with a major violent conflict occurring in 1782 just five years before Lin's uprising.[^27] When Lin's predominantly Zhangzhou forces began their rebellion, many Quanzhou settlers saw this not just as an anti-Qing uprising but as an extension of their ongoing ethnic conflict with the Zhangzhou people. Consequently, Quanzhou communities formed their own militias and cooperated with the Qing forces to resist Lin's armies.[^28] Similarly, the Hakka people, who had developed prosperous settlements in northern Taiwan, also sided with the Qing government.[^29]

Lin proclaimed himself a leader with royal ambitions, and some sources suggest the Tiandihui members gathered Ming loyalists around him, though he never formally declared himself king in the manner of some other Chinese rebels.[^30] His forces demonstrated tactical effectiveness in their initial campaigns, using their knowledge of local terrain and surprise tactics to overcome better-equipped Qing forces. The rebellion pushed government forces out of Lin's home base in Changhua and extended rebel control across vast territories.[^31]

By mid-1787, Lin Shuangwen stood at the height of his power, controlling most of southern and central Taiwan with an army of tens of thousands. The Qing court in Beijing could not ignore such a massive challenge to imperial authority, particularly on an island that, despite its peripheral status, remained formally part of the empire. The stage was set for a major imperial military campaign that would ultimately determine Lin's fate.

## Major Achievements

Despite his rebellion's ultimate failure, Lin Shuangwen achieved remarkable military and organizational successes that marked him as one of the most formidable rebel leaders in Qing Taiwan's history. His accomplishments demonstrate both his strategic capabilities and the depth of anti-government sentiment he was able to mobilize.

Lin's first major achievement was the rapid mobilization of massive popular support across Taiwan. Within weeks of launching the rebellion, he assembled a fighting force of 50,000 active combatants, with total participation reaching an estimated 300,000 people.[^32] This represented a substantial portion of Taiwan's Han Chinese population at the time and demonstrated Lin's ability to tap into widespread discontent with Qing rule. The speed of this mobilization caught Qing authorities completely off-guard and revealed the fragility of imperial control over the island.

The military campaign itself achieved stunning initial victories. After assassinating Governor Sun Jingsui on January 16, 1787, Lin's forces swept across southern and central Taiwan, capturing multiple cities and administrative centers.[^33] By February 1787, less than a month into the rebellion, Lin controlled almost the entire southern portion of the island with the notable exceptions of Zhuluo County and Lukang port.[^34] This represented one of the most successful rebel campaigns in terms of territory seized in the shortest time during the entire Qing dynasty.

Lin demonstrated considerable tactical acumen in his military operations. His forces successfully pushed government troops out of strategic locations including Changhua, his home base, as well as Fengshan, Tainan, and other key cities.[^35] At Changhua County, his forces killed approximately 2,000 civilians who were likely perceived as Qing sympathizers, demonstrating the rebellion's brutal nature but also its ability to project power.[^36] The rebels established fortifications and used ambush tactics effectively against the initial Qing response forces.

The rebellion's sustainability over more than a year represented another significant achievement. When the Qing court dispatched 50,000 troops under General Li Shiyao in early 1787, Lin's forces fought them to a stalemate for six months.[^37] This demonstrated that the rebellion was not merely an opportunistic riot but a sustained military campaign with organizational depth. The rebels maintained supply lines, coordinated operations across multiple locations, and preserved unit cohesion despite facing a numerically superior and better-equipped imperial army.

Lin also successfully leveraged ethnic networks and social organizations to sustain the rebellion. The Tiandihui provided the organizational backbone for the uprising, offering a pre-existing command structure and communication network that allowed rapid coordination across Taiwan's rugged terrain.[^38] Lin enlisted the support of Jin Niang, a Taiwan Plains Aboriginal female shaman, expanding the rebellion's appeal beyond Han Chinese communities to include indigenous participants.[^39] This multi-ethnic dimension gave the rebellion broader social reach than many other Qing-era uprisings.

The rebellion's impact forced the Qing imperial court to mount one of its most expensive and extensive military campaigns of the Qianlong era. The emperor ultimately classified the suppression of Lin's rebellion as one of his "Ten Great Campaigns," placing it alongside major military operations against foreign enemies and other large-scale rebellions.[^40] This designation reflected both the rebellion's scale and the resources required to suppress it. The Qing deployed nearly 40,000 troops in total, making this the costliest military operation in terms of troops and money of any civil uprising in Taiwan during the entire Qing period.[^41]

Lin's rebellion also achieved lasting political consequences. The uprising demonstrated to the Qing court that Taiwan required more substantial administrative and military attention than previously provided. After the rebellion, the Qianlong Emperor rewarded Zhuluo County by renaming it Jiayi County (嘉義縣), meaning "commendable righteousness," in recognition of its resistance to the rebels.[^42] The rebellion also prompted infrastructure improvements, including the conversion of wooden and bamboo defensive structures around Tainan into more substantial walled fortifications.[^43]

Perhaps most significantly, Lin Shuangwen's rebellion highlighted the chronic instability created by Qing policies toward Taiwan, including immigration restrictions, minimal governance, and failure to address ethnic tensions. After the rebellion, the pattern of violent conflict between Zhangzhou, Quanzhou, and Hakka communities gradually diminished, with serious island-wide conflicts ending by the 1860s.[^44] While this cannot be attributed solely to Lin's rebellion, the uprising's scale forced greater Qing attention to the underlying causes of Taiwan's instability.

The Qianlong Emperor commissioned extensive artistic commemoration of the rebellion's suppression, ordering seven court painters—Yao Wenhan, Miao Bingtai, Yang Dazhang, Jia Quan, Xie Sui, Zhuang Yude, and Li Ming—to create twelve elaborate paintings based on battle sketches sent from Taiwan.[^45] These were transformed into copper plate etchings using Western techniques introduced by Jesuit painters like Giuseppe Castiglione, blending Chinese and European artistic traditions.[^46] The resulting "Painting of the Pacification of the War in Taiwan" series stands as one of the most detailed visual records of any Qing military campaign and ensures that Lin's rebellion remained commemorated in imperial art and historiography.

## Controversies and Criticisms

Lin Shuangwen's rebellion, while representing resistance to Qing rule, was marked by significant violence, ethnic conflict, and actions that complicate any simple heroic narrative. Historical records and scholarly assessments reveal substantial controversies surrounding his methods, motivations, and the rebellion's consequences.

The most immediate criticism concerns the extreme violence employed by Lin's forces. At Changhua County alone, his troops killed approximately 2,000 civilians, a massacre that went beyond military necessity and targeted non-combatants.[^47] The rebellion's ethnic dimension exacerbated this violence, as Lin's predominantly Zhangzhou forces engaged in what became as much an ethnic conflict as an anti-Qing uprising. The existing tensions between Zhangzhou and Quanzhou communities, which had erupted in violent feuds as recently as 1782, were reactivated and intensified by the rebellion.[^48]

Scholars have questioned the political nature of Lin's uprising, noting that "the rebellion was not out of ethnic [or national] interest, and there was little desire for the rebels to pursue political power."[^49] This assessment suggests that despite invoking Ming loyalism and anti-Manchu rhetoric, Lin's rebellion lacked coherent political objectives beyond immediate grievances about the suppression of the Tiandihui and the arrest of his relatives. Unlike other major rebellions in Chinese history that articulated clear ideological platforms or governance programs, Lin never established an alternative government structure or issued manifestos outlining political reforms.

The rebellion's transformation of existing ethnic conflicts into violent confrontation represents another significant criticism. Rather than uniting Taiwan's Han Chinese population against Qing rule, Lin's uprising divided communities along Zhangzhou-Quanzhou-Hakka lines. Quanzhou settlers formed their own militias specifically to oppose Lin's forces, cooperating actively with Qing troops.[^50] The Hakka communities similarly organized against the rebellion, with Hakka militias mustering 1,300 men who marched to battle the rebels, suffering approximately 200 casualties.[^51] This fragmentation meant that Han Chinese in Taiwan spent much of the rebellion fighting each other rather than presenting a united front against Qing authority.

The involvement of Lin's lieutenant Zhuang Datian in recruiting Jin Niang, a Taiwan Plains Aboriginal female shaman, raises questions about the exploitation of indigenous peoples in the rebellion.[^52] While this could be viewed as multi-ethnic coalition building, Jin Niang's eventual capture and execution by lingchi (death by a thousand cuts) in Beijing represents one of the rebellion's tragic consequences.[^53] The Qing punishment extended to Zhuang Datian's family, with his four-year-old grandson Zhuang Amo being castrated by Qing authorities—a brutal form of collective punishment that reflected imperial vengeance but also the rebellion's consequences for participants' families.[^54]

The effectiveness of Lin's military leadership came under strain as the rebellion progressed. After initial successes, his forces demonstrated poor logistics and supply management. Historical accounts note that rebel troops "faltered due to supply shortages, internal desertions, and Qing reinforcements."[^55] The inability to maintain troop cohesion and adequate provisions during the extended campaign revealed organizational weaknesses that a more experienced military leader might have addressed.

Lin's strategic decisions during the rebellion's latter stages have also been criticized. When confronted with General Fuk'anggan's fresh reinforcements of 10,000 to 20,000 well-equipped troops landing at Lukang in late 1787, Lin failed to mount an effective defense of strategic positions.[^56] The Battle of Dawulong in January 1788 saw Lin's brother Lin Yong attempt to secure a mountain pass but fail against Qing forces, while Zhuang Datian was forced to flee deeper into the mountains.[^57] These defeats suggest tactical failures in the face of a more disciplined and better-led imperial army.

The rebellion's ultimate capture phase revealed Lin's diminishing support. He was betrayed by a friend who secretly reported his whereabouts to government troops when he fled to Laoquqi (in present-day Miaoli County) in early 1788.[^58] This betrayal indicates that even among his supporters, loyalty had eroded by the rebellion's final stages, whether due to war weariness, fear of Qing retribution, or disillusionment with Lin's leadership.

Qing sources, which dominate the historical record, portrayed Lin as a bandit and rebel against legitimate authority rather than a freedom fighter or nationalist hero. Emperor Qianlong's poem about Lin's capture stated: "to finally take (Lin) alive is the greatest good; eradicate the rest, root and branch."[^59] This directive led to harsh collective punishments: rebels were executed by lingchi, and sons of rebel leaders under fifteen were castrated and enslaved as eunuchs in the Imperial Summer Palace (Yuanmingyuan).[^60] While these brutal punishments reflect poorly on Qing justice, they also demonstrate the court's view of Lin as a serious criminal whose actions merited the harshest possible response.

Modern scholarly assessments note the rebellion's lack of lasting institutional or ideological impact. Unlike other major uprisings such as the Taiping Rebellion, which developed alternative religious and political systems, or the White Lotus Rebellion, which articulated millenarian visions, Lin's uprising left little intellectual or organizational legacy beyond its military history. The Tiandihui was dispersed to mainland China or sent into hiding after the rebellion's suppression, failing to establish any enduring political movement.[^61]

The economic costs of the rebellion to Taiwan's civilian population were substantial. The fighting disrupted agriculture, trade, and daily life across southern Taiwan for over a year. The eventual Qing military campaign involved nearly 40,000 troops marching through civilian areas, requisitioning supplies, and conducting pacification operations that likely caused significant collateral damage beyond the direct combat casualties.

## Personal Life

Historical records provide limited information about Lin Shuangwen's personal life, as contemporary documentation focused primarily on his rebellion rather than biographical details. The available evidence, largely from Qing government sources and later compilations, offers only fragmentary glimpses into his family relationships, personality, and private circumstances.

Lin's family background was modest. He was born into a Zhangzhou family in Zhangping, Fujian Province, and immigrated to Taiwan with his father in 1773 when he was seventeen years old.[^62][^63] This father-son migration was typical of the pattern of Chinese settlement in Taiwan during the Qing period, when immigration restrictions often prevented entire families from relocating together. The close relationship between Lin and his father is suggested by some sources indicating that his father was detained by local authorities, possibly in connection with Tiandihui activities, which may have contributed to Lin's grievances against Qing officials.[^64]

Lin's uncles also played a significant role in his life and in precipitating the rebellion. When Qing Governor Sun Jingsui discovered and moved to suppress the Tiandihui in 1786, he specifically arrested Lin Shuangwen's uncles.[^65] This action against his extended family appears to have been the immediate catalyst that transformed Lin from a secret society leader into an active rebel. The attempt to free his imprisoned relatives became the initial justification for organizing the Tiandihui members into revolt, suggesting strong family bonds and a sense of filial duty that aligned with Confucian values even as he rebelled against the Confucian-ordered imperial state.

Lin had at least one brother, Lin Yong, who participated actively in the rebellion. Lin Yong led forces during the Battle of Dawulong in January 1788, attempting to secure a strategic mountain pass against advancing Qing troops before being defeated.[^66] This involvement of his brother indicates that the rebellion was a family endeavor, with Lin Shuangwen's relatives fighting alongside him rather than distancing themselves from his dangerous enterprise.

Lin's occupation as an incense maker suggests he possessed certain skills and operated a small business before the rebellion.[^67] This trade would have required knowledge of materials, scents, and religious practices, as incense played an important role in Chinese religious observances and ancestor worship. The occupation placed him in the artisan class—above mere laborers but below merchants or scholar-gentry—and likely brought him into contact with temples and religious networks that may have facilitated his involvement with the Tiandihui.

By 1784, at age twenty-eight, Lin had risen to become the leader of the local Tiandihui chapter in central Taiwan.[^68] This leadership position indicates he possessed charisma, organizational abilities, and the trust of his community. The Tiandihui, while secret, was not a small organization, and leading it would have required managing complex social relationships, mediating disputes, and maintaining the secrecy necessary to avoid government detection. His three years of leadership before the rebellion (1784-1787) suggest he had established substantial credibility and authority among his followers.

Lin's relationship with Zhuang Datian, his chief lieutenant, appears to have been one of genuine partnership. The sources consistently mention both men as co-leaders who "organized the rest of the society members in a revolt" together.[^69] Zhuang Datian's enlistment of the aboriginal shaman Jin Niang suggests he had independent initiative, yet he remained loyal to Lin throughout the rebellion until both were eventually captured. This partnership indicates Lin possessed the ability to work collaboratively with other strong leaders rather than demanding total personal control.

The historical record provides no information about whether Lin Shuangwen was married or had children. This absence is notable given that family status was typically recorded for significant historical figures in Chinese sources. The silence might suggest he was unmarried, or alternatively, that his family circumstances were considered irrelevant by Qing officials who focused solely on his crimes. The Qing practice of executing male rebels and enslaving their young sons would normally have been documented if Lin had children, yet no such records appear in available sources.[^70]

Lin's personality must be inferred from his actions. The decision to assassinate Governor Sun Jingsui personally, or to order his assassination, demonstrates boldness and a capacity for violence.[^71] The rapid expansion of the rebellion suggests strong leadership and persuasive abilities. However, his ultimate capture after being betrayed by a friend indicates that even those close to him eventually chose self-preservation over loyalty, possibly suggesting his personal relationships had limits or that his cause had become clearly hopeless by early 1788.[^72]

After his capture on February 10, 1788, near present-day Changhua, Lin was transported to Beijing for trial and execution.[^73] The Qianlong Emperor had specifically ordered General Fuk'anggan to capture Lin alive and bring him to the capital, deploying more than 120 elite baturu (Manchu warriors) with this mission.[^74] This unusual emphasis on taking Lin alive rather than killing him on the battlefield suggests the emperor wanted to make a public example of him in the capital, demonstrating imperial justice and the consequences of rebellion.

Lin Shuangwen was executed in Beijing in April 1788, approximately two months after his capture.[^75] The execution completed a dramatic arc from humble incense maker to leader of Taiwan's largest rebellion to executed criminal, all within the span of just over two years (1786-1788). He was thirty-two years old at the time of his death.

## Legacy

Lin Shuangwen's rebellion left a complex legacy that shaped Taiwan's development, Qing imperial policy, and historical memory in ways that continue to resonate. Though the uprising failed in its immediate objectives and ended with Lin's execution, its scale and consequences ensured it would be remembered as one of the defining events of Taiwan's Qing period.

The rebellion's most immediate legacy was its impact on Qing governance of Taiwan. The uprising exposed the inadequacy of the minimal administrative and military presence the empire had maintained on the island. The rebellion cost the Qing more troops and money than any other civil uprising in Taiwan during the entire Qing period, requiring nearly 40,000 soldiers and taking over a year to suppress.[^76] This expensive lesson prompted the imperial court to take Taiwan more seriously as a strategic concern rather than treating it as a peripheral possession of limited value.

Administrative changes followed the rebellion's suppression. The Qianlong Emperor personally renamed Zhuluo County to Jiayi County (嘉義縣, meaning "commendable righteousness") to reward its inhabitants for resisting Lin's forces.[^77] This name remains in use today in Taiwan, serving as a permanent reminder of the rebellion and the ethnic divisions it exploited. Infrastructure improvements also resulted from the uprising; Tainan's defenses were substantially upgraded, with wooden and bamboo fortifications replaced by more substantial walled structures designed to prevent future rebellions from easily capturing the city.[^78]

The rebellion's classification as one of Emperor Qianlong's "Ten Great Campaigns" ensured its prominent place in official Qing historiography.[^79] By including the suppression of Lin's uprising alongside major military expeditions against the Burmese, Vietnamese, Gurkhas, and others, the emperor elevated what might have been viewed as a local police action into an important imperial achievement. This classification served Qing propaganda purposes by demonstrating the emperor's ability to maintain order throughout his vast realm, but it also acknowledged the rebellion's genuine magnitude and the challenge it posed to imperial authority.

The artistic commemoration commissioned by Qianlong represents one of the rebellion's most enduring legacies. The emperor ordered the creation of twelve elaborate paintings depicting key battles and events of the suppression campaign, based on sketches sent from Taiwan by General Fuk'anggan.[^80] Seven court painters—Yao Wenhan, Miao Bingtai, Yang Dazhang, Jia Quan, Xie Sui, Zhuang Yude, and Li Ming—executed these works, which were then transformed into copper plate etchings using Western techniques.[^81] These etchings survive as rare visual documentation of the rebellion and serve as important historical materials for scholars studying both the event itself and Qing military art. The combination of Chinese and Western artistic styles in these works also demonstrates the cultural exchanges occurring at the Qing court during this period.

The rebellion's impact on ethnic relations in Taiwan proved significant and lasting. The uprising had activated and intensified conflicts between Zhangzhou, Quanzhou, and Hakka communities, with different groups choosing sides based more on ethnic identity than political ideology.[^82] The Hakka formation of volunteer militias to fight against Lin's Zhangzhou forces, losing approximately 200 men in the process, became foundational to Yimin (義民, "righteous people") worship—the veneration of loyalist militiamen who died fighting for the Qing empire.[^83] This religious practice, particularly centered in Hakka communities, represents a direct legacy of the rebellion that continues in Taiwan today.

However, after the rebellion, the pattern of violent inter-ethnic conflict among Han Chinese communities began to diminish. Local feuds between Zhangzhou, Quanzhou, and Hakka groups appeared only sporadically through the early 19th century and came to an end by the 1860s.[^84] While many factors contributed to this pacification, the extreme violence and destructiveness of the Lin Shuangwen rebellion may have demonstrated to all communities the costs of such conflicts, encouraging gradual reconciliation and integration.

The rebellion influenced Qing policy regarding secret societies throughout the empire. The Tiandihui was actively suppressed, with members either dispersed to mainland China or forced into hiding.[^85] This crackdown extended beyond Taiwan to Fujian and other coastal provinces where the Heaven and Earth Society maintained presence. The Qing government's fear of secret societies as potential rebel organizations was confirmed by Lin's uprising, leading to intensified surveillance and suppression efforts that would continue throughout the dynasty's remaining years.

Ironically, while the Qing suppressed the Tiandihui in the short term, the society and its related organizations (often called "Triads" in Western sources) survived and eventually evolved into both political organizations and criminal networks. Some branches of these societies would later participate in anti-Qing revolutionary activities in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, suggesting that Lin's rebellion, though defeated, was part of a longer tradition of secret society resistance to Manchu rule.

The rebellion's legacy in immigration policy was more ambiguous. After the uprising, the Qianlong Emperor agreed that "leaving fertile lands to unproductive aborigines only attracted illegal settlers," using the rebellion as justification to reconsider restrictions on Han settlement in aboriginal territories.[^86] This policy shift contributed to increased Han expansion into previously protected indigenous lands, with profound long-term consequences for Taiwan's aboriginal peoples. In this sense, Lin's rebellion inadvertently facilitated Han colonization that might otherwise have proceeded more slowly.

The saying "every three years an uprising, every five years a rebellion" (三年一反、五年一亂) became firmly associated with Qing-era Taiwan, capturing the chronic instability that characterized the period.[^87] Lin Shuangwen's rebellion stood as the most dramatic example of this pattern, validating the maxim and shaping perceptions of Taiwan as a turbulent frontier region difficult to govern effectively.

In modern Taiwan, Lin Shuangwen's legacy remains contested and complex. During periods of Taiwanese nationalism, some have viewed him as an early anti-Chinese resistance figure, part of a tradition of Taiwanese opposition to mainland control. This interpretation, however, is problematic given that Lin himself was a recent immigrant from Fujian with no distinct Taiwanese identity. Moreover, the rebellion divided Taiwan's Han population rather than uniting it, and substantial numbers of Taiwanese residents actively fought against Lin on behalf of the Qing government.

Alternative interpretations view Lin as representing resistance to corrupt and oppressive governance rather than ethnic or national struggle. From this perspective, his rebellion reflected legitimate grievances about heavy taxation, official corruption, and the suppression of community organizations that provided essential social support in a frontier society where government services were minimal. The speed with which 50,000 fighters and 300,000 participants joined the uprising suggests widespread discontent that transcended any individual leader's ambitions.

Scholarly assessments emphasize the rebellion's complex motivations and the difficulty of categorizing it simply as either nationalist resistance or criminal banditry. The observation that "the rebellion was not out of ethnic [or national] interest, and there was little desire for the rebels to pursue political power" suggests that Lin's uprising occupied an ambiguous space between political revolution and social upheaval driven by immediate grievances rather than ideological vision.[^88]

The physical sites associated with the rebellion have become part of Taiwan's historical landscape. Changhua, Lin's home base, and the locations of major battles are recognized historical sites, though they are not as prominent in public memory as sites associated with later historical periods. The lack of any clear ideological legacy or political movement descended from Lin's rebellion has meant that commemoration remains primarily historical and academic rather than actively political.

The historical documentation of the rebellion, while extensive from the Qing government perspective, lacks rebel voices and perspectives. The copper etchings commissioned by Qianlong, the official histories, and bureaucratic records all present the imperial viewpoint, portraying Lin as a bandit whose rebellion threatened order and required suppression. Few if any documents preserve Lin's own words, proclamations, or justifications for rebellion, leaving historians dependent on government sources that had every reason to delegitimize his cause. This documentary imbalance shapes ongoing historical understanding and limits our ability to fully understand the rebellion from the participants' perspectives.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Some historical perspectives recognize Lin Shuangwen as a figure of resistance against oppressive governance and as a leader who successfully mobilized mass support against a powerful imperial state. These assessments emphasize the legitimate grievances underlying the rebellion and Lin's organizational capabilities.

Scholars noting that the rebellion drew 300,000 participants across Taiwan point to this extraordinary mobilization as evidence that Lin tapped into genuine popular discontent.[^89] The speed with which supporters rallied to his cause—growing from a small Tiandihui membership to 50,000 active fighters within weeks—suggests Lin possessed remarkable leadership abilities and that his message resonated with broad segments of Taiwan's population. Few leaders in Chinese history have achieved such rapid mass mobilization, indicating either exceptional charisma or widespread recognition that the rebellion addressed real grievances.

The rebellion's military achievements in its early phases demonstrate strategic competence and tactical skill. Capturing most of southern Taiwan within a month and fighting the initial Qing response force of 50,000 troops to a six-month stalemate represents significant military accomplishment.[^90] These successes occurred despite the rebels facing better-equipped and theoretically better-trained government forces, suggesting effective use of terrain, local knowledge, and guerrilla tactics that maximized the rebels' advantages.

Some perspectives view Lin's rebellion as part of Taiwan's long tradition of resistance to external control. The saying "every three years an uprising, every five years a rebellion" captures a pattern of Taiwanese pushback against governance perceived as illegitimate or oppressive.[^91] From this viewpoint, Lin stands in a tradition alongside earlier rebels like Zhu Yigui (1721) and anticipates later resistance movements, representing a continuity of popular opposition to authoritarian rule. This interpretation emphasizes the agency of Taiwan's population in resisting external imposition rather than passively accepting governance.

The multi-ethnic coalition that Lin assembled, including the participation of Jin Niang, a Taiwan Plains Aboriginal female shaman, suggests some scholars see potential for cross-community resistance to Qing rule.[^92] While the rebellion ultimately divided more than it united, the initial participation of diverse groups indicates possibilities for broader anti-government solidarity that transcended ethnic divisions, even if these possibilities were not ultimately realized.

Lin's rebellion exposed fundamental weaknesses in Qing governance of Taiwan, forcing the imperial court to acknowledge that minimal administration and military presence were inadequate for the island.[^93] The policy changes and increased attention Taiwan received after the rebellion improved governance and infrastructure, suggesting that Lin's uprising, despite its military defeat, achieved some reform objectives through forcing Qing reassessment of Taiwan policy.

The rebellion's inclusion in Emperor Qianlong's "Ten Great Campaigns" can be interpreted as acknowledgment of its significance and the serious challenge it posed to imperial authority.[^94] That the Qing emperor felt compelled to classify a domestic rebellion alongside major foreign military expeditions suggests Lin's uprising was no mere bandit outbreak but a substantial threat that required recognition even as it was suppressed. This imperial acknowledgment, intended to glorify the emperor's achievements, inadvertently validates the rebellion's historical importance.

Defenders of Lin's legacy note the brutal and oppressive nature of Qing rule in Taiwan during this period. Corrupt officials, heavy taxation despite natural disasters (as in the 1721 Zhu Yigui rebellion), suppression of community organizations, and harsh immigration restrictions that separated families created genuine suffering among Taiwan's population.[^95] From this perspective, Lin's rebellion represents a justified response to intolerable conditions rather than criminal banditry, and his willingness to risk death in opposing these conditions demonstrates moral courage.

The Tiandihui's evolution from anti-Qing political organization to mutual aid society reflected the needs of marginalized communities for social support and protection that the government failed to provide.[^96] Lin's leadership of this organization and his defense of it against suppression can be viewed as protecting essential community infrastructure rather than criminal conspiracy. The government's decision to suppress the Tiandihui, arrest Lin's relatives, and destroy these support networks created the conditions for rebellion.

### Negative Views

Critical assessments of Lin Shuangwen emphasize the rebellion's violence, its divisive ethnic dimensions, its lack of coherent political vision, and the ultimately destructive consequences for Taiwan's population. These perspectives view Lin more as an opportunistic rebel leader than a heroic resistance figure.

The extreme violence employed by Lin's forces, including the killing of approximately 2,000 civilians at Changhua County, represents a serious moral failing that critics emphasize.[^97] This massacre went beyond military necessity and targeted non-combatants, suggesting either Lin's inability to control his forces or his willingness to employ terror tactics against civilian populations. Such actions undermine claims that the rebellion represented righteous resistance to oppression, instead revealing it as a violent upheaval that caused tremendous suffering.

Scholars noting that "the rebellion was not out of ethnic [or national] interest, and there was little desire for the rebels to pursue political power" question the rebellion's fundamental purpose and legacy.[^98] Unlike revolutionary movements that articulate clear ideological platforms, governance programs, or visions for social transformation, Lin's uprising appeared motivated primarily by immediate grievances about the suppression of the Tiandihui and personal affronts (the arrest of his relatives). This lack of broader political vision meant the rebellion offered no alternative to Qing rule beyond simply removing current officials.

The rebellion's activation and intensification of ethnic conflicts between Zhangzhou, Quanzhou, and Hakka communities represents a significant negative legacy. Rather than uniting Han Chinese settlers against Qing rule, Lin's predominantly Zhangzhou forces fought against Quanzhou and Hakka militias who sided with the government.[^99] The 1782 Zhangzhou-Quanzhou feud was still fresh in memory, and Lin's rebellion reignited these ethnic tensions with deadly consequences. Approximately 200 Hakka militiamen died fighting against the rebels, creating lasting grievances and hardening ethnic divisions.[^100]

The rebellion's military failures in its later stages reveal strategic and organizational weaknesses. Lin's forces "faltered due to supply shortages, internal desertions, and Qing reinforcements," indicating poor logistics and declining morale.[^101] A more capable military leader might have maintained supply lines, prevented desertions, and developed defensive strategies to counter Qing reinforcements. The eventual collapse suggests Lin's organizational abilities, while sufficient for rapid mobilization, were inadequate for sustained military campaign against a determined imperial response.

Lin's ultimate betrayal by a friend who revealed his hiding place to Qing forces indicates that even close associates had lost faith in his cause by early 1788.[^102] This betrayal suggests the rebellion's collapse was not merely military but also reflected loss of legitimacy and support among those who knew Lin personally. Leaders who maintain genuine loyalty typically do not face betrayal from their inner circle, suggesting problems with Lin's leadership or the hopelessness of his cause by that point.

The brutal Qing punishments inflicted after the rebellion's suppression—executions by lingchi, castration of rebel leaders' young sons, enslavement as eunuchs—while reflecting poorly on Qing justice, also demonstrate the seriousness with which the empire viewed Lin's crimes.[^103] These harsh measures, however excessive, were reserved for those considered grave threats to social order. The Qing perspective that Lin was a dangerous criminal whose rebellion caused tremendous suffering and threatened imperial authority has merit given the death toll, destruction, and year-long disruption to Taiwan's society.

The rebellion's economic costs to Taiwan's civilian population were substantial and represent another negative legacy. Over a year of warfare disrupted agriculture, damaged trade networks, and created refugee populations fleeing combat zones. The eventual Qing military campaign involving nearly 40,000 troops marching through civilian areas requisitioning supplies caused additional hardship beyond the direct combat.[^104] The rebellion thus brought suffering not only through rebel actions but also through the inevitable destruction of the suppression campaign it provoked.

Critics point to the failure of the Tiandihui to establish any lasting political movement or ideological legacy after the rebellion's defeat. The organization was dispersed or driven underground, failing to develop into a sustained resistance movement.[^105] This organizational collapse suggests the Tiandihui lacked the ideological depth or institutional strength to transform from mutual aid society into effective political opposition. The ease with which it was suppressed indicates it never posed a genuine long-term threat to Qing rule despite its ability to generate a short-term rebellion.

The rebellion's inadvertent consequence of encouraging Han expansion into aboriginal territories represents another negative aspect of its legacy. The Qianlong Emperor used the rebellion to justify reconsidering restrictions on Han settlement in indigenous lands, arguing that leaving such lands to "unproductive aborigines" attracted illegal settlers and created ungovernable frontier zones.[^106] This policy shift, prompted partly by the rebellion, facilitated increased Han colonization of aboriginal territories with devastating long-term consequences for Taiwan's indigenous peoples.

Historical documentation reveals that substantial numbers of Taiwan residents actively opposed Lin's rebellion, forming militias and cooperating with Qing forces. The loyalty of Zhuluo County residents, rewarded with the county's renaming to Jiayi ("commendable righteousness"), demonstrates that many Taiwanese viewed Lin as a threat to their communities rather than a liberator.[^107] Quanzhou and Hakka populations similarly organized against the rebellion, suggesting it lacked the popular legitimacy necessary for success and was viewed by many contemporaries as a Zhangzhou ethnic movement rather than a genuinely popular uprising.

The absence of Lin's own voice in historical records—no preserved proclamations, manifestos, or statements of purpose—suggests either that he articulated no clear ideology or that his statements were so seditious that even recording them for posterity was forbidden. Either way, this documentary silence means Lin left no intellectual legacy, no memorable quotes or ideas that later movements could draw upon, reducing his historical impact to the mere fact of the rebellion rather than any enduring message or vision.

Modern assessments noting the rebellion's complex and ultimately destructive nature suggest Lin Shuangwen is best understood as a tragic figure who unleashed forces he could not control, bringing suffering to Taiwan's population without achieving any lasting positive change. The rebellion's military defeat, organizational collapse, failure to unite Taiwan's population, and lack of coherent political vision all point to a fundamentally flawed uprising that caused tremendous pain for limited purpose.

## Famous Quotes

> "To finally take (Lin) alive is the greatest good; eradicate the rest, root and branch." - Emperor Qianlong, from his poem about Lin Shuangwen's capture[^108]

This quote from the Qianlong Emperor's commemorative poem reveals the imperial perspective on Lin's rebellion. The directive to capture Lin alive for public trial and execution in Beijing, while eradicating his followers completely, demonstrates the emperor's desire to make an example of the rebel leader while thoroughly suppressing any remaining resistance.

> "Every three years an uprising, every five years a rebellion" (三年一反、五年一亂) - Traditional saying about Taiwan during the Qing period[^109]

While not a quote from Lin Shuangwen himself, this famous saying captured the chronic instability that characterized Taiwan under Qing rule and provided the context for Lin's rebellion. The maxim reflects the perception of Taiwan as an ungovernable frontier plagued by constant unrest, of which Lin's uprising was the most dramatic example.

**Note on Limited Quotes**: Historical records preserve few if any direct quotes or proclamations from Lin Shuangwen himself. The available documentation consists primarily of Qing government sources focused on suppressing the rebellion rather than recording rebel perspectives. This absence means Lin left no documented speeches, manifestos, or memorable statements that later generations could cite, limiting our understanding of his motivations and ideology to interpretation of his actions rather than his own words.
