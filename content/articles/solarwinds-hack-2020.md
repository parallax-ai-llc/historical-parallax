## Summary
The SolarWinds hack was a massive cyberespionage campaign discovered in December 2020, attributed to Russian intelligence. Hackers compromised the Orion software update system, infiltrating multiple US government agencies including Treasury, Commerce, and Homeland Security.

## Additional Notes
- The intrusion is commonly characterized as a supply-chain compromise: attackers inserted or distributed a malicious component through a trusted software update channel.
- Once inside victim networks, the activity often blended into normal administrative traffic, emphasizing stealth and long dwell time over destructive impact.
- Public reporting linked the campaign to a sophisticated espionage actor; attribution and naming differed across governments and security vendors.
- Incident response involved emergency guidance and coordinated remediation across agencies and contractors, including rebuilding trust in identity, endpoints, and update pipelines.
- The episode intensified calls for "secure by design" practices in enterprise software, stronger code-signing hygiene, and improved visibility into third-party dependencies.
- It is frequently cited as a catalyst for wider adoption of zero-trust principles and for policy efforts around software supply-chain transparency (e.g., SBOM-style approaches).

## Practical Lesson
Trust relationships between vendors and customers can become an attack surface when updates and authentication are not continuously verified.
