## Summary

Carlo Maria Giulini was one of the most revered Italian conductors of the twentieth century, celebrated for his deeply expressive interpretations of the Austro-German and Italian symphonic and operatic repertoire. Born in Barletta in 1914, he built an international career spanning six decades, leading the world's foremost orchestras with a combination of intellectual rigor and profound emotional sincerity. His long tenure with the Los Angeles Philharmonic in the late 1970s and 1980s brought him to the widest public attention, cementing his reputation as a conductor of rare spiritual depth.

Giulini was known for his meticulous preparation, his refusal to compromise artistic integrity for expediency, and his unusual personal humility. He frequently declined engagements he felt he could not perform to the highest standard, and he famously withdrew from opera conducting for over a decade at the height of his career. His recordings with the Philharmonia Orchestra, the Chicago Symphony, the Vienna Philharmonic, and the Los Angeles Philharmonic remain benchmarks in the discography of Brahms, Verdi, Beethoven, and Mahler.

## Early Life & Background

Carlo Maria Giulini was born on 9 May 1914 in Barletta, in the Puglia region of southern Italy. He showed early musical aptitude and moved to Rome to study at the Accademia Nazionale di Santa Cecilia, where he trained as a violist and later turned to conducting. His studies were interrupted by World War II; after being called up for military service, he went into hiding in 1944 rather than serve the German-controlled Italian Social Republic, spending months in concealment before Rome's liberation by Allied forces in June 1944. This wartime experience left a lasting mark on his character and worldview.

## Rise to Prominence

After the war, Giulini conducted radio orchestras for RAI and came to the attention of Arturo Toscanini, who endorsed his abilities. He made his operatic debut and rose quickly through Italian musical circles. His international breakthrough came when Victor de Sabata fell ill and Giulini stepped in to conduct a celebrated 1951 La Scala production of La traviata, staged by Luchino Visconti and featuring Maria Callas. The production was a landmark of postwar Italian opera. Throughout the 1950s he worked closely with Otto Klemperer and the Philharmonia Orchestra in London, recording acclaimed interpretations of Verdi's Falstaff and Don Carlos. In 1969 he made the decision to withdraw from opera entirely, citing the pressures of the institutional opera world as incompatible with his standards.

## Key Achievements

Giulini served as principal conductor of the Los Angeles Philharmonic from 1978 to 1984, elevating the orchestra to international prominence. His recordings of Brahms symphonies with the Chicago Symphony Orchestra (1969–72) and Mahler's Ninth Symphony with the Chicago Symphony (1976) are considered among the finest ever made. He recorded Verdi's Requiem multiple times, with the 1964 Philharmonia version remaining a touchstone. In 1982 he conducted a celebrated Brahms cycle in Vienna that was widely broadcast and released commercially.

## Controversies & Criticism

Giulini's perfectionism occasionally drew criticism for resulting in under-rehearsed concerts when preparations fell short, and some colleagues found his refusal of operatic engagements after 1969 an abdication of responsibility to a broader audience. A minority of critics considered his tempi excessively broad, particularly in later years, though most regarded this as a natural evolution toward greater profundity.

## Legacy & Influence

Giulini died on 14 June 2005 in Milan at the age of 91. He is remembered as a conductor who placed musical truth above career advancement, influencing generations of conductors who cite his integrity and depth as a model. His recordings continue to be reissued and studied, and the Los Angeles Philharmonic credits his tenure as foundational to the orchestra's modern international standing.
