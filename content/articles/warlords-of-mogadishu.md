## Summary

After Siad Barre's fall, Mogadishu was divided among clan warlords including Aidid, Ali Mahdi, and Ato. Their power struggles caused famine killing 300,000 and prompted US intervention.

## Collapse of central authority

The phenomenon is rooted in the breakdown of Somalia's central state after the fall of Siad Barre in 1991. With national institutions weakened or absent, armed factions tied to clan structures competed for territory, checkpoints, and revenue sources inside Mogadishu.

In such environments, violence is not only ideological but also economic: control of ports, markets, road access, and aid distribution can be converted into funding for militias through taxation, extortion, or capture.

## Humanitarian crisis and international intervention

Factional fighting and disruption of agriculture and trade contributed to famine and mass displacement. The resulting humanitarian crisis was a major driver behind international involvement, including UN and US-led operations intended to secure aid delivery.

The 1993 "Black Hawk Down" incident became a defining event in global perceptions of intervention in failed-state contexts, influencing future risk tolerance and rules of engagement for peacekeeping and humanitarian missions.

## Dynamics of warlord power

Warlord influence typically depended on a combination of:

- Armed capacity (militia size, weapons, discipline)
- Clan alliances and patronage networks
- Control of economic chokepoints (checkpoints, ports, warehouses)
- Ability to negotiate or coerce access for humanitarian actors

These dynamics can create self-reinforcing cycles, where revenue enables arms purchases, which enables more coercion, which in turn increases revenue.

## Legacy

The warlord era is often cited in analyses of state reconstruction and security-sector reform. It illustrates the difficulty of rebuilding governance when armed entrepreneurs can outcompete weak institutions and when security itself becomes a market.
