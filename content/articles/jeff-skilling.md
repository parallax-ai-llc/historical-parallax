## Summary

Enron CEO convicted of fraud, conspiracy, and insider trading for his role in the massive accounting fraud. Sentenced to 24 years, later reduced to 14.

## Additional Context

### Role at Enron

- Skilling joined Enron after a career in consulting and became a central executive figure in the company’s expansion beyond traditional energy pipelines.
- He is commonly associated with pushing Enron’s identity as a “market maker” in energy trading and related financial products.

### Business practices and accounting issues (high level)

- Enron’s collapse involved complex financial structures, aggressive accounting, and misleading disclosures that obscured debt and losses.
- Skilling’s management tenure is frequently discussed in connection with Enron’s performance culture and the use of mark-to-market accounting for certain contracts.

### Collapse and legal proceedings

- Enron filed for bankruptcy in late 2001 after a rapid loss of market confidence, credit downgrades, and revelations about off-balance-sheet entities.
- Skilling was tried and convicted on multiple counts related to fraud and conspiracy; the case became one of the highest-profile corporate prosecutions in U.S. history.

### Sentence and later developments

- He received a lengthy federal sentence that was later reduced as part of subsequent legal and procedural developments.
- The Enron scandal contributed to major regulatory and governance changes, including heightened corporate reporting requirements and stronger internal-control expectations.

### Legacy

- Skilling is often cited in case studies about corporate ethics, incentive design, and the failure modes of complex financial reporting.
- Enron remains a standard reference point in business education for discussions of risk, transparency, and accountability.
