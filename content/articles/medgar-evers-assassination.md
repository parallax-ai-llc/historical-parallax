## Summary

NAACP field secretary Medgar Evers was shot in the back in his driveway by white supremacist Byron De La Beckwith. It took three trials and 31 years to convict his killer.

## Background

Medgar Evers was a prominent civil rights organizer in Mississippi and served as the NAACP's first field secretary in the state. In the early 1960s he worked on voter registration, investigated racially motivated violence, and supported efforts to end segregation in public life.

## Investigation and early proceedings

The shooting drew national attention and quickly became a test of whether violent attacks on civil rights figures would be prosecuted in the Jim Crow South. Byron De La Beckwith, a segregationist and white supremacist, was arrested and charged soon after the assassination.

## Trials and conviction

Beckwith was tried twice in 1964, but both trials ended in hung juries and he was released. Decades later the case was revisited as part of a broader trend of re-examining civil-rights-era killings. In 1994, following renewed investigation and additional testimony and evidence, Beckwith was convicted of murder.

## Impact and legacy

The case became emblematic of the era's racial violence and the long delays that often characterized accountability for crimes against civil rights activists. The eventual conviction was widely viewed as an important milestone for the Evers family and for the civil rights movement's historical record.

## Key figures

- **Medgar Evers**: NAACP field secretary in Mississippi and a leading organizer for civil rights.
- **Byron De La Beckwith**: Defendant who was tried multiple times and ultimately convicted in 1994.
