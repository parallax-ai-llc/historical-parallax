## Summary

Burundian military leader who seized power twice in coups (1987 and 1996). He was convicted in absentia by a Burundian court for his role in the 1993 massacres. He died while serving as AU envoy.

## Political Context

Burundi’s post-independence politics were shaped by repeated cycles of military rule and violence linked to ethnic and political competition, particularly between Hutu and Tutsi elites. Buyoya emerged from the military establishment during this unstable period.

## Leadership and Coups

- **1987 coup**: Buyoya took power in a military takeover and ruled as head of state.
- **1990s transition pressures**: Burundi faced domestic unrest and regional pressure to move toward multiparty politics.
- **1996 coup**: Buyoya returned to power amid civil conflict and international concern.

## Civil War and Peace Efforts

Following the 1993 assassination of President Melchior Ndadaye, Burundi descended into civil war. Buyoya’s later government participated in regional mediation processes that aimed to end the conflict and create power-sharing institutions.

Peace talks associated with the **Arusha process** sought to lay the groundwork for a negotiated settlement, including reforms to security institutions and political representation.

## Legal Proceedings and Controversy

Buyoya’s historical legacy is disputed. A Burundian court convicted him in absentia in connection with violence linked to the 1993 crisis. Human rights organizations and observers have debated the fairness and political context of such proceedings, while victims’ groups have pushed for accountability for atrocities committed during the civil war era.

## Later Roles

After leaving office, Buyoya served in diplomatic and regional roles, including work connected to the African Union. He died in 2020.
