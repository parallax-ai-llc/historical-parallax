## Summary

Campaign of mass political violence by Ethiopia's Derg military junta that killed an estimated 500,000 people. Government forces and civilians were enlisted to execute suspected opponents.

## Background

The Red Terror is generally associated with the period of rule by the **Derg**, a military junta that took power after the Ethiopian Revolution. The junta faced multiple armed and political challenges and framed repression as necessary to secure the revolution and eliminate counterrevolutionary threats.

## Targets and Methods (High Level)

Violence was directed at real and perceived opponents, including rival leftist movements, political activists, students, and community members suspected of dissent. Reported practices included:

- **Arbitrary detention** and interrogation
- **Torture** and extrajudicial executions
- Public intimidation and coercion to force compliance

The campaign’s effects extended beyond direct killings by creating an atmosphere of fear and disrupting civil society.

## Scale and Documentation

Casualty estimates vary widely across sources, reflecting limited records, politicization, and the chaotic conditions of the period. Human-rights documentation, survivor testimony, and later judicial proceedings have been important for reconstructing what occurred.

## Aftermath and Accountability

After the Derg fell, Ethiopia pursued various forms of accountability, including prosecutions of officials associated with atrocities. The Red Terror remains a central reference point in Ethiopian debates about justice, reconciliation, and the legacy of revolutionary politics.

## Historical Significance

The Ethiopian Red Terror is often discussed in comparative studies of state repression because it illustrates how:

- Revolutionary regimes can escalate coercion during perceived existential threats.
- Mass violence can be decentralized through local structures and informants.
- Long-term social trauma can persist even after political transitions.
