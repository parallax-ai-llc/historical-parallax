## Summary

Saddam Hussein's systematic campaign against Iraqi Kurds killed 50,000-182,000 people. Included the Halabja chemical attack that killed 5,000 with mustard and nerve gas.

## Context

- The Anfal Campaign took place near the end of the Iran–Iraq War, during which the Iraqi state sought to crush Kurdish insurgent groups and depopulate areas it viewed as disloyal.
- The campaign is commonly described as a coordinated set of military operations combined with forced displacement, mass detention, and systematic killings.

## Key Actors (High Level)

- The Iraqi government under Saddam Hussein directed the operations through security and military institutions.
- Ali Hassan al-Majid (often referred to as "Chemical Ali") is widely cited as a key official associated with the campaign's implementation.

## Methods and Patterns Reported

- **Village destruction and depopulation:** Large-scale razing of villages and relocation of civilians.
- **Mass detention and disappearances:** Detention of civilians, including separations by age and gender, and widespread disappearances.
- **Chemical weapons use:** Chemical attacks occurred in multiple locations; Halabja is the most internationally known incident.

## Human Toll and Uncertainty in Estimates

- Casualty estimates vary significantly across sources due to the scale of the operations, incomplete records, and the challenges of documenting disappearances.
- Many accounts emphasize that, beyond direct killings, the campaign caused long-term harm through displacement, trauma, and the destruction of livelihoods.

## Aftermath, Trials, and Recognition

- After the 2003 invasion of Iraq, Iraqi courts prosecuted senior officials for crimes related to the Anfal Campaign.
- Several governments and institutions have described the campaign as **genocide** against the Kurdish population; terminology and legal findings depend on jurisdiction and evidentiary standards.
