## Summary

Massacre of 14 unarmed civil rights protesters by British soldiers in Derry, Northern Ireland on January 30, 1972. The Saville Inquiry found the killings were unjustified.

## Historical Context

- This article refers to **Bloody Sunday (1972)** in Derry/Londonderry, Northern Ireland, during the period known as **the Troubles**.
- The march was organized in the context of civil-rights activism and protests against internment without trial.

## What Happened (high level)

- British Army soldiers opened fire on demonstrators and others in the area.
- Fourteen people ultimately died from gunshot wounds; the victims have been widely described as unarmed.
- The event rapidly became one of the most significant and polarizing incidents of the Troubles.

## Investigations and Findings

- An early inquiry (often summarized as the **Widgery Tribunal**) was published shortly after the events and was heavily criticized by many in Northern Ireland for its conclusions.
- Decades later, the **Saville Inquiry** conducted a lengthy re-investigation, concluding that the shootings were unjustified and that those shot posed no threat that would have justified lethal force.
- In 2010, the UK Prime Minister issued a formal apology in the House of Commons following the Saville report.

## Impact and Legacy

- Bloody Sunday intensified distrust between communities and the state, and it is frequently cited as a catalyst for increased recruitment and support for militant groups.
- It also shaped later debates about policing, military rules of engagement, and accountability in internal security operations.
- The episode remains central to collective memory, public commemoration, and transitional-justice discussions in Northern Ireland.
