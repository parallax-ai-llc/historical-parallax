## Summary

Leif Erikson was the first European to reach North America, approximately 500 years before Columbus. He established a settlement at Vinland (present-day Newfoundland).

## Historical Context

- Leif Erikson is associated with the Norse exploration of the North Atlantic, following earlier settlement patterns connecting Scandinavia, Iceland, and Greenland.
- Accounts of voyages to lands west of Greenland appear in medieval Icelandic sagas, though the details are debated by historians.

## Vinland and Archaeological Evidence

- “Vinland” is the name used in saga traditions for a region in North America visited by Norse seafarers.
- L’Anse aux Meadows (Newfoundland) is widely cited as archaeological evidence for a Norse presence in North America around the turn of the first millennium.

## Exploration vs. Colonization

- The Norse presence is often characterized as exploratory and seasonal rather than a long-lasting, large-scale colony.
- Interactions with local Indigenous peoples are referenced in saga literature, but the records are limited and filtered through later written sources.

## Commemoration and Cultural Memory

- In the United States and elsewhere, Leif Erikson became an emblem in narratives of “pre-Columbian” European contact with the Americas.
- Modern commemoration can reflect both historical interest and later political or cultural identity projects, depending on country and period.
