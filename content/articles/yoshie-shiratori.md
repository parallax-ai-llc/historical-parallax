## Summary

Japanese prisoner famous for escaping from four maximum-security prisons through incredible means including dislocating his joints and using miso soup to corrode handcuffs.

## Background

Shiratori is typically discussed in the context of Japan’s pre- and post-war penal system, where harsh prison conditions and strict restraint practices were common topics in later accounts.

Public retellings often focus on the contrast between:

- **Highly controlled facilities** (locks, bars, restraints, guard routines)
- **Improvised methods** (patience, observation, and exploiting small weaknesses)

## Escape Methods (High-Level)

Accounts of his escapes commonly emphasize practical themes rather than a single “trick,” including:

- Studying guard schedules and the mechanical properties of restraints.
- Using everyday materials available to prisoners to weaken metal or create leverage.
- Exploiting moments of low supervision during transfers, work details, or routine checks.

Because many details come from later reporting and popularization, individual tactics should be treated as anecdotal unless corroborated by primary records.

## Legacy and Cultural Impact

Shiratori’s story has been cited as an extreme example of:

- The limitations of purely physical security without procedural discipline.
- The way folklore can grow around criminal biographies.
- How prison narratives become vehicles for broader debates about punishment and rehabilitation.
