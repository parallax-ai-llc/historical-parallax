## Summary

Dr. Margaret Chang Fung (1934-2010), also known as Barbara Fung, was a pioneering library science educator and government administrator who transformed Taiwan's library infrastructure through technology and policy reform. Born in China and educated across Hong Kong, Taiwan, and the United States, she became a visionary force in library automation, introducing computerized systems to Taiwan's academic libraries in 1977. Beyond academia, she served twelve years as a Member of the Examination Yuan (1990-2002), overseeing civil service reforms while championing women's rights and international library cooperation. Her instrumental role in drafting Taiwan's Library Law and her prolific scholarship—over ninety articles and multiple seminal books—established her as the "mother of Chinese library automation" whose legacy continues through annual leadership awards bearing her name.[^1][^2]

## Early Life and Background

Margaret Chang Fung was born in 1934 in China during a period of significant political upheaval and social transformation. Growing up in an era marked by the Chinese Civil War and subsequent political changes, her early years were shaped by the broader historical forces that would eventually lead to mass migrations across East Asia. Though specific details about her childhood and family background remain limited in available sources, her educational trajectory reveals a pattern of intellectual ambition and cross-cultural engagement characteristic of Chinese intellectuals of her generation.[^3]

Fung completed her undergraduate studies at National Taiwan University, one of the most prestigious institutions in Taiwan and a center for academic excellence in the Chinese-speaking world. This foundation in Taiwan's premier university not only provided her with rigorous academic training but also connected her to an influential network of scholars and professionals who would shape Taiwan's post-war development. The choice to pursue higher education during the 1950s—a time when educational opportunities for women remained limited—demonstrates her determination to overcome societal barriers.[^1]

After completing her bachelor's degree, Fung emigrated to the United States in 1959 to pursue graduate studies, earning her master's degree in library science from Maywood College. This decision to study abroad reflected the broader pattern of Chinese and Taiwanese students seeking advanced education in American institutions during the Cold War era. The experience of studying in America exposed her to cutting-edge developments in library science, particularly the nascent field of library automation and information technology that would later define her career.[^4]

Following her master's degree, Fung gained practical experience working at St. John's University and the Harvard Yenching Libraries, where she developed expertise in managing Chinese collections and understanding the unique challenges of organizing Chinese-language materials in Western institutional settings. In the 1960s, she returned to East Asia to work at Hong Kong Chinese University Library, where she undertook the challenging task of restructuring its collections. This early career phase established her reputation as an expert in library organization and Chinese studies resources.[^5]

Fung's commitment to advancing her expertise led her back to the United States for doctoral studies at Indiana University's School of Library and Information Science, one of the leading programs in the field. She completed her Ph.D. in 1983, focusing her research on information policy, library history, library automation, information technology, and database systems—areas that would define her scholarly contributions for decades. Her dissertation work positioned her at the intersection of traditional librarianship and emerging technological innovations, preparing her to lead Taiwan's library modernization efforts.[^6][^7]

## Rise to Prominence

Margaret Chang Fung's ascent to prominence in Taiwan's library science community began in 1977 when she was appointed professor and director of the University Library at National Taiwan Normal University. This appointment marked a turning point not only in her career but also in Taiwan's library development, as she introduced pioneering library automation systems to the country. At a time when most Taiwanese libraries still relied on traditional card catalogs and manual systems, Fung recognized that technological modernization was essential for Taiwan to compete in the global information economy.[^8]

As library director at National Taiwan Normal University, Fung spearheaded the implementation of computerized cataloging and circulation systems, making the institution one of the first in Taiwan to embrace automation. This was a monumental undertaking that required not only technical expertise but also organizational leadership, as she had to train staff, secure funding, overcome resistance to change, and adapt international library standards to Chinese-language materials. Her success at National Taiwan Normal University established a model that other Taiwanese academic libraries would follow, earning her recognition as a pioneer in Chinese library automation.[^2]

Beyond her administrative role, Fung expanded her influence through teaching positions at multiple prestigious universities across Taiwan. She taught at National Taiwan University, Catholic Fu-Jen University, Tamkang University, and National Cheng-chi University, training a generation of librarians in modern information science principles. Through these teaching appointments, she disseminated knowledge about library automation, database management, and information systems to hundreds of students who would go on to lead libraries throughout Taiwan and the broader Chinese-speaking world.[^9]

Fung's scholarly productivity during this period was extraordinary. Between 1979 and 1996, she published numerous influential books including "Library and Information" (1979), "A Primer of Library Automation" (1981), "On Library and Information Science" (1982), "Guide to Information Science" (1984), and "Reflections on Civil Service System Library/Information Science" (1996). These works, written in Chinese, provided crucial resources for library professionals seeking to understand and implement modern library technologies and practices. Her more than ninety published articles addressed topics ranging from technical standards to philosophical questions about libraries' role in society.[^10][^11]

In 1984, Fung's international reputation led to her appointment as executive director of the Chinese studies program at the Wang Institute of Graduate Studies in Boston, a position she held until 1987. This role demonstrated her expertise not only in library science but also in Chinese studies more broadly, bridging American and Chinese academic communities. Following this appointment, she served as a visiting professor at the University of Illinois and National Taiwan University from 1987 to 1990, further solidifying her status as a leading figure in international library cooperation.[^12]

Fung's leadership within professional organizations elevated her prominence even further. She became president of the Library Association of China (also known as the Library Association of the Republic of China, Taiwan) from 1997 to 1999, leading the profession during a critical period of technological transformation. In this capacity, she advocated for policy reforms, professional standards, and increased funding for libraries throughout Taiwan.[^13]

Her most significant policy achievement during this period was her instrumental role in drafting and advocating for passage of Taiwan's Library Law. As president of the Library Association, Fung worked closely with the Ministry of Education and legislators to create comprehensive legal framework governing library operations, funding, and professional standards throughout Taiwan. The passage of this law represented a landmark achievement that formalized libraries' role in Taiwan's educational and cultural infrastructure.[^14]

## Major Achievements

Margaret Chang Fung's most transformative achievement was pioneering library automation in Taiwan, fundamentally changing how information was organized, accessed, and shared across the island's academic institutions. When she became library director at National Taiwan Normal University in 1977, Taiwan's libraries operated almost entirely through manual systems. Fung introduced computerized cataloging and circulation systems, trained library staff in new technologies, and developed Chinese-language adaptations of international library standards. Her work demonstrated that library automation was not merely a technical upgrade but a comprehensive transformation of library services that could dramatically improve access to information.[^15]

Her advocacy for and contribution to Taiwan's Library Law stands as another monumental achievement. The law established legal frameworks for library funding, professional qualifications, collection development standards, and public access rights. Fung's expertise in both library practice and policy-making enabled her to craft legislation that balanced professional standards with practical implementation concerns. The Library Law continues to shape Taiwan's library development decades after its passage, representing one of her most enduring legacies.[^14]

Fung's twelve-year tenure as a Member of the Examination Yuan (1990-2002) represented her transition from academic leadership to government service at the highest levels. The Examination Yuan is one of five branches of Taiwan's government, responsible for civil service examinations and personnel administration. Her appointment in 1990 and subsequent reelection in 1996 demonstrated both her exemplary performance and the government's confidence in her abilities. In this role, she oversaw reforms to civil service examination systems, professional certification processes, and personnel management policies affecting tens of thousands of government employees.[^16][^17]

As an educator, Fung trained multiple generations of library professionals through her teaching at National Taiwan Normal University, National Taiwan University, Catholic Fu-Jen University, Tamkang University, and National Cheng-chi University. Her students went on to lead libraries, information centers, and archives throughout Taiwan and internationally, multiplying her impact far beyond her own direct contributions. Her pedagogy emphasized both theoretical foundations and practical skills, preparing students to navigate the rapid technological changes transforming the profession.[^9]

Fung's scholarly output was both voluminous and influential, with over ninety published articles and multiple books that became standard references in Chinese library science. Her "Primer of Library Automation" (1981) was particularly groundbreaking, as it provided Chinese-language library professionals with accessible explanations of computer technologies and their applications to library operations. Her "Collected Works of Margaret Chang Fung" (2004) compiled her most significant writings, ensuring that her intellectual contributions would remain accessible to future generations.[^10][^11]

In international library cooperation, Fung served as an active advocate and bridge-builder between Chinese-speaking library communities and international organizations. Her work helped integrate Taiwan's libraries into global networks of professional exchange, technology transfer, and collaborative projects. Through her involvement with organizations like the Chinese American Librarians Association (CALA) and connections to the American Library Association (ALA) and International Federation of Library Associations and Institutions (IFLA), she facilitated knowledge sharing that benefited library professionals across borders.[^18]

Fung's restructuring of the Hong Kong Chinese University Library collections in the 1960s represented an early achievement that demonstrated her ability to manage complex organizational projects. This work required deep knowledge of Chinese studies materials, understanding of academic research needs, and skills in collection development and organization—all capabilities she would later deploy to even greater effect in Taiwan.[^5]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Available historical sources reveal remarkably little controversy or criticism surrounding Margaret Chang Fung's career, a notable absence that itself invites analysis. Unlike many prominent academic and government figures, Fung appears to have navigated her leadership roles without generating significant public opposition or documented conflicts. This may reflect several factors: her focus on technical and administrative matters rather than politically divisive issues; her reputation for exemplary competence and dedication; or potentially the incomplete preservation of critical voices in English-language sources.[^19]

One potential area of criticism, though not explicitly documented in available sources, concerns the pace and scope of library automation implementation in Taiwan. While Fung pioneered these technologies, critics might have questioned whether the investment in expensive computer systems was appropriate when basic library services remained underfunded in many institutions. The tension between technological modernization and fundamental resource needs represents a common challenge in developing information infrastructure, though no specific criticisms of Fung's priorities have been preserved in accessible historical records.

Her dual role as both an advocate for library professionalization and a government official responsible for civil service examinations might have created potential conflicts of interest or at minimum the appearance of such conflicts. As president of the Library Association while simultaneously serving in the Examination Yuan, she occupied positions that could influence both professional standards and the certification processes for librarians. However, no documented accusations of impropriety or conflicts of interest have emerged in available sources.[^16]

The dominance of positive characterizations in available sources—descriptions of her as "visionary," "pioneering," and "exemplary"—raises questions about whether critical perspectives have been omitted or whether Fung genuinely maintained such a consistently positive reputation. The establishment of the CALA Outstanding Library Leadership Award in her memory and the universally laudatory tone of biographical accounts suggest her contributions were widely recognized and valued, but the near-complete absence of documented criticism or controversy is unusual for any prominent public figure.[^2]

Some library professionals might have disagreed with specific technical choices Fung made regarding automation systems, cataloging standards, or collection development priorities. However, such professional disagreements, if they occurred, have not been preserved in English-language historical sources. The technical nature of library science debates may have limited their visibility beyond specialist circles, even when substantive disagreements existed.

Her role in civil service reform at the Examination Yuan potentially involved contentious decisions about examination content, professional qualifications, and personnel policies. Civil service systems inherently involve winners and losers—those who pass examinations and those who fail, those whose qualifications are recognized and those whose credentials are deemed insufficient. While Fung's work was characterized as "exemplary," it seems improbable that twelve years overseeing such systems generated no opposition or criticism, suggesting that negative perspectives may simply not have been documented in accessible sources.[^17]

## Personal Life

Information about Margaret Chang Fung's personal life remains notably sparse in available historical sources, which focus overwhelmingly on her professional achievements rather than her private experiences. This pattern reflects both the conventions of biographical writing about academics and government officials and potentially Fung's own preferences for privacy regarding her personal affairs. What emerges from the limited available information is a portrait of someone whose personal choices were deeply intertwined with her professional commitments and whose family relationships supported her ambitious career.[^20]

Fung's family established the Dr. Margaret Chang Fung Memorial Endowment Fund after her death in 2010, making two major donations totaling $22,000 to the Chinese American Librarians Association (CALA). This significant financial commitment to honoring her legacy and supporting library leadership development suggests close family relationships and shared appreciation for her professional contributions. However, specific details about her spouse, children, siblings, or other family members are not documented in available English-language sources.[^21]

Her life trajectory involved multiple international relocations and extended periods living across different cultural contexts—China, Hong Kong, Taiwan, and the United States. This cosmopolitan experience required adaptability, language skills, and comfort with cultural transitions. Fung's ability to work effectively in American, Hong Kong, and Taiwanese institutional settings demonstrated not only professional versatility but also personal resilience in navigating different social and cultural expectations.[^3]

Colleagues and biographical accounts consistently emphasize her dedication to her work, describing her as someone who maintained high standards and expected excellence from herself and others. While such characterizations could be read as purely professional observations, they also suggest personality traits—discipline, commitment, perfectionism—that likely shaped her personal relationships and daily life. Her prolific publishing record and simultaneous management of multiple teaching positions indicate someone whose personal life accommodated intense professional demands.[^10]

Fung's involvement in women's rights movements, mentioned in biographical accounts, suggests personal commitment to gender equality that extended beyond her professional identity. As a woman who achieved prominence in academic and government leadership during periods when such positions were predominantly male-dominated, she personally experienced the barriers and biases that motivated her advocacy. However, specific details about her women's rights activism, the organizations she supported, or the causes she championed are not elaborated in available sources.[^2]

## Legacy

Margaret Chang Fung's legacy is most directly preserved through the CALA Outstanding Library Leadership Award established in her memory, which since 2013 has annually recognized a Chinese American or Chinese library leader who demonstrates outstanding achievement at national or international levels. This award ensures that each year, the library profession pauses to remember Fung's contributions while identifying contemporary leaders who embody similar values of excellence, innovation, and service. The award recipients—themselves distinguished library leaders—carry forward the standards she established and extend her influence into new generations.[^22]

Her impact on Taiwan's library infrastructure represents perhaps her most tangible legacy. The library automation systems she pioneered in 1977 established precedents and models that shaped Taiwan's transition to digital information management. Decades after her initial innovations, Taiwanese libraries continue to build upon the foundations she laid, with computerized systems now universal across the island's academic and public libraries. The technological transformation she initiated fundamentally changed how millions of Taiwanese citizens access information and knowledge.[^15]

The Library Law that Fung helped draft and pass continues to govern Taiwan's library operations, providing legal framework for funding, professional standards, and public access. This legislation represents one of her most enduring policy achievements, as laws typically outlast individual careers and shape institutional behavior long after their architects have passed. The law's provisions continue to influence debates about library funding, intellectual freedom, and information access in Taiwan today.[^14]

Fung's students—the generations of library professionals she trained at National Taiwan Normal University, National Taiwan University, Catholic Fu-Jen University, Tamkang University, and National Cheng-chi University—constitute a living legacy. Many rose to leadership positions in libraries, archives, and information centers throughout Taiwan and internationally, implementing the principles and practices she taught. This multiplier effect means her influence extended far beyond her own direct contributions, as her students applied and adapted her teachings in diverse institutional contexts.[^9]

Her scholarly works, particularly "A Primer of Library Automation" (1981) and the "Collected Works of Margaret Chang Fung" (2004), remain reference points for understanding the history of library automation in Chinese-speaking contexts. While some technical content may be dated, these works document crucial transitions in library practice and preserve Fung's analytical frameworks for future historians and practitioners. They demonstrate how Chinese library professionals adapted Western technologies and practices to local linguistic and cultural contexts.[^10][^11]

The Dr. Margaret Chang Fung Memorial Endowment Fund at CALA, established by her family with $22,000 in donations, provides ongoing financial support for library leadership development. This fund ensures that her legacy includes not just symbolic recognition but material resources that enable emerging leaders to develop their capabilities. The fund represents her family's commitment to sustaining her values and extending her impact beyond her lifetime.[^21]

Fung's example as a woman who achieved prominence in both academic and government leadership during periods when gender barriers remained formidable continues to inspire women library professionals and women in public service more broadly. Her career demonstrated that excellence, persistence, and strategic leadership could overcome structural barriers, providing a model for subsequent generations of women seeking to make similar contributions.[^2]

Her role in international library cooperation helped establish networks and relationships that continue to facilitate knowledge exchange between Chinese-speaking library communities and international organizations. The connections she built between Taiwan's libraries and institutions like ALA and IFLA created channels for ongoing professional dialogue that benefit practitioners on multiple continents.[^18]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and library professionals consistently characterize Margaret Chang Fung as a transformative figure who fundamentally modernized Taiwan's library infrastructure during a critical period of technological transition. The Chinese American Librarians Association's decision to establish an annual award in her memory reflects institutional recognition of her exceptional contributions, with award descriptions emphasizing her "substantial scholarship, achievements, contributions, leadership and dedication to the library profession" as "highly admirable and of lasting value." This institutional endorsement represents the collective judgment of library professionals across multiple countries and contexts.[^22]

Academic and professional biographers praise Fung's multifaceted contributions across several domains—library automation, education, scholarship, policy-making, and government service. The characterization of her as serving simultaneously as "a Chinese studies promoter, a visionary university library director, a highly respected library and information science professor, a widely published scholar, a pioneer in Chinese library automation, a skillful government official in civil service examinations and personnel administration, a leader in the women's rights movements, and an active advocator in international library cooperation" reflects admiration for the breadth and depth of her engagement.[^2]

Library historians recognize Fung's pioneering role in introducing library automation to Taiwan, crediting her with vision and courage in advocating for technological investments when their benefits were not yet widely understood. Her willingness to embrace emerging technologies and her skill in implementing complex systems positioned Taiwan's libraries to compete effectively in an increasingly digital information environment. Supporters argue that her early adoption of automation gave Taiwan a competitive advantage that continues to benefit the island's academic and research communities.[^15]

Education scholars praise her contributions to professional development, noting that her teaching at multiple prestigious universities trained generations of library leaders who carried her principles and practices throughout Taiwan and beyond. Her pedagogy, which balanced theoretical foundations with practical skills, prepared students to navigate rapid technological changes. Former students and colleagues remember her as a demanding but inspirational teacher who maintained high standards while supporting students' professional development.[^9]

Policy analysts credit Fung with successfully navigating the complex political processes required to draft and pass Taiwan's Library Law, demonstrating not only technical expertise but also political acumen. Her ability to work effectively with the Ministry of Education, legislators, and library professionals to create comprehensive legislation reflects skills in coalition-building, negotiation, and strategic advocacy that extend beyond library science per se.[^14]

Advocates for women's advancement cite Fung as an exemplary role model who achieved prominence in male-dominated fields through excellence, persistence, and strategic leadership. Her success in both academic and government leadership during periods when gender barriers remained significant demonstrates that women could overcome structural obstacles and make substantive contributions to national development. Her involvement in women's rights movements suggests she understood her success as connected to broader struggles for gender equality.[^2]

International library cooperation advocates praise her role as a bridge-builder between Chinese-speaking library communities and global professional organizations. Her work facilitated knowledge transfer, technology adoption, and professional standards development that benefited library professionals across borders. Through her involvement with CALA and connections to ALA and IFLA, she helped integrate Taiwan's libraries into international networks.[^18]

### Negative Views

Critical perspectives on Margaret Chang Fung's career are notably absent from available English-language historical sources, a gap that itself warrants scholarly attention. The near-universal praise in biographical accounts and professional obituaries raises methodological questions about whether critical voices have been systematically excluded, whether opposition existed but was not documented, or whether Fung genuinely maintained such a consistently positive reputation that substantive criticism never emerged.[^19]

Some library professionals might question whether the emphasis on technological modernization—library automation, computerized systems, digital databases—came at the expense of more fundamental needs such as collection development, staffing levels, or physical facilities. The significant financial investments required for automation projects might have diverted resources from other library services, particularly in institutions with limited budgets. While Fung's advocacy for technology is celebrated by supporters, critics might argue that a more balanced approach would have better served diverse user needs.

The concentration of Fung's teaching and leadership positions at elite universities—National Taiwan Normal University, National Taiwan University, Catholic Fu-Jen University, Tamkang University, National Cheng-chi University—might be criticized as reflecting an urban, elite-focused approach that neglected rural and disadvantaged communities. Critics could argue that her innovations primarily benefited already-privileged institutions while smaller libraries and underserved populations received less attention and support.

Her simultaneous roles as a library professional leader (president of the Library Association) and government official (Member of Examination Yuan) could be seen as creating potential conflicts of interest or at minimum concentrating too much influence in a single individual. Critics might question whether this dual positioning allowed for sufficient checks and balances or whether it enabled certain perspectives and interests to dominate policy discussions at the expense of alternative approaches.

Some observers might critique the apparently uncritical adoption of American library technologies and practices, questioning whether Fung's education and early career in the United States led her to transplant models that were not optimally suited to Taiwan's cultural, linguistic, and institutional contexts. A more critical perspective might argue for greater emphasis on developing indigenous approaches rather than adapting Western models.

The scarcity of information about her personal life and private experiences, while potentially reflecting her own preferences for privacy, might also be criticized as perpetuating a model of professional achievement that requires subordinating personal and family relationships to career demands. Feminist scholars might question whether celebrating her career necessarily validates a model of success that may have involved personal sacrifices not required of male counterparts.

The absence of documented controversies, conflicts, or failures in available sources might reflect hagiographic tendencies in biographical writing about deceased leaders rather than accurate historical representation. Critical historians might suspect that disagreements, mistakes, or contested decisions have been omitted from sanitized accounts, creating an unrealistically perfect portrait that obscures the complex realities of her career.
