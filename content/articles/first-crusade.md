## Summary

The First Crusade (1096–1099) was a military expedition launched by Western European Christians at the urging of Pope Urban II, culminating in the capture of Jerusalem on July 15, 1099.

It stands as one of the most consequential events of the medieval world, reshaping the political, religious, and cultural landscape of Europe, the Middle East, and the Byzantine Empire for centuries.

What began as a call to aid Eastern Christians against Seljuk Turkish expansion transformed into a massive armed pilgrimage that established Latin Christian kingdoms in the heart of the Islamic world.

The campaign drew tens of thousands of knights, peasants, clergy, and pilgrims from across Western Europe, united by religious fervor, papal authority, and the promise of spiritual reward.

The crusaders endured brutal hardships crossing Anatolia, survived a grueling siege at Antioch, and ultimately breached the walls of Jerusalem after a five-week siege.

Their victory, celebrated in Christendom as divine providence, was accompanied by a massacre of the city's Muslim and Jewish inhabitants that would leave a lasting stain on the historical memory of the Crusades.

The establishment of the Kingdom of Jerusalem and the other Crusader states created a Latin Christian enclave in the Levant that would endure, in various forms, for nearly two centuries.

The legacy of the First Crusade is vast and contested. It initiated a cycle of crusading that would occupy European attention for the next two hundred years and shaped Christian-Muslim relations in ways still felt today.

It provoked one of the earliest systematic waves of anti-Jewish violence in European history. It permanently strained relations between the Latin West and the Byzantine East.

And it introduced Europeans to the material and intellectual riches of the Islamic world, planting seeds for the Renaissance and the broader cultural transformations of the late medieval period.

## Historical Context

By the late eleventh century, the Christian world faced a dramatically altered geopolitical reality. The Seljuk Turks, a Muslim dynasty from Central Asia, had swept through Persia and into Anatolia, defeating the Byzantine army at the catastrophic Battle of Manzikert in 1071.

This defeat opened the Anatolian heartland — the Byzantine Empire's primary recruiting ground — to Turkish settlement and control, fatally weakening Constantinople's military capacity.

At the same time, the Seljuks had wrested control of Jerusalem and much of the Levant from the Fatimid Caliphate of Egypt, disrupting the flow of Christian pilgrims to the Holy City and increasing reports of mistreatment of Eastern Christians in the region.

In this context, Emperor Alexios I Komnenos of Byzantium sent emissaries to Pope Urban II in 1095, appealing for military assistance against the Seljuk threat.

Alexios likely envisioned a contingent of mercenary knights who could be integrated into Byzantine forces under imperial command. What he received was something far larger and far less controllable.

Pope Urban II, eager to assert papal authority and reunite the Latin and Greek churches after the Great Schism of 1054, transformed Alexios's request into a grand call to holy war at the Council of Clermont in November 1095.

Urban's sermon, delivered to a vast crowd of clergy and laity in an open field outside Clermont, France, called upon Western Christians to take up arms and march to the aid of their Eastern brethren, and to liberate Jerusalem from what he characterized as Muslim desecration.

The crowd reportedly responded with the cry "Deus lo vult!" — "God wills it!" — a phrase that became the rallying slogan of the entire crusading movement.

Urban offered a plenary indulgence to all who participated, promising full remission of the temporal penalties for sin to those who took the cross.

This spiritual incentive, combined with genuine religious zeal, economic opportunity, and the desire for adventure among the knightly class, produced a wave of recruitment unlike anything Western Europe had seen.

Within months, multiple armies were assembling across France, the Rhineland, and Norman Italy, preparing to march thousands of miles to the eastern Mediterranean.

## The Event

The First Crusade unfolded in two distinct waves. The first, known as the People's Crusade, was a disorganized mass movement of mostly non-combatant pilgrims led by the charismatic preacher Peter the Hermit and the knight Walter Sans-Avoir.

Setting out in spring 1096, months before the officially planned departure, this ragged army of perhaps 40,000 men, women, and children marched through the Rhineland.

Many in that army turned to violence against local Jewish communities, seeing no reason to march to distant lands to fight infidels while "enemies of Christ" lived among them.

They arrived in Constantinople to the dismay of Alexios I, who swiftly ferried them across the Bosphorus into Anatolia, where the Seljuks annihilated them at the Battle of Civetot in October 1096. Peter the Hermit survived, having returned to Constantinople before the slaughter.

The main crusading army — perhaps 60,000 to 100,000 people including around 7,000 to 10,000 mounted knights — departed in the summer and autumn of 1096 from various points across Europe.

The principal leaders included Godfrey of Bouillon, Duke of Lower Lorraine; Raymond IV of Toulouse; Bohemond of Taranto; Tancred of Hauteville; and Hugh of Vermandois.

These princes had sworn oaths to Alexios I to return to Byzantine control any former Byzantine territories they recovered — an oath most would subsequently ignore.

The armies converged on Constantinople in late 1096 and early 1097, creating enormous logistical and political tensions with their nominal Byzantine hosts. Alexios managed to move them across the Bosphorus by May 1097.

After successfully besieging Nicaea — which was surrendered to the Byzantines, not the crusaders, to Bohemond's fury — the army defeated a large Seljuk force at the Battle of Dorylaeum in July 1097 and pressed on through the scorching Anatolian plateau.

Men and horses died in great numbers from heat, thirst, and disease. By autumn 1097, Baldwin of Boulogne detached to seize Armenian Cilicia and the city of Edessa, establishing the first Crusader state.

The main army began the brutal Siege of Antioch, which lasted from October 1097 to June 1098. Antioch finally fell through treachery — a captain named Firouz opened a gate to Bohemond.

The crusaders then found themselves immediately besieged inside the city by a massive Seljuk relief army under Kerbogha of Mosul.

In a desperate sortie on June 28, 1098, the crusaders routed Kerbogha's army, an outcome they attributed to the miraculous discovery of the Holy Lance — the spear believed to have pierced Christ's side at the Crucifixion.

After months of political quarreling over Antioch (which Bohemond claimed and kept, violating his oath to Alexios), the crusading army resumed its march south toward Jerusalem in January 1099.

The Fatimid Caliphate of Egypt had actually recaptured Jerusalem from the Seljuks shortly before the crusaders arrived, calculating they might have better relations with the incoming forces.

The Fatimids offered the crusaders access to Jerusalem for unarmed pilgrims, but the crusading leadership rejected any settlement short of total control.

The army arrived outside Jerusalem's walls on June 7, 1099. After a failed initial assault, the crusaders constructed massive siege towers and launched a general assault on July 13–14.

In the early hours of July 15, 1099, Godfrey of Bouillon's men scaled the northern walls. By midday, the city had fallen.

What followed was a massacre of the population — Muslim civilians and soldiers, and the Jewish community who had sought refuge in a synagogue — that contemporary chroniclers described with a disturbing mixture of horror and triumph.

Blood reportedly ran through the streets. The Dome of the Rock was looted and temporarily converted to a church.

Godfrey of Bouillon declined the title of king, instead taking the designation "Advocate of the Holy Sepulchre," unwilling, he reportedly said, to wear a crown of gold where Christ had worn a crown of thorns.

## Multiple Perspectives

### Christian Crusader View

For the crusaders and their contemporaries in Western Christendom, the First Crusade was an act of sacred warfare — not merely a military campaign but a penitential pilgrimage armed with swords.

Pope Urban II had framed the expedition in the language of spiritual medicine: the knights of Europe, prone to violence and sin, could redirect their martial energy toward a holy purpose and earn remission of their sins in the process.

The theology of holy war drew on Augustine's concept of just war but extended it into new territory, promising not just justification for killing but positive spiritual reward for participation.

The cry "Deus lo vult" — God wills it — expressed a genuine belief that the expedition was divinely ordained and that the suffering and sacrifice of the march were part of God's providential design.

The Council of Clermont in November 1095 was the formal launching point, but the crusade's spiritual momentum was built by preachers across Europe in the months that followed.

Figures like Peter the Hermit drew crowds of thousands with vivid descriptions of Muslim atrocities against Jerusalem's Christians and the desecration of the Holy Sepulchre.

Contemporary chronicles — the *Gesta Francorum*, Fulcher of Chartres's history, Raymond of Aguilers's account — present the crusade as an unfolding divine drama, with God's hand visible in every victory and miraculous intervention.

The discovery of the Holy Lance at Antioch was interpreted as divine confirmation that the enterprise was blessed, and it restored the crusaders' morale at a critical moment of crisis.

When Jerusalem fell on July 15, 1099, crusader chroniclers wept with joy, describing the massacre as a righteous purging of a sacred space rather than an act of brutality to be ashamed of.

Godfrey of Bouillon became the iconic crusader hero: pious, selfless, refusing the royal title in Jerusalem. The establishment of the four Crusader states was understood as the fulfillment of God's promise.

For generations of European Christians, the First Crusade was a founding myth, a demonstration that a united Christendom, guided by the papacy, could achieve the impossible.

This narrative shaped crusading recruitment, crusading literature — the *chansons de geste* — and the political theology of the medieval papacy for two hundred years.

The concept of the indulgence, turbocharged by Urban II's promise at Clermont, became a permanent feature of medieval Catholic spirituality and eventually a major factor in the Protestant Reformation.

The crusader ideal — the knight as Christian soldier — shaped chivalric culture across Western Europe, from epic poetry to the founding of military orders like the Knights Templar and the Hospitallers.

### Muslim Perspective

For the Muslim world, the arrival of the crusaders was an unexpected and deeply traumatic shock. The Arabic sources of the period call the crusaders "Franj" — Franks — regardless of their actual origin.

The initial reaction in the Muslim world was one of fragmentation and disarray: the Seljuk empire was deeply divided by dynastic conflict, the Fatimid Caliphate was a rival power, and local Syrian rulers had little interest in coordinating a unified defense.

The fall of Jerusalem on July 15, 1099, and the massacre that accompanied it, left a profound mark on Islamic historical consciousness. Jerusalem — Al-Quds — is the third holiest city in Islam, the site of the Prophet Muhammad's Night Journey and Ascension.

The Crusaders' seizure of the city, their conversion of the Dome of the Rock into a church (the *Templum Domini*), and their slaughter of Muslim civilians were recorded by Muslim chroniclers with grief and outrage.

Ibn al-Athir, writing in the early thirteenth century, described rivers of blood and piles of Muslim dead in the streets and mosque courtyards, calculating the dead at 70,000 — likely an exaggeration, but expressive of the scale of the trauma.

The political response to the Crusader presence was slow to coalesce. For decades, the various Muslim powers of the region were as likely to ally with Crusader states against Muslim rivals as to unite against the Latin presence.

It was only with the rise of Zengi, the atabeg of Mosul, in the 1120s and 1130s that a coherent policy of Muslim reconquest began to emerge. Zengi's capture of Edessa in 1144 was celebrated across the Islamic world as the first major reversal of Crusader gains.

His successors, Nur ad-Din and then Saladin, would build on this foundation to construct the ideological framework of jihad — sacred struggle — as a mobilizing force against the Crusader presence.

In the long arc of Islamic historical memory, the Crusades became a defining episode of external aggression and eventual resistance. The narrative of Saladin's reconquest of Jerusalem in 1187 became a foundational story of Islamic resilience.

In the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, as European colonial powers carved up the Arab world, Muslim intellectuals and political leaders repeatedly invoked the Crusades as a historical template for understanding Western imperialism — a framework with enormous political consequences that persists to the present day.

### Jewish Perspective

The First Crusade inaugurated a catastrophic era for the Jews of Western Europe. As the crusading armies assembled and marched through the Rhineland in spring 1096, a question arose among many participants: why travel thousands of miles to fight the enemies of Christ when enemies of Christ lived in their midst?

This logic — brutal in its simplicity — drove crusader mobs and local participants to attack the thriving Jewish communities of Speyer, Worms, and Mainz.

The Rhineland Massacres of 1096 were among the worst episodes of anti-Jewish violence in European history to that point. In Worms, over 800 Jews were killed. In Mainz, a community of several thousand was almost entirely annihilated.

Some Jews chose martyrdom — the *kiddush Hashem*, or sanctification of God's name — killing their own children and themselves rather than submitting to forced baptism.

The sources for these events include both Latin chronicles and Hebrew martyrologies like the *Mainz Anonymous*, which preserve the Jewish community's own account of the horror.

The Bishop of Speyer attempted to protect his Jewish community and largely succeeded; the Bishops of Worms and Mainz were overwhelmed by the mobs. The massacres were not officially sanctioned by the crusading leadership or the papacy, but they were a direct product of the social and religious hysteria the crusade unleashed.

Jews also perished in Jerusalem when the city fell on July 15, 1099. The Jewish community there had sought refuge in the main synagogue, which the crusaders set alight. Jewish sources later preserved the memory of the burning of the community inside the synagogue.

The position of Jews in the Crusader states that followed was ambiguous at best: they were generally barred from living in Jerusalem itself under the Kingdom of Jerusalem.

The First Crusade marks a pivotal turning point in the history of Jewish life in medieval Europe. The Rhineland massacres established a template for the scapegoating of Jewish communities during moments of Christian religious excitement and social crisis.

Similar violence would accompany subsequent crusades and other episodes of religious fervor. The progressive restriction of Jewish civic life in medieval Europe — the exclusion from guilds, the association with moneylending, the blood libel accusations that emerged from the twelfth century onward — unfolded in the same cultural climate that the crusading movement had done so much to intensify.

### Byzantine View

Emperor Alexios I Komnenos had requested military assistance from Pope Urban II in the hope of recovering Byzantine territories lost to the Seljuk Turks, particularly in Anatolia.

He envisioned a manageable force of professional Western knights that could be integrated into Byzantine military operations under imperial command. What arrived at Constantinople was something very different: multiple independent armies led by proud Western princes who owed Alexios little loyalty.

The relationship between the Byzantine court and the crusading princes was difficult from the start. Alexios insisted that each crusading leader swear an oath to restore to Byzantine sovereignty any former imperial territories they recovered — a requirement most accepted grudgingly and few honored.

Bohemond of Taranto, a Norman prince with a long history of hostility to Byzantium, was treated with particular suspicion by Alexios, who rightly doubted his intentions.

When Antioch fell in June 1098, Bohemond claimed the city for himself in direct violation of his oath, establishing the Principality of Antioch as an independent Crusader state outside Byzantine control.

Some crusading contingents looted and pillaged Byzantine villages and towns along the route through Thrace and Macedonia. Godfrey of Bouillon's army engaged in open skirmishing with Byzantine forces outside Constantinople.

The princess Anna Komnena, daughter of Alexios, later wrote a detailed account of the crusade — the *Alexiad* — that portrays the crusading leaders with a mix of admiration and condescension, describing their courage and their avarice in equal measure.

Her account remains one of the most valuable primary sources for the First Crusade, and one of the few from a non-Latin perspective.

The long-term Byzantine consequences of the First Crusade were deeply negative. The establishment of independent Crusader states — particularly the Principality of Antioch — permanently strained Latin-Byzantine relations.

Byzantine emperors spent much of the twelfth century trying, with limited success, to assert suzerainty over the Crusader states. The mutual distrust and contempt that grew between the Latin crusaders and the Greek Byzantines across the twelfth century would culminate in the catastrophe of the Fourth Crusade in 1204, when the crusaders sacked Constantinople itself.

The seeds of that disaster were planted in the complex, contentious relationship between Byzantium and the First Crusade.

## Legacy & Consequences

The First Crusade's most immediate consequence was the establishment of four Latin Christian states in the Levant: the Kingdom of Jerusalem, the County of Edessa, the Principality of Antioch, and the County of Tripoli.

These states, defended by a thin layer of European settlers and the military orders of the Templars and Hospitallers, created a permanent Latin Christian presence in the eastern Mediterranean that would endure for nearly two centuries.

They also necessitated a continuous investment of crusading energy from Western Europe: the loss of Edessa in 1144 prompted the Second Crusade, Saladin's reconquest of Jerusalem in 1187 triggered the Third, and so on through seven or more major expeditions.

Contact with the sophisticated Islamic civilization of the Levant exposed Western Europeans to ancient Greek philosophy preserved in Arabic translation, to advances in mathematics, medicine, and astronomy, and to luxury goods that transformed European material culture.

The military orders founded to protect the Crusader states became major economic and political powers in their own right. The papacy emerged from the First Crusade with dramatically enhanced authority and prestige.

The human cost of the First Crusade was staggering. Tens of thousands of crusaders died on the march from disease, battle, and starvation. The populations of Antioch, Jerusalem, and numerous smaller cities suffered massacre or enslavement.

The Jewish communities of the Rhineland were devastated in the opening months of the campaign. The long-term geopolitical consequences — the deepening rift between Latin and Greek Christianity, the entrenchment of a narrative of civilizational conflict between Islam and the West, the acceleration of anti-Jewish violence in Europe — resonate across the centuries.

The First Crusade was a moment when religious idealism and brutal violence fused in a combination that would define the medieval world's most intense conflicts.

## Key Figures

**Pope Urban II (c. 1035–1099)** — The architect of the First Crusade. His sermon at Clermont in November 1095 set the entire enterprise in motion. Urban died in July 1099, two weeks after Jerusalem fell, reportedly before news of the victory reached him.

**Peter the Hermit (c. 1050–1115)** — The charismatic preacher who led the People's Crusade, the disorganized first wave of crusaders. His apocalyptic preaching drew thousands of non-combatants who were annihilated in Anatolia. Peter himself survived and returned to Europe.

**Godfrey of Bouillon (c. 1060–1100)** — Duke of Lower Lorraine and one of the principal leaders of the First Crusade. After Jerusalem's fall, he became "Advocate of the Holy Sepulchre," refusing the title of king out of piety. He died in Jerusalem in July 1100.

**Bohemond of Taranto (c. 1058–1111)** — The Norman leader who took Antioch for himself in violation of his oath to Alexios I. His establishment of the Principality of Antioch as an independent Crusader state was a defining act of the First Crusade's political aftermath. He was the most militarily skilled of the crusading princes.

**Raymond IV of Toulouse (c. 1042–1105)** — The oldest and richest of the crusading princes, Raymond was the pope's preferred lay leader for the campaign. He refused to swear full allegiance to Alexios I. After Jerusalem's fall, he established the County of Tripoli, the last of the four Crusader states.

**Emperor Alexios I Komnenos (1048–1118)** — The Byzantine emperor whose appeal to Pope Urban II set the crusade in motion, though the result was far larger and less controllable than he intended. His daughter Anna Komnena later wrote the *Alexiad*, one of the most important primary sources for the crusade.

**Kerbogha of Mosul (d. 1102)** — The Seljuk military commander whose relief army besieged the crusaders inside Antioch in June 1098. His defeat in the subsequent battle was the crusade's most dramatic military turning point and was attributed by the crusaders to miraculous divine intervention.

**Al-Afdal Shahanshah (1066–1121)** — The Fatimid vizier who recaptured Jerusalem from the Seljuks in 1098, shortly before the Crusaders arrived, and commanded the Fatimid forces defeated at the Battle of Ascalon in August 1099 — the First Crusade's final major engagement.

**Tancred of Hauteville (c. 1075–1112)** — A Norman crusader and nephew of Bohemond, Tancred was among the most aggressive military commanders of the First Crusade. He participated in the siege of Jerusalem, took a significant share of the spoils, and later became Prince of Galilee and regent of Antioch. He represents the crusading spirit at its most acquisitive.

**Baldwin of Boulogne (c. 1058–1118)** — Brother of Godfrey of Bouillon, Baldwin was the canniest political operator among the crusading leaders. He established the County of Edessa — the first Crusader state — through a combination of force and shrewd alliance with local Armenian Christians. After Godfrey's death, he became the first actual King of Jerusalem, founding the dynasty that ruled for nearly a century.

**Hugh of Vermandois (c. 1057–1102)** — The brother of King Philip I of France and one of the early crusade leaders, Hugh was the first of the major princes to arrive in Constantinople. He was effectively held hostage by Alexios I until he swore the required oath. His military role in the campaign was modest compared to the other princes, but his participation lent royal prestige to the enterprise.

**Firouz** — The Armenian captain of one of Antioch's towers who secretly negotiated with Bohemond and opened a gate of the city to the Crusaders in June 1098, ending the eight-month siege. His motives remain unclear — personal grievance against the city's Muslim garrison, religious sympathy with the Crusaders, or bribery. His act of treachery was one of the decisive moments of the entire First Crusade.

**Walter Sans-Avoir (d. 1096)** — A French knight who co-led the People's Crusade alongside Peter the Hermit. He led the advance guard of the People's Crusade into the Balkans and was among those killed at the Battle of Civetot in October 1096, when Seljuk forces ambushed and destroyed the disorganized pilgrim army in Anatolia.
