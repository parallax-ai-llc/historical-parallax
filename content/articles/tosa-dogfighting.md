## Summary

Traditional Japanese dogfighting using Tosa breed dogs continues in parts of Japan despite growing opposition. While regulated, animal welfare groups consider it animal cruelty.

## Practice and rules (high-level)

- Tosa dogfighting (often associated with Kōchi Prefecture) is sometimes described as being organized more like a sporting contest than clandestine dogfighting rings.
- Matches are typically supervised, with handlers and referees present, and rules may emphasize pushing and dominance rather than mauling.
- Even with rules and oversight, animal welfare advocates argue that the activity inherently causes stress, injury, and avoidable harm.

## Legal and social status

- Japan’s animal welfare framework and local regulations shape how such events can be conducted.
- Public attitudes have shifted over time, with increasing scrutiny from media and advocacy groups.
- Debates commonly focus on whether regulation can make the practice acceptable, or whether it should be prohibited as cruelty.

## Cultural framing and controversy

- Supporters sometimes present the events as part of regional tradition.
- Critics argue that tradition does not justify animal suffering, and that the practice conflicts with modern standards of animal welfare.
