## Summary

Fausto Coppi won the Tour de France twice and the Giro d'Italia five times. He was the first cyclist to win both in the same year.

## Racing Style and Reputation

Coppi became emblematic of modern stage-racing excellence: strong climbing, time-trial ability, and the capacity to dominate multi-week tours through both endurance and tactical intelligence. In Italian cycling lore he is often contrasted with Gino Bartali, with their rivalry seen as shaping post-war sport and culture.

## Major Achievements

Beyond his Tour de France and Giro d'Italia victories, Coppi accumulated wins in one-day classics and stage races, helping define what a "complete" road racer could look like in the mid-20th century.

## Later Years and Death

Coppi's death at a young age shocked the sporting world and contributed to his mythic status. Accounts emphasize how quickly illness progressed after his return from travel, and how limited diagnosis and treatment options were at the time.

## Legacy

Coppi is remembered as one of cycling's foundational legends. His palmarès, training approach, and the enduring narratives around his era continue to influence how champions are compared across generations.

## Selected Context

- **Giro–Tour double:** Winning both the Giro d'Italia and Tour de France in the same year is rare and is used as a shorthand for dominance across varied terrain and race formats.
- **Post-war cycling:** Coppi's peak years coincided with a period when cycling served as mass entertainment and national symbolism across Europe.
