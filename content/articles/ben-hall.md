## Summary

Australian bushranger active in New South Wales in the early 1860s, associated with a gang that carried out robberies and hold-ups. He was killed by police in 1865, becoming one of the best-known figures of the bushranging era.

## Historical Context

"Bushranging" refers to outlaw groups that operated in rural Australia, often targeting coaches, gold escorts, and small towns during the mid-19th century.

The phenomenon is commonly linked to:

- frontier conditions and weak policing over large distances
- economic volatility around the gold rush period
- local networks that could provide shelter, horses, and information

## Gang Activity (High-Level)

Ben Hall is associated with a group that staged multiple hold-ups, sometimes portrayed in later folklore as theatrical or non-lethal compared with other outlaws.

However, historical treatment generally stresses that bushranging involved:

- coercion and fear imposed on victims
- armed confrontation risks for civilians and police
- escalating government responses and special policing measures

## Death and Aftermath

Hall's death at the hands of police has been debated in popular memory, including questions about the circumstances of the shooting and the legal environment of the time.

His story remains a recurring subject in Australian cultural history, where bushrangers are alternately framed as:

- criminals preying on communities
- symbols used in narratives of resistance and national identity
