## Summary

US dropped 2.7 million tons of bombs on Cambodia, more than all bombs used in WWII combined. The secret bombing killed an estimated 150,000-500,000 civilians and destabilized the country.

## Operations and secrecy

The bombing campaign is commonly associated with U.S. operations such as **Operation Menu** (initially described as targeting Vietnamese communist sanctuaries) and later expanded strikes. A major element of the controversy is that parts of the campaign were conducted in secrecy, with limited transparency to the public and, at times, contested oversight within U.S. political institutions.

## Human and political impact

- The precise civilian death toll is disputed, with estimates varying widely across historians and datasets.
- Beyond casualties and physical destruction, the bombing is frequently cited as a destabilizing factor that intensified displacement, rural insecurity, and political radicalization.

## Relationship to broader wars

The bombing of Cambodia is usually analyzed as an extension of the Vietnam War into neighboring territory, reflecting the regional nature of the conflict and the difficulty of separating battlefields from national borders.

## Historical debate

Scholarly and political debate continues regarding:

- the military effectiveness of the campaign,
- the legality and constitutional process surrounding authorization,
- and how the bombing shaped Cambodia's subsequent trajectory in the 1970s.

## Sources and estimates

Because the campaign included classified components and because wartime record-keeping is imperfect, researchers have used multiple sources (official logs, archival releases, field surveys, and historical reconstructions) to estimate scale and impact, often reaching different numerical conclusions.
