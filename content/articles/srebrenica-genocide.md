## Summary

Bosnian Serb forces under Ratko Mladić executed over 8,000 Muslim men and boys in the worst massacre in Europe since WWII. Dutch peacekeepers failed to prevent the genocide.

## Context and Background

- The Srebrenica genocide occurred during the Bosnian War (1992–1995), a conflict that followed the breakup of Yugoslavia and included campaigns of “ethnic cleansing” across Bosnia and Herzegovina.
- In 1993, the United Nations designated Srebrenica and its surroundings a UN “safe area,” and Dutch UNPROFOR troops (Dutchbat) were deployed with a mandate that proved insufficient to deter a determined assault.

## July 1995: Key Events (High Level)

- Bosnian Serb forces advanced on the enclave in early July 1995 and entered Srebrenica on 11 July.
- Thousands of Bosniak civilians sought protection at the UN base in Potočari, while others attempted to flee through nearby forests toward territory held by the Army of the Republic of Bosnia and Herzegovina.
- Over the following days, Bosniak men and boys were separated, detained, and killed at multiple sites; mass graves were later used and, in many cases, bodies were moved to secondary graves to conceal evidence.

## Investigations, Trials, and Legal Findings

- International criminal investigations documented the scale of killings and the organized nature of the operation.
- The International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY) issued judgments that characterized the crimes at Srebrenica as genocide.
- In 2007, the International Court of Justice (ICJ) held that genocide occurred at Srebrenica and addressed state responsibility questions under the Genocide Convention.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- Srebrenica became a defining symbol of the failure of international protection during the Bosnian War and shaped later debates about peacekeeping mandates and civilian protection.
- Identification of victims has continued for decades through forensic recovery and DNA analysis, enabling burials and memorialization.
- Annual commemorations are held at the Srebrenica-Potočari Memorial and Cemetery, and the events remain a focal point of historical memory, political dispute, and genocide denial controversies.
