## Summary

Ethnic conflict between Hema and Lendu communities in Ituri province has killed tens of thousands. Armed groups backed by Uganda and Rwanda fought for control of gold and other resources.

## Drivers and Context

- The conflict is often described as an interaction between local inter-communal tensions (including land and identity disputes) and wider regional war dynamics in the Great Lakes region.
- Competition over gold and other resources, as well as control of trade routes and taxation, is frequently cited as a sustaining factor for armed groups.
- External involvement has been reported at different points, including support, cross-border movement, and shifting alliances among regional actors.

## Key Armed Actors (High-Level)

Public reporting commonly references:

- Hema- and Lendu-associated militias that mobilized around community security narratives.
- Broader Congolese armed group ecosystems that evolved during and after the Second Congo War.
- International and regional peacekeeping and stabilization efforts (including UN missions), with varying mandates and outcomes.

## Humanitarian Impact

- Large-scale civilian displacement, destruction of villages, and cycles of reprisal violence have been central features.
- The conflict has been associated with significant human rights abuses, including attacks on civilians and forced recruitment.
- Health and food security impacts are often amplified by disrupted farming, restricted mobility, and insecurity around markets.

## International Response and Aftermath

- Peacekeeping deployments and political agreements reduced some periods of large-scale fighting, but intermittent violence has persisted in parts of Ituri.
- The Ituri conflict is often used as a case study in how local disputes can be escalated and sustained by wartime economies and weak governance.
