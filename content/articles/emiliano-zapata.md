## Summary

Emiliano Zapata Salazar (1879-1919) was a leading figure in the Mexican Revolution and remains one of Mexico's most iconic revolutionary heroes. A mestizo peasant from Morelos, Zapata became the embodiment of agrarian reform and indigenous land rights through his steadfast advocacy for land redistribution under the slogan "Tierra y Libertad" (Land and Liberty). As commander of the Liberation Army of the South, he fought against successive governments to restore communal lands stolen from peasant villages during the Porfiriato dictatorship. His uncompromising commitment to the Plan de Ayala, which demanded immediate land reform, made him both a legendary champion of the dispossessed and a controversial figure who often clashed with more moderate revolutionaries seeking national unity and modernization.[^1][^2]

---

## Early Life and Background

Emiliano Zapata was born on August 8, 1879, in the village of Anenecuilco, Morelos, a small agricultural community located in south-central Mexico. He was the ninth of ten children born to Gabriel Zapata and Cleofas Jertrudiz Salazar, a mestizo family of modest means who worked as small-scale farmers and horse trainers. Unlike many peasants of his era, Zapata received some formal education, learning to read and write, which would later prove invaluable in articulating the demands of the agrarian movement. His family had a tradition of defending village lands, and his father and other relatives had been involved in local disputes against the expansion of haciendas (large estates) that encroached on communal properties.[^3][^4]

Growing up in Morelos during the long dictatorship of Porfirio Díaz (1876-1911), Zapata witnessed firsthand the systematic dispossession of indigenous and mestizo communities. The Porfiriato promoted modernization and foreign investment, but at tremendous cost to rural communities, whose communal lands were seized through legal manipulation and transferred to wealthy hacienda owners who cultivated cash crops like sugar cane. By the early 1900s, approximately 90% of rural Mexicans had lost access to land, forcing them into debt peonage on the haciendas. This experience of injustice profoundly shaped Zapata's worldview and political consciousness.[^5][^6]

As a young man, Zapata worked as a sharecropper and horse trainer, developing a reputation for his exceptional horsemanship and his dignified, independent character. In 1906, he attended a meeting in Cuautla where peasant leaders discussed legal strategies to recover village lands, an experience that deepened his understanding of the agrarian crisis. In September 1909, at age 30, the citizens of Anenecuilco elected him president of the village council, recognizing his literacy, integrity, and willingness to confront the hacienda owners. This position placed him at the forefront of the village's ongoing land dispute with the Hospital hacienda, setting him on the path toward revolutionary leadership.[^7][^8]

---

## Rise to Power

Zapata's emergence as a revolutionary leader coincided with the outbreak of the Mexican Revolution in November 1910, when Francisco I. Madero called for an uprising against the Díaz dictatorship. Zapata quickly organized armed peasants from Morelos and neighboring states, initially supporting Madero's Plan de San Luis Potosí, which promised political reform and some land redistribution. By March 1911, Zapata's forces had captured several towns in Morelos, and his Liberation Army of the South became one of the most effective revolutionary contingents. When Díaz resigned in May 1911, Zapata emerged as the dominant military and political leader in Morelos.[^9][^10]

However, Zapata's relationship with the new Madero government quickly deteriorated. While Madero advocated gradual, legalistic approaches to land reform, Zapata demanded immediate restoration of village lands. When Madero ordered Zapata to disarm his forces before land reform would be addressed, Zapata refused, recognizing this as a tactic to demobilize the agrarian movement. In November 1911, Zapata issued the Plan de Ayala, a revolutionary manifesto that declared Madero a traitor to the revolution and outlined a radical program of land redistribution. The Plan called for the expropriation of one-third of all hacienda lands for redistribution to landless peasants and the complete confiscation of properties belonging to those who opposed the revolution.[^11][^12]

The Plan de Ayala became the defining document of Zapata's movement and transformed him from a regional leader into a national symbol of agrarian radicalism. Unlike other revolutionary factions that focused on political restructuring or modernization, Zapata's movement centered exclusively on land reform and the restoration of indigenous communal traditions. His uncompromising stance attracted thousands of peasants to his banner, and by 1912, the Zapatista movement controlled much of Morelos and had supporters throughout southern and central Mexico. Zapata proved to be both an effective military commander and an astute political organizer, establishing a shadow government in Morelos that began implementing land reform even while fighting continued.[^13][^14]

Zapata's national prominence increased dramatically in December 1914 when, following the defeat of the Huerta dictatorship, the revolutionary coalition fractured. Zapata allied with Pancho Villa, the powerful revolutionary leader from northern Mexico, against the more moderate Constitutionalist faction led by Venustiano Carranza. In a famous meeting at Xochimilco, Zapata and Villa agreed to coordinate their military campaigns, and Zapatista forces briefly occupied Mexico City alongside Villista troops. However, Zapata showed little interest in national politics, quickly withdrawing to Morelos to continue implementing agrarian reform. This decision reflected his fundamentally localist vision: he sought to transform rural Mexico from the ground up through village-level land redistribution, not to capture state power for its own sake.[^15][^16]

## Major Achievements

Zapata's most significant achievement was the practical implementation of agrarian reform in the territories his forces controlled, particularly in Morelos. Between 1911 and 1919, Zapatista authorities oversaw the redistribution of hacienda lands to peasant villages, the restoration of communal property systems, and the establishment of pueblo-based governance structures. Villages received land titles, surveyed their properties, and established elected councils to manage collective resources. This represented the most comprehensive land reform program implemented during the revolutionary period and demonstrated that Zapata's movement was not merely destructive but had a constructive vision for rural society.[^17][^18]

The Zapatista administration in Morelos also attempted to maintain agricultural production and basic social services despite ongoing warfare. Zapata established a rudimentary tax system, organized the planting and harvesting of crops to prevent famine, and maintained some schools and municipal services. While conditions remained difficult due to military conflict and economic disruption, the Zapatista government proved that peasant movements could exercise administrative functions, challenging elite assumptions that rural Mexicans were incapable of self-governance. This practical demonstration of peasant capacity for autonomous organization became a crucial part of Zapata's legacy.[^19][^20]

Zapata's military leadership also constituted a major achievement. Despite facing better-equipped federal forces and lacking formal military training, he developed effective guerrilla tactics that allowed his outnumbered forces to resist government armies for nearly a decade. The Liberation Army of the South employed mobility, intimate knowledge of local terrain, and popular support to conduct hit-and-run attacks, ambushes, and sieges that repeatedly defeated federal expeditions into Morelos. Zapata's forces never exceeded 27,000 troops and often operated in smaller detachments, yet they successfully defended their territory against multiple offensives by armies several times their size.[^21][^22]

Beyond his immediate military and administrative accomplishments, Zapata's greatest achievement was articulating and embodying a vision of social justice that resonated across generations. The Plan de Ayala and his consistent advocacy for "Tierra y Libertad" gave voice to centuries of indigenous dispossession and peasant grievance. Zapata framed land reform not as charity or modernization but as restitution of stolen property and restoration of legitimate indigenous rights. This moral framework, combined with his personal integrity and refusal to compromise core principles, elevated him beyond a mere military leader to become a symbol of resistance against injustice. His image and ideals would inspire agrarian movements throughout Latin America for the next century.[^23][^24]

Zapata also contributed to Mexican revolutionary discourse by insisting that social reform, not just political change, was the revolution's fundamental purpose. While other revolutionary leaders focused on constitutional reform, political institutions, or national sovereignty, Zapata maintained that none of these mattered if peasants remained landless. This perspective forced the broader revolutionary movement to address agrarian issues seriously, ultimately contributing to the inclusion of Article 27 in the 1917 Constitution, which provided the legal foundation for subsequent land reform. Though Zapata did not participate in the constitutional convention, his armed resistance made clear that the revolution could not be consolidated without addressing peasant demands.[^25][^26]

Finally, Zapata achieved the rare distinction of maintaining his moral authority and popular legitimacy throughout his revolutionary career. Unlike many revolutionary leaders who became corrupted by power, accumulated wealth, or abandoned their principles, Zapata remained committed to his original goals and lived simply among his followers. He refused opportunities to enrich himself, declined offers of political positions that would have required compromising the Plan de Ayala, and consistently subordinated personal ambition to the agrarian cause. This incorruptibility became central to his legend and distinguished him from more opportunistic revolutionary figures.[^27][^28]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his legendary status, Zapata's movement and methods generated significant controversy and criticism, both during his lifetime and in subsequent historical analysis. His uncompromising approach to land reform and his refusal to negotiate with moderate revolutionary governments contributed to prolonging the civil war and may have delayed the consolidation of a stable post-revolutionary government. Critics argue that Zapata's intransigence prevented potential compromises that could have achieved significant land reform while avoiding years of additional bloodshed and destruction. His rejection of Madero's government, in particular, has been characterized by some historians as premature and counterproductive.[^29][^30]

Zapata's military campaigns, like all revolutionary warfare, involved considerable violence and brutality. While Zapatista forces generally maintained better discipline than many federal troops and avoided some of the worst atrocities committed by other factions, they nonetheless engaged in executions of prisoners, forced conscription, and appropriation of resources from local populations. Hacienda owners and their families were sometimes killed or expelled violently, and suspected government collaborators faced summary justice. While defenders contextualize this violence as inevitable in revolutionary warfare and often defensive in nature, critics point to it as evidence that Zapata's movement was not the purely virtuous struggle depicted in popular mythology.[^31][^32]

The Zapatista movement has also been criticized for its limited vision and localist orientation. Zapata showed little interest in or understanding of national politics, economic policy, or international affairs. His exclusive focus on land reform meant the Zapatista program offered no coherent vision for industrialization, urban development, labor relations, or other issues crucial to Mexico's modernization. When Zapatista forces briefly controlled Mexico City in 1914, they proved unable or unwilling to exercise national leadership, quickly withdrawing to their regional base. Critics argue this narrow focus reflected both the limitations of peasant consciousness and Zapata's personal intellectual constraints.[^33][^34]

Some scholars have questioned the democratic character of Zapata's leadership and the Zapatista movement more broadly. While village councils elected local leaders, Zapata himself wielded dictatorial military authority and made strategic decisions without broad consultation. His movement relied heavily on personal loyalty to Zapata as caudillo (strongman) rather than institutional structures or democratic processes. The Plan de Ayala, while rhetorically democratic, vested enormous power in the revolutionary leadership to determine land distribution and punish opponents. This authoritarian dimension, critics argue, contradicts the image of Zapata as a democratic champion of popular sovereignty.[^35][^36]

Zapata's alliance with Pancho Villa and his subsequent conflicts with the Constitutionalist government have also drawn criticism. Some historians argue that Zapata's decision to oppose Carranza's Constitutionalist faction was strategically misguided, as the Constitutionalists eventually implemented significant land reform through the 1917 Constitution. By aligning with Villa, who had his own authoritarian tendencies and limited commitment to agrarian issues, Zapata may have backed the wrong faction and prolonged the civil war unnecessarily. The Zapata-Villa alliance proved militarily ineffective and politically incoherent, raising questions about Zapata's political judgment.[^37][^38]

Economic historians have criticized the Zapatista land redistribution program for being economically regressive and inefficient. The restoration of communal landholding and subsistence agriculture, while socially just from a redistributive perspective, arguably hindered agricultural modernization and productivity. Haciendas, despite their exploitative labor relations, often produced crops more efficiently than small peasant plots. The destruction of haciendas and their replacement with subsistence farming may have reduced Mexico's agricultural output and contributed to food shortages. Critics argue that Zapata's vision represented backward-looking agrarian romanticism rather than a viable economic model for twentieth-century Mexico.[^39][^40]

Finally, some feminist scholars and historians have noted the patriarchal character of the Zapatista movement and Zapata's own personal life. Women participated in the revolution primarily as soldiers, cooks, and support staff, with limited leadership roles. The Plan de Ayala and Zapatista discourse made no mention of women's rights or gender equality. Zapata himself had multiple relationships and children with different women, reflecting traditional machismo attitudes. While this criticism must be contextualized within early twentieth-century Mexican norms, it complicates simplistic celebrations of Zapata as a liberator and highlights the movement's gender blindness.[^41][^42]

## Personal Life

Emiliano Zapata's personal life reflected the traditional values and customs of rural Morelos, though his revolutionary activities inevitably complicated his family relationships. In 1911, Zapata married Josefa Espejo in a Catholic ceremony in the village of Anenecuilco, though he had already fathered several children with other women. His marriage to Josefa, who came from a respected local family, provided him with social legitimacy and helped consolidate his position among the respectable villagers of Morelos. However, revolutionary warfare meant Zapata spent long periods away from home, and his relationship with Josefa remained somewhat distant.[^43][^44]

Throughout his life, Zapata maintained relationships with several women and fathered numerous children, both legitimate and illegitimate. He had a particularly significant relationship with Gregoria Zúñiga, with whom he had several children, and later with Petra Torres, who remained with him during much of his military campaigns. This pattern of multiple concurrent relationships was not unusual among Mexican men of his era and social class, though it has drawn some criticism from biographers. Despite these relationships, Zapata provided for his children and maintained connections with his various families when circumstances permitted.[^45][^46]

Contemporaries described Zapata as a man of dignified bearing and personal magnetism who commanded respect through his presence rather than verbal eloquence. Unlike the flamboyant Villa, Zapata was reserved and soft-spoken, preferring to listen rather than dominate conversations. He dressed distinctively, favoring the tight-fitting charro (Mexican horseman) outfits with silver buttons and ornate sombreros that became iconic elements of his image. His excellent horsemanship and skills with weapons enhanced his charisma among his predominantly rural followers, who valued these traditional masculine virtues.[^47][^48]

Zapata maintained strong emotional ties to his native village of Anenecuilco and to the state of Morelos more broadly. Even at the height of his power, he regularly returned to Anenecuilco and consulted with village elders about land disputes and community affairs. This rootedness in a specific place and community distinguished him from revolutionary leaders who operated on a national stage. Zapata's deep connection to Morelos meant he understood intimately the issues affecting his followers, but it also limited his ability to think strategically about national politics or build broader coalitions.[^49][^50]

Despite the violence and hardship of revolutionary warfare, Zapata maintained a reputation for personal integrity and simple living. Unlike many revolutionary generals who enriched themselves through confiscation of enemy property or corruption, Zapata lived modestly and avoided ostentation. He enforced strict discipline among his officers regarding the treatment of civilians and the distribution of confiscated goods, insisting that land and resources be distributed to villages rather than appropriated by military commanders. This ascetic quality contributed significantly to his moral authority and helped distinguish the Zapatista movement from mere banditry.[^51][^52]

## Legacy

Emiliano Zapata's assassination on April 10, 1919, in an ambush at the Hacienda de Chinameca, transformed him from a controversial revolutionary leader into a martyred symbol of agrarian justice and indigenous rights. His death at the hands of Carrancista forces, lured into a trap by Colonel Jesús Guajardo, generated widespread outrage and confirmed for many Mexicans the government's betrayal of revolutionary ideals. Within years of his death, Zapata's image began appearing in murals, corridos (folk ballads), and popular iconography, initiating a process of mythologization that would eventually make him one of Mexico's most revered national heroes.[^53][^54]

The Zapatista legacy profoundly influenced post-revolutionary Mexican land reform. Although Zapata did not live to see the comprehensive land redistribution of the 1920s-1930s, his armed resistance had forced the revolutionary government to take agrarian issues seriously. Article 27 of the 1917 Constitution, which provided the legal basis for land reform, reflected Zapatista demands, even if the Constitutionalist government initially implemented it cautiously. Under President Lázaro Cárdenas (1934-1940), massive land redistribution finally occurred, with millions of hectares transferred to ejidos (communal farms) in a program that explicitly invoked Zapata's legacy and sought to fulfill his vision. By 1940, approximately 49% of Mexico's arable land had been redistributed, representing the most comprehensive agrarian reform in Latin American history.[^55][^56]

Zapata became a powerful symbol that subsequent Mexican governments and political movements appropriated for their own purposes. The ruling Institutional Revolutionary Party (PRI), despite representing many of the moderate and authoritarian tendencies Zapata had opposed, consistently invoked his image and rhetoric to legitimize their rule. Every Mexican president from the 1920s onward claimed to be fulfilling Zapata's legacy, even when pursuing policies that contradicted his principles. This official appropriation created a sanitized, state-sponsored version of Zapata that obscured the radical and uncompromising character of his actual political project.[^57][^58]

Simultaneously, Zapata remained a symbol of resistance for movements challenging the Mexican state. Indigenous communities, peasant organizations, labor unions, and leftist political parties invoked Zapata to critique government corruption, demand land rights, and challenge neoliberal economic policies. The Zapatista Army of National Liberation (EZLN), which emerged in Chiapas in 1994, explicitly adopted Zapata's name and symbols to frame their indigenous rights movement, demonstrating his continuing relevance to struggles for social justice. This counter-appropriation of Zapata as a revolutionary rather than establishment figure reflects the contested nature of his legacy.[^59][^60]

Internationally, Zapata became an icon of peasant revolution and Third World liberation struggles. His image and story inspired agrarian movements throughout Latin America, Asia, and Africa during the twentieth century. Revolutionary leaders from Mao Zedong to Che Guevara referenced Zapata as a model of peasant-based revolution, and his photograph appeared alongside other revolutionary icons in movements worldwide. "Tierra y Libertad" became a slogan adopted by agrarian and anarchist movements far beyond Mexico, demonstrating how Zapata's particular struggle resonated with universal themes of land rights and resistance to oppression.[^61][^62]

In Mexican popular culture, Zapata occupies a unique position as perhaps the most beloved revolutionary figure. Unlike Villa, whose legacy remains controversial, or Carranza, who is often viewed as opportunistic, Zapata enjoys near-universal admiration across Mexican society. His image appears on currency, in countless murals and artworks, in film and literature, and in everyday conversation. The cultural representation of Zapata emphasizes his incorruptibility, his commitment to the poor, and his willingness to die for his principles, creating a mythic figure that embodies Mexican ideals of honor, courage, and social justice.[^63][^64]

Scholarly assessment of Zapata's legacy continues to evolve. Early histories, particularly those influenced by indigenismo and revolutionary nationalism, portrayed him as an almost saintly figure whose vision represented authentic Mexican values. More recent scholarship has complicated this narrative by examining the violence, limitations, and contradictions of the Zapatista movement while still recognizing Zapata's genuine commitment to agrarian reform. Debates continue about whether his uncompromising approach helped or hindered the revolutionary cause, whether his vision was economically viable, and how his localist perspective relates to national development. Despite these scholarly debates, Zapata's significance as a symbol of agrarian justice and indigenous rights remains undisputed.[^65][^66]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and admirers of Zapata emphasize his unwavering commitment to social justice and his incorruptibility as revolutionary virtues that distinguished him from more opportunistic leaders. Historian John Womack Jr., in his influential biography "Zapata and the Mexican Revolution," portrays Zapata as a reluctant revolutionary who took up arms only when legal channels for defending village lands proved futile, and who consistently prioritized the interests of peasant communities over personal ambition. Womack argues that Zapata's significance lies precisely in his refusal to compromise fundamental principles for tactical political advantages, making him a rare example of authentic revolutionary leadership.[^67][^68]

Latin American historians influenced by dependency theory and liberation theology have celebrated Zapata as a model of grassroots, bottom-up revolution that challenged not only Mexican elites but also the broader structures of capitalist modernity and Western imperialism. Mexican intellectual Adolfo Gilly argues that Zapatismo represented an alternative modernity rooted in indigenous communal traditions and collective ownership, offering a vision of development that prioritized social equity and cultural continuity over economic efficiency and integration into global markets. From this perspective, Zapata's "backwardness" was actually a prescient critique of capitalist development's destructive consequences.[^69][^70]

Indigenous rights advocates and scholars have praised Zapata for centering indigenous land rights and communal traditions at the heart of his revolutionary project. Unlike mestizo and criollo revolutionaries who viewed indigenous peoples as obstacles to modernization, Zapata recognized the legitimacy of indigenous territorial claims and the value of communal property systems. Anthropologist Eric Wolf characterized Zapata's movement as a "peasant war" that defended traditional communities against the forces of capitalist transformation, arguing that this resistance preserved cultural diversity and ecological sustainability threatened by hacienda expansion and commercial agriculture.[^71][^72]

Supporters also highlight Zapata's practical achievements in implementing land reform in Morelos, arguing that the Zapatista administration demonstrated peasant capacity for self-governance and productive agriculture. Historian Samuel Brunk notes that Zapatista-controlled territories maintained agricultural production, established schools, and provided basic social services despite ongoing warfare, challenging elite assumptions about peasant incompetence. The successful redistribution of land and restoration of village autonomy in Morelos proved that Zapata's vision was not merely rhetorical but could be translated into concrete social transformation.[^73][^74]

Contemporary social movements continue to find inspiration in Zapata's example, viewing him as a model of principled resistance to injustice. The Zapatista Army of National Liberation (EZLN) and other peasant organizations throughout Latin America invoke Zapata to frame their struggles for land rights, indigenous autonomy, and resistance to neoliberal globalization. Scholar June Nash argues that Zapata's legacy provides oppressed communities with a historical narrative of successful resistance and a moral framework for challenging contemporary forms of dispossession and exploitation, demonstrating his continuing political relevance.[^75][^76]

---

### Negative Views

Critics of Zapata argue that his uncompromising approach and narrow focus on agrarian issues contributed to prolonging the Mexican Revolution's destructive phase and prevented earlier consolidation of a stable government capable of implementing comprehensive reforms. Historian Alan Knight suggests that Zapata's rejection of the Madero government was premature and that continued cooperation might have achieved significant land reform without the additional years of civil war that devastated Morelos and cost thousands of lives. Knight argues that Zapata's political inflexibility, while morally consistent, was strategically counterproductive.[^77][^78]

Some scholars criticize Zapata's vision as fundamentally backward-looking and economically unviable in the context of early twentieth-century Mexico's need for modernization and industrialization. Historian Friedrich Katz argues that the restoration of communal subsistence agriculture could not provide the agricultural surplus necessary to support urban populations or industrial development, and that Zapata's program, if implemented nationally, would have condemned Mexico to economic stagnation and poverty. From this perspective, Zapata's agrarian romanticism represented nostalgia for a pre-capitalist past rather than a viable path forward.[^79][^80]

Critics also point to the authoritarian elements within the Zapatista movement, noting that despite its democratic rhetoric, the movement relied heavily on Zapata's personal charisma and military authority. Political scientist Enrique Krauze characterizes Zapata as a caudillo whose leadership style differed more in degree than in kind from other authoritarian revolutionary leaders. The movement's dependence on Zapata's personal leadership became evident after his assassination, when Zapatista forces quickly fragmented and lost political coherence, suggesting that the movement had not developed sustainable institutional structures or democratic processes.[^81][^82]

Some historians emphasize the violence and destructive aspects of the Zapatista insurgency, arguing that popular accounts romanticize guerrilla warfare while minimizing its human costs. The liberation of Morelos came at tremendous price: thousands died in combat, haciendas were destroyed, agricultural production collapsed, and the state's economy was devastated. Historian Michael Gonzales argues that while hacienda owners and federal troops committed atrocities, Zapatista forces also engaged in execution of prisoners, forced recruitment, and appropriation of property, and that warfare's brutality cannot be justified simply by the righteousness of the cause.[^83][^84]

Modernist critics argue that Zapata's localist orientation and lack of interest in national politics reflected intellectual limitations that made him unsuitable for leadership in a complex, modernizing society. Unlike revolutionary intellectuals such as Luis Cabrera or Andrés Molina Enríquez, who developed sophisticated analyses of Mexico's agrarian problems and proposed comprehensive solutions, Zapata offered only the simple formula of land redistribution based on pre-conquest precedents. Historian Arnaldo Córdova suggests that this intellectual simplicity, while appealing to peasants, meant Zapata could not contribute meaningfully to debates about constitutionalism, economic development, or Mexico's international position.[^85][^86]

Finally, some critics argue that the Zapata myth has been politically harmful, providing cover for governments and movements that invoke his legacy while betraying his principles. The PRI's appropriation of Zapata to legitimize a corrupt, authoritarian regime represents the ultimate distortion of his ideals, while contemporary movements that romanticize armed struggle sometimes overlook Zapata's defensive motives and specific historical context. Historian Ilene O'Malley argues that uncritical celebration of Zapata can obscure the complexity of agrarian issues and the real trade-offs between different development strategies, reducing serious policy debates to simplistic invocations of revolutionary symbolism.[^87][^88]

## Famous Quotes

> "I want to die a slave to principles, not to men." - Stated in a letter to Pancho Villa, reflecting his commitment to agrarian reform over political alliances

> "It's better to die on your feet than to live on your knees." - Often attributed to Zapata, though possibly apocryphal; became a rallying cry for revolutionary movements worldwide

> "The land belongs to those who work it with their hands." - Central principle of the Plan de Ayala and Zapatista land reform philosophy

> "I am resolved to struggle against everything and everybody." - From a 1918 letter expressing his determination to continue fighting despite military setbacks

> "If there is no justice for the people, let there be no peace for the government." - Articulating the Zapatista position that social reform must precede political reconciliation
