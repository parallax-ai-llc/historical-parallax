## Background

- **Full name:** François Duvalier.
- **Nickname:** "Papa Doc" (contrasted with his successor son "Baby Doc").
- **Training:** Educated as a medical doctor, with early work tied to public health and rural outreach.

## Rise to Power

- Duvalier’s political rise followed years of instability and military involvement in Haitian politics.
- He presented himself as a defender of Haiti’s Black majority and used populist messaging to build support.
- After taking office, he moved quickly to sideline rivals, weaken independent institutions, and build loyalty networks.

## Rule and Governance (High-Level)

- **Authoritarian consolidation:** Centralized decision-making around the presidency; opposition parties and independent media faced intimidation.
- **Security apparatus:** The regime relied heavily on loyalist forces rather than conventional checks and balances.
- **Political repression:** Widely associated with arrests, exile of opponents, and violence used to deter dissent.

## Tonton Macoute

- The Tonton Macoute became a symbol of Duvalier-era political violence.
- Functioned as a paramilitary force loyal to Duvalier personally, used to surveil, intimidate, and suppress opposition.
- Helped Duvalier counterbalance the regular military, reducing coup risk by fragmenting coercive power.

## Foreign Relations and International Context

- Haiti’s strategic position in the Cold War era shaped external engagement with the Duvalier government.
- International criticism often centered on human rights abuses and corruption, while pragmatic relationships persisted at times.

## Legacy

- Duvalier’s rule is commonly cited as a defining period in modern Haitian authoritarianism.
- The succession by his son institutionalized the Duvalier family’s grip on the state for another decade.
- Long-term impacts discussed in scholarship include weakened institutions, normalized political violence, and deepened mistrust of governance.
