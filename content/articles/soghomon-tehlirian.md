## Summary

Armenian assassin who killed Ottoman Grand Vizier Talat Pasha in Berlin in 1921 as part of Operation Nemesis to avenge the Armenian Genocide. He was acquitted by a German jury who sympathized with his motive.

## Historical background

Tehlirian's assassination of Talat Pasha occurred in the aftermath of World War I and the mass atrocities against Armenians in the Ottoman Empire that are widely referred to as the Armenian Genocide. Talat, a leading Ottoman official, was viewed by many Armenians as a central architect of the deportations and killings.

## Operation Nemesis

The killing is commonly described as part of "Operation Nemesis", a covert campaign organized by Armenian activists to target former Ottoman officials they held responsible for mass violence. The campaign mixed political symbolism with the practical reality that many perpetrators were outside the reach of postwar tribunals.

## The Berlin trial and its impact

Tehlirian was tried in Germany and ultimately acquitted. The trial became notable not only for the facts of the shooting but for how testimony and argument shifted attention to the atrocities that motivated the act. In later commentary, the case is sometimes framed as an early moment when a courtroom became a venue for public discussion of mass violence and accountability.

## Legacy

Tehlirian remains a polarizing figure:

- For many Armenians, he is remembered as an avenger acting in the absence of justice.
- For others, he is discussed as an example of political assassination and the ethical questions around vigilantism.

The story continues to appear in debates about transitional justice, collective memory, and how societies respond when state crimes go unpunished.

## Cultural memory

Tehlirian's trial and acquittal have been retold in books, documentaries, and political discourse, often as a narrative about the limits of legal accountability after mass atrocity. The way the episode is remembered varies significantly by community and by the broader politics of genocide recognition.
