## Summary

FBI agent who spied for the Soviet Union and Russia for 22 years, causing devastating damage to US intelligence. He compromised numerous agents and operations. Considered the worst spy in FBI history. Died in supermax prison.

## Espionage Overview

- **Position and access**: As an FBI agent, Hanssen had access to sensitive counterintelligence information that made his disclosures especially damaging.
- **Tradecraft**: Public accounts describe use of dead drops, coded communications, and compartmented handling to avoid detection for long periods.
- **Motivation and pattern**: Reporting commonly emphasizes money as a major motive, with intermittent pauses and resumptions of spying over decades.

## Damage Assessment (High-Level)

- Compromise of sources and methods is a recurring theme in assessments of the case.
- The case influenced later US counterintelligence reforms, including internal security practices, auditing, and monitoring of privileged access.

## Arrest and Aftermath

- Hanssen was arrested in 2001 after an investigation that relied on surveillance and operational security changes.
- He pleaded guilty and received a life sentence; the case remains a benchmark example in counterintelligence training and institutional memory.

## Notes

- Public narratives sometimes differ on the precise extent of damage because many details remain classified; most summaries focus on the long duration and the breadth of compromised information.

## Key Takeaway

- The Hanssen case is frequently used to illustrate how insider threats can persist when organizations rely too heavily on trust and insufficiently on verification.
