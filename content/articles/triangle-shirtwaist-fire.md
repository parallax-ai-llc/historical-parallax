## Summary

Fire at the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory killed 146 garment workers, mostly young immigrant women. Exits had been locked to prevent breaks; led to labor safety reforms.

## Background

The Triangle Shirtwaist Factory operated in the Asch Building near Washington Square in New York City.
The workforce included many recent immigrants, and much of the labor was performed by young women in crowded workrooms.
Industrial sewing and garment production created common fire risks at the time, including scrap fabric accumulation and limited building safeguards.

## Causes and Contributing Factors (High Level)

Accounts of the disaster and subsequent investigations emphasized several systemic factors:

- Combustible materials in and around work areas
- Insufficient fire prevention and suppression infrastructure
- Barriers to rapid evacuation, including restricted exits and narrow stairways
- Limited emergency planning and training relative to the number of workers on the upper floors

## Aftermath and Reforms

The scale of the loss of life helped galvanize public attention on workplace safety.
In New York, the tragedy contributed to a wave of labor and building-safety reforms, including strengthened fire codes, egress requirements, and inspection regimes.
It also became a major reference point in the broader history of organized labor in the U.S. garment industry.

## Legacy

The Triangle Shirtwaist Fire is frequently cited as a turning point in:

- Factory safety regulation (fire exits, sprinklers, alarms, occupancy limits)
- The politics of labor protections and enforcement
- Public understanding of industrial working conditions in the early 20th century
