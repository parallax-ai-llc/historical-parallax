## Background

Tomás de Torquemada (1420–1498) was a Spanish Dominican friar who became the first Grand Inquisitor of the Spanish Inquisition. The institution was established in 1478 under the Catholic Monarchs (Ferdinand II of Aragon and Isabella I of Castile) with the stated aim of maintaining Catholic orthodoxy in their realms.[^1]

Torquemada is sometimes described as the nephew of the theologian and cardinal Juan de Torquemada, and later tradition debated whether the family had converso (Jewish-convert) ancestry; modern scholarship and sources summarize this as a contested claim rather than a settled fact.[^1]

## Spanish Inquisition context

In late-15th-century Castile and Aragon, religious identity was tightly linked to social status, law, and political legitimacy. Large communities of Jews and Muslims lived within the kingdoms; pressure to convert and suspicion toward converts (conversos and, later, moriscos) formed an important backdrop to inquisitorial policy.[^1]

The Inquisition functioned as a church tribunal with royal backing. It investigated alleged heresy and related offenses, often relying on denunciations, interrogation, and (in some cases) torture to obtain confessions.[^1]

## Grand Inquisitor (1483–1498)

Appointed Grand Inquisitor in 1483, Torquemada oversaw the early institutional consolidation of the Inquisition, including the expansion of inquisitorial tribunals and the standardization of procedures across territories under the Crown of Castile and the Crown of Aragon.[^1]

His tenure is closely associated with aggressive policing of suspected crypto-Judaism among conversos and with the broader program of religious uniformity pursued by the monarchy. Wikipedia notes that Torquemada was a prominent supporter of the 1492 Alhambra Decree, which ordered the expulsion of Jews from Spain unless they converted.[^1]

## Reputation and legacy

Torquemada's name became a byword for religious intolerance and cruelty, reflecting both the coercive practices of the Inquisition (including executions by burning for those condemned) and the way later political and cultural narratives portrayed the period.[^1]

At the same time, historical assessments often distinguish between:

- Torquemada's individual role as an administrator and advocate within an evolving legal-religious system
- The broader monarchy-driven project of state formation and religious centralization
- The long afterlife of "Black Legend" portrayals of Spain in European polemics

## Notes on numbers and uncertainty

Popular accounts sometimes cite precise totals for executions attributed to Torquemada. Modern summaries typically treat exact counts as difficult to verify because surviving records are incomplete and because practices differed across regions and time periods.[^1]
