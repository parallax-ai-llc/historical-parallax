## Summary

Roy Glauber was an American physicist who won the 2005 Nobel Prize in Physics for his contribution to the quantum theory of optical coherence.

## Scientific Contributions (High Level)

Glauber's work is closely associated with the development of modern quantum optics, especially the theoretical description of optical coherence and the statistical properties of light. This framework helped connect classical concepts (like coherence and correlations) to quantum descriptions involving photons and quantum states.

A common way his contribution is summarized is that it provided tools to distinguish different kinds of light (for example, coherent laser light versus thermal light) using correlation functions and measurement statistics.

## Recognition and Career Context

Glauber spent much of his career in U.S. academic physics, and his Nobel Prize was awarded decades after the core theoretical work, reflecting the long arc between foundational theory and broad experimental/technological impact.

## Why It Matters

Quantum optics is central to fields such as precision measurement, photonics, and parts of quantum information science. Glauber's ideas are frequently referenced in discussions of how light is detected, how photon counting is modeled, and how experiments quantify "quantumness" in optical signals.

## Key Terms (Brief)

- Optical coherence: how predictable the phase and correlations of a light field are.
- Photon statistics: how counts fluctuate relative to classical expectations.
