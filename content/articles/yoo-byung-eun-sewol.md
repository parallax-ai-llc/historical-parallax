## Summary

South Korean cult leader and businessman who owned the company that operated the Sewol ferry. He went into hiding after the disaster and was found dead in a field, cause of death unknown.

## Background

Yoo Byung-eun was a South Korean businessman and religious leader best known internationally for his indirect connection to the 2014 sinking of the MV Sewol. He was associated with the Evangelical Baptist Church in Korea (often referred to in Korean media as "Guwon-pa"), and he maintained business interests that included companies linked to shipping and logistics.

## Connection to the Sewol Ferry

After the Sewol disaster, public scrutiny focused on the ownership and management structure behind the ferry operator and on regulatory oversight in the maritime industry. Yoo was reported to have exercised significant influence through corporate control and related entities, leading investigators and journalists to examine whether cost-cutting, safety practices, and governance failures contributed to the scale of the tragedy.

## Investigation and Manhunt

In the aftermath, South Korean authorities sought Yoo for questioning as part of a broader investigation into the business network associated with the ferry operator. Media coverage described a period in which he avoided arrest, while police conducted searches and pursued leads connected to his associates and properties.

## Death and Uncertainty

Yoo's body was discovered in a rural field in 2014 in a decomposed state, and early reporting emphasized uncertainty about the precise cause and circumstances of death. The case generated significant public debate and speculation, in part because the discovery occurred after an extended search and because of the broader political and social pressure for accountability following the disaster.

## Public and Historical Significance

Yoo Byung-eun's name remains tied to the Sewol tragedy in South Korean public memory, alongside discussions of corporate responsibility, state regulation, and the treatment of victims' families. The episode is often referenced as an example of how opaque ownership structures and weak safety governance can have catastrophic consequences in public-transport systems.

## Notes

Because the Sewol investigation involved many institutions and individuals, summaries often distinguish between Yoo's corporate influence and the direct operational decisions made by the ferry operator and regulators. Public conclusions about culpability have depended on evolving investigative findings and court outcomes for multiple parties.
