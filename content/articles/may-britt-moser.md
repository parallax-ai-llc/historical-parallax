## Summary

May-Britt Moser is a Norwegian neuroscientist who shared the 2014 Nobel Prize for discovering grid cells.

## Scientific Contribution (Grid Cells)

Grid cells are neurons in the brain’s entorhinal cortex that fire in a pattern corresponding to positions in space, forming a grid-like representation that supports navigation. May-Britt Moser and collaborators helped identify and characterize these cells, which are considered a core component of the brain’s internal “positioning system.”

## Career and Institutions

Moser has been associated with Norwegian neuroscience research environments, including leadership roles and research group building. Her work is frequently discussed alongside that of Edvard I. Moser (a long-term collaborator) and the broader Trondheim neuroscience community.

## Nobel Prize Context

The 2014 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine recognized discoveries related to spatial representation in the brain. The prize is commonly described as connecting:

- Place cells (hippocampus) and grid cells (entorhinal cortex) into a navigational circuit.
- Fundamental neuroscience to potential clinical relevance, including questions about memory and neurodegenerative disease.

## Impact

The grid-cell discovery has influenced computational neuroscience, robotics-inspired navigation models, and broader theories of how the brain encodes abstract spaces. It also helped elevate Scandinavian neuroscience as a visible center for systems-level brain research.

## Further Notes

Popular explanations often compare grid cells to a biological GPS-like coordinate system, though the underlying neural computations are more complex than a literal map.
