## Summary

The Zodiac Killer terrorized Northern California in the late 1960s and early 1970s, killing at least 5 people and sending taunting cryptographic letters to newspapers. Despite being one of the most famous unsolved cases, the killer was never identified.

## How Suspects Were Developed

Because the Zodiac case involved multiple crime scenes, shifting jurisdictions, and a large volume of public tips, “suspects” emerged from many pathways: witness descriptions, geographic proximity, criminal histories, handwriting comparisons, and claims made in books or media. Some individuals were investigated formally by police, while others were proposed later by private researchers.

## Common Evidence Themes

High-level categories of evidence discussed in relation to suspects include:

- **Witness descriptions**: limited and sometimes inconsistent, but frequently referenced.
- **Handwriting and document analysis**: comparisons of letters and known samples; these methods are not definitive and can be contested.
- **Forensic traces**: fingerprints and DNA-related claims have been reported, though public conclusions have been limited.
- **Behavioral arguments**: interpretations of offender psychology, communication style, or knowledge of locations.

## Notable Publicly Discussed Suspects (Overview)

Public discussion often concentrates on a small number of recurring names. In general, each candidate has a mix of circumstantial links and unresolved problems, and none has been publicly confirmed as the Zodiac Killer by a conclusive evidentiary finding.

## Why Many Suspects Remain Unresolved

- Much of the strongest case material is not fully public, limiting outside evaluation.
- Some physical evidence is incomplete, degraded, or ambiguous.
- Media attention can amplify speculative narratives that outpace verified facts.

## Notes

Lists of suspects are best read as a history of investigative leads and public theory-making, not as proof of guilt. The case remains officially unresolved in the public record.
