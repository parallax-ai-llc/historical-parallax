## Summary

American arms dealer who at age 21 won a $300 million Pentagon contract to supply ammunition to Afghan forces, then provided Chinese-made ammo in violation of the contract. His story inspired the film 'War Dogs.' Sentenced to 4 years.

## AEY Inc. and Contracting (High Level)

- **Early entry into arms trade:** Diveroli began dealing in weapons-related surplus and contracts while still very young, leveraging online sourcing and subcontracting.
- **Pentagon supply contract:** The Afghanistan ammunition deal became famous because of the contract size relative to the firm's age and resources.

## Fraud Mechanics (High Level)

- **Contract restrictions:** The case centered on supplying ammunition that violated contractual terms (including sourcing requirements).
- **Misrepresentation:** Prosecutors described deliberate concealment and relabeling practices intended to make the goods appear compliant.

## Outcome and Cultural Footprint

- **Sentencing:** He was convicted in U.S. federal court and served a prison sentence.
- **Public narrative:** The episode became a widely discussed example of wartime procurement risk, weak vetting, and the incentives created by large contracting systems.
- **Film adaptation:** The story's dramatization in popular media helped cement the case as a cautionary tale about entrepreneurship without ethics.

## Key Takeaways

- **Procurement vulnerability:** Large, fast-moving wartime logistics programs can create openings for underqualified vendors and fraud.
- **Compliance is contractual:** Sourcing rules (including country-of-origin restrictions) are enforceable requirements, not optional preferences.
- **Reputational fallout:** The case became a durable example of how a single contract scandal can define a public biography.
