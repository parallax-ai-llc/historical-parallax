## Summary

Pakistani terrorist who was the only attacker captured alive from the 2008 Mumbai attacks that killed 166 people over four days. He was photographed carrying an AK-47 at Mumbai's CST railway station. Executed by hanging.

## Background

Ajmal Amir Kasab was a Pakistani national who became a key figure in public understanding of the 2008 Mumbai attacks because he was captured alive. His interrogation and subsequent trial were widely covered in India and internationally, and were cited by Indian authorities as evidence of the planning, training, and cross-border coordination involved in the assault.

## Role in the 2008 Mumbai attacks

The attacks, carried out by multiple assailants across several locations in Mumbai, unfolded over several days and targeted public spaces and hotels. Images of Kasab carrying a rifle at Chhatrapati Shivaji Terminus (CST) became emblematic of the event. Investigators alleged that the attackers arrived by sea and used small teams to strike different sites in parallel.

## Trial and legal outcome

Kasab was tried in India on multiple charges including murder and waging war against the state. He was convicted and sentenced to death, with appeals proceeding through higher courts. His execution by hanging took place in 2012 at Yerwada Jail in Pune.

## Significance

Because Kasab survived the initial operation and was prosecuted in court, the case became a focal point for debates about counterterrorism, criminal procedure in high-profile terrorism cases, and India–Pakistan relations in the wake of mass-casualty attacks.
