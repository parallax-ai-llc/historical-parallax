## Summary

Victorian-era British criminal who was a master burglar and murderer. He led a double life as a respectable citizen while committing crimes across England. He was famous for his disguises and dramatic escape attempts. Hanged at Armley Gaol.

## Background

Charles Peace was born in Sheffield and spent much of his life in northern England. He became known for combining technical skill as a burglar with a talent for social camouflage—appearing in public as an ordinary, even respectable, man.

## Modus Operandi

Peace was associated with careful reconnaissance, use of disguises, and an emphasis on quiet entry rather than overt violence. Accounts describe him as adept at avoiding identification and at creating plausible cover stories when confronted.

## Notable Crimes and Investigation

One of the most discussed crimes linked to Peace is the 1876 killing of Arthur Dyson in Sheffield, amid a dispute connected to Peace's attention toward Dyson’s wife. Peace was later captured after a confrontation with police in London in 1878, which helped tie him more firmly to earlier offenses.

## Trial, Execution, and Aftermath

After conviction, Peace was executed at Armley Gaol in Leeds. In the Victorian press, his case became part true-crime spectacle and part moral lesson, reflecting anxieties about urbanization, anonymity, and crime in rapidly growing industrial cities.

## Legacy

Peace’s story has been retold in popular culture as an example of the “double life” criminal: someone who blends into everyday society while committing serious offenses. He remains a notable figure in discussions of Victorian policing and the evolution of modern criminal investigation.
