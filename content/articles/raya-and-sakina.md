## Summary

Egyptian sisters Raya Ali and Sakina Ali who murdered at least 17 women in Alexandria in 1920 to steal their gold jewelry. They were the first women to be executed in modern Egyptian history.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1920 | Murdered women in the Labban neighborhood of Alexandria |
| 1921 | Arrested and convicted |
| 1921 | Executed by hanging |

## Historical setting

- **Alexandria** in the early 20th century was a major Mediterranean port city with rapid social change, migration, and uneven policing capacity.
- The crimes attributed to Raya and Sakina are commonly situated in the post–World War I period, when economic stress and urban insecurity were salient themes in press coverage.

## Pattern of crimes (high-level)

Most summaries describe a consistent pattern:

- women were lured to a residence,
- victims were killed in order to steal **gold jewelry**,
- bodies were concealed to delay discovery.

The victim count in many accounts is given as **at least 17**, which is why the case is often cited in discussions of early 20th-century serial murder.

## Investigation and trial significance

The case is frequently remembered for two related reasons:

1. **Its scale**: a large number of victims concentrated in a short time window.
2. **Its judicial outcome**: the sisters’ execution is widely described as a landmark moment for women’s capital punishment in modern Egypt.

As with many high-profile serial cases, later retellings focus on the interplay between neighborhood rumor, emerging forensic practices, and the role of police interrogation and testimony in assembling a prosecutable narrative.

## Cultural legacy

Raya and Sakina became enduring figures in Egyptian true-crime memory. The story has been repeatedly referenced and adapted in Arabic-language popular culture (including stage and screen treatments), often as a cautionary tale about deception, greed, and vulnerability in urban life.
