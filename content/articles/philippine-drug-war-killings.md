## Summary

President Duterte's war on drugs resulted in 12,000-30,000 extrajudicial killings by police and vigilantes. Many victims were urban poor with no connection to drug trafficking.

## What “Drug War Killings” Refers To

- The term commonly refers to deaths during anti-drug operations, as well as killings by unknown assailants linked by observers to the broader campaign.
- Human rights groups distinguish between:
  - deaths officially recorded as occurring in police operations, and
  - a larger set of deaths alleged to be extrajudicial or vigilante-style killings.

## Patterns Reported by Critics

- Allegations included:
  - targeting of low-income communities,
  - weak evidentiary standards for raids and arrests,
  - fabrication or planting of evidence, and
  - coercion of suspects and witnesses.
- The campaign was also associated with a climate of impunity, where accountability mechanisms were viewed as ineffective or slow.

## Domestic and International Response

- Philippine institutions (courts, oversight bodies, and the legislature) faced pressure to investigate killings and police conduct.
- International organizations and foreign governments expressed concern about due process and the scale of deaths.
- The International Criminal Court (ICC) became a focal point for debate over jurisdiction and state cooperation.

## Investigations and Accountability (High-Level)

- Reported accountability measures included internal police reviews and selected prosecutions.
- Critics argued that isolated cases did not address systemic issues such as command responsibility, incentives, and operational doctrine.
- Supporters of the campaign argued it was aimed at public safety and combating narcotics-related crime.
