## Summary

Congolese revolutionary who overthrew Mobutu in 1997 with Rwandan and Ugandan support, then turned against his backers, triggering the Second Congo War—the deadliest conflict since WWII with 5.4 million dead. Assassinated by a bodyguard.

## Early Political Activity

Kabila was active in Congolese rebel politics for decades prior to taking national power.
He operated in eastern Congo and was associated with leftist and nationalist currents that opposed the long rule of Mobutu Sese Seko.

## Rise to Power (1996–1997)

Kabila emerged as the figurehead of the Alliance of Democratic Forces for the Liberation of Congo (AFDL), a coalition that advanced westward during the First Congo War.
The fall of Mobutu in 1997 led to the country being renamed the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC).

## Governance and Regional Relations

Once in office, Kabila consolidated authority and sought to reduce the influence of foreign military forces that had helped him seize power.
The rapid breakdown of relations with former allies contributed to the outbreak of the Second Congo War, which drew in multiple regional states and armed groups.

## Assassination and Succession

Kabila was assassinated in January 2001.
His son, Joseph Kabila, succeeded him as president, and the conflict continued for years amid complex peace efforts and shifting alliances.

## Legacy

Kabila remains a polarizing figure: credited by supporters with ending Mobutu’s era, and criticized by opponents for authoritarian governance and the instability that followed.
