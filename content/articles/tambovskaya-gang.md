## Summary

Powerful Russian organized crime group based in St. Petersburg that controlled much of the citys business and politics in the 1990s. Led by Vladimir Barsukov.

## Origins

The Tambovskaya (Tambov) group is generally described as emerging from networks of men who moved from Russia’s Tambov region to Saint Petersburg during the late Soviet and early post-Soviet period. In the 1990s, rapid privatization and weak institutions created opportunities for protection rackets and violent competition among criminal groups.

## Criminal Activity and Influence

Accounts of the gang’s activities include extortion, racketeering, and the use of intimidation to gain leverage over businesses. The group has also been associated with influence over ports, fuel trading, and other high-cash sectors, reflecting a broader pattern of organized crime intersecting with legitimate commerce in the era.

## Leadership

Vladimir Barsukov (also reported as Vladimir Kumarin/Kumarinov in some sources) is often named as a key leader. Media and investigative reporting have described a structure in which leaders used intermediaries and allied groups rather than a single centralized hierarchy.

## Law Enforcement Actions

By the mid-2000s, Russian authorities brought major cases against prominent figures tied to Saint Petersburg organized crime. Barsukov’s arrest in 2007 is frequently cited as part of a wider crackdown and a shift in the balance of power among criminal networks.

## Historical Context

The story of the Tambov group is commonly used as an example of how organized crime expanded during the turbulent transition of the 1990s. It also illustrates how criminal reputations can persist in public memory even as groups fragment or adapt over time.

## Terminology

In English-language sources, “Tambovskaya” is sometimes used as a catch-all label for allied networks operating in and around Saint Petersburg during the period. Analyses often note overlaps among groups, making precise boundaries difficult to define.
