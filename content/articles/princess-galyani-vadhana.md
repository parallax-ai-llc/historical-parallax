## Summary

Her Royal Highness Princess Galyani Vadhana, Princess of Naradhiwas (6 May 1923 – 2 January 2008), was a distinguished member of Thailand's Chakri dynasty and the elder sister of King Ananda Mahidol (Rama VIII) and King Bhumibol Adulyadej (Rama IX).[^1] An accomplished academic, she served as Professor of French at both Chulalongkorn and Thammasat universities, while dedicating her life to advancing education, music, science, and cultural preservation in Thailand.[^2] Her contributions spanned multiple fields including language education, classical music promotion, scientific olympiad development, and extensive charitable work.[^3] In 2021, UNESCO posthumously recognized her as one of the world's eminent persons for her remarkable contributions to culture and learning.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Princess Galyani Vadhana was born at 23:07 on Sunday, 6 May 1923, at No. 48 Lexham Gardens, London, England, to Prince Mahidol Adulyadej of Songkhla and Mom Sangwan Talapat (later known as Princess Srinagarindra, "the Princess Mother").[^5] As the granddaughter of King Chulalongkorn (Rama V) and Queen Savang Vadhana, she was born into one of Thailand's most significant royal lineages.[^6] Her father, Prince Mahidol, was a pioneering physician who studied medicine at Harvard University, while her mother came from a commoner background but earned deep respect throughout Thailand.[^7]

In 1933, when Galyani Vadhana was ten years old, her family moved to Lausanne, Switzerland, following the 1932 coup that ended Thailand's absolute monarchy.[^8] The princess later recalled this period fondly: "We were in a small country and we were just monsieur, mademoiselle, not prince or princess. Some people did not know we were a royal family. We were like Swiss children and we knew a simple life of ordinary people."[^9] This experience of living as ordinary students in Switzerland profoundly shaped her worldview and personality.

In 1935, she attended École supérieure de jeunes filles de la ville de Lausanne, a secondary school for girls, before joining the International School of Geneva in 1938 as a boarder.[^10] She proved to be an exceptional student, coming first in her school at the final examination and third in all of Switzerland.[^11] In 1942, she enrolled at the University of Lausanne to study chemistry, graduating with a bachelor of science degree in 1948.[^12] Concurrently, she pursued studies in social science and education for a Diplôme de Sciences Sociales Pédagogiques, involving teacher education, literature, philosophy, and psychology.[^13] It was during her Swiss education that she first encountered classical music, fostering a lifelong appreciation for the arts that would define much of her later work.[^14]

## Academic Career and Rise to Prominence

Upon returning to Thailand in 1950, Princess Galyani Vadhana began her illustrious academic career, becoming a Professor of French at Chulalongkorn University.[^15] Her love for the French language and culture led her to dedicate herself to teaching French language, history, and literature from 1950 to 1958.[^16] In 1969, she accepted a professorship at Thammasat University Faculty of Arts as the Head of Department of French Language and History, and concurrently as the Director of the Institute of French.[^17] She served at Thammasat University from 1969 to 1976, and in 1978, King Rama IX conferred upon her an Extraordinary Professorship of Thammasat, recognizing her dedication to academia.[^18]

Princess Galyani Vadhana was multilingual, knowing English, French, Italian, Chinese, and Sanskrit.[^19] From her extensive teaching experience, she recognized the problem of interrupted French language study between secondary and university levels.[^20] To address this issue, in 1977 she formed L'Association Thailandaise des Professeurs de Francais (The Association of Thai Professors Teaching French Language) to serve as a center for meeting and exchanging experiences among French language teachers and improving instruction at both secondary and university levels.[^21] She served as president of this organization between 1977 and 1981.[^22]

On 6 May 1995, her 72nd birthday, her brother King Bhumibol conferred upon her the noble title "Kromma Luang Naradhiwas Rajanagarindra" (Princess of Naradhiwas), making her the only female member of the Chakri royal family to have been bestowed this title during Bhumibol's reign.[^23] In announcing the honour, King Bhumibol stated that "the Princess, who was his only sister, had shared the joys and sorrows of life since their early years and was the most highly respected in that she had always given him support. Furthermore, it is a well-known fact that she has been steadfast in her devotion to the King in order that He would enjoy grace and glory."[^24] She had represented the king in carrying out various royal duties and taken exceptional care of their mother, which was a great relief to him.[^25]

## Major Achievements

### Educational Innovation and Science Promotion

Perhaps Princess Galyani Vadhana's most transformative contribution was her founding of the POSN Foundation (The Promotion of Academic Olympiad and Development of Science Education Foundation) in 1998, over which she presided as its first president.[^26] Her involvement with academic olympiads began after Thailand first participated in the International Mathematical Olympiad in 1989, when she saw the president of the Science Society of Thailand on television during a fundraising campaign and donated 50,000 baht of her own money.[^27] The aim of the POSN Foundation was to raise the standard of science and mathematics achievements nationwide to international levels.[^28]

In 2000, the POSN Foundation, in cooperation with the Ministry of Education and the Faculties of Science of leading national universities, established 17 POSN centers throughout the country.[^29] Following training in POSN centers, the foundation organized five National Science Olympiads in Mathematics, Chemistry, Physics, Biology, and Informatics to select students for international competitions.[^30] Her instrumental role in Thailand's participation in international science and mathematics olympiads expanded the horizons of Thai students, introducing them to global academic standards and fostering their potential to become world-class innovators.[^31]

### Classical Music and Cultural Preservation

A lover of classical music since her student days in Switzerland, Princess Galyani Vadhana supported various classical music foundations and was instrumental in promoting musical events, appreciation, and education in Thailand.[^32] In 2004, concerned about the lack of interest in promoting classical music in both governmental and private sectors, she established a Fund for Classical Music Promotion and became its President.[^33] The fund supported classical music and related activities, and provided assistance to Thai musicians for studying, competitions, and concerts both domestically and internationally.[^34]

Her commitment to the arts culminated in the establishment of the Princess Galyani Vadhana Institute of Music, initiated in 2007 as a royal project celebrating the occasion of her 84th birthday.[^35] Following her royal vision to develop Thai musicians to the highest standard and to educate new audiences, the Institute created its Education Populaire and Audience Development Projects for students, musicians, music scholars, and the general public to share in musical experiences, engage in dialogues, and learn from one another.[^36] The conservatory nurtures young Thai talent and exposes them to international music standards, with efforts continuing after her death to bring new audiences to classical music and raise more Thai musicians to international standards.[^37]

### Literary Contributions

Princess Galyani Vadhana was an accomplished author and translator. She wrote eleven poems about the royal family, translated three books, wrote ten tourist guidebooks covering Thai cultural sites, and published an academic article.[^38] Her interest in photography complemented her travel writings; she photographed important places during her travels at home and abroad for both artistic and academic purposes.[^39] She authored a memoir about her mother titled "Busy Fingers," written in both Thai and English.[^40] One notable translation was her French rendition of "The Spanish Coronation" by King Vajiravudh.[^41] As a child of nine, she had already published a journal called "Ruaen Rom," containing articles she had written with her friends at Srapathum Palace, demonstrating her early literary inclinations.[^42]

### Charitable and Social Work

Princess Galyani Vadhana's royal patronage projects encompassed traditional Thai arts, education, sports, and social welfare.[^43] She was president or honorary president of various organizations and foundations, including the Cardiac Children's Foundation (patronized by her since 15 June 1984), the Princess Mother's Charity Fund, and the Autistic Foundation of Thailand.[^44] The Cardiac Children's Foundation helps sick children, especially from poor families, receive treatment from professional cardiac doctors using mechanical heart valve replacement, heart pacemakers, balloon catheters, and surgery.[^45] The foundation also disseminates knowledge of cardiac diseases to doctors, nurses, and the public, and provides funds for pediatricians to study cardiac diseases either in Thailand or abroad.[^46]

She created her own foundation for funding the studies of young, gifted musicians, further demonstrating her commitment to nurturing talent.[^47] Her influence extended to cultural preservation through institutions bearing her name, such as the Princess Galyani Vadhana Institute of Cultural Studies at Prince of Songkla University in Pattani Province, established to safeguard the heritage of Thailand's southern border provinces.[^48]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Princess Galyani Vadhana's life and work were notably free from major controversies or public criticisms, which is unusual for such a prominent royal figure. However, certain aspects of her life merit examination for a balanced historical assessment.

### Marriage and Royal Protocol Controversies

In 1944, at age 21, Princess Galyani Vadhana renounced her royal status to marry Colonel Aram Rattanakul Serireongrit, a commoner and military officer.[^49] This decision, while personally significant, represented a break with royal tradition that required her to temporarily give up her royal title. The marriage ended in divorce around 1950, though few details about the reasons for the dissolution have been made public.[^50] While not a controversy in the modern sense, such decisions were uncommon in royal circles and represented personal choices that may have been viewed with some concern by traditionalists within the palace.

### Limited Public Engagement with Political Issues

Unlike some royal family members who took more visible stances on political matters, Princess Galyani Vadhana maintained a careful distance from Thailand's turbulent political landscape throughout the 20th century. During periods of military coups, democratic transitions, and political upheaval between the 1950s and 2000s, she focused primarily on educational and cultural work rather than political advocacy. While this approach allowed her to remain widely respected across political divides, some might argue that someone of her influence could have used her position to advocate more forcefully for democratic reforms or human rights issues.

### Elitist Focus of Educational Initiatives

While Princess Galyani Vadhana's educational programs, particularly the POSN Foundation, achieved remarkable success in elevating Thailand's scientific and mathematical achievements to international standards, critics might note that these programs primarily benefited already high-achieving students from schools with resources to support olympiad preparation. The focus on excellence and international competition, while valuable, could be seen as reinforcing educational inequality by concentrating resources on elite students rather than addressing fundamental educational deficiencies affecting the broader Thai population, particularly in rural areas.

### Resource Allocation and Royal Spending

Her elaborate funeral in November 2008, which cost 300 million baht (approximately $10 million or £7.1 million), drew some quiet criticism regarding the allocation of public resources during a period when Thailand faced economic challenges.[^51] While such ceremonies are traditional for senior royals, the expense raised questions about priorities when many Thais struggled with poverty and inadequate access to healthcare and education. However, these criticisms were muted out of respect for the monarchy and the grieving period.

## Personal Life

Princess Galyani Vadhana married twice during her lifetime. Her first marriage in 1944 to Colonel Aram Rattanakul Serireongrit resulted in one daughter, Thanpuying Dhasanawalaya Ratanakul Serireongrit (later Sornsongkram), born in 1945 in Switzerland.[^52] This marriage required her to renounce her royal status, though she would later have it restored. The marriage ended in divorce around 1950.[^53] In 1969, she married Prince Varananda Dhavaj, son of Prince Chudadhuj Dharadilok, Prince of Bejraburna and Mom Ravi Kayananda.[^54] Prince Varananda, a professional pilot, remained her husband until his death in 1990.[^55] Through her daughter from her first marriage, Princess Galyani Vadhana became a grandmother to Captain Jitat Sorasongkram.[^56]

Known as modest and self-effacing, the princess told an interviewer in 2000: "I don't like gala dinners. They're boring."[^57] She was described as one of the hardest working members of the Royal Family, going about her work quietly and without much fuss, and whatever she did, she did with her heart.[^58] She headed over 60 organizations and looked after each one herself with full knowledge and concentration.[^59]

Her personal interests were diverse and intellectually rigorous. She had a keen interest in photography, learning to take photographs not only as souvenirs but also for art and academic purposes, using these images in her travel writings.[^60] Her passion for classical music, which began during her studies in Switzerland, remained constant throughout her life; she studied piano at home with her younger brothers and liked to listen to classical music on the radio while doing homework.[^61] She was particularly interested in history and archaeology, viewing these fields as foundations of other subjects and believing in applying reasoning to support historical ideas.[^62] When visiting historical sites, she would research them beforehand and deeply question experts about restoration, excavation, and impact on neighboring communities.[^63]

Despite her royal status, she maintained intellectual humility, always saying that her thoughts were not always correct and she would accept new data if supported by reason and evidence.[^64] From childhood, she loved reading and writing; while studying Prathom 1 to Prathom 3 (primary grades 1-3), she read as many Thai language books as she could find.[^65]

## Final Years and Death

Princess Galyani Vadhana was admitted to Siriraj Hospital in June 2007, suffering from abdominal pains.[^66] Doctors discovered she had abdominal cancer and she remained hospitalized for treatment.[^67] In October 2007, her condition became more complicated when doctors reported that she had suffered an infarction on the left side of her brain as a result of occlusion of a cerebral artery.[^68] On 14 December 2007, the Royal Household Bureau released its 25th statement about her health, indicating she was feeling increasingly tired and becoming less responsive.[^69]

On 2 January 2008, the Royal Household Bureau announced that Princess Galyani Vadhana's condition had worsened during the night of 1 January, and she died at 02:54 on 2 January 2008 at Siriraj Hospital at the age of 84.[^70] There was a mourning period of 100 days starting from the day of her death.[^71] The prime minister announced that all government officials and agencies would wear black for 15 days while the cabinet would wear black for 100 days.[^72] In the 100 days of mourning, more than 2.3 million Thai citizens visited the Grand Palace to pay their respects.[^73]

The elaborate six-day funeral ceremony began on 14 November 2008 at the Grand Palace and ended on 19 November when her ashes were transferred to the Rangsi Vadhana Memorial at Wat Ratchabophit Sathit Maha Simaram.[^74] On 15 November, three processions comprised 3,294 soldiers, including the Phra Yannamat Sam Lam Khan (golden palanquin with three poles), an 18th-century seven-tonne palanquin carried by 60 men, and a two-century-old sandalwood golden teak urn holding her body seated upright atop an elaborately decorated 14-tonne golden carriage.[^75] At 22:00 on 15 November, King Bhumibol and Queen Sirikit, with the help of a hydraulic tappet, set alight a 40-metre-high funeral pyre.[^76] The temporary royal crematorium, constructed on the Sanam Luang parade ground over seven months, had been decorated with flowers, garlands, and carved banana stalks.[^77] The funeral was the first full royal funeral since 1995, when the king's mother Srinagarindra was cremated.[^78] Tens of thousands of Thais attended the funeral procession while millions watched on television.[^79]

## Legacy

Princess Galyani Vadhana's legacy continues to resonate throughout Thailand and beyond, particularly in the fields of education, music, and cultural preservation. Her advocacy for scientific and mathematical education, recognizing these fields as crucial to individual and national development, marked the beginning of Thailand's transition toward a knowledge-based society.[^80] The POSN Foundation continues to operate, maintaining the high standards she established and continuing to prepare Thai students for international academic competitions.[^81]

The Princess Galyani Vadhana Institute of Music stands as a living monument to her vision, nurturing young Thai musicians to international standards while making classical music accessible to broader audiences.[^82] Following her death on 2 January 2008, efforts continued with an eye toward bringing new audiences to classical music and raising more Thai musicians to international standards.[^83] The institute's Education Populaire and Audience Development Projects continue to serve students, musicians, music scholars, and the general public, fulfilling her vision of shared musical experiences.[^84]

In 2021, the United Nations General Assembly approved a resolution by UNESCO to include Her Royal Highness in its list of the world's eminent persons for her remarkable contributions to culture and learning.[^85] UNESCO recognized her as a "Person of International Importance" for her exemplary contributions related to her work in culture, education, and public service.[^86] This posthumous recognition acknowledged her significant contributions to promoting education in the applied sciences, medicine, public health, social administration, international relations, cultural exchange, religion, and arts for the benefit of Thais.[^87]

Numerous institutions throughout Thailand bear her name, ensuring her memory and ideals continue to inspire future generations. These include the Princess Galyani Vadhana Institute of Cultural Studies at Prince of Songkla University in Pattani Province, established to safeguard the heritage of Thailand's southern border provinces.[^88] Throughout her life, she was conferred many honorary doctorates and honors from foreign governments and international organizations in recognition of her continuous work in teaching and research of the French language in Thailand.[^89]

Her approach to royal duty—characterized by quiet dedication, intellectual rigor, and genuine care for Thailand's cultural and educational development—established a model of meaningful royal service that transcended ceremonial obligations. She demonstrated that royalty could make substantive, lasting contributions to national development through sustained commitment to education and culture.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and cultural observers consistently praise Princess Galyani Vadhana as one of the most accomplished and intellectually distinguished members of Thailand's royal family in the 20th century. Her academic credentials—including a degree in chemistry, multilingual abilities, and distinguished university professorships—set her apart as a genuine scholar rather than merely a ceremonial figure. UNESCO's 2021 recognition of her as one of the world's eminent persons validated the international significance of her contributions beyond Thailand's borders.[^90]

Educational historians credit her with transforming Thailand's approach to science and mathematics education through the POSN Foundation, which elevated Thai students to competitive levels in international academic olympiads.[^91] This achievement is particularly notable given Thailand's developing economy status during much of her active years; her vision anticipated the crucial role of scientific education in national development decades before it became conventional wisdom. The foundation's establishment of 17 centers across Thailand demonstrated her commitment to expanding opportunities beyond Bangkok's elite institutions.[^92]

Music educators and cultural preservationists celebrate her role in establishing institutional support for classical music in Thailand, where traditional Thai music had historically dominated the cultural landscape. The Princess Galyani Vadhana Institute of Music created Thailand's first world-class conservatory, producing musicians capable of competing internationally and enriching Thailand's cultural offerings.[^93] Her personal passion for music, developed during her Swiss education, translated into concrete institutional legacies that continue to benefit Thai musicians and audiences.

Her colleagues and students at Chulalongkorn and Thammasat universities remember her as a dedicated, rigorous educator who approached teaching with genuine passion rather than as a royal obligation. The establishment of L'Association Thailandaise des Professeurs de Francais demonstrated her practical approach to addressing systemic educational problems through professional organization and collaboration.[^94] Her intellectual humility—acknowledging that her thoughts were not always correct and accepting new data when supported by reason and evidence—exemplified scholarly virtue and stood in contrast to more authoritarian royal traditions.[^95]

Charitable organizations she supported, particularly the Cardiac Children's Foundation and the Autistic Foundation of Thailand, benefited from her hands-on involvement and genuine concern for vulnerable populations.[^96] Unlike some royal patrons who lend their names without substantive engagement, those who worked with her describe someone who educated herself about the issues and contributed meaningfully to organizational strategy and fundraising.

### Negative Views

Critical historians and social commentators, while generally respectful of Princess Galyani Vadhana's accomplishments, identify several limitations in her approach and impact. Her educational initiatives, while impressive in elevating elite students to international standards, did little to address fundamental inequalities in Thailand's educational system. The POSN Foundation's focus on identifying and training the most talented students for olympiad competitions, while successful on its own terms, reinforced a meritocratic approach that directed resources toward those already advantaged by quality schools and supportive families. Rural Thai students, who faced inadequate facilities, undertrained teachers, and economic pressures to leave school early, received little benefit from programs focused on olympiad excellence.

Some scholars note that her academic work in French language and literature, while personally accomplished, represented a somewhat colonial orientation toward European culture that was common among Thai elites of her generation. Her promotion of French language education and classical European music, critics argue, could have been balanced with greater emphasis on preserving and elevating traditional Thai cultural forms and Southeast Asian languages. While she did support some Thai cultural institutions, her primary passions lay with European high culture, reflecting the values instilled during her Swiss education rather than a fully decolonized cultural perspective.

Political analysts observe that Princess Galyani Vadhana's careful avoidance of political engagement, while allowing her to remain above partisan conflicts, represented a missed opportunity for royal influence during critical periods of Thailand's political development. During the military coups, democratic transitions, student uprisings, and political violence that characterized much of her adult life, she maintained focus on cultural and educational work without using her considerable moral authority to advocate for democratic reforms, human rights, or political reconciliation. While this neutrality was likely strategic and perhaps necessary given Thailand's lèse-majesté laws and political sensitivities, it meant she did not leverage her position for broader social transformation.

Development critics point out that the elaborate expenditure on her funeral—300 million baht during a period of economic challenges for many Thais—exemplified the resource allocation issues inherent in maintaining royal ceremonies and institutions.[^97] While such ceremonies serve cultural and symbolic functions, the contrast between lavish royal spending and widespread poverty raises questions about priorities and the distribution of Thailand's resources.

Finally, some feminist scholars note that despite her academic accomplishments and organizational leadership, Princess Galyani Vadhana operated within and largely accepted the patriarchal structures of Thai monarchy and society. Her decision to renounce her royal title to marry a commoner in 1944, while personally significant, reflected requirements that would not have applied to male royals in similar circumstances. Her later restoration to royal status and eventual elevation to the highest female title in the Chakri dynasty came through her brother's benevolence rather than independent right, illustrating the gendered nature of royal power in Thailand.

## Famous Quotes

While specific documented quotes from Princess Galyani Vadhana are limited in English-language sources, several significant statements reflect her character and philosophy:

> "We were in a small country and we were just monsieur, mademoiselle, not prince or princess. Some people did not know we were a royal family. We were like Swiss children and we knew a simple life of ordinary people." - Reflecting on her childhood in Switzerland, interview 2000[^98]

> "I don't like gala dinners. They're boring." - Interview 2000, expressing her modest, unpretentious nature[^99]

King Bhumibol's statement about his sister upon conferring her honorific title in 1995:

> "The Princess, who was his only sister, had shared the joys and sorrows of life since their early years and was the most highly respected in that she had always given him support. Furthermore, it is a well-known fact that she has been steadfast in her devotion to the King in order that He would enjoy grace and glory." - King Bhumibol Adulyadej, 6 May 1995[^100]

Those who knew her reported her intellectual philosophy:

> She always said that her thoughts were not always correct and she would accept new data if supported by reason and evidence. - Recollection of her academic approach[^101]
