## Summary

John Maynard Keynes (1883–1946) was a British economist whose ideas fundamentally transformed the theory and practice of macroeconomics. Working through the turbulent decades of the Great Depression and the Second World War, he developed a framework explaining why market economies could fall into prolonged slumps and how governments could intervene to restore prosperity.

His landmark 1936 work, *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money*, overthrew classical assumptions that markets naturally self-correct and introduced the concept of aggregate demand as the primary driver of economic output. The policy prescriptions that emerged from his thinking — deficit spending, fiscal stimulus, active monetary management — shaped the economic governance of Western nations for decades.

Keynes was equally influential as a public intellectual, a Treasury official, and an architect of the postwar international financial order. His imprint on modern economics, central banking, and international institutions remains profound more than 75 years after his death.

## Early Life & Background

John Maynard Keynes was born on 5 June 1883 in Cambridge, England, to John Neville Keynes, an economist and logician, and Florence Ada Brown, a social reformer and later Mayor of Cambridge. He grew up in an intellectually stimulating household deeply embedded in the university world.

He won a scholarship to Eton College, where he excelled in mathematics and classics. In 1902 he entered King's College, Cambridge, studying mathematics before gravitating toward economics under the influence of Alfred Marshall and Arthur Pigou. He graduated in 1905, took the Civil Service examination in 1906, and joined the India Office. He returned to Cambridge in 1908 as a lecturer and fellow of King's College, where he would remain a central figure for the rest of his life.

## Rise to Prominence

Keynes gained his first public profile with *Indian Currency and Finance* (1913), which impressed the Royal Commission on Indian Currency and Finance. During the First World War he served in the Treasury, advising on wartime finance with considerable distinction.

His international reputation crystallised with *The Economic Consequences of the Peace* (1919), a brilliant polemic condemning the punitive reparations imposed on Germany at Versailles. The book sold over 100,000 copies in its first year and made him a celebrated public figure. Through the 1920s he continued to critique deflationary orthodoxy, opposing Britain's return to the gold standard at pre-war parity in 1925 in his pamphlet *The Economic Consequences of Mr. Churchill*.

## Key Achievements

- **The General Theory (1936):** Redefined macroeconomics by arguing that aggregate demand determines output and employment, that economies can equilibrate below full employment, and that government spending can close the gap.
- **Bretton Woods (1944):** As chief British negotiator, Keynes was the principal architect of the international monetary framework that created the IMF and World Bank, anchoring postwar global finance.
- **Probability theory:** His earlier *A Treatise on Probability* (1921) remains a respected contribution to the philosophy of probability.
- **Arts patronage:** He founded the Arts Theatre in Cambridge (1936) and chaired the Arts Council of Great Britain from 1942 until his death.

## Controversies & Criticism

Keynes's framework drew sustained criticism from multiple directions. Friedrich Hayek and the Austrian school argued that government intervention distorts price signals and generates long-run instability. Milton Friedman and the monetarists attributed the stagflation of the 1970s to Keynesian demand management, contending that fiscal multipliers are smaller than Keynesians claimed and that monetary policy is a more reliable tool.

Critics also noted that Keynes's framework was largely static and short-run, with limited attention to growth dynamics or supply-side constraints. His famous quip — "In the long run we are all dead" — was taken to represent a broader disregard for the consequences of sustained deficits. Some historians have also pointed to elitist attitudes in his writings and personal correspondence, particularly regarding eugenics, reflecting views common among his social circle that later came under heavy moral scrutiny.

## Legacy & Influence

The post-1945 era of managed capitalism — characterised by full-employment policies, welfare states, and regulated international finance — was built substantially on Keynesian principles. Even after the monetarist critique gained ground in the 1980s, Keynesian ideas returned forcefully during the 2008 global financial crisis, when governments worldwide deployed large fiscal stimulus packages explicitly framed in his terms.

New Keynesian economics, incorporating rational expectations and price rigidity, became the dominant paradigm in academic macroeconomics and central bank modelling by the early 21st century. Keynes died on 21 April 1946, aged 62, shortly after attending the inaugural meetings of the IMF and World Bank he had helped create — institutions still operating under frameworks he designed.
