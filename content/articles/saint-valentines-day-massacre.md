## Summary

Mass murder of seven members of Bugs Morans gang in Chicago on February 14, 1929. The killers, disguised as police, were believed to have been sent by Al Capone.

## What Happened

The Saint Valentine’s Day Massacre was a 1929 gangland mass murder in Chicago in which seven men associated with George “Bugs” Moran’s organization were killed. The assailants were reported to have used police-like uniforms or a police ruse to control the victims before shooting them, contributing to the event’s enduring notoriety.

## Historical Context

The massacre took place during the Prohibition era, when organized crime groups fought for control of illicit alcohol distribution and related rackets. It became emblematic of the period’s violence and the public perception that criminal networks had grown powerful and brazen.

## Investigation and Legacy

Although Al Capone’s organization was widely suspected, the killings were never definitively solved in a way that produced universally accepted convictions for the planners. The case influenced popular culture for decades and is frequently referenced in books and films about Prohibition-era Chicago.

The event also contributed to the national profile of federal and local efforts against organized crime, shaping narratives about law enforcement’s capacity (and limits) in confronting powerful criminal syndicates.
