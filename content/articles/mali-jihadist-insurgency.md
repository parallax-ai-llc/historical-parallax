## Summary

Tuareg separatists and jihadist groups seized northern Mali before French intervention. The conflict has killed thousands and displaced millions across the Sahel region.

## Origins and drivers

The insurgency is commonly traced to the 2012 crisis in Mali, shaped by long-running grievances in the north, the return of armed fighters and weapons after the fall of Libya's government in 2011, and weak state presence in large desert regions. Early phases involved both separatist and jihadist factions, with shifting coalitions and rivalries.

## Main actors (high level)

Reporting and open-source analysis describe a complex landscape that has included Tuareg separatist groups, al-Qaeda-aligned coalitions, Islamic State-linked factions, local militias, and Malian state forces. Group names, leadership, and alignments have changed over time, complicating attribution and conflict mapping.

## International interventions

France intervened militarily in 2013 (Operation Serval), later transitioning to broader counterterrorism operations in the Sahel (including Operation Barkhane). The United Nations deployed a stabilization mission (MINUSMA) and regional/international partners have supported security and governance initiatives through training and assistance programs.

## Political instability

The conflict has unfolded alongside recurring political crises in Mali, including coups and contested transitions. Analysts often link these dynamics to governance challenges, civil-military relations, and public frustration with insecurity.

## Humanitarian and regional impact

The violence has contributed to civilian casualties, displacement, and disruption of livelihoods, with spillover effects into neighboring states. The insurgency is often cited as a central driver of the broader Sahel security crisis, affecting cross-border trade, migration, and communal relations.
