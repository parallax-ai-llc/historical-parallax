## Summary

Post-election violence in Kenya killed over 1,100 people and displaced 600,000 after the disputed 2007 presidential election. Ethnic cleansing and sexual violence were widespread.

## Background

- Kenya’s December 2007 presidential election was closely contested and followed intense political and ethnic polarization.
- Allegations of vote rigging and irregularities contributed to a widespread belief that the announced results lacked legitimacy.

## Major Patterns of Violence (2007–2008)

- **Inter-communal attacks:** Violence frequently tracked ethnic and regional lines, including retaliatory cycles.
- **Forced displacement:** Large numbers of civilians fled or were expelled from homes, creating prolonged internal displacement.
- **Sexual violence:** Reports documented sexual violence as part of the broader crisis.
- **Property destruction:** Homes and businesses were burned or looted in multiple locations.

## Political Response and Mediation

- International and regional actors pressed for de-escalation and negotiations.
- Former UN Secretary-General **Kofi Annan** led mediation efforts that resulted in a power-sharing arrangement intended to end the immediate crisis.

## Accountability and Legal Proceedings

- The violence became a major test of Kenya’s institutions and accountability mechanisms.
- Subsequent investigations and international attention included proceedings associated with the International Criminal Court (ICC), alongside domestic political debate over responsibility.

## Longer-Term Impact

- The crisis influenced constitutional and electoral reforms, with later efforts aimed at reducing the risk of repeat violence.
- The events remained a reference point in Kenyan politics, shaping discourse about national cohesion, justice, and election administration.
