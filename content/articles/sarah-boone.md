## Summary

Sarah Boone was an African American inventor who patented an improved ironing board in 1892, designed specifically for the sleeves and bodies of women's garments. Her narrow, curved board made pressing clothes easier and more efficient. She was one of the first African American women to receive a U.S. patent.

## Additional Details

- **Problem addressed**: traditional flat boards made it difficult to press fitted, curved garment areas; Boone’s design emphasized a narrower profile that better matched sleeves and bodices.[^1]
- **Design idea**: widely summarized as a board with a tapered, curved form that could be turned and repositioned inside tubular garment sections to reduce creasing.[^1]
- **Work context**: as a dressmaker, she would have been familiar with the time costs of pressing finished garments and the practical constraints of household tools in the late 19th century.[^1]
- **Historical significance**: her patent is frequently cited in discussions of African American inventors and Black women’s participation in U.S. patenting during the post–Civil War period.[^1]
- **Limitations of the record**: like many 19th-century inventors, public biographical detail is sparse; many summaries focus on the patent and its place in broader social history.[^1]
