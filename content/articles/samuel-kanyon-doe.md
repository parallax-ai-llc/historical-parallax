## Summary

Liberian master sergeant who led a coup and became president before being captured and tortured to death by Prince Johnson in an event filmed on video. His regime was marked by ethnic favoritism.

## Rise to power

Doe emerged from the Armed Forces of Liberia and became the leading figure in the 1980 coup that ended the True Whig Party’s long rule.
After the coup, the new military leadership formed the People’s Redemption Council, with Doe as the dominant actor.
His government sought international recognition and, during the Cold War, became important to some foreign partners as a regional ally.

## Governance and political conflict

Accounts of Doe’s presidency emphasize authoritarian rule, repression of opponents, and the politicization of security forces.
Liberian politics during this period became increasingly shaped by ethnic and factional alignments, which intensified mistrust and violence.
The 1985 election is widely described as contested and lacking credibility, and it was followed by further instability.

## Civil war and death

In 1989, Charles Taylor’s NPFL insurgency helped trigger the First Liberian Civil War, rapidly weakening Doe’s control outside Monrovia.
Doe was captured in 1990 by forces loyal to Prince Yormie Johnson, and he was killed shortly afterward.
The circumstances of his capture and killing became internationally notorious and are often cited in discussions of wartime atrocities.

## Historical assessment

Doe is commonly remembered as a pivotal figure in Liberia’s late-20th-century crisis, linking the end of one political order to the start of prolonged civil conflict.
His tenure is used in case studies on coups, state fragility, and how exclusionary politics can accelerate cycles of retaliation.
