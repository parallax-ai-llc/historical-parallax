## Summary

Clerow "Flip" Wilson was an American comedian and actor who became one of the most popular entertainers of the early 1970s. His self-titled variety program, *The Flip Wilson Show*, aired on NBC from 1970 to 1974 and at its peak was the second-highest-rated show on American television, making Wilson the first Black performer to host a successful weekly primetime variety series. His work helped reshape what was possible for Black entertainers in mainstream American broadcast television.

Wilson's genius lay in character comedy. His recurring persona Geraldine Jones — a sassy, confident Black woman whose catchphrase "The devil made me do it" entered American popular lexicon — demonstrated his ability to create fully realized comic figures that resonated across racial and demographic lines. He brought wit, warmth, and a distinctly Black comedic sensibility to a mass audience at a time of profound social change.

## Early Life & Background

Wilson was born on December 8, 1933, in Jersey City, New Jersey, the tenth of twenty-four children. His childhood was marked by severe poverty and instability; his mother abandoned the family and his father struggled to provide for the children. Wilson spent much of his youth in foster homes and reform schools after being declared a ward of the state at age seven.

At age sixteen he joined the U.S. Air Force, lying about his age to enlist. It was during his military service that he discovered his talent for comedy, entertaining fellow airmen and earning the nickname "Flip" for his flippant personality. After his discharge in 1954, he began performing in small clubs, honing his craft on the so-called "Chitlin' Circuit" of Black entertainment venues throughout the late 1950s and early 1960s.

## Rise to Prominence

Wilson's breakthrough came through persistent television appearances. His first *Tonight Show* spot in 1965 was a turning point; Johnny Carson invited him back eighteen times. He released several comedy albums throughout the 1960s, and *Cowboys and Colored People* (1967) brought him wider recognition. By 1968 he was a frequent guest on major variety programs, and NBC offered him his own show.

*The Flip Wilson Show* debuted on September 17, 1970. It won two Emmy Awards and a Golden Globe and consistently dominated its Thursday night timeslot, drawing audiences of 40 million or more at its height.

## Key Achievements

- Hosted *The Flip Wilson Show* (NBC, 1970–1974), winning the Emmy for Outstanding Variety Series in 1971
- Won the Golden Globe for Best TV Actor in a Musical or Comedy (1971)
- First Black performer to produce his own television variety show
- His character Geraldine Jones became a defining figure in 1970s American pop culture
- "The devil made me do it" became one of the era's most recognizable catchphrases

## Controversies & Criticism

Wilson retired abruptly from entertainment in 1974 to raise his four children as a single parent following his divorce, a decision that effectively ended his peak career momentum. Some critics noted that his comedy, while groundbreaking in reach, occasionally relied on stereotypical character tropes. His later years saw intermittent attempts at comeback, none of which recaptured his earlier success.

## Legacy & Influence

Wilson's influence on Black comedians in television is substantial. He demonstrated that a Black entertainer could anchor a mainstream American variety program and attract a broad, racially mixed audience. Performers including Eddie Murphy and Dave Chappelle have cited him as a precursor. He died on November 25, 1998, in Malibu, California, from liver cancer, at age 64. He was inducted into the Comedy Hall of Fame in 1987.
