## Summary

Indian stockbroker known as the 'Big Bull' who manipulated the Bombay Stock Exchange in 1992, causing a securities scam worth $1 billion. He exploited loopholes in the banking system to inflate stock prices artificially. Died during trial.

## How the 1992 scam worked (high level)

Accounts of the episode typically describe a strategy that combined aggressive stock purchases with misuse of weaknesses in the inter-bank securities settlement system.
By obtaining bank receipts or similar instruments and routing funds through channels not designed for equity speculation, the scheme could temporarily create the appearance of legitimate liquidity.
The resulting buying pressure pushed certain share prices upward, which in turn attracted further speculative participation.

## Aftermath

The scandal became a major event in India’s financial history and is frequently cited in discussions of:

- Market regulation and enforcement capacity
- Settlement mechanics and controls in banking systems
- The risks of concentrated speculation and leverage

It also led to prolonged legal proceedings and public debate about accountability across brokers, banks, and regulators.

## Public and cultural impact

Mehta’s story has remained prominent in Indian popular culture and media discussions about markets.
Retellings often frame the episode as a case study in how incentives, weak controls, and charismatic narratives can drive bubbles and fraud.
