## Scientific Context

In the first half of the 20th century, biologists debated whether **proteins** or **nucleic acids** carried hereditary information. Before the structure of DNA was established and before modern molecular biology matured, the “genetic material” question was central to understanding how traits are transmitted.

## The Hershey–Chase Experiment (1952)

Hershey and Martha Chase used **bacteriophages** (viruses that infect bacteria) to test what part of a virus enters a bacterial cell to direct the production of new viruses.

The high-level logic of the experiment was:

- label **DNA** and **protein** in different ways (using radioactive tracers)
- allow phages to infect bacteria
- separate phage coats from bacterial cells (a step popularly associated with a “blender” procedure)
- measure which label is found inside the bacteria and passed to progeny phage

Their results supported the conclusion that **DNA**, not protein, is the primary hereditary material in this system.

## Bacteriophage Genetics and Methods

Hershey is strongly associated with bacteriophage research as a model system. Phage experiments helped establish core concepts and techniques for molecular genetics because they enabled:

- rapid generation times and large sample sizes
- clear phenotypes (infection and plaque formation)
- quantitative approaches to mutation and recombination

## Recognition

Hershey shared the **1969 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine** (with Salvador Luria and Max Delbrück). The prize is commonly framed as recognizing the development of bacteriophages as tools for understanding the genetic mechanisms of viruses and cells.

## Legacy

The Hershey–Chase experiment is frequently taught as a “hinge” moment in biology education: it did not by itself create molecular biology, but it provided an influential line of evidence that aligned with other findings and helped make DNA-centered genetics the dominant framework.
