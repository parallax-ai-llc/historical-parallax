## Summary

Tribal groups in the Indian Rebellion of 1857 who joined the uprising against British East India Company rule. Many were executed or imprisoned after the rebellion's suppression.

## Background

The 1857 uprising involved a wide set of actors beyond the better-known sepoy mutinies and court politics, including rural and tribal communities reacting to local grievances, changing land arrangements, and violence tied to the collapse of authority.

The term “Pahari” is often used for hill peoples in parts of North India; in accounts of the rebellion it can refer to communities operating in difficult terrain, where mobility and knowledge of the landscape shaped tactics and the ability to evade capture.

## Role in the 1857 Rebellion

High-level patterns described in secondary summaries include:

- **Local alliance-building:** collaboration with neighboring rebel groups where interests aligned.
- **Raids and disruption:** attacks on outposts, supply lines, and symbols of colonial authority.
- **Irregular warfare:** use of hills/forests for concealment rather than set-piece battles.

## Suppression and Aftermath

After the rebellion was suppressed, British authorities carried out widespread reprisals across many regions. For groups labeled as rebels or “criminal” bands, common outcomes included:

- executions and summary punishments
- long prison sentences or transportation
- confiscation of property and collective fines

## Legacy

In later historical writing, smaller or less-documented rebel formations are often difficult to reconstruct in detail. They nonetheless matter for understanding how the 1857 conflict spread through local networks and why it was experienced as both a political rupture and a social crisis in many districts.
