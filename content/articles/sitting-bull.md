## Summary

Sitting Bull (c. 1831–1890) was a Hunkpapa Lakota leader who united the Sioux tribes of the American Great Plains against white settler encroachment and U.S. government policies. As principal chief of the entire Oceti Sakowin (Sioux Nation), he led the resistance during the Great Sioux War, including the famous Battle of Little Bighorn in 1876 where allied Native forces defeated General George Armstrong Custer. A spiritual leader and political strategist, Sitting Bull became an enduring symbol of Native American resistance, advocating for the preservation of Lakota sovereignty, culture, and traditional way of life until his death in 1890.[^1][^2][^3]

---

## Early Life and Background

Sitting Bull was born around 1831 near the Grand River in what later became the Dakota Territory (present-day South Dakota), though according to family oral tradition from his great-grandson, he may have been born along the Yellowstone River, south of present-day Miles City, Montana.[^4][^5] He was born into a prominent Hunkpapa Lakota family—both his father and two uncles were chiefs in the tribe, establishing a legacy of leadership that would shape his destiny.[^6][^7]

At birth, he was named Ȟoká Psíče (Jumping Badger) and earned the boyhood nickname "Húŋkešni" or "Slow," an allusion to his careful, deliberate, and unhurried nature—qualities that would later define his strategic approach to leadership.[^8][^9] His father bore the name Sitting Bull, and his mother was called Her-Holy-Door, who was remembered as a very social woman, a good talker who could make people laugh.[^10][^11]

The young Jumping Badger demonstrated exceptional ability from an early age. At just ten years old, he killed his first buffalo, a significant milestone in Lakota culture that proved his hunting prowess and ability to provide for his people.[^12] At fourteen, he joined a Hunkpapa raiding party and distinguished himself by knocking a Crow warrior from his horse with a tomahawk during his first combat experience.[^13] In celebration of the boy's bravery and warrior spirit, his father performed a traditional honor by relinquishing his own name and transferring it to his son. From that moment forward, Jumping Badger became known as Tatanka-Iyotanka, or "Sitting Bull," inheriting not just a name but a legacy of leadership and courage.[^14]

Sitting Bull's formative years were spent mastering the traditional ways of the Lakota people. He learned the skills necessary for survival on the Northern Plains—hunting, horsemanship, warfare, and the spiritual practices that connected his people to the land and the Great Spirit. His mother, Her Holy Door, remained a powerful presence in his life until her death in 1884, while his father was killed by a Crow warrior in 1859, losses that shaped his understanding of the ongoing conflicts between tribes and the precious nature of Lakota independence.[^15][^16]

Growing up during a period of increasing pressure from westward American expansion, Sitting Bull witnessed firsthand the gradual encroachment of white settlers onto traditional Lakota hunting grounds. These early experiences planted the seeds of resistance that would define his later leadership, as he recognized the existential threat posed to the Lakota way of life by the advancing tide of colonization.

## Rise to Power

Sitting Bull's ascent to leadership was not sudden but rather a gradual accumulation of respect earned through demonstrated courage, wisdom, and spiritual power. In Lakota society, leadership was not inherited but earned through merit, and Sitting Bull proved himself worthy through his mastery of the four cardinal virtues of the Lakota people: bravery, fortitude, generosity, and wisdom.[^17][^18]

His military prowess was established through numerous battles against traditional tribal enemies including the Crow, Assiniboine, and Shoshone. By 1857, his war record and embodiment of Lakota values earned him the rank of war chief of the Hunkpapa tribe, and in the same year, he became head chief of the Hunkpapa.[^19] His reputation extended beyond military matters—he was recognized as a spiritual leader who possessed powerful medicine, having received visions that guided his people through critical decisions.[^20]

A pivotal moment in Sitting Bull's rise came in 1866 when he became principal chief of the northern hunting Oceti Sakowin, with the legendary warrior Crazy Horse serving as his vice-chief.[^21] By approximately 1867, he was made principal chief of the entire Oceti Sakowin nation, though historians have clarified that this did not mean "Supreme Chief of the whole Sioux Nation" as some early accounts claimed, since Lakota society was highly decentralized with individual bands and elders making autonomous decisions.[^22][^23]

Between 1868 and 1876, Sitting Bull developed into one of the most important Native American political leaders of his era.[^24] His leadership style combined spiritual authority with practical strategic vision. He was known for his powerful oratory, his ability to unite disparate bands through force of personality and shared purpose, and his unwavering commitment to preserving the traditional Lakota way of life.[^25]

The discovery of gold in the Black Hills in the early 1870s dramatically escalated tensions between the Lakota and the United States government. The Black Hills, known to the Lakota as Paha Sapa, were sacred lands guaranteed to them under the Treaty of Fort Laramie (1868). When prospectors flooded into the region in violation of the treaty, Sitting Bull emerged as a unifying figure, rallying not just the Lakota but also Cheyenne and Arapaho tribes to defend their territories against this betrayal.[^26][^27]

Sitting Bull cultivated a reputation for strong medicine over eight years, ultimately becoming a leader of unprecedented civil authority when the Lakota turned to him in 1876 as white encroachment reached a critical point.[^28] His spiritual leadership played a crucial role in maintaining the morale and cohesion of his people, as his vision of a collective struggle against external threats inspired warriors to fight with extraordinary tenacity.[^29]

Unlike many Native leaders who sought accommodation with the United States government, Sitting Bull refused to sign treaties that would confine his people to reservations or surrender the traditional hunting grounds that sustained Lakota independence. This uncompromising stance, while ultimately unsustainable against overwhelming military and demographic forces, established him as the embodiment of resistance and the preservation of indigenous sovereignty.[^30]

## Major Achievements

Sitting Bull's most significant achievements centered on his role as a unifying leader who defended Lakota sovereignty during the critical period of American westward expansion. His greatest military and political triumph came during the Great Sioux War of 1876-1877, which arose from escalating tensions following the violation of the Treaty of Fort Laramie by gold prospectors invading the sacred Black Hills.[^31][^32]

### The Vision and the Battle of Little Bighorn

In June 1876, before the Battle of the Little Bighorn, Sitting Bull performed the Sun Dance ceremony, a sacred ritual of sacrifice and spiritual renewal. During this ceremony, he fasted and slashed his arms over one hundred times as a sign of sacrifice to Wakan Tanka (the Great Spirit).[^33] Following this ordeal, Sitting Bull received a powerful vision in which he saw many soldiers, "as thick as grasshoppers," falling upside down into the Lakota camp—a prophecy his people interpreted as a foreshadowing of a major victory in which many soldiers would be killed.[^34][^35]

This vision proved prophetic. On June 25, 1876, at the Battle of the Little Bighorn in Montana Territory, Sitting Bull and Crazy Horse led a coalition of Lakota, Northern Cheyenne, and Arapaho warriors—totaling some 10,000 Native Americans who had gathered in defiance of a U.S. War Department order—against the 7th Cavalry Regiment commanded by Lieutenant Colonel George Armstrong Custer.[^36][^37] While Sitting Bull did not take a direct military role in the battle itself, acting instead as the spiritual and political leader, his spiritual authority and strategic vision had united the various tribal bands into a formidable force.[^38]

The battle resulted in the complete annihilation of Custer's immediate command. Custer and approximately 200 men in his battalion were killed within about an hour by as many as 3,000 Native American warriors.[^39][^40] Sitting Bull's followers, led into direct combat by Crazy Horse, counterattacked and defeated Custer while surrounding and laying siege to the other two battalions led by Major Marcus Reno and Captain Frederick Benteen.[^41] The Battle of Little Bighorn became the most decisive Native American victory and the worst U.S. Army defeat in the long Plains Indian War, cementing Sitting Bull's reputation as one of history's most formidable indigenous resistance leaders.[^42]

### Building Intertribal Coalitions

One of Sitting Bull's most remarkable achievements was his ability to forge intertribal alliances during a period when Native American groups were often fragmented. He successfully built coalitions between the Lakota, Cheyenne, and Arapaho peoples, creating a unified front against U.S. military forces.[^43] This required exceptional diplomatic skill and the ability to transcend traditional tribal rivalries in service of a common cause—the preservation of indigenous autonomy and traditional ways of life.[^44]

### Exile and Return

Following the victory at Little Bighorn, intense military pressure forced Sitting Bull to make difficult choices. Refusing to surrender or accept confinement to a reservation, in May 1877 he led his band across the border into the North-West Territories of Canada (near Wood Mountain), where they remained in exile for four years.[^45][^46] This decision to maintain independence rather than accept reservation life demonstrated his unwavering commitment to Lakota freedom, even at tremendous personal cost.

During his Canadian exile, Sitting Bull refused a pardon and multiple offers to return to the United States, maintaining his position until deteriorating conditions—including the depletion of buffalo herds—made continued resistance untenable. His eventual return to the United States in 1881 was not a surrender of principles but a pragmatic recognition of changing realities.[^47]

### Cultural Preservation and Advocacy

Throughout his life, Sitting Bull served as a fierce advocate for the preservation of Lakota culture, spirituality, and sovereignty. He consistently articulated a vision of Native American rights that challenged the fundamental assumptions of U.S. expansion and manifest destiny.[^48] His famous statement about treaties—"What treaty have the Sioux made with the white man that we have broken? Not one. What treaty have the white man ever made with us that they have kept? Not one"—became a powerful indictment of U.S. Indian policy.[^49]

Even in his later years on the Standing Rock Reservation, Sitting Bull refused to fully assimilate into white society as reservation rules required, continuing to live with two wives and rejecting Christianity, maintaining his traditional beliefs and practices as an act of cultural resistance.[^50]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his status as a resistance hero, Sitting Bull's life and legacy have been subjects of historical debate, criticism, and controversy, both during his lifetime and in subsequent historical analysis.

### Historical Debates About His Leadership Role

A significant historiographical controversy centers on the exact nature and extent of Sitting Bull's authority. Historian Stanley Vestal claimed that between 1866 and 1868, Sitting Bull was made "Supreme Chief of the whole Sioux Nation," but later historians and ethnologists have refuted this characterization.[^51] Because Lakota society was highly decentralized, with individual bands and their elders making autonomous decisions including whether or not to wage war, the concept of a supreme chief with absolute authority was foreign to Lakota political structure.[^52]

Historians have corrected earlier misunderstandings of Sitting Bull as the "all powerful architect of Custer's last stand," recognizing instead that he was initially just the chief of a single band of "wild" Hunkpapa who cultivated his reputation over years, ultimately becoming a leader of unprecedented civil authority when circumstances demanded unified leadership.[^53] This more nuanced understanding acknowledges his genuine influence while respecting the decentralized nature of Lakota governance.

### Debates About the Plains Wars

Historian Margot Liberty, based on tribal oral histories, has theorized that many Lakota bands allied with the Cheyenne during the Plains Wars because they believed the Cheyenne nation was under attack by U.S. forces, suggesting the conflict should have been called "The Great Cheyenne War" rather than focusing on the Sioux.[^54] However, other prominent historians including Robert M. Utley and Jerome Greene have concluded that the Lakota coalition, of which Sitting Bull was the ostensible head, was indeed the primary target of the federal government's pacification campaign.[^55]

### Buffalo Bill's Wild West Show Controversy

In 1885, Sitting Bull joined Buffalo Bill Cody's Wild West show for four months, a decision that sparked considerable controversy.[^56] Under a contract that paid him $50 per week (equivalent to approximately $1,750 today) plus a bonus of $125 (equivalent to about $4,375 today), with the exclusive right to sell souvenir photographs, Sitting Bull's performance consisted of riding into the arena alone on his mustang and being taunted as the "murderer of George Armstrong Custer."[^57][^58]

Many Native Americans viewed his participation as a form of exploitation and a betrayal of his people, as the show essentially commercialized and sensationalized the conflicts in which so many had died.[^59] While he was generally booed in the United States, he was cheered in Canada where the show toured during the summer of 1885.[^60] However, others saw his participation as a way to share his story and educate people about Native American culture, and he developed a friendship with Buffalo Bill Cody and fellow performer Annie Oakley.[^61]

Historian Edward Lazarus wrote that Sitting Bull reportedly cursed his audience in Lakota during a railway celebration in 1884, though historian Robert Utley contends that such reports are unsubstantiated.[^62][^63] Whether true or not, these accounts reflect the complex position Sitting Bull occupied—simultaneously a celebrity attraction and a symbol of resistance, forced to navigate a world fundamentally transformed by forces beyond his control.

### Resistance to Reservation Life

Sitting Bull's steadfast refusal to assimilate created ongoing conflicts with U.S. authorities and Indian agents, particularly James McLaughlin at the Standing Rock Reservation. His insistence on maintaining traditional practices, including polygamy and rejection of Christianity, was seen by government officials as obstinate resistance to civilization and progress.[^64] This conflict between preservation of cultural identity and forced assimilation remains a central tension in evaluating both Sitting Bull's choices and U.S. Indian policy.

## Personal Life

Sitting Bull's personal life reflected traditional Lakota customs and his status as a prominent chief. Polygamy was common and accepted in many Native American tribes, and Sitting Bull had four, possibly five wives throughout his life, all of whom are documented along with the children from these unions.[^65][^66]

### Marriages and Family

His first two wives died, and historical records identify his last two wives as "Four Robes" (Four-Robes-Woman) and "Seen-by-the-Nation" (Seen-By-The-Nation), who gave him many children and stood behind him in family portraits.[^67][^68] Other wives mentioned in his family tree include Light Hair and Snow-on-Her.[^69] Even in his later years on the Standing Rock Reservation, Sitting Bull refused to give up his traditional way of life, continuing to live with two wives in defiance of reservation rules that required conformity to Christian monogamy.[^70]

### Children

In his later years, Sitting Bull's most favored children were a son named Crow Foot and a daughter named Standing Holy.[^71] Crow Foot, born in 1873, was known as his father's favorite son. Tragically, Crow Foot was killed alongside his father on December 15, 1890, during the violent confrontation with Indian police—shot down at just seventeen years old.[^72]

Other documented children include William Sitting Bull (born around 1878 to Sitting Bull and Four-Robes-Woman, lived 1878-1909); Her-Many-Horses (1863-1886), his daughter who died young but had two children; and Lodge-in-Sight (1875-1900), his third daughter who also died young but had one child.[^73][^74]

### Parents and Extended Family

Sitting Bull was the son of Returns-Again (also called Jumping Bull), a renowned Sioux warrior who bore the name "Sitting Bull" before transferring it to his son.[^75] Although a Crow warrior killed Sitting Bull's father in 1859, this loss deeply affected him and reinforced his understanding of the constant warfare that characterized Plains Indian life.[^76] His mother, Her Holy Door, remained a powerful presence in his household until her death in 1884, and she appears seated to his right in a surviving family photograph.[^77][^78]

### Personality and Character

Those who knew Sitting Bull described him as possessing a careful and deliberate demeanor—the same qualities that earned him the childhood nickname "Slow."[^79] He was known for his generosity, one of the four cardinal Lakota virtues, and his wisdom in counsel. Despite his reputation as a fierce warrior and uncompromising leader in political matters, he formed genuine friendships across cultural lines, including with Buffalo Bill Cody and sharpshooter Annie Oakley, whom he nicknamed "Little Sure Shot."[^80]

Sitting Bull highly valued a horse that Buffalo Bill gave him when he left the Wild West show, demonstrating his appreciation for gifts and his ability to maintain personal relationships even with former adversaries.[^81] The famous portrait of Cody and Sitting Bull together bears the title "Foes in '76—Friends in '85," encapsulating this remarkable relationship.[^82]

### Living Descendants

Sitting Bull's bloodline continues through his living descendants, including South Dakota author Ernie LaPointe. Through autosomal DNA analysis, LaPointe's connection as the great-grandson of Sitting Bull has been irrefutably confirmed, ensuring that the great chief's genetic and cultural legacy persists into the present day.[^83]

## Legacy

Sitting Bull's legacy extends far beyond his lifetime, establishing him as an enduring symbol of Indigenous resistance, cultural preservation, and the struggle for sovereignty against colonialism. His impact on Native American history and broader American consciousness remains profound and multifaceted.

### Symbol of Resistance and Indigenous Rights

Sitting Bull was and remains an icon of Indigenous resistance in North America.[^84] He became a symbol of Native American resistance against the encroachment of settlers and the U.S. military, particularly during pivotal events like the Battle of Little Bighorn.[^85] His fanatical stance against colonization continues to inspire many in Indian Country today, who have used Sitting Bull's legacy to create their own ongoing strategies of resistance and advocacy for Indigenous rights.[^86]

By 1869, Sitting Bull was considered the Supreme Chief of the Lakota Sioux Nation, and his leadership extended beyond military matters to cultural, spiritual, and political spheres.[^87] He played a crucial role in the resistance against U.S. government policies, advocating for the rights and sovereignty of Native Americans at a time when such advocacy carried enormous personal risk.[^88]

### Cultural Preservation

Sitting Bull's vision extended beyond military engagement—he sought to preserve Lakota culture and autonomy, making him a pivotal figure in the fight for Native American cultural survival.[^89] His refusal to abandon traditional practices, his rejection of forced assimilation, and his insistence on the validity of Lakota spiritual beliefs and social structures established a model of cultural resistance that resonates in contemporary Indigenous movements.

The legacy of Sitting Bull has become a central part of many Native American cultures and histories, ensuring that he continues to be remembered far into the future.[^90] His life story serves as a testament to the strength and resilience of Native American peoples in the face of overwhelming pressure to abandon their identities and ways of life.[^91]

### Historical Reassessment

As historians learned more about his life throughout the twentieth century, Sitting Bull gradually emerged as a great American leader—a complex man who accomplished much more than defeating the Seventh Cavalry at Little Bighorn.[^92] His reputation as a great leader of his people remains unchallenged, as he continues to be recognized as a symbol of Native American pride, honor, and traditional values, as well as for his stand against injustice.[^93]

Distinguished historian Robert M. Utley forged a compelling portrait of Sitting Bull, presenting the Lakota perspective for the first time and rendering an unbiased, historically accurate portrait of the man, helping to correct earlier stereotyped or romanticized accounts.[^94]

### Educational and Political Impact

Sitting Bull's life and legacy serve educational purposes, teaching subsequent generations about the complexities of American expansion, the costs of colonization for Indigenous peoples, and the sophisticated political and spiritual systems that characterized Plains Indian societies. His famous quotes and speeches continue to be cited in discussions of Indigenous rights, environmental stewardship, and resistance to oppression.

His legacy informs contemporary debates about sovereignty, treaty rights, land claims, and the ongoing relationship between Native American nations and the United States government. The Standing Rock Sioux Reservation, where he lived his final years and died, became the site of major protests in 2016-2017 against the Dakota Access Pipeline, with protesters explicitly invoking Sitting Bull's legacy of resistance and land protection.

### Memorialization

Sitting Bull was initially buried at Fort Yates, but his remains were moved in 1953 to Mobridge, South Dakota, where a granite shaft marks his resting place.[^95] Various institutions bear his name, including Sitting Bull College on the Standing Rock Reservation, ensuring his memory continues to educate and inspire new generations. His image appears in countless historical works, photographs from his lifetime remain iconic representations of Indigenous leadership, and artistic representations continue to proliferate.

The powerful legacy of Sitting Bull represents a testament to the strength and resilience of Native American peoples, marked by a relentless fight for rights, dignity, and cultural preservation that continues to inspire Indigenous and non-Indigenous people alike.[^96]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholarly assessment of Sitting Bull has evolved significantly over time, with modern historians presenting increasingly nuanced and respectful evaluations of his leadership, strategic vision, and historical significance.

Distinguished historian Robert M. Utley, one of the foremost scholars of the American West, forged a compelling and sympathetic portrait of Sitting Bull in his definitive biography, presenting the Lakota perspective for the first time and rendering what is widely considered an unbiased, historically accurate portrait of the man.[^97] Utley's work helped establish Sitting Bull not merely as a military adversary but as a sophisticated political leader navigating impossible circumstances with remarkable skill and conviction.

During the period 1868-1876, historians recognize that Sitting Bull developed into one of the most important Native American political leaders of his era, demonstrating abilities that extended far beyond military prowess.[^98] Scholars particularly praise his capacity for building intertribal coalitions, uniting traditionally fragmented groups under a common cause—a diplomatic and political achievement of the highest order.[^99]

His reputation as a great leader of his people remains unchallenged in contemporary scholarship, with historians recognizing him as a symbol of Native American pride, honor, and traditional values, as well as for his principled stand against injustice.[^100] Modern assessments emphasize his embodiment of the four cardinal virtues of the Lakota people—bravery, fortitude, generosity, and wisdom—and how this combination earned him the respect necessary for leadership in a society where authority had to be continually earned rather than inherited.[^101]

Scholars of Indigenous spirituality highlight Sitting Bull's role as a spiritual leader whose powerful visions and performance of sacred ceremonies like the Sun Dance played crucial roles in maintaining the morale and cohesion of his people.[^102] His spiritual authority provided a unifying force that complemented his political and military leadership, creating a holistic form of leadership that resonated deeply within Lakota culture.[^103]

As historians learned more about his life throughout the twentieth century, Sitting Bull gradually emerged as a great American leader—a complex, multidimensional figure who did much more than defeat Custer at Little Bighorn.[^104] This reassessment recognizes his advocacy for cultural preservation, his sophisticated understanding of the political forces threatening his people, and his unwavering commitment to principles even when pragmatic accommodation might have brought personal benefits.

Contemporary Indigenous scholars and activists view Sitting Bull as a founding figure in the ongoing struggle for Native American sovereignty and rights, with his legacy directly informing modern movements for Indigenous justice, land rights, and cultural preservation.[^105]

### Negative Views

Critical assessments of Sitting Bull, both historical and contemporary, center on several key debates about his role, decisions, and legacy, though many of these critiques reflect broader disagreements about methodology and perspective rather than personal failings.

Some historians have questioned earlier claims about the extent of Sitting Bull's authority. Stanley Vestal's assertion that Sitting Bull was made "Supreme Chief of the whole Sioux Nation" has been refuted by later historians and ethnologists who emphasize that Lakota society was highly decentralized, with individual bands and elders making autonomous decisions including whether to wage war.[^106][^107] Critics argue that romanticized accounts overstated his centralized authority, creating a misleading picture of Plains Indian political structures.

During his lifetime, U.S. government officials and military leaders viewed Sitting Bull as an obstinate troublemaker who prevented the "civilizing" of the Lakota people. Indian Agent James McLaughlin at the Standing Rock Reservation considered Sitting Bull a negative influence who undermined efforts to assimilate Native Americans into white society.[^108] From this perspective, Sitting Bull's refusal to adopt Christianity, abandon polygamy, or accept reservation agriculture was seen as reactionary resistance to inevitable and beneficial progress.

Some critics within Native American communities viewed Sitting Bull's participation in Buffalo Bill's Wild West show in 1885 as exploitation and a betrayal of his people, commodifying their suffering and the conflicts in which many had died for the entertainment of white audiences.[^109] This criticism suggests a problematic willingness to compromise principles for money and celebrity, though defenders counter that Sitting Bull used the platform to share Native perspectives and generate income to support his impoverished people.

Military historians have noted that Sitting Bull did not personally lead warriors in the Battle of Little Bighorn, with the direct military leadership falling to Crazy Horse and other war chiefs while Sitting Bull served primarily as a spiritual and political leader.[^110] Some critics suggest this diminishes his direct military contribution, though defenders argue that his spiritual leadership and the unifying vision he provided were equally crucial to the victory.

After Little Bighorn, some criticized Sitting Bull's decision to flee to Canada rather than continue fighting or negotiate from a position of strength following the significant victory.[^111] From this perspective, the Canadian exile represented a strategic failure that allowed U.S. forces to consolidate control over the region. However, defenders note that continued resistance would have led to the annihilation of his people given overwhelming U.S. military superiority.

Contemporary critical scholars sometimes debate whether Sitting Bull's uncompromising stance, while morally admirable, was strategically wise given the inevitable outcome of Indigenous-settler conflicts. Some argue that more accommodationist approaches by other Native leaders better served their people's long-term interests, preserving more of their population and land base, though such arguments remain deeply controversial and contested.

## Famous Quotes

> "Let us put our minds together and see what life we can make for our children."

This quote reflects Sitting Bull's vision of collective action and his concern for future generations, emphasizing cooperation and long-term thinking over individual gain.[^112]

> "Each man is good in the sight of the Great Spirit. It is not necessary for eagles to be crows."

A statement about respect for diversity and individual paths, this quote embodies Lakota spiritual philosophy about the inherent worth of all beings and the importance of being true to one's nature.[^113]

> "What treaty have the Sioux made with the white man that we have broken? Not one. What treaty have the white man ever made with us that they have kept? Not one."

This powerful indictment of U.S. treaty violations became one of Sitting Bull's most famous statements, articulating the fundamental dishonesty that characterized government dealings with Native peoples.[^114]

> "The white man knows how to make everything, but he does not know how to distribute it."

A critique of white society's material abundance alongside profound inequality, this observation highlighted the contrast between technological advancement and social justice.[^115]

> "Now that we are poor, we are free. No white man controls our footsteps."

Spoken during his Canadian exile, this quote captures Sitting Bull's prioritization of freedom and autonomy over material comfort, reflecting core Lakota values.[^116]

> "It is through this mysterious power that we too have our being, and we therefore yield to our neighbors, even to our animal neighbors, the same right as ourselves to inhabit this vast land."

This statement articulates a Lakota worldview that recognized the rights of all beings, contrasting sharply with concepts of human dominion over nature that justified settler colonialism.[^117]

> "Strangely enough they have a mind to till the soil and the love of possession is a disease with them."

An observation about fundamental cultural differences between Lakota and white societies, particularly regarding land ownership and relationship to the earth.[^118]

> "This is a good day to die. Follow me!"

His rallying cry at the Battle of Little Bighorn, expressing the Lakota warrior ethos that emphasized courage, acceptance of mortality, and honor in battle.[^119]
