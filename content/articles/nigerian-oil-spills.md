## Summary

Over 50 years of oil spills in the Niger Delta have contaminated water, destroyed farmland, and caused health crises for millions. Shell and other companies have been sued repeatedly.

## Context and Causes

Oil production in the Niger Delta expanded rapidly after commercial extraction began in the late 1950s. Spills have been attributed to a mix of factors including aging pipelines and facilities, operational failures, and third‑party interference such as illegal tapping and sabotage.

## Environmental and Health Impacts

Reported impacts include:

- **Water contamination** affecting drinking water sources and fisheries.
- **Soil degradation** that reduces agricultural yields and damages mangroves.
- **Air pollution** from associated gas flaring and burn-offs, contributing to respiratory issues.

The scale of harm varies by incident, but long-running contamination has been documented as creating chronic exposure risks for nearby communities.

## Accountability, Litigation, and Cleanup

Nigerian oil spills have generated sustained legal and political pressure, including lawsuits against multinational oil companies in Nigerian and foreign courts. Remediation efforts have included site-specific cleanups and broader policy initiatives; however, critics and community groups have repeatedly argued that response times, transparency, and long-term restoration have been insufficient.

A widely cited benchmark is the **United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP)** assessment of Ogoniland, which described extensive contamination and proposed a multi‑year, large-scale cleanup program.

## Community Response

Local communities, environmental NGOs, and activists have organized around demands for compensation, improved regulation, and meaningful restoration. Community concerns often focus on loss of livelihoods (fishing and farming), health impacts, and the unequal distribution of benefits from extraction.
