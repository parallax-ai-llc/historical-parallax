## Summary

Japanese bond trader who hid $1.1 billion in losses at Daiwa Bank over 11 years.

## Context and Background

- Daiwa Bank was a major Japanese financial institution with significant international operations, including a presence in the United States.
- Bond trading desks in the 1980s and early 1990s could accumulate large market risks, and weak internal controls made long-running concealment possible.

## Daiwa Bank Trading Loss Concealment

- Iguchi worked as a bond trader and was able to carry out unauthorized trading and related bookkeeping manipulations over an extended period.
- Public reporting described the concealed losses as roughly **$1.1 billion**, accumulated over **about 11 years**.
- The case became notable not only for the amount, but for the duration of the concealment and the governance failures implied by that duration.

## Exposure and Immediate Consequences

- The losses were exposed in **1995**, triggering regulatory and law-enforcement attention.
- The scandal led to major reputational damage for the bank and raised questions about supervision of overseas operations and trading controls.

## Legal and Regulatory Aftermath (High Level)

- The incident is commonly cited in discussions of:
  - segregation of duties (trading vs. settlement/recordkeeping),
  - audit independence and frequency,
  - limits, monitoring, and exception reporting for trading activities,
  - cross-border oversight when banks operate in multiple jurisdictions.

## Why This Case Still Matters

- The Daiwa losses are a frequently referenced example of how operational failures and weak controls can allow financial losses to compound.
- It is also used as a cautionary case for how delayed detection can amplify both financial damage and systemic trust issues.
