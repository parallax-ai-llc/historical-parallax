## Summary

Danish serial killer and baby farmer who murdered between 9 and 25 infants left in her care between 1913 and 1920. She strangled, drowned, or burned the children. Initially sentenced to death, commuted to life.

## Background: “Baby Farming”

Overbye is often described as a “baby farmer,” a term used in parts of Europe to describe people who took in infants—sometimes for payment—from mothers who could not raise them. The practice existed in a broader context of poverty, social stigma around unwed pregnancy, and limited social welfare systems.

## Crimes and Detection

Public accounts of the case emphasize that infants were left in Overbye’s care and later went missing, with the true scale of the killings becoming clearer only after investigation. The case is frequently referenced as an example of how social vulnerability and lack of oversight can create conditions for severe abuse.

## Trial and Sentencing

Overbye’s conviction is commonly summarized as involving a smaller number of legally proven murders than the total suspected by investigators. Reports often note that an initial death sentence was later commuted to life imprisonment.

## Legacy

The Overbye case has appeared in historical discussions of child protection, criminal justice, and the social history of early 20th-century Scandinavia. It is also cited in broader comparisons to other “baby farming” cases that prompted reforms and stronger welfare oversight in different countries.
