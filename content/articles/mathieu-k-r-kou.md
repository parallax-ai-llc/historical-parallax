## Summary

Military dictator of Benin who imposed Marxist-Leninist rule for 17 years. Staged a coup in 1972 and renamed the country, suppressing dissent throughout his rule.

## Rise to power

Kérékou emerged from the military officer corps and took power through a coup.
His government later adopted Marxist-Leninist language and institutions, shaping Benin's one-party period.
Renaming the state signaled an attempt to redefine national identity and political legitimacy.

## Rule and political control

Open-source summaries describe a security-centered approach to maintaining authority.
Opposition activity was constrained, and critics faced detention or other forms of coercion.
As with many Cold War-era authoritarian systems, internal party discipline and external alliances both mattered.

## 1990 transition and later role

The end of the one-party period is associated with a negotiated shift to multiparty politics.
Benin's transition is often highlighted as comparatively peaceful for the region and time.
Kérékou's later political trajectory is part of what makes his career unusual in narratives of African democratization.

## Legacy

Assessments of Kérékou typically split between condemnation of repression and recognition of the eventual transition.
Long-term evaluations also consider economic performance, institutional development, and civil liberties.
Because Benin's later democratic practices evolved over time, debates about his legacy remain politically charged.
