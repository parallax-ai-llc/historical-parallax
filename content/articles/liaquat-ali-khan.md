## Summary

Liaquat Ali Khan (1895-1951) served as Pakistan's first Prime Minister from 1947 until his assassination in 1951, earning the titles "Quaid-e-Millat" (Leader of the Nation) and "Shaheed-e-Millat" (Martyr of the Nation). A barrister by training and close associate of Muhammad Ali Jinnah, he played a crucial role in both the Pakistan Movement and the consolidation of the newly independent state. While Jinnah is credited with founding Pakistan, Liaquat is recognized for establishing its fundamental institutions, constitutional framework, and foreign policy direction. His tenure was marked by significant achievements in state-building but also controversies regarding religious policy, Cold War alignment, and Kashmir strategy. His unsolved assassination in Rawalpindi remains one of Pakistan's greatest political mysteries.

## Early Life and Background

Liaquat Ali Khan was born on October 1, 1895, in Karnal, located in present-day Haryana, India, into a wealthy and aristocratic Muslim family with strong ties to the British colonial administration.[^1] He was the second of four sons born to Rukn-ud-Daulah Shamsher Jung Nawab Bahadur Rustam Ali Khan, a prosperous landowner, and Mahmoodah Begum, daughter of Nawab Quaher Ali Khan of Rajpur in Saharanpur.[^2] The Khan family's privileged position in colonial India traced back to his grandfather, Nawab Ahmad Ali, who had provided substantial support to the British during the 1857-1858 Mutiny uprising, earning prestigious honors and complete remission of rent as rewards for his loyalty.[^3]

Growing up in this environment of wealth and British patronage, young Liaquat received his early education at home, where he studied the Qur'an, Hadith, and even took music lessons, reflecting the cultured upbringing typical of North Indian Muslim aristocracy.[^4] His family held deep respect for the Indian Muslim thinker and philosopher Syed Ahmad Khan, the founder of the Aligarh Movement, which advocated for modern education among Indian Muslims. Following this tradition, Liaquat's father desired his son to be educated in the British educational system, believing this would best prepare him for leadership in colonial India.[^5]

After completing his preliminary education in Karnal, Liaquat enrolled in 1913 at the prestigious Muhammadan Anglo-Oriental College in Aligarh (which later became Aligarh Muslim University), the intellectual center of Muslim modernism in India.[^6] There he earned a Bachelor of Science degree in Political Science and an LLB in 1918, immersing himself in both Western political thought and Islamic reformist ideas. That same year, he married his cousin, Jehangira Begum, though this marriage would later end in separation.[^7]

Following his father's death, Liaquat traveled to England to pursue further education at Oxford University's prestigious Exeter College, where he was awarded a Master's degree in 1921.[^8] He qualified as a barrister in England in 1922 before returning to India to practice law.[^9] This educational trajectory—Aligarh, Oxford, and the bar—was remarkably similar to that of Muhammad Ali Jinnah, the future Quaid-e-Azam, and would establish the foundation for their later partnership. The combination of traditional Islamic learning, modern Western education, and legal training would prove instrumental in shaping Liaquat's political philosophy and his approach to governance.

---

## Rise to Power

Liaquat Ali Khan's political career began in 1923 when he entered the political arena, being elected first to the provincial legislature of the United Provinces and subsequently to the central legislative assembly of British India.[^10] Initially, like many educated Muslims of his generation, he was involved in the broader Indian nationalist movement. However, the communal dynamics of Indian politics and the perceived marginalization of Muslim interests would gradually draw him toward the All-India Muslim League, the primary political organization advocating for Muslim rights in the subcontinent.

His association with Muhammad Ali Jinnah proved transformative for both men and for the future of Muslim politics in India. In July 1933, Liaquat traveled to England along with his second wife, Ra'ana Liaquat Ali Khan, and met Jinnah at his Hampstead residence in London, where he played a crucial role in convincing the disillusioned barrister to return to India to lead the Muslims, who were facing increasing political division and marginalization.[^11] This meeting marked the beginning of one of the most consequential political partnerships in South Asian history.

By the late 1930s and early 1940s, Liaquat had become Jinnah's most trusted lieutenant in the Muslim League. In 1943, when he was reelected as General Secretary of the Muslim League, Jinnah famously called him "my right hand," a testament to his indispensable role in the organization.[^12] Liaquat's organizational skills, his ability to mobilize support, and his dedication to the cause of Muslim political rights made him the natural second-in-command of the Pakistan Movement. While Jinnah provided the vision and charismatic leadership, Liaquat handled much of the practical political work, including negotiations with the British and coordination with provincial Muslim League organizations.

During the crucial 1940s, as the demand for Pakistan gained momentum, Liaquat played a vital role in articulating and defending Muslim League positions in the central legislative assembly. His financial expertise proved particularly valuable in 1946 when, as Finance Minister in the interim government of India, he introduced what became known as the "Poor Man's Budget," which proposed heavy taxation on business profits and sought to address economic inequalities.[^13] This budget, while controversial among business communities, demonstrated his commitment to social and economic justice and enhanced his reputation among ordinary Muslims as a leader who cared about the common people.

When independence came in August 1947 and Pakistan was born, Jinnah became the first Governor-General, and Liaquat was the obvious and unanimous choice as the country's first Prime Minister.[^14] His selection was not merely due to his proximity to Jinnah but reflected his proven administrative abilities, his broad acceptance across different Muslim constituencies, and his deep understanding of both domestic and international politics. He assumed office at a moment of unprecedented crisis, with Pakistan facing massive refugee flows, communal violence, administrative chaos, and existential threats to its survival.

---

## Major Achievements

Liaquat Ali Khan's tenure as Pakistan's first Prime Minister was marked by foundational achievements that shaped the country's political, economic, and constitutional framework. His most enduring contribution was the promulgation of the Objectives Resolution on March 12, 1949, which established the fundamental principles upon which Pakistan's constitution would be based.[^15] This resolution declared that sovereignty belongs to Allah alone and that the state would exercise its powers through the chosen representatives of the people within the limits prescribed by the Quran and Sunnah. It stipulated Pakistan to be an Islamic democracy where Muslims would be enabled to order their lives according to Islamic principles while guaranteeing fundamental rights to all citizens regardless of religion, race, or creed.[^16] The Objectives Resolution has been described as the "Magna Carta" of Pakistan's constitutional history and remains a part of Pakistan's constitution to this day.

In the realm of economic policy, Liaquat laid the groundwork for Pakistan's mixed economy model. One of the most significant achievements during his premiership was the establishment of the State Bank of Pakistan (National Bank) in November 1949, which provided the country with an independent monetary authority and the capability to manage its own currency and financial policy.[^17] He also oversaw the installation of a paper currency mill in Karachi, ending Pakistan's dependence on India for currency notes.[^18] Under his leadership, Pakistan adopted an economic policy that was planned but also maintained an open free market economy, attempting to balance state intervention with private enterprise.[^19]

Liaquat introduced several important policy frameworks including the Industrial Policy Resolution and the Agrarian Reform Policy, designed to lay the foundation for Pakistan's economic and political development.[^20] These policies aimed to encourage industrial growth while addressing the feudal land ownership patterns that characterized much of Pakistan's agricultural economy. His government also took initiatives to develop educational infrastructure and science and technology in the country. He appointed Salimuzzaman Siddiqui as his first government science adviser and asked Ziauddin Ahmed to draft educational policy for establishing a strong educational system in Pakistan.[^21]

Perhaps equally important was Liaquat's role in establishing Pakistan's foreign policy orientation. He created Pakistan's foreign policy framework that the country has largely followed ever since, positioning Pakistan within the Western bloc during the emerging Cold War.[^22] In May 1950, Liaquat made a historic visit to the United States, becoming the first Pakistani Prime Minister to do so, where he addressed a joint session of the U.S. Congress and met with President Harry S. Truman.[^23] During this visit, he articulated Pakistan's anti-communist stance, warning of "dark forces" threatening civilization and emphasizing shared values with the Western democracies.[^24] This visit set the course for Pakistan's subsequent alignment with the United States and its participation in Western alliance systems such as SEATO and CENTO.

In managing relations with India, Liaquat demonstrated both firmness and pragmatism. His efforts in signing the Liaquat-Nehru Pact on the minority issue in April 1950 reduced immediate tensions between the two countries following communal riots in East Bengal and West Bengal.[^25] This pact committed both governments to protecting their religious minorities and preventing forced migration. Additionally, Liaquat helped address the enormous humanitarian crisis created by partition, working with limited resources to settle millions of refugees and establish an effective administrative system for the new country.[^26]

Domestically, Liaquat is credited with maintaining democratic processes and civilian supremacy during a period when many newly independent countries were succumbing to authoritarian rule. He insisted on parliamentary procedures, freedom of the press, and the rule of law, even when these principles made governance more difficult. His refusal to tolerate political corruption among his cabinet ministers became part of his legend, though some historians believe this principled stance may have contributed to the conspiracy that led to his assassination.[^27]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his achievements, Liaquat Ali Khan's tenure and legacy have been subject to significant criticisms and controversies that continue to generate scholarly debate. The most persistent criticism concerns the Objectives Resolution itself. While supporters view it as a necessary framework for an Islamic state, critics argue that it opened the door for subsequent Islamization efforts, particularly during General Zia-ul-Haq's military regime in the 1980s.[^28] Detractors contend that instead of erecting a barrier against theocracy, the resolution provided religious parties with a constitutional base to impose their ideologies on the rest of Pakistan, undermining the country's pluralistic foundations and the vision of a modern democratic state.[^29]

Liaquat's foreign policy decisions, particularly regarding the Cold War alignment, remain controversial. A major point of contention involves his relationship with the Soviet Union. Pakistan had initially received an invitation to visit Moscow, but this visit never materialized, and instead, Liaquat visited the United States in 1950.[^30] Critics suggest this represented a fundamental strategic error that alienated the Soviet Union and locked Pakistan into dependence on the United States. However, defenders argue that the change of date was made by the Soviets themselves, and that Liaquat conveyed his reservations clearly, so Pakistan cannot be accused of unilaterally picking one world power over another.[^31] Nonetheless, this decision had reverberations in the subsequent Rawalpindi Conspiracy Case of 1951, where military officers allegedly plotted a communist coup.

His Kashmir policy drew fierce criticism from militant groups and nationalists in Pakistan who viewed his approach as too conciliatory toward India.[^32] Critics particularly condemned his decision not to dismiss three British service chiefs who refused to send Pakistani troops to Kashmir despite orders from Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah.[^33] This inaction has been interpreted by critics as an attempt by Liaquat to secure his government by appeasing British interests, rather than asserting Pakistani sovereignty and supporting the Kashmir cause more aggressively. The Kashmir issue, which erupted immediately after partition, would become a permanent source of conflict between India and Pakistan, and some historians argue that more decisive action in 1947-48 might have altered the outcome.

Disturbing allegations have been made regarding Liaquat's relationship with Muhammad Ali Jinnah during the Quaid's final days. Some accounts suggest that relations between the two men had deteriorated before Jinnah's death in September 1948.[^34] The most serious allegation concerns the circumstances of Jinnah's death: when the gravely ill Quaid was being transported by ambulance from the airport to Karachi after being flown from Quetta, the ambulance reportedly ran out of fuel, and Jinnah died during the delay.[^35] While there is no evidence of deliberate wrongdoing, critics have questioned why adequate arrangements were not made for the dying founder of the nation, suggesting at minimum a failure of administrative competence and at worst something more sinister.

Liaquat's centralization of power and his handling of provincial autonomy issues, particularly concerning East Bengal (later Bangladesh), have been criticized for sowing the seeds of future national disintegration. His government's policies were viewed by many Bengalis as favoring West Pakistan and ignoring the legitimate grievances of the eastern wing, which contained the majority of Pakistan's population. The language controversy, where Urdu was promoted as the sole national language despite Bengali being spoken by the majority, began during his tenure and would eventually contribute to the Bangladesh liberation movement.

Finally, questions have been raised about the administrative failures during the early refugee crisis and economic challenges. While Liaquat faced unprecedented difficulties, critics argue that his government could have been more effective in planning for partition's consequences, managing resource distribution, and preventing the massive human suffering that accompanied the birth of Pakistan. Some scholars suggest that Liaquat's life and actions generated controversies when there should have been none, and that his tenure as prime minister set a controversial path for the nation to follow.[^36]

## Personal Life

Liaquat Ali Khan's personal life was marked by two marriages that reflected both traditional and progressive dimensions of his character. His first marriage in 1918 was to his cousin, Jehangira Begum, following the customary practice of arranged marriages within aristocratic Muslim families of the time.[^37] However, this marriage eventually ended in separation, and in 1933, Liaquat entered into a second marriage that would prove both personally fulfilling and politically significant.

His second wife was Ra'ana Liaquat Ali Khan, born Irene Margaret Pant on February 13, 1905, in Almora, British India.[^38] She came from a Brahmin family that had converted to Christianity two generations earlier, making her marriage to Liaquat particularly controversial for the time.[^39] Ra'ana had earned a degree in economics from Lucknow University and was working as a lecturer in economics in Delhi when she met Liaquat.[^40] Her family was furious about the marriage since she was marrying a Muslim and, moreover, a married man, as this was Liaquat's second marriage.[^41] Despite the social barriers and family opposition, she converted to Islam and took the name Begum Ra'ana (Gul-i-Rana) Liaquat Ali Khan.

The marriage proved to be a true partnership. Ra'ana became Liaquat's constant companion and played a major role in the Pakistan Movement, being politically involved alongside her husband.[^42] She was present during the crucial meeting in July 1933 at Jinnah's Hampstead residence in London, where Liaquat convinced Jinnah to return to India to save the Muslims from further division.[^43] As Pakistan's first First Lady, she became a pioneering figure in her own right, advocating for women's rights, education, and social reform. Her background in economics and her progressive views complemented Liaquat's political vision and provided him with an intellectual partner who understood the complexities of nation-building.

The couple had two sons, Ashraf and Akbar, who were raised in the turbulent years of the Pakistan Movement and the early years of independence.[^44] Family life was necessarily subordinated to the demands of politics, particularly during the crucial years from 1940 to 1951, when Liaquat was deeply involved in the struggle for Pakistan and then in governing the new nation.

Those who knew Liaquat described him as a man of principle, integrity, and strong moral convictions. His refusal to tolerate corruption, his commitment to democratic processes, and his personal probity were noted by contemporaries.[^45] He was known for his oratorical skills, his command of English and Urdu, and his ability to navigate complex political negotiations. Unlike some leaders who cultivated a personality cult, Liaquat maintained a relatively modest public persona, preferring to work behind the scenes and through institutions rather than through charismatic mass appeal.

After Liaquat's assassination in 1951, Begum Ra'ana continued her services for the social and economic benefit of women in Pakistan until her death in Karachi on June 13, 1990.[^46] She remained a respected figure in Pakistani public life for nearly four decades after her husband's death, serving as Pakistan's ambassador to several countries and continuing her advocacy work. Their partnership, which crossed religious and social boundaries, represented the kind of modern, progressive vision that Liaquat had for Pakistan—a vision that would face many challenges in the decades following his death.

## Legacy

Liaquat Ali Khan's legacy is complex and multifaceted, reflecting both his substantial achievements in establishing Pakistan's foundational institutions and the controversies that surrounded his policies. Upon his death, he was posthumously given the honorific titles of "Shaheed-e-Millat" (Martyr of the Nation) and "Quaid-e-Millat" (Leader of the Nation), and he is widely regarded as the "martyr for democracy" in newly founded Pakistan.[^47] The common historical assessment holds that if Muhammad Ali Jinnah was the designer of Pakistan, Liaquat Ali Khan was its architect—the man who transformed vision into functioning reality.[^48]

Sir Olaf Caroe, former Governor of the North-West Frontier Province, offered this assessment: "Three years of Liaquat Ali Khan's leadership carried Pakistan through difficulty and crisis to the achievement of a degree of political stability rare in any democratic country."[^49] This observation captures the magnitude of Liaquat's achievement in steering Pakistan through its most vulnerable period. The challenges he faced were extraordinary: millions of refugees requiring settlement, communal violence threatening to tear apart the social fabric, empty treasuries, administrative chaos, hostile neighbors, and the fundamental task of building state institutions from scratch. That Pakistan survived this period as a functioning democracy, however imperfectly, owes much to Liaquat's leadership.

His constitutional legacy through the Objectives Resolution remains perhaps his most enduring contribution, for better or worse. The resolution established the framework for all subsequent constitutional development in Pakistan and continues to shape debates about the relationship between Islam and the state.[^50] His vision for a progressive and democratic Pakistan serving as a guiding principle continued to influence Pakistani politics and public discourse for decades.[^51] The institutions he helped establish—the State Bank of Pakistan, the planning commission, the foreign service, and various administrative structures—provided the scaffolding upon which the Pakistani state was built.

In foreign policy, Liaquat's strategic choices set Pakistan on a path that would define its international relationships for generations. His decision to align with the Western bloc during the Cold War established a pattern of alliance with the United States that persisted, with various modifications, for the remainder of the twentieth century and into the twenty-first.[^52] His successful state visit to the United States in 1950 demonstrated that Pakistan, despite being a young and impoverished nation, could engage with great powers as a sovereign equal and established Pakistan's profile on the world stage.

However, scholarly analysis also reveals complexities and criticisms of his legacy. His constitutional plans faced significant criticism from regional forces, and he did not possess the same level of political influence or charismatic authority as Jinnah.[^53] The centralization of power during his tenure and the perceived neglect of provincial autonomy, particularly regarding East Pakistan, planted seeds that would eventually lead to Bangladesh's secession in 1971. The Islamization enabled by the Objectives Resolution would later be invoked by military dictators like Zia-ul-Haq to justify authoritarian religious policies that moved Pakistan away from the pluralistic vision that many founding fathers had articulated.

The mystery surrounding his assassination has also contributed to his legacy. The fact that his murder remains unsolved despite multiple investigations has fueled conspiracy theories and prevented closure.[^54] Company Bagh, the site of his assassination, was renamed Liaquat Bagh in his honor, but the location gained tragic additional significance when it became the site of Benazir Bhutto's assassination in 2007, linking two of Pakistan's most prominent political murders across generations.

Perhaps most tellingly, the fact that no comprehensive English-language biography of Pakistan's first Prime Minister was written for many decades after his death demonstrates how his role has been somewhat neglected in historical scholarship, overshadowed by the towering figure of Jinnah.[^55] Recent scholarly works have begun to address this gap, reassessing Liaquat's contributions and providing more nuanced evaluations of his leadership, policies, and their long-term consequences for Pakistan's political development.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and admirers of Liaquat Ali Khan emphasize his crucial role in preventing Pakistan's collapse during its most vulnerable early years. Historians point out that the challenges facing Pakistan in 1947-1951 were arguably more severe than those facing India: a smaller economic base, fewer trained administrators, hostile neighbors, and the massive disruption caused by partition.[^56] That Pakistan survived these years as a democratic state speaks to the effectiveness of Liaquat's leadership and his commitment to constitutional governance.

Defenders of the Objectives Resolution argue that it represented a necessary compromise that allowed Pakistan to establish its identity as an Islamic state while maintaining democratic principles and protecting minority rights.[^57] They contend that Liaquat successfully navigated between secular extremism and religious fundamentalism, creating a framework that acknowledged Pakistan's Islamic character while preserving space for pluralism and democratic governance. The resolution's emphasis on social justice, equality, and fundamental rights reflected Liaquat's progressive vision for an Islamic democracy.

His economic policies are praised for their pragmatism and vision. The establishment of the State Bank of Pakistan provided the country with monetary independence and the tools for economic management.[^58] His Industrial Policy Resolution and attempts at agrarian reform demonstrated awareness of Pakistan's development challenges and a commitment to addressing feudal structures and economic inequality. Admirers note that under resource-constrained circumstances, he managed to lay the groundwork for Pakistan's industrial development and economic planning.

Scholars particularly appreciate his commitment to democratic norms and civilian supremacy during a period when many newly independent nations were succumbing to military coups or one-party rule. He maintained freedom of the press, respected parliamentary procedures, and worked within constitutional constraints even when autocratic power might have seemed more efficient.[^59] His refusal to tolerate corruption and his personal integrity set standards for public service that, even if not always followed by successors, established important normative expectations for Pakistani governance.

His foreign policy achievements are recognized by supporters who argue that alignment with the West was the only realistic option for a poor, newly independent Muslim state facing Soviet proximity and Indian hostility. The U.S. alliance brought economic aid, military assistance, and international recognition that Pakistan desperately needed.[^60] His successful navigation of Cold War politics demonstrated diplomatic skill and ensured Pakistan's survival in a hostile regional environment.

Contemporary observers like Sir Olaf Caroe praised his ability to maintain stability and democratic governance under extraordinarily difficult circumstances, while his organizational abilities and dedication to the Pakistan cause earned him the title "Quaid-e-Millat."[^61] His partnership with Begum Ra'ana is celebrated as representing progressive values and the possibility of transcending religious and social barriers, embodying the inclusive vision that some believe Pakistan should have pursued more consistently.

### Negative Views

Critics of Liaquat Ali Khan argue that his most significant legacy—the Objectives Resolution—fundamentally misdirected Pakistan's constitutional development. Opponents contend that by constitutionally establishing Pakistan as an Islamic state with sovereignty belonging to Allah, Liaquat created a framework that inevitably empowered religious parties and opened the door for theocratic interpretations that undermined democratic governance.[^62] They argue that instead of protecting Pakistan from religious extremism, the resolution provided constitutional legitimacy for subsequent Islamization efforts, particularly during Zia-ul-Haq's military dictatorship, which radically transformed Pakistan's legal system and social fabric.

His foreign policy decisions are criticized as strategically shortsighted. Critics argue that aligning so completely with the United States during the Cold War unnecessarily alienated the Soviet Union, created dependency on American aid and military support, and constrained Pakistan's foreign policy autonomy for decades.[^63] The failure to visit Moscow when invited, regardless of whose fault it was, represented a missed opportunity for strategic balance and non-alignment that might have served Pakistan's interests better in the long term. This alignment also entangled Pakistan in Cold War conflicts that had little to do with its core national interests.

His Kashmir policy faces sharp criticism from Pakistani nationalists who argue that he was too cautious and too willing to seek negotiated solutions when more aggressive military action might have secured more territory for Pakistan.[^64] The decision not to dismiss British military commanders who refused to support operations in Kashmir is seen as a betrayal of Pakistani sovereignty and the Kashmiri cause. Critics view this as evidence that Liaquat prioritized maintaining good relations with Britain over Pakistan's strategic interests.

The allegations regarding his relationship with Jinnah during the Quaid's final days, particularly the circumstances surrounding Jinnah's death, have never been satisfactorily explained.[^65] While direct evidence of wrongdoing is lacking, the administrative failures that led to the ambulance running out of fuel suggest at minimum a serious lack of competence in caring for the dying founder of the nation. Some critics see this as symptomatic of Liaquat's eagerness to assume power and his inadequate reverence for Jinnah's status.

His centralization policies and handling of East Pakistan grievances are criticized as laying the groundwork for the eventual secession of Bangladesh in 1971. The language controversy, economic disparities between East and West Pakistan, and perceived political marginalization of Bengali Muslims all began or worsened during Liaquat's tenure.[^66] Critics argue that a more federal approach and greater sensitivity to regional diversity might have prevented the eventual breakup of Pakistan.

Some scholars question whether Liaquat possessed the vision and political skill necessary for the enormous challenges he faced. They note that he lacked Jinnah's charismatic authority and ability to command universal respect across diverse constituencies.[^67] His constitutional plans faced significant opposition from regional forces, and he was unable to forge the kind of national consensus that might have given Pakistan a more stable foundation. The fact that his assassination led to prolonged political instability and constitutional paralysis suggests that his leadership had not created resilient institutions or developed a strong second tier of political leadership.

Finally, the enduring mystery of his assassination and the failure to bring anyone to justice is sometimes interpreted as evidence that he had created powerful enemies within Pakistan's own political and military establishment—enemies who successfully conspired against him and then covered their tracks.[^68] This interpretation, while speculative, suggests that Liaquat's Pakistan was already experiencing the dysfunction of elite conspiracy and impunity that would plague the country for decades.

## Famous Quotes

> "The movement of Pakistan which the Quaid-i-Azam launched was ethical in inspiration and ideological in content. The story of this movement is a story of the ideals of equality, fraternity and social and economic justice struggling against the forces of domination, exploitation, intolerance and tyranny."

*From speeches articulating the ideological foundations of Pakistan, emphasizing its basis in social justice rather than merely religious separatism.*[^69]

> "May God protect Pakistan. Pakistan zindabad!"

*His last words before being shot at Company Bagh in Rawalpindi on October 16, 1951, expressing his final thoughts for the nation he helped build.*[^70]

> "Dark forces are at work threatening to extinguish the torch of civilisation which liberal institutions are trying to keep alive."

*Statement during his 1950 visit to the United States, referring to communism and explaining Pakistan's alignment with Western democracies.*[^71]

> "If Jinnah founded Pakistan, I established it."

*Attributed statement reflecting his role in building Pakistan's institutions and administrative structures after independence.*[^72]
