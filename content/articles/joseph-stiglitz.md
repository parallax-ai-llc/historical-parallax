## Summary

Joseph Eugene Stiglitz (born February 9, 1943) is an American economist and public intellectual widely regarded as one of the most influential economists of the late 20th and early 21st centuries. A Nobel laureate and former Chief Economist of the World Bank, Stiglitz is best known for his foundational work on information asymmetry — the study of how unequal access to information distorts markets and produces inefficient outcomes.

Beyond academia, Stiglitz has been a persistent critic of unfettered free-market ideology, globalization as practiced by international financial institutions, and rising inequality. His ability to translate complex economic theory into accessible public argument has made him one of the discipline's most prominent voices in policy debates worldwide.

---

## Early Life & Background

Stiglitz was born in Gary, Indiana, a steel-industry city whose economic volatility left a lasting impression on him. He attended Amherst College, graduating in 1964, before pursuing graduate studies at MIT, where he earned his PhD in 1967 at just 24 years old. His early mentors included Paul Samuelson and Robert Solow, both Nobel laureates, and his doctoral work quickly signaled a talent for rigorous theoretical innovation.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Stiglitz joined the faculty at Yale in 1967 and later held positions at Stanford and Princeton before settling at Columbia University, where he remains a University Professor. His academic reputation was cemented in the 1970s and 1980s through a series of papers — co-authored with George Akerlof and Michael Spence — that established the economics of information as a core field.

His public profile expanded when President Bill Clinton appointed him to the Council of Economic Advisers in 1993, where he served as chairman from 1995 to 1997. He then became Chief Economist at the World Bank from 1997 to 2000, a tenure marked by sharp internal disagreements over IMF austerity programs during the Asian financial crisis.

---

## Key Achievements

In 2001, Stiglitz was awarded the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences, shared with Akerlof and Spence, for their analyses of markets with asymmetric information. His 1981 paper with Andrew Weiss on credit rationing and his work with Sanford Grossman on the "Grossman-Stiglitz paradox" — demonstrating that perfectly efficient markets are impossible — remain foundational texts.

His 2002 book *Globalization and Its Discontents* became an international bestseller, selling over one million copies and catalyzing mainstream debate about IMF and World Bank policies. Subsequent books including *The Price of Inequality* (2012) and *People, Power, and Profits* (2019) extended his critique to domestic U.S. economic policy and market concentration.

---

## Controversies & Criticism

Stiglitz's outspoken criticism of the IMF and U.S. Treasury during his World Bank tenure generated significant controversy, contributing to his departure in 2000. Some economists argue his policy prescriptions — favoring capital controls and graduated liberalization — have been selectively applied and insufficiently rigorous when translated from theory to practice. Conservative critics contend his framing of inequality systematically underweights the growth benefits of open markets. Others have noted that his public commentary sometimes outpaces the evidentiary standards he applies in academic work.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Stiglitz fundamentally reshaped how economists think about markets, demonstrating that information failures — not just externalities or monopoly power — are pervasive sources of market dysfunction. His work provided theoretical grounding for financial regulation, healthcare economics, and development policy. Generations of economists trained under his influence, and his public advocacy helped shift mainstream discourse on austerity, inequality, and globalization in measurable ways throughout the 2000s and 2010s.
