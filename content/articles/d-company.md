## Summary

Indian organized crime syndicate led by Dawood Ibrahim that is involved in drug trafficking, extortion, and terrorism. D-Company orchestrated the 1993 Mumbai bombings that killed 257 people.

## Key Details

### Origins and leadership

D-Company is commonly described as an organized crime syndicate originating in Mumbai and associated with Dawood Ibrahim. Public sources portray it as a networked organization rather than a single fixed group, with shifting alliances, overseas facilitation, and ties to illicit markets that extend beyond India.

### Criminal activities

Reporting and official designations have linked the syndicate to a range of activities such as smuggling, narcotics trafficking, extortion, contract violence, and financial crimes. The organization is also discussed in the context of illicit finance routes, including hawala-style transfers and the use of front businesses to move and store proceeds.

### Political and security concerns

D-Company is notable in part because it has been associated in public accounts with terrorism-linked activity, including the 1993 Mumbai bombings. This overlap—organized crime logistics intersecting with political violence—has made the group a frequent reference point in counterterrorism and organized-crime studies.

### International designations and enforcement

The syndicate has been the target of international law-enforcement efforts, sanctions, and watchlists, including terrorism-related designations reported in the early 2000s. These measures typically aim to restrict travel, freeze assets, and deter banks and intermediaries from providing services.

## Significance

The D-Company case is often used to illustrate how transnational criminal networks can leverage diaspora connections, informal finance, and cross-border safe havens while remaining difficult to dismantle through purely domestic policing.
