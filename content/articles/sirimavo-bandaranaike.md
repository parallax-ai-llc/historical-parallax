## Summary

Sirimavo Ratwatte Dias Bandaranaike made history on July 21, 1960, when she became the world's first female prime minister, leading Sri Lanka (then Ceylon) at a time when women's political leadership was virtually unprecedented.[^1] Born into a Kandyan aristocratic family, she entered politics following the assassination of her husband, Prime Minister S.W.R.D. Bandaranaike, in 1959.[^2] She served three terms as prime minister (1960-1965, 1970-1977, 1994-2000), implementing socialist policies, nationalizing industries, and transforming Ceylon into the republic of Sri Lanka.[^3] While celebrated globally as a trailblazer for women's political participation, her legacy remains deeply contested due to pro-Sinhalese policies that marginalized the Tamil minority and contributed to ethnic tensions that would eventually erupt into civil war.[^4] She died on October 10, 2000, shortly after casting her vote in parliamentary elections, leaving behind a complex legacy of groundbreaking achievement shadowed by sectarian division.[^5]

---

## Early Life and Background

Sirimavo Ratwatte was born on April 17, 1916, at Ellawala Walawwa in Ratnapura, British Ceylon, into one of the island's most prominent Kandyan aristocratic families.[^6] She was the eldest child of Barnes Ratwatte, the Disawe (provincial governor) of Sabaragamuwa, and Rosalind Mahawalathenna Kumarihamy, who belonged to the traditional Kandyan elite.[^7] Growing up in a household of privilege with four brothers and two sisters, young Sirimavo was immersed in both traditional Sinhalese Buddhist culture and the colonial English education system that characterized the lives of Ceylon's upper classes.[^8]

Her education reflected this dual cultural heritage. She attended Ferguson High School in Ratnapura before transferring to St. Bridget's Convent in Colombo, a Catholic, English-medium institution.[^9] Despite her Catholic schooling, Sirimavo remained a devout Buddhist throughout her life and was equally fluent in both Sinhala and English.[^10] This bilingual capability would later prove valuable in her political career, though her governments would controversially eliminate English as an official language.

After completing her schooling at age 19, Sirimavo did not pursue higher education but instead devoted herself to social welfare work, an acceptable activity for women of her class.[^11] She traveled to remote jungle villages distributing food and medicine, organizing medical clinics, and helping create rural industries to improve living standards for village women.[^12] This early exposure to rural poverty would shape her later socialist policy orientations. She served as treasurer of the Social Service League until 1940, demonstrating organizational skills that would later be deployed in political contexts.[^13]

In October 1940, Sirimavo married Solomon West Ridgeway Dias Bandaranaike, known as S.W.R.D. or "Banda," a highly educated aristocrat who had been educated at Oxford and was already emerging as a significant political figure.[^14] The marriage united two of Ceylon's most prominent families and would eventually lead Sirimavo into a political career she had never anticipated. For the next two decades, she devoted herself primarily to raising their three children and serving as hostess to her husband's numerous political associates, showing little interest in politics herself.[^15]

## Rise to Power

Sirimavo Bandaranaike's entry into politics was both sudden and tragic. On September 25, 1959, her husband, who had become Prime Minister in 1956 after founding the socialist Sri Lanka Freedom Party (SLFP) in 1951, was assassinated by a Buddhist monk at their family residence.[^16] The assassination shocked the nation and left the SLFP without its charismatic leader at a critical juncture. The party leadership, recognizing the emotional appeal and symbolic continuity that the widowed Sirimavo could provide, persuaded her to assume leadership of the SLFP despite her complete lack of political experience.[^17]

Initially reluctant and grieving, Sirimavo agreed to lead the party, later stating: "It was far from my mind to achieve any personal glory for myself when I assumed the leadership of the party at the request of its leaders."[^18] She embarked on an extensive campaign tour throughout the country, making emotional speeches in which she frequently burst into tears as she pledged to continue her late husband's policies.[^19] Her opponents mockingly called her "The Weeping Widow," but the strategy proved remarkably effective with voters who saw her as the rightful heir to her husband's political legacy.[^20]

The SLFP won a decisive victory in the July 1960 general election, defeating Prime Minister Dudley Senanayake's United National Party (UNP).[^21] On July 21, 1960, at age 44, Sirimavo Bandaranaike was sworn in as Prime Minister of Ceylon, becoming the world's first female prime minister—a distinction that brought her instant international recognition.[^22] The achievement was particularly remarkable given that it occurred in an era when women in many Western democracies still faced significant barriers to political participation.

However, her ascent was not purely based on grief and sympathy. Sirimavo quickly demonstrated political acumen and determination. She surrounded herself with experienced advisors, learned rapidly about governmental processes, and proved willing to make difficult decisions.[^23] When later asked how she functioned with an all-male cabinet, she responded: "I was often asked the question how I functioned with an all-male Cabinet. I must say that I had no problems. They all co-operated and gave me all the support necessary."[^24] Her political opponents would later grudgingly acknowledge her toughness, with one saying: "She was the only man in her cabinet."[^25]

## Major Achievements

Sirimavo Bandaranaike's tenure as prime minister across three separate terms was marked by ambitious attempts to transform Ceylon/Sri Lanka's economic, political, and social structures along socialist lines, combined with significant achievements in foreign policy and constitutional reform.

**Socialist Economic Transformation**

Bandaranaike pursued aggressive socialist policies aimed at reducing foreign control of the economy and redistributing wealth. She nationalized organizations across the banking, education, industry, media, and trade sectors, fundamentally reshaping Ceylon's economic structure.[^26] Her government took control of key industries, restricted free enterprise, and implemented land reform programs designed to break up large estates and redistribute land to small farmers.[^27] These policies reflected both ideological commitment to socialism and nationalist resentment of foreign economic control, particularly by British and Indian business interests.

The nationalization program extended to critical sectors including petroleum distribution, bus transportation, and port facilities. While these measures increased state control over the economy and reduced foreign influence, they also created inefficiencies and increased the government bureaucracy substantially.[^28] Nevertheless, her supporters argued that nationalization was necessary to prevent wealth from flowing out of the country and to ensure that economic development benefited ordinary Sri Lankans rather than foreign shareholders.

**Constitutional Reform and Republican Status**

One of Bandaranaike's most significant achievements was overseeing the transformation of Ceylon into a republic. In 1972, her government promulgated a new constitution that ended Ceylon's dominion status within the British Commonwealth, renamed the country the Democratic Socialist Republic of Sri Lanka, and created an executive presidency.[^29] This constitutional change formally severed colonial ties and asserted Sri Lanka's complete sovereignty and independence. The new constitution also declared Buddhism the foremost religion while guaranteeing freedom of worship to other religions, reflecting the government's pro-Buddhist orientation.[^30]

**Foreign Policy and Non-Alignment**

Bandaranaike achieved considerable international recognition for her foreign policy leadership. She developed and elevated Sri Lanka's non-aligned foreign policy to new heights, earning international fame for her diplomatic skills.[^31] While maintaining non-alignment between Cold War power blocs, she positioned Sri Lanka as a leader among developing countries, pushing forward their shared interests in global forums.[^32] In 1976, Sri Lanka hosted the fifth summit of the Non-Aligned Movement in Colombo, and Bandaranaike became its chairperson, placing her small island nation at the center of Third World political movements.[^33]

She successfully negotiated the Sirimavo-Shastri Pact with Indian Prime Minister Lal Bahadur Shastri in October 1964, addressing the contentious issue of the political status of plantation workers of Indian origin in Ceylon.[^34] This agreement, though controversial domestically, demonstrated her ability to conduct complex diplomatic negotiations and manage difficult bilateral relationships.

**Social Welfare Programs**

Bandaranaike's governments emphasized social welfare and rural development, contributing to improvements in healthcare, education, and infrastructure, particularly in underserved rural areas.[^35] Food subsidies, free education, and healthcare programs expanded access for poor and rural populations, though these programs also strained government finances.[^36] Her commitment to these programs reflected both socialist ideology and pragmatic political calculation, as rural voters formed her party's core constituency.

**Survival of Major Crises**

Bandaranaike demonstrated political resilience by surviving multiple major crises. In 1962, she foiled an attempted coup d'état led by military and police officers who opposed her socialist policies.[^37] In 1971, she faced a major insurrection by the radical Marxist JVP (Janatha Vimukthi Peramuna), which killed thousands and threatened her government's survival.[^38] Her decisive response to these challenges, though criticized for human rights violations, demonstrated political toughness and willingness to use state power to maintain control.

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite her historic achievements, Sirimavo Bandaranaike's legacy is severely tarnished by policies and actions that discriminated against minority communities, suppressed political opposition, and contributed to ethnic conflict that would devastate Sri Lanka for decades.

**Language Policies and Tamil Alienation**

One of Bandaranaike's first and most controversial actions was enforcing Sinhala as the only officially recognized language of government, eliminating English's official status.[^39] Within a year of her 1960 election, she declared a state of emergency following a civil disobedience campaign by the Tamil population who were outraged by this decision.[^40] The Tamil minority, comprising about 11% of the population, viewed the Sinhala-only policy as deliberate marginalization and a violation of their linguistic and cultural rights.[^41]

The language policy sparked immediate Tamil resistance and civil disobedience in Northern and Eastern provinces.[^42] Bandaranaike responded by curtailing Tamil political activity, decreeing a state of emergency under which strikers could be jailed for up to five years, banning the Tamils' Federal Party, and imprisoning more than 70 of its leaders, including all but one of its Members of Parliament.[^43] This harsh repression deepened Tamil alienation and contributed to the emergence of more radical Tamil nationalist movements.

**Educational Discrimination**

Tamil youth were particularly angered by the "standardization" policy that Bandaranaike's government introduced in 1973, which made university admission criteria lower for Sinhalese students than for Tamil students.[^44] This policy was justified as affirmative action to address regional educational disparities, but Tamils viewed it as discriminatory and designed to limit Tamil access to higher education and professional opportunities. The policy radicalized a generation of educated Tamil youth who saw their futures blocked by state-sponsored discrimination.

**Ethnic Policies and Civil War**

Bandaranaike's pro-Buddhist and pro-Sinhalese policies systematically alienated the Tamil minority, forcing them into positions of second-class citizenship.[^45] While she did not directly spark the civil war between Sinhalese and Tamils that erupted in 1983, she acted as a catalyst in creating the tensions and grievances that made armed conflict increasingly likely.[^46] Changing the administrative language from English to Sinhala and routinely campaigning on Sinhalese nationalist and anti-Tamil policies exacerbated discontent among the native Tamil population.[^47] Since 1983, approximately 68,000 people lost their lives in the civil war, a conflict whose roots can be traced in part to the discriminatory policies of Bandaranaike's governments.[^48]

**Authoritarianism and Press Suppression**

Bandaranaike became increasingly intolerant of criticism and opposition. She forced the shutdown of the Independent newspaper group and other critical media outlets.[^49] Her governments faced numerous accusations of suppressing political opposition and authoritarianism, with emergency regulations used to detain opponents without trial.[^50] The tendency toward statist authoritarianism became a troubling feature of both her governments and the SLFP more broadly.[^51]

**Civic Rights Revocation**

After her landslide defeat in the 1977 election, Bandaranaike was stripped of her civic rights in 1980 by the Sri Lankan parliament and barred from holding political office for seven years for alleged abuses of power during her tenure.[^52] Though she was granted a pardon in 1986 that restored her rights, the civic rights case highlighted concerns about corruption and abuse of authority during her governments.[^53]

**Human Rights Violations**

Bandaranaike's governments were accused of human rights violations, particularly against minority groups such as Tamils and Muslims.[^54] Her responses to the 1971 JVP insurrection and other security challenges involved extensive use of emergency powers, detention without trial, and alleged torture and extrajudicial killings, though the full extent of these violations remains debated.

## Personal Life

Behind the public persona of the world's first female prime minister was a complex woman shaped by aristocratic tradition, Buddhist faith, and personal tragedy. Sirimavo's marriage to S.W.R.D. Bandaranaike in October 1940 united two of Ceylon's most distinguished families and proved to be a genuine partnership, though one that placed Sirimavo firmly in a traditional supporting role for most of her married life.[^55]

Over the next twenty years, Sirimavo devoted herself to raising their three children and serving as hostess to her husband's political associates, showing little personal interest in politics despite being surrounded by it constantly.[^56] The couple had one son, Anura (born 1949), and two daughters, Sunethra Rupasinghe and Chandrika Kumaratunga (who would later be elected president of Sri Lanka in 1994).[^57] All three children were educated at prestigious universities abroad—Sunethra at Oxford, Chandrika at the University of Paris, and Anura at the University of London—before returning to serve in various capacities in Sri Lankan government and public life.[^58]

Sirimavo's personality combined traditional feminine qualities expected of upper-class Ceylon women with steely determination and political toughness. She considered herself a "Mother of the People," using maternal imagery to describe her relationship with the nation.[^59] This self-conception allowed her to exercise power while maintaining traditional gender roles rhetorically, though in practice she often demonstrated qualities her opponents grudgingly acknowledged as masculine strength.

Her Buddhist faith remained central to her identity throughout her life. Though educated in Catholic schools, she maintained her Buddhist practice and her governments actively promoted Buddhist institutions and values.[^60] This religious commitment was both genuine personal conviction and political calculation, as Buddhist Sinhalese formed her core political constituency.

Bandaranaike spoke both Sinhala and English fluently, moving easily between traditional Sinhalese Buddhist culture and the Anglicized world of Ceylon's elite.[^61] She maintained the dignity and bearing expected of her aristocratic background, even during her period of political exile after losing her civic rights in 1980. When asked about overcoming fear and political challenges, she stated: "The only way to overcome fear is to face it squarely," a principle she demonstrated repeatedly throughout her tumultuous political career.[^62]

Despite her groundbreaking role as a female leader, Bandaranaike did not place much personal or political emphasis on women's issues generally.[^63] She expressed pride in her status as a woman leader but did not use her position to significantly advance feminist causes or substantially increase women's political representation in Sri Lanka.[^64] Her approach to gender was conservative, rooted in traditional roles even as she herself transcended them.

## Legacy

Sirimavo Bandaranaike's legacy is profoundly contradictory, combining groundbreaking achievement in women's political leadership with destructive ethnic policies that contributed to decades of civil conflict. How she is remembered depends heavily on one's perspective and which aspects of her long political career receive emphasis.

**Global Pioneer for Women's Leadership**

Internationally, Bandaranaike is primarily remembered as a trailblazer who shattered gender barriers in political leadership. At a time when the idea of a woman leading a country was almost unthinkable to the public globally, she helped raise the global perception of women's capabilities.[^65] Her election in 1960 preceded female heads of government in most Western democracies by decades, demonstrating that women could exercise executive political power successfully. Her three terms in office spanning four decades proved that her initial election was not a fluke but represented genuine political skills and popular support.

However, political scholars have noted that Bandaranaike was symbolically powerful but ultimately had little direct impact on women's political representation in Sri Lanka itself.[^66] Her election did not significantly increase the number of women in Sri Lankan politics, and she did not champion women's issues as a political priority.[^67] Nevertheless, her example inspired women political leaders across South Asia, with both her daughter Chandrika Kumaratunga and her political rival's daughter eventually becoming leaders in their own right.

**Contested Domestic Legacy**

Within Sri Lanka, Bandaranaike's legacy is far more contentious. As one historical assessment notes: "Although Mrs. Bandaranaike's accomplishments in such fields as foreign affairs and social development are formidable personal achievements in any political career, her contribution to the politics of Sri Lanka in a crucial period of its post-colonial progress is, as a matter of historical assessment, rather more contentious."[^68]

Her governments' tendency toward "statist authoritarianism and a sectarian form of majoritarianism" remains deeply troubling in retrospect.[^69] The discriminatory policies toward Tamils and other minorities, particularly the language policies and educational standardization, created grievances that fueled ethnic conflict. While she did not directly spark the civil war that erupted in 1983, her policies acted as a catalyst for the tensions that made armed conflict increasingly inevitable.[^70]

**Economic Policy Legacy**

The economic legacy of Bandaranaike's socialist policies is also debated. Supporters argue that her emphasis on social welfare and rural development contributed to improvements in healthcare, education, and infrastructure, particularly for underserved populations.[^71] Sri Lanka achieved relatively high literacy rates and health outcomes compared to countries with similar income levels, in part due to investments in public services that her governments promoted.

Critics, however, contend that nationalization and state intervention created economic inefficiencies, discouraged private investment, and contributed to slower economic growth than might otherwise have been achieved.[^72] The legacy of state interventionism persisted long after her governments ended, shaping Sri Lanka's economic trajectory for decades.[^73]

**Foreign Policy Achievement**

Bandaranaike's foreign policy leadership, particularly her role in the Non-Aligned Movement and her skillful navigation of Cold War pressures, remains widely respected.[^74] She successfully positioned Sri Lanka as a leader among developing nations and maintained the country's independence from both Western and Soviet blocs, an achievement that transcended domestic political divisions.

**Family Political Dynasty**

Perhaps one of Bandaranaike's most tangible legacies is the political dynasty she established. Her daughter Chandrika Kumaratunga served as president from 1994 to 2005, and various family members have remained influential in Sri Lankan politics.[^75] This dynastic succession has been criticized as undermining democratic accountability and perpetuating elite control of politics, while defenders argue it reflects genuine popular support for the family's political vision.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Admirers and defenders of Sirimavo Bandaranaike emphasize her pioneering role in breaking gender barriers and her commitment to social justice and national sovereignty. Her supporters argue that she genuinely sought to uplift Sri Lanka's poor and rural populations through socialist policies that prioritized public welfare over profit.[^76] The nationalization programs, while economically problematic, represented an authentic attempt to reclaim national resources from foreign control and direct them toward national development rather than external enrichment.

Defenders also note that Bandaranaike governed during extremely difficult circumstances, facing attempted coups, insurgencies, and economic crises that would have challenged any leader.[^77] Her survival of these challenges and her ability to maintain democratic processes despite severe provocation demonstrated political skill and commitment to constitutional governance, even when authoritarian alternatives were available and tempting.

From a Third World and post-colonial perspective, Bandaranaike's emphasis on non-alignment and her leadership in forums like the Non-Aligned Movement represented principled resistance to neo-colonialism and great power domination.[^78] She used Sri Lanka's voice to amplify concerns of developing nations and refused to subordinate her country's interests to either Cold War bloc, maintaining genuine independence in foreign policy.

Supporters also argue that her governments' achievements in social welfare—particularly in education and healthcare—created lasting improvements in quality of life for ordinary Sri Lankans.[^79] The emphasis on free education and healthcare, though fiscally challenging, reflected genuine commitment to reducing inequality and providing basic services to all citizens regardless of income.

Some scholars credit Bandaranaike with successfully managing the transition from colonial dominion to independent republic, completing the decolonization process that her husband had begun.[^80] The 1972 constitution, whatever its flaws, represented Sri Lankan self-determination and the final rejection of colonial constitutional structures.

### Negative Views

Critics of Bandaranaike focus primarily on her ethnic policies and their catastrophic long-term consequences. The Sinhala-only language policy, educational standardization discriminating against Tamils, and general pro-Sinhalese sectarianism are viewed as deliberate marginalization of minorities that violated democratic principles and basic rights.[^81] These policies transformed Tamil grievances from negotiable political demands into existential concerns about survival and dignity, radicalizing Tamil politics and making violent conflict increasingly likely.

Scholars critical of Bandaranaike note her "effortless" embrace of "sectarian majoritarianism," suggesting that she exploited ethnic divisions for political advantage without serious consideration of long-term consequences.[^82] The civil war that eventually erupted in 1983, killing approximately 68,000 people over subsequent decades, is viewed by critics as having roots in the discriminatory policies of Bandaranaike's governments.[^83]

Critics also challenge the economic legacy of her socialist policies. The nationalization programs are viewed as ideologically driven rather than pragmatically designed, creating inefficient state enterprises that drained public resources while failing to deliver promised development.[^84] The restriction of free enterprise discouraged investment, innovation, and entrepreneurship, contributing to economic stagnation and missed development opportunities.

The authoritarian tendencies of her governments—emergency regulations, detention without trial, press suppression, and intolerance of opposition—are viewed as undermining democratic norms and creating precedents for abuse of power.[^85] The civic rights case that led to her temporary exclusion from politics is cited as evidence of genuine corruption and abuse of authority rather than purely political persecution.

Some feminist scholars argue that Bandaranaike's failure to advance women's issues or increase female political participation represents a missed opportunity.[^86] Rather than using her historic position to promote gender equality, she maintained traditional patriarchal structures while personally exercising power, thus providing symbolic representation without substantive advancement of women's rights.

Critics of dynastic politics view Bandaranaike's family political succession as undermining democratic accountability and perpetuating elite control of Sri Lankan politics.[^87] The concentration of power within political families is seen as antithetical to genuine democracy and representative government.
