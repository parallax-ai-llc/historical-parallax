## Summary

Jang Bogo (787–846), also known as Gungbok or Gungpa in childhood, was a Sillan naval commander, merchant, and warlord who rose from humble origins to become one of the most powerful maritime figures in East Asian history.[^1] Known as the "King of the Yellow Sea," he dominated trade between Silla, Tang China, and Heian Japan during the first half of the 9th century CE.[^2] Establishing the Cheonghaejin garrison in 828 with 10,000 troops, Jang eliminated piracy, protected Korean merchants from slavery, and created a lucrative monopoly on regional ceramics trade.[^3] His assassination in 846 by aristocratic rivals ended a remarkable career that saw a commoner rise to challenge Korea's rigid social hierarchy.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Jang Bogo was born in 787 CE (though some sources suggest 788) in the town of Cheonghae on Wando Island, located off the southwest coast of the Unified Silla Kingdom.[^5] His humble origins were particularly significant in a society characterized by strict social hierarchies and rigid class distinctions. Born as the son of a boatman, Jang's childhood name was Gungbok (also recorded as Gungpa), reflecting his provincial and common birth far from the aristocratic centers of power in the Silla capital of Gyeongju.[^6]

Little is documented about Jang's early education or formative years, as historical records from this period typically focused on aristocratic lineages rather than commoners.[^7] The surviving writings of the early medieval period tend to mix legend with fact, leaving behind a rather mysterious narrative about his youth.[^8] However, what is clear from multiple sources is that Jang demonstrated exceptional martial abilities from a young age, particularly in horsemanship and spear handling, skills that would prove crucial to his future success.[^9]

During his youth, Jang formed a close friendship with Jeong Yeon, a companion who would remain his closest adviser and military commander throughout his life.[^10] Historical records note that Jeong Yeon possessed remarkable abilities, including the legendary capacity to swim five li (approximately 2.5 kilometers) underwater without taking a breath, though this claim likely represents the period's tendency to embellish the capabilities of notable figures.[^11] This friendship would prove instrumental to Jang's later political and military successes.

As young men seeking opportunity beyond the limited prospects available to commoners in Silla's stratified society, Jang Bogo and Jeong Yeon made the consequential decision to travel to Tang China.[^12] This journey to the Chinese mainland represented a common path for ambitious Koreans of the period, as thousands of Silla subjects lived in expatriate communities in Jiangsu and Shandong provinces, where military service offered avenues for advancement unavailable at home.[^13] Their exceptional martial skills quickly gained recognition, and both men were appointed as Junior Generals of Wuning District in what is today Jiangsu province.[^14]

Jang's experience in Tang China proved formative in multiple ways. Serving as an officer in the Tang dynasty army, he fought in campaigns in the lower Huai River basin and gained valuable military experience.[^15] More importantly, he witnessed firsthand the plight of his fellow Koreans living abroad. In the unstable milieu of late Tang China, Silla subjects had become favored targets of coastal pirates and inland bandits who captured them and sold them into slavery.[^16] This brutal slave trade so incensed Jang that it became his primary motivation for returning to Korea and establishing a military force capable of protecting Korean merchants and travelers.[^17]

## Rise to Power

Jang Bogo's rise from obscure provincial origins to become one of the most powerful figures in East Asia represents one of the most remarkable social ascents in Korean history. Around 825 CE, after accumulating considerable wealth and experience in Tang China, Jang returned to his homeland with a formidable private fleet headquartered at Cheonghae on Wando Island.[^18] His return coincided with a period of increasing instability in the Later Silla period, when the kingdom faced both external threats from piracy and internal political turmoil.

The severity of the Korean slave trade had reached such proportions that in 823 CE, the Tang emperor issued an official edict ordering a halt to the enslavement of Koreans and demanding the return of all abducted Silla subjects.[^19] This imperial intervention demonstrated the scale of the problem, yet enforcement proved ineffective without sustained military action. Recognizing this gap, Jang petitioned King Heungdeok (r. 826-836) with a proposal that would transform the maritime security landscape of Northeast Asia.

Jang's petition emphasized the humanitarian crisis facing Korean travelers and merchants: "Everywhere in Tang they use our people as slaves. I beg Your Majesty to build headquarters at Chonghae to prevent pirates from kidnapping our people and shipping them west."[^20] He argued that only a permanent military presence with centralized command could effectively eradicate the Chinese pirates plaguing the East Asian seas and provide naval escorts for Koreans traveling by sea.[^21] Jang requested authority to recruit and train up to 10,000 troops from regional conscripts, emphasizing the garrison's role in restoring "clear seas" (cheonghae) free from banditry.[^22]

King Heungdeok recognized both the merit of Jang's proposal and the naval commander's demonstrated capability. In 828 CE, the king formally established the Cheonghae (淸海, "clear sea") Garrison at Wando Island off Korea's South Jeolla province, granting Jang command of an army of 10,000 men to establish and man the defensive works.[^23] This represented an extraordinary grant of military authority to a man of common birth, reflecting both the urgency of the piracy problem and Jang's exceptional reputation.

Jang's military campaign against piracy proved remarkably effective. Commanding his 10,000 troops with skill developed during his Tang military service, he swiftly eradicated the piracy plaguing Yellow Sea trade routes, restoring safe passage for merchants within just two years of the garrison's establishment.[^24] The Cheonghaejin garrison on Wando Island became a fortified naval base and central hub for East Asian trade, providing not only military protection but also docking facilities, defensive fortifications, and administrative oversight for merchant activities.[^25]

With the sea lanes secured, Jang leveraged his leadership of the Korean expatriate community on the Shandong peninsula to establish a lucrative monopoly on the region's ceramics trade.[^26] From his base, Jang's naval fleet controlled all maritime trade between China and Korea across the Yellow Sea and South Sea, as well as commerce with ancient Japan.[^27] This commercial dominance allowed for lucrative exchanges, including the export of Silla-produced porcelain, which became a significant revenue source as Jang sought to replicate and trade high-value Chinese ceramics.[^28]

## Major Achievements

Jang Bogo's major achievements span military, commercial, and political spheres, establishing him as one of the most consequential figures in 9th-century East Asian history. His accomplishments fundamentally reshaped maritime trade networks and demonstrated that exceptional ability could overcome even the most rigid social barriers of the Silla period.

### Elimination of Piracy and Protection from Slavery

Jang's most immediate and humanitarian achievement was the systematic elimination of piracy in the Yellow Sea region. The pirates who had terrorized Korean merchants and sold captured Silla subjects into slavery throughout Tang China were decisively defeated within two years of Cheonghaejin's establishment.[^29] This military success not only protected Korean lives but also restored confidence in maritime commerce, enabling a dramatic expansion of trade throughout Northeast Asia. Archaeological evidence from the Cheonghaejin site confirms the garrison's sophisticated defensive capabilities, including an 890-meter-long earthen fortress wall, various building sites, and a unique U-shaped stone embankment measuring 21 x 22 meters that served as a coastal defense structure unlike any found in China, Japan, or Korea.[^30]

### Domination of Maritime Trade

Once piracy was eliminated, Jang established what historians have described as an effective monopoly on maritime trade in Northeast Asia. He and the Silla merchants under his protection dominated commerce among Tang China, Unified Silla, and Heian Japan, with goods including precious metals, manufactured items from furniture to weapons, silk, tea, ginseng, and ceramics.[^31] His control was so comprehensive that he earned the title "King of the Yellow Sea," a designation that accurately reflected his unprecedented maritime dominance.[^32] From his Cheonghaejin base, Jang's naval fleet could control all maritime trade routes in the region, providing both military protection and commercial infrastructure that made him indispensable to merchants from all three nations.[^33]

### Buddhist Patronage and Cultural Exchange

Beyond military and commercial achievements, Jang Bogo significantly contributed to Buddhist cultural exchange throughout East Asia. He is credited with establishing the Buddhist temple of Bophwawon and its monastery at Shandong, which housed 28 monks and nuns and served as an important center for Korean Buddhist communities in China.[^34] His trading activities facilitated the spread of Buddhism across East Asia, as documented in the "Account of a Pilgrimage to Tang in Search of the Law" by the Japanese scholar-monk Ennin, which describes a voyage in one of Jang's naval vessels in 840 CE to the Buddhist monastery at Shandong.[^35] This cultural patronage demonstrated Jang's understanding that power derived not merely from military might and commercial wealth but also from religious and cultural influence.

### Political Kingmaking

Jang's accumulation of wealth and military power inevitably drew him into Silla's turbulent court politics. In 839 CE, when King Minae was killed and the capital descended into chaos, Jang proved instrumental in determining the succession. He dispatched a force of 5,000 men under the command of his closest companion and adviser Jeong Yeon in support of Kim Ujing's claim to the throne.[^36] When Jeong Yeon expressed concern about the mission, Jang reportedly told him, "No one else apart from you can settle this distress."[^37] The intervention succeeded, and Kim Ujing ascended the throne as King Sinmu (r. 839). In gratitude, the new king granted Jang the impressive title of Grand General of Cheonghae, formally recognizing his extraordinary status despite his common birth.[^38]

### Archaeological Legacy

Modern archaeological investigations have confirmed the scale of Jang's achievements. Since 1991, eight excavations conducted by the National Research Institute of Cultural Heritage on Jangdo Island have uncovered over 30,000 artifacts and revealed sophisticated infrastructure including wooden structures, a 6-meter-deep well built with an innovative log foundation system, drainage channels with flagstone bottoms and funnel-shaped entrances, and an underwater wooden barricade.[^39] These discoveries provide decisive evidence that the Historic Site of Cheonghaejin Fort was indeed Jang's headquarters and confirm the sophistication of 9th-century Korean maritime engineering.[^40]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his remarkable achievements, Jang Bogo's career was marked by significant controversies that ultimately led to his downfall. His rise challenged fundamental assumptions about social hierarchy in Silla society, generating intense opposition from aristocratic factions who viewed him as a dangerous upstart threatening the established order.

### Aristocratic Opposition and Social Class Tensions

The most persistent criticism of Jang Bogo centered on his common birth and perceived overreach beyond his social station. In 845 CE, Jang attempted to marry his daughter to King Munseong (r. 839-857), who was the son of King Sinmu and thus a figure Jang had helped place on the throne.[^41] This marriage proposal raised a "great tumult" in the Silla court, as noblemen were greatly opposed to the idea and openly hated Jang Bogo, painting him as a common-born opportunist seeking to infiltrate the royal bloodline.[^42] The aristocratic factions at court, fed up with the machinations of a man from obscure provincial origins outside Silla's aristocratic order, viewed the marriage proposal as an intolerable violation of social boundaries.[^43]

The Silla king, under intense pressure from aristocrats, ultimately denied Jang's marriage proposal.[^44] This rejection represented more than a personal setback; it demonstrated the ultimate limits of social mobility in Later Silla society. No matter how much wealth, military power, or political influence Jang accumulated, the hereditary aristocracy refused to accept him as an equal. Historical records indicate that Jang "was often hated by the Silla royal family members due to his prominent status and the fact that he was born a commoner, not a nobleman."[^45]

### Political Conspiracy and Rebellion

Following the rejection of his marriage proposal, historical sources indicate that Jang began to conspire against the king.[^46] The precise nature of this conspiracy remains unclear due to the fragmentary historical record, but it evidently involved leveraging his military forces at Cheonghaejin to pressure or threaten the court. This political plotting provided Jang's aristocratic enemies with the justification they needed to eliminate him permanently. From their perspective, a powerful military commander with a private army of 10,000 men and control over maritime trade routes who was willing to challenge royal authority represented an existential threat to the established order.

### Questions About Commercial Monopoly

While Jang's elimination of piracy was universally praised, his subsequent commercial monopoly generated some criticism. His control over the ceramics trade and dominance of maritime commerce in Northeast Asia, while economically beneficial to Silla, concentrated enormous wealth and power in the hands of a single individual operating with considerable autonomy from the central government.[^47] This economic concentration, combined with military power, created a regional power base that could challenge royal authority—precisely what Silla's aristocracy feared.

### Historical Interpretation Debates

The historical assessment of Jang Bogo has varied significantly across different periods. Interestingly, the Samguk Sagi and Samguk Yusa, compiled centuries after Jang's death, did not record him as a traitor.[^48] Later Joseon dynasty records even praised Jang for "suppressing the rebellion and the loyalty of pirates."[^49] However, the three primary sources for his life—the Chinese New Book of Tang, the Japanese Shoku Nihon Kōki, and the Korean 12th-century Samguk Sagi—were all compiled long after his death and contain inconsistencies.[^50] The Shoku Nihon Kōki, for instance, gives Jang's date of death as 841, while Korean sources record 846.[^51] This conflicting evidence has generated scholarly debate about the precise sequence of events leading to his assassination.

## Personal Life

Despite Jang Bogo's prominence in the historical record, details about his personal and family life remain remarkably sparse, reflecting the tendency of medieval Korean historians to focus on political and military achievements rather than intimate biographical details, particularly for figures of common birth.

### Family Background and Daughter

As previously noted, Jang was born as the son of a boatman, with his childhood name being Gungbok or Gungpa.[^52] Beyond this basic information, historical sources provide virtually no details about his parents, siblings, or early family circumstances. The one family member who features prominently in historical accounts is Jang's daughter, whose attempted marriage to King Munseong became the catalyst for his downfall.[^53] Unfortunately, even this daughter remains unnamed in surviving records, her identity subsumed entirely by the political controversy surrounding the proposed marriage. No information survives about Jang's wife or any other children he may have had, leaving significant gaps in our understanding of his personal life.

### Friendship with Jeong Yeon

The most fully documented personal relationship in Jang's life was his lifelong friendship and professional partnership with Jeong Yeon (정년; 鄭年). Historical records indicate that as young men, the two companions traveled together to Tang China, where both earned positions as Junior Generals through their exceptional martial skills.[^54] The Samguk Sagi relates that Jeong Yeon "could swim five li (about 2.5 km) underwater, without taking a breath," though this claim represents the biographical hyperbole common to the period.[^55] 

Their relationship transcended mere companionship, evolving into a crucial political and military partnership. When Jang needed a commander he could trust absolutely for the critical mission of supporting Kim Ujing's claim to the throne in 839, he chose Jeong Yeon, telling him that "no one else apart from you can settle this distress."[^56] This statement reveals not only Jang's deep trust in his friend but also his reliance on personal loyalty in navigating the treacherous world of Silla court politics. Jeong Yeon served as Jang's closest adviser and commanded forces of 5,000 men on his behalf, demonstrating that Jang rewarded loyalty and shared power with those he trusted.[^57]

### Personality and Character

While direct descriptions of Jang's personality are limited, his actions reveal several character traits. His primary motivation for returning to Korea—witnessing his countrymen enslaved in China and determining to end this practice—demonstrates a combination of patriotism and humanitarian concern unusual for the period.[^58] His petition to King Heungdeok emphasized protecting "our people" from slavery, suggesting a sense of national identity and collective responsibility that transcended his personal ambition.[^59]

Simultaneously, Jang clearly possessed significant personal ambition. His rise from the son of a boatman to a position where he could attempt to marry his daughter into the royal family represents an extraordinary trajectory driven by exceptional ability and relentless determination.[^60] His willingness to challenge social norms and aristocratic prejudices, while ultimately fatal, marked him as a figure willing to risk everything to transcend the limitations of his birth.

### Religious Affiliation

Jang's establishment of the Buddhist temple Bophwawon and its monastery at Shandong indicates genuine Buddhist faith, though whether this represented deep spiritual conviction or pragmatic recognition of Buddhism's importance in East Asian political culture remains debatable.[^61] His facilitation of Buddhist cultural exchange and provision of transportation for Buddhist monks like Ennin suggests at minimum a respectful relationship with Buddhist institutions that went beyond mere political calculation.[^62]

## Legacy

Jang Bogo's legacy extends far beyond his 9th-century lifetime, influencing military strategy, maritime commerce, national identity, and popular culture in ways that continue to resonate in modern Korea and throughout East Asia.

### Immediate Aftermath and Deification

Following Jang's assassination in 846 CE by Yeomjang, an emissary from the Silla court who concealed a knife in his garments and gained Jang's confidence by pretending to have fled from the capital before attacking him during a shared wine session, the Cheonghaejin garrison descended into turmoil.[^63] Jang's followers mounted resistance, but by early 851 CE, King Gyeongmun of Silla ordered the formal dissolution of Cheonghaejin in February, ending the military institution that had dominated East Asian maritime trade for over two decades.[^64]

Remarkably, despite his violent death and the aristocratic opposition that killed him, Jang Bogo was deified following his assassination and integrated into shamanistic practices, particularly among coastal communities.[^65] He became venerated as a guardian deity of the seas, with locals on Jangdo Island maintaining a shamanistic temple dedicated to the "Great General Song," a title they equate with Jang Bogo.[^66] This religious veneration spread beyond Korea—he was worshipped as a god in China, Korea, Japan, and Vietnam, suggesting that his maritime achievements and protection of merchants resonated across East Asian maritime communities.[^67]

### Modern Korean Reception and National Identity

There has been a remarkable renewal of interest in Jang Bogo in modern Korea, where he has been embraced as a national historical hero representing Korean maritime prowess and entrepreneurial spirit.[^68] This modern reception transforms him from a controversial figure who challenged social hierarchies into a symbol of Korean achievement during the golden era when Korea dominated the trade networks of Northeast Asia.[^69] In modern Korea, he is called the "sea king" (해왕), a nickname given in modern times that, while anachronistic, accurately reflects his historical influence on the East Asian seas.[^70]

### Military and Scientific Honors

The South Korean government and military have honored Jang's legacy in multiple concrete ways. The Republic of Korea Navy named its first Type 209 submarine "Admiral Chang Bogo" in his honor, recognizing his pioneering role in Korean naval history.[^71] In March 2009, when the ROK Navy formed the Cheonghae Anti-piracy Unit to combat piracy off the coast of Somalia, it was named after Cheonghaejin, the maritime base Jang created over a millennium earlier.[^72] This choice deliberately connected modern Korean naval operations against Somali pirates with Jang's historical campaign against Chinese pirates, creating a narrative of continuity in Korean maritime security.

Perhaps most remarkably, in 2014, South Korea's Antarctic research station was named the Jang Bogo Station, honoring him as an explorer and extending his symbolic reach to the southernmost regions of the globe.[^73] Additionally, a memorial dedicated to Chang Bogo opened in Shenyang, China in 2007, recognizing his historical significance beyond Korean borders.[^74]

### Cultural Impact and Popular Media

The 2004 KBS television drama "Emperor of the Sea" (해신), based on Choi In-ho's 2003 novel and starring Choi Soo-jong as Jang Bogo, brought his story to contemporary audiences on an unprecedented scale.[^75] The series depicted Jang's rise from slavery to his position as a powerful maritime figure and became highly successful, cultivating a dedicated following evidenced by sustained popularity of its original soundtrack and periodic streaming revivals.[^76] The drama explored the motif of maritime identity, with the sea symbolizing both liberation and hazard, and Jang Bogo's dominance over sea routes embodying national resilience and tying individual triumphs to broader cultural narratives of exploration and survival.[^77]

Beyond television, museums dedicated to Jang's memory have been established, most notably near the archaeological sites at Wando where Cheonghaejin once stood, allowing visitors to connect with both the historical figure and the physical evidence of his achievements.[^78]

### Archaeological Preservation

The extensive archaeological investigations at Jangdo Island, which have uncovered over 30,000 artifacts and revealed sophisticated infrastructure from Jang's era, ensure that his legacy rests not merely on historical texts but on tangible physical evidence.[^79] The designation of the site as a Historic Site of Cheonghaejin Fort provides protection for this cultural heritage and enables ongoing research that continues to deepen understanding of 9th-century Korean maritime capabilities.[^80]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Modern historians and scholars have increasingly recognized Jang Bogo as a transformative figure whose achievements transcended the limitations of his era. The World History Encyclopedia describes him as a "powerful Korean warlord, naval commander, and merchant who came to monopolise maritime trade in northeast Asia to such a degree that he was known as the 'King of the Yellow Sea' during the first half of the 9th century CE."[^81] This assessment emphasizes both his military prowess and commercial acumen, presenting him as a multifaceted leader whose influence extended across multiple domains.

Scholars particularly praise Jang's humanitarian motivation in combating the slave trade. His decision to return to Korea specifically to address the enslavement of his countrymen demonstrates a sense of national consciousness and collective responsibility that was relatively uncommon in the highly stratified society of Later Silla.[^82] As one historical analysis notes, his petition to King Heungdeok—"Everywhere in Tang they use our people as slaves. I beg Your Majesty to build headquarters at Chonghae to prevent pirates from kidnapping our people and shipping them west"—reveals a leader motivated by something beyond personal gain.[^83]

Modern Korean historiography has embraced Jang as a symbol of Korean maritime achievement and entrepreneurial excellence. The establishment of museums, military vessels, and even an Antarctic research station in his name reflects scholarly consensus that his achievements deserve commemoration and study.[^84] Korean historians emphasize that Jang's career demonstrates that the Later Silla period, often characterized primarily by aristocratic decadence and political decline, also produced figures of exceptional capability who could compete successfully in the international arena of East Asian commerce and politics.

The archaeological evidence from Cheonghaejin has strengthened positive scholarly assessments by providing physical confirmation of the sophistication of Jang's maritime infrastructure. The National Research Institute of Cultural Heritage's discoveries of unique defensive structures, sophisticated well-construction techniques, and advanced drainage systems demonstrate that Jang commanded not merely military forces but also significant engineering expertise.[^85] These findings support interpretations of Jang as a visionary leader who understood the importance of infrastructure in sustaining maritime dominance.

Internationally, scholars recognize Jang's role in facilitating cultural exchange, particularly in the transmission of Buddhism across East Asia. The Japanese monk Ennin's "Account of a Pilgrimage to Tang in Search of the Law" provides contemporary evidence that Jang actively supported Buddhist activities and provided safe transportation for religious figures, contributing to the spread of Buddhist teachings and practices.[^86] This cultural patronage demonstrates that Jang understood power in sophisticated terms that included religious and cultural dimensions alongside military and economic factors.

### Negative Views

Despite widespread scholarly praise, some historians and critics have questioned aspects of Jang Bogo's legacy and methods. The most persistent criticism concerns his political ambitions and willingness to challenge established social hierarchies. From this perspective, his attempt to marry his daughter into the royal family represented not admirable social mobility but dangerous overreach that threatened Silla's political stability.[^87] Critics argue that Jang's accumulation of military power (10,000 troops) and economic dominance created a regional power base that functioned almost independently of central government control, potentially undermining state authority.

The aristocratic opposition that ultimately killed Jang believed they were defending legitimate social and political order against a commoner whose wealth and power had made him dangerously ambitious.[^88] While modern egalitarian sensibilities tend to sympathize with Jang's challenge to rigid class barriers, critics point out that in the context of 9th-century Silla, social hierarchies were not merely prejudices but fundamental organizing principles of the state. Jang's rise, from this perspective, represented a destabilizing force during a period when Silla already faced multiple challenges.

Some scholars have questioned whether Jang's commercial monopoly truly benefited Silla as a whole or primarily enriched Jang himself and his associates. While he eliminated piracy and protected Korean merchants, he also concentrated enormous economic power in his own hands, potentially limiting opportunities for other Korean merchants and creating dependencies that served his interests.[^89] The fact that maritime trade networks declined following the dissolution of Cheonghaejin in 851 could be interpreted as evidence that Jang had created an unsustainable system overly dependent on his personal authority rather than building institutional structures that could survive his death.

Critics also note the fragmentary and sometimes contradictory nature of the historical sources for Jang's life. The Samguk Sagi, compiled three centuries after his death, "confusingly mixes legend and fact," making it difficult to distinguish Jang's actual achievements from later embellishments.[^90] The discrepancy between Japanese sources (which date his death to 841) and Korean sources (which record 846) raises questions about the reliability of all the details in his biography.[^91] From this critical perspective, the modern lionization of Jang may rest partly on romanticized accounts that exaggerate both his achievements and the opposition he faced.

Finally, some historians question Jang's conspiracy against King Munseong following the rejection of his marriage proposal. While the historical record is unclear about the precise nature of this conspiracy, the fact that it occurred at all suggests that Jang was willing to use his military power to threaten legitimate royal authority when his personal ambitions were thwarted.[^92] This potential willingness to foment rebellion raises questions about whether Jang should be celebrated as a patriotic hero or viewed more critically as an ambitious warlord whose loyalty to the state was conditional on personal advancement.

## Famous Quotes

> "Everywhere in Tang they use our people as slaves. I beg Your Majesty to build headquarters at Chonghae to prevent pirates from kidnapping our people and shipping them west." - Jang Bogo's petition to King Heungdeok, c. 827 CE[^93]

This petition demonstrates Jang's primary humanitarian motivation for establishing Cheonghaejin and reveals his sense of national identity and concern for his countrymen suffering in Tang China.

> "No one else apart from you can settle this distress." - Jang Bogo to Jeong Yeon when dispatching him with 5,000 troops to support King Sinmu's claim, 839 CE[^94]

This statement to his closest companion reveals Jang's deep personal trust and reliance on loyalty in navigating Silla court politics during a succession crisis.

> "Silla people were being captured by pirates in Tang and being sold as slaves." - Jang Bogo's report to King Heungdeok explaining conditions that motivated his return to Korea[^95]

This concise summary of the situation facing Korean expatriates in China captures the humanitarian crisis that Jang dedicated his career to addressing.
