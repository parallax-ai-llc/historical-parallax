## Summary

Ukrainian serial killer and former police forensics expert who murdered at least 37 girls and women between 1980 and 2005. His police background helped him evade detection for 25 years. Sentenced to life imprisonment.

## Methods and evasion (high-level)

- Tkach's professional experience in **police forensics** and investigative procedures was frequently cited as a factor that helped him avoid detection.
- Reports describe him as targeting **girls and young women**, exploiting situations where victims were isolated or lacked immediate protection.
- The long time span attributed to his crimes reflects both mobility across regions and systemic investigative gaps.

## Investigation and wrongful convictions

- Public accounts of the case note that, during the years before his arrest, multiple investigations failed to identify a single perpetrator.
- In some narratives, the prolonged failure to stop the killings was linked to **institutional dysfunction** and pressure on police to produce results.
- The case is often discussed in the context of **wrongful convictions** and investigative errors that can occur when serial crimes generate public fear.

## Arrest, evidence, and trial

- Tkach was arrested in 2005, with reporting emphasizing the role of **forensic/DNA evidence** in connecting him to earlier crimes.
- Following arrest, he reportedly provided extensive confessions; the number of claimed victims varied across accounts.
- He received a **life sentence** under Ukrainian law.

## Impact and legacy

- The case remains notable in Ukraine for the scale of violence attributed to a single offender and for the way it intersected with policing practices.
- It is frequently referenced as an example of how specialized knowledge can be abused and how modern forensic methods can later resolve long-running cases.
