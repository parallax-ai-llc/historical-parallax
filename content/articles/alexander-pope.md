## Summary

Alexander Pope (1688-1744) was an English poet, translator, and satirist of the Enlightenment era, widely regarded as one of the greatest poets of the early eighteenth century and the master of the heroic couplet.[^1] Born into a Catholic family during an era of severe religious persecution, Pope overcame debilitating physical disabilities and educational barriers to become the first full-time professional English writer, achieving financial independence through his celebrated translations of Homer's *Iliad* and *Odyssey*.[^2] His major works, including *An Essay on Criticism*, *The Rape of the Lock*, *The Dunciad*, and *An Essay on Man*, established him as the dominant literary figure of the Augustan Age.[^3] Despite his literary genius, Pope was notorious for his vitriolic feuds with critics and writers, making more enemies than perhaps any other writer of his time.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Alexander Pope was born in London on May 21, 1688, during the tumultuous year of the Glorious Revolution.[^5] His father, also named Alexander Pope, was a prosperous linen merchant in the Strand who had accumulated considerable wealth in business.[^6] His mother, Edith Turner, was the daughter of William Turner, Esquire, of York.[^7] The family's Catholic faith would profoundly shape young Alexander's life, subjecting them to the harsh restrictions of the Test Acts enacted after the abdication of the Catholic King James II.[^8]

These discriminatory laws barred Catholics from teaching, attending university, voting, holding public office, and even living within ten miles of London on penalty of perpetual imprisonment.[^9] Consequently, in 1700, when Alexander was twelve years old, the Pope family relocated to a small estate at Popeswood in Binfield, Berkshire, near Windsor Forest, to comply with these restrictions.[^10] The poet later recalled this landscape fondly, immortalizing it in his early pastoral poetry and the topographical poem *Windsor-Forest*.[^11]

Pope's education was severely restricted by anti-Catholic legislation. Excluded from public schools and universities, he received only sporadic instruction from a few Catholic schools of dubious quality before becoming largely self-educated.[^12] His aunt taught him to read, and the precocious child became an voracious reader, teaching himself French, Italian, Latin, and Greek.[^13] According to his own account, Pope discovered Homer at the tender age of six, beginning a lifelong devotion to classical literature.[^14] By age twelve, he had already composed his *Ode on Solitude*, demonstrating remarkable poetic facility.[^15]

At the same age, however, catastrophe struck. Pope contracted Pott's disease, a form of tuberculosis affecting the spine, which stunted his growth and severely deformed his body.[^16] He never grew taller than four feet six inches and developed a pronounced hunchback that required him to wear a stiff canvas brace throughout his life.[^17] He suffered constant headaches, respiratory problems, high fevers, inflamed eyes, and abdominal pain.[^18] As a child, he had once survived being trampled by a cow, but the tuberculosis proved a lifelong affliction requiring daily care and assistance.[^19] These twin disadvantages—his Catholicism and his physical infirmity—isolated Pope from mainstream English society, though his indomitable spirit would overcome these formidable obstacles to achieve literary immortality.[^20]

## Rise to Power

Pope's meteoric rise to literary prominence began in May 1709, when his *Pastorals* appeared in the sixth part of bookseller Jacob Tonson's *Poetical Miscellanies*.[^21] The work earned Pope instant fame and established his reputation as a poet of exceptional promise.[^22] Building on this early success, Pope published *An Essay on Criticism* in May 1711 at the age of just twenty-three.[^23] This brilliant didactic poem articulated many of the central tenets of eighteenth-century aesthetic and moral philosophy in memorable, aphoristic couplets that would become frequently quoted in English.[^24]

The appearance of *The Rape of the Lock* in 1712, expanded in 1714, catapulted Pope to wider fame.[^25] This mock-epic masterpiece satirized elite society by derisively recounting a high-society quarrel between Arabella Fermor (the poem's "Belinda") and Lord Petre, who had audaciously snipped a lock of hair from her head without permission.[^26] The poem showcased Pope's extraordinary command of the heroic couplet and his gift for combining humor, beauty, and social commentary in perfectly balanced verse.[^27]

Around 1711, Pope forged crucial friendships with prominent Tory writers Jonathan Swift, Thomas Parnell, and John Arbuthnot, who together formed the satirical Scriblerus Club.[^28] Founded in 1714, the club aimed to satirize ignorance and pedantry through the fictional scholar Martinus Scriblerus.[^29] The friendship between Pope and Swift, which lasted until Pope's death in 1744, became one of the most significant literary friendships of the eighteenth century.[^30] These collaborative relationships profoundly influenced Pope's work and provided creative ideas that members could use individually or collectively.[^31]

However, Pope's greatest financial triumph came with his translations of Homer. Published between 1715 and 1720, Pope's *Iliad* was a remarkable experiment in subscription publishing.[^32] Though many friends doubted the wisdom of attempting to sell the work at the unprecedented price of six guineas for six quarto volumes, the venture succeeded spectacularly.[^33] Pope received £1,290 in royalties and £4,837 from the sale of 750 copies of each volume, plus 200 guineas per volume from his publisher Lintot—half in advance and half on manuscript delivery.[^34] The translation lifted him "at one bound to affluence and power," providing financial independence unprecedented for a writer of his time.[^35]

Building on this success, Pope's *Odyssey* translation (1725-1726) earned him approximately £5,000, equivalent to roughly £940,500 in 2023.[^36] These translations were celebrated throughout his lifetime and beyond, with classicist Emily Wilson noting they "dominated the market" and shaped how future translators interpreted Homer.[^37] Pope thus became the first full-time professional English writer, supporting himself entirely through his literary work rather than relying on aristocratic patronage—a remarkable achievement for a Catholic barred from traditional avenues of advancement.[^38]

## Major Achievements

Alexander Pope's literary achievements fundamentally shaped eighteenth-century English literature, and the entire Augustan age's poetry was dominated by his towering presence.[^39] He is acknowledged as having perfected the heroic couplet—a verse form consisting of rhyming pairs of iambic pentameter lines—transforming it from the foundation laid by his idol John Dryden into a supremely flexible instrument capable of satire, philosophy, narrative, and lyric beauty.[^40] No other poet in the history of English literature has handled the heroic couplet with comparable flexibility and brilliance.[^41]

Pope's major poetic works established enduring standards for their respective genres. *An Essay on Criticism* (1711), composed when Pope was barely twenty-three, became one of the most quoted poems in the English language.[^42] Its brilliantly aphoristic lines articulated the neoclassical principles that defined Augustan aesthetics, providing memorable maxims like "To err is human; to forgive, divine" and "Fools rush in where angels fear to tread."[^43] The poem attacked contemporary literary critics while establishing Pope as a formidable critical voice.[^44]

*The Rape of the Lock* (1712, expanded 1714) represents the pinnacle of the mock-epic genre.[^45] This exquisite poem elevated a trivial society dispute into an epic parody complete with supernatural machinery, heroic language applied to petty concerns, and devastating social satire.[^46] Using ostentatious rhymes, heavy end-stopping, and inverted sentence structures, Pope's couplets created humorous parallels and contrasts between high and low subjects, often reserving a witty rhyme for each couplet's close.[^47] The work established the mock-epic as a major poetic form and remains widely studied today.[^48]

*The Dunciad* (1728, revised through 1743) stands as Pope's most savage satirical achievement.[^49] Initially attacking the Shakespearean editor Lewis Theobald, who had dared to criticize Pope's edition of Shakespeare in his 1726 work *Shakespeare Restored*, the poem expanded into a comprehensive assault on what Pope termed "duncery"—everything tasteless, dull, and degraded in contemporary culture and literature.[^50] Pope mocked specific contemporary literary figures while making larger arguments about the decline of art and culture.[^51] He continued revising this masterpiece throughout his life, with Book Four appearing in 1742 and a full revision in 1743.[^52]

*An Essay on Man* (1733-1734) represents Pope's most ambitious philosophical work.[^53] This philosophical poem attempted to "vindicate the ways of God to man" through a theodicy heavily influenced by the optimistic rationalism of Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, whose *Theodicy* (1710) proposed that we inhabit "the best of all possible worlds" despite evident suffering.[^54] Pope's famous assertion that "Whatever is, is right" encapsulated his optimistic philosophy, arguing that apparent evils serve necessary roles in cosmic harmony beyond human comprehension.[^55] The work articulated the Great Chain of Being concept, asserting that authentic good belongs to the universal whole rather than isolated parts.[^56] More than any other work, *An Essay on Man* popularized optimistic philosophy throughout England and Europe.[^57]

Beyond original composition, Pope's scholarly and editorial work proved influential, if controversial. His edition of Shakespeare's works, published in six volumes between 1723-1725, represented the second major eighteenth-century edition following Nicholas Rowe's 1709 version.[^58] Pope became the first editor to systematically consult all versions of Shakespeare's plays published during Shakespeare's lifetime to establish authoritative texts.[^59] However, he freely imposed eighteenth-century standards and his own judgment, "degrading" passages he deemed inferior by printing them at the bottom of pages in small type marked with asterisks, and regularizing Shakespeare's verse meter.[^60] Lewis Theobald's devastating scholarly critique in *Shakespeare Restored* (1726) exposed numerous errors in Pope's edition, provoking Pope to make Theobald the villain-hero of *The Dunciad*.[^61]

Pope also achieved fame as a landscape designer. His villa and gardens at Twickenham, where he moved in 1719, became one of the most important landscapes in European garden design history.[^62] He created one of the first picturesque gardens on about five acres, featuring a theatre, arcade, bowling green, grove, shell temple, spiral mound, vineyard, and obelisk dedicated to his mother.[^63] His famous grotto, constructed after 1720 as an underground tunnel connecting his house to his garden, featured remarkable effects including functioning as a camera obscura displaying river scenes, with shells and mirrors creating reflective light displays.[^64] Pope's garden and views of the Thames valley at Richmond inspired the English landscaping style later developed by Capability Brown.[^65] As a pioneer of the English landscape movement, he wrote in 1713 advocating for the "amiable Simplicity of unadorned Nature" against formal continental garden styles.[^66]

## Controversies and Criticisms

The New Catholic Encyclopedia observed that "Probably no writer ever made more enemies than Pope."[^67] As a satirist who wielded his pen like a rapier, Pope accumulated bitter enemies among critics, politicians, and prominent cultural figures who felt the sting of his sharp-witted attacks.[^68] These feuds, rooted in rivalries over patronage and literary standards, invited personal attacks on Pope's hunchback and Catholicism, though his verses prioritized exposing what he saw as intellectual corruption over mere personal revenge.[^69]

Among Pope's most vicious feuds was his conflict with the critic John Dennis, who hated Pope for the rest of his life after Pope satirized him.[^70] Save for a temporary reconciliation, Dennis dedicated his efforts to insulting Pope in print, to which Pope retaliated in kind, making Dennis the butt of much satire.[^71] Similarly, Pope waged war against the bookseller Edmund Curll, the historian John Oldmixon, and numerous other writers of lesser fame who became objects of his satirical attacks.[^72]

Pope's quarrel with Joseph Addison, though subtler, proved equally bitter. When Pope heard gossip of malice on Addison's part, he composed a satiric portrait of Addison's character, eventually published as the character of "Atticus," the insincere arbiter of literary taste, in *An Epistle to Dr. Arbuthnot* (1735).[^73] The portrait devastatingly captured what Pope perceived as Addison's insincerity and manipulation of the literary marketplace.

Perhaps Pope's most personally damaging feud involved Lady Mary Wortley Montagu, an aristocratic intellectual and writer. The two met in April 1715 in the London studio of their mutual friend, artist Charles Jervas.[^74] What began as friendship developed into lengthy correspondence when Montagu traveled to Turkey with her ambassador husband in 1716.[^75] Pope was clearly smitten, though Lady Mary's letters reveal she did not reciprocate his romantic feelings.[^76]

Their decades-long friendship ended abruptly around 1722, with no definitive explanation in the historical record.[^77] The most famous account, though disputed by scholars, suggests Pope made an unexpected passionate declaration of love that so surprised Lady Mary that she burst into laughter in his face.[^78] In 1728, Pope attacked Lady Mary in *The Dunciad*, inaugurating a decade in which most of his publications made allegations against her.[^79] He portrayed her as the dirty "Sappho" in his satiric works until she was enraged by her characterization in his *First Satire of the Second Book of Horace Imitated* (1733).[^80] Lady Mary, joining forces with John, Lord Hervey (whom Pope had savagely satirized as "Sporus," "Lord Fanny," "Adonis," and "Narcissus"), helped produce *Verses Address'd to the Imitator of the First Satire of the Second Book of Horace* (1733), which many critics consider the best satire of Pope written at that time.[^81] For both Pope and Montagu, this relationship became one of the most powerful and ultimately most damaging of their lives.[^82]

Pope's edition of Shakespeare drew significant scholarly criticism. Lewis Theobald's *Shakespeare Restored* (1726) devastatingly exposed the "many Errors" in Pope's insufficiently scholarly edition.[^83] Most later eighteenth-century editors dismissed Pope's creatively motivated approach to textual criticism, though his preface to the edition continued to be highly rated.[^84] Pope's practice of freely emending Shakespeare's text according to his own aesthetic judgment, regularizing meter, and relegating passages he deemed inferior to footnotes struck later scholars as presumptuous.[^85]

Even Pope's greatest philosophical work attracted criticism. Voltaire, initially an admirer of *An Essay on Man*, later renounced his admiration for Pope's and Leibniz's optimism, writing the novel *Candide* as a satire on their ethical philosophy.[^86] Critics questioned whether Pope's assertion that "Whatever is, is right" represented sophisticated theodicy or dangerous fatalism that excused injustice.[^87]

## Personal Life

Alexander Pope never married, though he maintained a lifelong friendship with Martha Blount, a fellow Catholic.[^88] The exact nature of their relationship remains debated by scholars. Some sources suggest Martha was allegedly Pope's lover, though Pope had to quiet rumors about their friendship that dishonored Martha's reputation, suggesting they were merely friends.[^89] Their correspondence reveals genuine affection, shared confidences, and mutual comfort amid Pope's constant illness.[^90] Martha was a constant presence at Pope's Twickenham villa, attending to him with compassion and intelligence.[^91]

Pope's health remained precarious throughout his adult life. Standing just four feet six inches tall, perpetually hunchbacked, and plagued by tuberculosis of the spine since age twelve, Pope endured constant physical suffering.[^92] The Pott's disease that stunted his growth and deformed his body also caused respiratory problems, high fevers, inflamed eyes, abdominal pain, and unrelenting headaches.[^93] His physical frailty required daily assistance with basic tasks throughout his adult life.[^94]

Pope's irascible nature and unpopularity in the press are often attributed to three interconnected factors: his membership in a persecuted religious minority, his physical infirmity, and his exclusion from formal education.[^95] Already removed from mainstream society as a Catholic, his poor health further isolated him.[^96] Yet despite these enormous disadvantages, Pope's poetic career testified to an indomitable spirit that refused to accept the limitations imposed by circumstance.[^97]

At his Twickenham villa, where he lived from 1719 until his death, Pope created a refuge and artistic sanctuary.[^98] The famous grotto he constructed became his pride, featuring ingenious optical effects, shells collected from various sources, and mirrors creating enchanting reflections.[^99] In 1739, after visiting a spa at the Avon Gorge, Pope decided to transform the grotto to resemble a mine, continuing to refine his creation until late in life.[^100] In 1732, he employed the prominent architect William Kent to extend the portico for the villa's riverfront.[^101]

Pope's friendships sustained him intellectually and emotionally. Beyond the Scriblerus Club members—Swift, Arbuthnot, Gay, and Parnell—Pope maintained relationships with influential cultural figures including Lord Burlington and William Kent.[^102] The Scriblerians generously shared creative ideas among themselves, developing an intellectual and creative sociability that profoundly influenced all their individual works.[^103] Gay's *The Beggar's Opera* grew from a Swift suggestion to the Scriblerus Club, while the imprint of Scriblerus on Swift's *Gulliver's Travels*, especially Book III's voyage to Laputa, is unmistakable.[^104]

Pope died at his Twickenham villa surrounded by friends on May 30, 1744, about eleven o'clock at night, of dropsy (edema) and acute asthma.[^105] He was fifty-six years old. He was buried in the nave of St. Mary's Church, Twickenham.[^106]

## Legacy

Alexander Pope's legacy as one of England's greatest poets remains secure, though his reputation has fluctuated significantly across different eras. The first half of the British eighteenth century is often referred to simply as "the age of Pope," reflecting his absolute dominance of Augustan poetry.[^107] As the acknowledged master of the heroic couplet and one of the primary tastemakers of the Augustan age, Pope established standards that defined neoclassical poetry.[^108]

Pope's lines were repeated often enough to contribute numerous clichés and proverbs to modern English usage.[^109] Phrases from *An Essay on Criticism* alone—"To err is human; to forgive, divine" and "Fools rush in where angels fear to tread"—have become so embedded in English that many who quote them remain unaware of their origin.[^110] His influence on poetic form and technique proved equally enduring. Pope is generally recognized as the greatest English poet between John Milton and William Wordsworth, occupying a unique position bridging the metaphysical and Romantic eras.[^111]

As one of the leading literary figures of his day, Pope's neoclassical influences spread widely, establishing the heroic couplet as the dominant verse form for serious poetry throughout the mid-eighteenth century.[^112] His mock epic *The Rape of the Lock* derides elite society with a wit and formal perfection that established the standard for the genre, while *An Essay on Criticism* and *An Essay on Man* articulated the central tenets of eighteenth-century aesthetic and moral philosophy that shaped literary discourse for generations.[^113]

However, Pope fell out of favor during the Romantic era as the neoclassical appetite for formal perfection was replaced by Romantic values of sincerity, spontaneity, and emotional authenticity.[^114] William Wordsworth, in particular, found Pope's style too decadent and artificial to represent genuine human experience.[^115] Pope's satires and poetry of manners did not fit Romantic and Victorian visions of poetry as a product of sincere emotion, leading critics to view him as a philosopher and rhetorician rather than a true poet—a dismissive assessment that persisted through the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.[^116]

The rise of modernism in the early twentieth century revived serious interest in pre-Romantic poetry.[^117] Pope's sophisticated use of poetic form, his mastery of irony and tone, and his witty manipulation of language made him of particular interest to the New Critics, who valued formal complexity and literary craftsmanship.[^118] In the latter half of the twentieth and beginning of the twenty-first centuries, Pope remained central to the study of what scholars term the long eighteenth century.[^119]

Modern scholars evaluate Pope as a major literary voice engaged with both high and low cultural scenes, a key figure in the sphere of letters, and an articulate witness to the rise of commercial printing and the development of modern English national identity.[^120] Recent criticism has interpreted Pope's work through the lenses of gender and authorial identity, analyzing female readings of his writings, his career choices emphasizing honesty and integrity connected to masculinity, and his literary attacks as responses to criticism.[^121] Pope stands with John Dryden and Samuel Johnson as one of the chief arbiters of his age.[^122]

Pope's landscape design legacy also endures. Though Baroness Howe purchased and demolished his villa in 1808, Pope's Grotto survives and is listed Grade II* by Historic England, opening to the public on thirty weekends annually.[^123] His garden design philosophy, advocating for naturalistic landscaping over formal continental styles, profoundly influenced the development of the English landscape garden movement.[^124]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Contemporary admirers and later scholars have consistently praised Pope's unparalleled mastery of poetic form and his ability to combine technical virtuosity with substantive content. His contemporaries recognized him immediately as a poetic genius, with his *Pastorals* earning instant fame and his subsequent works confirming his status as the dominant poet of his era.[^125] The translations of Homer's *Iliad* and *Odyssey* were celebrated during his lifetime and beyond, making him financially independent and establishing his reputation as a translator who could make ancient classics accessible to English readers while maintaining poetic beauty.[^126]

Modern scholars recognize Pope as a central figure in the development of professional authorship. As the first full-time professional English writer who supported himself entirely through literary work rather than relying on aristocratic patronage, Pope pioneered a new model of literary career that would become increasingly important in subsequent centuries.[^127] His success in subscription publishing for the Homer translations demonstrated that writers could achieve financial independence through direct appeal to readers, fundamentally altering the economics of literary production.[^128]

Recent criticism has increasingly appreciated Pope's sophisticated engagement with gender, identity, and the politics of authorship. Scholars analyze his career choices as emphasizing honesty and integrity connected to masculine ideals of independence, while his literary attacks represented not merely personal vindictiveness but principled responses to what he perceived as corruption in literary culture.[^129] His manipulation of the heroic couplet created a uniquely flexible instrument capable of expressing complex thoughts with epigrammatic concision, philosophical depth with satirical edge, and lyric beauty with intellectual rigor.[^130]

Pope's influence on garden design and landscape aesthetics receives growing scholarly attention. As a pioneer of the English landscape movement, his advocacy for naturalistic design over formal continental styles anticipated and influenced the work of later landscape designers like Capability Brown.[^131] His Twickenham garden represented not merely aesthetic preference but a philosophical commitment to "amiable Simplicity of unadorned Nature" that reflected neoclassical values of proportion and harmony.[^132]

The formal perfection of Pope's verse continues to command admiration. His ability to craft perfectly balanced couplets that function simultaneously as independent epigrams and as components of larger argumentative or narrative structures demonstrates technical mastery rarely equaled in English poetry.[^133] His use of ostentatious rhymes, heavy end-stopping, and inverted sentence structures created humorous parallels and contrasts between high and low subjects while maintaining strict formal discipline.[^134]

### Negative Views

Despite his technical brilliance, Pope has faced persistent criticism across multiple dimensions. The Romantic poets and their successors condemned what they perceived as Pope's excessive formalism and artificiality. William Wordsworth influentially dismissed Pope's style as too decadent to represent authentic human experience, inaugurating a century of criticism that denied Pope the title of true poet, relegating him instead to the categories of philosopher, rhetorician, or versifier.[^135] This Romantic critique argued that poetry should arise from sincere emotion and spontaneous expression rather than calculated wit and formal perfection.[^136]

Many critics, following nineteenth-century predecessors, dismissed *An Essay on Criticism* as a mere collection of well-worn epigrams and dated restatements of literary commonplaces rather than original philosophical insight.[^137] This criticism suggested that Pope's epigrammatic brilliance masked derivative thinking, rephrasing conventional wisdom in memorable couplets without genuine innovation.[^138]

Pope's editorial work on Shakespeare drew harsh scholarly criticism. Lewis Theobald's devastating *Shakespeare Restored* (1726) exposed numerous errors in Pope's edition, revealing that Pope had freely imposed eighteenth-century norms and his own aesthetic judgment on Shakespeare's text.[^139] His practice of "degrading" passages he deemed inferior, regularizing Shakespeare's meter, and emending texts according to personal taste struck later scholars as presumptuous and unscholarly.[^140] Most eighteenth-century editors who followed dismissed Pope's creatively motivated approach to textual criticism.[^141]

Pope's vicious personal feuds and savage satirical attacks have troubled critics who find his vindictiveness excessive and his attacks cruel. His portraits of enemies like John Dennis, Lewis Theobald, Lord Hervey, and Lady Mary Wortley Montagu combined literary criticism with personal mockery of physical appearance, social status, and private behavior in ways that strike modern readers as bullying.[^142] While Pope's defenders argue these attacks targeted genuine intellectual corruption, critics contend they reveal an irascible, insecure personality lashing out at perceived slights with disproportionate venom.[^143]

The philosophical content of *An Essay on Man* attracted significant criticism. Voltaire's renunciation of his earlier admiration for Pope's optimism, culminating in *Candide*'s satirical demolition of Leibnizian theodicy, questioned whether Pope's assertion that "Whatever is, is right" represented sophisticated philosophy or dangerous fatalism.[^144] Critics argued that Pope's optimistic system excused injustice and suffering by appealing to cosmic harmony beyond human comprehension, effectively counseling passive acceptance of evil.[^145]

Pope's lack of formal education occasionally showed in his work. Critics noted instances where his classical learning, acquired through self-education rather than university training, proved incomplete or superficial.[^146] His reliance on collaborators for the *Odyssey* translation raised questions about the extent of his genuine scholarly expertise.[^147]

## Famous Quotes

> "To err is human; to forgive, divine." - From *An Essay on Criticism* (1711), line 525.[^173] This maxim has become one of the most frequently quoted lines in English, encapsulating the neoclassical emphasis on human fallibility balanced by divine perfection and the moral imperative of forgiveness.

> "Fools rush in where angels fear to tread." - From *An Essay on Criticism* (1711), line 625.[^174] This aphorism warns against presumptuous confidence in matters requiring careful judgment and has entered the popular lexicon as a caution against reckless action in situations demanding wisdom.

> "Whatever is, is right." - From *An Essay on Man* (1733-34).[^175] This controversial assertion encapsulates Pope's optimistic theodicy, arguing that apparent evils serve necessary roles in cosmic harmony designed by divine wisdom, though critics like Voltaire attacked this philosophy as dangerous fatalism.

> "The proper study of mankind is man." - From *An Essay on Man*, Epistle II.[^176] This famous line argues that human beings should focus their intellectual energies on understanding human nature and society rather than attempting to comprehend divine mysteries beyond human capacity.

> "A little learning is a dangerous thing; / Drink deep, or taste not the Pierian spring." - From *An Essay on Criticism*.[^177] Pope warns that superficial knowledge often proves more harmful than complete ignorance, advocating for thorough mastery rather than cursory acquaintance with subjects, particularly in criticism and scholarship.
