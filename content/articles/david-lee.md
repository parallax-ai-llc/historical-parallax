## Early Life and Education

- Born in Rye, New York, Lee pursued physics during a period when low-temperature research was rapidly expanding.
- He studied physics at university and later completed doctoral training that prepared him for experimental work at cryogenic temperatures.

## Research Focus

Lee is best known for experimental condensed-matter physics, especially:

- **Quantum fluids and low-temperature phenomena**
- **Cryogenic experimental techniques** needed to reach millikelvin temperatures
- **Helium-3** as a model system for studying quantum mechanics at macroscopic scales

## Discovery of Superfluid Helium-3

Helium-3 is a rare isotope of helium that behaves very differently from helium-4 at extremely low temperatures. In the early 1970s, Lee and collaborators observed a phase transition indicating that helium-3 can enter a **superfluid** state—flowing with effectively zero viscosity and exhibiting distinct quantum phases.

Key points often emphasized in high-level accounts:

- The superfluidity in helium-3 arises from **pairing of fermionic helium-3 atoms**, analogous in concept to electron pairing in superconductivity.
- The discovery opened a major research program in identifying and characterizing multiple superfluid phases and their symmetry properties.

## Nobel Prize (1996)

Lee shared the **1996 Nobel Prize in Physics** with Robert C. Richardson and Douglas D. Osheroff. The award recognized their discovery of superfluidity in helium-3 and its significance for understanding quantum matter.

## Impact and Legacy

- The helium-3 results became a cornerstone for later work on **quantum phase transitions**, symmetry breaking, and emergent behavior.
- The experimental methods developed for the helium-3 work helped shape standards for precision measurement and ultralow-temperature apparatus in physics laboratories.
