## Summary

British gangster who led the Richardson Gang in South London, rival to the Krays. Known for 'torture trials' using electrodes and pliers. His gang's brutality was exposed in the 'Torture Trial' of 1967. Sentenced to 25 years.

## Background

Richardson was a prominent figure in postwar London organized crime, associated with a network of associates sometimes referred to as the Richardson Gang. He operated primarily in South London and became known as a rival to the Kray twins, another notorious criminal group in the same era.

## Criminal activity and reputation

Contemporary reporting and later accounts describe the Richardson group as engaging in armed robbery, extortion, and enforcement work. The phrase “torture trial” is commonly used for the 1967 proceedings that brought the group’s violence into public view; descriptions of methods used by the gang can vary by source and are often presented in sensational terms.

## 1967 trial and sentencing

In 1967, Richardson and associates were prosecuted in a high-profile case that resulted in significant prison sentences. Richardson received a lengthy term, and the trial became a reference point in histories of British organized crime because of the breadth of evidence and the public attention it attracted.

## Later life and legacy

After serving time, Richardson remained a figure of public fascination in crime histories and media portrayals of 1960s London. Discussions of his life are often framed alongside the Krays as part of a broader narrative about postwar social change, policing, and the mythology of “gangland” London.

## Public perception and media

Accounts of Richardson’s group appear in books, documentaries, and dramatizations about London’s criminal underworld. These portrayals often blend verified court records with anecdote, so details about hierarchy, specific incidents, and rivalries may differ between sources.
