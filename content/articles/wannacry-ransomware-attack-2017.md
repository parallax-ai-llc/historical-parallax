## Technical Details (High-Level)

WannaCry combined a self-propagating network worm with ransomware. It spread primarily by exploiting a vulnerability in Microsoft Windows' SMBv1 protocol (addressed by Microsoft in the MS17-010 security update released in March 2017). In practice, unpatched systems on internal networks enabled rapid lateral movement, which is why hospitals, telecoms, and large enterprises saw widespread disruption.

## Response and Attribution

- **Kill switch:** Early in the outbreak, a researcher registered a hard-coded domain used by the malware, which reduced the spread of one major variant by activating a built-in "kill switch" behavior.
- **Patching and mitigation:** Organizations accelerated patch deployment, disabled SMBv1 where possible, and improved backup practices after learning that perimeter defenses did not prevent worm-like propagation.
- **Attribution:** Public reporting and government statements in multiple countries later attributed the operation to the Lazarus Group linked to North Korea; attribution remains a matter of intelligence assessment rather than courtroom proof.

## Lessons and Legacy

WannaCry is frequently cited as a turning point demonstrating that:
- vulnerability disclosure and patching speed are critical for systemic resilience,
- legacy protocols (like SMBv1) can create outsized risk when widely deployed,
- ransomware impacts are often operational (service outages) as much as financial.
