## Summary

White mob destroyed the predominantly Black town of Rosewood, Florida, killing 6-27 people and burning the town to the ground. Survivors fled and never returned.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| Late 1800s–early 1900s | Rosewood developed as a small Florida community; by the early 20th century it was predominantly Black, with nearby Sumner being predominantly white. |
| Jan 1923 | Violence escalated after allegations involving a white woman in the nearby area; white mobs and participants from surrounding communities entered Rosewood. |
| Jan 1923 | Homes, churches, and businesses were burned; residents hid, fled through swamps/woods, and were evacuated in part by train and private assistance. |
| 1923 | The town was effectively abandoned after the attacks; survivors largely resettled elsewhere and the community did not recover. |
| 1994 | Florida enacted a compensation package for verified survivors and descendants, one of the first state-level reparations efforts for a specific act of racial violence in the U.S. |

## What Happened (High Level)

- **Location:** Rosewood, Levy County, Florida (near Sumner).
- **Timeframe:** Early January 1923.
- **Actors:** A white mob and armed participants from surrounding areas; Black residents and individuals who attempted to help them escape.
- **Outcome:** The town was burned and effectively wiped off the map; estimates of the death toll vary in historical accounts.

## Background and Context

- The region reflected **Jim Crow-era** segregation and racial hierarchy.
- Rosewood was known as a **predominantly Black community**, while nearby towns had different racial demographics and power structures.
- Rumors, racialized fear, and weak accountability mechanisms contributed to rapid escalation once violence began.

## Aftermath

- Survivors generally **did not return** due to continued danger and the destruction of housing and livelihoods.
- Rosewood’s destruction became an example of how **mob violence and arson** could erase entire communities.
- The episode remained **under-discussed publicly** for decades, resurfacing later through journalism, survivor testimony, and academic work.

## Legacy

- The Rosewood Massacre is frequently cited in discussions of:
  - racial terror and community destruction in U.S. history,
  - state and local failures to protect citizens,
  - later efforts at acknowledgment, memorialization, and reparations.
- A state investigation and public attention in the late 20th century helped lead to **Florida’s 1994 compensation law**.
