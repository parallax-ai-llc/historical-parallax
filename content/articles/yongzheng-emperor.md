## Summary

The Yongzheng Emperor (1678-1735), born Yinzhen, was the fourth emperor of the Qing dynasty and the third Qing emperor to rule over China proper. He reigned from 1722 to 1735, a period of thirteen years marked by intensive administrative reforms, anti-corruption campaigns, and fiscal consolidation.[^1][^2] His ascension to the throne remained shrouded in controversy, with persistent rumors that he had forged his father's will to usurp the position from his brothers.[^3] Despite his relatively short reign, Yongzheng's governance fundamentally transformed the Qing bureaucracy through centralization measures that strengthened imperial authority and laid the foundation for his son's prosperous reign.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Born on December 13, 1678, in Beijing, Yinzhen was the fourth son of the Kangxi Emperor to survive into adulthood.[^5] His mother was a court attendant from the Manchu Uya clan of the Plain Yellow Banner who held a low rank at the time of his birth and was therefore prohibited from raising her own children according to palace custom.[^6] For most of his childhood, Yinzhen was raised by Noble Consort Tong, the daughter of a powerful official, but she died when Yinzhen was only nine years old.[^7]

The Kangxi Emperor, recognizing the dangers of isolating his children within the palace walls, arranged a strict system of education for his sons and exposed them to the outside world through inspection trips and military campaigns.[^8] Yinzhen accompanied Kangxi on several inspection trips around the Beijing area and one journey further south, experiences that shaped his understanding of governance and administration.[^9] He served as the honorary leader of the Plain Red Banner during Kangxi's second battle against Mongol Khan Gordhun, gaining early military experience.[^10]

Yinzhen became deeply familiar with Confucianism and often led important ceremonies for his father, demonstrating his grasp of traditional Chinese philosophy and ritual.[^11] He also participated in political discussions at court, which helped him develop strong diplomatic skills and political acumen.[^12] These formative experiences prepared him for the complex political struggles that would define his path to the throne.

Yinzhen was made a beile (lord) in 1698 and was successively raised to the position of prince.[^13] Unlike some of his more flamboyant brothers, Yinzhen cultivated an image of diligence and reliability, often attending to administrative matters and presenting himself as a dutiful son to the aging emperor.[^14] However, his father Kangxi repeatedly admonished him for his rash nature and rapid mood swings, warning him to "Heed rashness and use perseverance."[^15]

## Rise to Power

The succession crisis that brought Yongzheng to power remains one of the most controversial episodes in Chinese imperial history. In 1712, the Kangxi Emperor removed his second son, Yinreng, as the designated heir and chose not to name a successor for the remaining decade of his reign, creating intense competition among his sons for the position of crown prince.[^16] This prolonged uncertainty spawned court factions and bitter rivalries among the princes.[^17]

When Kangxi died in 1722, Prince Yinzhen declared himself emperor, claiming his legitimacy from his father's last will and testament, which allegedly named "the fourth son" as heir and successor.[^18] Almost immediately, rumors spread that the new emperor had tampered with his father's testament or even murdered his predecessor.[^19] The most persistent allegation was that Yinzhen had altered the will by changing "fourteenth son" (Yinti) to "fourth son" (Yinzhen), though linguistic analysis has largely debunked this specific claim.[^20]

Modern historians generally accept that Kangxi likely considered both Yinzhen and Yinti as potential successors, ultimately choosing the son who was present in Beijing over the one commanding armies in the northwest.[^21] However, historian Qin Hui argues that it is largely recognized that Yongzheng forged the succession edicts together with Nian Gengyao, his trusted military commander.[^22] The controversy was serious enough that Yongzheng took the unprecedented step of publicly defending his legitimacy through his publication "Record of Great Righteousness to Dispel Confusion," though this failed to eliminate doubts about his succession.[^23]

Yinzhen's succession faced fierce opposition from former contenders including Yinti, Yinzhi, and Yinsi.[^24] The first years of his reign were consumed by consolidating power and neutralizing these threats. He imprisoned or executed some of his brothers and their supporters, including placing Yinti under house arrest at the imperial tombs west of Beijing despite Yinti being his brother by the same mother.[^25][^26] The biggest challenge was separating Yinsi's faction, which consisted of the eighth, ninth, and tenth princes and their supporters.[^27] Yongzheng stripped the tenth prince, Yin'e, of all his titles in May 1724 and sent him north to Shunyi.[^28]

## Major Achievements

### Administrative Centralization

The Yongzheng Emperor's greatest contribution was consolidating the power of the monarch within the bureaucracy, thus ending serious threats to imperial authority posed by Manchu princes.[^29] In 1729, he replaced the Grand Secretariat with the Grand Council as the top ministerial body, fundamentally transforming Qing governance.[^30] The Grand Council members worked directly with the emperor daily, handling business quickly and secretly, allowing him to personally scrutinize all important government matters.[^31] This formation had an enormous impact on the future of the Qing dynasty and represented a major step toward administrative centralization.[^32]

Yongzheng expanded the secret memorial system, which allowed officials to report directly to him, bypassing bureaucratic layers.[^33] This innovation increased his personal control over the government and proved instrumental in curbing corruption throughout the empire.[^34] He established a more organized civil service system emphasizing merit-based appointments over favoritism and launched sweeping reforms to combat widespread practices where landowners avoided taxes by "gifting" property to examination degree holders.[^35][^36]

### Fiscal and Tax Reforms

The Yongzheng Emperor implemented revolutionary tax reforms that fundamentally changed the imperial revenue system. He shifted head taxation to property taxation on landowners, greatly reducing the tax burden on ordinary civilians.[^37] The fusion of ding tax (individual tax) and field tax has been praised by historians as one of his most efficient and beneficent policies.[^38] This new system, in which people paid taxes based on the amount of land they owned rather than the number of people in their family, efficiently eased civilians' financial pressure and encouraged agricultural development.[^39]

The results were dramatic: treasury income increased from 32,622,421 taels in 1721 to about 60 million taels in 1730, surpassing records set during the Kangxi Emperor's reign.[^40] These fiscal improvements provided the economic foundation for the prosperity that characterized his son Qianlong's reign.[^41]

### Anti-Corruption Measures

A tireless worker known for his disciplined nature, Yongzheng focused intensely on fighting rampant corruption within the government and court society.[^42] He cracked down on corrupt officials and reformed personnel and financial administration with unprecedented vigor.[^43] He implemented severe punishments for officials found guilty of corruption, creating a climate of accountability that had been lacking in the final years of his father's reign.[^44] His anti-corruption efforts extended beyond rhetoric to systematic reforms that addressed structural weaknesses in the bureaucracy.[^45]

### Social Welfare and Reforms

Yongzheng initiated far-reaching social reforms that demonstrated his moralistic approach to governance. He issued a series of edicts between 1723 and 1730 attempting to remove the legal designations of "debased" people and the practice of hereditary bondage, known as the "Yongzheng emancipation."[^46] This sought to free all slaves to strengthen his authority through social leveling that created an undifferentiated class of free subjects under the throne, emancipating the vast majority of slaves.[^47] His reforms freed China's outcasts—singers, professional beggars, boat people, domestic slaves—and legally ended discrimination against them.[^48]

He supported construction of orphanages to shelter orphans, elementary schools to educate children, and poorhouses to house paupers.[^49] He also reconstructed irrigation systems and canals to assist agriculture and offered relief to areas affected by famine.[^50]

### Military Campaigns

In 1723, Yongzheng sent an army of 230,000 led by Nian Gengyao to quell a Dzungar uprising in Qinghai on the Qinghai-Tibet plateau.[^51] Though vastly superior in numbers, the Qing army was hampered by the geography of the terrain and had difficulty engaging the mobile enemy, but ultimately achieved victory and established full Qing control over Qinghai.[^52] When Tibet was torn by civil war in 1727-1728, he intervened militarily, and Qing troops arrived in Lhasa in September, punishing the anti-Qing faction by executing entire families.[^53] After withdrawing, he left a Qing Resident (the amban) and a military garrison to safeguard the dynasty's interests in Tibet.[^54]

In 1727, Yongzheng signed the Treaty of Kyakhta with Russia to finalize the Sino-Russian border and resolve commercial issues, demonstrating diplomatic as well as military capability.[^55]

## Controversies and Criticisms

### Succession Legitimacy Questions

The cloud of illegitimacy that hung over Yongzheng's succession never fully dissipated during his lifetime. Many claimed that he faked his father's will, with some suggesting the will had chosen Yinti, the 14th Prince, who was apparently Kangxi's favorite, as successor.[^56] Historian Qin Hui criticizes scholars who fail to explain why Yongzheng would have needed a forged edict if he had truly been the legal heir.[^57] Lin Qian suspects that Yongzheng invented certain events to justify his behavior, since no records mention them other than Shilu, which was written under Yongzheng's own supervision.[^58] Despite his tireless work ethic, he could never escape his greatest fear: that the calumnies of usurpation would be his lasting legacy.[^59]

### Brutal Treatment of Family and Political Rivals

Yongzheng proved utterly ruthless toward anyone who got in his way, including many of his siblings, creating a legacy of brutality and suspicion that tainted his name.[^60] He used murder, imprisonment, or exile against his brothers and their allies who rejected his legitimacy, adding to the resentment of his reign and heightening his paranoia.[^61] As emperor, Yinzhen attacked his political rivals, imprisoning many of his own brothers and executing their supporters.[^62] He was described as "a sovereign with many discredits and a controversial ending."[^63]

Even his most trusted general, Nian Gengyao, who had led the successful Qinghai campaign, fell victim to Yongzheng's suspicion. After Nian's stature and power grew following his military conquests, his desire for power increased until he sought to make himself equal to Yongzheng himself.[^64] When princes dismounted to greet him, Nian just nodded, demonstrating excessive pride.[^65] Yongzheng demoted Nian to general of the Hangzhou Commandery, and when Nian's ambitions did not change, he was given an ultimatum and committed suicide by poison in 1726.[^66]

### Literary Inquisition and Thought Control

The Yongzheng Emperor participated in an era of extensive literary inquisitions under Emperors Kangxi, Yongzheng, and Qianlong that lasted nearly 150 years.[^67] He continued and intensified the literary inquisition that became the Qing rulers' common instrument of thought control over citizens.[^68] If someone's speech was deemed offensive to the Qing regime, they could face execution by dismemberment, and their entire family or clan might suffer severe punishment.[^69] These literary inquisitions were intended to create a climate of fear among scholar-officials.[^70]

Most crimes dealt with punishment of writers who wrote anti-Qing literature and tried to revive the Ming, with the purpose of eradicating an ideology of nationalism among the Han Chinese and strengthening Manchu governance.[^71] One prominent case during Yongzheng's reign was the "Case of Examination Questions" in 1728, involving Cha Siting, a xiucai candidate.[^72] Under Yongzheng's influence, William Rowe writes, Qing academies "followed the earlier experience of prefectural and county schools: bureaucratization of management, formalization of curricula, and gradual emasculation as centers of independent scholarly inquiry."[^73]

### Religious Persecution

On February 11, 1724, the Yongzheng Emperor issued a decree proscribing Catholicism, marking a turning point in Catholic-Chinese relations that ended the Golden Era of Catholicism in China.[^74] This 1724 proscription ended a period that had begun with Matteo Ricci's arrival at the Forbidden City in 1601 and included the conversion of some 250,000 Qing subjects.[^75] While Yongzheng allowed foreign priests to remain in the capital to advise the court, the edict expelled missionaries working outside Beijing, requiring them to move to the Portuguese enclave at Macau.[^76][^77]

The simultaneous prohibition of Christianity and retention of foreign priests at court resulted from practical politics and the emperor's ideologically inspired desire to maintain the unity of the Three Teachings of dynastic China: Confucianism, Buddhism, and Taoism.[^78] Yongzheng was firmly against Christian converts among his own Manchu people.[^79] He reinforced anti-Christian policies in 1737, and persecution of Christians increased throughout the eighteenth century.[^80]

### Paranoia and Surveillance

The Yongzheng Emperor was vigilant to the point of paranoia, seeing threats even where none existed.[^81] He made use of the Manchus' astonishing web of secret communication and surveillance to round up conspirators and seek information about other possible dangers.[^82] This paranoia contributed to his reputation as a harsh and suspicious ruler who trusted few people completely.[^83]

## Personal Life

### Family and Consorts

The Yongzheng Emperor had eight consorts, including two empresses, two imperial noble consorts, three consorts, and one concubine.[^84] His primary consort was Empress Xiaojingxian of the Ula-Nara clan, while Empress Xiaoshengxian of the Niohuru clan was the mother of his successor, the Qianlong Emperor.[^85] Imperial Noble Consort Dunsu of the Nian clan was the sister of his powerful general Nian Gengyao, creating a family connection to his military commander.[^86]

The emperor had fourteen children, of which only five survived to adulthood.[^87] His fourth son, Hongli, later became the Qianlong Emperor and presided over what many consider the zenith of Qing power.[^88]

### Personality and Character

The Yongzheng Emperor possessed a complex personality marked by contradictions. He was a hard-working ruler who aimed to create a more effective government, demonstrating tireless dedication to administrative matters.[^89] Yet he was repeatedly admonished by his father Kangxi for his rash nature and rapid mood swings.[^90] After becoming emperor, Yongzheng remembered his father's warning to "Heed rashness and use perseverance."[^91]

He was known for his cruelty, implementing severe punishments for those he considered traitors.[^92] However, he also demonstrated pragmatism in talent enrollment, acknowledging that every person has both strengths and defects and tailoring tasks to suit their specific traits.[^93] The emperor was a superstitious man, affected by portents and omens, and a firm believer in Daoist longevity techniques.[^94]

### Cultural and Religious Interests

Yongzheng was a lover of art who did not follow traditional imperial practices.[^95] He was the first Qing emperor to systematically amass an art collection, taking a personal interest in expanding and enriching its holdings.[^96] Unlike his father who collected books for educational reasons and his son who became a mega-collector to demonstrate imperial grandeur, Yongzheng was a true connoisseur of art and antiquities who was intensely involved with his collections and took personal interest in their care and documentation.[^97]

He adopted Buddhist names and held Buddhist ceremonies in the palace, bringing together high monks from throughout the country.[^98] Yongzheng was a prolific writer of Chan literature and compiled works such as Yuexin ji (Essays That Entertain the Heart), Yuanming jushi yulu (The Recorded Sayings of Layman Yuanming), and Yuxuan yulu (Imperial Selections of Recorded Sayings).[^99] In 1733, dissatisfied with popular Chan anthologies, Yongzheng compiled the Yuxuan yulu (Imperial selection of recorded sayings).[^100]

## Legacy

The Yongzheng Emperor left a complex legacy that continues to generate scholarly debate. Despite his relatively short 13-year reign, he fundamentally transformed the Qing state through administrative reforms and fiscal consolidation that enabled his son's prosperous reign.[^101] He remains overshadowed by his father, Emperor Kangxi, and his son, Emperor Qianlong, making him an obscure figure in Chinese history with a rather bleak reputation in popular memory.[^102]

His greatest contribution was consolidating the power of the monarch within the bureaucracy, ending serious threats to imperial authority posed by Manchu princes and establishing the Grand Council system that would govern China for the next two centuries.[^103] He bequeathed to his son a more unified and prosperous China with a treasury swollen by his fiscal reforms and a bureaucracy chastened by his anti-corruption campaigns.[^104][^105]

Yongzheng's reign was significant in extending the power of the central government and consolidating imperial rule throughout China.[^106] His governance skills and attempts at comprehensive reform demonstrated administrative capability that modern scholars increasingly recognize.[^107] The foundation he laid for his son's splendor—through efficient administration, fiscal soundness, and bureaucratic centralization—represents his most enduring achievement.[^108]

However, the controversies surrounding his succession and his ruthless treatment of political rivals, including his own brothers, permanently stained his reputation.[^109] His literary inquisitions and religious persecutions demonstrated the dark side of his rule, showing a ruler willing to use terror to maintain control.[^110] The paranoia that characterized his reign created an atmosphere of fear and suspicion that limited intellectual freedom and independent inquiry.[^111]

His death in 1735, likely from elixir poisoning from an overdose of an "elixir of immortality," added an ironic footnote to his reign as the last Chinese ruler known to die from such poisonous substances consumed in pursuit of immortality.[^112][^113] His successor, the Qianlong Emperor, immediately evicted all Taoist priests from the palace, possibly as punishment for this incident.[^114]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Modern scholarly assessment of the Yongzheng Emperor has become increasingly nuanced and positive. Historians recognize him as "known for his disciplined and hardworking nature," noting how he "focused on reforming the bureaucracy, improving financial resources, and strengthening central authority, all while combating issues such as corruption and tax evasion."[^115] He remains "a complex figure in Chinese history, recognized for his governance skills and attempts at comprehensive reform."[^116]

A more positive view of the Qing period began a generation ago when strict Marxism was loosened, resulting in a flourishing of serious scholarship on all aspects of Qing economy, society, politics, and culture.[^117] This refurbished the view of the Qing from not-quite-a-dark age to an age of enlightenment, with strong, dedicated, and far-seeing leadership.[^118] Scholars note that he was "a shrewd statesman and an efficient administrator who fought rampant corruption within the government and court society, as well as a gifted artist and patron of the arts."[^119]

His social reforms, particularly the "Yongzheng emancipation" that freed China's outcasts and legally ended discrimination against them, demonstrate a moralistic approach to governance that modern scholars praise.[^120] His tax reforms shifting the burden from individuals to landowners have been recognized as "one of his most efficient and beneficent policies."[^121] As an art collector, he is celebrated as the first Qing emperor to systematically amass a collection with true connoisseurship rather than mere accumulation.[^122]

### Negative Views

Critics emphasize the legitimacy questions that plagued his reign. Historian Qin Hui argues that it is largely recognized that Yongzheng forged the succession edicts, and criticizes scholars for failing to address this fundamental issue.[^123] The brutal treatment of his brothers and political rivals reveals a ruler who "proved utterly ruthless toward anyone who got in his way, including many of his siblings, creating a legacy of brutality and suspicion."[^124]

His literary inquisitions and thought control policies represent "the Qing rulers' common instrument of thought control on citizens," creating a climate of fear that stifled intellectual freedom.[^125] Under his influence, Qing academies underwent "bureaucratization of management, formalization of curricula, and gradual emasculation as centers of independent scholarly inquiry."[^126] His 1724 proscription of Catholicism ended the Golden Era of Catholicism in China and initiated an era of religious persecution.[^127]

The expansion of the Sacred Edict, requiring twice-monthly readings and discussions even in local villages, has been characterized as "national indoctrination."[^128] Significantly, his son the Qianlong Emperor abolished this policy immediately after Yongzheng's death, suggesting even his successor found it excessive.[^129] His paranoia created an atmosphere of surveillance and suspicion that extended throughout the empire, with the emperor "seeing threats even where none existed."[^130]

Critics also note that despite his tireless work ethic and genuine administrative achievements, "he could never escape his greatest fear: that the calumnies of usurpation would be his lasting legacy."[^131] This fundamental insecurity about his legitimacy drove many of his harshest policies and contributed to the repressive atmosphere that characterized his reign.[^132]

## Famous Quotes

> "China has the teachings of China [...] and the West has the teachings of the West [...] The teachings of the West certainly cannot be applicable in China, and, as for the teachings of China, how can they be applicable in the West?" - From his 1724 edict on Christianity, expressing his philosophy of cultural separation[^133]

> "All three celestial objects share the quality of light yet retain their individuality." - Using astronomical metaphor to explain the unity of the three teachings (Confucianism, Buddhism, and Daoism)[^134]

> "Heed rashness and use perseverance." - His father Kangxi's warning that Yongzheng remembered and tried to follow after becoming emperor[^135]

> "Record of Great Righteousness to Dispel Confusion" - The unprecedented self-justification publication defending his succession legitimacy[^136]
