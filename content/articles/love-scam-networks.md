## Summary

Global romance fraud networks that steal billions annually by creating fake online relationships to manipulate victims into sending money. Often operated from West Africa and Southeast Asia.

## How Romance Fraud Commonly Works

- Scammers create **credible personas** on dating apps, social networks, or messaging platforms, often using stolen photos and fabricated life stories.
- The relationship is accelerated through frequent contact, emotional intimacy, and staged milestones ("exclusive," "future plans," etc.).
- Requests for money are introduced via a **trigger narrative**: emergency medical bills, travel problems, customs fees, business setbacks, or family crises.

## Networked Operations (High-Level)

- Many cases are run by **teams**, where different people handle profiling, conversation, payment instructions, and follow-up pressure.
- Tooling can include scripts, translation help, and reuse of identity assets; this is one reason the fraud scales across languages and time zones.

## Money Movement and Payment Rails

- Victims are often directed toward hard-to-reverse transfers (wire, gift cards, crypto, or money mules).
- Fraud proceeds may be laundered through layered accounts, cash-outs, or cryptocurrency exchanges; public reporting varies by region and enforcement capacity.

## Indicators and Defensive Practices

- Inconsistencies in photos, unwillingness to video call, repeated "emergencies," and pressure to move off-platform are common red flags.
- Verification steps (reverse image search, live video calls, and independently confirming stated details) reduce risk, but scammers adapt quickly.

## Notes on Measurement

- Loss estimates are typically compiled from reported complaints; actual totals may be higher due to **underreporting** and shame-related non-disclosure.
- Geography attributions (e.g., "West Africa" or "Southeast Asia") are frequently discussed in reporting, but individual cases can originate anywhere.
