## Summary

Kyaw Nyein (January 19, 1913 – June 29, 1986) was a Burmese lawyer, anti-colonial revolutionary, and one of the founding fathers of modern Burma. Recognized as a skilled diplomat, socialist theoretician, and one of the most dynamic politicians in Burma's democratic era, he held multiple ministerial portfolios including Deputy Prime Minister, Foreign Minister, and Home Minister under Prime Minister U Nu.[^1] A moderate socialist and architect of Burma's non-alignment policy, he co-founded the Asian Socialist Conference in 1953 and helped establish Burma's relations with Yugoslavia and Israel.[^2] His career was marked by both significant achievements in diplomacy and controversy over his domestic policies, culminating in a political split that destabilized Burma's democracy and led to his five-year imprisonment following Ne Win's 1962 military coup.[^3]

---

## Early Life and Background

Kyaw Nyein was born on January 19, 1913, in Pyinmana, British Burma, as the third child to Daw Thon and Po Toke.[^4] His father was a lawyer and leader of the General Council of Burmese Associations, providing young Kyaw Nyein with early exposure to anti-colonial politics and legal thinking. Growing up in Pyinmana during the height of British colonial rule, Kyaw Nyein witnessed firsthand the inequalities and injustices of the colonial system, which would shape his future commitment to Burmese independence and social reform.[^5]

He attended the King Edward Memorial School in Pyinmana, where he formed a lifelong friendship with Than Tun, who would later become chairman of the Communist Party of Burma despite their eventual political divergence.[^6] This early friendship would prove ironic, as Kyaw Nyein would later become one of the most prominent anti-communist democratic socialists in Burma. In 1930, Kyaw Nyein entered Mandalay College to study science and became engaged in university politics.[^7] After passing the Intermediate exam in 1933, he and Thein Pe Myint transferred from Mandalay College to Rangoon University, where he joined the English Honors program.[^8]

At Rangoon University, Kyaw Nyein's political consciousness deepened considerably. While hospitalized in the university infirmary in 1933, he met and befriended freshman student Aung San and later introduced him to Thein Pe Myint and Than Tun.[^9] In the same year, he ran in elections for the executive council of the Rangoon University Student Union (RUSU) together with Thein Pe Myint and Aung San, and after being elected became editor of the RUSU Bulletin.[^10] His intellectual formation during this period was significantly influenced by British Fabian socialist writings, particularly Bernard Shaw's works such as "The Intelligent Woman's Guide to Socialism."[^11] Unlike many of his contemporaries who were attracted to Marxism-Leninism, the young Kyaw Nyein gravitated toward the gradualist, democratic approach of Fabian socialism, which emphasized social reform through existing institutions rather than revolutionary upheaval.[^12]

Following his graduation in 1936, Kyaw Nyein entered law school and received his law degree in 1939, equipping him with legal expertise that would prove valuable in his political career.[^13] During his time at Rangoon University, he met his future wife Daw Nwe Nwe Yee, a Burmese honor student, while teaching English as a tutor sometime in 1936 or 1937.[^14] They married in 1943 and had seven children, four boys and three girls.[^15] One of their sons, Tun Kyaw Nyein, would later become a medical doctor, public intellectual, and political prisoner himself.[^16]

---

## Rise to Power

Kyaw Nyein's ascent to political prominence began during the turbulent period of the 1936 university strike, which would become a watershed moment in Burmese nationalist politics. During this strike, Nu, Raschid, Aung San, and Kyaw Nyein formed a strike council and set the strike strategy and objectives.[^17] The burden of negotiating and operational leadership fell to Aung San, Kyaw Nyein, and Raschid.[^18] The strike was sparked by the expulsion of prominent student leaders and as the protests persisted and spread to other schools and colleges throughout the country, they wrested major concessions from the university administration.[^19] After the strike, Kyaw Nyein and the other strike leaders established the All Burma Student Union (ABSU) to organize students nationwide, creating an organizational infrastructure that would prove crucial for the independence movement.[^20]

During World War II, Kyaw Nyein occupied a complex and somewhat contradictory position that reflected the difficult choices faced by Burmese nationalists. He served as Cabinet Secretary in the Civil Executive Administration under Prime Minister Dr. Ba Maw, and later as Vice Minister of Foreign Affairs in the Japanese-backed government.[^21] However, simultaneously, he was contributing to the anti-Japanese resistance, demonstrating the intricate political maneuvering required during the occupation. In August 1944, Kyaw Nyein participated in crucial negotiations between members of the Communist Party of Burma, the People's Revolutionary Party (PRP), and the Burma National Army that led to the founding of the anti-Japanese resistance organization, Anti-Fascist Organisation (AFO), which later became the Anti-Fascist People's Freedom League (AFPFL).[^22]

Kyaw Nyein was a member of the AFO Supreme Council, the Military Council, and the executive committee and was assigned as Political Commissar to Division (6), Pyinmana.[^23] After the war and the return of the British colonial administration, Kyaw Nyein, Thakin Mya, Ba Swe, and other AFPFL members founded the Burma Socialist Party (BSP) to counter the growing influence of the Communist Party of Burma within the AFPFL.[^24] This founding was a strategic move to prevent communist dominance in the independence movement and to provide an alternative leftist vision for post-independence Burma. After the post of chairman was abolished, the party was led jointly by Ba Swe and Kyaw Nyein.[^25]

Following World War II, Kyaw Nyein replaced Thakin Than Tun as general secretary of the AFPFL in July 1946, marking his emergence as a key leader in Burma's independence movement.[^26] When Burma achieved independence on January 4, 1948, Kyaw Nyein was appointed the first post-independence Minister of Home Affairs and in September 1948 assumed additionally the positions of Minister of Foreign Affairs and Deputy Prime Minister in the cabinet of Prime Minister U Nu.[^27] At age 35, he had become one of the most powerful politicians in the newly independent nation.

## Major Achievements

Kyaw Nyein's tenure in various ministerial roles produced several landmark accomplishments that shaped Burma's early years as an independent nation. His most celebrated achievement came in 1954 when, as Minister of Foreign Affairs, he achieved a breakthrough in negotiations with Japan on war reparations.[^28] In August 1954, Kyaw Nyein began negotiations with the Japanese government in Tokyo over reparation payments for damages inflicted on Burma during the war.[^29] U Kyaw Nyein, the head of the Burmese Mission, stated that the Burmese government wanted to incorporate reparations into their eight-year welfare state plan.[^30]

The negotiations were complex and politically sensitive. The Burmese maintained they could not politically accept less than the Philippines (which received $400 million), but suggested they might accept $200 million over ten years to fit into Burma's economic program.[^31] On September 25, 1954, Kyaw Nyein signed a reparation agreement with Japanese Foreign Minister Katsuo Okazaki that pledged to give Myanmar $200 million in reparations and $50 million to support economic cooperation over the next ten years.[^32] This settlement was followed by a peace pact between Japan and Burma, which he signed in Rangoon on November 6, 1954.[^33] Crucially, this agreement set a model for the Philippines and Indonesia, which were in deadlock with Japan over the same issue, demonstrating Kyaw Nyein's diplomatic prowess and influence in regional affairs.[^34]

Perhaps Kyaw Nyein's most enduring contribution to international politics was his role in founding and shaping the Asian Socialist Conference (ASC). Together with Ba Swe, he initiated the conference in January 1953, with the first meeting held in Rangoon from January 6-15, 1953.[^35] Kyaw Nyein served as the first treasurer of the conference, while Ba Swe was chairman, with headquarters in Rangoon.[^36] The ASC proposed the formation of a "Third Force" in world politics and adopted resolutions calling for support of democracy while condemning capitalism, communism, and imperialism.[^37] Kyaw Nyein envisaged a socialist Asia that would foster peace between the superpowers by forming an expanding zone of harmony that operated outside Cold War dynamics and tensions.[^38] Through the ASC, he managed to unite socialist revolutionaries from the Middle East, North Africa, and Asia, demonstrating his international influence.[^39]

Kyaw Nyein's foreign policy vision extended to establishing special bilateral relationships that reflected Burma's non-aligned stance. In July 1947, he visited Yugoslavia to study the country's constitution and socialist system, and certain features of the Yugoslav Constitution, particularly those relating to minorities, were eventually incorporated in Burma's 1947 constitution.[^40] Several high-ranking Burmese politicians, including Kyaw Nyein, expressed desires to transform Burma into the "Yugoslavia of Asia."[^41] Burma sought to establish international relations outside the East and West Blocs and access arms outside a restrictive defense treaty with the United Kingdom. After securing Yugoslavia as a major arms supplier, Burma terminated the contract with the British Services Mission.[^42]

Similarly, Kyaw Nyein led a high-level Burmese delegation to Israel in 1952, considering Israel a kindred country with a social democratic party in power and showing interest in its expertise in arms production and kibbutzim.[^43] Following this visit, Israel's Foreign Minister Moshe Sharett attended the Asian Socialist Conference in Rangoon in 1953, and diplomatic relations were established with David Hacohen appointed first Israeli ambassador to Burma in December 1953.[^44] Kyaw Nyein's Israel initiatives led to a friendlier pro-Israel attitude by Burma's UN delegation and marked the beginning of mutual visits in military, economic, and other fields.[^45]

On the domestic front, Kyaw Nyein helped shape the decolonization policies of post-independence Burma, from an active neutral foreign policy to building a welfare state, with particular focus on economic development and industrialization.[^46] Kyaw Nyein returned to U Nu's cabinet in 1951 and set up the Ministry of Cooperatives, which he handed over to U Tun Win in 1954 to create the Ministry of Industries.[^47] In 1956, he became Deputy Prime Minister of National Economy to oversee and coordinate the industrialization of Burma.[^48] He was described as "the brain of Burma's drive to socialism," envisaging socialism as being based on nationalization of industries and developing import substitution industrialization under government control.[^49]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his achievements, Kyaw Nyein's political career was marked by significant controversies that tarnished his reputation among certain segments of Burmese society. He drew severe criticism for his law and order policies as Home Minister at the height of the insurgencies.[^50] Burma's communists held him responsible for their failed revolution and to this day claim he pushed them "into the jungle."[^51] The authoritarian communists (Stalinists and Maoists) from the Communist Party of Burma and their sympathizers hated him, accusing him of being an anti-communist political opportunist and labeling him as a reactionary capitalist and mere nationalist.[^52]

Kyaw Nyein's role in handling ethnic conflicts proved particularly controversial. During the critical period following independence, Kyaw Nyein's Socialists used public paranoia to advance their aim of total elimination of conservative forces in general and the Karen in particular.[^53] He advocated for Bo Ne Win to be Defense Minister, as Ne Win's Fourth Burma Rifles was the only battalion the government could trust during the crisis.[^54] The Karen conflict became one of the most intractable problems in Burmese politics, and Kyaw Nyein's hardline approach contributed to the entrenchment of ethnic divisions that continue to plague Myanmar today.

A dramatic moment came in March 1949, at the height of the communist insurgency, when Kyaw Nyein and five fellow socialist ministers resigned from the government under unclear circumstances.[^55] According to autobiographical accounts, General Ne Win, the Chief of Staff of the Armed Forces, had forced their resignation, claiming he had struck a deal with the communists who were ready to join the government under the condition that all socialist ministers resigned.[^56] This incident highlighted the precarious political situation and the military's growing influence in civilian politics—a pattern that would ultimately lead to military dictatorship.

Kyaw Nyein's economic policies also drew criticism from more doctrinaire socialists. While initially advocating for nationalization and state control of industries, he gradually moved away from orthodox socialist policy toward a mixed economy.[^57] In March 1957, he declared his intention to create an economic climate conducive to foreign investment and private capital.[^58] This pragmatic shift was seen by hardline socialists as a betrayal of socialist principles and evidence of his supposed reactionary tendencies. Critics argued that his moderation and willingness to compromise with capitalist elements undermined the revolutionary potential of Burmese socialism.

The most consequential controversy of Kyaw Nyein's career was the 1958 AFPFL split, which destabilized Burma's democratic system and paved the way for military rule. The split was precipitated by a conflict over the appointment of the secretary of the AFPFL, a post regarded as essential for controlling patronage, electoral maneuverability, and ultimate power in Parliament and cabinet.[^59] U Nu supported a candidate whom he regarded as completely loyal and morally good in Buddhist fashion, while Kyaw Nyein rejected this candidate as excessively partisan and incompetent.[^60] In July 1958, the AFPFL formally split, with one group led by Prime Minister U Nu called the "Clean AFPFL," and the other led by Kyaw Nyein and Ba Swe known as the "Stable AFPFL."[^61]

On June 9, 1958, Ba Swe, Kyaw Nyein, and their colleagues sought to bring down Prime Minister U Nu by a no-confidence motion debated in a special session of the Chamber of Deputies.[^62] However, U Nu's Government survived with a small majority of eight votes, the voting being 119 for the motion and 127 against, helped by votes from the faction of "legal" communists in parliament.[^63] The majority of AFPFL MPs supported the Stable faction, but U Nu narrowly survived by securing opposition support.[^64] The rift within the ruling party destabilized the government and ultimately led to a military caretaker regime by Ne Win.[^65] In the 1960 elections, seen as a contest between the Clean AFPFL and Stable AFPFL, U Nu's Clean AFPFL won a decisive victory, capturing 157 of the 250 seats in the Chamber of Deputies.[^66] Many historians argue that Kyaw Nyein's political miscalculation in forcing this split inadvertently destroyed Burma's fragile democracy.

## Personal Life

Kyaw Nyein's personal life reflected the intellectual and political circles that dominated Burma's independence movement. He met his future wife, Daw Nwe Nwe Yee, a Burmese honor student, while teaching English as a tutor at Rangoon University sometime in 1936 or 1937.[^67] They married in 1943, during the Japanese occupation, and together raised seven children—four boys and three girls.[^68] Their third son, Tun Kyaw Nyein, became a medical doctor, public intellectual, and political prisoner, continuing his father's legacy of standing for democratic principles even at great personal cost.[^69]

Throughout his life, Kyaw Nyein maintained his intellectual curiosity and commitment to democratic socialism. He was known among his colleagues as the "brain" of Burmese socialism long before the military junta regime after 1962 perverted socialist ideals.[^70] His intellectual influences remained rooted in British Fabian socialism rather than Marxism-Leninism, setting him apart from more radical contemporaries.[^71] This ideological orientation made him an internationalist democratic socialist, a post-colonial intellectual, and decolonial organizer who ended up with conclusions similar to Trotskyist revolutionaries such as Hal Draper, Max Shachtman, and Sean Matgamna, despite his different starting point.[^72]

Kyaw Nyein maintained friendships across political divides, though these were often tested by the intense ideological battles of post-independence Burma. His youthful friendship with Than Tun, who became chairman of the Communist Party of Burma, exemplified the personal costs of political divergence.[^73] Despite being surrounded in his youth by figures like Ba Swe, Thein Pe Myint, and Than Tun who later led communist movements, Kyaw Nyein remained committed to democratic socialism and parliamentary democracy.[^74]

His relationship with Aung San, whom he befriended and mentored at Rangoon University, was particularly significant. Kyaw Nyein introduced Aung San to key figures in the independence movement and helped shape his early political development.[^75] After Aung San's assassination in 1947, Kyaw Nyein became one of the most important leaders carrying forward the vision of an independent, democratic Burma.

Kyaw Nyein's personality was characterized by intellectual rigor, political pragmatism, and diplomatic skill. He was known for his ability to negotiate complex political situations and his talent for building international coalitions. However, he could also be uncompromising in his opposition to communism and authoritarian tendencies, which sometimes made him political enemies. His willingness to take bold positions, such as demanding the restoration of democracy directly to Ne Win, demonstrated considerable personal courage.[^76]

## Legacy

Kyaw Nyein died in Rangoon after a long illness on June 29, 1986, aged 73.[^77] His death came during the height of Ne Win's socialist dictatorship, a system that had betrayed everything Kyaw Nyein stood for—democratic governance, mixed economy, and civil liberties. The years 1945-58 under the AFPFL government, during which Kyaw Nyein played a central role, are still seen as the country's greatest period of prosperity, far higher than conditions even in 2024.[^78] This retrospective assessment highlights the tragedy of Burma's political trajectory and the unrealized potential of the democratic period that Kyaw Nyein helped build.

Kyaw Nyein is recognized as one of the founding fathers of modern Burma, a skilled diplomat, socialist theoretician, and one of the most dynamic politicians in the country's democratic era.[^79] His contributions to Burma's non-alignment policy and his role in the Asian Socialist Conference had lasting impacts beyond Burma's borders. The ASC demonstrated that post-colonial nations could chart an independent course between the superpowers, influencing the broader Non-Aligned Movement. Kyaw Nyein's vision of a "Third Force" in world politics anticipated later developments in international relations and provided intellectual ammunition for newly independent nations seeking to avoid Cold War entanglements.

His diplomatic achievements, particularly the Japan reparations agreement, provided crucial resources for Burma's early development efforts and established a template for other Southeast Asian nations to follow. The $250 million total commitment helped fund infrastructure projects, industrial development, and social programs during Burma's critical early years of independence. Similarly, his initiative in establishing relations with Yugoslavia and Israel demonstrated creative diplomacy that served Burma's practical interests while maintaining its non-aligned principles.

On the domestic front, Kyaw Nyein's economic vision of a welfare state with mixed economy represented a pragmatic middle path between doctrinaire socialism and unfettered capitalism. His evolution from advocating nationalization toward supporting private investment and foreign capital reflected an ability to learn from experience and adjust policies accordingly. This pragmatism, though criticized by hardliners, might have provided a sustainable model for economic development had Burma's democratic system survived.

However, Kyaw Nyein's legacy is complicated by his role in the AFPFL split that precipitated the end of parliamentary democracy in Burma. While he cannot be held solely responsible for the military coup—Ne Win's ambitions and the military's institutional interests were determining factors—the political crisis created by the split provided the pretext for military intervention. Some historians argue that Kyaw Nyein's political ambition and unwillingness to compromise with U Nu ultimately undermined the democratic system both men claimed to serve.

His hardline policies toward communists and certain ethnic minorities also cast a shadow over his legacy. While anti-communist measures may have prevented a communist takeover, they also contributed to the insurgencies that plagued Burma for decades. His approach to the Karen conflict, in particular, helped establish patterns of ethnic conflict and military supremacy that continue to devastate Myanmar today.

Despite these complexities, Kyaw Nyein remains a significant figure in understanding Burma's democratic possibilities and failures. His internationalism, democratic socialism, and commitment to non-alignment represented an alternative vision for post-colonial development that was ultimately defeated by authoritarianism. The contrast between the prosperity of the AFPFL period and the stagnation under military socialism vindicated many of his economic ideas, even as his political legacy remained contested.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars of Burmese history recognize Kyaw Nyein as a sophisticated political thinker whose contributions to democratic socialism and non-alignment were significant not just for Burma but for the broader post-colonial world. He helped shape decolonization policies from an active neutral foreign policy to building a welfare state, demonstrating that newly independent nations could chart their own course.[^80] His role as founder of the Asian Socialist Conference established him as an internationalist who could unite socialist revolutionaries from diverse regions.[^81]

Academic assessments particularly praise Kyaw Nyein's diplomatic skills and his ability to negotiate complex international agreements. His success in the Japan reparations negotiations is frequently cited as exemplifying the quality of statesmanship that characterized Burma's early democratic period. Scholars note that his agreement set a precedent that benefited other Southeast Asian nations, demonstrating regional leadership and diplomatic innovation. His initiatives with Yugoslavia and Israel showed creative thinking that advanced Burma's practical interests while maintaining non-aligned principles.[^82]

Kyaw Nyein's intellectual contributions to democratic socialism have gained renewed appreciation in recent decades. His synthesis of Fabian gradualism with anti-colonial nationalism represented a distinctive third way between Western social democracy and Soviet-style communism. Scholars studying alternative development paths in post-colonial nations point to Kyaw Nyein's vision as representing possibilities that were foreclosed by military dictatorship. His evolution toward supporting mixed economy and foreign investment is now seen as prescient given the failures of autarkic socialist models.[^83]

Defenders of Kyaw Nyein argue that his anti-communist policies, while harsh, were necessary to prevent Burma from becoming a communist state or satellite. They note that the communist insurgencies posed existential threats to the young democracy and that Kyaw Nyein's firmness, though controversial, helped preserve Burma's independence and non-aligned status. From this perspective, his willingness to take unpopular but necessary security measures demonstrated political courage and clear-sighted realism about communist threats.[^84]

Some scholars emphasize Kyaw Nyein's personal integrity and commitment to democratic principles. His demand for restoration of democracy with mixed economy when advising Ne Win, despite having been imprisoned by the dictator, showed principled consistency even when politically costly.[^85] His five-year imprisonment and his subsequent refusal to endorse military socialism demonstrate that he remained faithful to democratic ideals throughout his life.

### Negative Views

Critics of Kyaw Nyein emphasize his role in precipitating the crisis that ended Burma's democracy. They argue that the 1958 AFPFL split was primarily driven by personal ambition and political rivalry rather than genuine policy differences. From this perspective, Kyaw Nyein's unwillingness to compromise with U Nu and his attempt to bring down the elected Prime Minister through parliamentary maneuvering demonstrated a willingness to sacrifice democratic stability for factional advantage. The fact that he required communist support to even come close to toppling U Nu's government is seen as particularly hypocritical given his anti-communist rhetoric.[^86]

Kyaw Nyein's policies toward ethnic minorities draw harsh criticism from scholars of ethnic conflict in Myanmar. His use of "public paranoia" to advance elimination of Karen political influence is seen as establishing patterns of ethnic scapegoating and militarization that have had catastrophic long-term consequences.[^87] Critics argue that his advocacy for military solutions to political problems empowered Ne Win and the military, creating the conditions for the eventual coup. His hardline approach foreclosed possibilities for political accommodation with ethnic minorities and helped entrench the military's role in ethnic affairs.

Communist and left-wing critics continue to view Kyaw Nyein as a class traitor who betrayed socialist principles by gradually accommodating capitalism and foreign investment. From this perspective, his evolution toward mixed economy represented capitulation to imperialist interests rather than pragmatic adjustment. His role in founding the Burma Socialist Party specifically to counter communist influence is seen as splitting the anti-colonial movement and weakening its revolutionary potential. The accusation that he "pushed communists into the jungle" resonates with those who believe armed struggle was forced upon communists by Kyaw Nyein's repression rather than chosen voluntarily.[^88]

Some scholars question whether Kyaw Nyein's diplomatic achievements were as significant as supporters claim. They note that the Japan reparations, while valuable, were inevitable given international law and precedent. The establishment of relations with Yugoslavia and Israel, while creative, did not fundamentally alter Burma's strategic position or economic development trajectory. The Asian Socialist Conference, despite its ambitious rhetoric, had limited practical impact and dissolved within a few years. From this skeptical perspective, Kyaw Nyein's reputation as a brilliant diplomat is overstated, reflecting the generally low bar for statesmanship in post-colonial Burma rather than exceptional achievement.[^89]

Critics also point to Kyaw Nyein's political misjudgments, particularly his underestimation of Ne Win's ambitions and his overestimation of his own political support. The 1960 electoral defeat of the Stable AFPFL showed that his political calculations were flawed and that U Nu retained greater popular support. His apparent surprise at U Nu's victory suggests a politician out of touch with popular sentiment, raising questions about his political judgment. The fact that Ne Win, whom Kyaw Nyein had supported for Defense Minister, ultimately imprisoned him adds tragic irony to his legacy and suggests fundamental failures in understanding military politics.[^90]

## Famous Quotes

> "We cannot politically accept less than the Philippines, but we might accept $200 million over ten years to fit into Burma's economic program."

*During Japan reparations negotiations, 1954. This statement demonstrated Kyaw Nyein's diplomatic skill in framing Burma's demands in terms of economic planning rather than retribution, making compromise easier while maintaining Burma's dignity.*[^91]

> "The danger of becoming associated with power blocs threatens our independence and neutrality."

*Speech in parliament opposing Burma's alignment with Western powers through potential Pacific security pacts, late 1940s. This encapsulated Kyaw Nyein's commitment to non-alignment and his vision of a Third Force in international politics.*[^92]

> "We must create an economic climate that is conducive to foreign investment and private capital."

*Declaration in March 1957, marking his evolution from orthodox socialist nationalization toward mixed economy. This represented his pragmatic adjustment of economic policy based on experience.*[^93]

> "Burma must become the Yugoslavia of Asia."

*Expressing his vision for Burma's development path, inspired by his 1947 visit to Yugoslavia. This reflected his belief that Burma could chart an independent socialist course distinct from both Soviet and Western models.*[^94]

> "We demand the return to democracy with a mixed economy."

*Bold statement made directly to dictator Ne Win during constitutional advisory committee meetings, despite having been imprisoned by Ne Win for five years. This demonstrated Kyaw Nyein's unwavering commitment to democratic principles even in the face of authoritarian power.*[^95]
