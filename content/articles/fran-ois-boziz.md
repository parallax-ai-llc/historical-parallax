## Summary

Former President of the Central African Republic who seized power in a coup and was later overthrown by Séléka rebels. UN sanctions imposed for undermining peace and fueling conflict.

## Rise to Power and Rule

- Bozizé took power in 2003 via a coup, a recurring pattern in the Central African Republic's post-independence political history.
- His presidency was marked by recurring rebellions, fragile state finances, and heavy dependence on foreign security and diplomatic support.

## Conflict Dynamics

- Multiple armed groups operated in the country, often driven by local grievances, regional spillover, and competition over resources and state authority.
- By 2012–2013, the Séléka coalition advanced on the capital and ultimately forced Bozizé from power.

## International Response

- The UN and other international actors used sanctions and peacekeeping tools in response to violence and alleged destabilization.
- Public documentation commonly frames the period as a governance and security crisis with severe humanitarian consequences.

## Legacy

- Bozizé remains a polarizing figure: some supporters emphasize security and state continuity, while critics focus on corruption claims, abuses, and the erosion of institutions.
- The episode is often referenced as part of the longer arc of state fragility and cyclical conflict in the CAR.
