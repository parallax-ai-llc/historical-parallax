## Medical significance

William Stewart Halsted was an American surgeon who helped shape modern surgical practice in the United States.

He emphasized strict aseptic technique, careful tissue handling, and standardized surgical training.

## Johns Hopkins and the “Big Four”

Halsted was one of the founding professors at Johns Hopkins Hospital, often grouped with William Osler, Howard Atwood Kelly, and William H. Welch as the institution’s “Big Four.”

At Johns Hopkins, he developed a structured residency training model that became highly influential in North American surgical education.

## Surgical innovations

Halsted is associated with major procedural and practice innovations, including:

- The radical mastectomy as an approach to breast cancer surgery in his era.
- The introduction of rubber surgical gloves in the operating room (initially to protect staff, later recognized as a key infection-control practice).

## Personal history

Historical accounts note that Halsted developed cocaine addiction through self-experimentation with anesthetics and later maintained a morphine dependence.

Despite this, he sustained a long academic and clinical career and left a large institutional legacy through trainees and the residency system.
