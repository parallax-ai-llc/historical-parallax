## Summary

See Tsuneo Mori and the United Red Army. Japanese radical who led the United Red Army's purge that killed 12 of its own members during winter training in 1972. This internal purge shocked Japan.

## Historical Context

The United Red Army emerged from the fragmentation and radicalization of Japan's New Left movements in the late 1960s and early 1970s. Ideological rigidity, security pressure, and factional distrust contributed to an atmosphere where internal discipline could become violent.

## The Purge ("United Red Army Incident")

During a period of training in mountainous areas, leaders enforced extreme "self-criticism" sessions. Members judged to be insufficiently committed were subjected to escalating punishment, and a dozen died. The episode became one of the most notorious cases of intra-movement violence in postwar Japan.

## Asama-Sansō and Public Reaction

The Asama-Sansō incident, broadcast widely, drew intense public attention to the group's extremism. It accelerated a broader backlash against militant politics and is often cited as a turning point in public perception of the radical left in Japan.

## Notes on Biography

Biographical details for underground militants are frequently summarized through police records, court proceedings, and journalism from the period. Where sources differ, high-level descriptions and widely corroborated facts are preferable to precise but uncertain claims.

## Legacy

The United Red Army's internal killings are often referenced in studies of political sectarianism, group psychology, and the dynamics of coercion inside closed organizations. In Japan, the episode remains a cautionary example of how revolutionary rhetoric can devolve into violent authoritarianism within a movement.

The story is also revisited in documentaries and books about the late-1960s protest generation and its aftermath.
