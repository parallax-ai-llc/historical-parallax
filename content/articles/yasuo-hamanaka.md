## Summary

Sumitomo Corporation copper trader known as 'Mr. Copper' who manipulated the global copper market for a decade. His unauthorized trades caused $2.6 billion in losses.

## Background and Role at Sumitomo

Yasuo Hamanaka worked as a copper trader at Sumitomo Corporation and developed an outsized reputation in the metals market, leading to the nickname “Mr. Copper.”

Copper is a globally traded industrial commodity, and large positions can meaningfully influence prices—especially when paired with strategies that constrain supply or exploit market structure.

## The Trading Scheme (High-Level)

Accounts summarized in public sources describe a long-running pattern of unauthorized trading and concealment.

Key elements often discussed include:

- Building large positions in copper derivatives.
- Attempting to influence prices by controlling deliverable supply.
- Hiding losses through off-balance-sheet arrangements and falsified reporting.

## Discovery and Consequences

When the scale of the losses became public in 1996, Sumitomo disclosed billions of dollars in damage, reported at approximately US$2.6 billion.

The scandal contributed to increased attention on risk controls, supervision of trader positions, and disclosure practices for large commodity exposures.

## Legal Outcome and Corporate Aftermath

Hamanaka faced criminal prosecution in Japan (as described in Wikipedia).

For Sumitomo and the wider industry, the case became a reference point for:

- The dangers of weak internal controls in trading operations.
- The difficulty of detecting long-term concealment when reporting channels are compromised.
- The systemic impact a single desk can have on a global commodity market.
