## Summary

Fijian military officer who carried out two coups in 1987, overthrowing the elected government. His coups were motivated by ethnic Fijian nationalism against Indo-Fijian political power. He later became PM through elections and again in 2022.

## 1987 coups and political context

Sitiveni Rabuka became internationally known for leading two coups in 1987 that removed an elected government in Fiji. The coups are frequently explained through the lens of ethnic politics: tensions between Indigenous Fijians (iTaukei) and Indo-Fijians, and fears among nationalist factions about political and economic influence.

The coups contributed to constitutional and institutional change, and they marked a turning point in Fiji's modern political history.

## Transition into electoral politics

After the coup period, Rabuka moved into formal political leadership, including terms as prime minister in the 1990s. His later career is notable because it reflects a shift from military intervention to participation in elections and parliamentary governance.

## Return to power in 2022

Rabuka's return as prime minister in 2022 is often discussed as evidence of Fiji's cyclical political realignments, where longstanding figures re-emerge through coalition politics. His government formed amid debates over governance standards, economic management, and Fiji's relationships with regional and global partners.

## Significance

Rabuka's career is frequently used as a case study in:

- The long-term effects of coups on constitutional order.
- Civil–military relations in small states.
- How leaders associated with anti-democratic interventions can later seek democratic mandates.
