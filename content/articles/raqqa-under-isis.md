## Summary

ISIS made Raqqa its de facto capital, imposing brutal Sharia law including public executions, crucifixions, and slavery. The city was devastated when recaptured by US-backed forces.

## How ISIS governed the city

After taking control, ISIS attempted to run Raqqa as a showcase for its self-declared caliphate. It established administrative bodies for policing, courts, taxation, and propaganda, and used a mix of coercion and basic service provision to maintain control.

Key features of rule described by reporting and witness accounts include:

- Strict social regulation and religious policing
- Targeting of political opponents, journalists, and perceived dissenters
- A pervasive security apparatus, including detention and surveillance

## Economy, services, and daily life

Raqqa’s economy and infrastructure were reshaped by wartime conditions, sanctions, and ISIS governance. Shortages, price spikes, and damage to utilities affected residents, while ISIS extracted revenue through taxation, confiscation, and control of trade. The group also used media offices and public messaging to project stability and legitimacy.

## Information control and propaganda

ISIS maintained extensive messaging operations in Raqqa. It restricted outside journalism, staged media events, and produced content aimed at international recruitment and intimidation. The city’s association with ISIS propaganda became one of the reasons it was treated as a high-priority strategic target.

## Resistance and external pressure

Even under heavy repression, residents and clandestine networks reportedly engaged in forms of resistance such as documenting abuses, communicating with outside groups, and providing intelligence. Over time, ISIS faced increasing military pressure from multiple actors in Syria and Iraq.

## Liberation and aftermath

ISIS lost Raqqa following the 2017 battle involving the Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF) with U.S.-led coalition support. The end of ISIS control did not immediately resolve humanitarian needs: widespread damage, displacement, and explosive remnants of war shaped the post-ISIS period.
