## Summary

Swedish military officer who shot and killed 7 people in Falun on June 11, 1994, during a psychotic episode fueled by alcohol. It was Sweden's worst peacetime mass shooting. Sentenced to life, later commuted; released in 2014.

## Background

Mattias Flink was a Swedish military officer whose 1994 shooting in Falun became a defining mass-casualty event in Sweden’s modern history. The attack occurred in a civilian setting and was widely covered as an unprecedented case of peacetime gun violence in the country.

## The 1994 Falun Shooting

Contemporary accounts describe Flink as intoxicated and experiencing severe psychological disturbance at the time of the killings. The victims included multiple people in a public area, and the event produced intense national debate about firearms access, mental health, and institutional warning signs.

## Legal Proceedings and Sentence

Swedish courts convicted Flink and imposed a life sentence. Later, the sentence was commuted under Swedish sentencing rules, and he was eventually released from prison in 2014.

## Public and Policy Discussion

The case is often referenced in Sweden in discussions of:

- The relationship between acute mental illness, substance use, and extreme violence.
- How military training and access to weapons intersect with public safety.
- Victim support and long-term community impact after mass shootings.

## Further Notes

Some Swedish coverage frames the event as an outlier in a country with comparatively low rates of gun homicide, which is part of why it remains culturally salient decades later.
