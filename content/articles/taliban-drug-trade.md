## Summary

The Talibans involvement in Afghanistans opium trade which generates an estimated $1.5-3 billion annually. Despite publicly banning drugs, the Taliban taxed and facilitated production for decades.

## Overview

Afghanistan has long been a major producer of opium poppy, which can be processed into opiates for international markets. Various armed groups, local power brokers, and trafficking networks have profited from this economy, and the Taliban have been repeatedly linked to it through taxation, protection, and regulation in areas under their influence.

## Revenue Mechanisms

Reporting commonly describes several ways insurgent or governing authorities can capture revenue from drug-linked activity:

- Taxes or “ushr/zakat” style levies on farmers and traders.
- Fees at checkpoints for transport of goods.
- Protection payments and informal licensing in controlled districts.

The exact scale and structure can vary over time and by region, and estimates are often contested.

## Policy Shifts and Bans

The Taliban have at times announced bans on poppy cultivation or drug trafficking, including a widely cited cultivation ban around 2000–2001. Analysts have debated motivations, enforcement capacity, and the degree to which bans have been sustained versus reversed under economic and political pressure.

## International and Humanitarian Dimensions

The opium economy intersects with poverty, debt, and rural livelihoods, making eradication policies politically and socially sensitive. International actors have also connected narcotics flows to corruption, organized crime, and financing of armed conflict, complicating counter-narcotics strategies during and after international military involvement in Afghanistan.
