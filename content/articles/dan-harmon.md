## Summary

Dan Harmon is an American television writer, producer, and podcaster best known as the creator of the NBC/Yahoo! Screen sitcom *Community* (2009–2015) and the co-creator of the Adult Swim animated series *Rick and Morty* (2013–present). His work is distinguished by rigorous structural analysis of narrative — most notably his popularization of the "Story Circle," a storytelling framework derived from Joseph Campbell's Hero's Journey — combined with self-referential humor and emotional sincerity. He has become one of the most intellectually influential voices in American television comedy.

Harmon's career is marked by remarkable highs and dramatic setbacks, including a very public firing from and reinstatement to *Community*, as well as significant personal controversies. Despite turbulence, his creative output has shaped modern serialized comedy and earned him a dedicated global fanbase.

## Early Life & Background

Dan Harmon was born on January 3, 1973, in Milwaukee, Wisconsin. He grew up in Menomonee Falls, a Milwaukee suburb, and displayed an early aptitude for performance and writing. He attended Marquette University briefly before dropping out, a decision that would later color the academic anxieties embedded in *Community*. During his early twenties, he co-founded the Los Angeles–based comedy troupe Channel 101, a short-film festival and channel that premiered homemade serialized shows and served as a launching pad for several prominent comedy writers, including Jack Black collaborator Rob Schrab.

## Rise to Prominence

Harmon's first significant Hollywood credit came as a co-writer on the 2006 Ben Stiller film *Monster House* (voice and story credit). He transitioned fully to television with *Community*, which premiered on NBC in September 2009. The show centered on a study group at a fictional community college and became celebrated for its genre parody episodes, emotional depth, and metafictional wit. Though never a ratings blockbuster, it cultivated an intensely loyal audience and critical respect.

In May 2012, Sony Pictures Television fired Harmon as showrunner ahead of *Community*'s fourth season, citing difficult working relationships. Season 4 was widely considered inferior, and Harmon was rehired for Season 5 in June 2013. The same year, *Rick and Morty* — co-created with Justin Roiland — debuted on Adult Swim, blending nihilistic science fiction with genuine pathos and quickly becoming a cultural phenomenon.

## Key Achievements

- Created *Community* (2009–2015), which ran for 110 episodes across six seasons on two networks.
- Co-created *Rick and Morty*, which by its fifth season was Adult Swim's highest-rated series and had been renewed for 70 additional episodes in a landmark 2018 deal.
- Popularized the "Story Circle" narrative framework, widely taught in screenwriting circles and online communities.
- Founded and ran Channel 101 (2003–present), incubating early work from writers who went on to staff major network shows.
- Hosts the long-running podcast *Harmontown* (2012–2019), which chronicled his creative process and personal struggles with unusual candor.

## Controversies & Criticism

In 2018, former *Community* writer Megan Ganz publicly accused Harmon of sexual harassment during the show's original run. Harmon issued an unusually detailed public apology via *Harmontown*, acknowledging the behavior and its professional harm to Ganz. Ganz accepted the apology, though the incident prompted wider discussion about power dynamics in writers' rooms. Harmon has also faced criticism for erratic behavior on set and difficult interpersonal dynamics with collaborators and network executives.

## Legacy & Influence

Harmon's narrative theories have permeated online screenwriting discourse, with the Story Circle referenced extensively in film schools and YouTube essays. *Rick and Morty* has generated billions in merchandise revenue and inspired a generation of animated series that blend absurdist comedy with serialized emotional arcs. *Community* is frequently cited as a template for cult television that rewards obsessive rewatching. His willingness to discuss failure, mental health, and creative process publicly — through podcasting and social media — has made him an unusual model of transparency in an industry defined by image management.
