## Summary

Chemical weapons attack by Iraqi forces on the Kurdish town of Halabja on March 16, 1988 that killed approximately 5,000 civilians. Multiple chemical agents including mustard gas and nerve agents were used.

## Additional Context

### Historical Background

The Halabja chemical attack occurred near the end of the Iran–Iraq War and is commonly discussed alongside Iraq’s broader Anfal campaign against Kurdish populations. In many historical accounts, the event is framed as a landmark instance of chemical weapons use against civilians in the late 20th century.

### What Is Commonly Reported

High-level summaries typically note:

- The attack took place on **16 March 1988** in and around Halabja, a Kurdish town in northern Iraq.
- Multiple chemical agents were reported, often including **mustard agent** and **nerve agents**.
- Civilian casualty estimates vary across sources, and the attack produced long-term health consequences for survivors.

### Legal and Political Aftermath

The event has been cited in:

- **International condemnation** of chemical weapons use
- **Domestic and international prosecutions** connected to Iraqi state officials
- **Kurdish collective memory and political mobilization**, including memorialization and recognition efforts

### Significance

Beyond the immediate human toll, Halabja is frequently referenced in discussions of:

- The evolution of norms and enforcement around **weapons of mass destruction**
- Evidence gathering and attribution challenges in wartime atrocities
- The long-term societal impacts of mass-casualty attacks on minority communities
