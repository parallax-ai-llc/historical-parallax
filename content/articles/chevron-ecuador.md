## Summary

Texaco (now Chevron) dumped 16 billion gallons of toxic waste in the Ecuadorian Amazon over 26 years. An Ecuadorian court ordered $9.5 billion in damages; Chevron has refused to pay.

## Background

- The dispute stems from oil operations in Ecuador’s Oriente region (including the Lago Agrio area) that began in the 1960s, initially involving Texaco Petroleum Company (TexPet) in a consortium.
- Indigenous communities and local residents alleged long-term environmental contamination and health impacts from production practices and waste handling.
- Texaco later merged into Chevron (Chevron acquired Texaco in 2001), after which Chevron became the corporate defendant in related proceedings.

## Litigation in Ecuador

- The most prominent phase of the case is often referred to as the “Lago Agrio litigation,” filed on behalf of affected residents and pursued in Ecuadorian courts.
- In 2011, a trial court in Ecuador issued a damages judgment against Chevron; Ecuador’s National Court of Justice later reduced the amount and removed a punitive component, leaving a multibillion-dollar award.
- Chevron has maintained that the Ecuadorian judgment was procured through misconduct and that Texaco’s remediation obligations were previously settled; the plaintiffs have disputed those claims.

## International arbitration and enforcement actions

- Because Chevron had limited assets in Ecuador, enforcement efforts were pursued in other jurisdictions (including attempts to recognize and enforce the Ecuadorian judgment abroad).
- Parallel proceedings included investor–state arbitration under bilateral investment treaty mechanisms, in which arbitral tribunals addressed claims related to the Ecuadorian court process and the state’s obligations.
- In the United States, Chevron brought civil claims alleging fraud and racketeering connected to the Ecuadorian judgment; the litigation involved attorney Steven Donziger and became a significant, separate controversy.

## Why the case is notable

- The Chevron–Ecuador dispute is frequently cited as one of the most consequential environmental and corporate-liability conflicts of the late 20th and early 21st centuries.
- It illustrates the complexity of cross-border enforcement, the interaction between national courts and international arbitration, and the high evidentiary and procedural stakes in mass-tort environmental claims.
