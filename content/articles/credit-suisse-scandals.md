## Summary

Credit Suisse collapsed amid decades of scandals including helping clients evade taxes, laundering money for dictators, and the Archegos and Greensill losses. Absorbed by UBS in 2023.

## Overview

The term “Credit Suisse scandals” is used to summarize a long sequence of controversies involving compliance failures, risk-management breakdowns, and reputational damage. While individual cases varied by jurisdiction and business line, together they contributed to market and depositor distrust.

## Recurrent Themes

- **Client due diligence and AML**: Allegations and enforcement actions tied to how the bank assessed politically exposed persons (PEPs), source-of-funds, and suspicious-activity monitoring.
- **Cross-border tax and secrecy issues**: Long-running scrutiny of Swiss private banking practices and allegations of facilitation of tax evasion.
- **Risk controls in investment banking**: Episodes where prime brokerage and structured products created concentrated exposures.

## Archegos and Greensill

In 2021 Credit Suisse suffered large losses linked to the failure of Archegos Capital Management (a family office) and the collapse of supply-chain finance funds associated with Greensill Capital. These events became emblematic of governance and oversight gaps, prompting internal reviews, leadership changes, and regulatory attention.

## Crisis and Resolution in 2023

By 2022–2023, the combination of losses, scandals, and market stress contributed to funding pressures and rapid loss of confidence. Swiss authorities brokered an emergency deal in which UBS agreed to acquire Credit Suisse, with extraordinary measures used to stabilize the system.

## Why It Matters

The episode is frequently referenced in discussions of “too big to fail” policy, bank supervision, and the difficulty of rebuilding trust after repeated compliance and risk incidents. It also highlights how reputational problems can become liquidity problems when counterparties and depositors react quickly.

- The UBS integration phase raised additional questions about liabilities, remediation, and future Swiss banking regulation.
