## Summary

The Colosseum (Flavian Amphitheatre) is an oval amphitheater in the center of Rome, Italyâ€”the largest ancient amphitheater ever built and a lasting symbol of Imperial Roman power and engineering. Constructed between 70-80 CE under the Flavian emperors Vespasian and Titus, the Colosseum could hold 50,000-80,000 spectators who gathered to watch gladiatorial contests, animal hunts, public executions, and dramatic re-enactments. Though earthquakes and stone-robbers left it partially ruined, the Colosseum remains Rome's most iconic landmark, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.

## Historical Background

### Nero's Golden House

**Domus Aurea:**
Emperor Nero (r. 54-68 CE) built lavish palace complex after Great Fire of Rome (64 CE). Included artificial lake where Colosseum now stands.

**Nero's Fall:**
Nero committed suicide (68 CE). Flavian dynasty (Vespasian, Titus, Domitian) replaced him, symbolically returning Nero's private land to public use.

### Flavian Dynasty

**Vespasian:**
Commissioned amphitheater (c. 70 CE). Funded partly by spoils from sack of Jerusalem (70 CE).

**Titus:**
Inaugurated Colosseum (80 CE) with 100 days of games. Thousands of animals and gladiators killed.

**Domitian:**
Completed hypogeum (underground tunnels) and added top level.

## Construction

### Engineering Marvel

**Dimensions:**
- Length: 189 meters (620 feet)
- Width: 156 meters (512 feet)
- Height: 48 meters (157 feet)
- Arena floor: 83 x 48 meters

**Capacity:**
50,000-80,000 spectators (estimates vary)

**Materials:**
- Travertine limestone (exterior, arches)
- Tuff (inner walls)
- Brick-faced concrete
- 300 tons of iron clamps held stones together

### Innovative Features

**Vomitoria:**
80 entrance/exit passages allowing rapid crowd flow. 50,000 could exit in minutes.

**Velarium:**
Retractable awning (sailcloth) provided shade. Operated by sailors.

**Hypogeum:**
Complex underground network:
- Animal cages
- Equipment storage
- Gladiator waiting areas
- Elevators/trap doors to arena

**Social Seating:**
Strict hierarchy:
- Bottom: Senators, officials
- Middle: Wealthy citizens
- Top: Common people, women
- Emperor's box (pulvinar) for best view

## Events

### Gladiatorial Combat

**Gladiators:**
Trained fighters (slaves, prisoners, volunteers). Different classes with specialized weapons:
- Murmillo (sword, shield)
- Retiarius (net, trident)
- Thraex (curved sword)

**Fights:**
Usually not to death (training expensive). Crowd and emperor decided wounded fighter's fate. Thumbs up/down signal debated by historians.

### Venationes (Animal Hunts)

**Exotic Animals:**
Lions, elephants, tigers, bears, hippos imported from across Empire.

**Scale:**
Inaugural games: 9,000 animals killed. Over centuries, may have contributed to extinction of some Mediterranean species.

### Executions

**Public Punishment:**
Criminals executed midday entertainment:
- Thrown to beasts (damnatio ad bestias)
- Crucifixion
- Burning

**Christian Martyrs:**
Traditional belief that Christians were martyred in Colosseum; limited historical evidence, but became powerful symbol.

### Naval Battles

**Naumachiae:**
Early period: arena flooded for mock sea battles. Discontinued after hypogeum built.

### Dramas

**Mythological Enactments:**
Condemned prisoners sometimes acted out myths ending in death.

## Decline

### Late Empire

**Last Games:**
- Last gladiatorial combat: 435 CE (possibly later)
- Last animal hunts: 523 CE

**Christianity:**
Rise of Christianity gradually eroded support for blood sports.

### Medieval Period

**Converted Uses:**
- Fortress (Frangipane family)
- Housing
- Workshops
- Religious shrine

**Earthquakes:**
Major damage 847 CE, 1231 CE, 1349 CE (southern side collapsed).

**Stone Quarry:**
Fallen stones carted away for other buildings:
- St. Peter's Basilica
- Palazzo Venezia
- River embankments

### Preservation

**18th Century:**
Pope Benedict XIV declared Colosseum sacred (Christian martyrs). Halted stone removal.

**19th-20th Century:**
Major restoration and archaeological work.

## Modern Era

### UNESCO World Heritage Site (1980)

Part of "Historic Centre of Rome" inscription.

### New Seven Wonders (2007)

Voted into list alongside Great Wall, Machu Picchu, etc.

### Tourism

- 7+ million visitors annually
- Italy's most visited attraction
- Night lighting, guided tours
- Underground tours available (hypogeum)

### Conservation

Ongoing challenges:
- Pollution damage
- Subway vibrations
- Visitor wear
- Recent restoration (~$33 million, 2016)

## Cultural Impact

### Symbol

- Icon of Rome and Roman civilization
- Represents ancient power and spectacle
- Image on Italian euro coins

### Modern References

Gladiator (2000 film) renewed popular interest.

### "While the Colosseum stands..."

Medieval prophecy:
> "While the Colosseum stands, Rome shall stand; when the Colosseum falls, Rome shall fall; when Rome falls, the world shall fall."
