## Summary

Unidentified man who hijacked a Northwest Orient Airlines flight in 1971, extorted $200,000 ransom, and parachuted into the night over the Pacific Northwest. He was never found. It remains the only unsolved skyjacking in US aviation history.

## Case details

The hijacking took place on a commercial flight departing Portland, Oregon, and landing in Seattle, Washington. After landing, the hijacker negotiated the release of passengers in exchange for ransom and parachutes, then ordered the aircraft to take off again before jumping from the rear stairs of a Boeing 727.

## Evidence and search

- The ransom was provided as marked bills with recorded serial numbers.
- Extensive ground and aerial searches were conducted in Washington state, but no definitive trace of the hijacker was found.
- In 1980, a portion of the ransom money was discovered on the bank of the Columbia River, providing one of the few confirmed physical links to the crime.

## Investigation (FBI)

- The FBI case file was known internally as "NORJAK" (Northwest Hijacking).
- Over the decades, investigators evaluated numerous suspects, but none were conclusively identified as the hijacker.
- The case became a long-running example of the limits of forensic and investigative methods when physical evidence is scarce and early leads are ambiguous.

## Cultural legacy

- The story became a durable American true-crime mystery, inspiring books, documentaries, and fictional adaptations.
- "D.B. Cooper" is widely used as shorthand for an unknown fugitive who successfully disappears.

## Open questions

Commonly debated points include the hijacker's true identity, the exact jump location, and whether he survived the parachute escape. None of these questions has been resolved to a generally accepted historical conclusion.
