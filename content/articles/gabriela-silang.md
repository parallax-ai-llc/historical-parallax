## Summary

María Josefa Gabriela Cariño de Silang (March 19, 1731 – September 20, 1763) was a Filipino military leader who became the first Filipina to lead a revolt against Spanish colonial rule.[^1] After her husband Diego Silang's assassination in May 1763, she assumed command of the Ilocano independence movement, leading approximately 2,000 rebel troops for four months against Spanish forces.[^2] Known as the "Joan of Arc of Ilocandia," she fought valiantly to liberate the Ilocos region from centuries of colonial oppression before being captured and publicly executed by hanging at age 32.[^3] Her legacy continues to inspire the Philippine women's rights movement, with the country's leading feminist organization GABRIELA named in her honor.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

María Josefa Gabriela Cariño was born on March 19, 1731, in barrio Caniogan, Santa, Ilocos Sur, in the Spanish-colonized Philippines.[^5] Her father, Anselmo Cariño, was a Spanish-Ilocano mestizo trader who ferried his wares from Vigan to Abra along the Abra River, and was descended from Ignacio Cariño, the first Galician from Spain to arrive in Candon in the late 17th century.[^6] Her mother was a Tinguian woman from an indigenous barrio in San Quintin (now Pidigan, Abra), making Gabriela of mixed Spanish, Ilocano, and indigenous Tinguian heritage.[^7]

Gabriela's early childhood was marked by separation from her parents. Her father was Christian while her mother remained pagan, creating a cultural divide that would shape Gabriela's upbringing.[^8] She was separated from her pagan mother in early childhood and subsequently raised as a Christian by Father Tomas Millan, the town's parish priest, who considered her an adopted daughter.[^9] Despite this separation, Gabriela stayed in her mother's village at some time in her youth, during which she developed a strong affinity for the Tinguian tribespeople, learned their language, and became familiar with their customs.[^10]

Living in two worlds—her father's Christian, Spanish-influenced sphere and her mother's indigenous community—gave Gabriela a unique perspective on colonial Philippine society. Her father harbored ambitions for his daughter's future, hoping she would marry a wealthy man and secure her social position.[^11] Gabriela received an elementary level education at the town's convent school, a rare opportunity for women at that time, where Father Millan personally oversaw her Catholic upbringing and instruction.[^12] This education would prove invaluable, providing her with literacy and knowledge that few women of her era possessed.

At the time of Gabriela's birth, the Philippines had been under Spanish colonial rule for almost 200 years, and the Ilocano people faced systematic oppression through heavy taxation, forced labor for the construction of churches and government buildings, and the imposition of colonial monopolies.[^13] The seeds of resistance were being sown throughout the islands, and Gabriela's formative years coincided with growing resentment against Spanish abuses that would eventually explode into open rebellion.[^14]

## Rise to Power

Gabriela's path to revolutionary leadership began with personal tragedy and transformation. Around 1751, at approximately age 20, she entered into an arranged marriage with Don Tomas Millan, a wealthy and prominent businessman in Vigan, Ilocos.[^15] The marriage, arranged by her father or guardian, introduced her to the world of commerce and expanded her understanding of the economic and social structures of colonial Philippines.[^16] However, this union was brief—Millan died of old age shortly after their marriage, leaving Gabriela a widow at the age of 23.[^17]

Her widowhood marked a turning point. After being widowed by her first husband, Gabriela met Diego Silang, a charismatic revolutionary leader who would become both her second husband and her partner in rebellion.[^18] They married in 1757, and this union would prove to be one of the most consequential partnerships in Philippine revolutionary history.[^19] Diego Silang was already emerging as a vocal critic of Spanish colonial rule, having been imprisoned after suggesting to Spanish authorities that they abolish the tribute tax and replace Spanish functionaries with native administrators.[^20]

The opportunity for revolution arrived in 1762 during the Seven Years' War, when British naval forces seized Manila in October, creating chaos in the Spanish colonial administration.[^21] The British occupation inspired uprisings throughout the Philippines, particularly in the northern regions of Ilocos Norte and Cagayan.[^22] Diego Silang seized this moment to ally with British forces in an attempt to overthrow Spanish rule and establish an independent Ilocano state.[^23] The British even appointed Diego Silang as governor of the province his army was conquering, though British support proved largely ineffectual as they remained confined to Manila and Cavite.[^24]

On December 14, 1762, Diego Silang declared the independence of Ilocandia, naming the state "Free Ilocos" and proclaiming Vigan as its capital.[^25] During this revolutionary period, Gabriela became far more than a revolutionary's wife—she emerged as one of Diego's closest advisors and his unofficial aide-de-camp during skirmishes with Spanish troops.[^26] She participated in strategic planning, understood military tactics, and gained the trust and respect of the rebel soldiers who served under her husband's command.[^27]

The spirit of charity and philanthropy sown in Gabriela's childhood found fruition in her close collaboration with her husband in the cause against oppression.[^28] Her unique background—educated by Spanish priests yet connected to indigenous Tinguian warriors, comfortable in both elite Spanish circles and among common Ilocano farmers—made her an invaluable bridge between diverse groups within the revolutionary movement.[^29] She understood the grievances of the colonized masses who suffered under Spanish taxation, forced labor, and systematic abuse, while also possessing the education and social connections to navigate the complex political landscape of colonial resistance.[^30]

---

## Major Achievements

Gabriela Silang's most significant achievements came during the four months following her husband's assassination, when she assumed unprecedented leadership as the first Filipina to command a military revolt against colonial forces.[^31] On May 28, 1763, Diego Silang was assassinated by Miguel Vicos and Pedro Becbec, Spanish-Ilocano mestizos whom church authorities had paid to murder the revolutionary leader.[^32] On the pretext of showing a summary of tax collections, these assassins gained entry into the Silang house past unsuspecting guards, and Vicos shot Diego with a blunderbuss before the body was stabbed several times.[^33]

Having narrowly escaped assassination herself, Gabriela swore to avenge her husband's death and continue his mission of liberation.[^34] She initially fled to Tayum, now part of Abra, seeking refuge in the house of her paternal uncle, Nicolás Cariño, who temporarily took command of the rebel forces and gathered 2,000 men loyal to Diego.[^35] Rather than retreating into widowhood as Spanish authorities expected, Gabriela rallied the troops under the leadership of Nicolas Cariño and other trusted aides of Diego, withdrawing to Pidigan, Abra—her mother's town—in search of support from Tinguian mountain warriors.[^36]

Gabriela's ability to mobilize indigenous Tinguian fighters proved crucial to her military campaign. Drawing on the relationships she had cultivated during her youth in her mother's village, she successfully recruited from the local Tinguian community to bolster her forces against the Spanish.[^37] The Silangs' strategy to broaden their power base by attracting a combined force of inland Tinguian warriors and Ilocanos bore fruit, as evidenced by Spanish losses of 35 soldiers in a battle at Sitio Banaoang, where Gabriela commanded forces with Tinguian support.[^38]

She formally assumed her husband's role as commander of the rebel troops and achieved a "priestess" status amongst her community and followers, demonstrating both military and spiritual leadership.[^39] She appointed her first two generals, Miguel Flores and Tagabuen Infiel, demonstrating sophisticated organizational capabilities and political acumen.[^40] Her popular image as the bolo-wielding "La Generala" on horseback stems from this period, when she personally led troops into combat against Spanish colonial forces.[^41]

One of her early military successes came when she organized a force that launched a dramatic surprise attack against Becbec and his followers in the town of Santa, putting her enemies to flight and avenging her husband's assassination.[^42] She then joined with Cariño's forces in the town of Cabugao, where a fortress for the revolutionary army was established, creating a strategic base of operations.[^43]

On September 10, 1763, Gabriela led her most ambitious military operation—an assault on the fortified Spanish colonial capital of Vigan.[^44] She descended on the city with her troops, commanding more than 2,000 soldiers against a formidable Spanish force of more than 6,000 men.[^45] In the suburb of Bantay, Gabriela's army set fire to the houses of the town's elites before advancing on the walled town, demonstrating both tactical boldness and revolutionary zeal.[^46] She took the lead on several attacks against Spanish forces, achieving some victories though suffering many casualties on both sides.[^47]

Beyond military leadership, Gabriela's greatest achievement was breaking the gender barrier in Philippine revolutionary movements. In an era when women were expected to remain in domestic spheres, she commanded armies, made strategic decisions, and inspired thousands to risk their lives for independence.[^48] Her courageous leadership became a symbol for the importance of women in Filipino society and their struggle for liberation during colonization.[^49] She proved that women possessed the capability, courage, and strategic intelligence to lead major political and military movements, challenging centuries of patriarchal assumptions.[^50]

## Controversies and Criticisms

While Gabriela Silang is celebrated as a national hero, several aspects of her revolutionary campaign and historical legacy remain controversial or subject to criticism. The most significant controversy surrounds the strategic alliance that Diego Silang—and by extension, Gabriela—maintained with British forces during their rebellion.[^51] The British occupation of Manila in 1762 created the opportunity for the Silang revolt, and Diego accepted appointment as governor from the British occupiers.[^52] Critics argue this alliance represented a pragmatic but problematic decision to collaborate with one colonial power to overthrow another, rather than fighting for genuine independence from all foreign control.[^53]

The British alliance ultimately proved disastrous for the revolutionary cause. The British forces remained confined to Manila and Cavite in a deteriorating situation, unable to extend control over the islands and unable to make good on their promised support for the uprising led first by Diego and later by Gabriela.[^54] This strategic miscalculation left the Ilocano rebels vulnerable to Spanish counterattacks once colonial authorities regrouped. Some historians suggest the Silangs' dependence on British support demonstrated poor strategic planning and unrealistic expectations about foreign assistance.[^55]

Gabriela's military campaign also suffered from significant tactical failures. Her attack on Vigan on September 10, 1763, has been described as "the perfect setup for an ambush," where enemy archers commanded by her former foster parent Father Millan rained arrows on her army's flanks while her forces were hit from the front by cannon balls and musket fire.[^56] The terrified Tinguian warriors retreated in panic, and Gabriela's forces were overwhelmed by the better-equipped and more numerous Spanish troops.[^57] The military advance did not remain secret, suggesting failures in operational security that allowed Spanish forces to prepare an effective defense.[^58]

After the failed assault on Vigan, Gabriela was forced to retreat into the mountains of Abra, where she continued a guerrilla resistance but never regained the initiative.[^59] Her four-month campaign, while courageous, achieved no lasting military victories and failed to liberate any significant territory from Spanish control. Some historians note that her leadership, while symbolically important, lacked the military experience and strategic sophistication needed to defeat professional Spanish colonial forces.[^60]

The brevity of Gabriela's leadership—only four months from Diego's assassination to her capture—raises questions about the sustainability and organization of the movement she led. Unlike other independence movements that built lasting institutional structures, Gabriela's revolt remained dependent on personal charisma and loyalty to her martyred husband rather than creating enduring revolutionary institutions.[^61]

Another controversy involves her official recognition as a national hero. On November 15, 1995, Gabriela Silang was included among nine Filipino historical figures recommended for recognition as national heroes, including Jose Rizal, Andres Bonifacio, Emilio Aguinaldo, and others.[^62] However, no action was taken after the report was submitted to the Department of Education, Culture, and Sports on November 22, 1995, probably because any action might cause numerous requests for proclamation or trigger debates involving historical controversies about the heroes.[^63] This bureaucratic limbo reflects ongoing debates about how to formally recognize figures like Gabriela within the national pantheon.

Historical documentation of Gabriela's life remains incomplete and contested. Historians disagree on certain details of her early childhood because she lived over two centuries ago during an era when very few written records existed.[^64] Many sources show vague information about her childhood years, and her parents' lives are largely unknown.[^65] The challenge of separating historical fact from cultural interpretation, dramatization, and nationalist mythology complicates scholarly assessment of her actual achievements versus her symbolic importance.[^66]

Finally, the association of her name with contemporary political movements has generated controversy. The GABRIELA women's organization, founded in 1984 in her honor, takes explicitly progressive and often anti-government positions that some argue politicize Gabriela Silang's legacy in ways disconnected from her 18th-century context.[^67] This appropriation of historical symbols for modern political purposes remains a subject of ongoing debate in Philippine political discourse.

## Personal Life

Gabriela Silang's personal life was deeply intertwined with the political and social tumult of 18th-century colonial Philippines. Born into a mixed heritage family—Spanish Ilocano father and indigenous Tinguian mother—she navigated between multiple cultural worlds from childhood.[^68] Her early separation from her parents, particularly from her pagan mother, created both loss and opportunity, as she was raised by Father Tomas Millan, who provided her with an education unusual for women of that era.[^69]

Her first marriage to Don Tomas Millan around 1751 represented a conventional path for an educated young woman of mixed heritage.[^70] The arranged marriage to a wealthy businessman in Vigan aligned with her father's ambitions for her social advancement.[^71] Little is known about the personal dynamics of this marriage, though it introduced her to commercial life and the economic structures of colonial society.[^72] The union lasted only about three years before Millan's death from old age left her a widow at age 23, a status that paradoxically granted her more personal freedom than she would have enjoyed as a married woman.[^73]

Her second marriage to Diego Silang in 1757 transformed her life trajectory entirely. This union was not merely romantic but represented a deep political and ideological partnership.[^74] The relationship between Diego and Gabriela has been described as one of the greatest love stories in Philippine history—a partnership forged in shared commitment to liberation and justice.[^75] Unlike her first marriage, which followed conventional social expectations, her marriage to Diego emerged from mutual respect, shared values, and common purpose in the anti-colonial struggle.[^76]

During their marriage, Gabriela was far more than a supportive spouse. She served as Diego's closest advisor and confidante, participating actively in strategic planning and military operations.[^77] Their relationship challenged traditional gender roles, with Diego treating Gabriela as an intellectual and political equal at a time when women were typically excluded from public affairs.[^78] This partnership enabled Gabriela to develop the leadership skills, strategic thinking, and military knowledge that she would later employ when forced to assume command after Diego's assassination.[^79]

Gabriela's personality emerges from historical accounts as complex and multifaceted. She possessed courage that inspired soldiers to follow her into battle against overwhelming odds.[^80] Her ability to move between different cultural worlds—Spanish colonial society, Ilocano farming communities, and indigenous Tinguian tribes—suggests sophisticated social intelligence and adaptability.[^81] She could command respect from educated elites who knew her from her first marriage, while also earning the loyalty of indigenous mountain warriors who recognized her maternal Tinguian heritage.[^82]

The psychological burden Gabriela carried after witnessing her husband's assassination must have been immense. Rather than succumbing to grief or fear, she channeled her emotions into revolutionary action, swearing to avenge Diego's death and continue his mission.[^83] This transformation from grieving widow to military commander required extraordinary personal resilience and strength of character.[^84]

Her relationship with Father Millan adds another layer of complexity to her personal life. The priest who raised her as an adopted daughter and provided her education would later command archers against her forces during the assault on Vigan, creating a tragic confrontation between adoptive parent and adoptive daughter on opposite sides of the colonial conflict.[^85] This personal betrayal must have been devastating, yet Gabriela continued fighting despite facing someone who had once been a parental figure.[^86]

Gabriela never had children with either husband, though historical records are incomplete on this matter.[^87] Her legacy would be carried not through biological descendants but through the inspiration she provided to subsequent generations of Filipino women fighting for rights, justice, and national liberation.[^88]

## Legacy

Gabriela Silang's legacy extends far beyond her four months of military leadership, establishing her as an enduring symbol of Filipino resistance, women's empowerment, and anti-colonial struggle. Her martyrdom and courage made her a lasting example to women throughout the Philippines, inspiring subsequent generations to challenge patriarchal limitations and fight for justice.[^89]

The most visible manifestation of her legacy is the naming of the Philippines' leading grassroots women's alliance in her honor. GABRIELA (General Assembly Binding Women for Reforms, Integrity, Equality, Leadership and Action), founded in April 1984, advocates for women's rights and issues across the archipelago.[^90] The organization's choice to invoke Gabriela Silang's name reflects her status as the preeminent symbol of Filipino women's political activism and resistance to oppression.[^91] Through GABRIELA, her spirit continues to animate contemporary struggles for gender equality, economic justice, and human rights in the Philippines.[^92]

Her story, celebrated in poetry, song, literature, and drama, has inspired other women to leadership in revolutionary movements throughout Philippine history.[^93] The widow-as-revolutionary-leader archetype that Gabriela embodied found echoes in figures like "Santa" in the province of Leyte in 1862, and its modern counterpart in Corazon Aquino, who was elected the country's first woman president in 1986 after the assassination of her husband, opposition leader Benigno Aquino Jr.[^94] This genealogy of widows who transformed personal tragedy into political leadership traces directly back to Gabriela's pioneering example.[^95]

Multiple monuments and memorials across the Philippines ensure that Gabriela's memory remains physically present in the national landscape. In Makati City's Ayala Triangle, a monument sculpted by Jose M. Mendoza in 1971 depicts Gabriela on horseback, placed by the Zobel de Ayala Family and inaugurated by her great-granddaughter Gloria Cariño and great-grandson Mario Cariño Merritt.[^96] The Gabriela Silang Memorial Shrine in Santa, Ilocos Sur, located in Sitio Pideg, Magsaysay, features a dramatic statue of Gabriela on horseback brandishing a bolo, symbolizing her resolute spirit and warrior ethos.[^97] The shrine sits within the Northern Luzon Heroes Hill National Park, a protected area established on July 9, 1963, cementing her recognition as a national hero.[^98]

Additional monuments stand at the Tangadan Welcome Tunnel in San Quintin, Abra (built in 2000), and in the town plaza of Pidigan, Abra, reminding visitors of the gallant stand and ideals for which she and her husband fought and died.[^99] These physical memorials serve as sites of pilgrimage, education, and civic pride, ensuring that new generations encounter her story.[^100]

Gabriela has become an icon of women's empowerment, honored through monuments, literature, stamps, and the contemporary women's rights movement, with her life reminding us that true leadership knows no gender.[^101] A 1974 Philippine postage stamp featuring her image brought her story into homes across the country, while her inclusion in school curricula ensures Filipino children learn about her contributions to national history.[^102]

Her legacy has particular resonance for indigenous and mixed-heritage Filipinos. As someone of Tinguian-Spanish-Ilocano descent who moved fluidly between different cultural worlds, Gabriela represents the possibility of hybrid identities that embrace multiple heritages rather than being torn apart by them.[^103] For the Igorot and Tinguian peoples of the Cordillera region, she stands as proof that indigenous women possessed the capability and courage to lead major historical movements.[^104]

In the broader context of anti-colonial struggles, Gabriela Silang occupies a unique position as one of the few 18th-century female military commanders anywhere in the colonized world who led armed resistance against European imperial powers.[^105] Her story resonates with other anti-colonial heroines like Queen Nzinga of Angola, Rani Lakshmibai of India, and Nanny of the Maroons in Jamaica, forming part of a global narrative of women's resistance to colonialism.[^106]

The contemporary Filipino diaspora has embraced Gabriela as a symbol of national pride and resistance to oppression wherever Filipinos face discrimination or exploitation.[^107] Filipino-American communities, particularly those engaged in labor rights and immigrant justice organizing, frequently invoke her example as inspiration for their activism.[^108]

Perhaps most significantly, Gabriela's legacy challenges the historical invisibility of women's contributions to nation-building and independence struggles. By demonstrating that women could command armies, make strategic decisions, and inspire mass movements, she dismantled assumptions about female capabilities that had justified women's political exclusion for centuries.[^109] Her life provides historical evidence that women's leadership is not a modern phenomenon but has deep roots in Philippine history, predating contemporary feminist movements by more than two centuries.[^110]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and historians who view Gabriela Silang favorably emphasize her groundbreaking role as the first Filipina to lead a military revolt against colonial powers, an achievement that shattered gender barriers in 18th-century society.[^111] Feminist historians particularly celebrate her as proof that women possessed the capacity for political and military leadership long before modern women's rights movements articulated theoretical justifications for gender equality.[^112]

Contemporary Philippine historians often describe her as a symbol of resistance not only to foreign colonization but also to patriarchal oppression, making her story relevant to multiple liberation struggles simultaneously.[^113] Her ability to unite diverse constituencies—indigenous Tinguian warriors, Ilocano farmers, and former supporters of her husband—demonstrates sophisticated political skills that many scholars argue have been underappreciated due to her gender and the brevity of her leadership.[^114]

Scholars of anti-colonial movements place Gabriela within a global context of 18th-century resistance to European imperialism, noting that her contemporaneous leadership coincided with similar uprisings across the colonized world during the Seven Years' War, when metropolitan conflicts created opportunities for colonial subjects to challenge European rule.[^115] This contextualization elevates her from a local Philippine figure to a participant in worldwide anti-colonial struggles.[^116]

Many historians emphasize the courage required for Gabriela to assume military command after her husband's assassination, when she could reasonably have retreated to safety and obscurity.[^117] Her decision to continue the fight despite overwhelming odds reflects a principled commitment to liberation that scholars argue deserves recognition alongside male revolutionary leaders who are more commonly celebrated.[^118]

Educational advocates point to Gabriela's rare literacy and education as factors that enabled her leadership, using her example to argue for women's education as a prerequisite for social transformation.[^119] The fact that she received elementary education from a convent school when most women remained illiterate demonstrates how education empowered her to transcend conventional gender limitations.[^120]

Cultural historians celebrate how Gabriela's story has inspired Filipino literature, art, drama, and political organizing across more than two centuries, demonstrating the enduring power of her example.[^121] The fact that the Philippines' largest women's organization bears her name testifies to her continuing relevance for contemporary feminist movements.[^122]

Scholars of indigenous Philippine history emphasize her Tinguian heritage and her ability to mobilize mountain warriors, recognizing her as an important figure in indigenous resistance narratives rather than solely within lowland Christian Filipino nationalist historiography.[^123] This perspective challenges nationalist narratives that have sometimes minimized indigenous contributions to Philippine independence struggles.[^124]

### Negative Views

Critical historians raise several concerns about how Gabriela Silang's legacy has been constructed and deployed. Some scholars argue that the romanticization of her story obscures the practical failures of her military campaign, which lasted only four months and achieved no lasting territorial gains against Spanish forces.[^125] From this perspective, celebrating strategic failures simply because they were led by a woman applies a double standard that ultimately diminishes women's achievements by suggesting female leaders should be praised regardless of outcomes.[^126]

The controversial British alliance remains a significant point of criticism. Historians skeptical of the Silang revolt argue that collaborating with British colonial forces to overthrow Spanish colonial forces represented opportunism rather than genuine anti-colonial commitment.[^127] Diego and Gabriela's willingness to accept British appointment and support suggests they sought to replace one colonial master with another rather than pursuing true independence, according to these critics.[^128]

Some military historians argue that Gabriela's lack of formal military training and strategic experience contributed to tactical errors that doomed the Vigan assault and led to unnecessary casualties among her followers.[^129] The failure to maintain operational security, allowing Father Millan to prepare an ambush, demonstrated the limitations of charismatic leadership unsupported by professional military expertise.[^130] Critics suggest that her appointment of generals and organization of forces, while impressive for someone without formal training, remained inadequate against professional Spanish colonial troops.[^131]

Skeptical historians note the difficulty of separating documented historical facts about Gabriela from nationalist mythology constructed in later centuries.[^132] The scarcity of contemporary 18th-century sources means much of what is "known" about her personality, motivations, and even specific actions derives from oral traditions recorded decades or centuries later, raising questions about historical accuracy.[^133] The disagreements among historians about basic biographical details reflect the fragmentary nature of evidence about her life.[^134]

Some scholars question whether Gabriela exercised genuine independent leadership or primarily served as a figurehead continuing her deceased husband's movement.[^135] The fact that Nicolas Cariño and other male lieutenants held significant power during her four-month command raises questions about whether she truly commanded or merely symbolically represented the rebellion while men made actual strategic decisions.[^136]

Critics of nationalist historiography argue that the elevation of Gabriela Silang to heroic status serves contemporary political purposes—particularly feminist and progressive agendas—rather than reflecting her actual historical significance in the 18th century.[^137] From this perspective, she has been conscripted into modern political battles in ways that distort historical understanding of her context and motivations.[^138]

Conservative historians sometimes express discomfort with celebrating a figure who took up arms against established authorities and violated gender norms of her era, arguing that her example could be interpreted as encouraging disobedience to both political authority and traditional social structures.[^139] While this view is less prominent in contemporary scholarship, it reflects persistent tensions about how to evaluate historical figures who challenged the social orders of their times.[^140]

The controversy surrounding her incomplete recognition as an official national hero—recommended in 1995 but never formally proclaimed—reflects ongoing debates about her proper place in Philippine national historiography and whether her brief military leadership warrants the same recognition as figures like Jose Rizal who had longer and more complex historical impacts.[^141]

## Famous Quotes

> "I will lead the Ilocanos to free themselves. We will overthrow this government of unjust officials." - *Pamumunuan ko ang mga taga-Ilocos para palayain ang kanilang mga sarili. Ibabagsak natin ang gobyerno ng mga hindi makatarungang opisyal na ito.*

Statement attributed to Gabriela upon assuming command after Diego's assassination, rallying her followers to continue the independence struggle against Spanish colonial authorities.[^168]

> "Women are not just objects to be kept in the kitchen, they too have the right to fight for their country's freedom."

Statement attributed to Gabriela Silang on women's capabilities and rights to participate in revolutionary movements, challenging 18th-century gender norms that restricted women to domestic spheres.[^169]

> "I swear to avenge my husband's death and continue his mission of liberation."

Attributed oath taken by Gabriela after narrowly escaping the assassination that killed Diego Silang, committing herself to leadership of the Ilocano independence movement.[^170]
