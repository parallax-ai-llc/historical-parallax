## Summary

Joseph-Louis Lagrange (1736-1813) was an Italian-born French mathematician, physicist, and astronomer who fundamentally transformed mathematics and physics through his pioneering work in analytical mechanics, calculus of variations, number theory, and celestial mechanics.[^1] His monumental treatise *Mécanique analytique* (1788) revolutionized classical mechanics by creating an entirely analytical framework independent of geometric diagrams, laying the foundation for modern mathematical physics.[^2] Lagrange succeeded Leonhard Euler as Director of Mathematics at the Berlin Academy and later became one of the most honored scientists under Napoleon, being buried in the Panthéon in Paris.[^3] His legacy endures through Lagrangian mechanics, Lagrange points in orbital dynamics, and numerous mathematical concepts bearing his name.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Joseph-Louis Lagrange was born Giuseppe Lodovico Lagrangia on January 25, 1736, in Turin, capital of the Kingdom of Sardinia-Piedmont (now Italy).[^5] He came from a family of French origin on his paternal side—his great-grandfather had been a French cavalry captain who left France to serve the Duke of Savoy.[^6] His father, Giuseppe Francesco Lodovico Lagrangia, held the position of Treasurer of the Office of Public Works and Fortifications in Turin, while his mother, Teresa Grosso, was the only daughter of a medical doctor from Cambiano near Turin.[^7]

Lagrange was the eldest of eleven children, though only two survived to adulthood—a tragically common occurrence in the eighteenth century.[^8] The family had once been prosperous, but Lagrange's father squandered their wealth through ill-advised financial speculations.[^9] Reflecting on this later in life, Lagrange remarked philosophically: "If I had not inherited a fortune I should probably not have cast my lot with mathematics."[^10] The loss of family wealth paradoxically liberated him to pursue intellectual rather than commercial interests.

In 1750, at age fourteen, Joseph became a student at the University of Turin.[^11] Initially, his favorite subject was classical Latin, and he found Greek geometry rather dull, showing no particular enthusiasm for mathematics.[^12] His trajectory changed dramatically when, at age seventeen, he accidentally came across a 1693 paper by Edmond Halley on the use of algebra in optics.[^13] This encounter ignited his mathematical passion. Alone and largely self-taught, he threw himself into mathematical studies with extraordinary intensity—within a year of incessant work, he had become an accomplished mathematician.[^14]

His precocious talent was quickly recognized. In 1755, at just nineteen years old, Charles Emmanuel III appointed Lagrange as "Sostituto del Maestro di Matematica" (assistant professor of mathematics) at the Royal Military Academy of the Theory and Practice of Artillery in Turin, where he taught courses in calculus and mechanics.[^15] This remarkable appointment at such a young age testified to his exceptional abilities and marked the beginning of one of the most brilliant careers in the history of mathematics.

## Rise to Prominence

Lagrange's ascent to international recognition began with a series of letters he wrote to Leonhard Euler between 1754 and 1756, describing his revolutionary results on the tautochrone problem.[^16] In these communications, he outlined his "δ-algorithm," which led directly to the Euler–Lagrange equations of variational calculus.[^17] This correspondence between the young Lagrange and the established master Euler initiated a new branch of mathematics—the calculus of variations.[^18]

Euler's response demonstrated extraordinary generosity and scientific integrity. Although Euler had already arrived at many of the same conclusions independently, he recognized that Lagrange's methods were superior to his own.[^19] According to historical accounts, Euler withheld a paper he had previously written covering some of the same ground, allowing the young Italian time to complete his work and claim undisputed credit for the invention of the new calculus.[^20] This chivalrous act, though disputed by some historians, exemplified the highest ideals of scientific collaboration.

Despite attempts by Euler and Pierre Louis Maupertuis to persuade Lagrange to move to Berlin as early as 1756, the young mathematician shyly refused, feeling he would be overshadowed by Euler's presence.[^21] Instead, he remained in Turin, where in 1758 he helped establish the Royal Turin Academy of Sciences and became its leading mathematician.[^22]

Lagrange's international reputation solidified when he won the Grand Prix of the Paris Academy of Sciences in 1764 for his memoir on the libration of the Moon—the apparent wobble in the Moon's orientation as seen from Earth.[^23] This achievement was followed by another prize in 1766 for his work on the motion of Jupiter's satellites.[^24] He would go on to win or share Academy prizes in 1772, 1774, and 1778, establishing himself as one of Europe's foremost mathematical minds.[^25]

When Euler departed Berlin for Saint Petersburg in March 1766, the intellectual landscape shifted. Jean le Rond d'Alembert actively encouraged Lagrange to reconsider the Berlin position, and Frederick II himself wrote directly to the mathematician, expressing the wish of "the greatest king in Europe" to have "the greatest mathematician in Europe" resident at his court.[^26] On November 6, 1766, at the age of thirty, Lagrange accepted the position and succeeded Euler as Director of Mathematics at the Prussian Academy of Sciences in Berlin.[^27]

## Major Achievements

### Analytical Mechanics and the *Mécanique Analytique*

Lagrange's most monumental achievement was his *Mécanique analytique* (Analytical Mechanics), published in 1788 after more than a quarter-century of development.[^28] This revolutionary treatise offered the most comprehensive treatment of classical mechanics since Isaac Newton's *Principia*, published 101 years earlier.[^29] What made Lagrange's work extraordinary was not merely its comprehensiveness but its entirely novel approach: it transformed Newtonian mechanics from a geometrically-based science into a branch of pure analysis.[^30]

The *Mécanique analytique* famously contained not a single diagram—an astonishing feature for a work on mechanics, a field traditionally dominated by geometric reasoning.[^31] Lagrange boasted that his analytical methods required no figures whatsoever, demonstrating the power of algebra and calculus to illuminate physical phenomena. The work consolidated into one unified and harmonious system the scattered developments of contributors including Alexis Clairaut, d'Alembert, Pierre-Simon Laplace, Euler, and Johann and Jacob Bernoulli.[^32]

The foundation of Lagrange's approach was the principle of virtual work, from which he derived equations of motion using the calculus of variations.[^33] This method, now known as Lagrangian mechanics, reformulated classical mechanics by adopting energy rather than force as its basic ingredient, leading to more abstract equations capable of tackling more complex problems.[^34] The Lagrangian formulation proved far more powerful than Newton's second law for systems with constraints, multiple bodies, or non-Cartesian coordinate systems.[^35]

### Calculus of Variations

Lagrange is credited as the principal founder of the calculus of variations, though he built upon earlier work by Euler.[^36] His "method of variations" created systematic techniques for finding functions that extremize functionals—essentially, finding curves or surfaces that maximize or minimize some quantity.[^37] The Euler-Lagrange equations, which provide necessary conditions for a functional to have an extremum, became central to this field and found applications far beyond their original scope.[^38]

Lagrange extended these methods to include constraints through what became known as Lagrange multipliers—a technique now fundamental in optimization theory across mathematics, economics, engineering, and the physical sciences.[^39] This method allows the solution of constrained optimization problems by converting them into unconstrained problems in a higher-dimensional space.

### Celestial Mechanics and the Three-Body Problem

In celestial mechanics, Lagrange made groundbreaking contributions to the notorious three-body problem—the challenge of predicting the motion of three mutually gravitating bodies.[^40] While a general solution proved (and remains) impossibly complex, Lagrange discovered special-case solutions by restricting the bodies to move in the same plane and assuming one mass was negligible.[^41]

In 1772, he identified five special solutions to the circular restricted three-body problem, now known as the Lagrange points (or Lagrangian points), labeled L1 through L5.[^42] Leonhard Euler had earlier discovered the three collinear equilibrium points (L1, L2, and L3) in 1767, but Lagrange found two additional triangular points (L4 and L5) where gravitational and centrifugal forces balance.[^43] These points form equilateral triangles with the two massive bodies and represent stable equilibria when the mass ratio exceeds approximately 24.96.[^44]

The practical importance of Lagrange points has proven immense in the space age. The L1 point of the Earth-Sun system provides an uninterrupted view of the Sun and hosts the Solar and Heliospheric Observatory (SOHO).[^45] The L2 point hosted the Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe (WMAP), currently hosts the Planck spacecraft, and is the permanent home of the James Webb Space Telescope.[^46] Several planets have trojan asteroids near their L4 and L5 points; Jupiter alone has more than one million such trojans.[^47]

### Number Theory

In number theory, Lagrange proved several fundamental results that had eluded other mathematicians. His most famous achievement in this domain was the four-square theorem, proven in 1770 and published in 1772.[^48] This theorem states that every non-negative integer can be represented as the sum of four non-negative integer squares.[^49] For example, 23 = 1² + 2² + 3² + 3². While the result had been conjectured by Claude Gaspard Bachet de Méziriac in 1621 and investigated by Euler, it was Lagrange who finally provided a complete proof.[^50]

Lagrange also made significant contributions to the theory of continued fractions and published important papers on prime number theory.[^51] His work on algebraic equations demonstrated why equations of degree up to four could be solved with radicals, laying groundwork that would later inspire Évariste Galois's revolutionary group theory.[^52]

### Differential Equations and Analysis

Between 1772 and 1785, Lagrange produced a long series of papers that essentially created the science of partial differential equations as a systematic discipline rather than a collection of isolated techniques.[^53] He invented the method of variation of parameters for solving differential equations, a technique that remains central to modern differential equations courses.[^54]

Lagrange introduced the ∂ notation still used today for partial derivatives and created systematic methods for solving various classes of differential equations.[^55] In calculus, he developed novel approaches to interpolation and Taylor's theorem, including the Lagrange form of the remainder in Taylor series expansions.[^56]

### The Metric System

During the turbulent years of the French Revolution, Lagrange played a crucial role in developing the metric system of measurement.[^57] In May 1790, he was appointed to the committee of the Académie des Sciences to standardize weights and measures.[^58] When other commission members including the chemist Antoine Lavoisier, Jean-Charles de Borda, Laplace, Charles-Augustin de Coulomb, and Mathurin Jacques Brisson were purged during the Reign of Terror in 1793, Lagrange became chairman of the Commission for the Reform of Weights and Measures.[^59]

After Lavoisier's execution in 1794, it was largely Lagrange who influenced the commission's choice of the meter and kilogram units with decimal subdivision in 1799.[^60] He advocated for the decimal base and helped establish the meter as one forty-millionth of a terrestrial meridian, from which derived the kilogram (unit of mass) and the liter (unit of volume).[^61] The metric system's rational, decimal-based structure reflected Lagrange's commitment to analytical clarity and practical utility.

## Controversies and Criticisms

Unlike many historical figures of comparable stature, Lagrange's life was remarkably free from personal scandals or major controversies. His temperament was mild, he detested conflict, and he deliberately avoided the political and intellectual disputes that ensnared many of his contemporaries.[^62] However, certain aspects of his work and career attracted criticism, both during his lifetime and retrospectively.

### Failed Reform of Calculus Foundations

Lagrange's most significant intellectual failure was his attempt to place calculus on a purely algebraic foundation, eliminating infinitesimals and Newtonian limits.[^63] In his 1797 treatise *Théorie des fonctions analytiques* (Theory of Analytic Functions), he tried to derive calculus from the algebraic properties of power series expansions.[^64] He sought to define the derivative as the coefficient of the linear term in a Taylor series expansion, avoiding the conceptually problematic notion of limits.

While ingenious, this approach "did not suffice" in achieving its goal of rigorous foundations.[^65] The method contained circular reasoning and couldn't handle functions that lacked Taylor series expansions. Nevertheless, Lagrange's bold attempt inspired Augustin-Louis Cauchy, Niels Henrik Abel, and Karl Weierstrass in the following century to develop the modern epsilon-delta definition of limits and rigorous foundations for analysis.[^66] His failure was thus productive, clarifying what needed to be achieved even if his particular method proved inadequate.

### Limitations in Astronomical Work

In celestial mechanics, despite Lagrange's brilliance, some of his methods proved limited in practice. His technique for determining cometary orbits from three observations, while theoretically elegant, "has not indeed proved practically available" according to later assessments.[^67] The computational complexity and sensitivity to observational errors made it impractical for actual astronomical work.

Additionally, in 1806, Siméon Denis Poisson demonstrated that Lagrange's formulae for planetary motion led to certain limits for orbital stability that Lagrange himself had not fully recognized.[^68] This didn't invalidate Lagrange's work but showed its boundaries and the need for further refinement by subsequent generations of celestial mechanicians.

### Teaching Effectiveness

While Lagrange's lectures at the École Polytechnique were described by some mathematicians as "almost perfect both in form and matter,"[^69] other contemporary accounts were less flattering. Joseph Fourier, who attended Lagrange's lectures at the École Normale in 1795, noted that his voice was very feeble, he had a pronounced Italian accent (pronouncing 's' like 'z'), and while professors gave him enthusiastic reception, students welcomed him poorly.[^70]

The Danish astronomer Thomas Bugge, attending lectures at the École Polytechnique in 1799, wrote that while Lagrange's content "deserved the highest consideration," he was "too abstract for youth."[^71] Lagrange's highly analytical approach, brilliant for advancing mathematics, sometimes failed to engage or inspire students at the introductory level. His teaching style reflected his own mode of thinking—rigorously abstract and algebraic—which was not universally accessible.

### Episodes of Depression and Withdrawn Periods

Though not a criticism of his work, Lagrange's recurring battles with severe depression limited his productivity at several crucial junctures.[^72] After his first wife's death in 1783, he experienced profound melancholy.[^73] Upon moving to Paris in 1786, he fell into such deep depression that the printed copy of his *Mécanique analytique*—the culmination of twenty-five years of work—lay unopened on his desk for more than two years.[^74]

During these dark periods, he convinced himself that mathematics was obsolete and turned instead to botany and religion.[^75] While these episodes were understandable human responses to grief and displacement, they temporarily deprived the mathematical world of one of its brightest lights. Only his 1792 marriage to the devoted Renée-Françoise-Adélaïde Le Monnier and his appointment as commission president restored his energy and focus.[^76]

## Personal Life

### Family Background and Early Years

As the eldest of eleven children in a family of declining fortunes, Lagrange experienced both privilege and precarity in his youth.[^77] His father's financial mismanagement through speculation meant that Lagrange, unlike many aristocratic contemporaries, could not rely on inherited wealth.[^78] This circumstance, which might have proven devastating, instead freed him to pursue mathematics with single-minded dedication.

### First Marriage: Vittoria Conti

In September 1767, soon after his arrival in Berlin, Lagrange married Vittoria Conti, a distant cousin.[^79] Unfortunately, Vittoria's health declined soon after the marriage, and she remained an invalid until her death in 1783, after sixteen years of marriage.[^80] Lagrange grieved deeply for her and fell into a depression that profoundly affected his health and work.[^81] Friends noted that his nervous system never fully recovered its tone, and he suffered from recurrent attacks of severe melancholy for years afterward.[^82]

### Second Marriage: Renée-Françoise-Adélaïde Le Monnier

In 1792, an unlikely romance transformed Lagrange's life. Renée-Françoise-Adélaïde Le Monnier, the twenty-four-year-old daughter of his friend, the astronomer Pierre Charles Le Monnier, was moved by compassion for the fifty-six-year-old mathematician's profound sadness and timidity.[^83] She insisted on marrying him despite the forty-year age difference.[^84]

Lagrange was initially appalled by the disparity in their ages and reluctant to involve himself in the relationship.[^85] However, they married in 1792, and contrary to what skeptics might have predicted, it proved a remarkably happy union for both.[^86] Renée's devotion and emotional support restored Lagrange's stability and gave him great happiness in his final two decades.[^87] The couple had no children, but their relationship provided Lagrange with the domestic peace and affection that had eluded him after Vittoria's death.[^88]

### Personality and Temperament

Lagrange was described as nervous and timid in character, detesting controversy and willingly allowing others to take credit for what he had accomplished to avoid conflict.[^89] In appearance, he was of medium height and slight build, with pale blue eyes and a colorless complexion.[^90] He was mild-mannered and did his best to avoid arguments and politics—a wise strategy during the volatile years of the French Revolution.[^91]

He did not relish being the center of attention. When he moved from Berlin to Paris and Louis XVI sent him 4,000 livres to cover travel expenses, Lagrange told the friend who brought the news: "Here is a fine thing, but you must not speak about me any longer."[^92] This modesty and reticence were genuine aspects of his character, not false humility.

During his Berlin years, his health was rather poor, exacerbated by nine years of unceasing labor that seriously affected both his physical and mental well-being.[^93] Doctors warned that unless he took rest and exercise, they could not be responsible for his reason or life.[^94] His tendency toward overwork and mental exhaustion was a persistent challenge throughout his career, occasionally incapacitating him for extended periods.

## Legacy

### Foundation of Modern Mathematical Physics

Lagrange's work laid the foundations for much of modern mathematics and profoundly influenced subsequent generations of mathematicians and scientists.[^95] His groundbreaking contributions spanned mechanics, algebra, calculus, number theory, and analysis, synthesizing ideas from different branches into unified wholes that revealed unexpected connections.[^96]

His concentration on analytical methods liberated mathematics, for the first time since antiquity, from dependence on Greek geometry.[^97] By showing that mechanics could be developed purely through algebra and calculus without geometric diagrams, he opened new vistas for mathematical physics. Napoleon, who made Lagrange first Senator of the French Republic and then Count of the Empire, defined him as "the lofty pyramid of the mathematical sciences."[^98]

### Lagrangian Mechanics in Modern Physics

Lagrangian mechanics remains a cornerstone of modern physics, with applications extending far beyond its original scope in classical mechanics.[^99] The Lagrangian formulation proved essential for developing quantum mechanics, relativity theory, and modern field theory.[^100] The mathematician and physicist William Rowan Hamilton extended Lagrange's work into Hamiltonian mechanics, which provided the mathematical framework for quantum mechanics.[^101]

The principle of least action, which underlies Lagrangian mechanics, became a fundamental concept in many areas of theoretical physics, including quantum mechanics, quantum field theory, and general relativity.[^102] Remarkably, Lagrange himself anticipated this extension, stating that future physics might approach mechanics as a geometry of four dimensions—three spatial coordinates combined with time—precisely the framework Albert Einstein employed in 1916 for his general theory of relativity.[^103]

### Influence on Pure Mathematics

Lagrange's invention and development of the calculus of variations became a field in itself and a powerful tool for physicists and mathematicians who followed him.[^104] The Lagrange multiplier method is now taught in virtually every multivariable calculus course and applies to optimization problems across economics, engineering, operations research, and numerous other fields.[^105]

His work on algebraic equations, particularly his investigation of why equations up to degree four could be solved with radicals, laid groundwork for the development of group theory.[^106] Évariste Galois and Niels Henrik Abel built upon Lagrange's insights when proving that no general formula exists for solving fifth-degree and higher polynomial equations.

His systematic approach to differential equations transformed the field from a collection of clever tricks into a coherent science with general principles and methods.[^107] The variation of parameters technique, Lagrange's remainder formula, partial differential equation theory, and numerous other contributions remain central to mathematical education and research.[^108]

### Space Exploration and the Lagrange Points

The Lagrange points discovered in 1772 have become crucial to modern space exploration.[^109] These gravitationally stable positions allow spacecraft to maintain their positions relative to Earth and Sun with minimal fuel expenditure. Major space telescopes and observatories occupy these points: SOHO at L1, the James Webb Space Telescope at L2, and future missions planned for other Lagrange points.[^110]

The existence of trojan asteroids at Jupiter's L4 and L5 points, predicted by Lagrange's theory, provides natural laboratories for studying solar system formation and dynamics.[^111] Earth itself has trojan asteroids at its L4 and L5 points, discovered in recent decades—a testament to Lagrange's theoretical prediction made more than two centuries earlier.

### Honors and Commemorations

Lagrange received numerous honors during his lifetime and posthumous recognition. In his final years, Napoleon awarded him the Grand Officer of the Legion of Honour in 1808, made him a Count of the Empire, and just two days before his death in 1813, bestowed upon him the Grand Cross of the Imperial Order of the Reunion.[^112] He was buried in the Panthéon in Paris, France's highest honor, among the nation's greatest citizens.[^113]

His name appears on one of the 72 plaques commemorating prominent French scientists on the first stage of the Eiffel Tower.[^114] Rue Lagrange in Paris's 5th Arrondissement bears his name, as do the lunar crater Lagrange and the asteroid 1006 Lagrangea.[^115] The Prix Lagrange, awarded by the Academy of Sciences of Turin, continues to honor outstanding contributions to mathematical physics.[^116]

### Continued Relevance

More than two centuries after his death, Lagrange's methods continue to shape modern science and engineering.[^117] Whether in the study of planetary motion, the design of satellites and space missions, the development of quantum field theories, the optimization of economic systems, or the exploration of abstract mathematical structures, Lagrangian methods remain indispensable.[^118]

His ability to synthesize ideas from different branches of mathematics into unified frameworks set a model for mathematical research that continues to inspire.[^119] The elegance and generality of his approach—seeking the most abstract and powerful formulations—exemplifies the mathematical ideal of achieving maximum insight with minimum assumption.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Historical and contemporary assessments of Lagrange place him among the absolute greatest mathematicians of all time. The eminent mathematical historian Eric Temple Bell included Lagrange among the supreme mathematicians, ranking him alongside Archimedes, Newton, Euler, and Carl Friedrich Gauss.[^120] His ability to work across diverse mathematical domains—mechanics, analysis, algebra, number theory, celestial mechanics—with equally distinguished results is exceptionally rare.

Contemporaries recognized his brilliance and unique approach. D'Alembert, himself one of the eighteenth century's leading mathematicians, actively promoted Lagrange's career and considered him the natural successor to Euler.[^121] The fact that Frederick the Great sought him as "the greatest mathematician in Europe" and that both revolutionary France and Napoleon honored him demonstrates that his reputation transcended political upheavals.[^122]

Later mathematicians acknowledged their debts to Lagrange. Hamilton's development of Hamiltonian mechanics explicitly built upon Lagrangian mechanics, extending and reformulating Lagrange's principles.[^123] Cauchy, Galois, Abel, and Weierstrass, who revolutionized nineteenth-century mathematics, all drew inspiration from Lagrange's work, whether from his successful methods or his illuminating failures.[^124]

Modern physicists particularly honor Lagrange's transformation of mechanics. Richard Feynman, one of the twentieth century's greatest physicists, emphasized the importance of Lagrangian and Hamiltonian mechanics in his lectures, showing how they provide insights unavailable through Newtonian formulations.[^125] The Lagrangian density concept in quantum field theory—fundamental to the Standard Model of particle physics—descends directly from Lagrange's eighteenth-century innovations.[^126]

Scholars emphasize Lagrange's methodological contributions: his insistence on generality, his search for unifying principles, his demonstration that analysis could replace geometric intuition, and his ability to synthesize scattered results into coherent theories.[^127] These methodological advances arguably influenced mathematics as profoundly as any specific theorem or technique he discovered.

### Negative Views

Critical assessments of Lagrange, while respectful of his achievements, note certain limitations. His attempt to ground calculus on algebraic series expansions, while ingenious, was fundamentally flawed and potentially hindered the development of rigorous analysis by proposing an inadequate foundation.[^128] Some historians argue that Lagrange's influence in this regard was negative, delaying the proper epsilon-delta approach that Cauchy and Weierstrass later established.

His teaching, while admired by fellow mathematicians, struggled to reach students effectively.[^129] The complaint that he was "too abstract for youth" suggests that his profound analytical gifts created a gap between his understanding and that of beginners.[^130] Some educational historians view this as a cautionary tale about the distinction between research brilliance and pedagogical effectiveness.

Lagrange's extreme modesty and conflict avoidance, while personally admirable, sometimes meant that his ideas were not promoted as vigorously as they might have been.[^131] His willingness to let others take credit, his reluctance to engage in priority disputes, and his tendency to withdraw during depressive episodes meant that some of his insights received delayed recognition or were rediscovered by others.

His focus on analytical methods at the expense of geometric and physical intuition has been questioned by some commentators. While his purely analytical approach to mechanics was revolutionary and powerful, critics suggest it sometimes obscured physical insight.[^132] The complete absence of diagrams in *Mécanique analytique*, though a point of pride for Lagrange, made the work less accessible and potentially less intuitive than it might otherwise have been.

Some historians note that Lagrange, despite his brilliance, was less original than Euler or Newton in the sense of opening entirely new mathematical territories.[^133] His genius lay more in perfecting, systematizing, and unifying existing ideas rather than in the explosive originality of his greatest predecessors. This is a subtle criticism—systematization and unification are themselves achievements of the highest order—but it distinguishes his type of mathematical creativity from that of the most revolutionary thinkers.

## Famous Quotes

> "As long as algebra and geometry have been separated, their progress have been slow and their uses limited; but when these two sciences have been united, they have lent each mutual forces, and have marched together towards perfection." - On the unity of mathematical disciplines[^161]

> "Newton was the greatest genius that ever existed, and the most fortunate, for we cannot find more than once a system of the world to establish." - Expressing admiration for Isaac Newton[^162]

> "It took them only an instant to cut off that head, but France may not produce another like it in a century." - On the execution of Antoine Lavoisier during the French Revolution[^163]

> "If I had not inherited a fortune I should probably not have cast my lot with mathematics." - Reflecting on how the loss of his family's wealth paradoxically freed him to pursue mathematics[^164]

> "I regard as quite useless the reading of large treatises of pure analysis: too large a number of methods pass at once before the eyes. It is in the works of applications that one must study them; one judges their ability there and one apprises the manner of making use of them." - On mathematical pedagogy and the importance of applications[^165]

> "An ancient writer said that arithmetic and geometry are the wings of mathematics; I believe one can say without speaking metaphorically that these two sciences are the foundation and essence of all the sciences which deal with quantity." - On the fundamental branches of mathematics[^166]
