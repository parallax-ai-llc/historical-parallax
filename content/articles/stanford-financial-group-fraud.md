## Summary

$7 billion Ponzi scheme operated by Allen Stanford through his Antigua-based bank. He sold fraudulent certificates of deposit promising above-market returns.

## Background

Stanford Financial Group centered on Stanford International Bank, an offshore bank based in Antigua. The group marketed certificates of deposit (CDs) to investors, often through a large network of financial advisers in the United States and elsewhere.

## How the scheme worked (high level)

Investigators alleged that the bank’s reported investment performance and financial statements were materially false. Funds from new investors were used to pay purported returns to earlier investors while Stanford controlled large portions of the money.

## Investigation and collapse

In 2009, U.S. regulators filed civil actions and the firm’s assets were frozen, triggering a rapid collapse of the business. A court-appointed receiver was tasked with taking control of assets and pursuing recoveries for victims.

## Legal outcome and aftermath

Allen Stanford was convicted in U.S. federal court in 2012 on multiple fraud-related counts and received a lengthy prison sentence. Recovery efforts continued for years through asset sales, litigation, and claims processes for affected investors.

## Significance

The case became a major example of the risks investors can face with opaque offshore structures and “too-good-to-be-true” yield claims, and it contributed to heightened scrutiny of complex wealth-management products and cross-border banking oversight.
