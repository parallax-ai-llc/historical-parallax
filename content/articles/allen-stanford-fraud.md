## Summary

American financier who ran a $7 billion Ponzi scheme through his Stanford International Bank in Antigua. He was a prominent cricket sponsor who was knighted by Antigua before his fraud was uncovered.

## Overview

The Stanford fraud case is typically described as a large-scale investment deception centered on certificates of deposit marketed through entities associated with Stanford International Bank. The scheme attracted attention not only for its size but also for its international footprint, involving offshore banking and cross-border regulation.

## How the scheme was marketed

Public reporting on the case often highlights recurring marketing themes:

- Promises of unusually consistent returns.
- Claims of safety and exclusivity.
- Reliance on personal relationships with brokers and clients.

## Enforcement and prosecution

U.S. authorities (including the SEC) pursued civil and criminal actions that alleged pervasive misrepresentation of assets and business operations. The criminal conviction and long sentence are commonly cited in discussions of deterrence for major financial fraud.

## Aftermath

The collapse of Stanford-related entities triggered complex recovery efforts, including receivership, claims processes, and litigation. Like many fraud cases, a central issue was the mismatch between investor expectations and recoverable assets.

## Broader significance

The case is frequently referenced in conversations about:

- Offshore financial centers and regulatory oversight.
- Due diligence failures and the limits of third-party audits.
- The social and political influence of wealthy sponsors in sports and philanthropy.
