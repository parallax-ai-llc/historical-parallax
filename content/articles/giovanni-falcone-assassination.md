## Summary

Murder of anti-Mafia judge Giovanni Falcone on May 23, 1992 when Cosa Nostra detonated a massive bomb on the A29 motorway near Capaci. His wife and three bodyguards were also killed.

## Background

Giovanni Falcone was a leading Italian magistrate known for pioneering investigative approaches against the Sicilian Mafia, including financial tracing and coordinated prosecution strategies. His work helped build large-scale cases that treated organized crime as an integrated enterprise.

## The Capaci Bombing

The assassination involved a large explosive device detonated under a motorway, targeting Falcone’s convoy. The scale of the attack signaled an intent to intimidate the state and to demonstrate the Mafia’s capacity for spectacular violence.

## Immediate Aftermath

Public reaction to Falcone’s death was intense, and the event is widely regarded as a turning point in Italian anti-Mafia mobilization. It strengthened public demands for decisive action and became a rallying symbol for civil society efforts against organized crime.

## Legal Consequences

Major Mafia attacks in this period spurred intensified investigations, prosecutions, and policy responses. Italian authorities pursued perpetrators through complex criminal trials, with multiple figures ultimately held responsible in court proceedings.

## Legacy

- Falcone’s methods influenced modern organized-crime prosecution
- The attack is commemorated as a defining event in Italy’s anti-Mafia history
- It highlighted the risks faced by magistrates, police, and witnesses confronting entrenched criminal organizations

The event is frequently discussed alongside subsequent attacks in 1992 as part of a broader campaign of Mafia violence.
