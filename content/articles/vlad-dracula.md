## Summary

Wallachian prince known as Vlad the Impaler who killed an estimated 40,000 to 100,000 people, often by impalement. He inspired Bram Stoker's Dracula and is a national hero in Romania.

## Additional Context

## Historical Setting

Vlad III (often called Vlad the Impaler) ruled Wallachia in a period when the region was contested between local dynasties and the expanding Ottoman Empire.

His reputation is shaped by overlapping factors:

- Warfare and frontier politics.
- Internal struggles among Wallachian elites.
- Propaganda and hostile pamphlets produced by rivals.

## Violence and Reputation

Descriptions of mass impalement and extreme punishments appear in multiple historical sources, but the exact figures and details vary widely.

Modern summaries often note two parallel realities:

- Vlad used terror as a political and military tool.
- Later accounts amplified brutality, sometimes for ideological or sensational purposes.

## Relationship to the Dracula Myth

The link between Vlad and the fictional vampire Dracula is indirect.

- Bram Stoker used the name "Dracula" and drew on general Eastern European associations.
- The modern cultural fusion of Vlad III with a supernatural vampire is largely a later popular interpretation.

## Legacy

Vlad remains a contested figure:

- In Romanian nationalism, he is often portrayed as a defender of sovereignty.
- In broader European memory, he is frequently portrayed as an archetype of cruelty.

His afterlife in literature, film, and tourism has made him one of the most recognizable medieval rulers worldwide.
