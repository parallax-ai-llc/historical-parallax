## Summary

Daniel Kahneman was an Israeli-American psychologist who fundamentally transformed the understanding of human judgment, decision-making, and economic behavior. Though trained as a psychologist, he was awarded the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences in 2002 — the first psychologist to receive the honor — for his work demonstrating that human beings are systematically irrational in predictable ways.

His decades-long collaboration with Amos Tversky produced Prospect Theory and a catalog of cognitive biases that challenged the dominant economic model of the rational actor. His 2011 bestseller *Thinking, Fast and Slow* brought these ideas to a global audience, selling millions of copies and reshaping fields from public policy to medicine to finance.

Kahneman died on March 27, 2024, at the age of 90, leaving behind a body of work that permanently altered how scientists, policymakers, and the public think about the human mind.

## Early Life & Background

Daniel Kahneman was born on March 5, 1934, in Tel Aviv, then part of British Mandatory Palestine, while his mother was visiting family. He grew up in Paris, France, where his father worked as a chemist. The family was Jewish, and Kahneman's early childhood was spent hiding from Nazi persecution during the German occupation of France — an experience he later cited as formative in his fascination with human psychology and how people perceive and judge others.

After the war, the family emigrated to British Palestine in 1948. Kahneman completed his undergraduate degree in psychology and mathematics at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem in 1954, then served in the Israeli Defense Forces, where he worked on psychological assessment of officer candidates. He earned his PhD in psychology from the University of California, Berkeley, in 1961.

## Rise to Prominence

Kahneman joined the faculty of Hebrew University after completing his doctorate. In the late 1960s, he began collaborating with fellow psychologist Amos Tversky, a partnership that would prove extraordinarily productive. Together they published a landmark 1974 paper in *Science*, "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases," identifying systematic mental shortcuts — anchoring, availability, representativeness — that lead to predictable errors in judgment.

Their 1979 paper introducing Prospect Theory, published in *Econometrica*, described how people evaluate potential losses and gains asymmetrically, with losses weighing roughly twice as heavily as equivalent gains. It became one of the most cited papers in economics.

Kahneman held positions at the University of British Columbia, University of California, Berkeley, and Princeton University, where he was Eugene Higgins Professor of Psychology and Professor of Public Affairs until his retirement.

## Key Achievements

- **Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences, 2002**, shared with Vernon Smith, for integrating psychological insights into economic science.
- Co-development of **Prospect Theory** (1979), a foundational framework in behavioral economics.
- *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011), which popularized the "System 1 / System 2" model of cognition, sold over 10 million copies worldwide.
- Presidential Medal of Freedom, awarded by President Barack Obama in 2013.
- Co-founded the field of **behavioral economics** alongside Tversky and Richard Thaler.

## Controversies & Criticism

Kahneman was candid about problems within his own field. In 2012 he wrote an open letter to researchers warning of a "train wreck" of irreproducibility in priming research — work that had appeared to support some of his ideas. Several studies underpinning the "cognitive ease" and priming sections of *Thinking, Fast and Slow* failed to replicate, and Kahneman publicly acknowledged that he had been too credulous.

His collaboration with psychologist Gary Klein, which produced the concept of "adversarial collaboration," was itself an attempt to reconcile conflicting findings on expert intuition — demonstrating his commitment to intellectual honesty over defending prior positions.

## Legacy & Influence

Kahneman's work fundamentally changed economics, medicine, law, and public policy. Behavioral economics, built substantially on his research, directly informed the UK's Behavioural Insights Team ("nudge unit") and similar government bodies worldwide. Richard Thaler, who won the 2017 Nobel Prize in Economics, credited Kahneman and Tversky as essential precursors.

His framework for understanding cognitive bias is now standard curriculum in business schools, medical training programs, and policy institutes globally. The concept of "loss aversion" alone has reshaped how financial products are designed and how health interventions are communicated.

He leaves a rare legacy: a scientist whose core ideas became common intellectual currency within his own lifetime.
