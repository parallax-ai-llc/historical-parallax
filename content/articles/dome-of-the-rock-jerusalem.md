## Summary

The Dome of the Rock (Arabic: Qubbat al-Sakhra) is an Islamic shrine located on the Temple Mount (known in Arabic as the Haram al-Sharif, or "Noble Sanctuary") in the Old City of Jerusalem. Completed in 691 CE under the Umayyad Caliph Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan, it is one of the oldest extant works of Islamic architecture and arguably the most recognizable monument in Jerusalem. The structure's distinctive golden dome, blue and green tilework, and octagonal form have made it an enduring symbol of the city.

The Dome of the Rock is the third holiest site in Islam, after Masjid al-Haram in Mecca and Al-Masjid an-Nabawi in Medina. The shrine encases the Foundation Stone — also known as the Sakhra — which is sacred to Jews, Christians, and Muslims alike. For Muslims, this rock is where the Prophet Muhammad began his miraculous Night Journey (Isra and Mi'raj) to heaven. For Jews, it is believed to be the site of the Holy of Holies of the Jewish Temple, and possibly the location where Abraham prepared to sacrifice his son. The Temple Mount on which it stands is considered the holiest site in Judaism.

The Dome of the Rock is not a mosque — it is a shrine (maqam) — and functions as a place of contemplation and veneration rather than congregational prayer. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the Old City of Jerusalem.

## History & Origins

The history of the site predates Islamic rule by millennia. The Temple Mount was the location of the First Temple, built by King Solomon around 950 BCE, which was destroyed by the Babylonian king Nebuchadnezzar II in 586 BCE. The Second Temple was subsequently built around 516 BCE, dramatically expanded by Herod the Great beginning around 20 BCE, and destroyed by Roman legions under Titus in 70 CE. The destruction of the Second Temple remains the central trauma of Jewish history, mourned annually on Tisha B'Av.

After the Roman destruction, Jerusalem was rebuilt as the Roman city Aelia Capitolina, and the Temple Mount was used for pagan shrines and later left largely desolate during the Byzantine Christian period. The Byzantine Empire controlled Jerusalem for centuries, with Christian churches dominating the city's religious landscape.

In 638 CE, Muslim forces under Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab captured Jerusalem from the Byzantines. According to accounts from this period, Umar visited the Temple Mount, which he found being used as a rubbish dump, and ordered it cleaned. He established a simple place of prayer on the mount, which the Jewish population reportedly assisted in identifying.

The formal construction of the Dome of the Rock was commissioned by the Umayyad Caliph Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan in 685 CE and completed in 691 CE. Historians have debated the political motivations behind the project. One influential theory, advanced by historian S.D. Goitein among others, suggests that Abd al-Malik built the shrine partly to divert pilgrims from Mecca and Medina, which were then controlled by his political rival Abd Allah ibn al-Zubayr. Others emphasize the desire to demonstrate the supremacy of Islam over Christianity and Judaism through a monument that deliberately rivaled and surpassed the nearby Church of the Holy Sepulchre.

The construction was entrusted to Byzantine architects and craftsmen — a fact reflected in the building's mosaic decoration, which draws heavily on late antique Byzantine artistic traditions while transforming them for an Islamic context. The inscription running along the inner octagonal arcade is one of the oldest surviving monumental inscriptions in Arabic and contains verses affirming the oneness of God and denying the Christian doctrine of the Trinity.

The building was restored and modified by subsequent rulers. The Abbasid Caliph al-Mamun had the name of Abd al-Malik removed from the building inscription and his own inserted — but forgot to update the date, preserving the original construction date in an act of pious fraud.

The Crusaders captured Jerusalem in 1099 and converted the Dome of the Rock into a Christian church, known as the Templum Domini (Temple of the Lord). The Knights Templar used the adjacent Al-Aqsa Mosque as their headquarters, which is one explanation for their name. Muslim rule was restored under Saladin in 1187, who had the cross atop the dome replaced with a crescent. The Mamluk and Ottoman periods brought further restorations and embellishments.

The distinctive tilework covering the exterior of the drum and the lower octagonal walls was added during the Ottoman period. Suleiman the Magnificent replaced the original mosaics on the exterior with Iznik ceramic tiles in 1545–1552, and the current tiles (which replaced those Ottoman tiles) date from a 1960s restoration under the Jordanian government.

The golden dome itself has gone through several incarnations. The current aluminum dome was covered in gold leaf in 1993 after the Jordanian King Hussein sold his residence in London to fund the gilding.

## Religious Significance

For Muslims, the Dome of the Rock marks the spot where the Prophet Muhammad ascended to heaven during the miraculous Night Journey (Isra and Mi'raj). According to the Quran (17:1) and subsequent hadith tradition, in approximately 621 CE, Muhammad was transported from the Masjid al-Haram in Mecca to the "Furthest Mosque" (al-Masjid al-Aqsa) in Jerusalem in a single night, and from Jerusalem he ascended through the seven heavens, ultimately reaching the presence of God. Islamic tradition identifies the Foundation Stone as the site of this ascent, and marks on the rock are attributed to the impression of the Prophet's foot.

For Jews, the Temple Mount and the Foundation Stone beneath the Dome of the Rock are of the highest possible religious significance. The Holy of Holies — the innermost sanctuary of the ancient Temple where the Ark of the Covenant was kept and where only the High Priest could enter, once a year on Yom Kippur — is believed to have stood on or near this rock. The destruction of the Temple and the inability of Jews to access and rebuild this site has been one of the defining features of Jewish religious consciousness for two millennia.

For Christians, the site is significant as the location of the Temple where Jesus taught, where he drove out the money changers, and where he predicted the Temple's destruction. The site also features in end-times theology across multiple Christian traditions.

This overlap of sacred claims makes the Temple Mount/Haram al-Sharif one of the most contested sacred spaces in the world, and the question of sovereignty over it has been a central issue in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.

## Architecture & Description

The Dome of the Rock is considered one of the masterpieces of world architecture. The building follows a complex plan: an inner circular structure is surrounded by an octagonal ambulatory, itself surrounded by an outer octagonal wall. The central rotunda is surmounted by the iconic dome, which rises to approximately 20 meters in diameter and 25 meters in height above the drum.

The exterior is richly decorated with Ottoman ceramic tiles in shades of blue, turquoise, green, white, and black, arranged in intricate geometric and floral patterns. The upper drum below the dome is covered in gilt aluminum. Four doorways in the cardinal directions provide access to the interior, framed by elaborate marble and tilework.

The interior is among the most beautiful in Islamic architecture. The inner and outer octagonal arcades are adorned with extraordinarily fine Byzantine-style mosaics of stylized vegetation, crowns, and jewels — with the deliberate absence of human or animal figures demanded by Islamic aesthetic principles. The mosaics of the inner ambulatory face are considered among the finest examples of early Islamic art. Columns of Byzantine marble support the arcades, their capitals reused from earlier structures.

The Foundation Stone (Sakhra) itself occupies the central space beneath the dome. It is an irregular piece of natural bedrock roughly 13.5 by 17.7 meters. Beneath the rock is a small cave accessible by stairs, known as the "Well of Souls" (Bir al-Arwah), where Islamic tradition holds that the souls of the dead await the Day of Judgment.

## Pilgrimage & Rituals

The Dome of the Rock is accessible to Muslim worshippers during regular hours, and also accessible to non-Muslim visitors at designated times (though non-Muslims are not permitted to enter the interior). The site is administered by the Jordanian-controlled Islamic Waqf.

For Muslim pilgrims, a visit to the Dome of the Rock and the broader Haram al-Sharif complex is a deeply meaningful act of devotion. The experience typically includes prayer at Al-Aqsa Mosque (also located on the platform), circumambulation of the Dome of the Rock, and reflection at the Foundation Stone.

Jewish religious law has historically been debated regarding whether Jews should ascend the Temple Mount, due to the risk of inadvertently entering the area of the Holy of Holies in a state of ritual impurity. The Chief Rabbinate of Israel has generally ruled against ascent, though an increasing number of rabbis and religious nationalists have encouraged it. Since 1967, Israeli law has technically granted Jews the right to ascend the Temple Mount, but prayer by non-Muslims is officially prohibited and has been a repeated flashpoint for violence.

## Modern Significance

The Dome of the Rock stands at the intersection of the most sensitive territorial and religious disputes in modern geopolitics. Since 1967, when Israel captured the West Bank including East Jerusalem during the Six-Day War, the status of the Temple Mount/Haram al-Sharif has been among the most contentious issues in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict and in broader relations between the Muslim world and Israel.

Under the current "status quo" arrangement, Israel controls security on the Temple Mount but day-to-day administration is left to the Jordanian Waqf. Jewish and other non-Muslim visitors may ascend during certain hours but may not pray openly. Any perceived attempt to change this status quo has triggered significant unrest: the visit of Ariel Sharon to the Temple Mount in September 2000 is widely cited as a trigger for the Second Intifada.

The Dome of the Rock was inscribed as part of the UNESCO World Heritage Site for the Old City of Jerusalem in 1981, at the request of Jordan. The site's exceptional universal value is recognized across religious and cultural traditions worldwide.

## Timeline

- **c. 950 BCE**: Solomon's Temple (First Temple) built on the Temple Mount.
- **586 BCE**: First Temple destroyed by Babylonian king Nebuchadnezzar II.
- **516 BCE**: Second Temple completed.
- **c. 20 BCE–64 CE**: Herod the Great dramatically expands the Second Temple complex.
- **70 CE**: Romans destroy the Second Temple; the Western Wall is all that remains of Herod's platform.
- **638 CE**: Muslim forces under Caliph Umar capture Jerusalem; the Temple Mount is cleaned and used for prayer.
- **685–691 CE**: Umayyad Caliph Abd al-Malik commissions and completes the Dome of the Rock.
- **1099**: Crusaders capture Jerusalem; the Dome of the Rock is converted to a Christian church.
- **1187**: Saladin recaptures Jerusalem; the Dome of the Rock is restored to Islamic use.
- **1545–1552**: Ottoman Sultan Suleiman the Magnificent re-tiles the exterior with Iznik ceramics.
- **1948**: Jordan gains control of East Jerusalem and the Old City.
- **1967**: Israel captures East Jerusalem; the status quo arrangement for the Temple Mount is established.
- **1993**: King Hussein of Jordan funds the gilding of the dome with gold leaf.
- **2000**: Ariel Sharon's visit to the Temple Mount triggers the Second Intifada.
- **Present**: The site remains under Jordanian Waqf administration; a central flashpoint in Israeli-Palestinian tensions.
