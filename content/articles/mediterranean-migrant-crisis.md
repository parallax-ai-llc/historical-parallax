## Summary

Ongoing crisis where thousands of migrants and refugees die attempting to cross the Mediterranean Sea from North Africa to Europe. Over 26,000 have died since 2014, making it the deadliest sea crossing in the world.

## Context

The Mediterranean routes became a major corridor for irregular migration and refugee movements, especially during the 2010s. The term “crisis” is used to describe both the scale of movement and the repeated loss of life at sea.

## Common drivers cited in reporting

- Armed conflict and political persecution.
- Economic insecurity and lack of safe legal pathways.
- Family reunification efforts and long-term displacement.

## Routes and risks (high-level)

- Multiple sea routes connect North Africa and the Eastern Mediterranean to Southern Europe.
- Risks include unseaworthy vessels, overcrowding, exploitation by smugglers, and delayed rescue.

## Policy and operational responses

- Search-and-rescue operations have alternated between expansion and restriction over time.
- European and regional policies have debated border control, asylum processing, and responsibility-sharing.
- Humanitarian NGOs have played visible roles in rescue and assistance, sometimes amid political controversy.

## Why it matters

- The crisis sits at the intersection of humanitarian law, asylum policy, maritime safety, and domestic politics.
- Long-run outcomes depend on conditions in origin countries, legal migration options, and coordinated regional responses.
