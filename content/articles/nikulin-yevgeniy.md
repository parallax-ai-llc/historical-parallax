## Notable Incidents (High-Level)

- **LinkedIn (2012 breach; data later widely circulated):** Commonly reported as involving a very large number of user records and password hashes, which later enabled password-reuse attacks across other services.
- **Dropbox (2012-era credential theft; later disclosed at scale):** Often discussed in the context of credential reuse and secondary compromise rather than a single isolated system exploit.
- **Formspring (early-2010s compromise):** Frequently cited alongside other consumer-service breaches from the same period.

## Why These Breaches Mattered

- Large credential datasets can be repurposed for:
  - **Credential stuffing** (automated login attempts on unrelated services)
  - **Targeted account takeover** when users reuse passwords
  - **Phishing and social engineering** using real account metadata
- Even when passwords are hashed, weak hashing or predictable passwords can lead to recovery and reuse.

## Common Legal/Investigative Framing (Context)

- Cases of this type are typically prosecuted under U.S. federal statutes related to:
  - unauthorized access to protected computers
  - wire fraud / identity theft-related theories (case-dependent)
- Investigations often rely on a mix of:
  - infrastructure and log correlation
  - seized devices/accounts
  - witness testimony and forensic analysis
