## Summary

King Sejong the Great (1397-1450) was the fourth monarch of Korea's Joseon Dynasty, ruling from 1418 to 1450, and is universally regarded as the greatest king in Korean history.[^1] His reign marked the Golden Age of Korean culture, characterized by unprecedented advancements in science, technology, literature, and music.[^2] Sejong is best remembered for creating Hangul, the Korean alphabet, which democratized literacy and became a cornerstone of Korean identity.[^3] Despite suffering from chronic health problems throughout his life, he sponsored countless innovations including the rain gauge, astronomical instruments, and comprehensive administrative reforms.[^4] His legacy endures in modern Korea, where he appears on the 10,000-won note and numerous monuments bear his name.[^5]

## Early Life and Background

Born Yi To on May 15, 1397, Sejong was the third son of King Taejong, the third monarch of the Joseon Dynasty.[^6] His birth position as the third son made his eventual ascension to the throne highly unlikely according to succession customs of the time.[^7] However, Sejong distinguished himself from an early age through his exceptional intelligence and scholarly dedication, qualities that would ultimately change his fate.[^8]

Sejong's father, Taejong (personal name Yi Bangwon), had himself risen to power through violent means during the Strife of Princes, making him acutely aware of succession crises.[^9] By 1406, Taejong had decided to abdicate while still alive to ensure a smooth transition of power.[^10] Sejong's oldest brother, Grand Prince Yangnyeong, was initially designated as heir apparent, but he proved unsuitable for kingship, frequently disobeying the king, neglecting his studies, and engaging in scandalous behavior.[^11]

Various records in the Veritable Records of Taejong indicate that young Sejong, known then as Prince Chungnyeong, was recognized as exceptionally intelligent and studious by both the king and members of the court.[^12] In mid-1418, after years of disappointment with Yangnyeong's conduct, Taejong deposed the crown prince and designated the 21-year-old Sejong as his successor.[^13] Just months later, Taejong abdicated, and Sejong was crowned king at the age of 22, beginning what would become one of the most illustrious reigns in Korean history.[^14]

Sejong's early reign was marked by a traumatic event when his father Taejong sentenced Sejong's father-in-law Shim On to death and demoted his mother-in-law to a government slave shortly after his ascension.[^15] This harsh action, likely intended to eliminate potential threats to the throne, cast a shadow over the beginning of Sejong's kingship but also demonstrated the ruthless political realities of Joseon court life.[^16]

Despite this difficult start, Sejong quickly established himself as a learned Confucian scholar with a deep commitment to improving the lives of his subjects.[^17] His educational background emphasized Confucian philosophy, which would profoundly influence his governance philosophy centered on benevolent rule and the welfare of the common people.[^18]

## Rise to Power

Sejong's path to the throne was neither direct nor inevitable. The succession crisis that brought him to power began with his older brother Yangnyeong, who had been groomed for kingship but consistently failed to meet expectations.[^19] Taejong's twelve sons created a complex succession landscape, but only Yangnyeong and eventually Sejong were seriously considered for the throne.[^20]

The transformation of Sejong from third prince to crown prince occurred in 1418, when Taejong finally concluded that Yangnyeong was irredeemable as a future monarch.[^21] This decision was not made lightly, as deposing a crown prince created political instability and challenged Confucian principles of primogeniture.[^22] However, Taejong prioritized the stability of the dynasty over rigid adherence to succession customs.[^23]

When Sejong ascended to the throne at 22, he inherited a relatively stable kingdom that his father and grandfather had consolidated through military might and political purges.[^24] However, Taejong continued to exert considerable influence as king emeritus until his death in 1422, creating a period where Sejong technically ruled but operated under his father's shadow.[^25] This four-year apprenticeship allowed Sejong to learn statecraft while having the security of his father's experience and authority to back his decisions.[^26]

After Taejong's death in 1422, Sejong emerged as a monarch in his own right and began implementing his vision for governance.[^27] Unlike his father and grandfather, who had risen through military prowess and political violence, Sejong wielded power through scholarship, innovation, and administrative reform.[^28] He established the Hall of Worthies (Jiphyeonjeon) in 1420, even before his father's death, signaling his commitment to intellectual pursuits and evidence-based policy making.[^29]

The Hall of Worthies became Sejong's primary instrument for consolidating power through knowledge rather than force.[^30] He staffed it with talented scholars including Jeong In-ji, Jeong Chang-son, Shin Sook-ju, and Seong Sam-moon, who would become instrumental in his various projects.[^31] Modern historians have likened the institution to a think tank, responsible for overseeing cultural and intellectual pursuits, governance issues, and the education of the king and crown prince.[^32]

By creating this scholarly apparatus, Sejong built a new power base that was loyal to him personally and shared his vision of enlightened governance.[^33] This strategic move allowed him to pursue ambitious reforms that might have faced resistance from traditional court factions.[^34]

## Major Achievements

### Creation of Hangul

King Sejong's most enduring achievement was the invention of Hangul (한글), the Korean alphabet, completed in 1443 and published in 1446.[^35] Before Hangul, Koreans used Hanja (Chinese characters), which were extremely difficult to learn and effectively limited literacy to the upper classes who could afford extensive education.[^36] Sejong was distressed by this situation, as he observed that common people who wished to express themselves in writing were unable to do so.[^37]

In the famous preface to the Hunminjeongeum (the document introducing Hangul), Sejong wrote: "The sounds of our country's language are different from those of the Middle Kingdom and are not confluent with the sounds of characters. Therefore, among the ignorant people, there have been many who, having something they want to put into words, have in the end been unable to express their feelings. I have been distressed because of this, and have newly designed twenty-eight letters, which I wish to have everyone practice at their ease and make convenient for their daily use."[^38]

The extent of Sejong's personal involvement in creating the script remains debated among scholars, with most believing he was significantly personally involved and worked alongside scholars from the Hall of Worthies, while a minority argue he was the sole creator.[^39] Regardless of the precise attribution, the creation of Hangul was a revolutionary achievement that democratized literacy.[^40]

Hangul consisted of 28 original letters (four of which have since become obsolete), designed to be simple enough that people of all ages and classes could learn to read and write Korean in a matter of days.[^41] The alphabet's scientific design, with letters representing the shape of the mouth and tongue during pronunciation, made it exceptionally logical and easy to learn.[^42]

### Scientific and Technological Innovations

Sejong's reign witnessed an explosion of scientific advancement unprecedented in Korean history.[^43] He sponsored numerous inventions and instruments that improved agriculture, astronomy, timekeeping, and military capabilities.[^44]

In 1441, following a proposal from his Crown Prince (later King Munjong), Sejong ordered the creation of the Cheugugi, the world's first standardized rain gauge.[^45] Prior to this invention, rainfall was measured primitively by examining the depth of water in puddles, which varied depending on soil absorption.[^46] In 1442, he established a nationwide rainfall observation network with 350 stations throughout the kingdom, creating the world's first state-administered meteorological monitoring system.[^47]

Sejong also commissioned the development of advanced astronomical instruments including celestial globes, astronomical maps, and an orrery (mechanical model of the solar system).[^48] In 1434, he invented the Jagyeongu, a water-powered alarm clock with automatic bells, gongs, and drums.[^49] His chief inventor, Jang Yeong-sil, a former government slave elevated to prominence purely on merit, created innovations including a self-striking water clock and improved sundial.[^50]

The development of jeongganbo in 1447 represented the first mensural notation system in Asia, revolutionizing how music could be documented and preserved.[^51] This grid-based notation used vertical columns for beats and horizontal lines for pitches, allowing court music to be accurately recorded for posterity.[^52]

### Agricultural and Economic Reforms

Sejong implemented comprehensive tax reforms that transformed Korean agriculture and improved the lives of common farmers.[^53] Work on designing the new tax system, called gongbeop (공법), began in 1427 and was completed in 1444 after an unprecedented 17-year process of surveying public opinion and debating with ministers.[^54]

The final system was remarkably sophisticated, based not only on land area but also on soil fertility (graded according to six levels) and the quality of the previous year's harvest (graded on a nine-point scale).[^55] This variable tax rate ensured fairness and prevented excessive burden during poor agricultural years.[^56] Despite lowering the land tax rate from 10% to 5% of the harvest, tax revenues did not decline significantly, as confiscations of Buddhist temple land and increases in agricultural productivity offset the difference.[^57]

In 1429, Sejong commissioned the agricultural treatise Nongsa chikseol, designed to be more suited to Korean agriculture than Chinese works.[^58] The treatise incorporated practical knowledge from experienced farmers throughout Korea and remained a foundational text in Korean agricultural studies until the 17th century.[^59] Sejong's restrictions on Buddhist land ownership increased productive farmland from 1.2 million gyeol to 1.72 million gyeol.[^60]

### Military Expansion

Sejong expanded Korea's northern borders to approximately their current extent through military campaigns against the Jurchens, nomadic tribes who frequently raided Korean settlements.[^61] In 1432, when the Hulun Udeha tribe attacked Joseon, Sejong responded with decisive military action.[^62]

In 1433, he sent a campaign commanded by Choe Yundok and Yi Cheon, and in 1437, General Kim Jong-seo led another major campaign.[^63] Kim's forces engaged Jurchen clans including the Odoli, Maolian, and Udige, capturing several castles and pushing north to the Songhua River.[^64] These campaigns resulted in the establishment of six garrisons in the region, securing the northern frontier.[^65]

Beyond military force, Sejong employed diplomatic strategies including opening trade with Jurchens to discourage raids and granting nominal military appointments to Jurchen leaders to facilitate their integration into the Joseon bureaucracy.[^66] This combination of military strength and diplomatic engagement successfully pacified the northern frontier.[^67]

### Cultural and Educational Contributions

Sejong's emphasis on scholarship and education transformed Joseon into a center of learning.[^68] In 1426, he established the practice of saga dokaseo, allowing Hall of Worthies scholars to conduct independent research without participating in government work—similar to modern research grants.[^69]

His court music reforms from 1420 to 1425 included the creation of hundreds of new instruments under his chief musicologist Pak Yeon.[^70] In 1430, Sejong ordered the development of new music for Confucian rites based on ancient Chinese musicology texts, but beginning in 1433, focus shifted to developing distinctively Korean musical practices rather than emulating Chinese models.[^71] These efforts culminated in the 1447 promulgation of Koreanized jeryeak for ancestral rites and the composition of Jongmyo Jeryeak, special ritual music performed during royal ancestral ceremonies.[^72]

### Progressive Labor Policies

Sejong implemented remarkably progressive labor policies for the 15th century.[^73] In 1426, he granted government nobi (servants/slaves) women 100 days of maternity leave after childbirth, which was extended by one month before childbirth in 1430.[^74] In 1434, he also granted husbands 30 days of paternity leave.[^75]

He also established legal protections for nobi, including the first prohibition against killing them issued in 1444, though enforcement proved difficult in practice.[^76] These reforms demonstrated Sejong's commitment to improving conditions even for the lowest social classes.[^77]

## Controversies and Criticisms

### Opposition to Hangul Creation

The creation of Hangul proved to be one of the most controversial projects of Sejong's reign.[^78] In 1444, shortly after the alphabet's invention, a group of dissidents from the Hall of Worthies, led by associate professor Choe Manri, submitted a memorial protesting the new script.[^79]

Choe and other Korean Confucian scholars believed Hanja to be the only legitimate writing system and likely viewed Hangul as a threat to their elite status.[^80] The protests were reasonable from their perspective—abandoning Chinese characters represented a radical break from centuries of East Asian scholarly tradition.[^81] Memorials and protests arrived in a flood, but Sejong proved implacable, denying each memorial immediately despite the scholars' arguments.[^82]

Although the scholars directly opposed the king's will, Sejong only rebuked them rather than imposing severe punishment.[^83] Choe Manri and several colleagues were imprisoned for a single day as a symbolic gesture.[^84] This restraint demonstrated Sejong's commitment to intellectual discourse even when facing opposition to his most cherished project.[^85]

### Buddhist Policies and Religious Restrictions

Sejong's policies significantly restricted Buddhism's influence in Joseon, despite his personal Buddhist faith.[^86] In 1424, he reduced the seven Buddhist schools to two (Seon and Kyo), with each sect allowed only 18 temples while all other temples were forced to close.[^87]

This contradiction between personal belief and public policy created considerable controversy.[^88] While Sejong and his predecessors privately practiced Buddhism, their policies systematically restricted Buddhist institutional power and influence.[^89] To quell anti-Buddhist criticisms, Sejong denied his faith to others numerous times.[^90]

Scholars from both the Hall of Worthies and Sungkyunkwan Academy criticized Sejong for his public worship of Buddha, arguing that he was leading Joseon astray from its goal of becoming a Confucian state.[^91] After the consecutive deaths of his sons Grand Prince Kwangpyeong and Grand Prince Pyeongwon in 1444 and 1445, followed by Queen Soheon's death in 1446, Sejong's Buddhist devotion deepened.[^92] He invited ministers who believed in Buddhism to court and erected a temple called Naebuldang inside the palace, further inflaming tensions with orthodox Confucian scholars.[^93]

### Tributary Relationship with Ming China

Although Sejong is portrayed as an independent king in modern times, Korea maintained a tributary relationship with Ming China during his reign.[^94] Cases existed where laws were passed because "China did it," and Korea had to procure special products as tribute to the Ming emperor, occasionally leading to fraudulent practices.[^95]

This tributary system was complex—it involved symbolic submission while preserving substantial domestic autonomy, but it also constrained Korea's foreign policy and required significant resources to maintain.[^96] Modern Korean nationalism sometimes glosses over this aspect of Sejong's reign, preferring to emphasize his independent achievements like Hangul creation.[^97]

### Nobi System Persistence

Despite implementing progressive labor reforms, Sejong maintained the nobi (servant/slave) system that dominated Joseon society.[^98] While he granted legal protections and maternity/paternity leave, the fundamental institution of hereditary servitude remained intact.[^99]

Scholars debate whether the nobi system constituted slavery or serfdom, with the consensus being that it was a complex system that defies simple categorization.[^100] Nobi possessed certain property rights and legal standing that differentiated them from chattel slaves, but they remained bound to masters and passed their status to their children.[^101] Sejong's reforms ameliorated the worst abuses but did not challenge the system's existence.[^102]

## Personal Life

### Marriage and Family

At age ten, Sejong married Sim Ui-gyeom's daughter (later Queen Soheon of the Cheongsong Sim clan, 1395-1446), cementing a political alliance.[^103] The marriage, arranged when both were children according to Joseon custom, would produce 8 sons and 2 daughters.[^104]

Beyond his marriage to Queen Soheon, Sejong had seven concubines who bore him a total of 18 sons and 4 daughters officially recognized in the genealogy.[^105] A 19th son, Prince Tang (born 1442), died in childhood and was never included in the family genealogy book.[^106] This large royal household was typical for Joseon monarchs, who were expected to produce multiple heirs to ensure dynastic continuity.[^107]

Among his children, Yi Hyang became King Munjong and Yi Yu became King Sejo, both playing crucial roles in Joseon history.[^108] However, Sejo's ascension was controversial—he usurped the throne from his nephew King Danjong, leading to a political crisis that resulted in the disbanding of the Hall of Worthies in 1456 when many of its scholars plotted to assassinate Sejo.[^109]

### Family Tragedies

The mid-1440s brought devastating personal losses to Sejong.[^110] His sons Grand Prince Kwangpyeong and Grand Prince Pyeongwon died in 1444 and 1445 respectively, followed by Queen Soheon's death in 1446.[^111] These consecutive deaths left Sejong emotionally devastated and deepened his devotion to Buddhism as he sought spiritual comfort.[^112]

The loss of his wife was particularly profound, as Queen Soheon had been his companion since childhood.[^113] Her death occurred during a period when Sejong was already struggling with deteriorating health, compounding his physical suffering with emotional anguish.[^114]

### Health Problems and Lifestyle

Sejong suffered from recurring and worsening health issues throughout his adult life.[^115] At age 22, he complained of knee and back pain.[^116] In his 30s, he experienced back pain, vision problems, excessive thirst, and excessive urination—symptoms consistent with diabetes.[^117] He also suffered from headaches, dysentery, leprosy, boils, hand tremors, and numbness in one leg.[^118]

The predominant medical hypothesis is that Sejong had either type 1 or type 2 diabetes, though medical researcher JiHwan Lee has argued that his symptoms more closely resemble ankylosing spondylitis, a type of inflammatory arthritis.[^119] Lee contends that diabetes would likely have been lethal sooner and that Sejong lacked a clear family history of the disease.[^120]

After 1439, complications from his illness caused Sejong to lose his eyesight progressively, and he was eventually completely blinded years before his death.[^121] Contributing factors to his poor health included his sedentary lifestyle, obesity, and reputation for enjoying meat while being extremely picky about food.[^122]

The Annals record that Sejong suffered from nervous disorders in his legs, abscesses, diabetes, failing eyesight, and joint disorders caused by his lack of exercise and obesity.[^123] Despite these debilitating conditions, he continued to govern actively until shortly before his death on April 8, 1450, at the age of 53.[^124]

### Personality and Character

Historical records depict Sejong as intellectually curious, compassionate toward his subjects, and occasionally stubborn when pursuing his convictions.[^125] His famous quote, "If the people prosper, how can the king not prosper with them? And if the people do not prosper, how may the king prosper without them?" encapsulates his governing philosophy centered on the welfare of common people.[^126]

Another quote demonstrates his view of kingship as service: "A king's office is to serve the people. That is all."[^127] This philosophy contrasted sharply with more authoritarian views of monarchy prevalent in East Asia and reflected his Confucian education.[^128]

Sejong also displayed intellectual humility, stating: "A wise ruler should not neglect minority opinions and give careful hearing. Nevertheless, it would be foolish for a monarch to make decisions based on a single person's opinion."[^129] This balanced approach characterized his governance—he sought diverse counsel but ultimately made firm decisions.[^130]

## Legacy

### Cultural Icon of Modern Korea

Sejong is regarded as an icon of Korean culture in South Korea, where he has received numerous tributes.[^131] Sejong City, established as a special administrative city in 2012, bears his name.[^132] In central Seoul, Sejongno (Sejong Avenue) and the Sejong Center for the Performing Arts honor his memory.[^133]

His image appears on the South Korean 10,000-won banknote alongside Irworobongdo (a folding screen for Joseon-era kings) and text from Yongbieocheonga, painted by artist Kim Ki-chang.[^134] A golden statue of Sejong, 6.2 meters high and weighing 20 tons, was unveiled in 2009 at Gwanghwamun Plaza in downtown Seoul and has been called "South Korea's most iconic statue."[^135]

In South Korea, King Sejong's birthday (May 15) is celebrated alongside National Teachers' Day, a fitting tribute to his enduring legacy as an educator and visionary leader.[^136] October 9 is celebrated as Hangul Day, a national holiday commemorating the creation of the Korean alphabet.[^137]

### Democratization of Literacy

Sejong's greatest legacy is undoubtedly Hangul, which fundamentally transformed Korean society by making literacy accessible to all social classes.[^138] Before Hangul, literacy was effectively limited to the yangban (ruling class) who could afford extensive education in Chinese characters.[^139] Hangul's simple design meant that peasants, women, and children could learn to read and write within days, democratizing access to written communication.[^140]

This democratization enabled the development of a rich Korean literary tradition that flourished in subsequent centuries.[^141] Korean literature, philosophy, and cultural expression could finally develop in the vernacular rather than remaining confined to classical Chinese.[^142] The alphabet also became a cornerstone of Korean national identity, distinguishing Korea culturally from China and Japan.[^143]

### Scientific Legacy

Sejong's patronage of science and technology established Korea as a leader in East Asian innovation during the 15th century.[^144] The rain gauge invented in 1441 predated similar Western instruments by over two centuries, and the nationwide meteorological monitoring network demonstrated sophisticated state organization.[^145]

His astronomical instruments and calendar reforms aligned Korean timekeeping with local celestial phenomena rather than relying solely on Chinese calendars.[^146] The development of jeongganbo music notation preserved Korean court music and allowed its transmission to future generations.[^147]

The Korean trajectory of innovation and accomplishment visible in modern Korea's technological prowess can be traced back to the culture of scientific inquiry Sejong established.[^148]

### Administrative and Social Reforms

Sejong's tax reforms demonstrated how evidence-based policy making could improve both government effectiveness and citizens' quality of life.[^149] The 17-year process of surveying public opinion before implementing the gongbeop system has been called "the world's first referendum" by some historians, though this characterization may be somewhat exaggerated.[^150]

His progressive labor policies—maternity and paternity leave in the 15th century—were centuries ahead of similar developments in Western societies.[^151] These policies reflected his philosophical commitment that rulers exist to serve the people rather than the reverse.[^152]

### Scholarly Tradition

The Hall of Worthies, though disbanded in 1456, established a tradition of scholarly governance that influenced subsequent Korean dynasties.[^153] Sejong's model of consulting experts, funding research, and basing policies on evidence rather than tradition alone created a template for enlightened governance.[^154]

His emphasis on Korean solutions to Korean problems—seen in the agricultural treatise tailored to Korean conditions and the shift from Chinese to Korean musical practices—fostered cultural independence while maintaining respect for valuable aspects of Chinese civilization.[^155]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Sejong has been evaluated by numerous historians as the greatest monarch in Korean history, a judgment that has remained remarkably consistent across centuries.[^156] His reign is universally recognized as the Golden Age of the Joseon Dynasty, characterized by cultural flowering, scientific advancement, and enlightened governance.[^157]

Modern Korean scholars emphasize how Sejong's vision for Korean identity—centered on the Korean language through Hangul—provided the cultural foundation for modern Korean nationalism.[^158] Without Hangul, Korea might have remained linguistically dependent on Chinese, potentially weakening its distinct national identity.[^159]

International scholars have also recognized Sejong's achievements. The Asia Society describes him as combining the roles of scholar, reformer, and monarch in ways rarely seen in world history.[^160] His creation of Hangul is often cited as one of the most important linguistic innovations in human history, with linguists praising the alphabet's scientific design and elegance.[^161]

Sejong's deliberative political style—consulting widely, gathering evidence, and carefully considering opposition arguments—contrasts favorably with more authoritarian monarchs of his era.[^162] Even when he ultimately overruled opponents (as with Hangul), he engaged with their arguments respectfully and avoided harsh punishments.[^163]

His philosophy that "a king's office is to serve the people" represented a progressive vision of governance that anticipated later democratic ideals.[^164] The labor reforms, tax adjustments based on harvest quality, and relief programs during natural disasters all demonstrated practical implementation of this philosophy.[^165]

### Negative Views

Despite overwhelming admiration, some historians have offered more critical perspectives on aspects of Sejong's reign.[^166] His maintenance of the nobi system, despite ameliorative reforms, represents a significant limitation from modern human rights perspectives.[^167] The system of hereditary servitude remained fundamentally unjust even with legal protections against the worst abuses.[^168]

His religious policies—suppressing Buddhism despite personal belief—can be seen as hypocritical or pragmatic depending on one's perspective.[^169] The closure of most Buddhist temples destroyed cultural institutions and displaced monks, representing a significant loss of religious freedom.[^170]

The tributary relationship with Ming China, while diplomatically necessary, required resources and constrained Korea's independence.[^171] Some nationalist historians argue that Sejong could have pursued a more assertive foreign policy, though such criticism may be anachronistic given 15th-century geopolitical realities.[^172]

His stubborn insistence on creating Hangul despite widespread elite opposition demonstrated both admirable conviction and potentially authoritarian tendencies.[^173] While history vindicated his decision, the process revealed his willingness to override consensus when he believed his judgment superior.[^174]

Some scholars note that Sejong's achievements relied heavily on exploiting the labor and talents of subordinates, including low-born inventors like Jang Yeong-sil who received little historical recognition compared to the king.[^175] This reflects broader patterns of how monarchical systems receive credit for collective achievements.[^176]

Finally, Sejong's health problems may have partially resulted from self-neglect—his sedentary lifestyle, poor diet, and excessive work habits contributed to his deteriorating condition.[^177] A monarch who better balanced personal health with state duties might have lived longer and accomplished even more.[^178]

### Scholarly Consensus

Despite these criticisms, the scholarly consensus remains overwhelmingly positive.[^179] Sejong's achievements in linguistics, science, administration, and culture far outweigh his limitations.[^180] He is universally recognized as having transformed Korea during his 32-year reign and establishing foundations that shaped Korean civilization for centuries.[^181]

The breadth of his accomplishments—spanning language, science, music, agriculture, military affairs, and governance—demonstrates rare versatility.[^182] Few monarchs in world history can claim achievements of comparable diversity and lasting impact.[^183]

## Famous Quotes

> "If the people prosper, how can the king not prosper with them? And if the people do not prosper, how may the king prosper without them?"

This quote encapsulates Sejong's governing philosophy that the ruler's welfare is inseparable from the people's welfare, reflecting his commitment to benevolent governance.[^184]

> "A king's office is to serve the people. That is all."

This concise statement demonstrates Sejong's understanding of monarchy as fundamentally about service rather than power, a progressive view for his era.[^185]

> "The sounds of our country's language are different from those of the Middle Kingdom and are not confluent with the sounds of characters. Therefore, among the ignorant people, there have been many who, having something they want to put into words, have in the end been unable to express their feelings. I have been distressed because of this, and have newly designed twenty-eight letters, which I wish to have everyone practice at their ease and make convenient for their daily use."

From the preface to Hunminjeongeum, this quote reveals Sejong's motivation for creating Hangul and his compassion for common people unable to express themselves through Chinese characters.[^186]

> "It is in order to avoid confusion in undertakings by the people that they have a king rule over them. How, then, could a king hope to live up to the dignity expected of him as a ruler when he refuses to hear out his people when they bring to his attention injustices done to him?"

This quote demonstrates Sejong's belief that rulers must listen to their subjects and address their grievances.[^187]

> "A wise ruler should not neglect minority opinions and give careful hearing. Nevertheless, it would be foolish for a monarch to make decisions based on a single person's opinion."

This balanced statement reflects Sejong's deliberative approach—valuing diverse perspectives while maintaining ultimate decision-making authority.[^188]
