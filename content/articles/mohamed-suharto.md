Mohamed Suharto is commonly referenced in English-language sources as **Suharto** (1921–2008), a central figure in modern Indonesian political history and the dominant leader of Indonesia’s “New Order” era.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|

| 1965 | Rose to national prominence during the political crisis surrounding the 30 September Movement. |

| 1970s–1980s | Presided over rapid economic development alongside political repression and centralized control. |

| 1997–1998 | Asian financial crisis weakened the regime amid unrest and political pressure. |
| 1998 | Resigned from the presidency after widespread protests, ending decades in power. |

## Historical Context (High-Level)

- **New Order governance:** His administration emphasized stability, centralized authority, and a strong role for the military in politics.
- **Economic policy:** The period is often associated with macroeconomic stabilization and growth, but also with favoritism toward well-connected business groups.
- **Political repression:** Opposition parties, civil society actors, and independent media faced restrictions, and the state frequently used coercive tools.
- **Human-rights controversies:** Large-scale violence in the mid-1960s and the conflict in East Timor remain among the most cited and debated legacies.

## Legacy

Suharto’s legacy is widely contested. Supporters often point to state-led development and long-run institutional continuity, while critics emphasize authoritarian rule, corruption allegations, and unresolved human-rights accountability.

## Key Facts

- Served as Indonesia’s second president and was the defining figure of the New Order era.
- His rule ended in 1998 amid mass protests during the Asian financial crisis.
- The period is associated with both economic growth and significant political repression.
