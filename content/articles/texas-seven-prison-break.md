## Summary

Seven inmates escaped from a Texas maximum-security prison and went on a crime spree that included the murder of a police officer. Six were captured; one committed suicide.

## Escape Planning and Method (Overview)

- The escape occurred at the John B. Connally Unit in Texas in December 2000.
- The group exploited routines and access controls, obtained uniforms and tools, and used deception to reduce scrutiny.
- After leaving the facility, they acquired vehicles and resources to remain on the run.

## Crimes While Fugitive

- The fugitives committed robberies to obtain money, weapons, and supplies.
- A major turning point was the killing of a police officer during a robbery in Irving, Texas (a crime that intensified the manhunt).
- The incident became a prominent example in U.S. discussions of how quickly an escape can escalate into public-safety threats.

## Manhunt and Capture

- The fugitives were identified through investigative leads and public tips.
- Six members were eventually arrested, while one (commonly described as the seventh) died by suicide when confronted.
- The case involved coordination across jurisdictions, reflecting the scale of resources mobilized when violent fugitives are at large.

## Aftermath and Lessons

- The escape prompted reviews of prison procedures, including tool control, staff supervision, and identification protocols.
- It is frequently cited in training and media as a cautionary case about insider knowledge, complacency, and the risk of coordinated action by inmates.
