## Summary

Egyptian-born chemist convicted of murder in Ciudad Juarez, Mexico, suspected of involvement in the wider Juarez femicides of young women.

## Additional Context

### Ciudad Juárez and the femicides

From the 1990s onward, Ciudad Juárez became internationally known for a sustained pattern of murders and disappearances of women, often referred to as the "Juárez femicides." The scale of violence, the vulnerability of many victims, and persistent failures in investigation created long-running national and international scrutiny of Mexican law enforcement and institutions.

### Sharif’s arrest and disputed role

Abdul Latif Sharif was convicted in connection with at least one murder, and authorities also portrayed him as a possible key suspect behind a broader pattern of killings. His alleged role has been widely disputed, with critics arguing that focusing on a single individual risked obscuring networks of violence, multiple perpetrators, or institutional failings.

### Institutional and human-rights critique

The Juárez cases are often discussed alongside issues such as:

- Weak investigative capacity and poor evidence handling
- Under-prosecution of gender-based violence
- Corruption and intimidation affecting witnesses
- The social and economic context of border-city labor markets

### Death in custody

Sharif died in prison in 2006. His death did not resolve the broader questions surrounding the femicides, which continued to be investigated, debated, and documented by journalists, activists, and human rights organizations.
