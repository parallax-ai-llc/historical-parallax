## Summary

Misty Copeland is an American ballet dancer who made history in 2015 when she became the first African American woman to be named principal dancer at American Ballet Theatre (ABT), one of the foremost classical ballet companies in the world. Her rise through the ranks of elite ballet challenged longstanding norms about body type and race in a field that had long been dominated by white, reed-thin performers. She is widely credited with broadening the public conversation about diversity and inclusion in classical dance.

Beyond her technical achievements, Copeland has become a cultural icon, using her platform to advocate for greater representation in the arts. She has appeared in advertising campaigns, written best-selling memoirs, and inspired a new generation of young dancers—particularly Black girls—to pursue ballet. Her visibility has had a measurable effect on enrollment in ballet programs among children of color across the United States.

## Early Life & Background

Misty Danielle Copeland was born on September 10, 1982, in Kansas City, Missouri. She grew up in a financially precarious household, at times living in motels with her mother and five siblings. She had no formal dance training until age 13, when she began studying ballet at the Boys & Girls Club of San Pedro, California, under instructor Cynthia Bradley. Her late start was highly unusual in a discipline where serious training typically begins by age 8 or 9.

Bradley quickly recognized Copeland's exceptional physical ability and natural musicality. Within three months of beginning lessons, Copeland was dancing en pointe—a milestone most students take years to reach. She won the Los Angeles Music Center Spotlight Award in 1998 and was admitted to the ABT summer intensive program on full scholarship.

## Rise to Prominence

Copeland joined ABT's Studio Company in 2000 and was promoted to the main company corps de ballet in 2001. She advanced to soloist in 2007, a title she held for eight years. During this period she danced leading roles in Swan Lake, The Firebird, and Romeo and Juliet, gradually gaining recognition for her athleticism and expressive intensity.

In June 2015, ABT artistic director Kevin McKenzie named Copeland a principal dancer, making her the first Black woman to hold that rank in the company's 75-year history. The announcement received widespread media coverage and was treated as a watershed moment for racial representation in classical ballet.

## Key Achievements

- First African American woman named principal dancer at ABT (June 2015)
- Published memoir *Life in Motion: An Unlikely Ballerina* (2014), a New York Times bestseller
- Named one of TIME magazine's 100 Most Influential People in 2015
- Starred in Under Armour's viral "I Will What I Want" campaign (2014)
- Published children's book *Firebird* (2014), illustrated to reflect diverse young dancers
- Appointed to the President's Council on Fitness, Sports & Nutrition under President Obama

## Controversies & Criticism

Copeland has spoken publicly about receiving a rejection letter from ABT at age 19 citing her "wrong body for ballet"—too muscular, too short, with breasts too large by classical standards. This letter became a symbol of the exclusionary norms she fought against. Some critics within the dance world have debated whether her promotion at ABT was influenced by external public-relations considerations rather than purely artistic merit, a charge her supporters reject by pointing to her two-decade tenure and demanding repertoire. Copeland herself has addressed these critiques directly in interviews, arguing that excellence and representation are not mutually exclusive.

## Legacy & Influence

Copeland's ascent permanently altered how the ballet world talks about diversity. ABT and other major companies have since launched more structured diversity initiatives. Her books have introduced classical ballet to audiences that had previously seen little of themselves in the art form. Dance schools across the country report increased enrollment from Black and brown children in the years following her 2015 appointment. She remains active both as a performer and as an advocate, ensuring her influence extends well beyond her own career.
