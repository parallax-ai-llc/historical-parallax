CIA operated secret prisons in Poland, Romania, Thailand, Lithuania, and elsewhere where terrorism suspects were tortured using 'enhanced interrogation' including waterboarding and sleep deprivation.

## What “Black Site” Means

In this context, a "black site" refers to a covert detention facility whose location, operations, and detainee lists are classified. The term became widely associated with the post‑9/11 CIA detention and interrogation program.

## How the Program Worked (High-Level)

- Detainees were held outside normal criminal justice processes and often without access to courts.
- Interrogations used so‑called "enhanced interrogation techniques" that critics and many legal scholars describe as torture.
- Operations relied on secret transfers of detainees between countries (extraordinary rendition) and cooperation from foreign intelligence services.

## Oversight, Exposure, and Accountability

The existence of secret CIA detention sites was reported by journalists and later investigated by multiple bodies, including U.S. congressional inquiries and European institutions. Public reporting and official reviews focused on legality, efficacy, and human rights impacts, and on the challenges of accountability when programs are highly classified.

## Impact and Legacy

Debates over CIA black sites have shaped international discussions on torture, rendition, and the limits of counterterrorism powers. They also influenced later policy and legal reforms around interrogation standards, detention practices, and intelligence oversight.
