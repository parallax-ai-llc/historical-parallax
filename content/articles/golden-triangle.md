## Definition

The “Golden Triangle” commonly refers to the mountainous border areas where Myanmar, Laos, and Thailand meet. For much of the late 20th century it was known internationally for opium production and heroin trafficking, though in more recent years analyses frequently emphasize large-scale synthetic drug (especially methamphetamine) production.

## Historical background

Key elements often noted in histories of the region include:

- rugged terrain and limited central-state reach, enabling armed groups and militias
- longstanding cultivation of opium poppy as a cash crop
- shifting patterns of control tied to insurgencies, ceasefires, and border politics

## Shift toward synthetic drugs

In contemporary reporting, the Golden Triangle is frequently connected to:

- industrial-scale manufacturing of methamphetamine tablets and crystal meth
- precursor chemical trafficking and cross-border supply chains
- rapid distribution networks serving Southeast Asia and beyond

## Why the term remains important

- **Geography as advantage**: porous borders and remote terrain complicate enforcement.
- **Political economy**: drug revenues can intersect with conflict financing and corruption.
- **Regional spillover**: impacts include public health crises and criminal violence in multiple countries.
