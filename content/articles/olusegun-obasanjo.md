## Summary

Former Nigerian president who oversaw both military and democratic governments. During his democratic tenure he attempted to change the constitution to serve a third term and was accused of corruption.

## Military Leadership and Transition

Obasanjo first came to national prominence as a senior military figure and later as Nigeria’s head of state in the late 1970s. His period in office is often discussed in relation to the handover from military rule to an elected civilian government, a transfer that shaped later expectations about political transitions in Nigeria.

## Democratic Presidency (1999–2007)

Returning to power through elections in 1999, Obasanjo led Nigeria during a major democratic transition after years of military government. His administration faced the typical pressures of a large, oil-dependent state: balancing macroeconomic stability, debt and fiscal policy, security challenges, and regional political demands.

## Third-Term Controversy

A major political flashpoint of his civilian tenure was the effort associated with extending presidential tenure beyond the constitutionally expected limits. The episode became a defining test of Nigeria’s post-transition institutions and remains a frequently cited case in debates about term limits in African democracies.

## Post-Presidency Role

After leaving office, Obasanjo continued to be active in public life, including involvement in mediation and advisory roles in African politics. He is a polarizing figure: credited by supporters for helping consolidate democratic rule, and criticized by opponents over governance, power consolidation, and corruption-related allegations.

## Legacy (High Level)

Obasanjo’s career is often used as a lens on Nigeria’s modern political arc: military government, democratization, and the institutional struggle over accountability and term limits. Even critics typically acknowledge that his two distinct periods in power made him unusually influential in shaping elite political norms.

In historical summaries, he is often grouped with leaders who straddled both uniformed and electoral legitimacy, which makes evaluations of his record especially contested and politically consequential.
