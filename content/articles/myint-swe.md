## Summary

Myint Swe (24 June 1951 – 7 August 2025) was a Myanmar military general and politician who served as Acting President of Myanmar following the February 2021 military coup.[^1][^2] The first ethnic Mon to reach the rank of Lieutenant General, he graduated from the Defence Services Academy in 1973 and rose through military ranks to become one of Myanmar's most powerful figures.[^3] He was best known internationally for leading the brutal 2007 Saffron Revolution crackdown on Buddhist monks and for his role in legitimizing the 2021 coup that plunged Myanmar into civil war.[^4][^5] A close protégé of former dictator Than Shwe, Myint Swe served as a ruthless enforcer before becoming a largely ceremonial figurehead president until his death from Parkinson's disease in 2025.[^6]

---

## Early Life and Background

Myint Swe was born on 24 June 1951 in Mandalay, Myanmar, to U Kyee Maung and Daw Kyi.[^7] He was of Mon descent, making him part of one of Myanmar's significant ethnic minorities.[^8] The Mon people have a long history in Myanmar, with their own distinct culture and language, and Myint Swe would later become the first Mon to achieve the rank of Lieutenant General in Myanmar's predominantly Bamar-dominated military hierarchy.[^9]

In 1969, Myint Swe entered the Defence Services Academy (DSA), Myanmar's premier military training institution located in Pyin Oo Lwin.[^10] He graduated in 1973 as part of the prestigious 15th intake and was commissioned as an officer.[^11] This intake designation would prove significant throughout his career, as it established his seniority within the military hierarchy—notably, he outranked Min Aung Hlaing, who graduated from the 19th intake.[^12]

Little is publicly known about Myint Swe's early family life or the circumstances that led him to pursue a military career. However, entering the Defence Services Academy during this period meant joining an institution that had been central to Myanmar's governance since the 1962 military coup led by General Ne Win.[^13] The academy trained officers who would go on to dominate Myanmar's political and economic landscape for decades.

Myint Swe married Khin Thet Htay, and together they had two children: a daughter, Khin Thet Htar Swe, and a son, Kyaw Phone Myint, who also served in the military before retiring with the rank of Captain.[^14][^15] His wife would later serve as acting First Lady of Myanmar during his tenure as acting president.[^16]

After his commissioning in 1973, Myint Swe embarked on a military career that would span more than four decades, gradually ascending through the ranks during a period of continuous military rule in Myanmar. His early assignments and postings during the 1970s and 1980s remain largely undocumented in public sources, but his steady progression through the military hierarchy demonstrated both competence and, crucially, the right connections within Myanmar's closed military establishment.

---

## Rise to Power

Myint Swe's ascent within Myanmar's military hierarchy accelerated significantly in the 1990s and early 2000s, propelled by both his military capabilities and his cultivation of relationships with senior junta leaders, particularly General Than Shwe, who would become Myanmar's paramount leader.[^17]

By 1997, Myint Swe had achieved the rank of Brigadier General and was appointed commander of Light Infantry Division 11, a significant field command that demonstrated his standing within the armed forces.[^18] This position gave him operational experience leading combat troops, an important credential in Myanmar's military, which had been engaged in multiple insurgencies across the country's ethnic minority regions.

In 2001, following a helicopter crash that killed his predecessor, Myint Swe was appointed Commander of the Southeastern Command and became a member of the State Peace and Development Council (SPDC), Myanmar's ruling military junta.[^19] This appointment marked his entry into the highest echelons of Myanmar's military government. Shortly thereafter, he was transferred to become Commander of Yangon Command and promoted to Major General, simultaneously serving as Chairman of the Yangon Division Peace and Development Council.[^20]

The Yangon Command position was particularly significant. As Myanmar's largest city and commercial capital, Yangon required a commander whom the junta's leadership could trust implicitly to maintain security and control. Myint Swe's appointment to this critical post reflected his growing importance within the regime and his close relationship with Than Shwe, who had become the SPDC's Chairman and Myanmar's de facto ruler.[^21]

Myint Swe's relationship with Than Shwe proved to be the cornerstone of his rise to power. He was known to be personally close to the dictator and enjoyed the favor of Than Shwe's wife, Kyaing Kyaing.[^22] This patronage relationship was crucial in Myanmar's military hierarchy, where loyalty to senior officers often determined career advancement. Myint Swe reportedly maintained regular contact with Than Shwe's family and looked after his business empire and extensive property portfolio, suggesting a relationship that went beyond simple military loyalty.[^23]

In 2004, Myint Swe played a pivotal role in one of the most significant power struggles within Myanmar's military regime. In October 2004, Prime Minister and Military Intelligence Chief General Khin Nyunt was suddenly arrested and purged along with many of his supporters.[^24] Myint Swe personally arrested Khin Nyunt at Yangon Airport, demonstrating his willingness to serve as Than Shwe's enforcer in eliminating potential rivals.[^25] Following the purge, Than Shwe appointed Myint Swe to take command of the sprawling military intelligence apparatus that had been Khin Nyunt's power base, making him Chief of Military Security Affairs.[^26] This appointment gave Myint Swe control over Myanmar's domestic surveillance and intelligence operations.

In 2005, Myint Swe achieved a historic milestone when he was promoted to Lieutenant General, becoming the first ethnic Mon to reach this rank in Myanmar's military history.[^27] The following year, in January 2006, he became Chief of Bureau of Special Operations-5 (BSO-5), further consolidating his control over security matters.[^28]

Myint Swe's bravado and increasing power stemmed directly from his closeness to Than Shwe. His actions as Yangon Commander and intelligence chief reflected the Than Shwe regime's security interests and paranoia about threats to military rule.[^29] This relationship positioned him as one of the most powerful and feared figures in Myanmar's military establishment by the mid-2000s.

## Major Achievements

From the military regime's perspective, Myint Swe's career included several "achievements" that secured his position within Myanmar's power structure, though many of these same actions would later be condemned internationally as human rights violations and crimes against the civilian population.

### Military Career Advancement

Myint Swe's rise from a junior officer to Lieutenant General represented a significant achievement within Myanmar's competitive military hierarchy. His promotion to Lieutenant General in 2005 was particularly notable as it made him the first person of Mon ethnicity to reach this elevated rank, breaking through barriers in an institution traditionally dominated by the Bamar majority.[^30] This achievement demonstrated both his military competence and his political acumen in navigating Myanmar's complex patronage networks.

### Command of Strategic Posts

Throughout his career, Myint Swe held several strategically important commands. His appointment as Commander of Yangon Command gave him control over security in Myanmar's most important city, while his subsequent role as Chief of Military Security Affairs following the 2004 purge of Khin Nyunt placed him at the center of Myanmar's domestic intelligence apparatus.[^31] These positions gave him enormous power over the lives of ordinary Burmese citizens and political dissidents.

### Consolidation of Military Intelligence

After Khin Nyunt's purge in 2004, Myint Swe successfully took control of and reorganized Myanmar's military intelligence structure.[^32] While the dismantling of Khin Nyunt's intelligence empire initially weakened the regime's capabilities, Myint Swe worked to rebuild these capabilities under direct military control. His management of this transition was seen within the military as competent handling of a sensitive restructuring operation.

### Political Transition to Vice President

In 2011, Myanmar began a carefully orchestrated transition from direct military rule to a quasi-civilian government, though one still dominated by the military and former military officers. Myint Swe successfully transitioned from active military service to a political role, serving as Chief Minister of Yangon Region from 30 March 2011 to 30 March 2016.[^33] During this period, he oversaw the administration of Myanmar's commercial capital during a period of significant economic and political change.

On 15 March 2016, Myint Swe was elected First Vice President of Myanmar, receiving 213 votes from military-appointed Members of Parliament in the Assembly of the Union.[^34] This position made him one of the three highest-ranking officials in Myanmar's government structure, serving in the cabinet of President Htin Kyaw, who had been nominated by Aung San Suu Kyi's National League for Democracy (NLD) party. As First Vice President, Myint Swe represented the military's interests within the nominally civilian government.

### Role in 2021 Coup

From the military junta's perspective, Myint Swe's most significant "achievement" was his role in the 2021 coup. On 1 February 2021, after the military arrested President Win Myint and State Counsellor Aung San Suu Kyi, Myint Swe, as First Vice President, automatically became Acting President.[^35] He immediately exercised constitutional powers to declare a one-year state of emergency, invoking articles 417 and 418 of the constitution and transferring legislative, executive, and judicial powers to Commander-in-Chief Senior General Min Aung Hlaing.[^36]

This action provided a veneer of constitutional legitimacy to what was, in effect, a military coup. By using his position as First Vice President to declare the emergency, the military could claim to be acting within constitutional procedures, even though the action clearly violated democratic norms and was undertaken at gunpoint.[^37] For the military, Myint Swe's willingness to serve this role was crucial in managing both domestic and international perceptions of the takeover.

### Maintaining Military Unity

Throughout his career, Myint Swe demonstrated loyalty to Myanmar's military institution and its senior leadership. He successfully navigated multiple leadership transitions and power struggles, including the sensitive period following Than Shwe's formal retirement from active politics in 2011. His ability to maintain the confidence of different military factions and leaders contributed to his longevity in positions of power.

## Controversies and Criticisms

Myint Swe's career was marked by numerous actions that drew severe criticism from human rights organizations, democratic activists, and the international community. His role as an enforcer for Myanmar's military regime resulted in widespread allegations of human rights abuses and violence against civilians.

### The 2007 Saffron Revolution Crackdown

The incident for which Myint Swe became most notorious internationally was his leadership of the violent suppression of the 2007 Saffron Revolution in Yangon. In August and September 2007, massive demonstrations erupted across Myanmar, initially triggered by sudden fuel price increases but quickly expanding into broad pro-democracy protests.[^38] Buddhist monks, who hold special moral authority in Myanmar's predominantly Buddhist society, took leading roles in the protests, giving the uprising its name.[^39]

As Yangon Regional Commander, Myint Swe led security operations against the protestors.[^40] On September 26-27, 2007, the military launched a brutal crackdown. Soldiers fired directly on peaceful protestors, including monks, in the streets of Yangon.[^41] According to human rights organizations, at least 32 people, including monks, were shot dead, though the actual death toll may have been higher.[^42] Hundreds more were subjected to arbitrary arrest, detention, interrogation, and torture.[^43] Troops raided monasteries at night, dragging monks from their quarters and beating them.[^44]

Myint Swe's Swan Arr Shin groups—pro-military militias—played a key role in the crackdown and surveillance efforts.[^45] The violence was captured on video and broadcast internationally, triggering worldwide condemnation of the Myanmar regime. For many international observers, Myint Swe became the face of the regime's brutality during this period.

Years later, when monks from the 2007 uprising sought a formal apology from the former regime leaders, Myint Swe, then serving as Chief Minister of Yangon Region, denied involvement in the violent crackdown.[^46] Some Saffron Revolution leaders called for him to face court for his actions.[^47] However, Myanmar's military-drafted 2008 Constitution provided immunity for actions taken by junta members during military rule, effectively shielding Myint Swe from legal accountability.[^48]

### Rohingya Crisis Investigation

In 2017, following military operations against Rohingya communities in Rakhine State that resulted in mass killings, sexual violence, and the displacement of more than 740,000 people to Bangladesh, Myanmar established an investigation commission.[^49] Vice President Myint Swe headed this commission, which held a news conference on August 6, 2017, following a nine-month inquiry.[^50]

The commission's findings were widely criticized as a whitewash. Myint Swe reportedly told journalists, "There is no possibility of crimes against humanity, no evidence of ethnic cleansing, as per UN accusations," and rejected the possibility that genocide had occurred.[^51] The commission claimed the military had acted "lawfully."[^52]

Human Rights Watch and other organizations condemned the investigation, noting that the commission's wholesale rejection of grave abuses despite considerable evidence from independent sources demonstrated the government's unwillingness to hold the military accountable.[^53] The commission, led by a former military general and including current and former military, police, and government officials, used investigative methods that produced incomplete, inaccurate, and false information.[^54] This investigation was widely seen as an attempt to provide cover for what many international observers and the United Nations later characterized as genocide against the Rohingya people.[^55]

### International Sanctions

Myint Swe's actions resulted in his designation for international sanctions by multiple countries and organizations. Following the 2021 coup, the United States Treasury Department sanctioned him as a retired Lieutenant General who played a direct role in the coup.[^56] He was also subject to sanctions by Canada, the European Union, and other nations.[^57][^58] These sanctions typically included asset freezes and travel bans.

Interestingly, Myint Swe had previously been included on a U.S. Treasury Department list of sanctioned Myanmar officials following the 2007 crackdown, but that designation was removed in 2016 as part of the Obama administration's policy of engagement with Myanmar during its democratic transition period.[^59] The re-imposition of sanctions following the 2021 coup reflected international condemnation of his role in ending Myanmar's democratic experiment.

### Unconstitutional Actions

Legal scholars and constitutional experts criticized Myint Swe's declaration of the state of emergency on February 1, 2021, as unconstitutional.[^60] Under Myanmar's constitution, only the sitting president has the authority to declare such an emergency, and the emergency must be based on genuine threats to national security, not political convenience.[^61] The deposed President Win Myint had not voluntarily ceded his role; indeed, he later testified that the military had tried to force him to relinquish power hours before the coup, warning him he could be seriously harmed if he refused.[^62]

By declaring the emergency after the military had already detained the legitimate president and other elected officials, Myint Swe was essentially providing a fig leaf of legality to an illegal military takeover. Critics argued that his actions violated both the letter and spirit of Myanmar's constitution and democratic principles.

### Civil War and Humanitarian Crisis

Myint Swe's role in legitimizing the 2021 coup made him complicit, in the eyes of critics, in the massive humanitarian crisis that followed. The coup triggered widespread civil disobedience, which the military met with lethal force. By early 2026, thousands of civilians had been killed by security forces, and Myanmar had descended into full civil war with multiple armed resistance groups fighting the military regime across the country.[^63] Millions of people were displaced, and the economy collapsed.[^64]

While Myint Swe held only a ceremonial role after declaring the state of emergency, with real power in the hands of Min Aung Hlaing, critics argued that he bore moral and legal responsibility for enabling the coup that triggered this catastrophe.

## Personal Life

Myint Swe maintained a relatively private personal life, with limited information available to the public about his interests, habits, or personal characteristics beyond his official roles. However, some details about his family, relationships, and personality emerged over his decades in the public sphere.

### Family

Myint Swe was married to Khin Thet Htay, who became a public figure in her own right due to her husband's high-ranking positions.[^65] She served as acting First Lady of Myanmar during the brief periods when Myint Swe served as acting president—first for ten days in March 2018 when President Htin Kyaw was abroad, and then more permanently following the February 2021 coup.[^66]

The couple had two children. Their daughter was named Khin Thet Htar Swe, and their son, Kyaw Phone Myint, followed his father into military service before retiring with the rank of Captain.[^67] All three family members survived Myint Swe and were present during his state funeral in August 2025.[^68] Little information is publicly available about his children's careers or lives, reflecting the general opacity surrounding Myanmar military elite families.

Myint Swe was born to U Kyee Maung and Daw Kyi in Mandalay.[^69] His family background was of Mon ethnicity, an important aspect of his identity in Myanmar's ethnically complex society, though one that did not appear to significantly shape his political stance or policies, which consistently favored military Bamar dominance.

### Personality and Character

Those who observed Myint Swe throughout his career noted his reputation for loyalty to his superiors, particularly Than Shwe. His willingness to carry out difficult and controversial orders, such as arresting Khin Nyunt and leading the crackdown on the Saffron Revolution, demonstrated a ruthless streak and an unwavering commitment to military hierarchy and authority.[^70]

Myint Swe's "bravado," as some observers noted, stemmed from his reported closeness to Than Shwe and the favor he enjoyed from the dictator's wife.[^71] This suggests a personality comfortable operating within patronage networks and willing to cultivate relationships with powerful figures. His deferential approach to Than Shwe was noted as a factor in his career advancement.[^72]

In his role as Yangon Commander and later as intelligence chief, Myint Swe developed a reputation as an enforcer—someone willing to use violence and intimidation to maintain the regime's control. His involvement in suppressing dissent and managing security operations suggested a personality that prioritized order and military control over democratic freedoms or human rights.

### Health Decline

In his final years, Myint Swe suffered from serious health problems that significantly diminished his capacity to carry out even the ceremonial duties of his acting presidency. Since early 2023, he experienced slower movement and difficulty eating, and medical tests confirmed he was suffering from Parkinson's disease—a progressive neurological disorder—along with related neurodegenerative conditions and peripheral neuropathy.[^73]

By 2024, his condition had deteriorated significantly. He experienced weight loss, loss of appetite, fever, cognitive decline, and was eventually placed in intensive care.[^74] In July 2024, citing medical leave, Myint Swe formally handed his powers as acting president to Min Aung Hlaing, who had been the de facto leader throughout.[^75] Myint Swe's declining health meant he was rarely seen in public during the final two years of his life, making him an even more shadowy figurehead than he had been previously.[^76]

### Lifestyle and Connections

As a close ally of Than Shwe, Myint Swe reportedly had access to significant wealth and maintained connections to the former dictator's extensive business interests and property holdings.[^77] This relationship suggested a lifestyle commensurate with Myanmar's military elite, who have long enriched themselves through control of state resources and business monopolies. However, specific details about his personal wealth, residences, or lifestyle remained largely hidden from public view.

## Legacy

Myint Swe died on 7 August 2025 at 8:28 AM at a military hospital in Naypyitaw, bringing to an end a career spanning more than five decades in Myanmar's military and political establishment.[^78] He was 74 years old. The military regime announced that he would receive a state funeral with full honors, which was held in Naypyitaw.[^79] His death prompted reflections on his role in Myanmar's turbulent history and the devastating civil war that followed the 2021 coup he had helped enable.

### Symbol of Military Authoritarianism

Myint Swe's most significant legacy is as a symbol of Myanmar's enduring military authoritarianism and the military's willingness to use violence against its own citizens to maintain power. From the 2007 Saffron Revolution crackdown to the 2021 coup, his career encompassed some of the darkest chapters in Myanmar's recent history.[^80] He represented the continuity of military control across different eras and under different guises—from direct junta rule to quasi-civilian government and back to military dictatorship.

### The Mon Military Pioneer

On a different level, Myint Swe's legacy includes his historic achievement as the first ethnic Mon to reach the rank of Lieutenant General in Myanmar's military.[^81] This broke a significant barrier in an institution traditionally dominated by the Bamar majority. However, this achievement is complicated by the fact that Myint Swe used his position to enforce military rule rather than to advance the interests of ethnic minorities, many of whom have long fought for autonomy and rights against the central military government.

### Enabler of the 2021 Coup

Perhaps most significantly, Myint Swe will be remembered as the man who provided constitutional cover for the 2021 military coup by declaring the state of emergency that transferred power to Min Aung Hlaing.[^82] This action triggered a series of events that plunged Myanmar into civil war, resulted in thousands of deaths, displaced millions, and set back Myanmar's development by decades.[^83] While he was never the primary decision-maker—that role belonged to Min Aung Hlaing and the military leadership—Myint Swe's willingness to play his part made him complicit in this tragedy.

At the time of his death, Myanmar remained in civil war, with resistance forces controlling significant territory and the military regime facing an uncertain future. In a November 2023 meeting of the National Defence and Security Council, Myint Swe himself had warned that the country was at risk of being "split into various parts" amid the conflict.[^84] This prescient observation, coming from someone who had helped trigger the crisis, highlighted the catastrophic consequences of the 2021 coup.

### A Puppet President

During his tenure as acting president from 2021 to 2024, Myint Swe was widely viewed as a figurehead with no real power.[^85] Min Aung Hlaing and the military leadership made all substantive decisions while Myint Swe provided a veneer of civilian authority. One analysis characterized the impotence of his position as taking a toll on him, though his declining health likely contributed more to his diminished public presence than any political frustration.[^86]

When Min Aung Hlaing formally assumed Myint Swe's presidential role in July 2024, it merely acknowledged what had been reality all along—that Myanmar was ruled by military commanders, not by the nominally civilian president.[^87] Myint Swe's legacy thus includes serving as proof that Myanmar's constitutional structures could be manipulated to provide cover for military dictatorship.

### Impact on Myanmar's Democratic Future

Myint Swe's role in ending Myanmar's democratic experiment has had profound consequences for the country's future. The brief period from 2011 to 2021 when Myanmar experimented with semi-democratic governance raised hopes that the country might transition peacefully to full democracy. The 2021 coup, which Myint Swe helped execute, crushed those hopes and demonstrated that Myanmar's military remained unwilling to cede power to civilian control.

His death in 2025 came at a moment when Myanmar's future remained deeply uncertain, with multiple armed groups controlling territory, the economy in ruins, and no clear path to peace or stability. In this sense, Myint Swe's legacy is one of destruction and lost opportunities.

### International Sanctions Legacy

Myint Swe died as a sanctioned individual, subject to asset freezes and travel bans by multiple countries including the United States, Canada, and the European Union.[^88][^89] This status reflected international condemnation of his role in both the 2007 crackdown and the 2021 coup. His inability to travel internationally or access assets in many countries was a symbolic acknowledgment of his culpability in human rights violations.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

From the perspective of Myanmar's military establishment and supporters of military rule, Myint Swe could be characterized as a loyal and effective officer who served his country and institution with dedication across multiple decades. His defenders within the military would point to several aspects of his career:

**Military Service**: Myint Swe spent his entire adult life in service to Myanmar's armed forces, rising through the ranks based on both competence and loyalty. He held important commands during periods of internal conflict and helped maintain security in key areas including Yangon.[^90]

**Ethnic Barrier Breaking**: As the first Mon officer to reach Lieutenant General rank, Myint Swe demonstrated that ethnic minorities could achieve the highest ranks in Myanmar's military, potentially opening doors for others.[^91] This represented a form of progress, however limited, in an ethnically stratified society.

**Institutional Loyalty**: Throughout various political transitions and power struggles, including the sensitive period following Than Shwe's retirement, Myint Swe maintained stability and demonstrated loyalty to Myanmar's military institution.[^92] From the military's perspective, this consistency was valuable during uncertain times.

**Constitutional Crisis Management**: Supporters of the 2021 coup within the military might argue that Myint Swe acted appropriately in invoking constitutional provisions to address what they characterized as electoral fraud in the 2020 elections. From this perspective, he was following established constitutional procedures, even if others disputed the legitimacy of those actions.[^93]

**Career Longevity**: Myint Swe's ability to remain in positions of power across multiple regimes and political configurations demonstrated political skill and adaptability. He successfully transitioned from active military service to political roles during the 2011-2021 quasi-democratic period, showing flexibility in changing circumstances.

However, it is important to note that these "positive" views are largely limited to those within Myanmar's military establishment or those who support authoritarian rule. They represent a minority perspective that prioritizes military control and institutional loyalty over democratic governance and human rights.

### Negative Views

The overwhelming international consensus and the view of democracy advocates, human rights organizations, and millions of Myanmar citizens is deeply negative toward Myint Swe's legacy and career:

**Human Rights Violator**: Myint Swe's leadership of the 2007 Saffron Revolution crackdown, which resulted in the deaths of peaceful protestors including Buddhist monks, marked him as a serious human rights violator.[^94] Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, and other organizations documented the abuses that occurred under his command.[^95] His denial of involvement and refusal to accept responsibility compounded this legacy.

**Genocide Denier**: Myint Swe's 2017 commission that denied evidence of crimes against humanity and genocide against the Rohingya people, despite overwhelming evidence from independent sources, represented a whitewash of one of the 21st century's most serious atrocities.[^96][^97] This denial made him complicit in the ongoing persecution of the Rohingya and the regime's refusal to accept accountability.

**Destroyer of Democracy**: By providing constitutional cover for the 2021 military coup, Myint Swe helped end Myanmar's democratic transition and triggered a catastrophic civil war that killed thousands and displaced millions.[^98] Political analysts characterize him as an enabler who allowed military hardliners to seize power while maintaining a veneer of legality.[^99]

**Puppet Figure**: Even from a critical perspective, Myint Swe was seen not as a powerful leader but as a willing puppet who served the interests of stronger figures—first Than Shwe, then Min Aung Hlaing.[^100] This characterization portrays him as lacking the moral courage to refuse orders or stand up for principles beyond military loyalty.

**Symbol of Impunity**: Myint Swe's career demonstrated how Myanmar's military officers could commit grave human rights violations without facing justice. The immunity provisions in the 2008 Constitution protected him from legal accountability for the 2007 crackdown, while his role in the 2021 coup occurred while he held high office.[^101] His death from natural causes rather than in a courtroom symbolized the continuing impunity enjoyed by Myanmar's military elite.

**Ruthless Enforcer**: Throughout his career, Myint Swe demonstrated a willingness to use violence, intimidation, and repression to maintain military control.[^102] Whether arresting Khin Nyunt, cracking down on monks, or legitimizing a coup, he consistently chose military power over human rights or democratic principles. This ruthlessness made him a feared figure among Myanmar's pro-democracy movement.

**Legacy of Destruction**: Myint Swe died leaving Myanmar in civil war, its democratic progress destroyed, its economy in ruins, and its people suffering under military dictatorship and armed conflict.[^103] While he was never the primary decision-maker, his willingness to play crucial supporting roles in military repression made him an accomplice to this destruction. The Irrawaddy, a respected Myanmar news outlet, characterized him in death as having gone "from ruthless enforcer to puppet president of Myanmar's military regime."[^104]

From this critical perspective, Myint Swe's legacy is one of tragedy—a man who repeatedly chose military loyalty and personal advancement over the wellbeing of Myanmar's people, contributing to some of the darkest chapters in the country's modern history.
