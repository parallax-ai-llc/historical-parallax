## Summary

Jimmy Connors won eight Grand Slam titles and held the world number one ranking for a record 268 weeks. He played professionally from ages 19 to 43.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|

| 1978 | Won the U.S. Open again, building a record-setting title count at the event |
| 1991 | Reached the U.S. Open semi-final at age 39, extending his reputation for longevity |

## Playing Style

Connors was known for aggressive baseline play, a flat, early-struck two-handed backhand, and a relentless competitive demeanor. His approach emphasized taking the ball early, pressuring opponents with pace, and turning matches into psychological battles.

## Rivalries and Era

His career overlapped with multiple generations of champions, leading to high-profile rivalries and memorable matches that helped define televised tennis in the 1970s and 1980s. Connors' confrontational on-court persona made him both polarizing and highly marketable.

## Longevity and Conditioning

One of Connors' distinguishing traits was competitive durability: he remained a relevant tour player well beyond the typical peak age for the sport. Deep runs later in his career contributed to a broader appreciation for professional fitness, recovery, and adaptation across decades.

## Honors and Records (High Level)

In addition to his major singles titles, Connors accumulated a large number of tour-level championships and spent extensive time ranked world No. 1. His overall win totals and sustained success are frequently used as benchmarks in discussions of all-time great careers.

## Legacy

Connors' influence is often described in terms of attitude and entertainment value: he helped popularize a more openly emotional, crowd-engaging style of star. For many fans, his career represents an era when tennis became a mainstream television sport in the United States.

He was later inducted into the International Tennis Hall of Fame.
