## Summary

Beryl Markham was a British-born Kenyan aviator, racehorse trainer, and author who led one of the most remarkable lives of the twentieth century. Raised in colonial Kenya, she became the first person to fly solo nonstop from east to west across the North Atlantic — against the prevailing winds — completing the crossing on September 4–5, 1936. Her memoir *West with the Night* (1942) is considered a classic of aviation and African literature.

Markham defied the conventions of her era at every turn: she was the first woman licensed as a professional horse trainer in Kenya, a bush pilot who scouted elephant herds for big-game hunters, and a writer whose prose drew lavish praise from Ernest Hemingway. Her life spanned three continents and encompassed achievements that would have been extraordinary for anyone, regardless of gender or era.

## Early Life & Background

Beryl Clutterbuck was born on October 26, 1902, in Ashwell, Leicestershire, England. In 1906, her father Charles Clutterbuck relocated the family to British East Africa (present-day Kenya), settling on a farm near Njoro. Her mother returned to England shortly afterward, leaving Beryl to be raised in an unconventional frontier environment.

She grew up alongside the children of Nandi farm workers, learning to hunt with spears and speak Swahili and Nandi fluently. Formal schooling was minimal. Her father trained racehorses, and Beryl developed an early and deep affinity for horses that would shape her first career. When her father's farm failed in 1919, rather than leave Kenya, she struck out on her own.

## Rise to Prominence

At 18, Markham obtained her horse trainer's license — the first woman in Kenya to do so — and within a decade had built a successful stable, producing multiple winners including Wise Child, who won the Kenya St. Leger in 1926. Her reputation as a trainer attracted the attention of influential figures in colonial Kenyan society.

In the late 1920s, she learned to fly, earning her commercial pilot's license in 1933. She worked as a bush pilot across East and West Africa, scouting elephant herds from the air and delivering mail and supplies to remote locations — dangerous work that honed her navigation skills under demanding conditions.

## Key Achievements

Markham's defining achievement came on September 4, 1936, when she departed Abingdon, England, in a Percival Vega Gull named *The Messenger*. After approximately 21 hours and 25 minutes, she crash-landed near Baleine Cove, Cape Breton Island, Nova Scotia — having crossed the Atlantic east to west solo and nonstop, a feat no one had accomplished before. The westward direction was significantly harder than Lindbergh's 1927 eastward crossing due to headwinds.

Her memoir *West with the Night*, published in 1942, received strong initial reviews but went out of print. Reissued in 1983, it became an international bestseller and is now widely regarded as one of the finest memoirs of the twentieth century.

## Controversies & Criticism

Questions have persisted about the authorship of *West with the Night*. Her third husband, writer Raoul Schumacher, is believed by some scholars to have substantially written or rewritten the book, based on letters and testimony from those who knew both. Markham consistently denied this. The extent of his contribution remains unresolved and contested among biographers.

Her personal life was turbulent: she married three times, had numerous affairs (including, reportedly, with the Prince of Wales), and was estranged from her son Gervase for much of his life.

## Legacy & Influence

Markham returned to Kenya in the 1950s and resumed training racehorses with considerable success, winning the Kenya Derby four times. She trained the 1986 Kenya Derby winner just months before her death on August 3, 1986, in Nairobi, at age 83.

Her transatlantic flight record and her memoir have secured her a lasting place in aviation history and twentieth-century literature. The Beryl Markham Trust in Kenya commemorates her contributions to the country she considered home.
