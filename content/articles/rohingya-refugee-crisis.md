## Summary

Over 1 million Rohingya refugees are living in camps in Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh after fleeing genocide in Myanmar. Conditions are dire; trafficking and exploitation are rampant.

## Background

The Rohingya are a predominantly Muslim ethnic minority from Myanmar's Rakhine State. Long-standing discrimination, statelessness, and restrictions on movement and access to services contributed to vulnerability well before the 2017 escalation.[^1]

## Key facts (high-level)

- **Scale**: One of the largest and fastest-growing refugee movements in the region in recent decades.[^1]
- **Location**: The largest concentration of refugees is in and around **Cox's Bazar**, Bangladesh.[^1]
- **Protection risks**: Overcrowding, limited formal work opportunities, and insecurity can increase risks of exploitation, trafficking, and gender-based violence.
- **Infrastructure strain**: High population density and fragile terrain make camps vulnerable to monsoon rains, landslides, and fires.

## Humanitarian situation

Living conditions in the camps are often characterized by:

- Limited space and shelter durability
- Pressure on water, sanitation, and health services
- Constraints on education and livelihoods
- Ongoing protection and mental health needs

## International response (overview)

Responses have included emergency shelter, food assistance, health services, protection programming, and coordination through UN agencies and humanitarian partners. Diplomatic efforts have largely focused on accountability for atrocities, humanitarian access, and conditions for safe repatriation.[^1]

## Why the crisis persists

Several structural barriers are frequently cited:

- Safety and security concerns in places of origin
- Lack of legal status and citizenship protections for Rohingya in Myanmar
- Limited durable solutions (return, local integration, or resettlement) at sufficient scale
- Chronic underfunding of humanitarian operations over time
