## Summary

Turkish human trafficker who organized deadly boat crossings of migrants across the Aegean Sea to Greece. His network was responsible for several capsized boats that killed dozens.

## Context: Aegean Migration Route

The Aegean Sea route between Turkey and Greece became one of the most visible corridors of irregular migration during the mid-2010s, particularly as conflicts and instability drove people to attempt entry into the European Union. Smuggling operations often relied on overcrowded, unseaworthy vessels and nighttime departures to evade patrols.

## Smuggling Networks (High-Level)

Human-trafficking and migrant-smuggling networks typically involve recruiters, transport coordinators, boat operators, and intermediaries handling payments and logistics. When boats capsize, fatalities can result from overcrowding, poor weather, lack of life jackets, and limited rescue windows.

## Law Enforcement and Prosecution

Turkish and Greek authorities, alongside European agencies, have periodically conducted investigations and interdictions targeting organizers rather than only boat operators. Cases tied to organizers are often prosecuted under human trafficking and facilitation statutes, with sentencing influenced by factors such as deaths at sea, repeat conduct, and the scale of the operation.

## Significance

Cases like Aydın's are frequently referenced in broader debates about:

- humanitarian protection for refugees and asylum seekers
- deterrence versus safe-legal pathways
- maritime search-and-rescue responsibilities
- how criminal enforcement intersects with migration policy
