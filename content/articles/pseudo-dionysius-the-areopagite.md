## Summary

Pseudo-Dionysius was an anonymous Syrian Christian theologian who wrote under the pseudonym of Dionysius the Areopagite, a convert of the Apostle Paul. His works, including The Celestial Hierarchy, The Divine Names, and Mystical Theology, profoundly influenced both Eastern and Western Christian mysticism. He developed the concepts of apophatic (negative) theology and the via negativa — approaching God by saying what God is not. His writings shaped medieval theology from Thomas Aquinas to Meister Eckhart.

## Authorship and Pseudonym

The name "Pseudo-Dionysius" is used because the author adopted the identity of Dionysius the Areopagite mentioned in Acts 17. This attribution helped the writings circulate with an aura of apostolic authority, even though modern scholarship generally dates them to the late 5th or early 6th century.

## Major Works

The core Dionysian corpus is typically described as:

- *The Divine Names* (on the ways God can be named and spoken of)
- *Mystical Theology* (a short, influential text on entering "unknowing")
- *The Celestial Hierarchy* (on angels and symbolic mediation)
- *The Ecclesiastical Hierarchy* (on church rites and orders)

The works combine Christian themes with concepts drawn from late antique Neoplatonism, especially the language of ascent, participation, and emanation.

## Key Ideas

- **Apophatic theology (via negativa):** God is beyond complete conceptual capture; language about God must include negation and humility.
- **Symbol and hierarchy:** Created realities can function as symbols that point beyond themselves, and structured "orders" can guide spiritual understanding.
- **Ascent and union:** Spiritual life is framed as a movement toward God that culminates not in clearer concepts but in a kind of contemplative union.

## Influence and Reception

Pseudo-Dionysius became a major source for Byzantine theology and later entered Latin medieval thought through translation and commentary. Thinkers such as Maximus the Confessor engaged and interpreted the corpus in the East, while figures in the West cited Dionysius in scholastic and mystical contexts.

The corpus also influenced Christian discussions about angels, liturgy, and the relationship between philosophy and theology.
