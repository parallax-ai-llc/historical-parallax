## Summary

Over 6.8 million Syrians have fled the civil war, creating Europe's worst refugee crisis since WWII. Millions more are internally displaced; neighboring countries bear the heaviest burden.

## Additional Context

### Drivers of displacement

Large-scale displacement from Syria is closely tied to the civil war’s violence, economic collapse, and the breakdown of basic services. Many families experienced repeated internal displacement before crossing international borders.

### Primary host countries

Neighboring states—particularly Türkiye, Lebanon, and Jordan—hosted substantial Syrian refugee populations for extended periods. Hosting at this scale affected housing, labor markets, education systems, and public services, and generated ongoing policy debates within host communities.

### Europe and irregular migration routes

From 2014–2016, increased arrivals in Europe brought the Syrian refugee crisis into the center of European politics and humanitarian response planning. Routes shifted over time in response to border policies, enforcement patterns, and bilateral agreements.

### Humanitarian needs and long-term challenges

Key issues frequently highlighted include legal status, access to work, education for children, health services, and the risks faced by refugees without stable documentation. Over time, the situation increasingly resembled a protracted displacement crisis rather than a short-term emergency.

### Durable solutions

Policy discussions often frame “durable solutions” as a mix of voluntary return when conditions allow, local integration where feasible, and third-country resettlement for vulnerable groups. Each option is constrained by security conditions, host-country capacity, and international political will.
