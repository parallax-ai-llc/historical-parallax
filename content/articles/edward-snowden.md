## Summary

American former NSA contractor who leaked classified surveillance programs in 2013, revealing mass global surveillance. Hailed as whistleblower and condemned as traitor. Fled to Russia where he received citizenship.

## Background

Snowden worked as a contractor and systems administrator with access to classified networks supporting U.S. intelligence activities. Open sources describe his access as enabling him to collect and remove large sets of documents later shared with journalists.

## The 2013 Disclosures (High Level)

The reporting associated with the leaked materials described surveillance programs and intelligence collection capabilities affecting both domestic and international communications. Public discussion often focused on:

- The scope of signals intelligence collection and metadata analysis.
- Legal authorities and oversight mechanisms governing surveillance.
- Implications for allied governments and cross-border data flows.

## Legal Status and Exile

U.S. authorities charged Snowden under the Espionage Act. He left the United States and ultimately remained in Russia, where his long-term residency status evolved over time, including later citizenship as reported in open sources.

## Public Debate and Policy Impact

Snowden’s case became a central reference point in debates about civil liberties, national security, transparency, and whistleblowing. It also contributed to:

- Increased scrutiny of intelligence oversight and secrecy.
- Litigation and legislative discussions regarding surveillance authorities.
- Changes in how major technology companies communicate about government data requests and encryption.

## Notes on Characterization

Whether Snowden is framed as a whistleblower, leaker, or traitor varies by legal definitions, political position, and moral evaluation. Many summaries distinguish between the factual disclosures and the normative judgments about their justification.
