## Summary

On October 2, 1187, the city of Jerusalem surrendered to the forces of Saladin, Sultan of Egypt
and Syria and founder of the Ayyubid dynasty.
The fall of the city ended eighty-eight years of Crusader control over Christendom's holiest site,
shocked the Latin Christian world, and triggered the Third Crusade.
It stands as one of the most significant military and religious events of the medieval period,
with consequences that reverberated across centuries of relations between the Islamic world,
Christendom, and the city itself.

The campaign that culminated in Jerusalem's fall was set in motion by Saladin's decisive victory
at the Battle of Hattin on July 4, 1187 — one of the most catastrophic defeats in the history of
the Crusader states.
The annihilation of the main Crusader field army, the capture of the relic of the True Cross,
and the taking prisoner of King Guy of Lusignan left Jerusalem and the other cities of the Latin
East almost entirely defenseless.
Saladin moved systematically through the coastal cities and interior towns before turning to
Jerusalem itself.

What distinguished Saladin's conquest most sharply in the historical record — and in popular memory
— was his conduct after the city's surrender.
In stark contrast to the 1099 Crusader conquest, in which the Muslim and Jewish populations of
Jerusalem were massacred, Saladin negotiated a conditional surrender that allowed Christians to
ransom their freedom and leave the city alive.
This act of restraint, widely noted by both Muslim and Christian contemporaries, cemented Saladin's
reputation as a model of chivalric virtue that would endure in both Islamic and European cultural
traditions for centuries.

## Historical Context

Jerusalem fell to the armies of the First Crusade in July 1099, after a siege of five weeks.
The city's Muslim and Jewish inhabitants were killed in a massacre that contemporary chronicles
describe with graphic horror and that medieval Islamic sources remember with lasting grief and
outrage.
The Crusaders established the Latin Kingdom of Jerusalem, a transplanted Western feudal polity in
the heart of the Islamic world, which at its greatest extent controlled the coastal Levant from
the Gulf of Aqaba to northern Syria.
For nearly a century, the kingdom maintained itself through military prowess, the defensibility of
its castles, the fragmentation of the surrounding Muslim world, and the continuing arrival of
crusading forces from Europe.

The political fragmentation of the Muslim Near East was the structural condition that made Crusader
survival possible.
After the initial shock of 1099, the Muslim response was hampered by the rivalry between the
Fatimid Caliphate of Egypt (Shia) and the Sunni Abbasid Caliphate of Baghdad, by divisions among
Seljuk Turkish successor states in Syria and Iraq, and by competition among local dynasties
(the Zengids, the Artuqids, the rulers of Damascus) for regional dominance.
Occasional Muslim counteroffensives — the recapture of Edessa by Zengi in 1144, which triggered
the unsuccessful Second Crusade — demonstrated the vulnerability of the Latin states but did not
produce the unified military pressure needed to destroy them.

Saladin's rise changed this structural situation fundamentally.
Born in Tikrit in 1137 to a Kurdish military family serving the Zengid dynasty, Saladin
(Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub — "Righteousness of the Faith, Joseph, Son of Ayyub") rose through
military service to become the most powerful ruler in the Muslim Near East.
Beginning as a subordinate and then successor to the Zengid ruler Nur ad-Din, he seized control
of Egypt in 1169, abolished the Fatimid Caliphate in 1171, reunified Egypt and Syria under his
personal rule after Nur ad-Din's death in 1174, and by the mid-1180s had encircled the Crusader
kingdom on virtually all sides.
His consistent political aim, articulated in religious terms as jihad, was the recovery of Jerusalem
and the expulsion of the Crusaders.

## The Event

### Trigger: Raynald of Châtillon's Truce-Breaking

The immediate trigger for the 1187 campaign was the breaking of a truce by Raynald of Châtillon,
lord of the Transjordan castle of Kerak, who attacked a Muslim caravan — which reportedly included
members of Saladin's family — in violation of a ceasefire agreed the previous year.
Raynald's repeated provocations, including an attempted naval raid on the Red Sea to threaten the
holy cities of Mecca and Medina, had long infuriated Saladin.
This latest violation gave him both a legitimate cause and a political opportunity to declare war
on the kingdom.

### The Battle of Hattin (July 4, 1187)

Saladin assembled a large army at the Golan Heights and crossed into Crusader territory in late
June 1187.
The Crusader barons debated how to respond.
Wiser counselors, including Raymond III of Tripoli, argued that the Crusaders should avoid a
pitched battle and rely on the kingdom's network of castles to wear down the Muslim forces.
But the aggressive faction — led by Raynald of Châtillon and the Master of the Temple, Gerard de
Ridefort — prevailed on King Guy to march to relieve the besieged city of Tiberias.

The march in the blazing July heat toward Tiberias proved fatal.
Saladin's forces harassed the Crusader column with mobile cavalry and archers while cutting off
access to water.
By the morning of July 4, the army — exhausted, thirsty, and encircled — was trapped on the slopes
of the twin hills called the Horns of Hattin.
The battle that followed was a massacre:
- The Crusader infantry was virtually annihilated.
- King Guy of Lusignan was captured.
- Raynald of Châtillon was captured and personally executed by Saladin.
- The True Cross relic was seized.
- Captured Knights Templar and Hospitaller were executed as too dangerous to release.

### The Fall of the Coastal Cities

With the field army destroyed, the cities and castles of the kingdom fell in rapid succession.
Acre, Jaffa, Sidon, Beirut, and Ascalon surrendered within weeks.
Only Tyre held out, defended by Conrad of Montferrat, who had arrived from Europe and organized
a determined resistance.
This Crusader holdout at Tyre would later serve as the launching point for the Third Crusade.

### The Siege and Surrender of Jerusalem (September 20 – October 2, 1187)

Jerusalem itself was garrisoned by a skeleton force of civilians and non-combatants under the
command of Balian of Ibelin, who had escaped Hattin.
Balian had been granted safe passage to Jerusalem by Saladin (to collect his family) but was
persuaded by the city's desperate inhabitants to take command of its defense.
He reportedly knighted every noble boy old enough to fight and organized the population for
resistance.

Saladin began the formal siege on September 20, 1187.
Jerusalem's walls were breached within days.
Balian negotiated with Saladin for terms of surrender.
Saladin's first offer — that Christians could purchase their freedom at forty dinars per man,
ten per woman, five per child — was rejected as impossible: too many of the city's poor could not pay.

After further negotiation, a collective arrangement was agreed:
- For a lump sum (variously reported as 30,000 or 100,000 dinars), the city's Christian population
  would be allowed to leave under safe conduct.
- Those who could not pay would be enslaved — permitted under Islamic laws of war.
- Saladin and his brother al-Adil reportedly released large numbers of the poorest prisoners
  without payment, though the exact extent of this generosity is debated.

On October 2, 1187 — a Friday, and the 27th of Rajab in the Islamic calendar, observed as the
anniversary of the Prophet Muhammad's Night Journey to Jerusalem (Isra and Miraj) — Balian
formally surrendered the city.
Saladin entered Jerusalem.
The churches were converted to mosques.
The fate of the True Cross relic became uncertain.
Eighty-eight years of Crusader rule over Jerusalem came to an end.
Between 15,000 and 20,000 Christians left under safe conduct.

## Multiple Perspectives

### Islamic Perspective: Saladin and the Concept of Jihad

For Saladin and the Muslim world, the reconquest of Jerusalem was far more than a military victory
— it was the fulfillment of a religious obligation that had haunted the Islamic world since 1099.
The fall of Jerusalem to the Crusaders had been experienced as a profound humiliation:
the third holiest city in Islam, home to the al-Aqsa Mosque and the Dome of the Rock, under the
control of Christian invaders who had desecrated it with a massacre of its inhabitants.
For nearly ninety years, Muslim rulers had been called upon to reclaim it, and all had failed.

Saladin's success was celebrated throughout the Islamic world as divine vindication.
His court historian Imad ad-Din al-Isfahani and biographer Baha ad-Din ibn Shaddad left vivid
accounts of the emotional impact of Jerusalem's recapture — of Saladin weeping with joy, of the
restoration of the al-Aqsa Mosque to Islamic worship, of jubilant delegations arriving from
Baghdad and across the Muslim world to offer congratulations.
The significance of the date — the anniversary of the Isra and Miraj — was understood as no
coincidence; it confirmed that the reconquest was an act of divine providence.

Saladin's political program consistently framed the campaign against the Crusaders in terms of
jihad — not merely as a political or military objective but as a sacred duty.
His court produced elaborate propaganda connecting the recovery of Jerusalem to the unity of the
Muslim *umma* (community), and the campaign validated Saladin's own authority against rival Muslim
rulers who questioned his legitimacy.
The treatment of the Christian population after surrender — the restraint, the negotiations,
the pardons — was not simply humanitarian impulse but also calculated political theater,
demonstrating Saladin's virtue as a Muslim ruler in contrast to the savagery of 1099.

### Crusader Christian Perspective

For the Latin Christian world, the fall of Jerusalem in 1187 was a catastrophe of almost
incomprehensible magnitude — a divine punishment visited on the sins of the Crusader states and
of Christendom as a whole.
Pope Gregory VIII issued the crusading bull *Audita tremendi* within weeks of receiving the news,
attributing the disaster to the moral failings of Christians and calling for repentance and a
new crusade.
The loss of the True Cross was, if anything, more psychologically devastating than the loss of
the city itself: the relic had been the army's talismanic protection, and its capture seemed to
signal the withdrawal of God's grace from the Latin enterprise in the East.

Crusader writers grappled with the disaster in terms of providential history.
The failure was blamed on internal divisions — on the factional conflict between Guy of Lusignan
and Raymond of Tripoli, on Raynald of Châtillon's recklessness, on the moral laxity of a Crusader
nobility grown comfortable in the East and forgetful of the stern virtues of crusading's founders.
The practical lesson drawn was the need for a united Crusade led by the great kings of Europe.

Yet even in Christian accounts, Saladin's conduct at Jerusalem's surrender generated grudging
respect.
The contrast with 1099 was impossible to ignore: a Muslim sultan had shown more mercy to
conquered Christians than Christians had shown to conquered Muslims.
European writers increasingly portrayed Saladin as a model of chivalric virtue — generous, merciful,
faithful to his word — even as they condemned the religion he represented.
This ambivalence fed into the complex medieval European literary tradition that produced Boccaccio's
laudatory portrait of Saladin in the *Decameron* and Lessing's dramatization of interfaith nobility
in *Nathan the Wise* (1779).
Saladin became, paradoxically, an icon of the very Christian chivalric values his victories had
challenged.

### Modern Comparative Analysis

Modern historians approach the events of 1187 with a range of analytical tools unavailable to
medieval contemporaries.
Military historians have examined the Battle of Hattin as a case study in the vulnerabilities of
Crusader strategy — the tension between aggressive and defensive schools of military thinking,
the logistical constraints on heavy cavalry armies in the arid Near Eastern summer, and the
excellence of Saladin's operational planning in forcing the Crusaders to choose between battle and
the abandonment of Tiberias.

The question of Saladin's "mercy" and its meaning has received particular scholarly attention.
Historians including Andrew Ehrenkreutz and Jonathan Phillips have pointed out that Saladin's
conduct at Jerusalem was not uniformly or consistently merciful:
- The execution of Raynald of Châtillon and the Templar and Hospitaller prisoners at Hattin,
  while explicable in political terms, was not gentle.
- The enslavement of those unable to pay ransom — which Islamic law permitted — affected thousands.
- The extent of Saladin's personal generosity in releasing impoverished prisoners is debated
  and may be exaggerated in hagiographic sources.

The contrast with 1099, while real, should not obscure the fact that Saladin's "mercy" operated
within the framework of medieval Islamic laws of war, and that its extent was partly determined
by practical and political calculations rather than purely humanitarian sentiment.

Modern scholarship also considers 1187 within the longer arc of Crusader history.
The siege demonstrates — in Saladin's deliberate cultivation of his own image of magnanimity —
how medieval rulers understood the political value of narrative, and how the story told about
conquest could matter as much as the conquest itself.
Saladin's reputation, carefully curated by his court historians, outlasted his dynasty and shaped
perceptions of Islamic governance in both the medieval and modern worlds.

## Legacy & Consequences

The most immediate consequence of Jerusalem's fall in 1187 was the Third Crusade (1189–1192),
led by three of Europe's greatest monarchs:
- Frederick I Barbarossa of the Holy Roman Empire (who drowned crossing a river in Anatolia before
  reaching the Holy Land)
- Philip II of France
- Richard I of England (Richard the Lionheart)

The Third Crusade successfully recaptured Acre after a prolonged siege and advanced toward
Jerusalem, but Richard ultimately concluded that the city could not be held without a larger force.
The Treaty of Jaffa (September 1192) between Richard and Saladin left Jerusalem in Muslim hands
but guaranteed Christian pilgrims safe access to the holy sites — a reflection of both the military
stalemate and the mutual respect that had developed between the two commanders.

Saladin died in Damascus on March 4, 1193, just months after the treaty.
He had given away most of his wealth in charity and left barely enough to cover his funeral expenses.
His reputation — already legendary in his lifetime — grew still further after death, as both
Islamic and European writers elaborated his virtues into an ideal of enlightened rulership.
In the twentieth century, Saladin became a central figure in Arab nationalist political culture:
Gamal Abdel Nasser and Saddam Hussein both invoked his memory explicitly, and his image appeared
on Syrian and Iraqi currency.

Jerusalem itself entered a long period of Muslim rule that lasted, with the brief interruption of
the Crusader reoccupation of 1229–1244, until the British conquest of 1917.
Under Saladin and the Ayyubid dynasty, and subsequently under the Mamluks, the city was maintained
as a site of Islamic scholarship and piety.
Christian pilgrimage continued under the terms negotiated in 1192.
The experience of 1187 also shaped the theology and practice of later Crusades: the emphasis on
penitence, the search for more efficient military organization, and the persistent theological
question of why God had permitted the loss of His city — a question that fueled crusading fervor
but also prompted the first stirrings of critical reflection on the entire enterprise.

## Key Facts at a Glance

- **Date of Jerusalem's surrender:** October 2, 1187
- **Saladin's dynasty:** Ayyubid (Kurdish origin)
- **Years Jerusalem was under Crusader rule:** 88 (1099–1187)
- **Decisive prior battle:** Battle of Hattin, July 4, 1187
- **Crusader king captured:** Guy of Lusignan
- **Commander of Jerusalem's defense:** Balian of Ibelin
- **Key distinction from 1099:** Negotiated surrender; Christians allowed to ransom themselves
- **Islamic significance of the date:** 27th Rajab — anniversary of the Prophet's Night Journey
- **Consequence:** Triggered the Third Crusade (Richard I vs. Saladin)
- **Treaty outcome:** Treaty of Jaffa (1192) — Jerusalem in Muslim hands; Christian pilgrims allowed
