## Summary

The horrific sea journey in which enslaved Africans were transported across the Atlantic in chains. Up to 1.8 million died in the crossing from disease, abuse, and suicide.

## Context and Scope

- **Definition:** The “Middle Passage” refers to the central leg of the transatlantic slave trade, transporting enslaved Africans primarily to the Americas.
- **Triangular trade:** Often described as part of a broader Atlantic commercial system linking Europe, Africa, and the Americas.
- **Geography:** Major embarkation regions included West and West-Central Africa; primary destinations included the Caribbean, Brazil, and North America.

## Conditions and Mortality Drivers

- **Confinement:** People were typically packed into cramped holds with limited ventilation and sanitation.
- **Disease:** Outbreaks of dysentery, smallpox, and other infectious diseases were common in the close quarters.
- **Violence and coercion:** Abuse by crews, forced feeding, and punishment were documented features of the crossing.
- **Psychological trauma:** Suicides and acts of self-harm were reported; shipboard trauma shaped long-term community memory.

## Resistance and Disruption

- **Shipboard resistance:** Enslaved people resisted through sabotage, hunger strikes, and organized uprisings at sea.
- **Crew response:** Captains and crews invested in restraints and surveillance to prevent revolts, increasing brutality.
- **Economic impact:** Resistance and mortality affected profits and influenced how ships were outfitted and managed.

## Abolition and Aftermath

- **Legal suppression:** Britain (1807) and the United States (1808) banned the Atlantic slave trade, though trafficking continued illegally for decades.
- **Naval enforcement:** Anti-slavery patrols (notably the British Royal Navy’s West Africa Squadron) intercepted some slaving vessels.
- **Persistence:** The trade to some destinations, especially in the Caribbean and Brazil, continued into the 19th century despite bans.

## Legacy

- **Demographic transformation:** The Middle Passage contributed to the forced African diaspora that reshaped societies across the Atlantic world.
- **Historical documentation:** Surviving ship logs, testimony, and abolitionist records are key sources for reconstructing conditions and scale.
- **Commemoration:** The Middle Passage is central to public memory of slavery, including museum interpretation and memorial projects.
