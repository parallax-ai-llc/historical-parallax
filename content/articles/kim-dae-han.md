## Summary

Perpetrator of the 2003 Daegu subway fire who killed 192 people by igniting containers of gasoline on a train. Sentenced to life imprisonment.

## Background

The Daegu subway fire took place in South Korea during a period of rapid urban growth and heavy reliance on mass transit. The incident exposed vulnerabilities in emergency response procedures, rolling-stock safety features, and crisis communication inside underground stations.

Kim Dae-han was identified as the perpetrator who deliberately set the fire, turning an ordinary commute into one of South Korea's deadliest peacetime disasters.

## The Daegu subway fire (2003)

On February 18, 2003, a fire was started on a train in the Daegu Metropolitan Subway. The blaze spread quickly, producing dense smoke and extreme heat.

A major contributor to the death toll was the combination of flammable interior materials, the underground environment, and failures in evacuation and operational decisions. Many victims died from smoke inhalation rather than burns.

## Legal outcome

Kim was arrested, tried, and sentenced to life imprisonment. The case remained prominent in South Korea as an example of mass-casualty arson and as a catalyst for safety reforms.

## Public impact and reforms

After the disaster, South Korean authorities and transit operators faced public pressure to improve fire safety standards, emergency training, and station design. The event is commonly cited in discussions of disaster preparedness and the importance of clear, rapid decision-making by operators during emergencies.
