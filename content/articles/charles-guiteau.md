## Summary

American who assassinated President Garfield in 1881. Shot him at a train station believing he deserved a political appointment.

## Background

- Guiteau was a lawyer and religious writer who struggled professionally and financially, and he developed grandiose beliefs about his own role in politics.
- He attached himself to the Republican Party’s internal factional disputes of the era, expecting rewards for what he considered political service.

## Assassination of President Garfield

- On July 2, 1881, Guiteau shot President James A. Garfield at a railroad station in Washington, D.C.
- Garfield did not die immediately; his death later that year is often discussed in medical history because complications and infection were exacerbated by contemporary treatment practices.

## Trial and Execution

- Guiteau’s trial became a major public spectacle, with significant attention to questions of sanity, responsibility, and motive.
- He was convicted and executed by hanging in 1882.

## Significance

- The assassination contributed to calls for civil-service reform, as it underscored the dangers of patronage expectations and political spoils.
- Guiteau remains a recurring example in discussions of political violence, notoriety, and the limits of the insanity defense in high-profile cases.

## In Popular Memory

- The event is frequently revisited in biographies of Garfield and in histories of the Gilded Age.
- Primary sources include trial transcripts and contemporary newspaper coverage.
