## Summary

North Korean hackers stole $620 million from the Ronin Network bridge used by the Axie Infinity game. It was the largest cryptocurrency hack at the time.

## What Happened

The exploit targeted the **Ronin Bridge**, a cross-chain bridge that allowed users to move assets between the Ronin sidechain (built for Axie Infinity) and Ethereum. Attackers gained control of a majority of the bridge’s validator keys and used that access to authorize fraudulent withdrawals.

Public reporting around the incident emphasized:

- The bridge relied on a small validator set for signing withdrawals.
- Attackers obtained enough validator approvals to bypass normal withdrawal security.
- The stolen funds were primarily denominated in **ETH** and **USDC**.

## Response and Aftermath

Key high-level consequences and responses described in coverage of the event include:

- **Disclosure and bridge shutdown:** The exploit was discovered and disclosed in late March 2022, after which the bridge was paused to prevent further withdrawals.
- **Attribution:** U.S. government statements and blockchain-tracing analyses publicly attributed the hack to North Korea’s **Lazarus Group**.
- **Recovery efforts:** Investigations, law-enforcement coordination, and on-chain tracking were used to pursue recovery; some funds were later reported as recovered or frozen, while substantial amounts remained laundered through mixing and intermediary services.

## Broader Significance

The Ronin hack is frequently cited as a case study for bridge risk:

- Bridges can concentrate risk when validator sets are small or key management is weak.
- Operational security (key custody, access controls, monitoring) can be as critical as smart-contract correctness.
- Large, game-linked ecosystems can become high-value targets when they custody significant liquidity.
