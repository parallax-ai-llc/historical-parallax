## Summary

Car bombing in Omagh, Northern Ireland by the Real IRA on August 15, 1998 that killed 29 people including a woman pregnant with twins. It was the deadliest single attack of the Troubles.

## Background

The Omagh bombing occurred during a fragile phase in the Northern Ireland peace process, shortly after the Good Friday Agreement. The Real IRA opposed the settlement and continued a campaign of violence.[^1]

## Attack and Immediate Aftermath

The bombing is remembered both for its human toll and for the confusion surrounding warnings and the car’s location. The details of warnings, policing response, and the sequence of events have been subject to extensive scrutiny.[^1]

Key elements frequently discussed include:

- the placement of the car bomb in Omagh town centre,
- the timing and wording of telephone warnings,
- the rapid escalation from warning to detonation,
- emergency response and community support after the blast.[^1]

## Investigations and Legal Actions

Criminal investigations and prosecutions faced difficulties, and the case also involved significant civil litigation. The event prompted changes and debate around counter-terrorism, intelligence coordination, and how to handle dissident republican violence.[^1]

## Significance

The bombing is often described as a turning point that reduced public tolerance for dissident paramilitary campaigns and reinforced momentum for political settlement, even as it deepened grief and trauma for victims’ families.[^1]
