## Summary

Merck's painkiller Vioxx caused an estimated 27,785 heart attacks before being withdrawn. Internal documents showed Merck knew of the risks but concealed them. Paid $4.85 billion in settlements.

## What Vioxx Was

Vioxx (rofecoxib) was a prescription painkiller in the COX-2 inhibitor class, developed to reduce pain and inflammation while aiming to lower certain gastrointestinal side effects associated with older NSAIDs. Its blockbuster status made it one of the most prominent drug launches of its era.

## Safety Signal and Withdrawal (High Level)

The scandal narrative centers on allegations that cardiovascular risk signals were visible in studies and post-market data while marketing remained highly aggressive. Estimates of harms vary across analyses; figures commonly quoted in the public record come from specific epidemiological methods and assumptions.

The product was ultimately withdrawn from the market after evidence of elevated cardiovascular risk became widely accepted.

## Litigation and Corporate Aftermath

The case became a landmark in modern pharmaceutical litigation, with outcomes including:

- large-scale civil settlements,
- intense scrutiny of how clinical trial data were interpreted and communicated,
- and pressure for stronger post-market surveillance and risk communication.

## Broader Significance

Vioxx is often cited in policy debates about the tradeoffs between rapid access to new therapies and the time needed to detect rare but severe side effects at population scale.
