## Summary

Donald Trump convicted of 34 felony counts of falsifying business records to conceal hush money payments to adult film actress Stormy Daniels before the 2016 election.

## Background (High-Level)

- "Hush money" in U.S. political and legal contexts typically refers to payments made to prevent the public disclosure of potentially damaging personal allegations.
- In this matter, public reporting described a payment to Stormy Daniels (Stephanie Clifford) in the period leading up to the 2016 U.S. presidential election.
- The legal dispute centered on how related reimbursements and records were characterized in business documentation.

## Core Legal Theory (Generalized)

- The case is widely summarized as involving **falsification of business records**, with prosecutors arguing that records were falsified to conceal the true purpose of payments.
- Such cases often turn on: (1) what the business entries said, (2) what the payments were for, and (3) whether intent existed to mislead or conceal.

## Key People Commonly Mentioned

- **Donald J. Trump:** Defendant.
- **Stormy Daniels (Stephanie Clifford):** Recipient of the hush-money payment described in reporting.
- **Michael Cohen:** Former attorney to Trump, frequently discussed in connection with arranging or facilitating the payment in public accounts.

## Procedural Notes

- Criminal cases of this type typically involve pretrial motions, jury selection, witness testimony, and documentary evidence focused on accounting entries and communications.
- Post-verdict proceedings commonly include sentencing, appeals, and litigation over evidentiary or legal rulings.

## Significance

- The case drew attention as a rare instance of a former U.S. president facing (and being convicted of) felony charges in a state criminal court.
- It is often cited in debates about campaign-related conduct, disclosure norms, and how personal scandals intersect with election timelines.
