## Summary

Nigerian general and oil billionaire accused of complicity in the murders of leaders during military coups. Became one of Africa's richest men through oil concessions.

## Background and military career

Danjuma joined the Nigerian Army and rose through the officer corps during a period marked by repeated coups and counter-coups. He is commonly associated with the 1966 counter-coup that brought a change in military leadership and contributed to deepening ethnic and political tensions in Nigeria.

He later served in senior command roles, including as Chief of Army Staff in the mid-1970s. In that capacity he was part of the military establishment that oversaw another transfer of power following the 1975 coup that removed General Yakubu Gowon.

## Business interests and wealth

After leaving top military posts, Danjuma built substantial business interests, particularly in the petroleum sector. He has been linked in public reporting to oil and gas concessions and private-sector holdings that contributed to his reputation as one of Nigeria’s wealthiest individuals.

Wealth accumulation through resource concessions and political connections is a recurring theme in commentary about his post-military career, especially given Nigeria’s history of patronage networks around extractive industries.

## Philanthropy and public role

Danjuma has also been associated with charitable giving and organized philanthropy, including initiatives that fund health and education programs. Public philanthropy has helped shape his image domestically and internationally alongside more controversial narratives about his military-era influence.

In later years he periodically made public statements about security and governance challenges in Nigeria, reflecting continued engagement with national affairs despite formal retirement from government.

## Controversies

Allegations connected to the coup era and political violence have followed Danjuma’s public profile for decades, with critics asserting complicity in or knowledge of killings during periods of military rule. Such claims are politically charged and are often discussed in the broader context of Nigeria’s unresolved accountability for coup-period abuses.
