## Summary

First major dark web marketplace for drugs and illegal goods operated by Ross Ulbricht from 2011-2013. It processed over $200 million in bitcoin transactions before FBI shut it down.

## Additional Context

### How the Marketplace Worked

Silk Road operated as an online marketplace accessible via the Tor network, which obscures user locations and helps provide anonymity. The site used Bitcoin for payments and incorporated an escrow and feedback system, attempting to mimic legitimate e-commerce patterns while facilitating illegal trade.

### Ross Ulbricht and “Dread Pirate Roberts”

Ross Ulbricht was identified by U.S. authorities as the primary operator and was associated with the alias “Dread Pirate Roberts.” His case became widely discussed because it combined internet anonymity, cryptocurrency finance, and questions about how to attribute responsibility for third-party transactions conducted through a platform.

### Investigation and Takedown

The FBI seized Silk Road in 2013 after a multi-agency investigation. Public accounts describe the investigation as involving undercover purchases, tracing of communications, server identification and seizure, and digital evidence gathered from the operator’s devices.

### Legal and Policy Significance

Silk Road is often treated as a landmark event for modern cybercrime enforcement. It accelerated attention to cryptocurrency tracing, motivated improvements in operational security among criminal markets, and influenced debates about online platform liability and the scope of punishment for facilitating illicit commerce.

### Aftermath and Successor Markets

The takedown did not end darknet markets; instead, multiple successor markets emerged, often copying the same model. This created a continuing cycle in which markets appear, grow, and are eventually disrupted through hacks, scams, or law enforcement action.
