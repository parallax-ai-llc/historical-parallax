## Summary

Richard Henderson is a British molecular biologist who shared the 2017 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for cryo-electron microscopy.

## Scientific Context

- Structural biology seeks to determine the three-dimensional structures of biological macromolecules (such as proteins) to understand how they work.
- **Cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM)** became a transformative method because it can visualize biomolecules without requiring crystallization.

## Research Themes (high level)

- Henderson is associated with early and influential work on membrane proteins, including **bacteriorhodopsin**.
- His career is often discussed alongside the broader development of electron microscopy methods applied to biological systems.

## Nobel Prize (2017)

- The 2017 Nobel Prize in Chemistry recognized advances that enabled cryo-EM to produce high-resolution structures of biomolecules.
- The prize is frequently described as marking cryo-EM’s shift into a mainstream tool for modern biochemistry and drug discovery.

- Cryo-EM is often described as bridging the gap between X-ray crystallography and earlier lower-resolution electron microscopy by enabling high-resolution structures for many complexes.
- Henderson’s work is frequently referenced in discussions of methodological progress in structural biology.
