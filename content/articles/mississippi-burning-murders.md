## Summary

Three civil rights workers — Chaney, Goodman, and Schwerner — were murdered by KKK members and police in Mississippi. Their disappearance prompted a massive FBI investigation.

## Background

- In the summer of 1964, civil rights organizations ran the "Freedom Summer" campaign in Mississippi to register Black voters and challenge segregation.
- Violence and intimidation by white supremacist groups, including the Ku Klux Klan (KKK), were common, and local law enforcement in some areas was complicit or hostile to civil rights activity.

## The Victims

- **James Earl Chaney** — a local Black activist from Mississippi.
- **Andrew Goodman** — a white volunteer from New York.
- **Michael Schwerner** — a white organizer working with civil rights efforts in Mississippi.

## Disappearance and Murder (June 1964)

- The three men traveled to investigate the burning of a Black church and were stopped by local law enforcement.
- After their release, they were abducted and murdered by a group including KKK members; their bodies were later concealed.

## Federal Investigation

- Their disappearance triggered a major FBI effort (commonly referred to as "MIBURN" in FBI files) because state and local authorities were unwilling or unable to pursue the case effectively.
- The case became a national symbol of the dangers faced by civil rights workers and of the limits of local justice in the Jim Crow South.

## Prosecutions and Legal Aftermath

- Because Mississippi did not pursue murder charges at the time, the initial major prosecutions were **federal civil-rights conspiracy** cases.
- Decades later, additional state-level proceedings occurred; the case remained a reference point in discussions of delayed justice for civil-rights-era crimes.

## Impact and Legacy

- The murders helped galvanize public support for federal civil rights enforcement and contributed to broader momentum for civil-rights legislation in the mid-1960s.
- The events have been widely documented in journalism, scholarship, and popular culture; they are often cited in accounts of Freedom Summer and the struggle for voting rights.

## Notes

- This article uses the common shorthand "Mississippi Burning Murders" for the 1964 killings of Chaney, Goodman, and Schwerner.
