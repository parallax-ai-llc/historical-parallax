## Historical Context

Psamtik III (also rendered *Psammetichus III*) was the final ruler of Egypt’s 26th Dynasty (the Saite period). He came to the throne during a time of intense external pressure from the expanding Achaemenid Persian Empire under Cambyses II.

Key backdrop points:

- The 26th Dynasty had reasserted Egyptian independence after earlier periods of foreign domination.
- By the mid-6th century BCE, Persia was consolidating power across the Near East, making Egypt a strategic target.
- Egyptian defense depended on internal cohesion and regional alliances that were difficult to sustain.

## Reign and Fall

Psamtik III’s reign is typically dated to about 526–525 BCE and is remembered primarily for the Persian conquest of Egypt.

High-level sequence:

- Accession after the reign of Amasis II.
- Persian invasion led by Cambyses II.
- Defeat of Egyptian forces and capture of the pharaoh.

## Capture and Death (Traditional Accounts)

Later narratives emphasize that Psamtik III was taken prisoner after Egypt’s defeat and brought under Persian control. Some accounts describe subsequent execution after he was suspected of involvement in plots or rebellion against Persian rule.

Because surviving sources are uneven and often written long after the events, many details are presented in simplified or moralized form in popular retellings.

## Significance and Legacy

Psamtik III is significant less for policies than for what his fall represents:

- The end of the Saite dynasty and the close of a major phase of native pharaonic rule.
- Egypt’s incorporation into the Persian Empire as a satrapy, reshaping its political and military landscape.
- A transition point often used by historians to frame the broader shift from Late Period independence to successive eras of imperial rule.
