## Summary

Alicia Keys (born Alicia Augello Cook, January 25, 1981) is an American singer, songwriter, pianist, and producer whose fusion of R&B, soul, and classical piano placed her among the defining artists of the 2000s. Her debut album *Songs in A Minor* (2001) announced her as a rare combination of technical virtuosity and emotional depth at a moment when the music industry seldom produced classically trained pop stars.

Over a career spanning more than two decades, Keys has sold over 65 million records worldwide, won 15 Grammy Awards, and become a prominent philanthropist and social activist. Her influence extends beyond music into film, Broadway, and global advocacy work, particularly around HIV/AIDS awareness through the organization Keep a Child Alive.

---

## Early Life & Background

Keys was born and raised in the Hell's Kitchen neighborhood of Manhattan, New York City, to a flight attendant mother and an absent father. She began studying classical piano at age seven and was accepted into the Professional Performing Arts School, from which she graduated as valedictorian at 16. She briefly enrolled at Columbia University on a scholarship before leaving to focus on her music career.

Her classical training at the Manhattan Conservatory of Music instilled a technical foundation that distinguished her from peers in the R&B landscape. She signed with Columbia Records as a teenager but clashed creatively with the label before moving to Clive Davis's J Records, where she would record her breakthrough debut.

---

## Rise to Prominence

*Songs in A Minor*, released June 5, 2001, debuted at number one on the Billboard 200, selling 236,000 copies in its first week. The album produced the iconic single "Fallin'," which topped the Billboard Hot 100 for six weeks. Keys won five Grammy Awards at the 2002 ceremony, including Best New Artist and Song of the Year.

Subsequent albums — *The Diary of Alicia Keys* (2003), *As I Am* (2007), and *The Element of Freedom* (2009) — each debuted at number one and generated hits including "If I Ain't Got You," "No One," and "Try Sleeping with a Broken Heart." Her 2012 collaboration with Jay-Z on "Empire State of Mind" became a cultural anthem for New York City.

## Key Achievements

- 15 Grammy Awards from 36 nominations across multiple categories
- Over 65 million records sold worldwide
- Co-founder of Keep a Child Alive, an HIV/AIDS charity founded in 2003
- Co-wrote and produced her Broadway musical *Hell's Kitchen* (2023), which won six Tony Awards including Best Musical
- Served as a coach on NBC's *The Voice* across multiple seasons
- Received a star on the Hollywood Walk of Fame in 2020

---

## Controversies & Criticism

Keys faced criticism early in her career following a 2008 interview in which remarks about figures like Tupac Shakur were interpreted as glorifying criminal iconography, prompting backlash she later clarified. Her 2010 marriage to producer Swizz Beatz, who was still legally married to another woman at the time, drew tabloid scrutiny and public criticism. She has also been criticized by some observers for perceived inconsistency between her advocacy positions and certain commercial partnerships.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Keys reshaped expectations for what an R&B artist could be — classically trained, compositionally sophisticated, and commercially dominant simultaneously. Artists including John Legend, H.E.R., and Jazmine Sullivan have cited her as a direct influence. *Hell's Kitchen*, her semi-autobiographical Broadway debut, demonstrated a creative range that secured her standing not just as a pop figure but as a serious theatrical voice. Her philanthropy through Keep a Child Alive has raised tens of millions of dollars for communities affected by HIV/AIDS in Africa and the United States.
