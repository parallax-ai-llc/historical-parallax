## Summary

Ruth Bader Ginsburg (March 15, 1933 – September 18, 2020) was an American jurist who served as an Associate Justice of the Supreme Court of the United States from 1993 until her death in 2020. Appointed by President Bill Clinton, she was only the second woman ever confirmed to the Court, following Sandra Day O'Connor. Over a career spanning more than five decades, Ginsburg became one of the most consequential legal advocates and jurists in American history.

Before joining the bench, Ginsburg was a pioneering litigator who systematically dismantled gender-based legal discrimination. As co-founder of the ACLU's Women's Rights Project in 1972, she argued six landmark cases before the Supreme Court, winning five. On the Court, she became a reliable member of its liberal wing and, in her later years, a cultural phenomenon known by the moniker "the Notorious RBG."

## Early Life & Background

Joan Ruth Bader was born on March 15, 1933, in Brooklyn, New York, to Celia and Nathan Bader, Jewish immigrants from Eastern Europe. Her mother, who never attended college, instilled a deep love of learning in her daughter before dying of cervical cancer the day before Ginsburg's high school graduation in 1950.

Ginsburg enrolled at Cornell University on a full scholarship, graduating first in her class in 1954. She married Martin D. Ginsburg that same year. She entered Harvard Law School in 1956, one of only nine women in a class of approximately 500. When her husband took a job in New York, she transferred to Columbia Law School, where she tied for first in her class and earned her law degree in 1959.

Despite her academic record, Ginsburg was rejected by multiple law firms and was denied a Supreme Court clerkship by Justice Felix Frankfurter due to her gender.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Unable to find work at a private firm, Ginsburg clerked for a federal district judge and later joined the faculty of Rutgers Law School in 1963. In 1972, she became the first tenured female professor at Columbia Law School. That same year, she co-founded the ACLU's Women's Rights Project, through which she strategically litigated cases to establish constitutional equality between men and women under the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause.

President Jimmy Carter appointed her to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit in 1980, where she served for 13 years before President Clinton nominated her to the Supreme Court in 1993. She was confirmed by the Senate 96–3.

---

## Key Achievements

- Argued and won landmark gender discrimination cases including *Reed v. Reed* (1971) and *Frontiero v. Richardson* (1973).
- Served 27 years on the Supreme Court, authoring opinions in major cases including *United States v. Virginia* (1996), which struck down the Virginia Military Institute's male-only admissions policy.
- Wrote notable dissents in *Ledbetter v. Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co.* (2007), which prompted Congress to pass the Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act of 2009, and in *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013), defending the Voting Rights Act.
- Became the first Supreme Court Justice to officiate a same-sex wedding (2013).

---

## Controversies & Criticism

Ginsburg faced substantial criticism from legal scholars and progressives for declining to retire during President Obama's second term (2013–2016), when a replacement could have been confirmed by a Democratic Senate. She stated publicly she did not believe any president would nominate an unqualified justice, a judgment critics considered naive given subsequent political developments.

In 2016, she drew rare public rebuke after calling then-candidate Donald Trump a "faker" in multiple press interviews, later acknowledging the remarks were "ill-advised."

---

## Legacy & Influence

Ginsburg's death on September 18, 2020, eight weeks before the presidential election, triggered an immediate political crisis over her replacement. President Trump nominated Amy Coney Barrett, who was confirmed in October 2020.

Her legal legacy is substantial: her pre-judicial advocacy helped establish constitutional sex-equality doctrine, and her judicial opinions shaped civil rights, reproductive rights, and voting rights law for decades. The "Notorious RBG" cultural phenomenon — spawning books, films, a 2018 documentary, and an opera — made her one of the most publicly recognized jurists in American history.
