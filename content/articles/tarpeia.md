## Historical and Mythic Context

Tarpeia appears in Rome’s foundation legends during the early conflicts between Romans and Sabines. The story is transmitted through later Roman and Greek authors rather than contemporary records, and it is typically treated as mythic tradition rather than verifiable biography.

In common retellings, Tarpeia is associated with the Capitoline Hill and its citadel. The episode functions as an origin story for a moral lesson: betrayal motivated by personal gain leads to ruin.

## The “What They Wore on Their Arms” Motif

Many versions emphasize the ambiguity of Tarpeia’s demand for what the Sabines “wore on their arms.” In some tellings, this refers to gold bracelets; in others, it also includes shields. The motif reinforces the idea that a bargain made in bad faith can be turned against the betrayer.

## Legacy in Roman Moral Imagination

- **Exemplar of treachery:** Roman writers used the tale to illustrate the social and religious taboo against betraying the community.
- **Place-name associations:** The Tarpeian Rock (Saxum Tarpeium) on the Capitoline later became linked in Roman tradition with punishment for certain crimes, especially treason.
- **Cultural afterlife:** Tarpeia appears intermittently in later literature and art as a symbol of corruption, greed, or political betrayal.

## Notes on Sources

Because Tarpeia is a legendary figure, accounts vary across authors and periods. When citing Tarpeia historically, it is usually more accurate to describe her as a character in Roman foundation mythology than as a documented person.
