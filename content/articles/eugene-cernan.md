## Summary

Eugene Andrew Cernan was an American astronaut and naval aviator who holds the distinction of being the last human to walk on the Moon. On December 14, 1972, during the Apollo 17 mission, Cernan stepped back into the lunar module after a final EVA on the lunar surface — a moment that remains unmatched more than five decades later. His three spaceflights spanned the Gemini and Apollo programs, making him one of only three people to travel to the Moon twice.

Cernan's career embodied the ambition and sacrifice of the Space Race era. A consummate pilot and mission commander, he combined technical precision with a deep sense of mission. His famous parting words on the Moon — "We leave as we came, and, God willing, as we shall return, with peace and hope for all mankind" — captured both the achievement and the unfinished promise of human space exploration.

## Early Life & Background

Eugene Cernan was born on March 14, 1934, in Chicago, Illinois, to a Czech-Slovak immigrant family. He grew up in the Chicago suburbs and developed an early interest in aviation and engineering. He earned a Bachelor of Science in Electrical Engineering from Purdue University in 1956, then joined the U.S. Navy, completing flight training and becoming a carrier-based naval aviator. He later earned a Master of Science in Aeronautical Engineering from the U.S. Naval Postgraduate School in Monterey, California in 1963, the same year NASA selected him as part of its third astronaut group.

## Rise to Prominence

Cernan's first spaceflight came on Gemini 9A in June 1966, where he performed a spacewalk — only the third American to do so. The EVA proved grueling; without adequate restraints, Cernan struggled to complete his tasks and his suit overheated, forcing an early return inside. The experience directly shaped how NASA redesigned EVA equipment and procedures for future missions. He flew again on Apollo 10 in May 1969, serving as lunar module pilot alongside Tom Stafford and John Young. Apollo 10 was the dress rehearsal for the Moon landing, descending to within 15.6 kilometers of the lunar surface without landing — a mission that required extraordinary discipline given how close they came.

## Key Achievements

Cernan commanded Apollo 17, which launched on December 7, 1972 — the only nighttime launch of the Saturn V rocket. The mission carried geologist Harrison Schmitt, the first professional scientist on the Moon. Cernan and Schmitt spent three days on the lunar surface in the Taurus-Littrow valley, conducting three EVAs totaling 22 hours and 4 minutes. They collected 110.5 kilograms of lunar samples and deployed scientific instruments across the surface. Apollo 17 set records for longest lunar landing mission (12.5 days), longest time on the lunar surface, and largest lunar sample return. Cernan accumulated over 566 hours in space across his three missions.

## Controversies & Criticism

Cernan was occasionally criticized for his outspoken advocacy that the United States return humans to the Moon, which some viewed as self-promotional. His 1999 memoir *The Last Man on the Moon* and subsequent documentary of the same name were praised for candor but drew scrutiny for his frank discussion of marital strain caused by his career devotion. He was also a vocal critic of NASA's direction under the Obama administration, signing a 2010 open letter alongside Neil Armstrong and Jim Lovell opposing the cancellation of the Constellation program.

## Legacy & Influence

Cernan's legacy is inseparable from the symbolic weight of being last. His footprints on the Moon remain undisturbed, a monument to a period of human achievement that has not been repeated. He became a prominent advocate for space exploration throughout his later life, testifying before Congress and inspiring generations of engineers and astronauts. NASA's Artemis program, which returned astronauts to lunar orbit in 2022 and aims to land humans on the Moon again, is in part a direct response to the challenge Cernan issued for decades. He died on January 16, 2017, in Houston, Texas, at age 82.
