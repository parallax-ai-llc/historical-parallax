## Summary

Colombian-French politician kidnapped by FARC guerrillas while campaigning for president and held captive in the jungle for over six years. She was rescued in a dramatic Colombian military operation.

## Background

- Betancourt is a Colombian politician with strong ties to France, often described as Colombian–French in international coverage.
- Her presidential campaign took place during a period in which kidnappings by armed groups were a major feature of Colombia’s internal conflict.

## Kidnapping and Captivity

- She was seized while traveling in a conflict-affected area and remained in FARC custody for years.
- Reports about her captivity emphasize harsh conditions, frequent relocation, and the strategic value of high-profile hostages in insurgent bargaining.

## Rescue Operation

- The 2008 rescue operation is widely portrayed as a major intelligence and military success for the Colombian state.
- The event had significant political and diplomatic effects, influencing perceptions of the conflict and Colombia’s security institutions.

## Public Role After Release

- After her release, Betancourt engaged in public advocacy related to hostages, human rights, and Colombia’s peace-and-security debates.
- She has remained a visible figure in discussions about reconciliation, accountability, and the long-term impacts of political violence.

## Notes on Sources

- Accounts of the kidnapping, captivity, and rescue are covered across Colombian media, international press, and official statements; narratives can differ in emphasis depending on the source and political framing.
