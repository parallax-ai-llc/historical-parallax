## Summary

Cuban revolutionary and younger brother of Fidel who served as Cuba's head of state from 2008 to 2018. He commanded the armed forces for decades and oversaw political repression. He implemented limited economic reforms.

## Context and Background

Raúl Castro Ruz emerged as a key figure in the Cuban Revolution alongside his brother Fidel Castro. After 1959, he became one of the most powerful officials in the revolutionary government, particularly through his long tenure leading the Revolutionary Armed Forces.

## Governance and Policy

As head of state, Castro was associated with a cautious approach to economic change. His government expanded limited market mechanisms and encouraged small-scale private enterprise while maintaining one-party political control.

## Security and Human Rights Concerns

International human rights organizations and foreign governments have criticized the Cuban state during Castro’s era for restrictions on political pluralism, limits on free expression and association, and the use of security services to monitor and suppress dissent.

## Foreign Relations

Castro’s leadership overlapped with a period of shifting regional and global dynamics for Cuba, including changes in relations with the United States and continued ties with partners such as Venezuela and Russia. Cuba also sustained an active foreign policy in Latin America and Africa through diplomacy, intelligence, and security relationships.

## Legacy

Castro is often described as a continuity leader: a long-serving institutional figure who preserved the revolutionary political system while authorizing incremental economic adjustments. His retirement from formal offices marked a generational transition, though the core political structure he helped build remained in place.

Analysts often distinguish between his decades-long influence within the military and party apparatus and his later, more formal role as head of state.
