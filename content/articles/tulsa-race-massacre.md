## Summary

White mobs destroyed the prosperous Black neighborhood of Greenwood in Tulsa, Oklahoma, killing 100-300 people and burning 1,256 homes. Known as 'Black Wall Street,' the district was obliterated.

## Background

Greenwood in Tulsa, Oklahoma, was a thriving African American community in the early 20th century, with a dense concentration of Black-owned businesses, professionals, and institutions. The district became widely nicknamed "Black Wall Street" (a later popular label) because of its economic activity and relative self-sufficiency under segregation.

## Spark and Escalation

The violence followed an accusation involving Dick Rowland, a Black teenager, and Sarah Page, a white elevator operator. As local newspapers inflamed tensions and a lynching was feared, Black veterans and other community members went to the courthouse to help prevent Rowland from being taken by a white mob. Confrontations near the courthouse escalated into shootings and then into a larger, organized attack on Greenwood.

## The Attack (May 31–June 1, 1921)

Over roughly 18 hours, white mobs looted and burned large portions of Greenwood. Contemporary and later accounts describe widespread arson, killings, and the destruction of homes, churches, schools, and businesses. Official numbers at the time severely understated deaths; modern scholarship commonly estimates roughly 100–300 people were killed, with many more injured and displaced.

## Immediate Aftermath

Thousands of Black residents were left homeless and many were temporarily detained in makeshift holding areas. Property losses were immense; more than a thousand houses were destroyed, along with much of the commercial district. Insurance claims were often denied on the grounds that the damage was related to "riot" conditions, complicating rebuilding.

## Long-Term Impact and Legacy

Greenwood residents rebuilt parts of the neighborhood in the following years, but the massacre’s trauma and economic damage reverberated across generations. For decades, the event was omitted from many public histories and school curricula, and survivors’ testimonies were often marginalized.

In the late 20th and early 21st centuries, renewed research and public attention led to official investigations, including a state commission report (early 2000s). Debates continue regarding restitution, reparations, and the responsibilities of local and state institutions.

## Commemoration and Public Memory

The centennial in 2021 prompted expanded public programming, memorialization efforts, and additional historical work. There have also been efforts to locate and identify victims’ burial sites, including archaeological and forensic investigations in Tulsa.
