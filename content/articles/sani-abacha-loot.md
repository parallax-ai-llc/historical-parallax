## Summary

$5 billion stolen by Nigerian dictator Abacha, portions still being recovered from banks worldwide decades later.

## Background

The term “Sani Abacha loot” is commonly used to describe funds and assets allegedly stolen from the Nigerian state during the period of military rule under Sani Abacha.
Recovery efforts have involved multiple jurisdictions and years of legal actions, settlements, and repatriation agreements.

## What Was Taken (High-Level)

Public reporting describes a mix of:

- Direct diversion of state funds and central-bank resources.
- Use of intermediaries and front companies to move money abroad.
- Deposits and asset holdings in foreign financial centers.

Figures vary by source, and the commonly cited headline number is on the order of billions of US dollars.

## Recovery and Repatriation (High-Level)

Recovery has typically required:

1. Identifying assets tied to the Abacha-era networks.
2. Freezing orders or restraints in relevant jurisdictions.
3. Negotiations and court processes for forfeiture or return.
4. Repatriation arrangements to Nigeria, sometimes with external monitoring or conditions on use.

Countries and territories frequently mentioned in reporting include Switzerland, the United Kingdom, the United States, and certain offshore jurisdictions.

## Why It Matters

The Abacha loot is often cited as a landmark example of:

- Cross-border corruption and kleptocracy.
- The role of banks and intermediaries in enabling illicit flows.
- The complexity and duration of asset recovery, even when governments cooperate.
