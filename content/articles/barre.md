## Identity and naming

- In Somali political history, “Barre” in this context typically refers to **Mohamed Siad Barre** (often styled *Siad Barre*), who ruled Somalia from 1969 until 1991.
- Some sources and indexes list him under the surname “Barre,” but he is most commonly discussed in English-language works as Siad Barre.

## Rise to power

- Barre came to power through a military coup in 1969, following the assassination of President Abdirashid Ali Sharmarke.
- The Supreme Revolutionary Council (SRC) became the governing body, and political parties were banned.

## Governance and ideology (high-level)

- The regime promoted “scientific socialism” and pursued state-led modernization, literacy campaigns, and centralized administration.
- Over time, critics and human-rights reporting described increasing authoritarianism, security-service repression, and politicization of clan identities.

## Foreign relations and conflict

- Somalia’s Cold War alignments shifted over time, influenced by regional conflict and superpower competition.
- The **Ogaden War** (1977–1978) against Ethiopia is often treated as a major turning point, with long-term military, economic, and political consequences.

## Decline and collapse

- By the late 1980s, armed opposition movements expanded and state authority weakened.
- The regime was overthrown in 1991, and Somalia entered a prolonged period of civil war and fragmented governance.

## Legacy

- Assessments of Barre’s rule are sharply divided: some emphasize early-state-building efforts, while others foreground repression and the role of the dictatorship in contributing to state collapse.
- His period in power remains central to debates about Somali nationalism, clan politics, and the challenges of post-conflict reconstruction.
