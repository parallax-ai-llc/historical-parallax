## Summary

Henny Youngman was a British-born American comedian and violinist whose rapid-fire one-liner style made him one of the most influential stand-up comics of the twentieth century. Best known for his signature line "Take my wife... please," he performed relentlessly for over six decades, earning the title "King of the One-Liners." His economy of language and precise comic timing set a template that shaped generations of comedians.

Youngman bridged the worlds of Borscht Belt comedy, radio, television, and live performance, remaining a working comedian well into his nineties. His prolific output — estimated at thousands of jokes — made him a cultural fixture in American entertainment from the 1930s onward. More than a performer, he was a craftsman who treated joke-writing as a discipline and comedy as a trade.

## Early Life & Background

Henry Youngman was born on March 16, 1906, in London, England, to a Jewish family that emigrated to the United States when he was an infant, settling in Brooklyn, New York. Growing up in Brooklyn's working-class neighborhoods, he developed an early interest in music and comedy. He studied violin as a child and considered a career as a professional musician before gravitating toward comedy.

His entry into show business came through the Catskill Mountains resort circuit — the "Borscht Belt" — where Jewish comedians honed their craft before predominantly immigrant audiences. This environment rewarded speed, wordplay, and a relentless pace, qualities that became hallmarks of Youngman's style.

## Rise to Prominence

Youngman gained his first major national exposure on radio, appearing on Kate Smith's radio program in 1937 after being invited as a last-minute replacement. His performance was a success, and bookings followed rapidly. Throughout the 1940s and 1950s he became a staple of New York nightclub and theater circuits, performing hundreds of dates per year.

Television expanded his audience considerably. He appeared on numerous variety programs and talk shows, including multiple appearances on *The Ed Sullivan Show* and *The Tonight Show*. His willingness to perform anywhere — banquets, corporate events, nightclubs, casinos — kept him in constant demand even as comedic fashions changed around him.

## Key Achievements

Youngman recorded multiple comedy albums and published joke collections, including *500 All-Time Greatest One-Liners*. He appeared in several films and television series, including a memorable cameo in *Goodfellas* (1990). He performed his final shows in his early nineties, completing an active career spanning roughly seven decades. His estimated repertoire of 10,000 jokes made him one of the most prolific joke writers in the history of American comedy.

## Controversies & Criticism

Youngman's humor reflected the sensibilities of mid-twentieth century American comedy and drew criticism in later decades for relying on ethnic stereotypes and wife jokes that critics found dated or dismissive. His material was largely unchanged across decades, which some reviewers noted as a limitation. He was also criticized for prioritizing quantity of performance over artistic evolution.

## Legacy & Influence

Youngman's influence on stand-up comedy is substantial. His one-liner format informed comedians including Rodney Dangerfield, Steven Wright, and Mitch Hedberg. The phrase "Take my wife... please" entered the cultural lexicon as a shorthand for the form itself. He died on February 24, 1998, in New York City, at age 91, still regarded as the definitive practitioner of the compressed comic form.
