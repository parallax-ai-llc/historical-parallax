## Definition

The “Golden Crescent” is a term used to describe a major opium- and heroin-producing region centered on Afghanistan and extending into neighboring Pakistan and Iran. It is often discussed alongside the “Golden Triangle” in Southeast Asia as one of the world’s historically significant drug-production areas.

## Drivers of production

Analyses of the region commonly point to overlapping factors:

- prolonged conflict and weak state control in rural areas
- high-value, low-weight agricultural economics (opium poppy as a cash crop)
- corruption and rent-seeking along trafficking routes
- global demand for opiates and the profitability of refining and smuggling

## Trafficking and enforcement

Opiates produced in Afghanistan have been trafficked along multiple routes, including:

- overland routes through Iran and Turkey toward Europe
- routes through Pakistan toward maritime shipment
- regional distribution networks supplying nearby markets

Efforts to reduce production have included eradication campaigns, alternative-livelihood programs, interdiction operations, and (at times) bans by Afghan authorities. Results have varied by year and by local security conditions.

## Broader impacts

- **Public health**: heroin markets drive addiction and overdose risk in destination countries.
- **Regional security**: revenues can fuel armed groups and criminal organizations.
- **Economic distortion**: dependence on illicit crops can undermine governance and legal development.
