## Summary

Western governments seized over $58 billion in Russian oligarch assets after the Ukraine invasion including superyachts worth hundreds of millions. Some oligarchs rushed to hide their vessels.

## Background and Context

After Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, the United States, the European Union, the United Kingdom, and other partners expanded sanctions programs targeting Russian elites and state-linked entities. Superyachts became prominent targets because ownership could be linked to sanctioned individuals and because high-value vessels are difficult to move or sell while under sanctions.

## How Seizures and Detentions Worked

Common pathways used by governments and ports included:

- **Sanctions enforcement**: freezing assets connected to designated individuals/entities.
- **Customs and documentation actions**: detentions based on registration, export-control, or import violations.
- **Civil forfeiture / judicial seizure**: court-authorized seizure where authorities alleged criminal or sanctions-evasion conduct.
- **Port-state control**: administrative holds for safety/compliance issues (often separate from sanctions but sometimes coinciding).

## Notable Publicly Reported Cases (Examples)

- **Amadea** (Fiji → United States): seized in 2022 after court proceedings; became a high-profile U.S. case tied to sanctions enforcement efforts.
- **Tango** (Spain): detained in 2022 in connection with sanctions targeting a designated oligarch.
- **Axioma** (Gibraltar): detained amid disputes over ownership/financing; later sold through legal proceedings.
- **Phi** (United Kingdom): detained in London in 2022 under U.K. sanctions-related measures.
- **Scheherazade** (Italy): detained in 2022; widely reported as linked to sanctioned interests, with ownership and control contested in public reporting.

*(Specific ownership allegations and legal outcomes vary by jurisdiction and case; some actions were detentions or freezes rather than permanent forfeiture.)*

## Operational Challenges

Authorities and ports faced recurring issues:

- **Complex ownership structures** (shell companies, trusts, layered registration) that obscured beneficial owners.
- **High custody costs** for crews, maintenance, insurance, and dockage while vessels were held.
- **Litigation and diplomatic friction** over evidence standards, due process, and competing claims.
- **Asset deterioration risk** if maintenance funding or access to service providers was restricted.

## Aftermath and Disposition

Outcomes generally fell into a few buckets:

- **Prolonged detention** pending litigation or sanctions review.
- **Managed sale** under court or insolvency processes (in some cases).
- **Transfer to government custody** with ongoing cost debates.
- **Release** where sanctions status changed or courts found insufficient basis for continued detention.
