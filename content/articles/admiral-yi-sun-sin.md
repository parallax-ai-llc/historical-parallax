## Summary

Admiral Yi Sun-sin (1545-1598) stands as one of history's greatest naval commanders, celebrated for his undefeated record in 23 naval battles during the Japanese invasions of Korea (1592-1598). Despite facing political persecution, torture, and imprisonment from his own government, Yi revolutionized naval warfare through his development of the turtle ship (geobukseon) and brilliant tactical innovations including the crane wing formation. His most legendary victory came at the Battle of Myeongnyang, where he defeated a Japanese fleet of 133 ships with only 13 vessels, demonstrating extraordinary strategic genius and unwavering courage.[^1][^2] Yi died as he lived—in battle—killed by a stray bullet while pursuing retreating Japanese forces, his final words urging his men to conceal his death until victory was secured.[^3]

---

## Early Life and Background

Yi Sun-sin was born on April 28, 1545, in Geoncheon-dong Street, Hanseong (present-day Inhyeon-dong, Jung-gu District, Seoul), into the prestigious Deoksu Yi clan during the reign of King Injong of the Joseon Dynasty.[^4] Though born into a yangban (aristocratic) family, Yi's childhood was marked by poverty and political hardship. His grandfather, Yi Baeg-nok, had retired from politics following the execution of neo-Confucian reformer Jo Gwang-jo in the Third Literati Purge of 1519, retreating to a village near where Jo was buried.[^5] Yi's father, Yi Jeong, shared this disillusionment with court politics and chose not to enter government service as expected of their noble lineage, instead living a modest life away from the capital.[^6]

In 1552, after Yi's father was wrongly imprisoned and tortured by the government, the family moved from Seoul to Asan in Chungcheongnam-do province, his mother's hometown, where Yi spent most of his formative years.[^7] This early exposure to injustice and his family's diminished status would profoundly shape Yi's character, instilling in him both a deep sense of principle and a wariness of court politics that would later prove prescient.

Yi received a traditional Confucian education during his childhood, excelling in both literary and martial disciplines. His parents initially wanted him to continue the family tradition of literary scholarship, and he studied Chinese history and philosophy extensively.[^8] However, in 1566, at age 21, Yi made a decisive choice that would alter the course of Korean history—he decided to pursue a military career and began studying military science, archery, horseback riding, and swordsmanship.[^9] He married Lady Bang in 1564, and together they would have four children: three sons named Hoe (born 1567), Yo (born 1571), and Myon (born 1577), and one daughter.[^10] Keeping with the practices of the time, he also had a concubine with whom he fathered four additional children.[^11]

Yi's characteristic determination and stamina were first noted in 1572 during his military examinations when he fell from his horse and broke his leg. Rather than withdraw, he is said to have rigged a makeshift splint from a willow branch and completed the riding exam despite his injury.[^12] After an initial failure, he finally passed the Military Service Examination (mukwa) in February 1576 at the relatively late age of 32, beginning his career as a defense soldier at the bottom rank in the northern frontier region.[^13] One of the most consequential events of his early life was meeting and befriending Ryu Seong-ryong, a prominent scholar who would later hold key official positions and whose support would prove crucial to Yi's survival and success during the war years.[^14]

## Rise to Power

Yi Sun-sin's military career began modestly but was marked from the start by both exceptional competence and persistent obstacles created by jealous superiors and court politics. After passing the military examination in 1576, he served at various army and navy posts along Korea's vulnerable northern frontier, defending against Jurchen tribal incursions.[^15] In 1583, Yi successfully led a campaign against Jurchen tribes, demonstrating early military leadership that caught the attention of higher authorities.[^16]

However, Yi's rigid adherence to principles and refusal to ignore corrupt practices quickly earned him powerful enemies. Two years after his first posting, he refused to overlook corrupt practices, earning the enmity of his immediate superiors who stripped him of his command.[^17] During an inspection tour, Admiral Yi Yong reported Yi Sun-sin for three cases of absent shifts found in Balpo, though considerable absences were witnessed in other posts under Yi Yong's command. Yi conducted his own investigation which forced charges to be dropped, but his admiral continued attempts to discredit him in official evaluations.[^18] Yi's stubborn refusal to play the political game would have terrible repercussions throughout his career.

His brilliance and accomplishments so early in his career made his superiors jealous, leading to false accusations of desertion during battle in a conspiracy led by General Yi Il. Yi was stripped of his rank, imprisoned, and tortured during this earlier incident—a brutal preview of the persecution he would face again at the height of his success.[^19] In 1567, King Seonjo cleared Yi Sun-sin's father's name after coming to power, providing some measure of family redemption.[^20]

Yi's path to naval command came through the recommendation of his childhood friend Ryu Seong-ryong, who recognized both Yi's military genius and his integrity. In 1591, Yi was appointed commander of the naval forces in Left Jeolla province, a position that would prove providential as war loomed on the horizon.[^21] Upon taking command, Yi immediately concentrated on rigorous training of his men, stocking equipment and supplies, and developing the renowned geobukseon (turtle ship)—an armored warship that would become legendary in Korean naval history.[^22] He introduced strict discipline, ensuring consistent crew performance under pressure, and transformed uncoordinated coastal vessels into a formidable naval force.[^23]

Yi's preparations proved visionary. In 1592, Toyotomi Hideyoshi gave the order to invade Korea with the ultimate goal of sweeping through the peninsula to conquer Ming China. The first assault wave of over 24,000 men, carried in over 800 ships, arrived at Busan in May 1592, catching the unprepared Korean land forces off guard and advancing rapidly northward.[^24] While Korean armies collapsed before the Japanese onslaught, Yi's well-prepared naval forces would prove to be Korea's salvation, controlling the seas and severing Japanese supply lines in a series of brilliant victories that established him as the nation's greatest defender.

## Major Achievements

Admiral Yi Sun-sin's military achievements represent one of the most remarkable records in naval history. He fought in at least 23 naval battles during the Japanese invasions of Korea and won every single one without losing a ship, often while commanding forces that were outnumbered and poorly supplied.[^25] His victories were not merely tactical successes but strategic masterstrokes that fundamentally altered the course of the war and saved Korea from complete subjugation.

**Early Naval Victories (1592)**

Despite never having commanded a naval battle in his life prior to the war, Yi won the Battle of Okpo, Battle of Sacheon, and several others in quick succession after the Japanese invasion began.[^26] At the Battle of Okpo in May 1592, the Korean fleet of 91 ships detected 50 Japanese vessels plundering a harbor. The Korean fleet encircled the Japanese ships and destroyed all 11 warships with artillery bombardments, completing the victory without losing a single vessel.[^27] The Battle of Sacheon in July 1592 marked the first combat deployment of the turtle ship. Yi ordered a feigned withdrawal to lure 12 Japanese ships out of the harbor's safety, then countered with the turtle ship leading the charge, successfully destroying all enemy vessels. Yi was shot in his left shoulder during this battle but survived.[^28]

**Battle of Hansan Island (August 15, 1592)**

The Battle of Hansan Island stands as one of Yi's greatest tactical masterpieces. Yi employed the "hakikjin" (crane wing maneuver), a siege-and-annihilation method traditionally used only for land battles, adapting it brilliantly for naval warfare.[^29] Yi sent a small detachment forward to lure the Japanese fleet into open waters off Hansan Island. When the Japanese took the bait, Yi's fleet spread out in the shape of a crane's wing, surrounding the enemy, and using powerful warship cannons, all ships began firing simultaneously.[^30] The Korean fleet managed to destroy roughly 100 Japanese ships in this encounter, halting Japanese naval operations along the southern coast and securing control of the vital sea lanes.[^31]

**The Turtle Ship Innovation**

Although many credit Yi with inventing the turtle ship, it was actually a centuries-old Korean creation that Yi improved and integrated into his tactics.[^32] Yi's turtle ships were fitted with iron spikes—camouflaged during battle by mats—on the curved upper deck to discourage boarding. The ships featured a dragon's head through which cannons could be fired or sulfurous smoke projected to intimidate enemies.[^33] The upper deck was covered with armored plates to protect the crew, and spikes and knives attached to the plates prevented enemies from boarding.[^34] From their first appearance in the Battle of Sacheon, turtle ships contributed to winning 16 battles against the Japanese fleet.[^35]

**Political Persecution and Dismissal (1597)**

At the height of his success, Yi fell victim to court intrigue. In 1597, he was relieved of command, placed under arrest, and taken to Seoul in chains to be imprisoned and tortured almost to the point of death.[^36] The Joseon government, plagued by factionalism driven by jealousy, manipulated King Seonjo to view Admiral Yi as a potential traitor. It is believed that King Seonjo and his court feared Yi's victories and reputation amongst the people as foundations for a revolt.[^37] King Seonjo wanted Yi executed, but his supporters at court, chiefly Prime Minister Ryu Seong-ryong, convinced the king to spare him due to his past service record.[^38]

Yi was replaced by Won Gyun, an incompetent naval commander who immediately squandered the Joseon navy's strength through ill-conceived maneuvers. Without adequate reconnaissance, Won Gyun attacked with the entire naval force of 150 warships operated by 30,000 men. The Japanese ambushed the Joseon fleet at the Battle of Chilchonryang on August 28, 1597, virtually wiping it out, with only 13 warships surviving.[^39] Won Gyun was killed, and Yi Sun-sin was hastily reinstated as Supreme Commander of the Regional Navies.[^40]

**Battle of Myeongnyang (October 26, 1597)**

With only 120 naval soldiers and 12 battle ships surviving the disaster, Yi faced his greatest challenge. He studied numerous sites for his last stand and chose the Myeongnyang Strait, approximately 300 meters wide at its narrowest point, notorious for powerful tidal currents reaching speeds up to 10 knots that reversed direction every three hours.[^41] The terrain provided multiple advantages: currents, eddies, and whirlpools allowed ships to enter safely only a few at a time; the north-south tidal flow limited Japanese offensive time; and the strait's narrowness prevented the Japanese from flanking or enveloping Yi's numerically inferior fleet.[^42]

On that day, heavy mist dramatically reduced visibility in favor of the Joseon fleet, and the deep shadows of surrounding hillsides provided concealment. Yi led his 13 ships to victory against a Japanese fleet of at least 133 vessels, destroying over 30 Japanese ships without losing a single Korean vessel or suffering significant casualties.[^43] Korean ships with flat bottoms provided more stable and accurate cannon firing platforms than the Japanese ships with keel bottoms, while the melee-focused Japanese found themselves unable to fight effectively against superior Joseon ranged fire.[^44]

Before this crucial battle, Yi left one of his most famous quotes, instructing his generals: "According to the Principles of Strategy, he who seeks his death shall live, and he who seeks his life shall die."[^45] When King Seonjo determined that the kingdom's naval forces could not defeat Japanese forces, Admiral Yi responded with words that would echo through Korean history: "Your Majesty, we still have 12 ships left."[^46]

**Final Victory and Death (December 16, 1598)**

Yi's final battle came at Noryang on December 16, 1598. A huge Japanese fleet under Shimazu Yoshihiro was amassed in Sachon Bay. Yi commanded 82 panokseon and three turtle ships with 8,000 soldiers, alongside Ming Chinese naval forces.[^47] The battle began around 2:00 am. As the Japanese retreated, Yi ordered a vigorous pursuit. During this time, a stray arquebus bullet struck Admiral Yi near his left armpit.[^48] Sensing the wound was fatal and fearing his death would demoralize his troops, Yi uttered his final words: "The war is at its height—wear my armor and beat my war drums. Do not announce my death!"[^49] Only his eldest son Hoe and his nephew Wan witnessed his death, struggling to regain their composure and carrying the admiral's body into his cabin before others could notice.

Yi's ability to disrupt Japanese supply lines stands as one of his lasting legacies. His technological and strategic innovations sparked a revolution in Asian naval warfare and initiated both the "modern" naval force and style of combat.[^50] Yi's battle strategies remain famous enough to be published in modern naval academy textbooks of the United States, the United Kingdom, and Japan.[^51]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his legendary status, Admiral Yi Sun-sin faced significant controversies during his lifetime, primarily stemming from political persecution and court intrigue rather than military failures. The primary controversies surrounding Yi relate to the unjust treatment he received from his own government and the political machinations that nearly cost Korea its greatest defender.

**Political Persecution and Court Intrigue**

The Joseon government during Yi's time was deeply plagued by factionalism, with the court split into "Easterners" and "Westerners" struggling for favor and position throughout the war.[^52] Yi Sun-sin, seen as an outsider by established scholar officials, became a target of the West Faction.[^53] Since he lacked political and economic power and was rigidly stuck to principles, he often had tensions with and faced false accusations by his superiors.[^54]

The ministers despised the successful and virtuous admiral, manipulating King Seonjo to view Yi as a potential traitor.[^55] The contrast in their public images was stark: King Seonjo accomplished nothing during the war, and his cowardly flight to Uiju left his reputation in ruins, while Yi's victories made him beloved by the people.[^56] This created a dangerous dynamic where the king viewed his most successful general with suspicion and fear rather than gratitude.

**The 1597 Imprisonment and Torture**

Admiral Yi was apparently ordered to destroy Japanese naval units far out at sea under conditions in which he could not but fail. Some historians suggest there were no Japanese naval forces operating in that area, and Yi knew this, but the court was so set against him that if he failed to engage the non-existent force, it would be perceived that he was a coward or that he warned the enemy.[^57] When Yi refused to follow what he believed was a strategic trap set by the Japanese, scheming Korean court members seeking to undermine the Easterner Faction cited Yi's "failure" to act and accused him of "disloyalty and disobedience."[^58]

Yi was tortured using simple torture tactics such as whipping, flogging, burning, the cudgel, and leg-breaking torture, almost to the point of death.[^59] Despite this brutal treatment, Yi refused to confess to the false charges brought against him.[^60] The persecution demonstrated the dysfunction of the Joseon court at a critical moment when national survival hung in the balance.

**Modern Historical Debates**

In modern times, some historians have criticized the hero-making narrative surrounding Yi Sun-sin. Scholarly assessment has evolved, with some asserting that a historical view centered purely on hero-making and patriotism should be abandoned in favor of a more humanistic approach to Yi's life, viewing him as a person rather than solely as a mythical hero.[^61] A drama series about Yi was criticized for taking many artistic licenses, such as depicting Yi as weak and lonely in his early life and taking liberties with the events surrounding his death.[^62]

However, these modern criticisms focus primarily on the mythologization of Yi rather than on substantive critiques of his actual military conduct or character. There is very little genuine criticism of his military accomplishments in historical scholarship.[^63] Today, King Seonjo is regarded as one of the most incompetent rulers in Joseon history due to his inability to control factionalism in his government and his unjust mistreatment of Admiral Yi Sun-sin.[^64]

The tragic irony of Yi's career is that his greatest obstacles came not from the enemy he defeated repeatedly on the battlefield, but from the government and king he served with unwavering loyalty. His ability to overcome political persecution while simultaneously defending his nation against overwhelming odds speaks to both the strength of his character and the dysfunction of the political system he operated within.

## Personal Life

Admiral Yi Sun-sin's personal life reveals a complex man who struggled with profound emotional burdens while maintaining outward stoicism and unwavering dedication to duty. His war diary, the Nanjung Ilgi, provides intimate glimpses into his inner world, showing a leader who wrestled with anxiety, depression, chronic pain and illness, alcohol consumption, grief, and mounting frustration with his own government and fellow officers.[^65]

**Family and Relationships**

Yi married Lady Bang in 1564 when he was 19 years old, and they had four children together: three sons—Hoe (born 1567), Yo (born 1571), and Myon (born 1577)—and one daughter.[^66] His sons played important roles in his professional life, with his eldest son Hoe serving alongside him and witnessing his final moments at the Battle of Noryang.[^67] Lady Bang had a very strong personality, and no one in the house disobeyed her words. She did not approve of her son-in-law Hongbi because he was small in stature and did not allow him into the house.[^68]

Tragically, Yi lost his third son as a consequence of his victory at Myeongnyang. The defeated Japanese forces raided his home in Asan and retaliated against his family, killing his son in an act of vengeance.[^69] His war diary reveals his constant concern for his family, especially his mother and his sons, showing the personal cost of his military service.[^70]

The friendship between Yi and Ryu Seong-ryong, which began in their youth, proved crucial throughout Yi's career. Ryu held key official positions including Dochaechalsa and was in command of the military during the Japanese invasions. His political support was critical to Yi's achievements, and he saved Yi from execution twice, convincing King Seonjo to spare his childhood friend due to his past service record.[^71] Ryu later wrote "Jingbirok," a memoir of the Imjin War, which helped preserve Yi's story for future generations.[^72]

**Personality and Character**

Admiral Yi is remembered for remarkable personal qualities that combined military genius with profound humanity. While his prowess as a military reformer earned him accolades, he is also admired for his compassion. In the face of bitter conflict, Yi never lost sight of the plight of victims of war.[^73] Though he bore terrible wounds and calmly faced death with thoughts only for his men and the preservation of his state, his stoicism was not so complete as to protect him from the pain arising from personal disgrace and the loss of close friendships and family.[^74]

After his mother's funeral, Yi began to grieve over his own plight, lamenting "What shall I do? Is there anyone else under heaven in such a pitiful plight as myself?—the sooner I die the better." He broke into tears when dismounting from his horse and sat up late at night all alone as sadness incessantly swept over him.[^75] Yet despite these moments of despair, he bent but did not break; his will was strengthened by his sense of duty.[^76]

Yi was dutiful, stoic and brave in battle, earning the respect of his men as well as the common people of Joseon Korea.[^77] He was supported by the people not only for his victories but for his kindness and gratitude toward those affected by the hardships of war.[^78] According to historical assessments comparing him to Admiral Horatio Nelson, "Nelson is far behind Yi in terms of personal character and integrity."[^79]

Internal squabbling among his peers and royal politics resulted in him being imprisoned and tortured not once but twice.[^80] His stubborn adherence to principle over political expediency made him vulnerable to those who manipulated the court system, yet he remained incorruptible even when torture threatened his life. This unwavering integrity, combined with his military genius and compassion for ordinary people, explains why Yi Sun-sin remains beloved in Korea more than four centuries after his death.

## Legacy

Admiral Yi Sun-sin's legacy extends far beyond his military victories, establishing him as the defining symbol of Korean courage, resilience, and patriotic devotion. His impact on Korean national identity and his recognition as one of history's greatest naval commanders ensure his memory remains vibrant in both scholarly discourse and popular culture.

**Status in Modern Korea**

Yi is widely renowned in modern-day South Korea as the greatest general in Korean history.[^81] In a 2019 poll by Gallup Korea, Yi Sun-sin was chosen as the most respected person among South Koreans, winning 14% of the vote—an extraordinary testament to his enduring influence more than four centuries after his death.[^82] His evolution into a national hero reflects important aspects of Korean history from the late Joseon period to modern times, with his achievements having been continuously reevaluated and recognized at the national level since the late Joseon period.[^83]

**Physical Memorials**

Prominent statues of Admiral Yi have been erected throughout Korea, with the most famous standing in Gwanghwamun Square in central Seoul, considered one of the city's major landmarks.[^84] Another major statue stands at Busan Tower in Busan. The city formerly known as Chungmu on the southern coast of Korea, now renamed Tongyeong, honors his posthumous title, Chungmugong (Lord of Loyalty and Martial Valor).[^85]

**Posthumous Honors and Shrines**

Following his death, King Seonjo decreed a posthumous promotion elevating Yi to Yeonguijeong, the paramount role of Chief State Councillor, and conferred the prestigious posthumous title of Chungmugong ("Duke of Loyalty and Martial Valor").[^86] He received multiple royal honors including enrollment as a First-class Merit Subject of War, a titular enfeoffment as Prince of the Court of Deokpung, and a posthumous office of Yeongijeong (Prime Minister).[^87]

In 1707, King Sukjong ordered the establishment of Hyeonchungsa Shrine to honor Yi's spirit, personally bestowing a signboard for the shrine. Originally built in 1706 and rebuilt in 1932, the shrine in Asan honors Admiral Yi and several generations of his family.[^88] Shrines, both official and unofficial, were constructed in his honor throughout Korea, including Chungnyeolsa in Tongyeong, which displays a monument to his achievements and nine ceremonial items sent by the Emperor of Ming.[^89]

**Cultural and Educational Significance**

The National Museum of Korea's exhibition "The Great Admiral Yi Sun-sin" presented Yi's legacy through 369 artifacts, including six National Treasures such as his handwritten diary "Nanjung Ilgi."[^90] This diary, written almost daily from January 1592 through November 1598 until days before his death, is described as being without equal in world history as a commander's battlefield accounts and was designated as a UNESCO Memory of the World.[^91]

Yi's battle strategies remain so influential that they are published in modern naval academy textbooks of the United States, the United Kingdom, and Japan.[^92] His technological and strategic innovations sparked a revolution in Asian naval warfare and initiated both the "modern" naval force and style of combat.[^93] The turtle ship he developed is perceived and believed by many to be the world's first armored ship, though this claim remains debated among historians.[^94]

**International Recognition**

Yi's reputation extends beyond Korea's borders. Admiral Togo Heihachiro, the mastermind behind the Japanese defeat of Russia in the Battle of Tsushima, stated: "It may be proper to compare me with Nelson, but not with Korea's Yi Sun-Shin for he has no equal."[^95] Admiral Tetsutaro Sato of the Imperial Japanese Navy mentioned: "Among admirals, two further tactical geniuses may be named: in the East, Yi Sun-sin of Korea, and in the West, Horatio Nelson of England."[^96]

Admiral Yi Sun-sin remains an enduring symbol of Korean resilience, military genius, and unwavering loyalty, continuing to inspire South Koreans and military strategists worldwide in the 21st century. His life demonstrates how exceptional character combined with brilliant tactical innovation can overcome seemingly insurmountable odds—a lesson that transcends cultural and temporal boundaries.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Admiral Yi Sun-sin receives overwhelmingly positive assessment from military historians and scholars worldwide, recognized as one of history's preeminent naval commanders. In 1921, British Admiral George Alexander Ballard (1862-1948) directly compared Yi to Lord Nelson, stating that Yi "was entitled to be regarded as Nelson's equal" as "his work was so complete under each variety of circumstances as to defy criticism."[^97]

Yi is considered one of history's greatest naval commanders, known for his strategic vision, intelligence, innovations, and personality. It is generally accepted that he fought in at least 23 naval battles, achieving victory in all, often commanding forces that were outnumbered and poorly supplied.[^98] Some military historians like Joseph Cummins and George Alexander Ballard place Yi on par with Admiral Horatio Nelson, with Cummins noting that Yi's most dramatic success at Myeongnyang, where he led 13 ships to victory against at least 133, demonstrated unparalleled tactical genius.[^99]

The comparison to Nelson is particularly significant in naval history. Yi fought 23 battles without losing a single ship and won every engagement, while dying in his final battle similarly to Nelson at Trafalgar.[^100] However, Admiral Togo's assessment suggests Yi's achievement may be even more remarkable: "It may be proper to compare me with Nelson, but not with Korea's Yi Sun-Shin for he has no equal."[^101]

Modern scholarship increasingly adopts a humanistic approach to understanding Yi. Until the 2000s, no history major in Korea received a doctorate purely through research on Yi Sun-sin, with most dealing with him in relation to the Japanese Invasion of Korea or the political history of the King Seonjo period. In the late 2000s, full-fledged research on Yi Sun-sin began, including the creation of the Yi Sun-sin Research Institute at Soonchunhyang University.[^102] This scholarly attention has deepened understanding of Yi not merely as a military commander but as a complex individual who navigated political persecution while defending his nation.

Western military institutions have recognized Yi's genius. His technological and strategic innovations sparked a revolution in Asian naval warfare and initiated both the "modern" naval force and style of combat.[^103] His battle strategies are published in modern naval academy textbooks of the United States, the United Kingdom, and Japan, demonstrating the enduring relevance of his tactical innovations.[^104]

Scholars also praise Yi's character and integrity. He is remembered for remaining undefeated after commanding as many naval battles as he did, despite never having received naval training or participating in naval combat prior to the war, and constantly being outnumbered and outsupplied.[^105] His war diary reveals a leader who wrestled with profound personal struggles yet maintained his commitment to duty, earning him admiration not just for his victories but for his humanity.

### Negative Views

Genuine criticism of Admiral Yi Sun-sin's military accomplishments is remarkably scarce in historical scholarship. Most negative perspectives focus not on Yi himself but on the mythologization of his legacy or the political dysfunction that surrounded him during his lifetime.

Some modern historians argue that a historical view centered purely on hero-making and patriotism should be abandoned in favor of more critical analysis.[^106] These scholars suggest that descriptions about Yi Sun-sin in popular culture could be different from historical facts during the Imjin War, with dramatic portrayals taking artistic licenses that distort the historical record.[^107] A drama series about Yi was criticized for depicting him as weak and lonely in his early life and taking liberties with the events surrounding his death, suggesting that popular representations sometimes prioritize narrative appeal over historical accuracy.[^108]

The political context of Yi's elevation to national hero status has also drawn scholarly scrutiny. Yi Sun-sin began to be described as a loyal man of character by reformist nationalistic lines after the middle of the 1920s, suggesting a major frame in understanding him as a national hero overcoming a national crisis.[^109] During the Park Chung-hee regime, there was intensive postwar idolization of Yi Sun-sin as a military hero, with motion pictures reverting to familiar nationalistic narratives that won approbation from the authoritarian government.[^110] Some scholars argue this political appropriation of Yi's legacy for nationalistic purposes has obscured more nuanced historical understanding.

The most substantial criticism during Yi's lifetime came from his political enemies at court rather than from objective assessment of his military performance. The Joseon government's factionalism led ministers to despise the successful admiral and manipulate King Seonjo to view Yi as a potential traitor.[^111] King Seonjo and his court feared Yi's victories and reputation among the people as potential foundations for revolt, leading to his arrest and torture.[^112] However, these attacks were politically motivated rather than based on legitimate military failures.

Today, the harshest historical judgment falls not on Yi Sun-sin but on those who persecuted him. King Seonjo is now regarded as one of the most incompetent rulers in Joseon history due to his inability to control factionalism in his government and his unjust mistreatment of Admiral Yi.[^113] The court intrigue that nearly cost Korea its greatest defender is viewed as a cautionary tale about the dangers of political dysfunction during national crises.

The scarcity of substantive criticism of Yi's actual military conduct or character suggests that his reputation as one of history's greatest naval commanders rests on solid historical foundation. The debates surrounding Yi focus primarily on how his memory has been used rather than on the validity of his achievements themselves.

## Famous Quotes

> "According to the Principles of Strategy, he who seeks his death shall live, and he who seeks his life shall die. Again the Strategy says, if one home defender stands on watch at a strong gateway he may drive terror deep into the heart of the enemy coming by a ten thousand."

_Said to his generals before the Battle of Myeongnyang (September 15, 1597), encapsulating his philosophy that courage in the face of overwhelming odds can achieve the impossible._[^114]

> "Your Majesty, we still have 12 ships left."

_Response to King Seonjo in 1597, when the king believed the kingdom's naval forces could not defeat Japanese forces after the Chilchonryang disaster. This defiant statement became legendary, representing Yi's refusal to accept defeat regardless of circumstances._[^115]

> "The war is at its height—wear my armor and beat my war drums. Do not announce my death!"

_Yi's final words on December 16, 1598, spoken to his eldest son Hoe and nephew Wan after being struck by a fatal bullet at the Battle of Noryang. Even in death, Yi prioritized the morale of his troops and the success of the battle._[^116]

> "What shall I do? Is there anyone else under heaven in such a pitiful plight as myself?—the sooner I die the better."

_From his war diary (Nanjung Ilgi) after his mother's funeral, revealing the profound personal struggles and depression Yi wrestled with while maintaining outward stoicism and leadership._[^117]
