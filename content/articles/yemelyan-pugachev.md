## Summary

Don Cossack who impersonated Tsar Peter III and led a massive rebellion that threatened Catherine the Great.

## Background and Rise

Pugachev was a Don Cossack who had served in the Russian military during the Seven Years' War and other campaigns. In the early 1770s he became a fugitive and moved through Cossack communities on Russia's southeastern frontier, where resentment toward state controls, taxation, and serfdom was widespread.

His claim to be the deposed Tsar Peter III (Catherine II's husband) was politically useful even if widely doubted: it offered a legitimizing story that could unite different grievances under a single banner.

## Pugachev's Rebellion (1773–1775)

The rebellion is often described as the largest peasant uprising in Russian history prior to the nineteenth century. It combined:

- Frontier Cossack demands for autonomy and relief from central regulation
- Peasant and serf anger at landlords and forced labor obligations
- Local conflicts involving Bashkirs, Tatars, and other groups in the region

Imperial authorities treated the revolt as an existential threat, dispatching experienced commanders and large forces. After a series of battles and reversals, Pugachev was betrayed by some of his own followers and handed over to the government.

## Aftermath and Legacy

Catherine the Great used the uprising as a warning about instability in the countryside. The state strengthened internal security and administrative control in affected areas, while also tightening oversight of frontier regions.

In later Russian history and literature, Pugachev became an ambiguous figure: a symbol of popular revolt against oppression to some, and a violent usurper to others. His rebellion remains a key case study for how frontier politics, social hierarchy, and imperial governance interacted in eighteenth-century Russia.
