## Summary

World's longest-serving president who has ruled Equatorial Guinea since 1979 after overthrowing his uncle. His regime is one of the most corrupt in Africa despite massive oil wealth.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1979 | Overthrew uncle Francisco Macías Nguema |
| 2023 | Won seventh presidential election with 95% of vote |

## Background

- Obiang came to power in a 1979 coup and has remained the central political figure in Equatorial Guinea for decades.
- The discovery and expansion of offshore oil production transformed the country's macroeconomic profile, while governance indicators and living standards have been widely debated.

## Governance and Criticism

- His presidency is frequently described as highly centralized, with limited political competition and strong executive control over state institutions.
- International organizations and press reporting have repeatedly raised concerns about civil liberties, electoral conditions, and the treatment of political opponents.

## Oil Wealth, State Finances, and Corruption Allegations

- Equatorial Guinea became a major oil producer in sub-Saharan Africa relative to its population size, generating significant state revenues.
- Obiang and his family have faced corruption allegations in multiple jurisdictions, including investigations and court actions involving luxury assets.
- Supporters have pointed to infrastructure development and state spending, while critics argue that benefits have been unevenly distributed.

## International Relations

- The government has maintained security and economic relationships with a range of partners, shaped by energy interests and regional diplomacy.
- External engagement has often combined pragmatic cooperation with periodic human-rights criticism from foreign governments and NGOs.

## Succession and Domestic Politics

- Long tenure has made succession planning a recurring topic in analysis of Equatorial Guinea's political future.
- Family members, including Teodoro Nguema Obiang Mangue (often referred to as "Teodorín"), have been prominent in government and in international legal cases.
