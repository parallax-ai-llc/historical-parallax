## Summary

Colombian drug cartel led by Pablo Escobar that at its peak supplied 80% of cocaine to the US and was worth $30 billion. It waged war against the Colombian state killing judges, politicians, and civilians.

## Background

The Medellín Cartel was part of the broader expansion of the transnational cocaine trade in the late 20th century. Reporting on the group often highlights both its commercial scale and its use of violence and intimidation.

## Organization and methods (high-level)

- **Trafficking logistics**: moving cocaine from production areas through transit routes and onward to major consumer markets.
- **Corruption and coercion**: combining bribery with threats to influence officials and institutions.
- **Violence as strategy**: attacks intended to deter enforcement and shape political decisions.

## Political and social context

- The cartel’s campaign of violence intersected with Colombia’s internal security challenges and institutional strain.
- U.S.–Colombia cooperation against narcotics trafficking became a major feature of the period.

## Aftermath and legacy

- The decline of the Medellín Cartel did not end the cocaine trade; it contributed to shifts in actors, routes, and enforcement strategies.
- The cartel remains a reference point in debates about drug policy, state capacity, and the costs of militarized enforcement.
