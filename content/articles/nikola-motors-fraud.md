## Summary

Electric truck startup Nikola's founder Trevor Milton lied about the company's technology. A promotional video showed a truck rolling downhill rather than driving. Milton convicted of fraud.

## What Was Alleged

Public reporting and court filings described a pattern of statements that overstated the readiness of Nikola’s technology, including claims about proprietary battery and hydrogen capabilities, vehicle performance, and the state of engineering prototypes.

A frequently cited episode involved a marketing video in which a vehicle was presented as operating under its own power, while subsequent scrutiny alleged it had been positioned to roll downhill.

## Investigations and Enforcement

After short-seller allegations and media coverage, Nikola and its founder became the subject of scrutiny by U.S. regulators. Investigations involved evaluating public statements to investors and the public, the accuracy of technical demonstrations, and whether disclosures matched internal realities.

## Court Outcomes

Trevor Milton was convicted on fraud-related charges in U.S. federal court. The case is often referenced as an example of how misrepresentation risks increase in sectors with complex, hard-to-verify technical claims, particularly during periods of intense capital formation.

## Broader Impact

The Nikola fraud controversy contributed to heightened skepticism toward early-stage electric vehicle and hydrogen claims, and it is sometimes discussed alongside tighter due diligence expectations for:

- prototype demonstrations and third-party validation,
- customer and partnership announcements,
- disclosure practices for pre-revenue companies.
