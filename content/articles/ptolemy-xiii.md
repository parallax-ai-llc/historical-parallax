## Summary

Egyptian pharaoh who murdered Pompey the Great and fought a civil war against his sister Cleopatra.

## Background

- Ptolemy XIII Theos Philopator was a king of the Ptolemaic dynasty (a Macedonian Greek royal house ruling Egypt after Alexander the Great).[^1]
- He came to the throne as a minor and ruled in a co-regency arrangement that was politically dominated by court officials and guardians.[^1]
- The Ptolemaic court at this time faced internal factionalism and heavy pressure from Roman power politics in the eastern Mediterranean.[^2]

## Co-rule and Conflict with Cleopatra

- Ptolemy XIII initially co-ruled with his older sister Cleopatra VII, as was typical of Ptolemaic dynastic practice.[^1]
- Rival court factions aligned with either Ptolemy XIII or Cleopatra, contributing to a breakdown of the co-regency.[^1]
- Cleopatra was pushed out of Alexandria during the ensuing struggle, and a civil war began between the siblings’ supporters.[^1]

## Pompey’s Assassination

- After losing to Julius Caesar in the Roman civil war, Pompey the Great fled to Egypt seeking refuge and support.[^3]
- Ptolemy XIII’s advisers chose to have Pompey killed shortly after his arrival, presenting the act as a political gesture meant to curry favor with Caesar.[^1][^3]
- Ancient and modern accounts commonly interpret the murder as a grave miscalculation: rather than stabilizing Egypt’s position, it entangled the court more deeply in Roman conflict.[^2][^3]

## Caesar in Alexandria and the Alexandrian War

- Caesar arrived in Alexandria soon after Pompey’s death and became directly involved in the Egyptian dynastic dispute.[^1]
- The confrontation escalated into the Alexandrian War, with fighting centered around Alexandria and the royal court.[^1]
- Cleopatra returned to the political stage during this period, and Caesar supported a settlement that restored her position against the faction backing Ptolemy XIII.[^1]

## Death and Aftermath

- Ptolemy XIII died in 47 BCE during the conflict, reportedly drowning while attempting to retreat across the Nile during a battle.[^1]
- After his death, Cleopatra’s rule was consolidated with Roman backing, and the Ptolemaic kingdom’s dependence on Roman power intensified.[^1][^2]

## Legacy (High-Level)

- Ptolemy XIII is remembered primarily for two interconnected outcomes: the assassination of Pompey and the acceleration of Roman intervention in Egypt’s dynastic politics.[^1][^3]
- His short reign is often treated as an example of how court intrigue and factional decision-making can backfire under external great-power pressure.[^2]
