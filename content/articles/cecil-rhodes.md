## Summary

British mining magnate and politician who founded Rhodesia and De Beers diamonds. He believed in Anglo-Saxon superiority and his British South Africa Company conquered territory through violence and forced labor. His legacy remains deeply controversial.

## Rise through mining and empire

Rhodes became wealthy through diamond mining in southern Africa and was a central figure in the consolidation of the diamond industry, culminating in the formation of De Beers. His business success translated into political power in the Cape Colony, where he served as prime minister.

## British South Africa Company and expansion

Rhodes is closely associated with the British South Africa Company (BSAC), a chartered company used as an instrument of imperial expansion. Under company rule, territory was acquired through treaties, coercion, and armed force, with Indigenous communities subjected to dispossession and labor exploitation.

The term “Rhodesia” reflects the degree to which the project of territorial expansion was personalized around Rhodes and his political ambitions.

## Jameson Raid and political fallout

The failed Jameson Raid (1895–1896) attempted to trigger an uprising against the South African Republic (Transvaal). Its failure damaged Rhodes’s political standing and highlighted the destabilizing role that private interests could play in regional conflict.

## Legacy and controversy

Rhodes left a bequest that established the Rhodes Scholarships, which have supported generations of students and became one of the most prominent international scholarship programs.

At the same time, his beliefs in racial hierarchy and his role in colonial violence and extraction have made him a focal point of debate about memorialization, especially in controversies over statues, building names, and institutional ties to colonial-era benefactors.

In historical writing, Rhodes often symbolizes the fusion of finance, ideology, and state power that characterized late-19th-century imperialism.
