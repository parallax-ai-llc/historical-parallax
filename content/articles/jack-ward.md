## Summary

English pirate who converted to Islam and became one of the most notorious Barbary corsairs. Inspiration for Captain Ward in the play 'A Christian Turned Turk.'

## Context and Significance

- Ward was active during a period when North African corsair ports such as Tunis operated as semi-autonomous maritime powers linked to the Ottoman world.
- Many English and Dutch sailors turned to privateering or piracy in the early 17th century amid war, demobilization, and shifting patronage.

## Activities in the Mediterranean

- Contemporary accounts describe Ward preying on commercial shipping and using North African ports as safe harbors for refit, trade, and negotiations.
- His conversion to Islam and adoption of a new name are commonly noted features of his later life in Tunis, reflecting patterns of renegotiated identity among corsairs.

## Legacy

- Ward became a subject of English pamphlets and theater, shaping early modern English popular images of renegades and Barbary corsairs.
- Later retellings often blend documented events with moralized narrative, so details can vary between sources.

## What Sources Typically Emphasize

- The shift from English seafaring to corsair life, including the use of North African political protection.
- The economic logic of corsairing: capturing ships, ransoming crews, and selling cargo through local markets.
- The role of religion and allegiance as flexible categories in early modern Mediterranean conflict.

This article is a high-level overview and does not attempt to reconcile all competing narratives in the early modern source record.
