## Summary

Maurice Béjart (1927–2007) was one of the most influential and provocative choreographers of the twentieth century, renowned for transforming ballet from an elite art form into a mass spectacle capable of filling sports arenas and opera houses alike. Born Maurice-Jean Berger in Marseille, he fused classical ballet with contemporary dance, Eastern philosophy, theatre, and popular culture to create a bold, eclectic style that defied conventional boundaries.

His company, Ballets du XXe Siècle (Ballet of the 20th Century), founded in Brussels in 1960, became one of the world's premier dance companies, and his works reached audiences numbering in the tens of thousands at a time. Béjart believed passionately that dance was a universal language and a spiritual act, and his choreography — intense, sensual, and often mystical — reflected that conviction throughout a career spanning six decades.

## Early Life & Background

Maurice-Jean Berger was born on January 1, 1927, in Marseille, France. His father, the philosopher Gaston Berger, was a significant intellectual influence. His mother died when he was seven, a loss that shaped his intense spirituality and lifelong search for meaning. He began studying ballet in Marseille and later trained in Paris under Léo Staats and Madame Bourgat, then in London with Vera Volkova and in Stockholm with Madame Edwards. He adopted the stage name Béjart, drawn from the wife of Molière, as a young performer.

## Rise to Prominence

In the early 1950s Béjart danced with various European companies, including Roland Petit's Ballets de Paris. In 1954 he co-founded Les Ballets de l'Étoile in Paris, where he began choreographing seriously. His 1959 production of *Rite of Spring* (*Le Sacre du Printemps*) to Stravinsky's score, staged at the Théâtre Royal de la Monnaie in Brussels, was a landmark moment — its raw eroticism and powerful physicality caused a sensation and brought him international recognition. This success led directly to his appointment as resident choreographer in Brussels and the founding of Ballets du XXe Siècle in 1960.

## Key Achievements

Béjart created over 200 works during his career. Among his most celebrated pieces are *Boléro* (1961), set to Ravel's score with a soloist dancing atop a circular table surrounded by the corps, which became one of the most performed ballets in history. *The Merry Widow* (1934 music, 1975 staging) and *Notre Faust* (1975) demonstrated his range. He also choreographed *A la recherche de Don Juan* (1993) and tributes to Freddie Mercury and Michael Jackson, illustrating his appetite for popular culture. In 1987 he relocated his company to Lausanne, Switzerland, renaming it Béjart Ballet Lausanne, and in 1992 he founded the Rudra Béjart Lausanne school. He received the Prix Benois de la Danse and numerous honorary degrees recognizing his lifetime contribution to dance.

## Controversies & Criticism

Béjart's work was persistently controversial. Critics frequently accused him of excess, spectacle over substance, and a tendency toward bombast. His unabashedly erotic choreography — particularly his focus on the male body and overt homoeroticism at a time when such themes were taboo in ballet — drew both censure and ardent admiration. Some dance scholars argued his populist ambitions diluted artistic rigour, while others contended that his critics were rooted in elitist assumptions about what ballet should be. His conversion to Sufism and embrace of Eastern mysticism also polarised audiences.

## Legacy & Influence

Béjart died on November 22, 2007, in Lausanne at the age of 80. He left behind a company, a school, and a body of work that permanently altered the landscape of European dance. By staging ballet in stadiums and sports halls and insisting on its relevance to contemporary life, he democratised the art form and influenced generations of choreographers. Béjart Ballet Lausanne continues to perform his works worldwide under artistic director Gil Roman, preserving his repertoire and philosophy.
