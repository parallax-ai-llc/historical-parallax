## The Hulse–Taylor binary pulsar

In 1974, Taylor and Russell Hulse identified the system now known as PSR B1913+16, a pulsar in a compact binary orbit. Precise timing of the pulsar’s radio pulses made it possible to measure tiny changes in the orbital period over time.

## Indirect evidence for gravitational waves

General relativity predicts that a tight binary system should lose orbital energy by emitting gravitational radiation, leading to a gradual inspiral and a decreasing orbital period. The observed orbital decay of the Hulse–Taylor system closely matched the relativistic prediction and became one of the strongest early confirmations of gravitational-wave physics.

## Broader scientific impact

The techniques developed for pulsar timing helped establish pulsars as precision astrophysical laboratories. Work on compact binaries informed later research on neutron stars, strong-field gravity, and ultimately the direct detections of gravitational waves by interferometric observatories decades later.

## Recognition

Taylor and Hulse received the 1993 Nobel Prize in Physics for the discovery and its significance as a test of gravitation.

## Key takeaway

The Hulse–Taylor system showed that careful measurement of pulse arrival times can turn an astronomical object into a high-precision experiment, linking observational radio astronomy to fundamental physics.

It remains a canonical example in astrophysics courses of how theory and measurement can be matched at extraordinary precision.
