## Summary

Izumi Shikibu (c. 976–1033) was one of the greatest poets of the Heian period and classical Japanese literature, renowned for her passionate and emotionally intense waka poetry. A member of the imperial court, she gained fame not only for her literary brilliance but also for her scandalous romantic affairs with two imperial princes, which both elevated and complicated her reputation. Her poetry collection, the Izumi Shikibu Monogatari and the Izumi Shikibu shū, contains over 1,500 poems characterized by their depth of feeling, technical mastery, and bold exploration of love and Buddhist themes. Despite facing social criticism for her unconventional personal life, she secured her place as one of Japan's most celebrated female poets, influencing Japanese literature for centuries.

---

## Early Life and Background

Izumi Shikibu was born around 976 CE during the mid-Heian period, an era renowned as the golden age of Japanese courtly culture and vernacular literature.[^1] She was born into the provincial governor class, a middle-ranking aristocratic family—her father, Ōe no Masamune, served as the governor of Echizen Province, while her mother was the daughter of another provincial official.[^2] This social position placed her family below the highest courtiers but still within the educated elite that had access to court life and classical learning.

Her birth name is unknown; "Izumi Shikibu" is a court designation derived from her father's position as governor of Izumi Province and "shikibu" refers to her father's post in the Ministry of Ceremonial.[^3] During the Heian period, women of aristocratic families rarely had their personal names recorded, instead being identified by male relatives' titles or their own court positions.[^4]

Growing up in an educated household, Izumi Shikibu received the classical education typical for aristocratic women of her time, which emphasized poetry composition, calligraphy, music, and Chinese classics (though women primarily wrote in Japanese rather than Chinese).[^5] The Heian period placed enormous cultural value on poetic ability, particularly the composition of waka—31-syllable poems in the 5-7-5-7-7 pattern—which served as the primary medium for courtly communication, romantic correspondence, and social interaction.[^6]

Her early education would have included studying the Kokinshū, the first imperial poetry anthology compiled in 905, which established the aesthetic principles and vocabulary that dominated Heian poetry.[^7] She also would have been exposed to vernacular prose works such as The Tale of Genji by Murasaki Shikibu and The Pillow Book by Sei Shōnagon, both written by her near-contemporaries at the imperial court.[^8]

The Heian court culture in which Izumi Shikibu came of age was highly refined but also intensely competitive, particularly for women whose primary avenue to status and recognition was through literary talent and romantic relationships with powerful men.[^9] This environment shaped both her poetic development and the trajectory of her personal life, as success at court required not only artistic ability but also skillful navigation of complex social hierarchies and romantic politics.[^10]

## Rise to Prominence

Izumi Shikibu's entry into court life and her rise to poetic fame were inseparable from her romantic relationships with members of the imperial family. Around 995, she married Tachibana no Michisada, a provincial official and member of the middle-ranking aristocracy, with whom she had a daughter.[^11] Through this marriage, she gained entry to the circles surrounding the imperial court, though she did not yet hold a position at court herself.

Her trajectory changed dramatically when she began a passionate affair with Prince Tametaka, the third son of Emperor Reizei, around 998.[^12] This relationship was both socially transgressive—she was married, and he was of imperial blood—and artistically fruitful, producing an extensive poetic correspondence that demonstrated her exceptional talent.[^13] The affair became widely known at court, bringing her both notoriety and recognition for her poetic gifts. Her husband, unable to tolerate the scandal, divorced her, and she continued her relationship with the prince openly.[^14]

The romance ended tragically when Prince Tametaka died suddenly in 1002, reportedly from illness, leaving Izumi Shikibu devastated.[^15] Her grief poems from this period are among her most powerful works, expressing profound sorrow with remarkable emotional directness unusual for the typically restrained Heian aesthetic.[^16] These poems circulated widely at court and enhanced her reputation as a poet of exceptional emotional depth.

In an even more controversial development, shortly after Tametaka's death, Izumi Shikibu began an affair with his younger brother, Prince Atsumichi, around 1003.[^17] This relationship caused enormous scandal, as it violated both social propriety and what many saw as appropriate mourning behavior.[^18] Court gossip was savage; she was criticized for impropriety, accused of having caused the first prince's death through her passionate nature, and labeled as morally corrupted.[^19]

However, Prince Atsumichi, apparently deeply in love, defied social pressure and made Izumi Shikibu his official consort around 1004, bringing her formally into his household and giving her a recognized position at his court.[^20] This liaison produced extensive poetic exchanges that were collected and circulated, further establishing her literary reputation even as her personal reputation remained controversial.[^21]

When Prince Atsumichi died in 1007, Izumi Shikibu again faced grief and social vulnerability.[^22] However, her poetic fame was now firmly established. Around 1009, she received appointment as a lady-in-waiting to Empress Shōshi, consort of Emperor Ichijō, joining the brilliant literary circle that included Murasaki Shikibu, author of The Tale of Genji.[^23] This position represented both social rehabilitation and recognition of her status as one of the era's foremost poets, despite her scandalous past.

Murasaki Shikibu, in her famous diary, provides a contemporary glimpse of Izumi Shikibu at court, describing her as someone who "writes with grace and ease" but whose personal behavior was "improper" and who "corresponded with unsuitable people."[^24] This ambivalent assessment captures Izumi Shikibu's dual reputation: universally acknowledged as a brilliant poet, yet perpetually suspect in her personal conduct.

## Major Achievements

Izumi Shikibu's primary achievement was her extraordinary body of poetic work, which established her as one of the supreme masters of classical Japanese poetry. Her personal poetry collection, the Izumi Shikibu shū, contains approximately 1,500 poems, making it one of the largest individual collections from the Heian period.[^25] The range and emotional intensity of this work set her apart from contemporaries and secured her enduring reputation.

Her poems were included in numerous imperial poetry anthologies, the highest honor for a Heian poet. The Goshūi Wakashū (Later Collection of Gleanings, compiled 1086) included 67 of her poems—more than any other poet in that collection.[^26] Subsequent imperial anthologies continued to draw heavily on her work: the Kin'yō Wakashū included 43 poems, the Shika Wakashū included 116, and the Shinkokin Wakashū included 59.[^27] In total, approximately 246 of her poems were selected for imperial anthologies, a testament to their enduring quality and influence.[^28]

The Izumi Shikibu Nikki (Izumi Shikibu Diary), a poetic memoir or fictionalized diary recounting her relationship with Prince Atsumichi, represents another major literary achievement.[^29] This work, which may have been written by Izumi Shikibu herself or by someone in her circle, combines prose narrative with poetry to create an intimate account of a passionate love affair.[^30] The diary form allowed for extended emotional expression and character development beyond what individual poems could achieve, making it an important example of Heian zuihitsu (miscellaneous writing) and nikki bungaku (diary literature).[^31]

Izumi Shikibu's poetic style was characterized by exceptional technical skill combined with unusual emotional directness. While Heian court poetry typically valued subtle allusion, elegant restraint, and adherence to conventional imagery drawn from nature and the seasons, Izumi Shikibu frequently broke these conventions to express raw emotion with startling immediacy.[^32] Her love poems, in particular, were noted for their passionate intensity and frank acknowledgment of physical desire, unusual in an aristocratic culture that prized indirection.[^33]

Her religious poetry represents another dimension of her achievement. Many of her poems explore Buddhist themes of impermanence, suffering, and the search for spiritual liberation, often with the same emotional intensity she brought to love poetry.[^34] These Buddhist poems demonstrate her engagement with religious thought and her ability to bring personal feeling to devotional subjects, creating works that function both as spiritual expression and as poetry.[^35]

One of her most famous poems, included in the Hyakunin Isshu (One Hundred Poets, One Poem Each), the canonical anthology that has introduced Japanese poetry to students for centuries, reads:

> *Arazaran*  
> *kono yo no hoka no*  
> *omoide ni*  
> *ima hitotabi no*  
> *au koto mo gana*

Translation: "Soon I shall be gone / from this world—let me have / a keepsake: / just once more / to meet you."[^36]

This poem, addressed to a lover (likely Prince Atsumichi), exemplifies her ability to combine Buddhist awareness of mortality with passionate romantic longing, creating emotional complexity within the brief waka form.[^37]

Izumi Shikibu's influence extended beyond her own work. As a member of Empress Shōshi's literary salon, she participated in the vibrant poetic culture that produced some of Japanese literature's greatest works.[^38] Her example of combining technical mastery with emotional authenticity influenced subsequent generations of poets, particularly women poets who looked to her as a model of artistic achievement despite social constraints.[^39]

Her work also contributed to the development of poetic diction and expanded the acceptable range of subjects and emotions in classical Japanese poetry. By bringing greater directness and psychological realism to her poems, she helped broaden the expressive possibilities of the waka form.[^40]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Izumi Shikibu's life and reputation were marked by persistent controversy stemming from her romantic relationships and behavior, which violated Heian court propriety norms. The most significant scandal was her consecutive affairs with the imperial brothers Prince Tametaka and Prince Atsumichi, relationships that shocked contemporaries and damaged her social standing.[^41]

Her affair with Prince Tametaka while she was still married to Tachibana no Michisada was considered a serious breach of marital duty and social propriety, even in a court culture where aristocratic marriages were often political arrangements and romantic affairs were common.[^42] The openness of the relationship, conducted through widely circulated poetry exchanges, made it impossible to ignore or excuse, forcing her husband to divorce her to preserve his own honor.[^43]

The subsequent relationship with Prince Atsumichi, beginning shortly after his brother's death, provoked even greater outrage. Court gossips accused her of improper mourning, of having possibly contributed to Tametaka's death through the intensity of their relationship, and of shameless behavior in pursuing another prince.[^44] Some courtiers suggested she had used witchcraft or unnatural feminine wiles to ensnare both brothers, reflecting misogynistic assumptions about women's sexuality and power.[^45]

Murasaki Shikibu, her contemporary at Empress Shōshi's court, recorded harsh judgments in her diary. While acknowledging Izumi Shikibu's poetic gifts, Murasaki wrote that she was "frivolous" and "lacking in judgement," someone whose correspondence with inappropriate men revealed poor character.[^46] Murasaki's criticism reflects the perspective of a woman who maintained more conventional propriety while achieving literary success, suggesting tension between different models of female behavior at court.

Other court ladies reportedly ostracized or whispered about Izumi Shikibu, viewing her as a negative example and perhaps feeling threatened by her romantic successes and poetic fame despite her scandalous reputation.[^47] This social isolation would have been painful in the intensely hierarchical and interconnected world of the Heian court, where one's standing depended on reputation and social acceptance.

Critics also pointed to her prolific romantic correspondence and the frankly erotic quality of some poems as evidence of inappropriate behavior. While elegant romantic poetry was expected and valued, Izumi Shikibu's emotional intensity and willingness to express physical desire crossed boundaries of decorum.[^48] Conservative courtiers viewed this as unwomanly and potentially corrupting to public morals.

Her appointment to Empress Shōshi's service after Prince Atsumichi's death was itself controversial. Some questioned whether someone with her reputation should serve the empress, arguing that it reflected poorly on the empress's household to include a woman of questionable virtue.[^49] That she received the appointment anyway suggests both the power of her poetic reputation and possible support from influential patrons who valued literary talent over strict propriety.

Religious critics took issue with some of her Buddhist-themed poetry, suggesting that someone of her moral character could not authentically engage with Buddhist teachings about renouncing worldly attachment and desire.[^50] These criticisms reflect the double standard applied to women seeking spiritual expression while having lived unconventional lives.

Later historical periods sometimes intensified these criticisms. Medieval commentators, working in more morally conservative eras, occasionally portrayed Izumi Shikibu as a cautionary tale about women's passions leading to social ruin, oversimplifying her complex life and work to make moral points.[^51]

## Personal Life

Izumi Shikibu's personal life was marked by intense romantic relationships, complex family connections, and the challenges of navigating court society as a woman of literary talent but controversial reputation. Her first marriage to Tachibana no Michisada, arranged according to aristocratic custom, produced a daughter named Koshikibu no Naishi, who herself became an accomplished poet.[^52]

Koshikibu no Naishi followed her mother into court service and poetic achievement, serving as a lady-in-waiting and earning inclusion in the Hyakunin Isshu anthology alongside her mother—one of only three mother-daughter pairs in that canonical collection.[^53] The relationship between mother and daughter appears to have been close, with evidence of mutual support and shared poetic interests, though Koshikibu died young, around 1025, predeceasing her mother.[^54]

Izumi Shikibu's relationship with Prince Tametaka, which began around 998, was characterized by passionate intensity and prolific poetic exchange. Contemporary sources suggest genuine emotional connection beyond mere court intrigue or social climbing, evidenced by the depth of grief in poems she composed after his death in 1002.[^55] The relationship cost her her marriage and social respectability, but she apparently considered it worthwhile, never expressing regret in her poetry about the affair itself, only sorrow at its end.

Her subsequent relationship with Prince Atsumichi appears similarly passionate and emotionally authentic, despite the scandal it caused. The prince's willingness to make her his official consort and face social criticism to maintain their relationship suggests strong mutual affection.[^56] The Izumi Shikibu Nikki provides an intimate (if perhaps partly fictionalized) account of their courtship, presenting a relationship of emotional and intellectual partnership alongside romantic passion.[^57]

After Prince Atsumichi's death in 1007, Izumi Shikibu entered a period of grief and uncertainty. As a woman in her early thirties who had lost two high-ranking lovers and lacked independent means, her position was precarious.[^58] Her appointment to Empress Shōshi's service provided both economic security and social rehabilitation, allowing her to continue her literary work in a prestigious setting.

Sources suggest she later married Fujiwara no Yasumasa, a provincial governor, around 1009, moving with him to his posting in Tango Province.[^59] This marriage appears to have been more conventional and less passionate than her earlier relationships, perhaps representing acceptance of greater stability over romantic intensity as she aged.[^60] She maintained her poetic work during this period, composing poems about provincial life and continuing her Buddhist spiritual exploration.

Izumi Shikibu's personality, as revealed through her poetry and contemporary descriptions, combined remarkable emotional depth with intellectual sophistication. Her poems display psychological insight, self-awareness, and willingness to examine complex feelings, including acknowledging her own contradictions and struggles.[^61] Unlike some Heian poets who maintained elegant emotional distance, she wrote with raw honesty about desire, grief, spiritual longing, and doubt.

Her Buddhist faith appears to have been genuinely important to her, though complex and questioning rather than simply devotional. Many poems express struggle with religious teachings, particularly the Buddhist emphasis on detaching from worldly desires when she remained deeply attached to human love and beauty.[^62] This honest grappling with spiritual challenges makes her religious poetry more psychologically compelling than more conventionally pious works.

Contemporary accounts suggest she could be witty and engaging in conversation, with Murasaki Shikibu noting (despite her other criticisms) that Izumi Shikibu's letters were charming and her poetic skills impressive.[^63] She apparently maintained friendships and literary relationships with other women at court, participating in poetry contests and collaborative literary activities.

She died around 1033, likely in her late fifties, having outlived both her imperial lovers, her first husband, and her daughter.[^64] The circumstances of her death are not recorded in detail, though some traditions suggest she spent her final years in Buddhist devotion, possibly taking religious vows as many aristocratic women did in old age.[^65]

## Legacy

Izumi Shikibu's legacy as one of classical Japan's greatest poets has endured for nearly a millennium, transcending the personal controversies that marked her lifetime. Her work fundamentally shaped the development of Japanese poetry, particularly in expanding the emotional range and psychological depth possible within the highly constrained waka form.[^66]

In the immediate aftermath of her death, her poetry continued to circulate and gained increasing recognition. Each successive imperial anthology included substantial numbers of her poems, with later compilers recognizing her as a master worthy of study and emulation.[^67] The Shinkokin Wakashū (New Collection of Ancient and Modern Poetry, 1205), compiled during a period of intense poetic innovation, included 59 of her poems, reflecting the high esteem in which medieval poet-scholars held her work.[^68]

Medieval poetry critics and commentators consistently ranked her among the supreme poets of the Heian period. Fujiwara no Shunzei (1114–1204), one of Japan's most influential poetry critics, praised her emotional depth and technical skill, though he also noted her occasional departure from strict poetic decorum.[^69] His son Fujiwara no Teika (1162–1241), compiler of the Hyakunin Isshu, selected her poem for that canonical anthology, ensuring that generations of Japanese students would encounter her work as part of their basic literary education.[^70]

The Izumi Shikibu Nikki became a classic of Heian diary literature, studied alongside works by Murasaki Shikibu and Sei Shōnagon as an exemplar of women's vernacular prose and poetry.[^71] Its intimate portrayal of romantic love influenced later Japanese literature's treatment of relationships and emotional life, particularly in providing a model for female-authored first-person narrative.

During the medieval and early modern periods (12th–19th centuries), Izumi Shikibu's reputation underwent various reinterpretations. Some Buddhist-influenced commentators emphasized her religious poetry and presented her as an example of someone who found spiritual redemption despite a passionate youth.[^72] Others, working in more morally conservative periods, treated her as a cautionary tale about women's uncontrolled passions, though even these critics acknowledged her poetic genius.[^73]

The Edo period (1603–1868) saw renewed scholarly interest in Heian literature. Scholars produced annotated editions of her poetry collection and the Izumi Shikibu Nikki, making her work more accessible and establishing her firmly in the classical literary canon.[^74] Her life story inspired plays, novels, and artistic representations, though these often emphasized romantic drama over literary achievement.

Modern scholarship, beginning in the late 19th century with the professionalization of Japanese literary studies, has produced extensive analysis of her work. Literary historians have examined her technical innovations, her contributions to expanding poetic diction and subject matter, and her influence on subsequent poets.[^75] Feminist scholars have been particularly interested in her navigation of gender constraints, her assertion of female desire and subjectivity, and her ability to achieve artistic recognition despite social marginalization.[^76]

Contemporary Japanese culture maintains strong awareness of Izumi Shikibu. Her poem in the Hyakunin Isshu ensures that virtually all educated Japanese readers encounter her work, as this anthology is widely studied in schools and remains popular in traditional card games.[^77] References to her poetry appear regularly in contemporary Japanese literature, film, and popular culture, demonstrating her continued cultural presence.

Her legacy extends beyond Japan through translation and cross-cultural literary study. Translations of her work into English and other languages have introduced international audiences to her poetry, contributing to global appreciation of classical Japanese literature and women's literary achievements.[^78] Scholars of comparative literature have examined her work alongside that of other medieval women poets, including Sappho, Rabi'a al-Basri, and Hildegard of Bingen, exploring common themes and challenges faced by women writers in patriarchal societies.[^79]

Izumi Shikibu's life and work raise enduring questions about the relationship between artistic genius and personal conduct, the social constraints on women's expression and behavior, and the possibility of achieving recognition despite violating social norms. Her example demonstrates that exceptional talent could, even in a rigidly hierarchical and patriarchal society, secure a measure of respect and lasting fame, though not without significant personal cost.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and critics across the centuries have consistently recognized Izumi Shikibu as one of the supreme masters of classical Japanese poetry, praising her emotional depth, technical virtuosity, and innovative expansion of poetic possibilities. Medieval poetry critic Fujiwara no Shunzei (1114–1204) ranked her among the greatest Heian poets, noting that her work possessed exceptional "emotional depth" (*kokoro no fukasa*) and authentic feeling rare in court poetry.[^80]

Modern literary historians have provided detailed analysis of her technical achievements. Donald Keene, the distinguished scholar of Japanese literature, described her as "the greatest of the women poets of the Heian period" and praised her ability to express "the most intimate emotions with a directness rare in Japanese poetry."[^81] Edwin Cranston, in his comprehensive study of Heian poetry, argued that Izumi Shikibu "pushed the boundaries of poetic expression" and brought unprecedented psychological realism to the waka form.[^82]

Feminist literary scholars have particularly valued her work for asserting female subjectivity and desire. Historian Ivan Morris wrote that she "refused to accept the conventional restrictions on women's emotional and sexual expression" and that her poetry represented "an authentic female voice asserting its own values."[^83] Scholar Esperanza Ramírez-Christensen has argued that Izumi Shikibu's frank treatment of sexuality and her refusal to apologize for her desires represented a form of resistance to patriarchal constraints, making her a proto-feminist figure.[^84]

Her religious poetry has earned praise from scholars of Japanese Buddhism. William LaFleur noted that her Buddhist poems display "genuine spiritual searching" and an honest struggle with religious teachings that makes them more compelling than conventionally pious works.[^85] Her willingness to acknowledge doubt and difficulty in achieving detachment, rather than simply affirming orthodox teachings, gives her religious poetry unusual authenticity and psychological depth.

Contemporary poets and writers have acknowledged her influence. Japanese poet and critic Ooka Makoto (1931–2017) described her as a poet who "wrote from the body and heart" with unusual directness, influencing modern Japanese poetry's embrace of personal expression.[^86] Translator Jane Hirshfield has praised the "startling immediacy" and "emotional fearlessness" of Izumi Shikibu's poetry, arguing that it speaks powerfully to contemporary readers across cultural and temporal boundaries.[^87]

Defenders of her personal conduct have argued that she should be judged by her artistic achievements rather than conformity to social conventions. They note that male poets of her era who had multiple romantic relationships faced no comparable criticism, revealing double standards in how female behavior was judged.[^88] Some scholars suggest that the scandals surrounding her relationships have obscured recognition of her courage in prioritizing emotional authenticity and artistic expression over social respectability.

Cultural historians have positioned her as exemplifying the complex achievement of Heian court culture, which granted women unusual opportunities for literary expression and achievement despite the era's patriarchal structures.[^89] Her success in gaining recognition despite social transgression demonstrates the powerful value placed on artistic talent in Heian court society, where poetic skill could mitigate (though not erase) other social liabilities.

---

### Negative Views

Throughout history, critics have questioned Izumi Shikibu's moral character and suggested that her personal behavior undermined her artistic achievements. The most severe criticisms came from contemporary sources and later moralists who viewed her relationships as evidence of poor judgment and inappropriate conduct for an aristocratic woman.

Murasaki Shikibu's contemporary assessment, while acknowledging poetic talent, was notably harsh: "Izumi Shikibu corresponds with unsuitable people... she is frivolous, lacking in judgment, and her behavior is improper."[^90] This criticism from a fellow female poet at the same court carries particular weight, suggesting that Izumi Shikibu's behavior troubled even women who themselves were pushing boundaries through literary achievement.

Medieval and early modern commentators working in more conservative eras sometimes portrayed her as morally corrupted by passion. Some Buddhist-influenced critics argued that her poetry, despite its beauty, promoted attachment to worldly desires and romantic love, potentially leading readers away from spiritual development.[^91] These moralists suggested that her work should be approached with caution, as its emotional intensity might corrupt impressionable readers.

Literary critics have occasionally argued that her emotional directness, while powerful, sometimes crossed into excessive sentimentality or violated aesthetic principles of restraint and suggestion fundamental to Japanese poetic tradition. Fujiwara no Teika, while including her in the Hyakunin Isshu, also noted that some of her poems lacked the sophisticated indirection (*yūgen*) valued in the highest classical poetry.[^92]

Some scholars have questioned whether her scandalous reputation inflated her literary fame, suggesting that the dramatic circumstances of her relationships generated interest that enhanced the reception of her poetry beyond its intrinsic merit.[^93] These critics argue that more technically accomplished but personally conventional poets received less recognition because their lives provided less dramatic biographical narrative.

Conservative cultural commentators, particularly during the Edo period and early modern era, sometimes used Izumi Shikibu as a cautionary example of women's dangerous passions and the social disorder caused by failure to maintain proper moral boundaries.[^94] These interpretations subordinated her literary achievement to moralistic messaging about female virtue and social order.

Modern critics have occasionally suggested that her poetry's psychological realism and emotional directness, while powerful, lack the refined aesthetic complexity of poets like Saigyō or Ki no Tsurayuki, whose work demonstrates greater technical sophistication and deeper engagement with poetic tradition.[^95] From this perspective, her innovation came partly at the cost of classical elegance and technical refinement.

Some feminist scholars have complicated celebratory readings of her work by noting that her relationships with powerful men, however passionate, still operated entirely within patriarchal structures—she gained status and security through male patrons rather than achieving independent agency.[^96] Her relative freedom to pursue relationships may have resulted from her beauty and charm rather than representing genuine gender equality or female empowerment.

Critics have also pointed out that the dramatic focus on her romantic life has sometimes obscured other aspects of her work and reduced her to a romantic figure rather than a complex artist engaged with religious, philosophical, and aesthetic questions.[^97] The persistent emphasis on scandal may have actually limited full appreciation of her literary range and intellectual depth.

## Famous Quotes

> *Kuraki yori / kuraki michi ni zo / irinu beki / haruka ni terase / yama no ha no tsuki*  
> "From darkness / into the path of darkness / I must enter: / shine on me from afar, / moon at the mountain's rim"

Context: One of her most celebrated poems, composed as she prepared to enter Buddhist devotion. This poem expresses spiritual seeking while acknowledging uncertainty and the difficulty of the religious path. The moon symbolizes enlightenment that remains distant but offers hope.[^98]

---

> *Arazaran / kono yo no hoka no / omoide ni / ima hitotabi no / au koto mo gana*  
> "Soon I shall be gone / from this world—let me have / a keepsake: / just once more / to meet you."

Context: Addressed to Prince Atsumichi, expressing the desire for one more meeting with full awareness of mortality. Selected for the Hyakunin Isshu, this poem exemplifies her ability to combine Buddhist awareness of impermanence with passionate romantic longing.[^99]

---

> *Mono omoeba / sawa no hotaru mo / waga mi yori / akugaruru / tamashii ka to zo miru*  
> "When I am lost in longing, / even fireflies in the marsh / seem to be / wandering souls / torn from my body"

Context: A poem expressing the intensity of romantic longing so powerful that it seems to separate the soul from the body. The image of fireflies as wandering spirits demonstrates her innovative use of natural imagery for psychological expression.[^100]

---

> *Aruji naki / yado wa mukashi no / nokiba kana / onaji nagara ni / hito zo kayowanu*  
> "Without its master / this dwelling is only / its former eaves: / though everything remains unchanged / you come here no more"

Context: Composed after Prince Tametaka's death, expressing how a physical space remains unchanged while being utterly transformed by the absence of the beloved. This poem demonstrates her ability to use concrete imagery to express profound grief.[^101]

---

> *Kurokami no / midarete kesaya / waga sode ni / ase o oku keri*  
> "My black hair / disheveled— / must confusion reign / even on my sleeves / where dew forms?"

Context: A frankly erotic poem using disheveled hair and dew as imagery for the aftermath of intimate encounter. The poem's directness about physical love was unusual in court poetry and contributed to both her fame and controversial reputation.[^102]
