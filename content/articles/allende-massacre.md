## Summary

Mass killing by Los Zetas in Allende, Coahuila in March 2011 that killed an estimated 300 people. The cartel destroyed entire homes with backhoes and burned bodies in what was covered up for years.

## Additional Context

- The Allende massacre is associated with Los Zetas, a cartel known for extreme violence and for operating with a quasi-military structure during parts of its history.
- Accounts commonly describe the attack as involving targeted retaliation, followed by broad terror against families and communities, including arson and demolition of property.
- The scale of the killings was not widely acknowledged at the time, and later reporting emphasized how fear, local power dynamics, and institutional weakness contributed to years of silence.

## Investigation and Public Reckoning

- Later investigations and journalism helped clarify the extent of the destruction and highlighted missing-person cases tied to the events.
- The episode became emblematic of the wider crisis of enforced disappearances and mass violence in Mexico during the peak years of the “war on drugs.”

## Why It Matters

The massacre is often cited in human-rights discussions as an example of how cartel violence can erase communities and how delayed acknowledgment can compound harm to survivors.
