## Summary

Chen Qimei (陳其美, January 17, 1878 – May 18, 1916), courtesy name Yingshi (英士), was a Chinese revolutionary activist and one of the founding fathers of the Republic of China.[^1] A key figure in the Green Gang, he served as a close political ally of Sun Yat-sen and the influential mentor of Chiang Kai-shek.[^2] Chen played a pivotal role in the 1911 Revolution, leading revolutionary forces to occupy Shanghai and serving as the city's military governor.[^3] His assassination in 1916 by agents of warlord Yuan Shikai cut short a career marked by revolutionary dedication, political violence, and the blending of underground criminal networks with republican ideals.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Chen Qimei was born on January 17, 1878, in Wuxing (now part of Huzhou), Zhejiang Province, as the second of three sons in a family of modest means headed by a businessman father with scholarly inclinations.[^5] His early years were shaped by the economic uncertainties of late Qing China, which directed him away from traditional scholarly pursuits toward commerce. He received his initial education in his hometown, entering a private school around age six before the family's circumstances redirected him toward business.[^6]

At approximately 15 sui (around 1891–1892), Chen began a 12-year apprenticeship at a pawnbroker's shop in Shihmen Town, Chongde County, near Wuxing, gaining practical experience in trade amid the Qing dynasty's economic challenges.[^7] This formative period exposed him to the harsh realities of commerce and the declining fortunes of traditional Chinese society under Manchu rule. Around 1900, Chen relocated to Shanghai, a major hub for anti-Manchu revolutionary activities, where he took employment in a silk trading firm.[^8] Shanghai's cosmopolitan environment and its International Settlement, which enjoyed extraterritorial status, would later prove crucial to his revolutionary work.

In 1906, Chen went to Japan to study, entering the Tokyo Police Supervision School.[^9] This trip proved transformative. In Tokyo, he met Sun Yat-sen and joined the anti-Qing Tongmenghui (Chinese Revolutionary Alliance), which had been founded by Sun on August 20, 1905, as a coalition of anti-Qing groups advocating republicanism and the overthrow of the Manchu dynasty.[^10] His entry into the organization, shortly after its establishment and the launch of its organ Minbao, aligned him directly with Sun's leadership and the broader exile revolutionary network in Japan, marking the inception of his lifelong commitment as a devoted supporter of Sun's cause.[^11]

While in Japan, Chen befriended fellow Zhejiang native Chiang Kai-shek, and in 1908, Chen inducted Chiang into the Tongmenghui.[^12] This relationship would prove historically significant, as Chiang would later become the leader of Nationalist China. Within the Tongmenghui, Chen rapidly emerged as an active operative, leveraging his Zhejiang connections and organizational skills to recruit members across provinces including Zhejiang, Beijing, and Tianjin from 1906 to 1909, building networks essential for coordinated anti-Qing actions.[^13]

---

## Rise to Power

After joining the Tongmenghui in Tokyo in 1906, Chen returned to Shanghai and established a secret headquarters in the International Settlement, leveraging its extraterritorial status to coordinate revolutionary plots in the Jiangnan region.[^14] By 1908, Chen had also joined the Green Gang in Shanghai, Hangzhou and other places, liaising with the secret society to support revolutionary activities.[^15] This dual engagement with both political revolutionaries and criminal organizations exemplified the pragmatic alliances that characterized China's republican revolution.

Chen was himself one of the most powerful gangster leaders of Shanghai in the first years of the Republic of China.[^16] His ability to mobilize Green Gang militias would prove decisive during the revolutionary upheaval of 1911. The Green Gang, which focused on opium trafficking, extortion, gambling, and prostitution, reframed its use of violence as disciplinary, revolutionary, and nationalistic to gain public legitimation during this period.[^17] Li Zhengwu, a Green Gang leader, poured his private family fortune into organizing local Green Gang forces in support of the 1911 Revolution, and members claimed that the Green Gang had made critical contributions to the founding legacy of the new republic.[^18]

When the Wuchang Uprising erupted on October 10, 1911, sparking revolutionary uprisings across China, Chen seized the opportunity in Shanghai. On November 3, 1911, Shanghai's Tongmenghui, Guangfuhui and merchants led by Chen Qimei, Li Pingsu, Zhang Chengyou, and Song Jiaoren organized an armed rebellion in Shanghai.[^19] He enlisted gang militias for the seizure of Shanghai on November 3, 1911, exemplifying pragmatic alliances between revolutionaries and illicit networks.[^20] The rebels captured the Jiangnan Workshop on November 4th and captured Shanghai soon after.[^21]

On November 8, 1911, they established the Shanghai Military Government and elected Chen Qimei as the military governor.[^22] For a brief period, Shanghai was controlled by National Revolutionary Forces led by Chen Qimei, who pledged support for Sun Yat-sen.[^23] At that time, Chen had reached out to the French Concession police through Pockmarked Huang, forming a relationship between revolutionary forces and the foreign concessions.[^24] In the early days of the Revolution of 1911, Chen Qimei and Huang Xing were the right and left arm of Sun Yat-sen, serving as key leaders within the revolutionary movement.[^25]

Chen's position as Shanghai military governor represented the apex of his power and influence. He was the eldest member of what later came to be known as the Chen Family, one of the four most powerful and influential families at the time.[^26] However, his tenure as military governor would be brief, as the revolutionary period gave way to the challenges of consolidating republican rule under President Yuan Shikai.

## Major Achievements

### The 1911 Revolution and Shanghai's Liberation

Chen Qimei's most significant achievement was his successful leadership of the Shanghai uprising during the 1911 Revolution. After the Wuchang Uprising, Chen's forces occupied Shanghai, and he was made military governor of the region.[^27] His ability to coordinate revolutionary forces, Green Gang militias, and merchant support demonstrated exceptional organizational and leadership capabilities. The capture of Shanghai, China's most important commercial center and gateway to the West, was strategically crucial to the revolutionary cause.

As military governor, Chen worked to maintain order in Shanghai while supporting Sun Yat-sen's revolutionary government. He coordinated with other revolutionary generals, including Xu Shaozhen, to form a united army to strike Nanjing together.[^28] This cooperation among revolutionary leaders helped consolidate republican control over the Jiangnan region during the critical early months of the revolution.

### Building the Revolutionary Network

Chen's recruitment work for the Tongmenghui from 1906 to 1909 helped build the organizational infrastructure that made the 1911 Revolution possible.[^29] His networks spanned provinces and connected revolutionaries, secret societies, merchants, and overseas Chinese supporters. His role in inducting Chiang Kai-shek into the Tongmenghui in 1908 would have profound historical consequences, as Chiang would eventually become the leader of Nationalist China and one of the most important figures of 20th-century Chinese history.[^30]

### Resistance to Yuan Shikai's Dictatorship

After Yuan Shikai consolidated power and moved toward dictatorship, Chen became a leader of the opposition. During the Second Revolution in July 1913, Chen Qimei announced Shanghai's independence and commanded the anti-Yuan forces in Shanghai.[^31] On July 14, 1913, amid the Second Revolution launched by Sun Yat-sen's Kuomintang against Yuan's dissolution of parliament and suppression of opposition, Chen directed military actions in Shanghai, including ordering Chiang Kai-shek to seize the Gaochangmiao Arsenal to deny weapons to Yuan's forces.[^32] Yuan responded by branding Chen, alongside Huang Xing and Bai Wenwei, as traitors on the same day the KMT declared resistance.[^33]

Although the Second Revolution failed by September 1913, Chen's willingness to resist Yuan's authoritarianism demonstrated his commitment to republican principles. He fled to Japan with Sun Yat-sen upon the failure of the 1913 Second Revolution against Yuan Shikai's dictatorship.[^34]

### Formation of the Chinese Revolutionary Party

Exiled to Japan with Sun Yat-sen after the Second Revolution's failure, Chen continued coordinating anti-Yuan activities from abroad, leveraging his networks in secret societies and revolutionary cells.[^35] On July 8, 1914, Sun Yat-sen formed the secret Chinese Revolutionary Party (Gemingdang), and Chen Qimei emerged as one of the few leaders willing to sign the oath of personal loyalty to Sun and attach their fingerprints.[^36] While many of Sun's old revolutionary comrades, including Huang Xing, Wang Jingwei, Hu Hanmin and Chen Jiongming, refused to join or support his efforts, Chen remained loyal.[^37]

Chen had been Sun's chief lieutenant in the party during this difficult period when most Revolutionary Alliance members refused to join Sun's exiled Chinese Revolutionary Party.[^38] His loyalty and organizational abilities made him indispensable to Sun's continued revolutionary efforts.

### Anti-Yuan Assassination Operations

After returning to China in 1915, Chen continued his opposition to Yuan Shikai through clandestine operations. As Yuan prepared to declare himself emperor, Chen returned to China to organize anti-Yuan activities, including the assassination of pro-Yuan governor of Shanghai Zheng Rucheng (also known as Cheng Jucheng).[^39] On November 10, 1915, Chen sent someone to assassinate Shanghai garrison commander Zheng Rucheng at the Waibaidu Bridge.[^40] His 1915 plots, including the assassination of Yuan's commander Cheng Jucheng on November 11 and an attempted seizure of the gunboat Chao-ho in December, underscored his commitment to republican ideals.[^41]

These operations demonstrated Chen's willingness to use political violence and assassination as tools of revolutionary struggle, reflecting the brutal realities of early republican China where political disputes were often settled through violence rather than electoral processes.

## Controversies and Criticisms

### Association with Organized Crime

One of the most controversial aspects of Chen Qimei's career was his deep involvement with the Green Gang, Shanghai's most powerful criminal organization. He was described as one of the most powerful gangster leaders of Shanghai in the first years of the Republic of China.[^42] The Green Gang focused on opium trafficking (which was supported by local warlords), extortion, gambling, and prostitution.[^43] Chen's willingness to collaborate with criminal elements raised questions about the moral foundations of the revolutionary movement and blurred the lines between political revolution and criminal enterprise.

Critics could argue that Chen's alliance with organized crime corrupted the republican cause and established a dangerous precedent for the Kuomintang's later cooperation with criminal syndicates. Chiang Kai-shek's friendship with Chen Qimei signaled an association with Shanghai's criminal syndicate (the Green Gang headed by Du Yuesheng and Huang Jinrong), a connection that would continue throughout Chiang's career.[^44]

### Political Assassinations

Chen Qimei was directly implicated in several political assassinations that raise serious ethical questions. The most notorious was the assassination of Tao Chengzhang, a rival revolutionary leader, in 1912. In early 1912, a dispute arose between Chen Qimei and Tao Chengzhang, an influential member of the Revolutionary Alliance who opposed both Sun Yat-sen and Chen.[^45] Tao sought to avoid escalating the quarrel by hiding in a hospital, but Chen's agents discovered him there.[^46]

On January 14, 1912 (some sources cite February 15, 1912), Chen sent Chiang Kai-shek and Wang Zhuqing to assassinate Tao Chengzhang, the leader of the Restoration Society, at Shanghai's Guangci Hospital.[^47] Chiang Kai-shek shot and killed Tao Chengzhang at point-blank range as Tao lay sick in a Shanghai French Concession hospital, thus ridding Chen Qimei of his chief rival.[^48] This cold-blooded murder of a fellow revolutionary in a hospital bed represents one of the darkest chapters in Chen's career and established a pattern of political violence within the republican movement.

The International Concession police records show an arrest warrant for Chiang for armed robbery related to this period, indicating that Chen's operations sometimes crossed the line into criminal activity.[^49]

### Authoritarian Tendencies

Chen's willingness to sign Sun Yat-sen's 1914 oath of personal loyalty for the Chinese Revolutionary Party, which many other prominent revolutionaries found unacceptable, suggests a degree of authoritarian thinking.[^50] The oath demanded that members pledge personal loyalty to Sun rather than to republican principles or democratic institutions, and it granted different levels of political privileges based on when members joined, creating a hierarchical, anti-democratic structure.[^51]

### Questionable Historical Evidence

Modern historians have expressed skepticism about some accounts of Chen Qimei's activities. Episodes about Chiang Kai-shek's meeting with Chen Qimei mainly had to rely upon Chiang's memoirs, which suggests historians approach some accounts about Chen Qimei with caution due to limited source verification.[^52] Miyazaki Tōten's uncertainty about Chen Qimei's visit, saying "Chen says he came to my home, but I do not know the fact," makes the description that Chen Qimei and Chiang Kai-shek visited Miyazaki and Sun Zhongshan "very uncertain."[^53] This raises questions about how much of the Chen Qimei narrative has been constructed or embellished by later Kuomintang historiography.

### Use of Violence and Terrorism

Chen's revolutionary methods relied heavily on violence, terrorism, and assassination rather than mass mobilization or democratic organization. His attempted seizure of arsenals, assassination of political opponents, and use of criminal networks represented a conspiratorial, elitist approach to revolution that differed from more populist revolutionary movements. Critics might argue that these methods undermined the democratic ideals the revolution supposedly championed.

## Personal Life

Little detailed information about Chen Qimei's personal life has been preserved in historical records. The available sources focus primarily on his political career and revolutionary activities rather than his personal family life, including details about marriage or direct descendants.[^54] This gap in the historical record reflects both the tumultuous nature of his revolutionary career and the tendency of republican-era historiography to focus on political achievements rather than personal matters.

Chen was the uncle of CC Clique leaders Chen Guofu and Chen Lifu.[^55] Chen Lifu and his older brother Chen Guofu were nephews of Chen Qimei, who until his assassination by Yuan Shikai in 1916 was the mentor of Chiang Kai-shek.[^56] The Chen brothers later established the CC Clique within the KMT, deriving much of its influence from control over the Central Bureau of Investigation and Statistics, a powerful intelligence agency, as well as dominance over major newspapers.[^57] From the conclusion of the Northern Expedition to the outbreak of the Second Sino-Japanese War, the CC Clique held long-standing control over the Kuomintang's party apparatus under Chiang Kai-shek's authorization.[^58]

The prominence of his nephews in later Kuomintang politics suggests that the Chen family maintained significant influence within republican China, with Chen Qimei's revolutionary legacy providing a foundation for his family's continued political power.

### Personality and Character

Historians have noted Chen's ability to inspire loyalty and his skill at building networks across different social groups. Chiang Kai-shek was able to experience patriarchal authority and love when he worked with Chen Qimei and Sun Yat-sen, both of whom appreciated Chiang's loyalty and talents and treated him as a son.[^59] This assessment by Wang Jisheng of Beijing University highlights Chen Qimei's role as a mentor figure in shaping Chiang's personality and career.

Chiang served in the revolutionary forces, leading a regiment in Shanghai under his friend and mentor Chen Qimei, as one of Chen's chief lieutenants.[^60] The close bond between Chen and Chiang, who became like a father to the younger revolutionary, suggests that Chen possessed charismatic leadership qualities and the ability to inspire deep personal devotion.[^61]

Chen's friendships extended to Shanghai businessmen and millionaires, including Li Houyi and Zhang Jingjiang, who was connected to Du Yuesheng, leader of the Green Gang.[^62] These connections demonstrate Chen's ability to navigate between different worlds—revolutionary politics, criminal underworld, and business elites—building coalitions that transcended traditional social boundaries.

## Legacy

### Founding Father of the Republic

Chen Qimei is perceived as one of the early revolutionary heroes and one of the founding fathers of the Republic of China.[^63] His leadership of the Shanghai uprising in 1911 and his service as military governor during the critical early days of the republic secured his place in Chinese republican history. Sun Yat-sen highly praised Chen Yingshi as "the chief minister of the revolution," recognizing his crucial contributions to the revolutionary cause.[^64]

### Influence on Chiang Kai-shek and Nationalist China

Perhaps Chen's most enduring legacy was his mentorship of Chiang Kai-shek. By inducting Chiang into the Tongmenghui in 1908 and serving as his mentor and patron throughout the revolutionary period, Chen shaped the trajectory of modern Chinese history.[^65] After Chen's assassination on May 18, 1916, agents of Yuan Shikai killed Chen Qimei while he was trying to organize an insurrection in Shanghai.[^66] Chiang then succeeded Chen as leader of the Chinese Revolutionary Party in Shanghai.[^67]

Chiang's later career as leader of the Kuomintang, President of the Republic of China, and one of the most important figures of 20th-century Chinese history can be traced back to Chen's influence and patronage. The methods Chen employed—combining political organization with criminal networks, using assassination as a political tool, demanding personal loyalty, and relying on military force—would all characterize Chiang's later rule.

### The Chen Family Political Dynasty

Chen was the eldest member of what later came to be known as the Chen Family, one of the four most powerful and influential families in republican China.[^68] Through his nephews Chen Guofu and Chen Lifu, who led the powerful CC Clique within the Kuomintang, Chen's influence extended well beyond his death.[^69] The CC Clique controlled the party apparatus and intelligence services for decades, demonstrating the lasting impact of Chen's revolutionary networks.

### Educational and Commemorative Legacy

The university which had been originally named after him has become a part of today's Fudan University and Zhejiang University after the Chinese Civil War.[^70] A monument to him is located in Huzhou, Zhejiang, China.[^71] These memorials reflect his status as a revered revolutionary figure, though the full complexity of his career—including his involvement in organized crime and political assassinations—is often downplayed in official commemorations.

### Formation of the Kuomintang

Chen and Sun Yat-sen formed the Chinese Revolutionary Party while in exile in Japan, which later was reorganized into the Kuomintang in 1919.[^72] This organizational work during the difficult years of exile helped lay the foundation for the Kuomintang's eventual return to power and its dominance of Chinese politics for decades.

### Complex Historical Assessment

Chen's assassination in 1916 cut short his revolutionary career, but his legacy as a founding figure of the Republic and his role in mentoring Chiang Kai-shek secured his place in Chinese republican history.[^73] However, modern historians recognize the complexity of his legacy, acknowledging both his contributions to the republican revolution and his troubling embrace of political violence, organized crime, and authoritarian methods.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Supporters and admirers of Chen Qimei emphasize his dedication to the republican cause and his practical effectiveness as a revolutionary organizer. He is celebrated as one of the founding fathers of the Republic of China who helped end over two thousand years of imperial rule.[^74] His successful leadership of the Shanghai uprising demonstrated exceptional military and political skills, while his ability to build coalitions across different social groups—from Tongmenghui intellectuals to Green Gang gangsters to Shanghai merchants—showed pragmatic leadership during a revolutionary period that demanded flexibility and adaptability.

Sun Yat-sen's high praise of Chen as "the chief minister of the revolution" reflects the regard in which he was held by the revolutionary leadership.[^75] Chen's loyalty to Sun during the difficult exile period of 1913-1916, when many other prominent revolutionaries abandoned Sun's cause, demonstrates his steadfast commitment to the revolutionary movement even when prospects seemed bleak.[^76]

Defenders might argue that Chen's use of Green Gang networks and his willingness to employ political violence were necessary tactics in the brutal struggle against Qing imperial rule and Yuan Shikai's dictatorship. The Qing dynasty and Yuan's regime both used violence and repression against revolutionaries, making armed resistance and clandestine operations essential survival strategies. Chen's reframing of the Green Gang's activities as revolutionary and nationalistic helped mobilize resources and manpower for the republican cause.[^77]

His role as mentor to Chiang Kai-shek is viewed positively by those who see Chiang's later leadership of China during the War of Resistance against Japan as heroic. Chen's ability to recognize Chiang's potential and provide him with opportunities for advancement demonstrates his skill at identifying and developing leadership talent.

The fact that Chen was willing to sacrifice his life for the revolutionary cause—returning to Shanghai in 1916 despite the dangers to organize resistance against Yuan Shikai—demonstrates his personal courage and dedication to republican principles.[^78] His assassination by Yuan's agents made him a martyr for the republican revolution.

### Negative Views

Critics of Chen Qimei focus on his embrace of political violence, his deep involvement with organized crime, and the authoritarian methods he helped establish within the republican movement. The assassination of Tao Chengzhang in 1912—a fellow revolutionary murdered in a hospital bed—represents an inexcusable act of political terrorism that violated both revolutionary ethics and basic human decency.[^79] This willingness to murder political rivals within the revolutionary movement itself foreshadowed the violent internal purges that would characterize later Kuomintang and Communist Party politics.

Chen's position as one of the most powerful gangster leaders of Shanghai in the first years of the Republic raises fundamental questions about the nature of the republican revolution.[^80] Rather than establishing the rule of law and democratic governance, Chen's model of revolutionary politics relied on criminal networks involved in opium trafficking, extortion, gambling, and prostitution.[^81] This corruption of republican ideals with criminal enterprises undermined the moral foundations of the new republic from its very beginning.

The connection between Chen's methods and Chiang Kai-shek's later authoritarian rule suggests that Chen bears some responsibility for the Kuomintang's failure to establish genuine democracy in China. By modeling a political style based on personal loyalty networks, secret society connections, political assassination, and military force rather than democratic institutions and mass participation, Chen helped establish patterns that would plague Chinese republicanism for decades.

Chen's willingness to sign Sun Yat-sen's 1914 oath of personal loyalty, which established hierarchical privileges and demanded allegiance to an individual rather than to principles or institutions, demonstrated anti-democratic tendencies that contradicted the supposed goals of the republican revolution.[^82] Many other prominent revolutionaries, including Huang Xing, refused to join this organization precisely because they recognized its authoritarian character.[^83]

Skeptical historians also question the reliability of the historical narrative surrounding Chen, noting that much of what is known comes from Chiang Kai-shek's memoirs and Kuomintang historiography, sources that had strong incentives to embellish Chen's role and minimize his darker activities.[^84] The uncertain historical evidence suggests that the Chen Qimei of republican mythology may differ significantly from the historical reality.

## Famous Quotes

Unfortunately, the search results did not reveal any documented quotes or revolutionary writings directly attributed to Chen Qimei. The available sources focus primarily on his biographical information and revolutionary activities rather than his specific words or writings.[^85] If documented quotes exist, they would likely be found in Chinese historical archives, academic journals specializing in Republican-era China, or biographical works specifically devoted to Chen Qimei in Chinese language sources.

However, historical records preserve one important statement about Chen:

> "The chief minister of the revolution" - Sun Yat-sen's description of Chen Qimei[^86]

This praise from Sun Yat-sen reflects the high regard in which Chen was held by the revolutionary leadership and his central importance to the 1911 Revolution.
