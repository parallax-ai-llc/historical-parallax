## Summary

Mochtar Kusumaatmadja was an Indonesian legal scholar, diplomat, and statesman who fundamentally shaped Indonesia's maritime sovereignty and international legal standing. Born in Jakarta in 1929, he was the principal architect of the archipelagic state concept that transformed Indonesia's territorial boundaries and later became enshrined in international law through the 1982 United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS). As Indonesia's Minister of Justice (1974-1978) and Foreign Minister (1978-1988), he championed legal reforms domestically while securing international recognition for Indonesia's maritime claims, effectively expanding the nation's territory by one-third. Recognized posthumously as a National Hero in 2025, Kusumaatmadja's intellectual contributions to legal theory and diplomatic achievements continue to influence Indonesian law and international maritime jurisprudence.[^1][^2]

---

## Early Life and Background

Mochtar Kusumaatmadja was born on February 17, 1929, in Batavia (now Jakarta), Indonesia, during the final years of Dutch colonial rule.[^3] He was born to M. Taslim Kusumaatmadja, a renowned pharmacist from Sukapura, West Java, and Sulmini Soerawisastra, a teacher from Cirebon.[^4] His family background provided him with educational opportunities that were relatively uncommon for Indonesians during the colonial period.

Mochtar and his brother Sarwono were raised partly by their mother's sister Moespiah and her husband Dr. Sudarsono Mangoenadikusumo, alongside their cousin Juwono Soedarsono, who later became Minister of Education and Culture under President Habibie and subsequently Minister of Defense.[^5] This extended family environment exposed young Mochtar to intellectual discourse and public service values that would shape his future career.

His early education took place during the tumultuous period of Japanese occupation and Indonesia's struggle for independence. After completing high school, Kusumaatmadja pursued higher education at the Faculty of Law and Social Sciences at the University of Indonesia, graduating with a bachelor's degree in 1955, specializing in international law.[^6] His academic excellence and interest in legal scholarship marked him as an exceptional student during a period when Indonesia was still building its educational institutions as a newly independent nation.

The intellectual foundation laid during these formative years, combined with exposure to both traditional Javanese values and modern legal education, would prove instrumental in Kusumaatmadja's later work reconciling international legal principles with Indonesian national interests. His upbringing in a family committed to education and public service instilled in him a dedication to using legal expertise for national development that would define his entire career.[^7]

---

## Rise to Prominence

Kusumaatmadja's ascent to national prominence began with an extraordinary educational opportunity that would transform his intellectual trajectory. He became one of the first Indonesians to receive a Fulbright grant to study in the United States, and the very first Indonesian to earn a Master of Laws degree from an American university when he graduated from Yale Law School in 1958.[^8] This American education exposed him to the legal philosophy of Roscoe Pound, particularly the concept of law as a tool of social engineering, which Kusumaatmadja would later adapt to the Indonesian context as his theory of "development law" (hukum pembangunan).[^9]

After returning to Indonesia, Kusumaatmadja briefly worked at Bank Indonesia's Foreign Exchange Institute before accepting a position as a lecturer at Padjadjaran University (Unpad) in Bandung, where he taught international law with a specialty in the law of the sea.[^10] He obtained his doctorate from Padjadjaran University in 1962 with a dissertation on "The Problem of Territorial Sea Width at the Geneva Law of the Sea Conferences 1958 and 1960," establishing himself as Indonesia's foremost expert on maritime law.[^11]

His academic career advanced rapidly. In 1962, he became Dean of the Faculty of Law at Unpad, serving until 1973.[^12] During this period, he played a pivotal role in reforming Indonesia's legal education curriculum in 1972, introducing changes that gave law students more freedom and emphasized producing lawyers who would actively improve Indonesia's legal system rather than passively accepting it.[^13] In 1970, he received the title of professor from Unpad, and in 1973, he was inaugurated as the university's fifth Rector.[^14]

However, Kusumaatmadja's most significant early contribution occurred in 1957, when as a young lecturer at Padjadjaran University, he presented the conceptual framework for what became known as the Djuanda Declaration during a cabinet meeting under Prime Minister Djuanda Kartawidjaja.[^15] This declaration asserted that all waters surrounding and between Indonesia's islands constituted an integral part of the nation's territory under Indonesian sovereignty—a radical claim at the time that challenged prevailing international maritime law. This single intellectual contribution would occupy much of his professional life and ultimately redefine international law of the sea.

Political turbulence in the mid-1960s brought complications to Kusumaatmadja's career. His concept of law as a tool of social engineering contradicted the Sukarno government's views on law, and he was forced to reside overseas temporarily.[^16] Although he was dismissed from his position as Dean during this period, this setback provided him an opportunity to deepen his studies in international private law. The transition from Sukarno's "Old Order" to Suharto's "New Order" regime created new opportunities for Kusumaatmadja, as the new government sought legal experts to modernize Indonesia's legal framework and international relations.[^17]

## Major Achievements

### Archipelagic State Concept and UNCLOS

Mochtar Kusumaatmadja's most enduring achievement was his successful campaign to secure international recognition for the archipelagic state principle through the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS). From 1957 until 1982—a span of 25 years—Kusumaatmadja fought diplomatically at various United Nations conferences to have this principle accepted as part of international law of the sea.[^18]

The Archipelagic State Principle asserts that in an archipelagic state such as Indonesia, territorial water boundaries are calculated by drawing an imaginary line connecting all the outermost points of the outermost islands, and then calculating 12 nautical miles from this maritime baseline.[^19] This meant that all waters enclosed within these baselines became Indonesian internal waters, subject to full Indonesian sovereignty—a claim that was virtually unheard of at the time outside the context of war and domination.[^20]

Kusumaatmadja championed this concept at the first UNCLOS conference in Geneva, Switzerland, in February 1958, continuing through the second session in April 1960 and the third session beginning in December 1973.[^21] His efforts culminated in success when UNCLOS III was signed in Montego Bay, Jamaica, on December 10, 1982, with the archipelagic state principle appearing in Articles 46-54 of the convention.[^22] As head of Indonesia's delegation to Jamaica, Kusumaatmadja secured acceptance of this principle by the international community, with the convention eventually being ratified by nearly all nations.[^23]

This achievement effectively increased Indonesia's territory by one-third, transforming the nation's strategic position and resource base.[^24] The diplomatic maneuvering of Kusumaatmadja, working alongside Hasjim Djalal, was integral to this acknowledgment of archipelagic states in international law, with Article 47 granting Indonesia the right to draw straight baselines around its outermost islands and exercise full sovereignty over the enclosed waters, airspace, seabed, and subsoil.[^25]

### Legal Reform as Minister of Justice

Appointed as Minister of Justice in the Second Development Cabinet on January 22, 1974, Kusumaatmadja served in this capacity until 1978.[^26] One of his first actions was reviving the Institute of National Law Reform, which he renamed, provided with increased budget allocations, and restructured organizationally to bring legal academics closer to the lawmaking and law reform processes.[^27]

His tenure saw significant legal reform activities. A workshop to coordinate legal research was held in September 1974, while a workshop aimed at documenting and indexing legal information was held in December 1974.[^28] Another significant result was the establishment of agreed-upon legal terms, which Kusumaatmadja described as important to make legal language "uniform, usable, and understandable to the people."[^29] These initiatives reflected his philosophy that law should serve as an instrument of development and social change, not merely a conservative force maintaining order.

### Diplomatic Leadership as Foreign Minister

Kusumaatmadja was appointed Minister of Foreign Affairs in the Third Development Cabinet in 1978, continuing in this position until 1988—serving a total of ten years as Indonesia's chief diplomat.[^30] During this period, he shaped Indonesian foreign policy around the concept of "concentric circles," with ASEAN at the inner circle as the cornerstone of Indonesian foreign policy, followed by major neighboring East Asian countries, then the wider Asia Pacific region, with diminishing priority for outer perimeters.[^31]

One of his hallmarks as Foreign Minister was support for cultural diplomacy, aimed at improving Indonesia's image overseas.[^32] This concept was promoted through initiatives such as the First International Gamelan Festival held in conjunction with Expo 86 in Vancouver, showcasing Indonesian culture to international audiences.

### Cambodia Peace Mediation

During Kusumaatmadja's tenure as Foreign Minister, civil war raged in Cambodia following Vietnam's invasion. At a special meeting of ASEAN Foreign Ministers in Jakarta in 1984, it was decided that Indonesia, specifically Kusumaatmadja, would act as ASEAN's negotiating partner with Vietnam to resolve the Cambodia issue, as Indonesia had good relations with Vietnam and Kusumaatmadja was viewed as ideally suited due to his superior diplomatic abilities.[^33]

Facing the challenge of Cambodian factions' intransigence and reluctance to meet, Kusumaatmadja introduced the innovative concept of "cocktail party" diplomacy—informal gatherings where disputing parties could mix and mingle without the tense and rigid atmosphere of formal meetings.[^34] This approach created a forum that brought together Vietnam and several warring Cambodian factions. The first Jakarta Informal Meeting was held in Bogor from July 25-28, 1988, followed by subsequent meetings in Jakarta that eventually contributed to the Paris Peace Agreements inaugurating an era of peace and reconstruction for Cambodia.[^35]

Though Kusumaatmadja's term as Foreign Minister ended in 1988 and he was replaced by Ali Alatas, many parties praised and honored him as a creator of peace between Cambodia and Vietnam.[^36] His innovative diplomatic approach demonstrated that informal methods could succeed where formal negotiations had failed.

### ASEAN Security Cooperation

During the 1980s, Kusumaatmadja, alongside Malaysian Foreign Minister Abu Hassan Omar, called for ASEAN military arrangements and defense community development, demonstrating commitment to regional security cooperation.[^37] He authored scholarly works including "Some Thoughts on ASEAN Security Co-Operation: An Indonesian Perspective," published in Contemporary Southeast Asia: A Journal of International and Strategic Affairs in 1990, contributing intellectual frameworks for regional security architecture.[^38]

Kusumaatmadja met with the foreign press every Friday, elaborating on Indonesian diplomacy in detailed discussions, making Indonesian foreign policy more transparent and accessible to international observers.[^39]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his overwhelmingly positive legacy, Kusumaatmadja's career was not without controversy or criticism. During his tenure as Foreign Minister promoting Indonesia's cultural diplomacy concept, an Asmat tribe art exhibition in London drew criticism due to what observers characterized as disrespectful treatment of Asmat culture by Indonesian academics involved in the exhibition.[^40] This incident highlighted tensions between promoting Indonesian culture internationally and adequately representing and respecting the diverse indigenous cultures within Indonesia's borders.

Earlier in his career, during the Sukarno era, Kusumaatmadja faced political difficulties when his concept of law as a tool of social engineering contradicted the government's prevailing views on law, leading to his forced residence overseas and dismissal from his position as Dean of the Faculty of Law at Padjadjaran University.[^41] This episode reflected the political tensions of the time and the risks faced by intellectuals whose ideas challenged government orthodoxy.

More broadly, Kusumaatmadja served as a senior minister throughout the New Order regime of President Suharto from 1974 to 1988—a period later criticized for authoritarianism, human rights abuses, and corruption. While Kusumaatmadja's personal reputation remained largely untarnished and his specific areas of responsibility (justice and foreign affairs) were not the primary sites of the regime's most egregious violations, his long association with the Suharto government has been a subject of debate. His designation as a National Hero in 2025 alongside Suharto himself provoked protests from student organizations such as the BEM UI Alliance, which urged the government to revoke Suharto's title, though Kusumaatmadja's designation was not specifically contested.[^42]

Some critics of his legal theory have questioned whether the "law as a tool for development" concept, while intellectually sophisticated, may have been used to justify top-down legal reforms that prioritized state-led development over participatory democratic processes during the authoritarian New Order period. However, such criticisms have been relatively muted in scholarly discourse, which has generally emphasized the progressive and modernizing aspects of his legal philosophy.

## Personal Life

Mochtar Kusumaatmadja married Ida Kusumaatmadja, who passed away in 2014, seven years before his own death.[^43] The couple had at least two children who achieved distinction in their own fields.

His daughter, Armida Salsiah Alisjahbana (née Kusumaatmadja), married Andi Alisjahbana, a grandson of the famous poet Sutan Takdir Alisjahbana.[^44] Armida became a distinguished economist and international civil servant, serving as Executive Secretary of the United Nations Economic and Social Commission for Asia and Pacific, while also holding a professorship in the Faculty of Economics at Padjadjaran University—following in her father's footsteps at the same institution where he built his academic career. Armida fondly recalled their family time together, particularly during their stay in the United States in the mid-1960s when Kusumaatmadja was pursuing further studies.[^45]

His son, Rachmat Askari Kusumaatmadja, also maintained the family's tradition of public service and intellectual achievement, though he maintained a lower public profile than his sister.[^46]

Kusumaatmadja's extended family connections reflected Indonesia's interconnected elite networks. His cousin Juwono Soedarsono served as Minister of Education and Culture under President Habibie and later as Minister of Defense, demonstrating the family's multi-generational commitment to Indonesian public service.[^47]

Colleagues and students remembered Kusumaatmadja as possessing a lucid mind and exceptional analytical abilities, with a capacity to explain complex legal concepts in accessible terms.[^48] Despite his intellectual prowess and high government positions, he maintained relationships with academic institutions throughout his career, continuing to value scholarly work and mentorship of younger legal scholars. His weekly Friday press briefings as Foreign Minister demonstrated his belief in transparency and his willingness to engage in substantive discussions rather than merely issuing formal statements.[^49]

## Legacy

Mochtar Kusumaatmadja's legacy extends across multiple dimensions of Indonesian national life and international law. His death on June 6, 2021, at age 92, prompted widespread recognition of his contributions, with his body laid in state at a funeral home in Kebayoran Baru, South Jakarta, before burial at Kalibata Heroes' Cemetery on the same day.[^50] Former State Secretary Yusril Ihza Mahendra immediately proposed that Kusumaatmadja be designated a National Hero, a proposal that came to fruition in 2025 when President Prabowo Subianto officially conferred the title during a ceremony at the State Palace on November 10, 2025.[^51]

### Maritime Law and Territorial Sovereignty

Kusumaatmadja's most visible legacy is the archipelagic state concept that fundamentally transformed Indonesia's territorial extent and legal status. The Wawasan Nusantara (Archipelagic Outlook) concept he developed remains Indonesia's foundation for determining territorial boundaries and fostering national unity across the archipelago.[^52] This achievement expanded Indonesia's maritime territory by one-third and established legal control over waters that had previously been considered international, with profound implications for resource exploitation, security, and national identity.[^53]

Beyond Indonesia, the archipelagic state principle incorporated into UNCLOS Articles 46-54 influenced international law globally, providing a legal framework subsequently adopted by other island nations including the Philippines, Fiji, and Papua New Guinea. Kusumaatmadja's work thus contributed not merely to Indonesian national interests but to the evolution of international maritime law itself.[^54]

### Legal Philosophy and Development Law Theory

Kusumaatmadja's adaptation of Roscoe Pound's "law as a tool of social engineering" into his own theory of development law (hukum pembangunan) created an influential school of legal thought in Indonesia.[^55] His theory emphasizes that law must be adaptive to social change and empowered to direct change and development in orderly ways, rather than merely functioning conservatively to maintain existing social order.[^56] This synthesis of American legal realism with Indonesian developmental needs, incorporating elements of Northrop's cultural theory and Laswell and McDougal's policy-oriented theory, provided intellectual justification for legal reforms during Indonesia's rapid development.[^57]

Contemporary legal scholars continue to debate and develop Kusumaatmadja's theories, with recent academic work "re-reading" his development law theory to explore its philosophical basis and consider its continuing relevance.[^58] His influence on Indonesian legal education remains profound, with his textbook "Pengantar Hukum Internasional" (Introduction to International Law), co-authored with Etty R. Agoes, serving as a mandatory text for international law students throughout Indonesia.[^59]

### Diplomatic Innovation

Kusumaatmadja's diplomatic legacy includes his innovative approaches to conflict resolution, particularly his "cocktail party" diplomacy that helped initiate Cambodia peace processes.[^60] Senior diplomats have emphasized that his struggle in Indonesian diplomacy on the international stage demonstrated "extraordinary services" and that his contributions are recognized and appreciated by the international community, with his ideas remaining relevant and continuing to develop.[^61]

### Institutional Impact

After serving as Foreign Minister, Kusumaatmadja was appointed as a member of the International Law Commission (ILC) in Geneva, becoming the first Indonesian to hold this position—a testament to his international standing in the legal community.[^62] His reforms of Indonesia's legal education curriculum, the Institute of National Law Reform, and his mentorship of generations of Indonesian lawyers and diplomats created institutional legacies that extended far beyond his lifetime.

Padjadjaran University, where he served as professor, dean, and rector, has honored his memory by conferring the "Mochtar Kusumaatmadja Award" to distinguished figures in international law, including Professor Tommy Koh.[^63] The Pasupati Flyover in Bandung was officially renamed to honor him, providing a physical landmark commemorating his contributions.[^64]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholarly and official assessments of Kusumaatmadja's contributions have been overwhelmingly positive, emphasizing his role as a visionary who successfully navigated between Indonesian national interests and international legal norms. The Indonesian government's designation of him as a National Hero in the field of "Law and Politics Struggle" represents official recognition of his profound contributions to Indonesian sovereignty and legal development.[^65]

International law scholars recognize Kusumaatmadja as having fundamentally shaped contemporary maritime law. His persistent diplomatic efforts over 25 years to secure recognition of the archipelagic state principle demonstrated exceptional dedication and strategic acumen. The incorporation of this principle into UNCLOS—a multilateral treaty eventually ratified by 168 countries—represents a remarkable achievement for a legal concept initially advanced by a single developing nation.[^66]

Academic colleagues and former students consistently praise Kusumaatmadja's intellectual rigor and clarity. Yu Un Oppusunggu's memorial tribute at the University of Indonesia's Faculty of Law characterized him as a legal wonk with a lucid mind, capable of making complex legal concepts accessible while maintaining analytical sophistication.[^67] His development law theory continues to be taught and debated in Indonesian law faculties, with scholars describing it as a distinctively Indonesian contribution to legal philosophy that successfully adapted Western legal concepts to local contexts.[^68]

Diplomatic historians credit Kusumaatmadja with elevating Indonesia's international standing and advancing ASEAN's role in regional affairs. His approach to the Cambodia conflict demonstrated that creative diplomatic methods could achieve breakthroughs where conventional approaches had failed, establishing models for conflict resolution that remained relevant decades later.[^69] His emphasis on cultural diplomacy anticipated contemporary "soft power" approaches to international relations, recognizing that international influence derives not only from military or economic strength but also from cultural appeal and persuasiveness.[^70]

### Negative Views

Critical assessments of Kusumaatmadja's legacy, while relatively rare in published scholarship, focus primarily on his association with the authoritarian Suharto regime. Critics note that regardless of his personal integrity and the apolitical nature of much of his work, his service as a senior minister from 1974 to 1988 made him part of a government later condemned for human rights violations, suppression of dissent, and systematic corruption. The fact that his National Hero designation in 2025 occurred alongside Suharto's controversial designation prompted some observers to question whether sufficient critical distance had been established from New Order governance practices.[^71]

Some legal scholars have questioned whether Kusumaatmadja's development law theory, despite its intellectual sophistication, may have provided theoretical justification for top-down legal reforms that prioritized state-directed development over participatory democratic processes. The emphasis on law as a tool for engineering social change could be interpreted as privileging technocratic expertise over popular sovereignty, particularly when implemented within an authoritarian political context where genuine public participation in lawmaking was limited.[^72]

Regarding the archipelagic state concept itself, while universally recognized as a triumph for Indonesian sovereignty, some critics have noted that the extensive maritime claims it enabled have occasionally created tensions with neighboring states over boundary delimitation and passage rights. The principle that enhanced Indonesian territorial control simultaneously created new complexities in maritime boundary negotiations with Malaysia, Singapore, the Philippines, and Australia—issues that remain unresolved in some cases decades after UNCLOS came into force.[^73]

Environmental advocates have also noted that while the archipelagic state concept secured Indonesian sovereignty over vast marine areas, it did not automatically ensure sustainable management of those marine resources. Subsequent decades have seen significant challenges in marine conservation, illegal fishing, and resource depletion within Indonesia's extended maritime zones—issues that were beyond Kusumaatmadja's direct responsibility but represent ongoing challenges to fully realizing the potential of the territorial framework he established.[^74]

Finally, some observers have noted that Kusumaatmadja's cultural diplomacy initiatives, while well-intentioned, occasionally displayed the tensions inherent in presenting a unified "Indonesian" culture internationally while the nation encompasses extraordinary ethnic, linguistic, and cultural diversity. The criticism of the Asmat exhibition in London exemplified these tensions, suggesting that promoting Indonesian culture abroad required more careful attention to representing diverse indigenous communities respectfully rather than subsuming them into a homogenized national culture.[^75]

## Famous Quotes

> "Law must not only function to maintain the orderliness of people's lives—a conservative function—but must also be empowered to direct change and development in order. Thus the law can be a tool of social engineering."

Context: This statement encapsulates Kusumaatmadja's development law theory, adapting Roscoe Pound's concept to Indonesian developmental needs.[^76]

> "All waters surrounding and between the islands of Indonesia are an inseparable part of the land and are under the sovereignty of Indonesia."

Context: The core principle of the Djuanda Declaration that Kusumaatmadja conceptualized, asserting Indonesian sovereignty over archipelagic waters.[^77]

> "Legal language must be uniform, usable, and understandable to the people."

Context: Kusumaatmadja's statement as Minister of Justice explaining the importance of standardizing legal terminology to make law accessible to ordinary citizens.[^78]
