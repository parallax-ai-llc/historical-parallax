## Summary

Spanish conquistador who led a murderous rebellion during an Amazon expedition, declaring independence from Spain and killing dozens of followers. He was called 'The Wrath of God' and was killed by his own men.

## Background

- Lope de Aguirre was a Basque-born Spanish soldier who participated in the Spanish conquest and colonial wars in South America.
- He became infamous for violence and insubordination, culminating in a dramatic mutiny during an expedition in search of wealth (often associated with the El Dorado legend).

## Amazon expedition and mutiny

- Aguirre joined an expedition led by **Pedro de Ursúa** traveling downriver through the Amazon basin.
- During the journey, he helped organize a mutiny in which Ursúa was killed.
- The rebels elevated **Fernando de Guzmán** as a leader; Aguirre later turned on Guzmán as well, consolidating power through intimidation and killings.

## Break with Spain

- Aguirre is known for a confrontational letter to **King Philip II of Spain**, framing himself as in revolt against royal authority.
- In chronicles, he and his followers described themselves as "traitors" to the king while claiming a new political identity, a rare and extreme stance for a conquistador-era expedition.

## Final campaign and death

- The mutineers moved toward the Caribbean coastline and into what is now Venezuela.
- Aguirre's support eroded as Spanish forces closed in.
- He was killed by his own men in 1561 when his position became untenable.

## Historical reputation

- He has been portrayed as a symbol of the brutal extremes of conquest-era violence and of rebellion within the Spanish imperial project.
- Later literature and film sometimes romanticize or mythologize him, but the core historical record is built from colonial chronicles and official reports.
