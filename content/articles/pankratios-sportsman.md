## Summary

Arrhichion of Phigalia was an ancient Greek pankratiast who won the Olympic pankration three times. In his final bout, he was killed by his opponent's chokehold but was still declared the winner because his opponent submitted first.

## Context: Pankration

Pankration was a combat sport in the ancient Olympic Games combining striking and grappling. While rules varied by period and city, it was generally closer to "no-holds-barred" competition than modern regulated combat sports, with only a small set of prohibitions.

## Why the Story Is Remembered

Arrhichion's final Olympic bout became famous as an extreme example of competitive ethos in Greek athletics. The core legend is that, even while being choked, he executed a counter that forced his opponent to signal submission, after which Arrhichion was discovered to have died. The officials then awarded the victory because the opponent submitted first.

## Historical Notes

Ancient sources often blend athletic reporting with moral exemplars about courage, discipline, and glory. Modern retellings typically emphasize the symbolic value of the incident rather than treating every detail as verifiable reportage.

## Legacy

The episode is frequently cited in discussions of early combat sports, the cultural status of Olympic victors in Greece, and the evolution of safety norms in athletic competition.

## Reception

Because the account is preserved through later writers, it is commonly treated as part athletic history and part cultural myth-making. Regardless of strict historicity, the story functions as a durable illustration of how ancient Greeks framed victory, honor, and endurance.
