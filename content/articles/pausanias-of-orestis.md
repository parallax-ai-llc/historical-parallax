## Summary

Bodyguard who assassinated King Philip II of Macedon, father of Alexander the Great.

## Historical Context

- Philip II (r. 359–336 BCE) had transformed Macedon into the dominant military power in Greece and was preparing a campaign against the Persian Empire at the time of his death.
- The assassination occurred during a public festival at Aegae (modern Vergina), a major royal center and burial site of the Argead dynasty.

## Assassination of Philip II

- Pausanias struck Philip II with a dagger during the procession, exploiting the moment when the king was separated from his bodyguards.
- Contemporary reconstructions emphasize the event’s public setting: a ceremonial procession where access to the king was briefly less controlled than on campaign.
- Ancient accounts differ on motive. Commonly reported themes include:
  - a personal grievance connected to a perceived insult and lack of redress,
  - political intrigue in the Macedonian court, and
  - the possibility of accomplices (reported, but not definitively proven).

## Immediate Aftermath

- Pausanias attempted to flee after the attack but was killed shortly afterward by members of Philip’s entourage.
- Philip’s death accelerated the succession of Alexander III (“the Great”), who secured the throne quickly and proceeded with the Persian campaign soon after.

## Significance

- The assassination removed the architect of Macedonian hegemony in Greece, but it did not reverse Macedon’s position; instead, it passed a consolidated state and army to Alexander.
- For historians, the event is a focal point for studying:
  - court politics and personal rivalries in late Argead Macedon,
  - the security and ritual choreography of royal appearances, and
  - how later narratives attempted to explain a dramatic political rupture.

## Notes on Sources

- Surviving narratives are primarily from later Greek and Roman writers and are shaped by their political and moral framing.
- Because Pausanias was killed immediately, direct testimony is absent; motive and conspiracy claims rely on retrospective accounts.
