## Summary

Huldrych Zwingli (1484-1531) was a Swiss Protestant Reformer who led the Reformation in German-speaking Switzerland, establishing a theological movement parallel to but distinct from Martin Luther's reforms in Germany. As the people's priest at the Grossmünster in Zurich, Zwingli initiated reforms that transformed the city into a Protestant stronghold, emphasizing the authority of Scripture over church tradition and rejecting Catholic practices he deemed unbiblical. His theological disagreements with Luther, particularly over the nature of the Eucharist, created a lasting division within Protestantism. Zwingli's life ended tragically on the battlefield at Kappel in 1531, where he served as chaplain to Zurich's forces in a religious civil war, cementing his legacy as both a reformer and a political-religious leader willing to die for his convictions.

## Early Life and Background

Huldrych (also spelled Ulrich) Zwingli was born on January 1, 1484, in the Toggenburg valley of Switzerland, specifically in the mountain village of Wildhaus, just weeks after Martin Luther's birth in Germany.[^1] He was the third child of Bartholomew Zwingli, a prosperous farmer and local magistrate who served as the chief magistrate of Wildhaus, and Margaret Meili, daughter of a prominent local family.[^2] The family was relatively well-off by peasant standards, which enabled them to provide Huldrych with an excellent education—a privilege uncommon for rural families of the era.

Zwingli's early education began at age six in Basel under the tutelage of his uncle, Bartholomew, who was a priest and dean at Wesen.[^3] Recognizing the boy's intellectual gifts, his family sent him to study in Bern at age ten under the renowned humanist scholar Heinrich Wölflin (Lupulus), where he received thorough training in Latin and was introduced to music, at which he excelled.[^4] He learned to play several instruments, including the lute, harp, violin, flute, and dulcimer, and music would remain a lifelong passion.[^5]

At thirteen, Zwingli moved to Vienna to attend the university there, studying under the celebrated humanist Conrad Celtis and immersing himself in the studia humanitatis—grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, and moral philosophy.[^6] The University of Vienna was a center of humanist learning, and Zwingli absorbed the Renaissance emphasis on returning ad fontes ("to the sources"), which would profoundly shape his later approach to biblical interpretation.[^7] After two years in Vienna, he returned to Basel in 1502 to complete his education at the University of Basel, earning his Bachelor of Arts in 1504 and his Master of Arts in 1506.[^8]

During his Basel years, Zwingli studied under Thomas Wyttenbach, a theology professor who planted seeds of reformist thinking by questioning indulgences and emphasizing Christ's death as the only price for redemption—ideas that would later blossom in Zwingli's theology.[^9] He was ordained as a Catholic priest in September 1506, just after completing his master's degree, and took his first pastoral position in Glarus, a small town in eastern Switzerland.[^10]

---

## Rise to Power

Zwingli served as parish priest in Glarus from 1506 to 1516, a formative decade during which he deepened his humanist studies and began developing reformist convictions.[^11] He taught himself Greek to read the New Testament in its original language and immersed himself in the works of Erasmus, the great Dutch humanist whose critical edition of the Greek New Testament (published in 1516) became foundational to Zwingli's exegetical method.[^12] Zwingli later claimed he had memorized all of Paul's epistles in Greek, demonstrating his commitment to scriptural scholarship.[^13]

During this period, Zwingli served as chaplain to Swiss mercenary troops, accompanying them on military campaigns to Italy in 1513 and 1515.[^14] These experiences exposed him to the brutality and moral corruption of the mercenary system, in which Swiss soldiers were rented out to foreign powers, often dying in conflicts that served no Swiss interest.[^15] The devastating Swiss defeat at the Battle of Marignano in 1515, where he witnessed thousands of his countrymen slaughtered, intensified his opposition to the mercenary trade—a stance that would later cause political friction.[^16]

In 1516, Zwingli accepted a position as people's priest at the pilgrimage church in Einsiedeln, one of Switzerland's most important religious sites, housing a famous Black Madonna statue that attracted thousands of pilgrims.[^17] At Einsiedeln, Zwingli began preaching more boldly against superstitious practices and the sale of indulgences, even as he benefited from the pilgrimage economy.[^18] His reputation as a learned preacher grew, and he continued his intensive study of Scripture and the Church Fathers, particularly Augustine.[^19]

Zwingli's decisive breakthrough came in December 1518, when he was elected people's priest (Leutpriester) at the Grossmünster, the principal church in Zurich, Switzerland's most important city.[^20] He assumed his duties on January 1, 1519, his thirty-fifth birthday, and immediately announced a revolutionary preaching program: he would abandon the traditional lectionary and instead preach systematically through the entire Gospel of Matthew, verse by verse, explaining Scripture in its plain sense.[^21] This marked a decisive break with medieval homiletic practice and signaled Zwingli's commitment to sola scriptura—Scripture alone as the authority for Christian faith and practice.[^22]

Zwingli's expository preaching attracted large crowds and established him as Zurich's leading religious voice.[^23] In late 1519, plague struck Zurich, killing approximately one-third of the population.[^24] Zwingli himself contracted the disease and nearly died, an experience that deepened his sense of dependence on God's sovereignty and strengthened his reformist convictions.[^25] His survival enhanced his reputation and moral authority in the city.

---

## Major Achievements

Zwingli's most significant achievement was the establishment of the Swiss Reformed tradition, creating a form of Protestantism distinct from Lutheranism and deeply influential on later Reformed theology.[^26] Between 1519 and 1525, Zwingli led Zurich through a systematic reformation that transformed the city's religious, social, and political life, creating a model that other Swiss cities would follow.[^27]

The First Zurich Disputation of January 1523 marked the formal beginning of the Reformation in Zurich and demonstrated Zwingli's strategic brilliance.[^28] The Zurich city council convened a public debate to adjudicate religious controversies, inviting clergy from throughout the region to challenge or defend Zwingli's teachings.[^29] Zwingli presented his Sixty-Seven Articles, a comprehensive summary of his theology emphasizing Scripture's supreme authority, Christ's unique mediatorship, and the rejection of unbiblical Catholic practices.[^30] When his opponents failed to refute him from Scripture, the council ruled in Zwingli's favor, giving official sanction to continue his reforming preaching—an unprecedented victory that made Zurich's government the sponsor of religious reform.[^31]

Subsequent disputations in October 1523 addressed images and the Mass, resulting in decisions to remove images from churches and to replace the Catholic Mass with a simpler communion service.[^32] Zwingli composed a new liturgy, the Action or Use of the Lord's Supper (April 1525), which replaced the Latin Mass with a vernacular service emphasizing remembrance rather than sacrifice.[^33] The communion elements were distributed to congregants seated in pews, using wooden plates and cups rather than precious vessels, symbolizing the memorial character of the rite.[^34]

Zwingli's reforms extended beyond worship to encompass education, social welfare, and moral discipline. He helped establish the Prophezei (Prophecy), a continuing education program where clergy gathered daily to study Scripture in the original languages—a prototype for Reformed theological education and biblical scholarship.[^35] The lectures were open to the public and conducted in both Latin and German, democratizing biblical knowledge.[^36]

He also reformed Zurich's social welfare system, transforming monastic properties and endowments into resources for poor relief, education, and healthcare.[^37] The dissolution of monasteries in Zurich redirected substantial wealth toward the common good, creating a comprehensive welfare system that provided for the elderly, widows, orphans, and the sick.[^38] Zwingli advocated for universal education, arguing that an educated citizenry was essential for a godly commonwealth.[^39]

Zwingli's theological writings articulated a distinctive Reformed theology. His Commentary on True and False Religion (1525) was the first systematic theology of the Reformation, predating John Calvin's Institutes by more than a decade.[^40] In it, Zwingli developed his understanding of the sacraments as signs and seals of grace rather than means of grace, rejecting any notion that physical elements could convey spiritual benefits ex opere operato.[^41]

His theology of the Lord's Supper became his most controversial position. Against Luther's doctrine of the real bodily presence of Christ in the Eucharist, Zwingli argued that Christ's words "This is my body" should be understood figuratively as "This signifies my body," since Christ's ascended body was in heaven and could not be physically present in the bread.[^42] This symbolic or memorialist interpretation created an unbridgeable divide with Luther, dramatically demonstrated at the Marburg Colloquy of 1529.[^43]

At Marburg, Philip of Hesse attempted to unite the Protestant forces by reconciling Luther and Zwingli.[^44] The two reformers agreed on fourteen of fifteen articles but could not resolve their eucharistic differences.[^45] Luther famously wrote "Hoc est corpus meum" ("This is my body") in chalk on the table and refused to budge, declaring Zwingli to be of a different spirit.[^46] The failure at Marburg permanently divided German and Swiss Protestantism and weakened the Protestant political alliance.[^47]

Zwingli's political achievements included forging the Christian Civic Alliance, a defensive league of Reformed Swiss cities and territories established in 1527-1529 to counter Catholic cantons and external threats.[^48] He envisioned a confederation of Reformed territories that could defend religious freedom and advance evangelical preaching throughout Switzerland and beyond.[^49] This political activism reflected Zwingli's conviction that civil authorities had a divine mandate to promote true religion and suppress idolatry.[^50]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Zwingli's career was marked by significant controversies that tarnished his reputation and contributed to his violent death. His entanglement of religion and politics, willingness to use coercion, and perceived betrayal of more radical reformers generated substantial criticism both during his lifetime and subsequently.[^51]

The Anabaptist crisis revealed the authoritarian dimension of Zwingli's reformation. Former disciples including Conrad Grebel and Felix Manz, convinced that Zwingli was not pushing reform far enough, particularly regarding baptism, broke with him in 1523-1525.[^52] They argued that only believers should be baptized, rejecting infant baptism as unbiblical, and began rebaptizing adults—hence the name Anabaptist (re-baptizer).[^53] Zwingli vehemently opposed this movement, viewing it as a threat to social order and the unified Christian commonwealth he envisioned.[^54]

With Zwingli's support, the Zurich council issued increasingly harsh mandates against Anabaptists, culminating in a January 1526 decree making rebaptism a capital offense.[^55] Felix Manz, Zwingli's former colleague, became the first Protestant martyred by other Protestants when he was drowned in the Limmat River in January 1527—a grotesque irony given that drowning was chosen to mock the Anabaptist emphasis on water baptism.[^56] Zwingli bore direct responsibility for this persecution, having provided the theological justification and urged the civil authorities to act.[^57] Modern scholars widely condemn this episode as a grievous violation of religious liberty and a stain on Zwingli's legacy.[^58]

Zwingli's aggressive political-military strategy generated controversy and ultimately proved disastrous. He advocated using Zurich's military and economic power to pressure Catholic cantons to accept Reformed preaching, even supporting a blockade of food supplies to the rural Catholic cantons in 1529.[^59] This economic warfare caused severe hardship and deepened animosity, making religious civil war increasingly likely.[^60]

Critics accused Zwingli of violating Christian principles by advocating armed conflict over religious differences.[^61] Unlike Luther, who distinguished sharply between the spiritual and temporal kingdoms and generally opposed religious warfare, Zwingli developed a theocratic vision in which the godly magistrate had a duty to suppress false religion by force if necessary.[^62] This position shocked many contemporaries and troubled later Reformed theologians who sought to distance themselves from theocratic excesses.[^63]

Zwingli's personal life also generated scandal. In 1522, before clerical marriage was legal, he secretly married Anna Reinhart, a widow, though they did not publicly acknowledge the marriage until 1524.[^64] More damagingly, evidence suggests Zwingli had engaged in sexual relationships during his earlier priesthood, possibly violating his vow of celibacy.[^65] In a 1523 letter, he obliquely admitted to past moral failures, though he defended himself by noting he had never violated a virgin or another man's wife.[^66] While such behavior was common among Renaissance clergy, it provided ammunition for Catholic critics and raised questions about his moral authority.[^67]

Zwingli's elitism and occasional arrogance alienated some supporters. He could be dismissive of theological opponents, showing particular contempt for Anabaptists whom he regarded as ignorant fanatics.[^68] His close identification with Zurich's ruling elite and comfort with using state power to enforce religious conformity contrasted unfavorably with the more populist and egalitarian impulses of the early Reformation.[^69]

The catastrophic outcome of Zwingli's political-military strategy at Kappel vindicated his critics. Against the advice of more cautious colleagues, Zwingli supported renewed military action against the Catholic cantons in 1531.[^70] When Zurich's forces were ambushed and routed at the Second Battle of Kappel on October 11, 1531, Zwingli accompanied the troops as chaplain and was killed in the fighting.[^71] His body was quartered and burned by Catholic soldiers, his ashes mixed with dung and scattered—a gruesome end that shocked Protestant Europe.[^72]

## Personal Life

Huldrych Zwingli married Anna Reinhart (née Adlischweiler) sometime in 1522, though the marriage was kept quiet until April 1524, when it was publicly celebrated after clerical marriage became legally recognized in Zurich.[^73] Anna was a widow with three children from her previous marriage to Hans Reinhart, a prosperous Zurich patrician who had died in 1517.[^74] The marriage appears to have been genuinely affectionate and brought Zwingli domestic stability during the tumultuous years of reform.[^75]

Anna and Huldrych had four children together: Regula (born 1524), Wilhelm (born 1526), Huldrych (born 1528), and Anna (born 1530).[^76] Zwingli was reportedly a devoted father who delighted in his children, though his demanding schedule limited family time.[^77] The family lived in relative comfort in the Grossmünster parish house, supported by Zwingli's salary and Anna's inheritance.[^78]

Tragically, Anna became a widow for the second time when Huldrych was killed at Kappel in 1531, leaving her to raise four young children, including an infant.[^79] She survived her husband by many years, dying in 1538, and was remembered as a dignified and pious woman who bore her losses with Christian fortitude.[^80]

Zwingli was described by contemporaries as vigorous, athletic, and blessed with a robust constitution—qualities that served him well during his military chaplaincy and energetic pastoral ministry.[^81] He stood of medium height with a strong build, had a full beard in his later years, and maintained an active lifestyle that included hiking in the Swiss mountains.[^82]

Music remained a central part of Zwingli's life and personality. He was an accomplished musician who played multiple instruments with skill and composed music, including hymns and patriotic songs.[^83] Ironically, despite his personal love of music, Zwingli removed congregational singing and instrumental music from worship, fearing it distracted from Scripture and prayer—a position that distinguished him from Luther, who championed congregational hymnody.[^84] This seeming contradiction troubled some admirers: Zwingli personally cherished what he liturgically banned.[^85]

Contemporaries described Zwingli as charismatic, eloquent, and intellectually formidable, with a quick wit and sharp tongue that could charm or wound.[^86] He possessed immense energy and worked with singular focus, often studying late into the night.[^87] Friends noted his warmth and generosity, while opponents experienced his polemical ferocity.[^88]

Zwingli maintained extensive correspondence with reformers throughout Europe, including Oecolampadius in Basel, Bucer in Strasbourg, and various contacts in German territories, revealing his pan-European vision for evangelical reform.[^89] He was less personally intimate with Luther than these others, and their few direct interactions were marked by tension and mutual suspicion.[^90]

## Legacy

Huldrych Zwingli's legacy profoundly shaped Reformed Protestantism, Swiss national identity, and the broader trajectory of Western Christianity, even as his reputation remained more contested than that of other major reformers.[^91] His sudden death in 1531 cut short his career just as Luther's continued for another fifteen years and Calvin's had not yet begun, limiting Zwingli's direct influence.[^92] Nonetheless, his theological innovations, institutional reforms, and martyrdom created a lasting template for Reformed Christianity.[^93]

Zwingli's most enduring legacy was establishing the theological and ecclesiological foundations of the Reformed tradition, distinct from both Catholicism and Lutheranism.[^94] His emphasis on God's absolute sovereignty, predestination, the regulative principle of worship (that corporate worship should include only what Scripture commands), and a memorial view of the sacraments became hallmarks of Reformed theology.[^95] While John Calvin later systematized and propagated these ideas more influentially, Zwingli pioneered them, creating the intellectual framework Calvin inherited.[^96]

The Zurich reformation model—reformation by magistrate rather than by prince, enacted through public disputations, and creating a godly commonwealth integrating church and civil authority—was replicated in Bern, Basel, Strasbourg, and other cities.[^97] This urban, republican form of Protestantism contrasted with the princely Lutheranism of Germany and offered a template for Protestant development in city-states and representative governments.[^98]

Zwingli's educational legacy proved remarkably durable. The Prophezei he established evolved into the Zurich Academy and eventually the University of Zurich, continuing his vision of rigorous biblical scholarship in original languages.[^99] This model of ministerial training, combining linguistic competence, exegetical rigor, and theological depth, became standard in Reformed theological education worldwide.[^100]

In Switzerland, Zwingli occupies a unique place in national consciousness as both religious reformer and patriot who died defending his homeland.[^101] His opposition to the mercenary system is celebrated as prescient civic virtue, and his death at Kappel is memorialized as martyrdom for faith and fatherland.[^102] Numerous monuments, churches, and institutions bear his name throughout Switzerland, particularly in Zurich, where he remains a civic hero.[^103]

Yet Zwingli's legacy remains more ambivalent than Luther's or Calvin's in broader Protestant memory. The Anabaptist persecution haunts his reputation, particularly among Free Church Protestants who trace their ancestry to the martyred radicals.[^104] Mennonites, Baptists, and other believers' church traditions remember Zwingli as a persecutor, not a hero—a legacy of religious intolerance that contradicts Protestant principles of sola scriptura and liberty of conscience.[^105]

Zwingli's willingness to use violence in religion's cause troubled many later Protestants. While Calvin also countenanced religious coercion (most infamously in the Servetus case), Zwingli's personal presence on the battlefield shocked sensibilities.[^106] The image of a reformer dying sword in hand seemed to contradict the gospel of peace and suggested a dangerous confusion of spiritual and carnal warfare.[^107]

The Zwinglian eucharistic position, while influential in some Reformed circles, was largely superseded by Calvin's mediating view, which affirmed a real spiritual (though not bodily) presence of Christ in the Supper.[^108] Most later Reformed confessions followed Calvin rather than Zwingli on this point, seeing Zwingli's position as inadequately capturing the sacrament's spiritual efficacy.[^109]

In ecumenical contexts, Zwingli represents both Protestant division and the limits of Protestant unity. The Marburg Colloquy's failure symbolizes the tragic inability of Protestants to transcend theological differences for political and ecclesial unity—a failure with lasting consequences for European history.[^110] Some historians speculate that a united Protestant front might have fundamentally altered the Reformation's trajectory and Europe's religious geography.[^111]

Modern scholarship has produced more nuanced assessments of Zwingli, recognizing his genuine theological creativity, pastoral commitment, and humanist erudition while not excusing his intolerance and militarism.[^112] He is increasingly studied not merely as a forerunner to Calvin but as an important thinker in his own right, whose integration of humanism and reformed theology offered a distinctive path within Protestantism.[^113]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Defenders and admirers of Zwingli have emphasized his theological acuity, biblical fidelity, and courageous leadership during a revolutionary period.[^114] Protestant historians have long celebrated him as a pioneer of Reformed Christianity who grasped Scripture's authority more purely than Luther and applied it more consistently across all areas of faith and practice.[^115]

Reformed theologians particularly appreciate Zwingli's robust doctrine of divine sovereignty and his resistance to any mediating elements between God and believers.[^116] His rejection of sacramental ex opere operato efficacy and insistence that faith alone appropriates grace aligned perfectly with Protestant principles.[^117] Twentieth-century theologians like Karl Barth, himself Swiss Reformed, appreciated Zwingli's Christological focus and his understanding that revelation comes through God's Word alone, not through material elements or human traditions.[^118]

Scholars praise Zwingli's intellectual breadth and humanist learning, noting that he brought Renaissance philology and rhetoric into the service of biblical interpretation.[^119] His mastery of Greek and Hebrew enabled direct engagement with Scripture in its original languages, making his exegesis more sophisticated than many contemporaries.[^120] Historian Bruce Gordon argues that Zwingli's humanist formation gave him interpretive tools Luther lacked, enabling more nuanced readings of difficult texts.[^121]

Zwingli's social and educational reforms receive high marks from historians of social welfare and education. His transformation of monastic wealth into social services created one of Europe's most advanced welfare systems and demonstrated practical application of Reformed principles.[^122] The Prophezei and related educational initiatives elevated biblical literacy and created an educated clergy and laity capable of informed religious participation.[^123]

Swiss historians, particularly in Zurich, celebrate Zwingli as a founding father of modern Switzerland, whose opposition to mercenary service promoted national integrity and whose reforms strengthened civic institutions.[^124] His vision of covenant community, in which religious and civic identity merged in mutual obligation, is seen as foundational to Swiss republican ideals.[^125] The monument to Zwingli in Zurich, depicting him with Bible and sword, captures this dual legacy as spiritual reformer and civic patriot.[^126]

Some scholars argue that Zwingli's willingness to die for his convictions, even in the ambiguous circumstances of Kappel, demonstrates authentic Christian commitment.[^127] Unlike reformers who theorized about martyrdom from safety, Zwingli embodied his theology, accepting death rather than compromise.[^128] This ultimate sacrifice, whatever its prudential wisdom, commands respect.[^129]

### Negative Views

Critics of Zwingli, both historical and contemporary, have focused on his religious intolerance, theocratic politics, and theological errors.[^130] Catholic polemicists from the sixteenth century onward portrayed him as a heretic who led Switzerland into schism, destroyed sacred traditions, and died a fitting death as a rebel against God and church.[^131] The desecration of his corpse, while shocking, was seen by some Catholics as divine judgment on a false prophet.[^132]

The most devastating criticism concerns Zwingli's persecution of Anabaptists. Historians of religious liberty, from Sebastian Castellio in the sixteenth century to Roland Bainton in the twentieth, have condemned Zwingli's abandonment of Protestant principles when dealing with dissenters.[^133] If sola scriptura means anything, critics argue, it must include the right to interpret Scripture according to conscience—precisely what Zwingli denied the Anabaptists.[^134] His approval of Felix Manz's execution represents a betrayal of the Reformation's liberating promise.[^135]

Lutheran critics, beginning with Luther himself, attacked Zwingli's eucharistic theology as rationalistic, spiritualistic, and destructive of sacramental mystery.[^136] Luther accused Zwingli of subjecting Scripture to human reason rather than accepting its plain words, and of reducing the Supper to a mere memorial devoid of Christ's presence.[^137] This Lutheran critique has persisted, with confessional Lutheran theologians viewing Zwinglian sacramentology as a gateway to liberalism and subjectivism.[^138]

Some historians criticize Zwingli's fusion of religion and politics as theocratic and dangerous. Unlike Luther's two-kingdoms theology, which maintained (in theory) a distinction between spiritual and temporal authority, Zwingli's vision of the godly commonwealth subordinated individual conscience to communal conformity and made dissent treasonous.[^139] Political theorists have traced authoritarian strands in later Reformed political thought back to Zwingli's willingness to use state power to enforce religious orthodoxy.[^140]

Zwingli's military adventurism and the disaster at Kappel drew sharp criticism from contemporaries and later historians. Even sympathetic Reformed historians acknowledge that Zwingli's aggressive political strategy was imprudent and contrary to Christian teaching about peacemaking.[^141] His death on the battlefield seemed to vindicate Catholic charges that the Reformation promoted rebellion and disorder.[^142]

Some scholars view Zwingli as intellectually and theologically inferior to both Luther and Calvin. His systematic theology lacks Luther's existential depth and experiential power, while his exegesis lacks Calvin's comprehensive scope and logical rigor.[^143] In this view, Zwingli was a transitional figure—important historically but superseded theologically by more capable minds.[^144]

Feminist and social historians have criticized Zwingli's patriarchal assumptions and the limited benefits of his reformation for women and marginalized groups.[^145] While he supported education, it remained gendered and hierarchical; while he reformed welfare, it operated paternalistically; while he married, he maintained strict male headship.[^146] The Zurich reformation, in this reading, reinforced social hierarchies even as it challenged religious ones.[^147]

## Famous Quotes

> "In the things of this life, the laborer is worthy of his keep, but in divine things, he who serves for gain is a slave." - From his criticism of the church's financial corruption and the sale of spiritual offices.[^148]

> "The truthfulness of God cannot deceive, therefore Christ is not bodily in the bread." - Expressing his memorial view of communion, which became central to the Reformed tradition's understanding of the Lord's Supper.[^149]

> "For God's sake do not put yourself at odds with the Word of God. For truly it will persist as surely as the Rhine follows its course. One can perhaps dam it up for a while, but it is impossible to stop it." - From a 1522 sermon, expressing confidence in Scripture's ultimate triumph over human opposition.[^150]

> "I know that God can take away my life... If God wills to break the dish to pieces, of what use is it? Let His will be done." - Spoken during his illness with plague in 1519, reflecting his theology of divine sovereignty.[^151]

> "I would rather die than see the truth of God suppressed." - Statement of conviction that proved prophetic given his death at Kappel defending his reformed vision.[^152]
