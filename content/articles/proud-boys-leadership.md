## Context and Organizational Background

The **Proud Boys** are a far-right extremist organization founded in 2016 in the United States. Public reporting commonly describes the group as a male-only “fraternal” organization that has been involved in political street violence and confrontations.

While the organization has had a central figure at different points, “leadership” in practice has often referred to a mix of national-level roles (e.g., chairman) and regional chapters.

## Notable Leadership Figures (Publicly Reported)

The names below are included because they are widely referenced in mainstream reporting and court records in relation to leadership roles or coordinating activities:

- **Gavin McInnes** — Founder (2016). He later publicly stated he had stepped away from the organization (commonly reported as occurring in 2018).
- **Enrique Tarrio** — Often described as a national chairman in the years leading up to January 6, 2021.
- **Ethan Nordean**, **Joseph Biggs**, **Zachary Rehl** — Described in U.S. federal prosecutions as key organizers/leaders in the group’s activities around January 6.

> Note: Specific titles and internal roles can vary by time period and by chapter, and are best treated as approximate unless corroborated by primary documents.

## January 6 and Subsequent Prosecutions (High-Level)

After the January 6, 2021 attack on the U.S. Capitol, multiple Proud Boys members and leaders were prosecuted in federal court. Several high-profile defendants were convicted in 2023, including convictions for **seditious conspiracy** in a major case tied to planning and coordination efforts.

Key points commonly reported about the case(s):

- The prosecutions focused on **planning, communications, and coordinated movement** by members and associates.
- Convictions and sentencing varied by defendant and by charges.
- Court records and trial evidence were central sources for many later summaries.

## What “Leadership” Typically Means in Reporting

In many articles and summaries, “Proud Boys leadership” functions as a shorthand for:

- Individuals with formal or informal authority inside the organization
- People alleged to have coordinated tactics or logistics
- Defendants highlighted in indictments as organizers or decision-makers

Because the group operates through chapters and informal networks, leadership attributions in media reporting may refer to influence rather than a fixed corporate-style hierarchy.
