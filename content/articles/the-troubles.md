## Summary

Ethno-nationalist conflict in Northern Ireland from 1968-1998 between republicans and loyalists that killed over 3,500 people. The conflict ended with the Good Friday Agreement.

## Parties and core issues

The conflict involved multiple armed groups and political actors, most prominently:

- **Irish republican/nationalist** organizations seeking a united Ireland and an end to British governance in Northern Ireland.
- **Ulster loyalist/unionist** organizations seeking to maintain Northern Ireland’s status within the United Kingdom.
- **State forces**, including the Royal Ulster Constabulary (RUC) and the British Army, which became deeply involved in security operations.

Underlying issues included constitutional status, political representation, discrimination and community inequality, policing legitimacy, and cycles of retaliatory violence.

## Common forms of violence

- Bombings and shootings by paramilitary groups.
- Sectarian attacks, intimidation, and punishment assaults.
- Security-force operations, raids, and contested uses of force.

## Major political milestones (high level)

While the violence fluctuated over time, political efforts included attempts at power-sharing and reforms to policing and governance. The 1998 Good Friday Agreement established a new devolved framework, cross-border institutions, and mechanisms for decommissioning weapons and prisoner releases.

## Legacy

The Troubles left long-term impacts on victims and survivors, community relations, policing, and politics in Northern Ireland. Disputes over truth recovery, accountability for past violence, and commemoration have continued well after the main settlement.
