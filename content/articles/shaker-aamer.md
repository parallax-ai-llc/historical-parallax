## Summary

Last British resident held in Guantanamo, detained for 13 years without charge before release.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2001 | Captured during the early phase of the U.S.-led war in Afghanistan |
| 2002 | Transferred to U.S. custody at Guantanamo Bay detention camp |
| 2007 | A U.S. administrative review reportedly cleared him for release (not implemented at the time) |
| 2015 | Released to the United Kingdom after more than a decade in detention |

## Context

- Aamer became widely described as the **last British resident** held at Guantanamo Bay.
- He was held **without a criminal trial** for years; his case became a prominent focus of legal and civil-society campaigns in the UK.

## Detention and public campaign (high level)

- Media and advocacy groups reported allegations of mistreatment in detention and noted periodic hunger strikes among detainees.
- UK parliamentarians, lawyers, and human-rights organizations repeatedly called for his return, citing the length of detention and family circumstances.
- His release in 2015 was widely covered as a significant moment in the UK debate about Guantanamo and post‑9/11 detention policy.

## After release

- After returning to the UK, reporting focused on reintegration and the ongoing public discussion about accountability, evidence standards, and national-security policy.

## Key facts

- Detained at Guantanamo Bay for roughly 2002–2015.
- Frequently described in UK coverage as the last British resident held there.
- Released without a U.S. criminal conviction.

## Legal Status and Clearance (High Level)

Public summaries often distinguish between:

- criminal prosecution in U.S. federal courts (which did not occur in his case)
- administrative detention decisions and review processes used for Guantanamo detainees

In accounts of his case, he was repeatedly described as having been **cleared for release** by U.S. review mechanisms years before he was actually transferred out, illustrating the gap that can exist between administrative determinations and diplomatic or security decisions.

## UK–US Diplomatic Dimension

The campaign for Aamer’s return is often framed as involving:

- sustained advocacy by lawyers and human-rights groups
- political pressure from UK parliamentarians
- negotiations and coordination between UK and U.S. authorities over transfer conditions

Because he was a UK resident rather than a U.S. citizen, the case also became a reference point in debates about what obligations governments have toward residents or nationals held abroad in security-related detention systems.

## Place in the Broader Guantanamo Debate

Aamer’s detention and release are frequently cited in broader discussions about:

- the evidentiary standards used to justify long-term detention
- due process and transparency in national-security systems
- the human and political costs of indefinite detention

These themes extend beyond any single detainee and remain central to historical analysis of the post‑9/11 era.
