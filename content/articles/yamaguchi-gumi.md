## Summary

Japans largest and most powerful yakuza syndicate with roots dating to 1915. At its peak it had 45,000 members and generated billions from drugs, gambling, and extortion.

## Background

The Yamaguchi-gumi is widely described as Japan’s largest yakuza syndicate, headquartered historically in the Kansai region with a long association with Kobe. It is frequently referenced in discussions of **boryokudan** (designated violent groups) and Japan’s modern approach to organized crime.

## Leadership and Expansion

Public accounts often highlight periods of expansion in the postwar era, with leadership building a federation-like structure that connected many affiliated groups. Over time, the syndicate became a central example of how yakuza organizations could combine:

- Local territorial control
- Inter-group alliance management
- Business infiltration and coercive influence

## Fragmentation and Factional Conflict

A major split in the mid-2010s led to rival factions. In reporting, this is typically framed as both a struggle over leadership and a structural response to increasing legal and financial pressure on yakuza groups.

## Significance

The group’s size and visibility mean that its internal developments are often treated as bellwethers for broader yakuza trends, including:

1. Membership decline and aging demographics
2. Shifts from overt street presence toward lower-visibility finance and fraud
3. Intensified regulation by national and prefectural authorities

## Notes on Sources

Counts of membership and estimates of revenue vary by source and time period, and are often presented in the media as approximations rather than audited figures.
