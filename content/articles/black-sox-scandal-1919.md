## Summary

Eight Chicago White Sox players conspired with gamblers to intentionally lose the 1919 World Series. All eight were banned from baseball for life despite being acquitted in court.

## Additional Context

- The scandal became one of the defining integrity crises in early professional baseball.
- It accelerated demands for stronger governance, as gambling influence and loose oversight were widely seen as systemic vulnerabilities.

## Key Elements (High Level)

- **Match-fixing allegation:** a group of players were accused of accepting money from gamblers in exchange for intentionally poor performance.
- **Public trust:** the episode damaged the league’s credibility and created pressure for decisive action.

## Institutional Aftermath

- Major League Baseball appointed a powerful commissioner (Kenesaw Mountain Landis) with broad authority to protect the sport’s integrity.
- The lifetime bans—imposed even after acquittals—are frequently discussed as an example of **league discipline operating independently of criminal courts**.

## Cultural Legacy

- The phrase “Black Sox” remains shorthand for sports corruption.
- The story has been retold in books and films and is often used as a case study in incentive design and governance.

## Practical Takeaways

- When pay is low or incentives are misaligned, athletes can become vulnerable to gambling approaches.
- Integrity systems often require both enforcement power and transparent investigative process to regain trust.
- The scandal is frequently compared to later match-fixing cases across sports for governance lessons.
