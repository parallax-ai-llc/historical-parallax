## Key Contributions (High-Level)

- **Radioactivity research:** Helped establish radioactivity as a quantitative field of study, including careful measurement and isolation of radioactive substances.
- **Discovery and isolation work:** With Pierre Curie, identified **polonium** and **radium**, and later produced highly purified radium compounds.
- **Scientific institutions:** Built enduring research capacity through the **Radium Institute/Institut Curie** in Paris and links to the Warsaw institute.

## Context and Impact

Maria Skłodowska-Curie’s work mattered beyond a single discovery: it provided methods, materials, and institutional models that shaped 20th‑century physics, chemistry, and medicine.

- **Nobel Prizes:** Widely recognized for receiving Nobel Prizes in **Physics (1903)** and **Chemistry (1911)**, reflecting both collaborative discovery and later independent chemical work.
- **Medical applications:** Radium research contributed to the early development of radiation-based medical treatments, even as long-term health risks were not yet well understood.

## World War I Work

During World War I, she promoted practical radiology for battlefield medicine, helping organize mobile X‑ray units (often called “little Curies”) and training personnel for diagnostic imaging.

## Legacy

- An enduring symbol of scientific rigor and persistence, especially in the face of institutional barriers for women in science.
- Her family and research networks continued to shape European scientific life and radiation medicine through the 20th century.
