## Summary

Italian banker known as Gods Banker found hanging under Blackfriars Bridge in London in 1982. He had connections to the Vatican, Mafia, and Masonic lodge P2.

## Background

Calvi rose through Italian banking during the postwar period and built his reputation inside Banco Ambrosiano, a Milan-based institution that expanded aggressively across borders.
His public image as a well-connected financier contrasted with mounting allegations that the bank relied on opaque offshore structures.

## Banco Ambrosiano Collapse

Banco Ambrosiano became central to a major Italian financial scandal when large amounts of money were found missing and the bank failed in 1982.
Investigations focused on complex transactions involving foreign subsidiaries and shell entities that made liabilities difficult to trace.
The scandal also drew attention to ties among business elites, political networks, and clandestine organizations in late-20th-century Italy.

## Death and Investigations

Calvi fled Italy shortly before the bank collapsed and was later found dead in London under Blackfriars Bridge.
His death was initially treated as suicide, but later inquiries and forensic reassessments fueled suspicions of homicide.
The case became intertwined with allegations involving the Mafia, the P2 Masonic lodge, and actors linked to Vatican financial affairs.

## Aftermath and Legacy

The collapse contributed to wider scrutiny of Italian banking governance, cross-border supervision, and the use of offshore vehicles.
Calvi’s death remains a widely cited example of how financial crime, political influence, and organized crime can intersect in high-profile scandals.
