## Summary

Sengoku warlord notorious for assassinating a shogun and burning Tōdai-ji. Committed suicide by exploding himself with a prized tea kettle.

## Additional Context

- Matsunaga Hisahide was active during Japan’s Sengoku period, when regional warlords competed for power amid a weakening Ashikaga shogunate.
- He is often portrayed as an archetypal “treacherous retainer,” but many surviving accounts are shaped by later political narratives and war chronicles.
- His association with tea culture reflects how the tea ceremony functioned as elite diplomacy: prized utensils signaled status and were used to cement alliances.

## Why He Matters

- The 1560s saw violent factional conflict in Kyoto; assassinations and coups were part of the era’s political toolkit, not isolated acts.
- Stories about his death (including the destruction of a famous tea kettle) persist because they combine warfare, prestige objects, and theatrical defiance.

## Legacy

- In popular history he appears as a cautionary figure about ambition and betrayal.
- For historians, the case illustrates how later sources can amplify sensational details while obscuring the strategic realities of Sengoku politics.
