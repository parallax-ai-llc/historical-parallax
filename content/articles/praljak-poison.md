## Summary

Slobodan Praljak, the Bosnian Croat general who dramatically drank cyanide in the ICTY courtroom upon hearing his conviction upheld. His death was broadcast live on television worldwide.

## Courtroom Incident

On 29 November 2017, Slobodan Praljak took poison during a session of the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY) after judges upheld his conviction and sentence. He declared that he rejected the verdict, drank from a small container, and was removed from the courtroom; he later died.

## Legal Context

Praljak was one of several Bosnian Croat political and military figures prosecuted by the ICTY for crimes committed during the Bosnian War. The tribunal’s cases examined command responsibility, treatment of detainees, and campaigns of persecution and violence.

## Media and Public Reaction

Because the event occurred in open court and was captured on live broadcast feeds, it became one of the most widely viewed courtroom deaths in modern history. Reactions split along political lines: some viewed the act as protest and martyrdom, while others emphasized accountability for wartime atrocities.

## Why the Event Is Historically Notable

- Demonstrated the emotional and political intensity surrounding war-crimes trials even decades after the conflict.
- Raised questions about **security procedures** and how a lethal substance entered a courtroom.
- Became a reference point in discussions of **international justice**, public legitimacy, and contested narratives of the Yugoslav wars.

## Aftermath

The ICTY and related institutions reviewed procedures following the incident. The broader body of ICTY jurisprudence continued to shape international criminal law and the historical record of atrocities in the former Yugoslavia.

## Related Topic

The incident is sometimes cited alongside other high-profile courtroom disruptions as an example of how legal proceedings can become political theatre.
