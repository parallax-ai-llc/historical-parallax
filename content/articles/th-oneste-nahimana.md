## Summary

Rwandan academic and co-founder of Radio Télévision Libre des Mille Collines who was convicted of genocide for inciting Hutu violence through media. His conviction was a landmark in media incitement law.

## Background

- Nahimana was trained as an academic and was associated with higher education in Rwanda prior to 1994.
- During the early 1990s, Rwanda experienced severe political violence and propaganda-driven polarization leading up to the 1994 genocide against the Tutsi.

## RTLM and the Role of Broadcast Media

- Radio Télévision Libre des Mille Collines (RTLM) became widely known for programming that promoted ethnic hatred and contributed to mobilization for violence.
- The case is commonly discussed as an example of how mass media can amplify dehumanizing narratives and provide operational encouragement during atrocity crimes.
- Analyses of RTLM often focus on the interaction between rhetoric (incitement), audience reach, and the broader political-military context.

## The ICTR "Media Trial" (Landmark Case)

- Nahimana was tried at the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR) alongside other media figures in what became known as the "Media Trial."
- The proceedings examined whether and how speech acts (including broadcasts) can satisfy elements of crimes such as direct and public incitement to commit genocide.
- The judgments are frequently referenced in international criminal law discussions about the boundary between protected expression and criminal incitement.

## Conviction, Appeals, and Significance

- The ICTR convicted Nahimana in 2003 (trial judgment), with subsequent appellate review shaping the final outcome.
- Even where particular findings are adjusted on appeal, the case remains influential for articulating standards of responsibility connected to media leadership and editorial control.
