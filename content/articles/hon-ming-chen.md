## Summary

Hon-Ming Chen (陳恆明) is a Taiwanese religious leader who founded the syncretic new religious movement known as Chen Tao (真道), also called the True Way or God's Salvation Church. The group blended elements of Buddhism, Taoism, Christianity, and UFO mythology into a unique cosmological framework centered on the belief that God would intervene in human affairs through flying saucers.

Chen gained international notoriety in 1997–1998 when he relocated approximately 150 followers from Taiwan to Garland, Texas, predicting that God would appear on American television on March 25, 1998, and that flying saucers would arrive shortly thereafter to rescue believers from an impending nuclear apocalypse. When the prophecy failed to materialize, the movement became one of the most widely studied cases of failed prophecy and cognitive dissonance in modern religious studies.

## Early Life & Background

Hon-Ming Chen was born in 1955 in Taiwan. Before founding his religious movement, he worked as a professor of social science at Chien-Kuo Institute of Technology in Changhua County, Taiwan. His academic background gave him an air of intellectual credibility that helped attract early followers. During the 1990s, Chen became increasingly drawn to a mixture of spiritual traditions and developed an elaborate cosmological system that synthesized Eastern and Western religious ideas with beliefs about extraterrestrial life.

## Rise to Prominence

In the early 1990s, Chen founded the Chen Tao movement in Taiwan, gathering a devoted following of middle-class, well-educated Taiwanese adherents. His teachings centered on the idea that the universe was governed by a spiritual hierarchy involving God, Jesus, and Buddha, all of whom communicated through flying saucers. Chen taught that human souls were placed on Earth by extraterrestrials and that salvation would come through divine intervention from the skies.

In 1997, Chen directed his followers to relocate to Garland, Texas — a suburb of Dallas chosen in part because "Garland" sounded like "God's Land." Approximately 150 followers sold their possessions and moved with him, purchasing homes in the same neighborhood and wearing distinctive white clothing and white cowboy hats.

## Key Achievements

Chen Tao attracted significant academic and media attention as a case study in millennial religious movements. Chen published a series of books outlining his theology, including *God's Descending in Clouds (Flying Saucers) on Earth to Save People* and *Practical Evidence and Study of the World of God and Buddha*. His movement's relocation to Texas and his specific, testable prophecy drew researchers studying how religious groups handle prophetic failure — a subject famously explored in Leon Festinger's theory of cognitive dissonance.

## Controversies & Criticism

Chen's most controversial moment came with his prediction that God would appear on Channel 18 of every television in North America at 12:01 AM on March 25, 1998, followed by a physical appearance in Garland on March 31. He further predicted that God would arrive in a flying saucer to collect the faithful before nuclear war destroyed the Earth in 1999. When March 25 passed without incident, Chen held a press conference acknowledging that the prophecy had not been fulfilled and reportedly offered to be crucified or stoned as punishment. Critics accused him of psychological manipulation and exploiting his followers financially, as many had given up their livelihoods to join him in Texas.

## Legacy & Influence

Following the failed prophecy, the Chen Tao movement gradually dissolved. Some followers returned to Taiwan, while others remained in the United States. Chen himself largely retreated from public life. The episode remains a significant reference point in the academic study of new religious movements, prophetic failure, and the social dynamics of apocalyptic groups. Scholars have noted that many followers rationalized the failure rather than abandoning their beliefs entirely, consistent with Festinger's predictions about disconfirmed expectations.
