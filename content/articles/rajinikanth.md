## Summary

Rajinikanth, born Shivaji Rao Gaekwad on December 12, 1950, in Bangalore, is one of the most iconic and commercially successful actors in the history of Indian cinema. Known simply as "Thalaivar" (Leader) by his devoted fanbase, he has built a career spanning over five decades across Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, Malayalam, and Hindi films. His stylized screen presence, distinctive dialogue delivery, and larger-than-life persona have elevated him to a near-mythological status in South Indian popular culture.

His influence extends far beyond box office numbers. Rajinikanth commands a following that transcends regional, linguistic, and generational boundaries, with fan clubs operating across India, Japan, and Sri Lanka. His films are cultural events as much as entertainment, routinely generating record-breaking openings and inspiring mass celebrations among fans. He remains one of the highest-paid actors in Indian cinema history and a figure whose cultural footprint rivals that of global superstars.

## Early Life & Background

Rajinikanth was born into a Marathi family in Bangalore, Karnataka. His mother, Ramabai, died when he was five years old, and he was raised by his father, Ramoji Rao Gaekwad, a police constable. He attended Acharya Pathasala in Bangalore and later the Vivekananda Balaka Sangha, where he developed an early interest in acting through stage performances.

After completing school, he worked as a coolie, carpenter, and bus conductor with the Bangalore Transport Service. It was during this period that his natural flair for performance attracted the attention of film director K. Balachander, who encouraged him to pursue acting professionally. He trained at the Madras Film Institute in 1973, which became the gateway to his film career.

## Rise to Prominence

Rajinikanth made his film debut in K. Balachander's Tamil drama *Apoorva Raagangal* (1975), initially cast in antagonist roles. His breakthrough as a leading man came in the late 1970s and early 1980s with films like *Billa* (1980) and *Murattu Kaalai* (1980), where his unique screen mannerisms — flipping cigarettes, stylized coin tosses, punchy one-liners — began capturing mass audiences. By the mid-1980s, he had overtaken contemporaries to become Tamil cinema's dominant superstar. His 1991 film *Thalapathi*, directed by Mani Ratnam, is considered one of the finest performances of his career.

## Key Achievements

- Starred in over 170 films across multiple Indian languages spanning five decades.
- *Enthiran* (2010), a science-fiction blockbuster, became one of the highest-grossing Indian films of its time with a budget of approximately ₹1.5 billion.
- *2.0* (2018), its sequel, grossed over ₹6.5 billion worldwide, among the highest for any Indian production.
- Received the Padma Bhushan (2000) and Padma Vibhushan (2016), India's third and second highest civilian honours respectively.
- Awarded the Dadasaheb Phalke Award in 2021, Indian cinema's highest honour, for lifetime achievement.

## Controversies & Criticism

Rajinikanth announced his entry into Tamil Nadu politics in December 2017, forming the Rajini Makkal Mandram. However, citing health concerns following a hospitalization in December 2020, he withdrew from electoral politics before the 2021 Tamil Nadu assembly elections — a decision that disappointed many supporters who had anticipated his entry into governance. Critics questioned the sincerity of his political ambitions, and the episode drew significant media scrutiny.

His occasional public statements on social and political issues have also generated debate, with some critics arguing his positions lack consistency or clarity on sensitive matters.

## Legacy & Influence

Rajinikanth's impact on Indian popular culture is difficult to overstate. He redefined the template of the Tamil mass-action hero, influencing a generation of actors including Vijay, Ajith Kumar, and others who followed in his stylistic footsteps. His fan network — one of the most organized in the world — has been studied as a sociological phenomenon. Films starring Rajinikanth regularly spark academic and cultural commentary about stardom, mythology, and spectatorship in India.

Beyond performance, his rags-to-riches biography — from bus conductor to international superstar — has made him an enduring symbol of aspiration across class lines in India.
