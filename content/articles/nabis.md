## Summary

Tyrant of Sparta who used a torture device called the Iron Apega and terrorized the population.

## Background and Rise to Power

- Nabis emerged during a turbulent period after the Battle of Sellasia (222 BCE), when Spartan power had declined and outside powers (Macedon, the Achaean League, and later Rome) competed for influence in the Peloponnese.
- Ancient sources describe him as taking power in Sparta by force and intimidation, presenting himself as a reformer while relying on coercion to secure control.

## Policies and Internal Rule

- Nabis is associated with redistributing property and weakening traditional Spartan elites, partly by targeting wealthy opponents.
- He expanded the citizen body by elevating or enfranchising groups outside the old Spartan social structure, which helped him build a loyal base.
- His regime is remembered in hostile accounts for confiscations, purges, and the use of terror as a tool of governance.

## The “Apega” Torture Device (Later Tradition)

- Some ancient narratives claim Nabis used a mechanical device nicknamed the “Apega” (after his wife), depicted as a statue-like contraption with spikes used to extract money from victims.
- Modern summaries often treat this as part of the propagandistic tradition surrounding Nabis; it remains a famous anecdote, but the details come primarily from adversarial sources.

## Foreign Policy and Wars

- Nabis aligned with larger powers when it suited Spartan interests, at times cooperating with Macedon and later maneuvering amid Roman intervention in Greece.
- He captured Argos (c. 197 BCE) with support from Philip V of Macedon, which became a central issue in regional politics.
- Rome and its Greek allies (notably the Achaean League) campaigned against him; the conflict culminated in a Roman-led war against Sparta (195 BCE).

## Death and Aftermath

- Nabis was assassinated in 192 BCE amid shifting alliances; accounts describe Aetolian involvement in the plot.
- After his death, Sparta’s autonomy continued to erode as the Achaean League and Rome increased their leverage over Greek affairs.

## Legacy

- Ancient authors often cast Nabis as a “tyrant” and emphasize cruelty, but he also represents the late attempt to reshape Spartan society under severe external pressure.
- His rule sits at the intersection of internal social conflict in Sparta and the larger geopolitical transition to Roman dominance in Greece.
