## Summary

Mauritania was the last country to legally abolish slavery in 1981 and the last to criminalize it in 2007. An estimated 1-4% of the population remains in hereditary slavery.

## Additional Context

- Contemporary discussions of slavery in Mauritania often focus on **hereditary slavery** and caste-like social hierarchies that can persist even after legal abolition.
- The term **Haratine/Haratin** is commonly used for a community historically associated with enslavement and its aftermath, though identity and usage can be politically sensitive.

## Legal and Enforcement Issues (High Level)

- Abolition on paper does not automatically end exploitation; enforcement depends on policing, courts, victim protection, and the willingness to prosecute powerful actors.
- Criminalization laws may be undermined by underreporting, social coercion, and barriers to documentation, housing, and employment for survivors.

## Activism and International Attention

- Local anti-slavery activists and NGOs have played a major role in documenting cases and pressing for prosecutions.
- International human-rights reporting has repeatedly highlighted gaps between legal reforms and lived reality.

## Why It Matters

- The issue is frequently cited as a case study in how deeply embedded social systems can outlast formal legal change.
- It also raises broader questions about measurement: estimates vary because hidden exploitation is difficult to quantify consistently.
