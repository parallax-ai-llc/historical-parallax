## Summary

Ernst Werner von Siemens (1816-1892) was a German inventor, electrical engineer, and industrialist who founded the global electrical engineering conglomerate Siemens AG.[^1] He is widely recognized as the founding father of electrical engineering in Germany and made groundbreaking contributions including the invention of the pointer telegraph, the dynamo-electric principle, and the world's first electric tramway.[^2] His name became the SI unit of electrical conductance, the siemens, honoring his transformative impact on the electrical age.[^3] Through his company Siemens & Halske, founded in 1847, he revolutionized telecommunications and electric power generation, establishing one of the world's most enduring technology enterprises.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Ernst Werner Siemens was born on December 13, 1816, in Lenthe (now part of Gehrden), near Hannover, in the Kingdom of Hanover within the German Confederation.[^5] He was the fourth child of fourteen born to Christian Ferdinand Siemens, a tenant farmer and estate manager, and Eleonore Deichmann.[^6] His father worked as a tenant farmer for the Siemens family, an old family of Goslar, but the family struggled financially throughout Werner's childhood.[^7]

Due to the family's precarious financial situation and high debt burden, Werner could not afford university tuition fees.[^8] Instead of pursuing studies at the prestigious Bauakademie Berlin as he had hoped, the seventeen-year-old joined the Prussian army in 1834 to receive a free technical education.[^9] Between 1835 and 1838, he attended the Prussian Military Academy's School of Artillery and Engineering in Berlin, studying mathematics, physics, and chemistry.[^10] Siemens later recalled these three years at the Berlin Artillery and Engineering School as "the happiest of my life."[^11]

Tragedy struck the Siemens family when both parents died within a year of each other—his mother Eleonore in 1839 and his father Christian Ferdinand on January 16, 1840.[^12] As the eldest surviving son, twenty-four-year-old Werner suddenly bore responsibility for his nine surviving siblings, forcing him to take on the financial burden of caring for and educating his younger brothers and sisters.[^13] This early responsibility would shape his character and drive his entrepreneurial ambitions.

Among Werner's thirteen siblings, four brothers would become famous as inventors, scientists, and engineers: Wilhelm Siemens (later known as Sir William Siemens in England), Carl (Heinrich) von Siemens who managed the Russian operations, Hans Siemens, and Friedrich Siemens.[^14] This remarkable family would collaborate to build an international business empire spanning three continents.

## Rise to Prominence

Werner von Siemens's path to prominence began during a brief imprisonment at Magdeburg fortress in the early 1840s, where he had acted as a second in a duel between fellow officers—a serious military offense.[^15] While confined to his cell, he conducted chemistry experiments that led to his first invention in 1842: an electroplating process that improved upon existing galvanization techniques.[^16] This early success demonstrated his ability to transform adversity into opportunity through scientific inquiry.

Siemens's breakthrough came with his work on telegraph technology. While serving in the Prussian military, he was involved in the army's transition from optical to electrical telegraphy.[^17] He invented a telegraph that used a needle to point to specific letters rather than employing Morse code—a significant improvement in user-friendliness known as the pointer telegraph.[^18] In 1847, he received information about gutta-percha, a natural latex from tropical trees, from his brother William and immediately recognized its potential for electrical insulation.[^19]

Siemens invented a revolutionary machine—described as working "like a macaroni machine"—that could seamlessly extrude gutta-percha insulation around telegraph wires while hot, then cool it to create a waterproof covering.[^20] This innovation solved the critical problem of moisture damage to underground and submarine telegraph cables, as previous strip-application methods left seams where water could penetrate.[^21] His gutta-percha insulation technology made the first underground and submarine telegraph cables possible and was later widely applied to electric-light cables.[^22]

Based on these innovations, Werner persuaded a young precision mechanic named Johann Georg Halske to establish a telegraph manufacturing company with him.[^23] On October 1, 1847, they founded "Telegraphen-Bauanstalt von Siemens & Halske" (Telegraph Construction Company of Siemens & Halske) in a small Berlin workshop with just ten employees.[^24] Siemens's vision was ambitious from the start. He declared his aim to create "an enterprise of world standing comparable to that of Fugger," stating "For me, what I've founded is more an empire and something I'd like to leave undiminished to my descendants."[^25]

The young company achieved its first major success in building the army's first underground telegraph line in Germany in 1847.[^26] On March 28, 1849, Siemens & Halske inaugurated the first long-distance telegraph line in Europe—an impressive 500 to 660 kilometers connecting Berlin to Frankfurt am Main.[^27] In April 1850, Werner presented a memoir on his telegraph experiments—"Mémoire sur la Télégraphie Électrique"—before the prestigious French Academy of Sciences, receiving the Academy's acknowledgment and gaining international recognition.[^28]

---

## Major Achievements

Werner von Siemens's most transformative achievement came in 1866 with his discovery of the dynamo-electric principle, which fundamentally changed the generation and application of electrical power.[^29] His self-excited generator, or dynamo, could be set in motion by the residual magnetism of its powerful electromagnet rather than requiring inefficient steel magnets or batteries.[^30] This breakthrough solved one of the great problems in electrical engineering—the previous need for DC batteries to generate electric current and high voltage—and paved the way for large-scale electricity generation through mechanical means.[^31] Siemens proclaimed enthusiastically: "Technology has been given the means at present to generate electric power of unlimited strength inexpensively and easily wherever labor is available."[^32]

The dynamo-electric principle launched what contemporaries called a "new era in electromagnetism."[^33] Heavy-current engineering developed at a breathtaking pace based on Siemens's experiments, enabling innovations like electric streetlights in Berlin and electric railways.[^34] His dynamo laid the foundation for the modern age of electricity and established Siemens as a household name throughout the world.[^35]

Siemens & Halske expanded internationally almost immediately after its founding, with Werner's brothers representing the firm abroad: William managed the English operations (later becoming Sir William Siemens), while Carl oversaw the Russian branch.[^36] The company advanced from a small precision-engineering workshop to one of the world's leading electrical firms within just a few decades.[^37] In Russia, Siemens & Halske secured lucrative government contracts to construct a telegraph network stretching 9,000 kilometers from the Baltic to the Black Sea and to lay the world's first submarine telegraph cable.[^38]

One of the company's most ambitious projects was the Indo-European Telegraph Line, which Carl Siemens accomplished in the remarkable timeframe of two years.[^39] This Herculean undertaking created a continuous telegraph line from London to Calcutta (modern-day Kolkata), revolutionizing communication between Europe and India.[^40] The line extended from Lowestoft to Emden in Prussia, through Berlin, Warsaw, Odessa, across the Caucasus through Tiflis and Erevan, into Persia via Tehran, underwater to Karachi, and finally through India to Calcutta.[^41] On January 11, 1868, the Persian Shah Naser al-Din granted the Siemens brothers a concession to build and operate the telegraph from Tehran to the Russian frontier at Jolfā.[^42]

The personal cost of these international projects was substantial: Walter Siemens died on his way home from Persia in 1868, and Otto Siemens died while supervising construction in 1871, both succumbing to illness in the South Caucasus and buried at Tiflis in Georgia.[^43] Beginning in 1873, the Siemens brothers undertook laying transatlantic telegraph cables connecting Europe with America.[^44] In 1874, their cable ship Faraday laid the first successful transatlantic telegraph cable for the brothers, which remained operational until 1931.[^45] From 1873 to 1894, Siemens Brothers laid almost all new transatlantic cables for clients including French, Anglo-American, and Commercial Cable companies.[^46]

Siemens pioneered electric transportation with revolutionary public demonstrations. At the Berlin Trades Exhibition from May 31 to September 30, 1879, he showcased the world's first practical electric locomotive—a small railway running on an oval track about 300 meters in circumference.[^47] Building on this success, on May 16, 1881, Siemens & Halske inaugurated the Gross-Lichterfelde Tramway in a Berlin suburb—the world's first commercially successful electric tram and first public electric tramway used for permanent service.[^48] The 2.5-kilometer line ran on 100-volt current and carried 26 passengers at 48 kilometers per hour.[^49] In 1880, he built the world's first electric elevator.[^50] In 1882, he developed the "Elektromote," an early trolleybus system.[^51]

Beyond telegraph and transportation innovations, Siemens diversified into resource extraction. In 1864, the Siemens brothers purchased a copper mine at Kedabeg in the Caucasus, with Carl relocating to Tiflis to manage the copper mine and later the brothers' oil operations in the region.[^52]

## Controversies and Criticisms

While Werner von Siemens himself maintained a relatively unblemished personal reputation during his lifetime, certain business practices and the later history of his company have attracted criticism. In the early 1870s, during a period of increased industrial activity and growing worker solidarity in Germany, Siemens faced labor disruptions and demands for higher wages.[^53] Historical research has characterized his response as "welfare capitalism" designed specifically to prevent labor unrest rather than purely altruistic concern for workers.[^54]

Early in his career, Siemens published a pamphlet criticizing faults in Prussian state telegraph lines and suggesting remedies.[^55] The Prussian government considered this unauthorized public comment highly improper, and in consequence discontinued all official connections with his company for several years—a serious setback for the young enterprise.[^56]

The Siemens company became embroiled in its first major international scandal in January 1914, shortly after Werner's death. The "Siemens Scandal" involved collusion between high-ranking members of the Imperial Japanese Navy, the British company Vickers, and Siemens AG.[^57] Siemens had secured a virtual monopoly over Japanese naval contracts in return for a secret 15% kickback to Japanese naval procurement authorities.[^58] When exposed, this bribery scheme led to the fall of the cabinet of Prime Minister Yamamoto Gonnohyoe and severely damaged the company's reputation in Asia.[^59]

The darkest chapter in Siemens company history occurred during the Nazi era, well after Werner's death but under the leadership of his grandson Carl Friedrich von Siemens. Under this leadership, the Siemens Group became one of Germany's largest weapons manufacturers in the mid-1930s.[^60] The company participated in the use of forced labor during Nazi Germany, forcing Jews and other prisoners into slave labor conditions.[^61] This moral failure stands in stark contrast to Werner von Siemens's stated social democratic views and vision against factory "slaves of work" in favor of worker cooperatives.[^62]

In the modern era, Siemens AG was at the center of one of the largest corporate corruption scandals in history. Between the 1990s and 2006, the company systematically paid bribes to government officials and civil servants across the globe—from Bangladesh, Vietnam, Russia, and Mexico to Greece, Norway, Iraq, and Nigeria.[^63] Following investigations by US and German prosecutors, Siemens paid more than $1.6 billion in fines, penalties, and disgorgement of profits in 2008, including $800 million to US authorities—the largest monetary sanction ever imposed under America's Foreign Corrupt Practices Act since its enactment in 1977.[^64] While this occurred more than a century after Werner's death, it raised questions about corporate culture and ethical standards at the company he founded.[^65]

## Personal Life

Werner von Siemens married twice during his lifetime. His first marriage in 1852 was to Mathilde Drumann (1824-1867), daughter of the prominent historian Wilhelm Drumann.[^66] Together they had four children: Arnold, Käthe (Anna), Willy (Georg Wilhelm), and another child.[^67] The marriage ended tragically when Mathilde died on July 1, 1867, leaving Werner to care for their young children alone.[^68] Arnold von Siemens and Georg Wilhelm von Siemens both followed their father into the business.[^69]

During these years of single parenthood, Werner's children often had to do without their beloved father for weeks at a time, as he was intensely focused on establishing Siemens & Halske and frequently traveled on business.[^70] Six-year-old Anna and her siblings experienced particular hardship after losing their mother.[^71]

In 1869, at age fifty-four, Werner fell in love with Antonie Siemens (1840-1900), a distant relative from Hohenheim in southern Germany.[^72] The couple married and created what contemporaries described as a "patchwork family," blending Werner's children from his first marriage with the new relationship.[^73] With Antonie, Werner had two more children: Hertha von Siemens (1870-1939), who later married Carl Dietrich Harries in 1899, and Carl Friedrich von Siemens, who would eventually lead the company.[^74]

By the time of his second marriage, Werner's business was well-established, and he felt free to spend more time with his family than had been possible during the company's early struggling years.[^75] The Siemens family demonstrated a pronounced sense of social responsibility characteristic of all the brothers.[^76] Werner's company leadership eventually passed to his brother Carl, his sons Arnold, Wilhelm, and Carl Friedrich, his grandsons Hermann and Ernst, and his great-grandson Peter von Siemens, establishing a multigenerational family business dynasty.[^77]

Werner von Siemens was described by colleagues as a good soldier who received various military medals during his service.[^78] He contributed to military technology by inventing electrically-charged sea mines used during the First Schleswig War.[^79] Despite his military background, he held progressive social views for his era, advocating for social democracy and worker rights.[^80]

In his autobiography, published in 1893 shortly after his death as "Personal Recollections of Werner Von Siemens," he reflected on the state of science during his youth, observing: "At that time there existed an unabridged gulf between pure and applied science."[^81] Throughout his career, Siemens sought to bridge that gulf, stating: "The process of invention is useful and clearly successful only when it is closely linked to production for the purpose of solving today's problems."[^82]

## Legacy

Werner von Siemens left an extraordinary legacy that fundamentally shaped the modern electrical age and continues to influence technology today. His name was adopted as the SI unit of electrical conductance—the siemens—by the International Electrotechnical Commission in 1935, with official approval by the 14th General Conference on Weights and Measures in 1971.[^83] This honor places him among a select group of scientists whose names became fundamental units of measurement, ensuring his contributions are invoked countless times daily in electrical engineering worldwide.[^84]

Siemens made sufficient contributions to electrical engineering that he became known as the founding father of the discipline in Germany, even coining the German term "Elektrotechnik."[^85] His 1866 discovery of the dynamo-electric principle started a new era of electricity and established Siemens as a household name throughout the world.[^86] The dynamo laid the foundation for the modern age of electricity, enabling the large-scale generation and distribution of electrical power that powers contemporary civilization.[^87]

The company he founded, Siemens AG, grew from a ten-employee workshop in 1847 to become one of the largest and most important electrical engineering companies in the world.[^88] Today, Siemens AG continues to make significant contributions to fields including automation, digitalization, electrification, and sustainable technology.[^89] The company's longevity—operating continuously for over 175 years—stands as a testament to Werner von Siemens's vision of creating "an empire" to leave "undiminished to my descendants."[^90]

His innovations in telecommunications fundamentally transformed global communication. The Indo-European telegraph line, transatlantic cables, and gutta-percha insulation technology created the infrastructure for the first truly global communication network, connecting continents and enabling near-instantaneous transmission of information across vast distances.[^91] This work laid the groundwork for all subsequent telecommunications advances, from telephone networks to modern internet cables.[^92]

In transportation, Siemens pioneered electric mobility more than 140 years ago. His 1879 electric locomotive, 1881 electric tramway in Lichterfelde, 1880 electric elevator, and 1882 trolleybus created templates for electric urban transportation that remain relevant as cities worldwide transition away from fossil fuels.[^93] The Lichterfelde tramway, in particular, demonstrated the commercial viability of electric public transportation and sparked electric tram systems in cities across the globe.[^94]

Beyond technical innovations, Siemens pioneered progressive labor practices that influenced industrial relations for generations. His 1872 pension fund for employees—implemented seventeen years before Chancellor Bismarck's nationwide pension system in 1889—represented groundbreaking social welfare capitalism.[^95] The fund provided pensions for employees, widows, and orphans without requiring worker contributions, alongside profit sharing, annual bonuses, reduced work hours, and annual company celebrations.[^96] Research has shown these policies increased labor productivity and reduced strikes, proving that ethical treatment of workers could align with business success.[^97]

The Werner von Siemens Ring, one of the highest awards for technical sciences in Germany, honors his memory and continues to recognize outstanding contributions to engineering.[^98] His scientific and technical papers, translated from German and published in two volumes by John Murray in London (1892 and 1895), remain valuable historical documents charting the development of electrical science.[^99]

Siemens's autobiography, "Personal Recollections of Werner Von Siemens," published in 1893 and translated by W.C. Coupland, provides insight into the mind of one of the 19th century's most important inventors and entrepreneurs.[^100] The Siemens Historical Institute now offers the autobiography in German and English as a free iPad app, ensuring new generations can learn from his experiences.[^101]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Historians and engineers overwhelmingly recognize Werner von Siemens as one of the most important figures in the history of electrical engineering, whose contributions had a profound and lasting impact on modern civilization.[^102] He is celebrated as "the outstanding founder figure in the German electrical engineering industry" and a pioneer who transformed electrical science from experimental curiosity into practical industrial application.[^103]

Technical historians credit Siemens with solving fundamental problems that enabled the electrical age. His 1866 dynamo-electric principle is viewed as revolutionary, breaking the dependency on batteries for electrical generation and making large-scale electric power economically viable for the first time.[^104] Engineering historian Wilfried Feldenkirchen's authoritative biography "Werner von Siemens: Inventor and International Entrepreneur" portrays him as a visionary who possessed the rare combination of scientific genius, technical skill, and business acumen.[^105]

Scholars praise his practical philosophy of innovation. His insistence that invention must be "closely linked to production for the purpose of solving today's problems" represented a modern approach to applied research that anticipated contemporary innovation management.[^106] His efforts to bridge "the unabridged gulf between pure and applied science" helped establish engineering as a respectable academic discipline rather than mere craftwork.[^107]

Siemens's international vision receives particular commendation from business historians. His strategy of establishing family members in key markets—William in England, Carl in Russia—created one of the first truly multinational corporations and demonstrated sophisticated understanding of globalization decades before the term existed.[^108] The Siemens family's transnational coordination and kinship-based business networks are studied as early models of global enterprise management.[^109]

Social historians acknowledge his progressive labor policies as genuinely pioneering for the industrial era. His 1872 pension fund, profit-sharing arrangements, and advocacy for worker cooperatives over factory "slaves of work" positioned him well ahead of his contemporaries on labor issues.[^110] While some scholars characterize these as "wily welfare capitalism" designed to prevent strikes, most agree the policies delivered real benefits to workers regardless of motivation.[^111]

His scientific contributions earned recognition from prestigious institutions during his lifetime. He received an honorary doctorate from the University of Berlin in 1860, appointment to the Royal Prussian Academy of Sciences in 1873, investiture as a member of the Prussian Order Pour le Mérite for Science and the Arts in 1886, and knighthood the same year.[^112] His ennoblement as "Werner von Siemens" by Emperor Frederick III in 1888 represented the highest recognition the German state could bestow, acknowledging his contributions to national industrial development.[^113]

Contemporary assessments emphasize Siemens's lasting relevance. The adoption of his name as the SI unit for electrical conductance ensures his legacy remains embedded in the language of modern science.[^114] Education scholars use his life to teach innovation, entrepreneurship, and the practical application of scientific principles.[^115]

### Negative Views

Critical historians point to several problematic aspects of Werner von Siemens's legacy, particularly regarding labor relations and the subsequent trajectory of his company. While his 1872 pension fund is often celebrated, revisionist scholarship characterizes it as "welfare capitalism" primarily motivated by strike prevention rather than genuine concern for worker welfare.[^116] Research by economic historians demonstrates that the pension fund's structure—including forfeiture provisions and vesting requirements—was designed to discourage labor mobility and reduce workers' willingness to engage in collective action.[^117]

Critics note that despite Siemens's stated opposition to factory "slaves," working conditions at Siemens & Halske facilities were typical of harsh industrial-era standards, with long hours, dangerous conditions, and authoritarian management.[^118] The labor disruptions in the early 1870s that prompted the pension fund creation suggest significant worker dissatisfaction with wages and conditions.[^119]

Some scholars question whether Siemens deserves singular credit for innovations often attributed to him. The dynamo-electric principle, while perfected and commercialized by Siemens in 1866, built upon earlier work by multiple researchers including Ányos Jedlik, William Sturgeon, and others who had experimented with electromagnetic generators.[^120] Similarly, gutta-percha insulation was discovered by William Montgomerie and brought to the attention of Werner by his brother William—Werner's contribution was the extrusion machine, not the insulating material itself.[^121]

Business ethics critics point to early questionable practices, including the Prussian government's decision to sever relations with the company for several years after Siemens published unauthorized criticisms of state telegraph systems.[^122] While this might be defended as technical honesty, the government viewed it as commercially motivated interference designed to undermine public competitors.[^123]

The most damning criticisms concern the company's conduct in the decades following Werner's death, though he cannot be held personally responsible for decisions made after 1892. Nevertheless, critics argue that the corporate culture he established may have contributed to later ethical failures.[^124] The 1914 Japanese bribery scandal revealed a willingness to engage in corrupt practices to secure contracts—behavior that escalated dramatically in the 2006 global corruption scandal.[^125]

The company's complicity in Nazi crimes, particularly the use of slave labor, represents an irreconcilable stain on the Siemens legacy.[^126] While Werner had been dead for four decades when these atrocities occurred, they were committed under the leadership of his grandson Carl Friedrich von Siemens, raising questions about how family culture and values were transmitted across generations.[^127]

Some technology critics note that Siemens's emphasis on practical application over pure science, while commercially successful, may have discouraged fundamental research within German electrical engineering.[^128] His famous quote about the "unabridged gulf" between pure and applied science suggests he viewed theoretical work as less valuable than immediate problem-solving, a perspective some argue hampered German scientific creativity in later decades.[^129]

Environmental historians observe that Siemens's technologies—while revolutionary—accelerated industrialization and resource consumption with little consideration for ecological consequences, establishing patterns of unsustainable development that persist today.[^130] The copper and oil extraction operations in the Caucasus exemplified extractive colonial-era resource exploitation with minimal concern for local environmental or social impacts.[^131]

## Famous Quotes

> "Technology has been given the means at present to generate electric power of unlimited strength inexpensively and easily wherever labor is available." - On the implications of his dynamo-electric principle[^132]

> "This whole thing has great potential for development and can pave the way for a new era in electromagnetism! … Magnetic electricity will be cheaper as a result and now light, galvanic metallurgy, etc., and even small electromagnetic machines that get their power from large ones will be possible and useful!" - Letter to his brother William about electromagnetic developments[^133]

> "The process of invention is useful and clearly successful only when it is closely linked to production for the purpose of solving today's problems." - On the philosophy of practical innovation[^134]

> "At that time there existed an unabridged gulf between pure and applied science." - Reflecting on the state of scientific research in his youth[^135]

> "The deeper the insight we obtain into the mysterious workings of nature's forces... the more we are convinced that we are still standing in the vestibule of science; that an unexplored world still lies before us; and however much we may discover, we know not whether mankind will ever arrive at a full knowledge of nature." - On the endless frontier of scientific discovery[^136]

> "For me, what I've founded is more an empire and something I'd like to leave undiminished to my descendants." - On his vision for Siemens & Halske[^137]
