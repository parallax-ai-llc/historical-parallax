## Summary

Mungo Park was the first European to explore the Niger River in West Africa. He wrote a bestselling account of his first journey. He died during his second expedition.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|

| 1799 | Published 'Travels in the Interior Districts of Africa' |
| 1805 | Began second expedition; died on the Niger |

## Background

- **Training**: Park was trained in medicine and became connected to British scientific and exploratory circles.
- **African Association**: His expeditions were linked to European efforts to map West African geography and trade routes, especially the course of the Niger River.

## First Expedition (1795–1797)

- **Goal**: Determine the Niger’s direction and relationship to other river systems.
- **Hardship**: Narratives of the journey emphasize illness, captivity, and reliance on local assistance.
- **Geographic contribution**: His account is often summarized as the first widely circulated European confirmation of key stretches of the Niger.

## Publication and Influence

- **Travel narrative**: *Travels in the Interior Districts of Africa* became influential in Britain, shaping public perceptions of West Africa and inspiring later expeditions.
- **Limits of the era**: Modern historians often note that such travel writing mixed observation with the assumptions of imperial-era science and commerce.

## Second Expedition and Death (1805–1806)

- **Large party**: Park returned with a bigger expedition, which suffered severe losses.
- **End on the Niger**: He died during the attempt to navigate downstream, with accounts commonly placing his death near Bussa rapids.

## Legacy

Mungo Park’s legacy sits at the intersection of:

- exploration history and geographic knowledge production,
- the early British imperial gaze toward West Africa,
- and the human cost of expeditionary projects.
