## Summary

President of Sierra Leone who transformed the country into a one-party state and plundered diamond revenues. Left the country bankrupt and set the stage for civil war.

## Rise to power

- Stevens was a leading figure in the All People's Congress (APC) and became prime minister/president amid political instability in the late 1960s.
- His consolidation of power involved security services, patronage networks, and the marginalization of political opponents.

## One-party rule and governance

- Sierra Leone was declared a one-party state under the APC, a shift often associated with weakened checks and balances.
- Critics and later analyses describe an expansion of corruption and clientelism under Stevens' rule.
- Institutions, including the civil service and judiciary, were widely regarded as politicized during this era.

## Diamonds, corruption, and economic decline

- Control of diamond production and export revenues was central to political power.
- Smuggling and the diversion of state resources contributed to fiscal crisis and deteriorating public services.
- The long-term weakening of the state is commonly cited as a factor that increased vulnerability to later conflict.

## Transition and legacy

- Stevens retired and arranged a succession to Joseph Saidu Momoh.
- Historical assessments frequently place Stevens among the key architects of Sierra Leone's late-20th-century governance collapse.
