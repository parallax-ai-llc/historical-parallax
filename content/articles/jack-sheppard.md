## Summary

English thief who became famous for his daring prison escapes. He escaped from Newgate Prison four times, becoming the most famous criminal in 18th century London. His exploits inspired books and plays. Hanged at Tyburn.

## Background

Sheppard was born in London and trained as a carpenter before turning to theft. His notoriety grew less from the value of his crimes than from the way his repeated escapes embarrassed authorities and fascinated the public.

## Escapes and Publicity

In early 18th-century London, public executions and prison narratives were part of popular culture. Sheppard’s escapes from custody—especially from Newgate—were circulated in pamphlets and retellings, helping turn him into a celebrity criminal.

His story intersected with the period’s printing trade and street culture: accounts of his escapes were sold to readers as moral lessons, sensational entertainment, or both.

## Cultural Legacy

After his death, Sheppard became a recurring figure in English popular history. Plays, ballads, and later novels reused his escapes as material, reflecting a long-running tension between condemnation of crime and fascination with defiance of authority.
