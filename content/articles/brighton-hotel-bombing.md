## Summary

IRA bombing of the Grand Brighton Hotel on October 12, 1984 targeting British PM Margaret Thatcher and her cabinet during the Conservative Party conference. 5 were killed but Thatcher survived.

## Background: The Troubles

The attack occurred during “the Troubles,” a period of political conflict and violence linked to the constitutional status of Northern Ireland.
The Provisional Irish Republican Army (IRA) carried out bombings and other attacks in the United Kingdom and Northern Ireland as part of its campaign.
Political conferences and senior government figures were viewed as high-value symbolic targets.

## The Attack (High Level)

The bombing took place at a hotel hosting the Conservative Party conference, where members of the UK government were staying.
The stated intent was to target the prime minister and Cabinet leadership.
The explosion caused fatalities and injuries and led to extensive structural damage.

## Investigation and Prosecution

Large-scale counterterrorism investigations typically combine forensic evidence, intelligence work, and cross-jurisdiction coordination.
In this case, Patrick Magee was convicted for his role in the bombing.
Sentencing and later release arrangements became part of wider debates about terrorism-related imprisonment and political settlements.

## Political and Security Aftermath

The bombing intensified scrutiny of protective security for public events and for officials traveling outside London.
It also influenced public perceptions of the conflict, reinforcing the sense that violence could reach the heart of British political life.
Despite the attack, the conference proceeded, and Thatcher publicly emphasized continuity of government.
