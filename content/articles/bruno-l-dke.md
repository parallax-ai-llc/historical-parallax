## Summary

German man accused of murdering 85 women during the Nazi era, making him supposedly one of history's most prolific serial killers. Modern historians believe the Nazis fabricated his confession and he may have been innocent.

## Historical Context

Bruno Lüdke’s story is frequently discussed less as a straightforward criminal biography and more as an example of how policing, propaganda, and coercion can shape public narratives—especially in authoritarian settings.

## Disputed Claims and Reassessment

Accounts that attribute extremely large numbers of murders to Lüdke have been questioned by later researchers. At a high level, modern discussion often emphasizes:

- **Reliability of confessions:** Confessions obtained under coercive conditions are not considered reliable historical evidence on their own.
- **Documentation gaps:** Many sensational claims depend on secondary accounts rather than verifiable, contemporaneous records.
- **Political incentives:** Nazi-era institutions had incentives to produce “clear” culprits and dramatic criminal stories that could be used for control and messaging.

## Why the Case Matters

The Lüdke case is often cited in broader conversations about:

- the ethics and methods of criminal investigation
- the dangers of scapegoating and misinformation
- historical revision and the importance of primary-source validation
