## Summary

Hong Kong gangster known as 'Big Spender' who kidnapped billionaires' sons Victor Li and Walter Kwok for enormous ransoms. Executed in Guangzhou.

## Additional Context

## Criminal Profile

Zhang Ziqiang, often nicknamed “Big Spender” in English-language accounts, is remembered as a high-profile Hong Kong–linked gangster associated with large-ransom kidnappings in the 1990s. The public narrative around his crimes emphasizes:

- **Target selection:** extremely wealthy business families, where ransom demands could reach extraordinary levels.
- **Operational boldness:** kidnappings that relied on intimidation and negotiation rather than secrecy alone.
- **Cross-border dimensions:** planning and enforcement that touched both Hong Kong and mainland China.

## High-Profile Kidnappings and Ransoms

The best-known cases involve kidnappings connected to prominent tycoons’ families, which became widely discussed in Hong Kong as examples of the vulnerability of elites even in a modern, highly policed city. Retellings often highlight the unusual fact that victims or families sometimes cooperated quickly to secure release, reflecting the perceived threat level.

## Legal Outcome

Zhang Ziqiang was ultimately tried and executed in mainland China. The case is frequently cited in the context of:

- **Jurisdiction and enforcement:** how severe crimes with cross-border elements were handled across legal systems.
- **Deterrence and public messaging:** the role of capital punishment in signaling state capacity.

## Broader Significance

Beyond the specific crimes, Zhang Ziqiang’s story is often used to illustrate:

- The criminal opportunities created by rapid wealth accumulation and visible elites.
- The role of organized crime networks that can leverage firearms, logistics, and intimidation.
- The public anxiety produced by high-profile kidnappings, even when they are relatively rare.
