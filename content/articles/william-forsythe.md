## Summary

William Forsythe is an American choreographer who revolutionized classical ballet through his radical deconstruction of traditional technique. As director of the Frankfurt Ballet and his own company, he pushed ballet into new territory with works like In the Middle Somewhat Elevated.

## Style and Contributions

Forsythe became associated with a "deconstruction" of classical ballet: using the vocabulary of ballet (turned-out legs, line, pointe work, partnered lifts) while extending it through off-axis balances, extreme articulation, and improvisational structures. Many of his works foreground geometry, speed, and the visibility of decision-making on stage.

He also influenced contemporary dance pedagogy through projects that treated choreographic ideas as systems that can be studied and practiced, not only performed.

## Selected Works and Projects

- *In the Middle, Somewhat Elevated* (1987) — created for the Paris Opera Ballet; often cited as a landmark of late-20th-century ballet.
- Works for the Frankfurt Ballet period that helped define his reputation internationally.
- The Forsythe Company (founded 2004) — a vehicle for new stage works and interdisciplinary projects.

## Legacy

Forsythe is widely credited with expanding what "ballet" can mean in the contemporary era, influencing choreographers and companies across Europe and North America. His output is frequently discussed in terms of its impact on technique, composition, and the relationship between classical form and experimental movement.

## Further Notes

Forsythe’s work is frequently studied in both ballet and contemporary-dance contexts because it treats technique as something that can be reorganized without abandoning its classical roots.
