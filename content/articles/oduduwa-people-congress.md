## Additional Context

### Origins and Ideology

The Oduduwa People’s Congress (OPC) is commonly described as a Yoruba socio-cultural and political organization that emerged in Nigeria during the 1990s. The name references Oduduwa, a foundational figure in Yoruba origin traditions, and the movement has been associated with Yoruba nationalism and claims of defending Yoruba interests.

### Security, Vigilantism, and Public Order

In several Nigerian cities, groups labeled as vigilantes have sometimes filled security gaps where residents perceive formal policing as inadequate. The same dynamic can produce conflict: organizations that claim community protection may also be accused of coercion, factional violence, or involvement in criminal activity, particularly when they control territory, collect “fees,” or police informal markets.

### Political Context

OPC activity is often discussed against the backdrop of Nigeria’s transitions between military and civilian rule in the late 20th century, and the broader political tensions among regional and ethnic constituencies. In public debate, such groups can be framed either as defenders of community rights or as destabilizing militias, depending on the speaker and the incident.

### Splits, Factions, and Attribution

Large movements can develop internal factions, with differing leadership claims and local structures. When violence is reported, attribution to “the organization” may mask variation across regions, splinter groups, or individual members acting without central coordination.
