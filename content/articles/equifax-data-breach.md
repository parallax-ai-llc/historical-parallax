## Summary

Data breach at credit reporting agency Equifax in 2017 that exposed personal information of 147 million Americans. Chinese military hackers were later charged.

## Additional Context

- **Attack vector:** Public reporting has tied the intrusion to an unpatched vulnerability in the **Apache Struts** web framework (often cited as CVE-2017-5638), illustrating how delayed patching can have systemic consequences.
- **Data exposed:** Accounts typically describe exposure of highly sensitive identifiers, including **names, Social Security numbers, dates of birth, addresses**, and in some cases **driver’s license numbers** and **payment card data**.
- **Timeline:** The breach occurred in 2017 and was disclosed publicly later that year; the delay between intrusion and disclosure became part of the public and regulatory scrutiny.
- **Regulatory response:** U.S. regulators and state attorneys general pursued action that resulted in a major settlement package, including consumer remediation and security program requirements.
- **Attribution and charges:** In 2020, the U.S. Department of Justice announced charges against **four members of China’s People’s Liberation Army** in connection with the incident (charges do not imply a criminal conviction without adjudication).

## Why It Matters

The Equifax breach is commonly cited as a landmark case in:

- **Consumer identity risk** (long-lived identifiers like SSNs are difficult to replace).
- **Corporate governance and disclosure** expectations around cybersecurity.
- The practical importance of **asset inventory, vulnerability management, and incident response** in large enterprises.
