## Summary

Cynisca of Sparta was the first woman to win at the ancient Olympics, winning the four-horse chariot race in 396 and 392 BC. As a Spartan princess, she challenged gender norms.

## Background

Cynisca (also spelled Kyniska) was a member of the Spartan royal family, commonly identified as a daughter of King Archidamus II and sister of Agesilaus II. In the ancient Olympic Games, women were generally barred from competing and, in some cases, from attending, making her victories especially notable.

## How the Chariot Victory Worked

In ancient chariot events, the official victor was the **owner** of the horses and chariot rather than the charioteer. This rule created a pathway for elite women with resources to be credited with victory even when they did not drive the chariot themselves.

## Olympic Victories

Ancient sources record Cynisca as winning the four-horse chariot race (tethrippon) at Olympia in 396 BCE and again in 392 BCE. Her repeated victories suggest a sustained, organized investment in training and stable management.

## Commemoration

Cynisca is associated with a commemorative monument at Olympia, including an inscription celebrating her achievement. The monument is often cited as evidence of how exceptional her recognition was within the norms of the period.

## Historical Significance

Cynisca’s wins are frequently discussed in studies of gender, sport, and status in classical Greece. They illustrate how legal and social rules could both restrict participation and, in limited cases, be navigated by elites to claim public honor.

## Key Points

- First recorded female Olympic victor (as owner) in ancient tradition
- Example of Spartan elite women’s comparatively distinctive social position
