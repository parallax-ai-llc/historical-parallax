## Summary

Sei Shōnagon (清少納言, c. 966–1017 or 1025) was a Japanese court lady, writer, and poet who served Empress Teishi during the middle Heian period. She is celebrated as the author of *The Pillow Book* (*Makura no Sōshi*), a masterpiece of Japanese literature that inaugurated the zuihitsu genre of miscellaneous writing. Her work offers an unparalleled glimpse into the refined aesthetic sensibilities and intricate social dynamics of the imperial court at Heian-kyō (Kyoto). Despite being criticized by her contemporary Murasaki Shikibu for her perceived conceit and ostentation, Sei Shōnagon's literary legacy has endured for over a millennium, influencing countless generations of Japanese writers and readers.[^1][^2]

---

## Early Life and Background

Sei Shōnagon was born around 966 CE into the Kiyohara family, a lineage descended from Emperor Temmu (r. 673–686) that had distinguished itself primarily through literary rather than political achievements by her lifetime.[^3] Her father was Kiyohara no Motosuke, a provincial governor of Higo Province and an accomplished waka poet whose verses appeared in several imperial anthologies, most notably the *Gosen Wakashū* (951).[^4] Her great-grandfather, Fukuyabu, was also a celebrated poet, establishing a family tradition of literary excellence that would profoundly shape Sei Shōnagon's education and worldview.[^5]

The Kiyohara family belonged to the middle-ranking courtier class and faced financial difficulties, likely because they were not granted revenue-producing offices despite their literary prestige.[^6] Evidence within *The Pillow Book* suggests that Sei Shōnagon received an extensive education in both Japanese and Chinese literature, which was relatively uncommon for women of her era, though not unheard of among aristocratic families.[^7] This education would later enable her to display considerable knowledge and wit at court, though it would also attract criticism from rivals who viewed such displays as inappropriate for women.

Her actual given name remains unknown to history; "Sei Shōnagon" is itself a court title, with "Sei" deriving from an alternate pronunciation of her family name "Kiyohara," and "Shōnagon" meaning "lesser councilor of state," though her precise relationship to this governmental post remains unclear.[^8] Some scholars have suggested her given name may have been Nagiko, but this remains speculative.[^9]

Little is definitively known about her childhood and early years before entering court service. Around the age of sixteen, approximately in 982, she may have married Tachibana no Norimitsu, a government official, and possibly bore a son named Norinaga, though these biographical details remain uncertain and contested among scholars.[^10] By the time she entered the service of Empress Teishi in 993 at the age of twenty-seven, she may have been divorced or widowed.[^11]

The literary environment of the Kiyohara household provided the foundation for Sei Shōnagon's later achievements. Growing up surrounded by poetry, classical texts, and scholarly discourse, she developed the aesthetic sensibility and literary skill that would distinguish her writing. Her father's position as a compiler of imperial poetry anthologies would have exposed her to the highest standards of literary composition and the competitive nature of court literary culture.[^12]

---

## Rise to Prominence

Sei Shōnagon's rise to prominence began in 993 when she entered service as a lady-in-waiting to Empress Teishi (also known as Sadako), the consort of Emperor Ichijō, at the imperial court in Heian-kyō (present-day Kyoto).[^13] This appointment marked the beginning of approximately seven years of court service that would form the basis of her literary masterwork and secure her place in Japanese literary history.

Empress Teishi's court was renowned as a center of cultural refinement and intellectual brilliance during the 990s. The empress herself was highly educated and cultivated an environment that attracted talented women who could contribute to the court's reputation for wit, poetry, and aesthetic sophistication.[^14] Sei Shōnagon's ready intelligence and sharp wit quickly secured her a prominent position among Teishi's ladies-in-waiting, despite her relatively modest family background compared to some of her peers.[^15]

The political context of Sei Shōnagon's court service was complex and ultimately tragic. Empress Teishi was the daughter of Fujiwara no Michitaka, who served as regent until his death in 995. Following Michitaka's death, political power shifted to his younger brother Fujiwara no Michinaga, who maneuvered to install his own daughter, Shōshi (also called Akiko), as a second empress to Emperor Ichijō in 999.[^16] This political rivalry between the two branches of the Fujiwara family created competing court factions, with Teishi's court representing a losing political cause even as it flourished culturally.

During her years at court, Sei Shōnagon distinguished herself through her literary talents, particularly her skill in composing impromptu poetry and her quick-witted responses in verbal exchanges, which were highly valued accomplishments in Heian court culture.[^17] The competitive nature of court life, where women's reputations depended significantly on their ability to demonstrate cultural sophistication, created an environment that both encouraged and rewarded Sei Shōnagon's talents.

Her prominence at court was not without controversy, even during her active service. Her confident display of learning, particularly her knowledge of Chinese literature and characters, which was considered a masculine domain, attracted both admiration and criticism.[^18] Unlike many court ladies who concealed their Chinese learning to appear more conventionally feminine, Sei Shōnagon openly displayed her education, an audacity that would later be condemned by her contemporary Murasaki Shikibu.[^19]

The years of Sei Shōnagon's service to Empress Teishi, from 993 to approximately 1000, coincided with a period of increasing hardship for the empress. Teishi suffered the death of her father, the birth of several children under difficult circumstances, and the humiliation of seeing her husband take a rival empress from the politically ascendant branch of the Fujiwara family.[^20] Empress Teishi died in childbirth in 1000 at the age of twenty-four, effectively ending Sei Shōnagon's court career and the period documented in *The Pillow Book*.[^21]

## Major Achievements

Sei Shōnagon's paramount achievement is *The Pillow Book* (*Makura no Sōshi*), written between approximately 994 and 1001 during and shortly after her service to Empress Teishi.[^22] This work stands as one of the masterpieces of Japanese literature and represents an innovative literary form that would influence Japanese writing for centuries. The work consists of more than three hundred entries covering her years at court, written in a miscellaneous genre known as zuihitsu, meaning "following one's brush," which allowed for spontaneous and free-flowing composition.[^23]

*The Pillow Book* inaugurated and perfected the zuihitsu genre, characterized by its mixture of lists, anecdotes, diary entries, character sketches, poetry, and reflections on aesthetic and emotional experiences.[^24] Translator Meredith McKinney describes it as "a special case," "a genre-bending miscellany of short, largely unrelated pieces" that defies easy categorization.[^25] This formal innovation represented a significant departure from more structured literary forms and established a practice that remains alive in Japanese literature to the present day.

The content of *The Pillow Book* reveals Sei Shōnagon's extraordinary powers of observation and her refined aesthetic sensibility. The work opens with one of the most celebrated passages in Japanese literature, describing the beauty of different times in each season: "In spring it is the dawn that is most beautiful. As the light creeps over the hills, their outlines are dyed a faint red and wisps of purplish cloud trail over them. In summer the nights. Not only when the moon shines, but on dark nights too, as the fireflies flit to and fro, and even when it rains, how beautiful it is!"[^26] This passage exemplifies the concrete, vivid style that captures beauty with an economy of language that anticipates the later development of haiku.[^27]

Throughout *The Pillow Book*, Sei Shōnagon demonstrates remarkable versatility in her writing. Her famous lists—"Things That Make One's Heart Beat Faster," "Hateful Things," "Elegant Things," "Things That Lose by Being Painted"—reveal a systematic attempt to categorize and understand aesthetic and emotional experience.[^28] These lists are interspersed with longer anecdotes describing court life, conversations with the empress, encounters with various courtiers, and reflections on literature, nature, and human behavior.

The work's historical value cannot be overstated. As the only surviving example of its kind from the Heian period, *The Pillow Book* provides invaluable insight into the daily life, social customs, aesthetic values, and material culture of the imperial court around the year 1000.[^29] Scholars of Japanese history rely on Sei Shōnagon's detailed observations to understand everything from architectural features and seasonal festivals to the complex protocols governing interactions between different ranks of courtiers.

Sei Shōnagon's literary style in *The Pillow Book* displays characteristics that scholars have identified as particularly influential. Her prose achieves a clarity and elegance that has been held up as a model of classical Japanese writing.[^30] She employs concrete, specific details rather than abstract generalizations, allowing readers to visualize scenes and experiences with remarkable immediacy. Her wit manifests in both gentle humor and sharp satire, never more apparent than in her devastating character sketches of individuals who failed to meet her exacting standards of taste and behavior.

The work's influence on Japanese literary tradition was both immediate and enduring. During the Heian period itself, *The Pillow Book* was popular, widely imitated, and frequently quoted in other works, though it was later eclipsed in prominence by Murasaki Shikibu's *The Tale of Genji*.[^31] The zuihitsu genre that Sei Shōnagon pioneered became a established form in Japanese literature, with later writers continuously returning to her work as a foundational model.[^32]

Beyond its formal innovations, *The Pillow Book* contributed to establishing the literary authority of women writers in Japanese culture. Sei Shōnagon and her contemporaries created a literary heritage that makes Japan unique in world history—no other culture produced literature of such quality and quantity by women at such an early period.[^33] Her work demonstrated that women's observations and perspectives deserved serious literary treatment and could achieve aesthetic heights equal to any male-authored texts.

## Controversies and Criticisms

The most famous controversy surrounding Sei Shōnagon centers on her rivalry with Murasaki Shikibu, author of *The Tale of Genji* and lady-in-waiting to Empress Shōshi, the political rival of Sei Shōnagon's mistress, Empress Teishi.[^34] Though the two women probably never met in person, as Murasaki entered court service after Sei Shōnagon had retired, Murasaki's scathing assessment of Sei Shōnagon in her diary has echoed through the centuries and shaped perceptions of both writers.[^35]

In *The Diary of Lady Murasaki*, Murasaki wrote: "Sei Shōnagon ... was dreadfully conceited. She thought herself so clever, littered her writing with Chinese characters, [which] left a great deal to be desired."[^36] This criticism touches on a central controversy regarding Sei Shōnagon's behavior at court—her open display of learning, particularly in Chinese literature and language, which were considered masculine domains. Unlike Murasaki, who concealed her Chinese learning to appear more conventionally feminine and modest, Sei Shōnagon was "ostentatious and flirtatious, as well as outspoken about her knowledge of Chinese."[^37]

The rivalry appears to have been largely one-sided. While Murasaki denounced Sei Shōnagon's personality and achievement in her diary, Sei Shōnagon never wrote about Murasaki Shikibu in *The Pillow Book*, possibly because Murasaki entered court service after Sei Shōnagon's active period had ended.[^38] Nevertheless, scholars have suggested that Murasaki may have been brought to Empress Shōshi's court specifically as a literary rival to counter Sei Shōnagon's reputation and the popularity of *The Pillow Book*.[^39]

The nature of the criticism reveals fundamental differences in temperament and values between the two writers. Murasaki was withdrawn, sensitive, and concerned with appearing humble and proper according to gender norms, while Sei Shōnagon was witty, clever, and unabashed in her confidence.[^40] The rivalry has endured in Japanese literary culture, with readers and scholars continuing to divide themselves into "camps" favoring one writer or the other.[^41]

Beyond this famous rivalry, Sei Shōnagon faced criticism for the elitism and occasional cruelty evident in *The Pillow Book* itself. Though capable of great tenderness, she showed little sympathy for those whose ignorance or poverty rendered them ridiculous in her eyes, and she could be merciless in displaying her wit at others' expense.[^42] Her writing reveals a person acutely concerned with maintaining strict social hierarchies and aesthetic standards, with harsh judgment for those who failed to meet her expectations.

*The Pillow Book* contains numerous passages in which Sei Shōnagon mocks individuals for social gaffes, poor taste, or inadequate education. Her lists of "hateful things" and "unsuitable things" display an exacting and sometimes unforgiving sensibility. Critics have noted that while this quality makes her writing vivid and entertaining, it also reveals a character that could be snobbish and unkind, particularly toward those of lower social status.[^43]

The question of how *The Pillow Book* came to be circulated has also been a source of controversy and scholarly debate. According to one tradition recorded in the work itself, Sei Shōnagon's pillow book (a private notebook kept by one's bedside) was unexpectedly seen by court officials, leading to its circulation against her wishes.[^44] However, scholars debate whether this account is accurate or represents a conventional fiction designed to excuse the presumption of a woman publishing her private writings.

The moral legend that grew up around Sei Shōnagon's later life likely reflects criticism of her character and behavior. According to tradition, she spent her old age in misery and loneliness, dying impoverished and alone, which some interpretations present as divine punishment for her pride and vanity.[^45] While scholars generally dismiss this legend as an invention, its persistence suggests discomfort with Sei Shōnagon's confident and unapologetic self-presentation, which violated conventional expectations for women's modest behavior.

## Personal Life

The personal life of Sei Shōnagon remains largely mysterious, as no direct biographical or historical records about her have survived beyond what can be inferred from *The Pillow Book* and scattered references in other contemporary sources.[^46] Information about her life before and after her court service is fragmentary and often conjectural, with scholars disagreeing about fundamental facts regarding her marriages, relationships, and children.

According to some sources, Sei Shōnagon may have married Tachibana no Norimitsu, a government official, around 983 at the age of sixteen, and this union may have produced a son named Norinaga.[^47] However, the relationship between Sei Shōnagon and Tachibana no Norimitsu is disputed. Although he is mentioned in *The Pillow Book*, translator Ivan Morris considers it unlikely that Shōnagon was married to him, suggesting instead that the pair may have had an amorous relationship rather than a formal marriage.[^48]

If Sei Shōnagon was indeed married to Tachibana no Norimitsu, that marriage had apparently ended by the time she entered court service to Empress Teishi in 993, as she may have been divorced or widowed.[^49] Some scholars believe that after leaving court service following Empress Teishi's death in 1000, Sei Shōnagon married Fujiwara no Muneyo, who became governor of Settsu Province, and that she had a daughter, Koma no Myōbu, with him.[^50] However, this information is equally uncertain, and other sources suggest she may have died unmarried and without children.[^51]

*The Pillow Book* itself contains evidence of intimate relationships beyond any formal marriages. Sei Shōnagon alludes to amorous encounters, some pleasant and others regrettable, though always with the discretion and circumspection expected of Heian court literature, which typically obscured precise identities and circumstances of romantic relationships.[^52] The courtly culture in which she lived accepted and even expected aristocratic women to have romantic relationships outside of marriage, though such relationships were governed by complex protocols and aesthetic expectations.

The personality that emerges from *The Pillow Book* reveals a complex individual. Sei Shōnagon demonstrates keen aesthetic sensibility, sharp intelligence, confidence in her own judgment, and a capacity for both warmth and cutting satire. She reveals herself as someone who took deep pleasure in beauty—whether natural scenes, elegant objects, or well-composed poetry—and who suffered genuine distress when confronted with ugliness or impropriety.[^53] She values wit, education, and social grace, and she judges others harshly when they fail to display these qualities.

Her relationship with Empress Teishi appears to have been one of genuine affection and loyalty. Throughout *The Pillow Book*, Sei Shōnagon consistently portrays the empress with admiration and tenderness, defending her against implicit criticism and celebrating her beauty, intelligence, and kindness.[^54] The devotion Sei Shōnagon displayed toward Teishi during the empress's political difficulties and personal tragedies suggests a depth of feeling that transcended mere professional duty.

After Empress Teishi's death in 1000, Sei Shōnagon disappears from historical record. The circumstances of her remaining years and her death are unknown. Legend suggests she died impoverished and alone, possibly after taking religious vows and entering a convent, but scholars generally regard these traditions as moralizing inventions designed to provide a cautionary tale about the dangers of pride and vanity rather than historical fact.[^55] The true circumstances of her later life and death remain one of the enduring mysteries surrounding this remarkable figure.

## Legacy

Sei Shōnagon's legacy rests primarily on the enduring influence of *The Pillow Book* and its role in establishing the zuihitsu genre as a distinctive form in Japanese literature. More than a millennium after its composition, her work remains widely read, celebrated for its linguistic purity, aesthetic sophistication, and intimate portrait of Heian court culture.[^56] The simplicity and charm of her style continues to be held up as an example of the finest classical Japanese prose.[^57]

The zuihitsu genre that Sei Shōnagon pioneered—characterized by miscellaneous, loosely connected observations and reflections that follow the spontaneous movement of the writer's brush—became a established tradition in Japanese literature.[^58] Later writers continuously returned to her work as a foundational model, and the genre remains vital in contemporary Japanese writing. Her innovation in creating a literary form that valued spontaneity, personal observation, and aesthetic response over narrative structure or argumentative development represented a significant contribution to literary history.

*The Pillow Book* serves as an invaluable historical document, providing scholars with detailed information about the material culture, social customs, aesthetic values, and daily rhythms of Heian court life.[^59] As the only surviving work of its kind from this period, it offers insights into tenth-century Japanese aristocratic society that cannot be obtained from any other source. Historians rely on Sei Shōnagon's observations to understand everything from architectural details and clothing to seasonal observances and the protocols governing social interactions.

The literary influence of Sei Shōnagon's style extends beyond the zuihitsu genre. Her concrete, vivid approach to describing sensory experience and her economy of language anticipated elements of later Japanese poetic forms, particularly haiku, with its emphasis on capturing immediate perception through carefully chosen details.[^60] Western writers and scholars have also recognized her influence, finding in her work qualities that resonate with modernist sensibilities regarding the literary value of fragmentary, subjective observation.

Sei Shōnagon's legacy is inseparable from her role in establishing the authority and significance of women's writing in Japanese literature. Together with her contemporaries, particularly Murasaki Shikibu, she contributed to a remarkable flowering of women's literature in the Heian period that was unparalleled in world history at that time.[^61] In no other culture did women produce literature of such quality and quantity at such an early period, and this achievement fundamentally shaped the subsequent development of Japanese literary culture.

The influence of Sei Shōnagon can be traced in the work of countless later Japanese writers, particularly women writers who found in her example a model of confident literary voice and aesthetic authority.[^62] Her demonstration that a woman's observations, judgments, and responses deserved serious literary treatment helped establish a tradition of women's writing that continues to be vital in Japanese literature.

Contemporary readers and scholars continue to find *The Pillow Book* relevant and engaging. Modern translations into English and other languages have introduced Sei Shōnagon to global audiences, who respond to the immediacy of her voice and the universality of many of her observations about beauty, human nature, and the pleasures and frustrations of social life.[^63] Her work transcends its specific historical and cultural context to speak to fundamental human experiences and preoccupations.

The rivalry between Sei Shōnagon and Murasaki Shikibu remains a subject of fascination, with readers and scholars continuing to divide themselves into competing camps over which writer they prefer.[^64] This enduring rivalry itself testifies to the lasting power of both writers' work and the investment that readers continue to feel in the literary culture of Heian Japan. Contemporary adaptations, including plays imagining encounters between the two writers, demonstrate the ongoing cultural vitality of Sei Shōnagon's legacy.[^65]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and critics have consistently praised Sei Shōnagon for her literary innovations and the enduring quality of her prose style. *The Pillow Book* is celebrated for its "linguistic purity" and continues to be studied as a model of classical Japanese writing at its finest.[^66] Literary historians emphasize that her work represents not merely a historical curiosity but a living literary achievement that continues to reward careful reading and aesthetic appreciation.

The formal innovation represented by *The Pillow Book* receives particular scholarly attention. By creating the zuihitsu genre, Sei Shōnagon established a literary form that valued spontaneity, personal observation, and aesthetic response, offering an alternative to more structured genres dominated by narrative or argumentative development.[^67] This innovation demonstrated the literary possibilities inherent in fragmentary, miscellaneous writing organized around subjective perception rather than external formal constraints.

Feminist scholars and critics have increasingly appreciated Sei Shōnagon's confident assertion of her own voice, judgment, and authority in a patriarchal society that typically demanded female modesty and self-effacement.[^68] Her willingness to display her learning, assert her opinions, and judge others—including men of higher social rank—represents a form of proto-feminist resistance to gender expectations. Her open acknowledgment of her education in Chinese literature, despite criticism from contemporaries like Murasaki Shikibu, demonstrated courage in refusing to conform to limiting gender norms.

Historians value *The Pillow Book* as an unparalleled source of information about Heian court culture. Sei Shōnagon's detailed observations of daily life, material culture, social customs, and aesthetic values provide insights that cannot be obtained from official historical records or other literary sources.[^69] Her work allows modern readers to enter imaginatively into the world of the Heian court with a degree of intimacy and immediacy that few historical sources achieve.

Literary scholars emphasize the sophistication of Sei Shōnagon's aesthetic sensibility and her ability to articulate complex aesthetic and emotional responses with precision and elegance.[^70] Her famous opening passage on the seasons exemplifies her capacity to capture ephemeral beauty through concrete, specific observation while avoiding both sentimentality and excessive abstraction. This aesthetic sophistication represents a genuine philosophical achievement in articulating principles of beauty and emotional response.

Contemporary scholars note that Sei Shōnagon's influence extends beyond Japanese literature to inform comparative literary studies and discussions of women's writing across cultures.[^71] Her work provides an important counterexample to narratives of literary history that assume women's writing emerged primarily in European contexts, demonstrating instead the global and transhistorical presence of women's literary achievement.

### Negative Views

Critics of Sei Shōnagon, both contemporary and modern, have focused on her perceived character flaws, particularly her pride, elitism, and occasional cruelty. Murasaki Shikibu's famous condemnation—that Sei Shōnagon was "dreadfully conceited"—articulates a criticism that many readers have felt upon encountering *The Pillow Book*'s more judgmental and dismissive passages.[^72] The work reveals a person who could be harsh in her judgments, particularly toward those of lower social status or those who failed to meet her exacting aesthetic standards.

Some scholars and readers find Sei Shōnagon's writing marred by snobbery and a lack of human sympathy. Though capable of tenderness, she showed little compassion for those whose ignorance or poverty rendered them ridiculous in her eyes, and she displayed her wit at others' expense with apparent relish.[^73] This quality makes her writing entertaining but also reveals character traits that many find unattractive—a preoccupation with social hierarchy, an unforgiving attitude toward human failings, and a capacity for mockery that sometimes crosses into cruelty.

Critics have noted that Sei Shōnagon's work lacks the psychological depth and narrative sophistication of Murasaki Shikibu's *The Tale of Genji*. While *The Pillow Book* offers brilliant observations and aesthetic insights, it does not attempt the kind of sustained character development, complex plotting, or philosophical reflection that distinguishes the greatest works of fiction.[^74] As a result, some literary historians have ranked Sei Shōnagon's achievement below that of her rival, seeing *The Pillow Book* as a minor masterpiece of observation and style rather than a major work of world literature.

The moral tradition that emerged around Sei Shōnagon's later life—suggesting she died in poverty and loneliness as punishment for her pride—reflects a persistent cultural discomfort with her confident self-presentation and unwillingness to conform to gender expectations of modesty and self-effacement.[^75] While scholars generally dismiss these legends as inventions, their persistence reveals that Sei Shōnagon's assertive personality and intellectual pride provoked criticism and moral censure in ways that male writers' confidence typically did not.

Some scholars have questioned whether Sei Shōnagon's display of Chinese learning represented genuine scholarly depth or superficial showing-off. Murasaki Shikibu's criticism that Sei Shōnagon "littered her writing with Chinese characters" that "left a great deal to be desired" suggests that her classical education may have been less profound than she wished to appear.[^76] Whether this criticism is fair or reflects rivalry and gender politics remains debated, but it raises questions about the nature and extent of Sei Shōnagon's learning.

Contemporary critics sometimes note that the very qualities that make *The Pillow Book* historically valuable—its detailed attention to court customs, aesthetic judgments, and social hierarchies—can also make it feel remote and alienating to modern readers unfamiliar with Heian culture. The work's focus on aristocratic preoccupations and its assumption of shared cultural knowledge can create barriers to appreciation for readers outside the specific context of Heian court society.[^77]

## Famous Quotes

> "In spring it is the dawn that is most beautiful. As the light creeps over the hills, their outlines are dyed a faint red and wisps of purplish cloud trail over them. In summer the nights. Not only when the moon shines, but on dark nights too, as the fireflies flit to and fro, and even when it rains, how beautiful it is! In autumn, the evenings, when the glittering sun sinks close to the edge of the hills and the crows fly back to their nests in threes and fours and twos; more charming still is a file of wild geese, like specks in the distant sky. When the sun has set, one's heart is moved by the sound of the wind and the hum of the insects. In winter the early mornings."
— Opening passage of *The Pillow Book*, describing the beauty of the seasons[^78]

> "If letters did not exist, what dark depressions would come over one!"
— On the importance of written correspondence in maintaining social connections[^79]

> "In life there are two things which are dependable. The pleasures of the flesh and the pleasures of literature."
— Expressing her fundamental values and sources of satisfaction[^80]

> "After all, the cherry blossom blooms every year, but does anyone find it the less lovely for that?"
— Reflecting on the value of recurring beauty and the appreciation of familiar pleasures[^81]

> "I shall say absolutely nothing about the spindle tree."
— A characteristically witty moment in which Sei Shōnagon ironically draws attention to something by declaring she will not discuss it[^82]
