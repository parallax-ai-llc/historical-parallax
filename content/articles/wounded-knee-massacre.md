## Summary

US Army soldiers massacred approximately 300 Lakota men, women, and children at Wounded Knee Creek. Twenty soldiers received the Medal of Honor for the massacre.

## Background

The massacre occurred during the broader period of U.S. military campaigns and federal policies aimed at controlling and displacing Indigenous peoples in the late 19th century. Tensions were heightened by the spread of the Ghost Dance movement, which U.S. authorities often interpreted as a threat.

A group of Lakota under Chief Spotted Elk (also known as Big Foot) was intercepted by the U.S. 7th Cavalry near Wounded Knee Creek in present-day South Dakota.

## What Happened

On December 29, 1890, soldiers attempted to disarm the Lakota camp. A shot was fired amid confusion, and the situation rapidly escalated. U.S. troops opened fire, killing a large number of Lakota people, including many women and children.

The event is commonly regarded as the last major armed conflict of the Indian Wars and as a symbol of the violent suppression of Indigenous communities in the United States.

## Aftermath and Controversy

- **Medal of Honor awards:** The U.S. Army awarded Medals of Honor to multiple soldiers; these awards remain controversial and have been the subject of calls for rescission.
- **Historical interpretation:** Many historians and Indigenous advocates characterize the killings as a massacre rather than a battle.
- **Memory and commemoration:** Wounded Knee remains a significant site in Lakota history and in U.S. discussions of colonial violence.

## Later Significance

The name "Wounded Knee" resurfaced in 1973 during the Wounded Knee Occupation by activists of the American Indian Movement (AIM), connecting the site to modern Indigenous rights struggles.
