## Summary

Mass murder of 347-504 unarmed Vietnamese civilians by US Army soldiers on March 16, 1968 during the Vietnam War. The atrocity was covered up for a year before being exposed.

## Background and Context

The killings took place during the Vietnam War in Quảng Ngãi Province, an area where U.S. forces were conducting counterinsurgency operations and where units faced casualties from mines and ambushes. The village area was often described in U.S. military reporting as being influenced by the National Liberation Front (Viet Cong), which shaped how the operation was planned and perceived.

## What Happened (High-Level)

On 16 March 1968, soldiers of Charlie Company, 1st Battalion, 20th Infantry Regiment, 11th Brigade, Americal Division entered Sơn Mỹ village, including the hamlets known as My Lai. Large numbers of unarmed civilians—men, women, children, and elderly people—were killed. Survivors and subsequent investigations described shootings at close range and killings carried out in groups.

## Immediate Aftermath and Cover-up

Initial reports up the chain of command framed the operation as a successful engagement against enemy combatants. For roughly a year, the scale and nature of the civilian deaths were not publicly acknowledged. The gap between internal reporting and later evidence became a central part of the scandal.

## Exposure and Investigations

The massacre became widely known after investigative work and whistleblowing brought it to public attention in 1969. U.S. Army investigations, including the Peers Commission, documented failures in reporting and accountability. The episode also elevated questions about rules of engagement, command responsibility, and oversight.

## Legal Proceedings

Several soldiers were investigated, but only Lieutenant William Calley Jr. was convicted (1971) for the murders. His sentence was later reduced, and he ultimately served a limited period under house arrest. The narrow scope of convictions relative to the scale of the event remains a major point of controversy.

## Legacy

My Lai became one of the most infamous incidents of civilian harm in the Vietnam War and is frequently cited in discussions of war crimes, military ethics, and the treatment of civilians in armed conflict. It influenced public opinion about the war in the United States and continues to be studied in military and academic settings.
