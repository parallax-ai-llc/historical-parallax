## Summary

Serbian man who assassinated Prime Minister Zoran Đinđić on March 12, 2003, as part of a conspiracy involving organized crime and former security forces. Đinđić was the first democratic PM of Serbia.

## Additional Context

### Historical and Political Context

The assassination of Serbian Prime Minister Zoran Đinđić in March 2003 was a major political shock in Serbia's post-Milošević period. Đinđić was widely associated with a reformist and pro-democratic agenda, and his death intensified debates about the state's ability to confront organized crime and elements linked to former security structures.

### Conspiracy and Criminal Proceedings (High-Level)

Public sources describe the assassination as involving multiple participants and coordination rather than a lone actor. The subsequent investigation and trial became a high-profile case, with courts addressing the roles of individuals connected to organized criminal groups and former special units.

### Sentencing and Legal Outcome

Lukić is described in reference accounts as having been convicted and sentenced to a long prison term. The case is frequently cited in discussions of how post-conflict and transitional societies attempt to reassert rule-of-law institutions in the face of entrenched criminal networks.

### Wider Aftermath

The Serbian state's response to the assassination included large-scale security operations and renewed focus on criminal justice capacity. In historical summaries of the period, the event is often treated as a turning point affecting domestic politics, security policy, and public confidence.
