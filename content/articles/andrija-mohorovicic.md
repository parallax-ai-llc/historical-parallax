## The Mohorovičić Discontinuity ("Moho")

In 1909, Mohorovičić analyzed seismic waves from an earthquake near Zagreb and observed that some waves arrived earlier than expected. He interpreted this as evidence of a boundary inside the Earth where seismic wave velocities change abruptly.

That boundary—between the **Earth's crust and mantle**—is now called the **Mohorovičić discontinuity**, or **Moho**. It became a central concept in seismology and geophysics, providing one of the earliest strong observational constraints on Earth's layered structure.

## Scientific Impact

Mohorovičić's work helped establish:

- the use of **seismic travel-time curves** to infer internal structure,
- the idea that Earth's interior can be mapped using indirect measurements,
- a foundation for later studies of crustal thickness and tectonics.

## Legacy

The Moho remains a standard reference surface in Earth science. Modern methods (including reflection seismology and receiver functions) continue to refine Moho depth estimates globally, building on the interpretive approach he pioneered.

## Broader Work

Mohorovičić worked at observatories and contributed to both meteorology and seismology during a period when these disciplines were becoming more quantitative. His career illustrates how careful measurement, combined with mathematical interpretation, can reveal structures that cannot be observed directly.
