## Summary

Egyptian-American who murdered his two teenage daughters Amina and Sarah in an 'honor killing' in Dallas, Texas on January 1, 2008. He fled and was a fugitive for 12 years before capture. Sentenced to life without parole.

## Background

Yaser Abdel Said is an Egyptian-born man who lived in the United States and later became the focus of a high-profile double-homicide investigation in Texas. Reporting on the case commonly frames it within the broader issue of so-called "honor"-motivated violence, a term used for crimes justified by perpetrators as responses to perceived family or community shame.

## The 2008 Killings

On January 1, 2008, Amina and Sarah Said were killed in Dallas, Texas. The case drew national attention both because the victims were teenagers and because of the allegations around motive and prior family dynamics. Law enforcement identified Yaser Abdel Said as the primary suspect, and he became a fugitive shortly afterward.

## Fugitive Period and Capture

For approximately 12 years, Said evaded arrest. Investigators periodically renewed public appeals for information, and the case remained open as an active fugitive search. In 2020, authorities announced his capture, ending a long-running manhunt.

## Trial and Sentencing

Following his arrest, the case proceeded through the Texas criminal justice system. In 2022, he was convicted and received a sentence of life imprisonment without the possibility of parole. The outcome is frequently cited in discussions about accountability in domestic-violence-related homicide cases and the challenges of pursuing fugitives over long time spans.

## Public Impact

The case has been referenced in advocacy and policy conversations about protecting at-risk youth, recognizing warning signs of family violence, and ensuring access to safety resources. It is also used as an example of how cultural narratives can be misused to rationalize violence, while legal systems treat the acts as criminal homicide.

## Related Topics

Discussions of this case often intersect with broader work on domestic violence prevention, victim services, and community-based intervention strategies. It is also used in media-analysis contexts to examine how terms like "honor killing" are defined and applied in public discourse.
