## Context

Richardson is best known for work in low-temperature physics, a field that studies how matter behaves as it approaches absolute zero. One of the landmark phenomena in this area is **superfluidity**: a phase in which a liquid can flow with extremely low (effectively vanishing) viscosity under certain conditions.

Helium provides multiple routes into superfluid behavior depending on the isotope and the temperature regime. The discovery highlighted in this article concerns **helium-3**, an isotope whose quantum behavior becomes prominent at ultra-low temperatures.

## Why the helium-3 superfluid discovery mattered

- It expanded the catalog of known quantum phases of matter beyond the earlier superfluid state observed in helium-4.
- It provided a real-world system where ideas about quantum pairing and collective behavior can be tested experimentally.
- It helped establish low-temperature techniques and measurements as a durable toolset for condensed-matter physics.
