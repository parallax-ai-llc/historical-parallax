## Summary

Hua Mulan is a legendary Chinese warrior who disguised herself as a man to serve in the army in place of her aged father. Her story, told in a 6th-century ballad, has inspired countless adaptations.

## Sources and Historicity

- Mulan is best understood as a figure from Chinese legend and literature rather than a fully documented historical person.
- The best-known early source is the **“Ballad of Mulan”** (木蘭辭), a narrative poem traditionally dated to the Northern Wei era or later transmission.
- Because the story evolved over centuries, details (family name, setting, length of service) can differ between versions.

## Core Narrative Elements

Common versions share a set of motifs:

- filial duty (taking a parent’s place in conscription),
- concealment of gender within a male-dominated military setting,
- exceptional competence and loyalty in service,
- refusal of official rewards in favor of returning home.

## Cultural Legacy

- The Mulan narrative has been adapted into operas, plays, novels, film, animation, and television.
- Adaptations often reflect the values and politics of their times, emphasizing themes such as loyalty, family obligation, and personal agency.
- Mulan has become a widely recognized symbol in discussions of gender roles, heroism, and national identity.

## Common Points of Variation Across Retellings

- The identity and status of the ruler served.
- The duration of military service (often expressed as many years).
- The circumstances in which Mulan’s identity is revealed.
- Whether the ending emphasizes reunion, tragedy, or moral exemplars.

## Related Topics

- **Northern Wei:** a dynasty sometimes associated with the ballad’s setting.
- **Chinese ballad tradition:** oral and textual transmission that can produce multiple “authoritative” versions over time.
