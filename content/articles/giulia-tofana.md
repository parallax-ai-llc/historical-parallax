## Summary

Italian poisoner who sold a slow-acting poison called Aqua Tofana to women wanting to kill their husbands in 17th century Rome and Naples. She helped murder an estimated 600 men over 20 years.

## Context

Giulia Tofana is a figure associated with early-modern Italy and the long history of poison as a method of covert killing. Accounts about her are shaped by a mix of legal records, later retellings, and folklore, which can make precise details difficult to verify.

## Aqua Tofana

Aqua Tofana is generally described as a colorless, slow-acting poison marketed to women seeking an undetectable way to kill abusive or unwanted husbands.

Common motifs in the story include:

- the poison being dispensed in small doses over time
- efforts to disguise the substance as a cosmetic or devotional product
- secrecy networks involving sellers, intermediaries, and clients

## Investigation and execution

Narratives typically describe an eventual crackdown by authorities, confessions extracted under torture, and executions of those implicated. The story is often used to illustrate both the brutality of the period’s criminal justice system and the social constraints facing women.

## Legacy

- **True-crime and folklore**: Tofana appears in later crime histories, novels, and popular retellings.
- **Gender and power**: the episode is frequently discussed in relation to domestic power imbalances in early-modern Europe.
- **Poison panic**: it sits alongside other “poisoner” cases that fueled public fear and moral panic.
