## Summary

American white supremacist and former KKK leader who killed three people at Jewish community centers in Overland Park, Kansas in 2014. Ironically, none of his victims were Jewish.

## Ideology and Activities

- Miller was active for decades in white supremacist organizing, including leadership roles in Ku Klux Klan-affiliated groups and later activity tied to broader neo-Nazi networks.
- His 2014 attack targeted Jewish sites, reflecting an ideological fixation rather than any verified connection between victims and the communities he claimed to oppose.
- The case is frequently cited as an example of how long-running extremist involvement can culminate in lone-actor violence.

## Legal Outcome

He was convicted of capital murder and related charges and received a death sentence. His death in custody ended the possibility of further appeals or a carried-out execution.

## Key Takeaways

- The attack is often discussed under the framework of domestic terrorism motivated by extremist ideology.
- Target selection in hate crimes can be symbolic, with perpetrators attacking places associated with an identity group regardless of victims’ individual backgrounds.
- Long-term extremist involvement can provide both motivation and reinforcement leading up to acts of violence.
