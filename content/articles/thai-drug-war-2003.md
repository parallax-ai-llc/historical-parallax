## Summary

PM Thaksin Shinawatra's 3-month drug war killed approximately 2,800 people. Human rights groups said many were extrajudicially executed; a government investigation confirmed this.

## Additional Context

### Background

- Thailand faced significant concern about methamphetamine (often referred to as *yaa baa*) trafficking and use in the early 2000s.
- The government framed the 2003 campaign as an urgent public-safety crackdown aimed at disrupting drug networks.

### Key Controversies

- Human rights organizations and some journalists alleged that a substantial portion of the deaths were **extrajudicial killings**, including cases involving mistaken identity or personal vendettas.
- Official rhetoric emphasizing "results" and rapid suppression drew criticism that it encouraged excessive force by police and local officials.

### Investigations and Accountability

- Thai authorities later acknowledged that some people killed were not connected to drugs, and that some names on blacklists were erroneous.
- The extent of direct state responsibility has remained politically contested, with debates over whether deaths were caused by security forces, rival dealers, or a mix of both.

### Aftermath and Legacy

- The 2003 drug war is frequently cited in discussions of rule of law, policing standards, and rights protections in Thailand.
- It has also been referenced in later Thai political debates as a model of "tough" governance versus a cautionary example of state overreach.
