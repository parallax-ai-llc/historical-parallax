## Summary

Japanese camera maker Olympus concealed $1.7 billion in investment losses through fraudulent acquisitions. Whistleblower CEO Michael Woodford was fired for exposing the scheme.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- The scandal centered on a long-running effort to conceal investment losses on Olympus’s books.
- The concealment strategy is often described in reporting as a form of “loss-hiding” that relied on complex transactions and the use of intermediaries.
- When questions arose about unusually large advisory fees and acquisition prices, internal scrutiny intensified.

## Whistleblowing and Leadership Crisis

- Michael Woodford, appointed as CEO, raised concerns about the acquisition-related payments and accounting.
- The board removed Woodford shortly after, which amplified media attention and triggered deeper external investigation.

## Investigations, Findings, and Accountability

- Olympus publicly acknowledged a multi-year cover-up.
- Regulators, auditors, and law enforcement investigated the company’s disclosures and accounting practices.
- Several former executives were prosecuted and convicted in connection with falsifying financial information.

## Impact on Olympus and Corporate Governance

- The scandal damaged investor trust and highlighted governance weaknesses, including board oversight and internal controls.
- Olympus faced pressure to strengthen compliance, improve transparency, and reform governance structures.
- The episode became a widely cited case study in corporate accounting and board accountability in Japan.
