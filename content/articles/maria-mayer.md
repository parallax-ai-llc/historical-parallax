## Additional Context

### Naming note

“Maria Mayer” is commonly used as a shortened reference to **Maria Goeppert-Mayer**, a German–American theoretical physicist and Nobel laureate. Many biographical sources use her full married name (Goeppert-Mayer) to distinguish her from other individuals named Maria Mayer.

### Scientific contributions (high level)

She is best known for the **nuclear shell model**, a framework that helped explain why certain numbers of protons or neutrons (“magic numbers”) produce unusually stable nuclei. For this work she shared the **1963 Nobel Prize in Physics** (jointly with J. Hans D. Jensen; the other half of the prize went to Eugene Wigner).

Her earlier work also included foundational theoretical contributions such as the description of **two-photon absorption**, which later became experimentally accessible with the development of lasers.

### Career context

Goeppert-Mayer’s career is often discussed in the context of the constraints faced by women scientists in mid-20th-century academia, including rules and norms that limited formal appointments. Despite this, she produced influential research at multiple major institutions and contributed to wartime scientific programs.

### Legacy

She is frequently cited as a landmark figure in nuclear physics and as a symbol of belated recognition of women’s scientific contributions. Awards and lectureships named in her honor are part of that continuing legacy.

In many histories of physics, her work is presented as a key bridge between empirical nuclear data and a coherent theoretical structure.
