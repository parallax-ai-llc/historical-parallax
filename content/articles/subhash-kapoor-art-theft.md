## Summary

New York art dealer convicted of trafficking $143 million in looted antiquities from India, Nepal, and other countries. His gallery sold stolen objects to major museums worldwide.

## Background and Modus Operandi

Kapoor operated as a dealer in South Asian antiquities, using his gallery business to source, export, and sell objects that investigators and claimant countries alleged were looted from temples and archaeological sites. Such trafficking typically relies on falsified provenance documents, intermediaries in source countries, and cross-border shipment routes that make it difficult for buyers to verify origin.

## Legal and Restitution Context

Cases involving antiquities trafficking often lead to parallel proceedings: criminal charges against dealers or smugglers, and civil or diplomatic efforts to repatriate objects. When museums or collectors discover that an item was stolen or illicitly exported, the resolution can include returns to the country of origin, settlements, and tightened acquisition policies.

## Why the Case Matters

The Kapoor case is frequently cited in discussions about the global market for cultural property and the responsibilities of institutions that acquire antiquities. It also contributed to greater scrutiny of provenance research, particularly for objects lacking documented ownership history prior to modern cultural-heritage laws.

## Notes on Due Diligence

Museums and collectors are generally expected to conduct due diligence before acquisition, including checking databases of stolen art, reviewing export documentation, and evaluating gaps in ownership history. High-profile trafficking prosecutions have pushed many institutions to adopt stricter standards, publish provenance details, and respond more quickly to credible claims.

## Common Red Flags (High Level)

- Missing or vague pre-1970 provenance (a widely used benchmark tied to UNESCO cultural property efforts)
- Sudden appearance of a major object on the market without excavation or collection history
- Reliance on photocopied paperwork or unverifiable “private collection” claims
- Export permits that cannot be independently confirmed with source-country authorities
