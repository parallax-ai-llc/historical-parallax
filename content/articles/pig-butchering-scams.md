## Summary

Massive fraud scheme originating from Southeast Asian scam compounds where trafficked workers are forced to conduct romance and cryptocurrency scams. Losses exceed $75 billion globally.

## Additional Context

### Term and Basic Mechanism

"Pig butchering" is a translation of a Chinese phrase used to describe long-con grooming frauds in which targets are gradually persuaded to transfer increasing amounts of money, often through purported investment platforms. The "fattening" metaphor refers to building trust and escalating stakes before the final theft.

### Common Tactics

Many descriptions emphasize a repeatable workflow:

- **Initial contact** via dating apps, social platforms, or wrong-number messages.
- **Trust building** over weeks or months, sometimes involving scripted personal narratives.
- **Investment framing**, frequently tied to cryptocurrency, foreign exchange, or "insider" tips.
- **Platform manipulation**, where fake dashboards show gains and encourage larger deposits.
- **Withdrawal blocks** using invented taxes, fees, or identity checks to extract more funds.

### Scam Compounds and Forced Labor

A widely reported dimension is the industrial organization of these scams in compound-like facilities in parts of Southeast Asia. Investigations describe trafficking, debt bondage, and coercion of workers who are made to run scripts and maintain multiple online identities.

### Financial Infrastructure

The fraud ecosystem typically relies on fast-moving payment rails and cross-border money movement, including cryptocurrency wallets, laundering intermediaries, and rapid account turnover. This complicates recovery efforts and makes attribution difficult.

### Law Enforcement and International Attention

The scale of losses and the trafficking component have prompted investigations by national police, financial regulators, and international organizations. Public reporting highlights periodic raids, rescues, and arrests, alongside the persistence of networks that relocate across jurisdictions.

## Notes on Sources

Aggregate loss estimates vary by methodology and reporting period. The most reliable figures usually come from a combination of consumer complaint data, financial intelligence reporting, and investigative journalism, and should be treated as approximations.
