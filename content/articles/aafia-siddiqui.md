## Summary

Pakistani neuroscientist convicted of attempting to murder US soldiers and FBI agents in Afghanistan. Known as Lady al-Qaeda, her imprisonment has been a rallying cause for jihadist groups worldwide.

## Education and Professional Background

Aafia Siddiqui has been described in public sources as having an academic background in neuroscience and related fields, with periods of study in the United States. Her biography is often discussed in part because it contrasts a highly educated profile with later allegations connected to militancy and violence.

## Disappearance and Arrest

Accounts of Siddiqui’s 2003 disappearance and her subsequent reemergence years later have been contested and remain a major part of public controversy. Authorities and commentators have offered differing narratives about where she was during this period and what activities she may have been involved in.

In 2008, she was detained in Afghanistan. The U.S. prosecution case centered on an incident in which Siddiqui allegedly grabbed a firearm and fired at U.S. personnel during an interrogation setting; no one was killed in the incident described in court records.

## Trial, Conviction, and Sentence

Siddiqui was tried in the United States and convicted on charges including attempted murder of U.S. nationals and related offenses. She received a lengthy prison sentence. The case has been cited in debates about the handling of terrorism-related prosecutions, evidentiary standards, and the treatment of detainees.

## Public and Political Significance

Siddiqui’s imprisonment has become a prominent political issue in Pakistan and among some advocacy groups internationally. Supporters argue that she was wrongfully convicted or mistreated, while others point to the court verdict and to security concerns. Her name has also appeared in extremist propaganda and in hostage incidents in which perpetrators demanded her release.

## Notes

Because of the politicized nature of the case, descriptions of Siddiqui in media and activist materials can be highly polarized; careful sourcing is important when summarizing claims beyond the court outcome.
