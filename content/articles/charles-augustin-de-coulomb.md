## Summary

Charles-Augustin de Coulomb formulated Coulomb law describing the electrostatic force between charges. The SI unit of electric charge (coulomb) is named after him.

## Key Contributions (High-Level)

- **Coulomb's law**: Established an inverse-square relationship for the force between electric charges, a foundational result for classical electromagnetism.
- **Torsion balance experiments**: Used sensitive torsion-based instruments to quantify very small forces in electrostatics and magnetism.
- **Engineering and mechanics**: Contributed to applied problems such as friction, material strength, and the behavior of structures—work that connected theory to practical design.

## Why He Matters

Coulomb's work helped move electricity and magnetism from qualitative observations toward **measurable, mathematical laws**. Later developments in field theory and electrical engineering build on this shift from description to quantification.

## Legacy

- The SI unit **coulomb (C)** honors his impact on the science of electric charge.
- His experimental approach—careful instrumentation plus clear mathematical modeling—became a template for later physics research.

## Related Concepts

- **Electric field** and **potential**: later formalisms that reorganize Coulomb's force law into field-based language.
- **Inverse-square laws**: a broader mathematical pattern also seen in gravitation and other central-force problems.
- **Electrostatics in materials**: real-world effects (polarization, screening) that modify ideal point-charge behavior.
