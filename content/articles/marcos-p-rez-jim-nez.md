## Summary

Venezuelan military dictator whose secret police SEGURIDAD NACIONAL tortured and killed hundreds. Fled with the national treasury and lived in luxury in Miami and Madrid.

## Additional Context

### Path to power

Pérez Jiménez was a Venezuelan military officer who rose through the ranks during an era of coups and unstable civilian–military relations. He was a key figure in the 1948 coup that removed an elected government and helped establish military rule.

### Regime characteristics

His government combined rapid state-led modernization with heavy repression. The regime’s security apparatus (often summarized as the *Dirección de Seguridad Nacional*) is widely associated with surveillance, imprisonment of opponents, and the suppression of dissent.

### Development and “modernization” narrative

The period is frequently remembered for large public-works projects—roads, housing, and monumental state buildings—enabled in part by oil revenues. At the same time, critics emphasize corruption, the concentration of power, and the use of development projects to bolster legitimacy.

### Fall from power and exile

After growing opposition and mass demonstrations, he was overthrown in January 1958 by a military move backed by broad civilian mobilization. The post-1958 period in Venezuela is often framed as a transition toward party-based democracy, with the dictatorship serving as a negative reference point.

### Historical significance

Pérez Jiménez remains a polarizing figure: some highlight infrastructure and economic expansion, while others foreground state violence, political imprisonment, and corruption as defining features of his rule.

The contrast between “order and development” narratives and human-rights critiques is central to how the era is remembered.
