## Summary

Chinese criminal organization formed by former Red Guards who fled to Hong Kong in the 1970s. They became one of the most violent Asian crime groups involved in robbery, drug trafficking, and counterfeiting.

## Origins and Composition

- The group is commonly described as being formed by individuals associated with mainland political upheaval who later moved into Hong Kong’s criminal underworld.
- Reporting often frames them less as a single unified mafia and more as shifting crews linked by shared background, language, and contacts.

## Criminal Activities (High-Level)

- Alleged activities include armed robbery, extortion, narcotics trafficking, smuggling, and the production/distribution of counterfeit goods.
- In public accounts, violence and intimidation are frequently cited as tactics used to control territory and enforce agreements.

## Geographic Reach

- The name is used in some sources as an umbrella label for Chinese-speaking criminal networks operating in Hong Kong, Southeast Asia, and overseas Chinese communities.
- Law-enforcement and media reporting has connected related crews to cases in North America and Europe, though attribution varies by jurisdiction and time period.

## Law Enforcement Focus

- Crackdowns on organized crime in Hong Kong and cross-border cooperation have periodically disrupted operations.
- Many modern discussions emphasize financial tracking, border controls, and coordinated prosecutions as key tools against transnational networks.

## Legacy

- “Big Circle Boys” remains a recurring reference point in analyses of late-20th-century Chinese diaspora organized crime and its evolution alongside migration and trade.
