## Summary

Rodney Dangerfield was an American stand-up comedian and actor whose self-deprecating persona — built entirely around the catchphrase "I don't get no respect!" — made him one of the most recognizable comic voices of the 20th century. After a long and frustrating early career filled with false starts and near-abandonment of show business, he reinvented himself in his forties and rose to become a fixture on late-night television and in Hollywood films.

His influence on comedy was substantial and lasting. Dangerfield's rapid-fire one-liner style, rooted in Jewish vaudeville traditions, bridged the gap between old-school nightclub comics and the modern stand-up era. He also became a significant talent scout and champion of emerging comedians, nurturing the careers of performers who would redefine American comedy in the 1980s and beyond.

## Early Life & Background

Born Jacob Rodney Cohen on November 22, 1921, in Babylon, New York, Dangerfield grew up in a troubled household. His father, entertainer Phil Roy, was largely absent, and his mother was emotionally cold and distant — experiences he would later mine extensively for material. The family moved frequently and lived in poverty. He began writing jokes as a teenager and performed locally under the name Jack Roy while working odd jobs, including as a singing waiter, to support himself.

## Rise to Prominence

Dangerfield first attempted a comedy career in his twenties, performing as Jack Roy through the late 1940s. Discouraged by limited success, he quit performing around 1950 and spent over a decade selling aluminum siding in New Jersey to support his family. He returned to stand-up in the early 1960s, this time adopting the stage name Rodney Dangerfield and refining the "no respect" persona that would define him.

His breakthrough came with appearances on *The Ed Sullivan Show* in 1967 and repeated visits to *The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson*, where his nervous, self-deprecating delivery connected with mass audiences. In 1969 he opened Dangerfield's, a comedy club in New York City that became a launching pad for major talent through the 1970s and 1980s.

## Key Achievements

Dangerfield released several successful comedy albums, including *No Respect* (1980), which won the Grammy Award for Best Comedy Recording. His film career produced two defining roles: the brash nouveau-riche golfer Al Czervik in *Caddyshack* (1980) and the crude but lovable Thornton Melon in *Back to School* (1986), the latter of which he also co-wrote and produced. He appeared on HBO comedy specials throughout the 1980s that drew record viewership. His club, Dangerfield's, ran for over three decades and gave early exposure to comedians including Jerry Seinfeld, Jim Carrey, and Andrew Dice Clay.

## Controversies & Criticism

Dangerfield's humor relied heavily on stereotypes — nagging wives, ugly children, failing bodies — that drew occasional criticism for being retrograde. His memoir *It's Not Easy Bein' Me* (2004) also detailed long struggles with depression, anxiety, and drug use, including marijuana and prescription medications, which he discussed openly and without apology.

## Legacy & Influence

Dangerfield died on October 5, 2004, following complications from heart valve replacement surgery. He was 82. His "no respect" persona entered American vernacular as a shorthand for chronic underappreciation. Comedians from Jerry Seinfeld to Louis C.K. have cited his one-liner economy and comic timing as foundational influences. His willingness to return to stand-up after a decade-long absence and reinvent himself in middle age remains a touchstone story in American comedy.
