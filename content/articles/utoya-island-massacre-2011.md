## Summary
On July 22, 2011, Anders Breivik shot 69 people dead at a Workers Youth League summer camp on Utøya island after bombing government offices in Oslo (killing 8). It was the deadliest mass shooting by a lone gunman in history at the time.

## Background
The events leading to Utoya Island Massacre 2011 were shaped by complex social, political, and criminal factors in Utoya Island, Norway. [^1]

## Additional Notes
- The attacks combined a bombing in Oslo with a mass shooting at a political youth camp, demonstrating a deliberate multi-stage approach intended to maximize fear and attention.
- Breivik’s ideology and writings were widely discussed in the context of far-right extremism and online radicalization.
- Norway’s national response emphasized mourning and democratic resilience, while also confronting the need for practical improvements in emergency response.
- Official reviews examined preparedness, coordination, and the speed of police response, shaping later reforms and training.
- Survivors and families have played a prominent role in public memory, testimony, and ongoing debates about extremism and prevention.
- Memorialization efforts—through commemorations, education, and site design—aim to honor victims while avoiding glorification of the attacker.

## Legacy
The event remains a central reference point in European conversations about lone-actor violence, policing, and safeguarding open societies.
