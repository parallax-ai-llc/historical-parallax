## Background

Samuel Kanyon Doe rose from the Armed Forces of Liberia as a noncommissioned officer. His ascent mattered historically because it marked a major rupture in Liberia’s political order: a military takeover by an Indigenous Liberian leader against an Americo-Liberian–dominated establishment.

## Rule and Governance

Doe governed first through the People’s Redemption Council (a military junta) and later as an elected president. His tenure is commonly associated with:

- Militarization of politics and reliance on security services.
- Accusations of authoritarian rule, patronage, and ethnically polarized power.
- Periodic liberalization steps (party legalization and elections) that did not resolve legitimacy concerns.

## Civil War, Capture, and Death

By the late 1980s, economic stress, political repression allegations, and factional rivalry contributed to violent conflict. In 1990, as the civil war intensified, Doe was captured by a rival militia faction and killed—an event that symbolized the breakdown of central authority and foreshadowed years of instability.

## Legacy

In historical assessments, Doe is often treated as a transitional figure between Liberia’s long-standing one-party dominance and the era of civil war. Debates about his rule frequently focus on human rights, corruption, and the role of external patrons during the Cold War period, alongside the structural fragility of the Liberian state.
