## Summary

Charles Ponzi was an Italian-born con artist active in the United States and Canada in the early 20th century. He is most closely associated with the “Ponzi scheme,” a fraud in which returns to earlier investors are paid using funds from later investors rather than from legitimate profit.

## Background

- Ponzi emigrated from Italy and lived in several countries before becoming prominent in the U.S.
- Prior to his famous fraud, he held a variety of jobs and was repeatedly short of money, a pattern that set the stage for later deception.

## The Ponzi Scheme (in brief)

Ponzi’s 1919–1920 operation centered on **international reply coupons (IRCs)**, which could be exchanged for postage in different countries. He claimed he could profit from buying coupons cheaply in one country and redeeming them at higher value in another.

In practice:

- The advertised “arbitrage” was not executed at the scale claimed.
- New investor cash was used to pay “returns” to earlier investors, creating the appearance of a successful business.
- The structure required continuous inflows; once inflows slowed, the scheme could not sustain promised payouts.

## Impact and Legacy

- The term **“Ponzi scheme”** became a standard label for a class of fraudulent investment operations.
- Common warning signs associated with such schemes include:
  - unusually consistent or high returns,
  - opaque strategies that are difficult to verify,
  - reliance on continual new capital to meet obligations,
  - pressure to reinvest or to recruit additional investors.
- Modern financial regulators and law enforcement often reference Ponzi’s case as an archetype when explaining investment fraud dynamics.
