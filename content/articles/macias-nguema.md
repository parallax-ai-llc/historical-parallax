Macias Nguema (Francisco Macías Nguema) was the first president of Equatorial Guinea and one of the most repressive leaders in post-colonial Africa. His rule is widely associated with mass repression, the collapse of state institutions, and severe economic decline.

## Background

- Born in 1924 in what was then Spanish Guinea.
- Worked within the colonial administration prior to independence.

## Rise to Power

- Became a prominent political figure during the transition to independence.
- Won the 1968 presidential election, becoming head of state after independence from Spain.

## Presidency (1968–1979)

### Governance and Political System

- Concentrated power in the presidency and eliminated political opposition.
- Moved toward a one-party, personalist dictatorship.
- Used security forces and patronage networks to enforce loyalty.

### Repression and Human Rights

- Numerous reports describe widespread imprisonment, torture, and extrajudicial killings.
- Targeted real and perceived opponents, including elites, professionals, and minority groups.
- Large numbers of citizens fled into exile during his tenure.

### Economic and Social Impact

- Economic management deteriorated sharply; administrative capacity collapsed.
- Public services and education suffered as professionals were purged or fled.
- The period is often described as one of state breakdown in Equatorial Guinea.

## Overthrow and Death

- Deposed in a 1979 coup led by Teodoro Obiang Nguema Mbasogo.
- Tried and executed in 1979.

## Legacy

- Commonly cited as an extreme case of post-independence authoritarianism.
- His overthrow marked a transition to a new regime that has itself remained authoritarian, though with different patterns of governance.
