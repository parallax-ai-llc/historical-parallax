## Summary

French economist known for research on income and wealth inequality and for bringing long-run historical data on distribution to broad public debate. He is widely associated with the argument (popularized in *Capital in the Twenty-First Century*) that when returns to capital outpace economic growth, inequality tends to rise absent countervailing institutions and policies.

## Key Themes

- **Distribution over time:** uses historical series (tax records and other archives) to study how income/wealth shares change across decades and centuries.
- **Institutions and politics:** emphasizes that inequality is shaped by laws, property regimes, education, and political coalitions—not only by “market forces.”
- **Policy debate:** frequently discusses progressive taxation, transparency, and the role of public services as tools that can counter inequality.

## Roles and Affiliations

Commonly cited affiliations include the Paris School of Economics (PSE) and academic work connected to French and international research institutions. His work sits at the intersection of public economics and economic history.

## Public Reception (Overview)

- Celebrated for making inequality research legible to non-specialists.
- Debated by economists and commentators over interpretation, measurement, and policy implications.
