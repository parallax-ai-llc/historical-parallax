## Summary

On August 6, 1945, the United States dropped an atomic bomb nicknamed "Little Boy" on the Japanese city of Hiroshima, marking the first wartime use of a nuclear weapon in history. The bomb was released from the B-29 bomber *Enola Gay* at 8:15 a.m. local time, detonating approximately 600 meters above the city center. The blast instantly killed an estimated 70,000–80,000 people, with total deaths reaching 90,000–166,000 by the end of 1945 due to injuries, burns, and acute radiation sickness.

The bombing came after years of brutal Pacific warfare and followed the Allied demand for Japan's unconditional surrender in the Potsdam Declaration of July 1945. Three days later, a second bomb was dropped on Nagasaki, and Japan announced its surrender on August 15, 1945, formally ending World War II.

Hiroshima remains one of the most consequential single events of the 20th century, reshaping global politics, spurring the nuclear arms race, and permanently altering humanity's relationship with warfare and technology. The city has since become a symbol of nuclear devastation and a center for international peace advocacy.

## Background & Causes

By mid-1945, the Pacific War had grown extraordinarily costly. American forces had fought grinding campaigns at Iwo Jima and Okinawa, suffering tens of thousands of casualties. Military planners projected that a land invasion of the Japanese home islands — Operation Downfall — could result in over one million Allied casualties and potentially millions of Japanese deaths.

Japan's military leadership remained committed to fighting despite devastating conventional firebombing campaigns that had already destroyed much of Tokyo and other major cities. The United States, having successfully tested the first nuclear device at the Trinity test site in New Mexico on July 16, 1945, saw the atomic bomb as a means to force rapid surrender and avoid the invasion. President Harry S. Truman authorized its use. Hiroshima was selected as a primary target partly because it was a major military headquarters and industrial center largely undamaged by prior bombing, making it useful for assessing the weapon's effects.

## Key Figures

- **Harry S. Truman** — U.S. President who authorized the bombings, believing they would end the war quickly and save lives on both sides.
- **Col. Paul Tibbets** — Pilot of the *Enola Gay*, who commanded and flew the mission over Hiroshima.
- **J. Robert Oppenheimer** — Scientific director of the Manhattan Project, which designed and built the atomic bombs.
- **Emperor Hirohito** — Japanese emperor whose radio address announced Japan's acceptance of surrender terms.
- **Setsuko Thurlow** — Hiroshima survivor (hibakusha) who became a prominent international advocate for nuclear disarmament, accepting the Nobel Peace Prize on behalf of ICAN in 2017.
- **Sadako Sasaki** — A child exposed to radiation who became a symbol of atomic bomb victims; her story of folding 1,000 paper cranes inspired global peace movements.

## Consequences & Impact

The immediate destruction was catastrophic: roughly 5 square miles of Hiroshima were obliterated, and approximately 69% of the city's buildings destroyed. Deaths from radiation sickness, burns, and injuries continued for months and years. Survivors — known as *hibakusha* — faced elevated rates of leukemia and other cancers for decades.

Japan's surrender formally ended World War II, averting a land invasion but initiating a new era of geopolitical tension. The bombings accelerated the Soviet Union's nuclear weapons program, directly igniting the Cold War arms race. The United States and USSR eventually amassed tens of thousands of warheads between them.

Internationally, the bombings prompted landmark arms control efforts, including the 1968 Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty. Hiroshima's Peace Memorial Park and Museum, built near the hypocenter, draw over one million visitors annually. The city holds a memorial ceremony every August 6.

## Perspectives

**The strategic necessity argument** holds that the bombs saved lives overall by forcing a swift Japanese surrender, avoiding a land invasion projected to cause far greater casualties on both sides.

**The moral opposition view** argues that targeting civilian populations constituted a war crime, and that Japan was already near collapse — making the bombings unnecessary acts of mass destruction, possibly motivated in part by signaling power to the Soviet Union.

**Japanese national memory** has largely centered on victimhood and anti-nuclear advocacy, though debate persists within Japan about imperial wartime conduct and the broader context of Japanese aggression in Asia.

**Revisionist scholarship** has questioned whether Soviet entry into the Pacific War (August 8, 1945) rather than the bombs was the decisive factor in Japan's surrender decision.
