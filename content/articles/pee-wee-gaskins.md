## Summary

American serial killer who claimed to have murdered between 100 and 110 people in South Carolina, making him potentially one of America's most prolific killers. Executed by electric chair for a prison murder.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|

| 1982 | Murdered fellow inmate on death row |
| 1991 | Executed by electric chair |

## Background

- Donald Henry “Pee Wee” Gaskins was active primarily in South Carolina.
- He became a subject of extensive true-crime coverage, with narratives often mixing confirmed cases with his later self-reported claims.

## Confessions vs. Confirmed Victims

- Publicized totals (e.g., 100+) are largely drawn from Gaskins’s confessions, which investigators and historians treat with caution.
- A smaller number of murders were formally tied to him through convictions and corroborated evidence.

## Legal Process and Death Sentence

- His death sentence is associated with a murder committed while incarcerated (a fellow inmate), which became the basis for capital punishment.
- The case is frequently cited in discussions about reliability of jailhouse confessions and the incentives for notoriety.

## Cultural Footprint

- Gaskins’s story is widely known through the book *Final Truth* by Wilton Earle (often summarized as a memoir-style account), which is also criticized for sensationalism and disputes over accuracy.

## Notes on Sources

- Many secondary sources repeat high victim-count figures; careful summaries distinguish between admitted, suspected, and legally confirmed killings.
