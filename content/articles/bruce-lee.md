## Summary

Bruce Lee revolutionized martial arts and brought kung fu to global audiences. He founded Jeet Kune Do. He died at 32 and remains the most influential martial arts figure ever.

## Background

Bruce Lee was born in San Francisco and raised largely in Hong Kong, later returning to the United States as a young adult. He trained in multiple martial arts traditions and combined athletic practice with a strong interest in pedagogy and philosophy.

## Jeet Kune Do (High-Level)

Jeet Kune Do (JKD) is commonly described as Lee’s approach to martial arts rather than a fixed “style.” At a high level, it emphasizes:

- **Practical effectiveness:** Favor techniques that work under pressure rather than adhering to rigid forms.
- **Adaptability:** Select and refine methods based on context, distance, timing, and the opponent.
- **Simplicity and directness:** Reduce unnecessary movement and focus on clear, efficient actions.

## Film and Cultural Legacy

Lee’s screen presence helped shape how global audiences perceived Chinese martial arts cinema. His films and unfinished projects influenced later action choreography, training culture, and the broader entertainment industry.

## Influence

- **Martial arts instruction:** Contributed to modern cross-training and the idea that techniques can be evaluated empirically.
- **Representation:** Became an enduring symbol of Asian representation in Western popular culture.
- **Athletic culture:** Helped popularize disciplined conditioning, mobility work, and technical drilling within mainstream audiences.
