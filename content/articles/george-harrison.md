## Summary

George Harrison (1943–2001) was an English musician, singer, and songwriter best known as the lead guitarist of the Beatles, the most commercially successful and critically acclaimed band in popular music history. Though often overshadowed by the Lennon-McCartney songwriting partnership, Harrison's contributions were essential to the Beatles' artistic evolution, and his solo career further established him as a formidable artist in his own right.

Harrison was instrumental in introducing Indian classical music and the sitar to Western popular music, profoundly influencing the psychedelic and world music movements of the 1960s and beyond. His spiritual journey through Hinduism and Vedic philosophy shaped not only his music but also his public persona, making him one of the most philosophically distinctive figures in rock history.

Beyond performance, Harrison demonstrated a lasting commitment to humanitarian causes, most notably organizing the Concert for Bangladesh in 1971 — one of the first major charity rock concerts in history. His influence as a guitarist, songwriter, and cultural ambassador continues to resonate decades after his death.

## Early Life & Background

George Harrison was born on February 25, 1943, in Wavertree, Liverpool, England, the youngest of four children of Harold and Louise Harrison. He grew up in the working-class neighborhood of Speke, attending Dovedale Primary School and later Liverpool Institute, where he met Paul McCartney. His mother, an enthusiastic supporter of his musical interests, encouraged his early fascination with the guitar, which he took up around age 13 after being inspired by skiffle pioneer Lonnie Donegan.

## Rise to Prominence

Harrison joined John Lennon's skiffle group, the Quarrymen, in 1958 at the age of 14, impressing the older Lennon with his guitar proficiency. The group evolved into the Beatles, and Harrison became their lead guitarist. The band's 1963 debut album *Please Please Me* launched Beatlemania across Britain, followed by their 1964 US debut, which triggered a global cultural phenomenon. Harrison contributed songwriting from an early stage, with tracks like "Don't Bother Me" (1963) appearing on Beatles albums. His growing interest in Indian music, sparked by his encounter with the sitar on the set of *Help!* (1965), led to collaborations with sitarist Ravi Shankar and influenced landmark tracks such as "Norwegian Wood" and "Within You Without You."

## Key Achievements

- Co-wrote and performed on all 12 Beatles studio albums (1963–1970)
- Released *All Things Must Pass* (1970), a triple album widely regarded as one of the greatest solo rock albums ever made; it produced the number-one single "My Sweet Lord"
- Organized the Concert for Bangladesh (August 1, 1971) at Madison Square Garden, raising funds for refugees and setting a precedent for benefit concerts
- Co-founded HandMade Films in 1978, producing acclaimed films including *Monty Python's Life of Brian* and *Time Bandits*
- Formed the Traveling Wilburys in 1988 alongside Bob Dylan, Tom Petty, Roy Orbison, and Jeff Lynne; their debut album reached number 3 on the Billboard 200
- Inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame as a Beatle in 1988

## Controversies & Criticism

Harrison faced a significant legal controversy in 1976 when a US court found that "My Sweet Lord" had unconsciously plagiarized the Chiffons' 1963 hit "He's So Fine." The case, *Bright Tunes Music v. Harrisongs Music*, set an enduring legal precedent regarding subconscious copyright infringement. Some critics also noted that his tenure as a film producer with HandMade Films ended in financial and legal disputes with business partner Denis O'Brien, who Harrison later accused of mismanaging millions of dollars of his funds. Additionally, Harrison was occasionally characterized as difficult during the Beatles' later years, tensions documented in the *Let It Be* sessions (1969) and later confirmed by the 2021 *Get Back* documentary.

## Legacy & Influence

Harrison died on November 29, 2001, from lung cancer at age 58. His influence as a guitarist — characterized by melodic precision, slide guitar mastery, and innovative use of non-Western instruments — shaped generations of musicians. His album *All Things Must Pass* remains a touchstone of early 1970s rock. The Concert for Bangladesh established a model for humanitarian rock events followed by Live Aid, Farm Aid, and countless others. In 2004, *Rolling Stone* ranked him 11th on its list of the 100 Greatest Guitarists. His son Dhani Harrison has continued his musical legacy, and posthumous releases including the 2001 album *Brainwashed* have kept his catalog vital.
