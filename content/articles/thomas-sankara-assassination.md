## Overview

Thomas Sankara, the first President of Burkina Faso (in office 1983–1987), was killed during the 15 October 1987 coup in Ouagadougou that installed Blaise Compaoré as president.[^2][^3]

In many references, the assassination is discussed as part of the broader 1987 Burkina Faso coup d'état rather than as a standalone topic.[^3]

## Context (high-level)

Sankara led a revolutionary government that pursued major social and economic reforms, including public health campaigns, literacy drives, and anti-imperialist foreign policy.[^2]

The coup narrative summarized on Wikipedia emphasizes political and diplomatic tensions, including disputes over relations with neighboring states and former colonial power France, as part of the justification offered by Compaoré.[^3]

## Events of 15 October 1987

Wikipedia's coup article states that Sankara and twelve other officials were killed during an armed attack at the presidential palace.[^3]

After the coup, some Committees for the Defense of the Revolution (CDRs) mounted resistance for several days before the new authorities consolidated control.[^3]

## Aftermath and Legacy

The coup article describes a policy shift under Compaoré that reversed many of Sankara's policies and reoriented Burkina Faso toward the IMF and World Bank.[^3]

Sankara's killing and burial became a long-running political and legal issue; Wikipedia notes later exhumation and autopsy work reported after the 2014 political transition.[^3]
