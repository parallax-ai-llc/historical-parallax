## Summary

Sir Michael Philip Jagger (born July 26, 1943) is the lead vocalist, co-founder, and principal lyricist of the Rolling Stones, one of the best-selling and longest-running rock bands in history. With a career spanning over six decades, Jagger is widely regarded as one of the greatest rock frontmen of all time, known for his distinctive voice, frenetic stage presence, and charismatic persona.

Jagger's influence extends well beyond music. His androgynous style, rebellious image, and prolific output helped shape rock and roll culture from the 1960s onward. Alongside guitarist Keith Richards, he formed one of popular music's most celebrated songwriting partnerships, producing iconic songs that remain cultural touchstones decades after their release.

## Early Life & Background

Mick Jagger was born in Dartford, Kent, England, to Eva Mary Ensley and Basil Fanshawe Jagger, a physical education teacher. He attended Dartford Grammar School, where he briefly reconnected with childhood acquaintance Keith Richards in 1961 — a chance encounter on a train platform that would prove pivotal. Jagger enrolled at the London School of Economics in 1961 on a grant, studying economics and finance, but left before completing his degree to pursue music full time.

His early exposure to American rhythm and blues — particularly artists like Chuck Berry, Muddy Waters, and Little Richard — proved foundational to his musical development.

## Rise to Prominence

Jagger co-founded the Rolling Stones in London in 1962 alongside Richards, Brian Jones, Charlie Watts, and Bill Wyman. The band released their debut single "Come On" in June 1963 and quickly built a following as a rawer, blues-driven alternative to the Beatles. Their 1965 single "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" reached number one in multiple countries and cemented their global stardom.

Through the late 1960s and into the 1970s, Jagger and Richards crafted a string of landmark albums — *Beggars Banquet* (1968), *Let It Bleed* (1969), *Sticky Fingers* (1971), and *Exile on Main St.* (1972) — widely considered among the greatest rock records ever made. Jagger's stage persona evolved into something theatrical and provocative, influencing countless performers after him.

## Key Achievements

- Co-wrote and performed on more than 30 Billboard Hot 100 top-40 singles with the Rolling Stones.
- Inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame as a member of the Rolling Stones in 1989.
- Knighted by Queen Elizabeth II in 2003 for services to popular music.
- The Rolling Stones' "A Bigger Bang Tour" (2005–2007) grossed over $558 million, a world record at the time.
- Pursued a solo career alongside the Stones, releasing four studio albums between 1985 and 2001.

## Controversies & Criticism

Jagger's personal life attracted substantial tabloid attention throughout his career, including high-profile relationships with Chrissie Shrimpton, Marianne Faithfull, Jerry Hall (with whom he had four children), and model L'Wren Scott. His 1967 drug arrest alongside Richards became a cause célèbre in British media. Critics have periodically questioned the band's ongoing commercial touring operation, arguing that financial motivations have overshadowed artistic ones in later decades. His decision to accept a knighthood was publicly criticized by Richards, reflecting long-standing tensions within the band.

## Legacy & Influence

Jagger's impact on rock performance, fashion, and celebrity culture is immeasurable. His physical, high-energy stage style directly influenced artists including David Bowie, Prince, Iggy Pop, and Lenny Kravitz. The Rolling Stones' sustained commercial and creative longevity — still touring into the 2020s — is in large part attributable to his drive and discipline. He is consistently ranked among the greatest singers in rock history by publications including *Rolling Stone* magazine, which placed him fourth on its list of the 100 greatest singers.
