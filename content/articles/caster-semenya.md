## Summary

Caster Semenya won Olympic 800m gold at 2012 London and 2016 Rio. Her career was marked by a controversial ruling requiring her to take testosterone-suppressing medication to compete.

## Athletic achievements

Semenya became one of the dominant 800-meter runners of her generation, winning multiple global titles and Olympic medals. Her racing style, tactical strength over the final lap, and consistent championship performances made her a central figure in middle-distance athletics.

## Eligibility rules controversy

Her career intersected with the evolution of eligibility rules related to sex classification in sport. Track and field’s governing body (now World Athletics, formerly the IAAF) introduced regulations affecting some athletes with differences of sex development (DSD), particularly for certain events.

The rules and their enforcement triggered intense debate over scientific evidence, competitive fairness, medical ethics, privacy, and the rights of athletes. Semenya became the most visible person associated with that debate because her competitive future depended on compliance with hormone-related eligibility criteria.

## Litigation and appeals

Semenya challenged the regulations through sports arbitration, including proceedings at the Court of Arbitration for Sport (CAS), and later through other legal avenues. Public reporting often frames the case as part of a broader struggle over how international sport should balance inclusion with sex-segregated categories.

The 2023 European Court of Human Rights decision is widely discussed as a procedural milestone in the legal trajectory of the dispute, even as the underlying policy debate continues.

## Legacy

Beyond medals, Semenya’s impact is frequently measured by how her case forced sporting institutions to clarify, defend, and revise policies. Her story also highlights how elite sport can turn private biological questions into public controversy with long-lasting consequences for athletes.

The debate around her has influenced discussions in other sports and federations that maintain sex-segregated competition categories.
