## Summary

German Nazi propagandist who published Der Stürmer, a virulently antisemitic newspaper. He was convicted at Nuremberg of crimes against humanity for inciting genocide through his propaganda, though he held no military or political power at war's end.

## Additional Context

### Positions and Party Role

Streicher was a long-time Nazi Party activist and served as **Gauleiter of Franconia**, making him a powerful regional figure during the party’s consolidation of power. He also held national-level visibility through party structures and propaganda networks, even when his direct administrative authority fluctuated.

### Der *Stürmer* and Propaganda

*Der Stürmer* was a propaganda outlet focused on **extreme antisemitic agitation**. Historical accounts emphasize that Streicher’s significance was less about command authority and more about persistent **incitement and dehumanization** through mass media—an influence that supported broader Nazi persecution.

### Removal from Office vs. Continued Influence

He was removed from key party positions around **1940**, but continued to be associated with propaganda work for years afterward. This distinction—loss of formal office while maintaining a propaganda platform—became relevant in later legal evaluations of responsibility.

### Nuremberg Conviction and Legal Significance

At the Nuremberg trials, Streicher was convicted of **crimes against humanity** on the basis that he continued to publish inflammatory material while aware of the ongoing mass murder of Jews. The judgment is frequently cited as an early, prominent example of international tribunals treating **incitement** as criminally significant even without direct battlefield command.

### Biographical Notes

Before his political rise, Streicher trained and worked as a **schoolteacher**, and served in **World War I**, receiving military decorations. Post-war radicalization and propaganda activity shaped his later career.
