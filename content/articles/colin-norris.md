## Summary

Scottish nurse convicted of murdering 4 elderly patients at Leeds hospitals in 2002 by injecting them with insulin. He targeted vulnerable patients during night shifts. Sentenced to life with 30-year minimum.

## Background

Norris worked as a nurse in the United Kingdom and was accused of harming elderly patients while on duty. The convictions focused on a cluster of patient collapses and deaths in hospital wards during night shifts.

## Case Details (High Level)

- Prosecutors alleged insulin was administered to non-diabetic patients.
- The case relied heavily on medical evidence and inference about opportunity and pattern, rather than eyewitness testimony.
- The victims were older and medically fragile, making causation and intent a central issue.

## Appeals and Controversy

The case has been cited in discussions about the limits of statistical reasoning and expert testimony in healthcare-related homicide prosecutions. Reporting and advocacy around the conviction have emphasized debates over insulin testing, alternative explanations, and the interpretation of clinical records.

## Why the Case Matters

Norris is often referenced alongside other “angel of death” cases because it highlights how difficult it can be to distinguish negligence, natural deterioration, and deliberate harm in complex clinical settings.


## Key Takeaways

- Healthcare homicide cases can hinge on complex expert interpretation.
- Patterns across multiple patients may be used as circumstantial evidence.
