## Background

Richard Whitney was a prominent American financier who rose through elite banking and brokerage circles and ultimately became president of the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) during the Great Depression era.

## Public Role at the NYSE

Whitney’s tenure is remembered for episodes in which exchange leadership attempted to project stability during periods of market panic. Accounts of the 1929 crash era often mention highly visible “confidence” actions on the trading floor, reflecting the norms of the time.

## Embezzlement and Conviction

Investigations later found that Whitney diverted funds from client accounts and internal pools (including an NYSE-related gratuity fund) to cover personal financial losses. He was convicted of embezzlement in 1938 and served a prison sentence.

## Aftermath

The Whitney case is often cited as a cautionary example of:

- weak internal controls and conflicts of interest in early 20th-century finance
- reputational risk and governance failures in major market institutions
- the broader push toward stricter oversight and financial regulation after the Depression
