## Summary

Mozambican-Greek assassin who stabbed South African Prime Minister Hendrik Verwoerd to death in parliament on September 6, 1966. He was a mixed-race man opposed to apartheid. Found mentally unfit for trial; died in a psychiatric hospital.

## Historical Context

Hendrik Verwoerd is widely associated with the formal design and enforcement of apartheid-era policy in South Africa, which is why the assassination has a lasting political resonance beyond the immediate event.

Parliamentary violence of this kind is rare and tends to be interpreted through competing lenses: personal history, ideological motive, and state narratives.

## Mental Health and Interpretation

Contemporary handling of Tsafendas emphasized mental unfitness, and later commentary has debated how mental health assessments, political context, and institutional incentives shaped the official story.

Because records and interpretations vary, careful sourcing is important when describing motive or intent.

## Legacy

The incident is frequently referenced in discussions of political dissent under apartheid and of how states classify acts as criminal, political, or pathological.
