## Summary

Angolan rebel leader who founded UNITA and waged civil war for 27 years with US and South African backing. The war killed over 500,000 people and he was killed in a government ambush.

## Additional Context

## Origins and Ideology

Savimbi emerged from Angola’s anti-colonial politics and became a central figure in the country’s long post-independence conflict. UNITA positioned itself as a rival to the ruling MPLA, and Savimbi’s movement was shaped by shifting ideological language over time as well as alliances driven by Cold War geopolitics.

## Civil War and International Backing

The Angolan Civil War involved extensive external involvement. UNITA received varying degrees of support from foreign states at different times, while the MPLA government also relied on international allies. The conflict is frequently analyzed as a proxy battleground during the Cold War era, with severe humanitarian consequences for Angola.

## Elections, Peace Accords, and Renewed Fighting

A key turning point came with the early-1990s peace process and multi-party elections. Disputes over the results and implementation contributed to renewed warfare. Later agreements, including the Lusaka Protocol (mid-1990s), sought demobilization and power-sharing, but fighting continued intermittently.

## Conflict Financing and Humanitarian Impact

Reporting and research on the war often highlight the role of natural resources—especially diamonds—in sustaining armed actors. The prolonged conflict resulted in mass displacement, widespread landmine contamination, and significant civilian casualties.
