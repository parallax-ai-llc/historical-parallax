## Summary

Indian businessman and founder of Satyam Computer Services who confessed to a $1.5 billion accounting fraud in 2009—India's largest corporate fraud. He fabricated cash balances and revenues for years. Known as 'India's Enron.'

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1987 | Founded Satyam Computer Services |
| 2009 | Confessed to fabricating accounts; $1.5 billion fraud |
| 2015 | Convicted; sentenced to 7 years |

## Satyam Scandal (High-Level)

- The Satyam case is often summarized as a prolonged manipulation of:
  - reported cash and bank balances,
  - revenues and profits,
  - supporting documentation and internal controls.
- The confession in 2009 triggered an immediate collapse in investor confidence and emergency actions to stabilize the company.

## Corporate Governance and Oversight Themes

- The scandal became a major reference point in India for discussions about:
  - auditor independence and scrutiny,
  - board oversight and risk management,
  - disclosure standards and enforcement capacity.
- It is frequently used in business education as an example of how sustained falsification can persist when checks are weak or compromised.

## Market and Policy Impact

- The case prompted heightened attention to regulatory frameworks and corporate reporting.
- It also affected perceptions of India’s outsourcing and IT-services sector during a period when it was growing in global prominence.

## Company Resolution and Continuity

- After the fraud became public, Satyam’s operations were restructured under new ownership/management to preserve the underlying business.
- Analyses of the episode often distinguish between:
  - the operating company and its employees/clients,
  - and the leadership and accounting practices that produced the falsified reporting.

## Why It Matters

- The Satyam scandal is commonly compared to other large corporate frauds because it illustrates:
  - systemic incentives to overstate performance,
  - the cascading effects on employees, clients, and markets,
  - the importance of credible financial statements in public-company governance.
