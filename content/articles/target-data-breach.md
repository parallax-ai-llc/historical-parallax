## Summary

Data breach at retailer Target during the 2013 holiday season that exposed credit card data of 40 million customers and personal information of 70 million.

## How the Breach Happened (High Level)

Public reporting and later investigations described a supply-chain style compromise in which attackers gained access using credentials associated with a third-party vendor (often reported as an HVAC contractor). From there, the intruders moved through Target’s internal network and deployed point-of-sale (POS) malware designed to capture payment-card data as transactions were processed.

## Data Exposed

The incident is commonly summarized in two parts:

- Payment card data for tens of millions of customers.
- Personal information (such as names and contact details) for additional customers.

Specific counts vary by source, but the breach is widely cited as one of the largest U.S. retail compromises of its era.

## Response and Aftermath

Target faced significant costs related to incident response, customer notification, card re-issuance, and legal settlements. Leadership changes, including the CEO’s departure, became part of the public narrative around accountability following major cybersecurity failures.

## Industry Impact

The breach accelerated retailer focus on network segmentation, third-party access controls, and monitoring of POS environments. It also contributed to momentum for wider adoption of EMV (chip) payment cards in the United States and helped popularize the idea that vendor access can be a critical weak link.

## Lessons Commonly Cited

Security retrospectives frequently cite the incident as a reminder that detection tooling is only effective when alerts are acted on quickly and when high-risk environments (like POS networks) are isolated from broader corporate systems.
