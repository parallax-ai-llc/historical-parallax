## Summary

Three ISIS suicide bombers attacked Istanbul's Atatürk Airport killing 45 people. The attackers were from Russia, Uzbekistan, and Kyrgyzstan.

## Context

Istanbul Atatürk Airport was one of Europe’s busiest international hubs in 2016, serving as a major transit point between Europe, the Middle East, and Asia. In the mid-2010s, Turkey faced repeated attacks linked to the Islamic State (ISIS/ISIL) and other militant groups, with targets including public spaces, tourism sites, and transportation infrastructure.

## Attack

- **Date:** 2016-06-28
- **Location:** Istanbul Atatürk Airport (international terminal area and approaches)
- **Method:** Coordinated armed assault followed by suicide bombings
- **Immediate impact:** Dozens killed and hundreds injured; airport operations were disrupted as the site was secured.

Reports describe assailants first opening fire before detonating explosive devices, a tactic intended to create panic and maximize casualties in crowded civilian areas.

## Casualties and Damage

Casualty figures are typically reported as **45 killed** (excluding the attackers) and **many injured**. The incident caused significant disruption to air traffic and heightened security concerns around major transport nodes in Turkey.

## Investigation and Attribution

Turkish authorities attributed the attack to **ISIS**. Public reporting identified the attackers as originating from **Russia, Uzbekistan, and Kyrgyzstan**, reflecting ISIS recruitment and facilitation networks that extended beyond the Middle East.

Investigations focused on identifying logistical support, safe houses, travel routes, and any local facilitators who enabled the attackers to reach and operate in Istanbul.

## Aftermath

In the immediate aftermath, the airport was temporarily closed and flights were rerouted or canceled while emergency services responded and security forces conducted sweeps. The attack drew international condemnation and renewed debate about aviation and perimeter security, crowd management at terminals, and intelligence coordination against transnational militant networks.
