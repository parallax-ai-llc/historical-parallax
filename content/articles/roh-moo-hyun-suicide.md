## Summary

Former President Roh Moo-hyun committed suicide by jumping from a cliff amid a corruption investigation. His death sparked national mourning and debate about prosecutorial overreach.

## Context

- Roh Moo-hyun (노무현) served as President of South Korea from 2003 to 2008.
- His death occurred while prosecutors were investigating allegations of illegal payments linked to people in his circle.
- The incident became a major national event, with intense debate about politics, media pressure, and prosecutorial practice.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- Date: 2009-05-23 (early morning).
- Location: Bongha Village area near Gimhae, South Gyeongsang Province, South Korea.
- Widely reported accounts describe Roh leaving his residence and later falling from a rocky area/cliff; he was transported to a hospital where he was pronounced dead.
- Reports also described a short note attributed to Roh asking not to blame others and expressing distress; details are commonly summarized in contemporary coverage.

## Investigation and Public Debate

- The investigation frequently referenced in coverage involved a businessman (Park Yeon-cha) and alleged transfers of money to associates connected to Roh.
- Roh publicly denied personal wrongdoing, while acknowledging political responsibility and expressing regret in public statements during the period.
- Commentators and citizens debated whether the intensity of the investigation and surrounding media coverage contributed to psychological pressure.
- The case became a touchstone in discussions about due process, political polarization, and the social consequences of high-profile prosecutions.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- Large-scale mourning was visible across South Korea, including memorial altars, public gatherings, and extensive online condolences.
- Roh’s death significantly affected South Korean political discourse; he remained an influential symbol for many progressives and a contested figure for critics.
- The episode is often cited when discussing the fragility of democratic norms during partisan conflict and the human cost of political scandal cycles.
