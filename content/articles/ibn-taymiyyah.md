## Summary

Ibn Taymiyyah (1263-1328) was one of medieval Islam's most influential and controversial scholars, whose theological and legal opinions continue to shape Islamic thought seven centuries after his death. Born in Harran (present-day Turkey) and raised in Damascus, he became a prominent Hanbali jurist known for his strict interpretation of Islamic texts and fierce opposition to what he considered religious innovations. His writings on theology, jurisprudence, and politics challenged established religious authorities and led to multiple imprisonments throughout his life. Today, Ibn Taymiyyah remains a polarizing figure—revered by Salafi movements as a reformer who purified Islam, while criticized by others as an extremist whose ideas fuel religious intolerance. His extensive body of work, comprising hundreds of volumes, continues to influence contemporary Islamic movements worldwide.

## Early Life and Background

Taqi ad-Din Ahmad ibn Taymiyyah was born on January 22, 1263 (10th or 12th of Rabi' al-Awwal, 661 AH) in Harran, a city in what is now southeastern Turkey near the Syrian border[^1]. He was born into a family of distinguished Hanbali scholars; his grandfather, Abu al-Barakat Majd ad-Din, was a renowned jurist whose works remained influential for generations[^2]. The family belonged to the Taymiyyah clan, which had produced several notable Islamic scholars, and young Ahmad grew up immersed in religious learning from his earliest years[^3].

When Ibn Taymiyyah was six years old, in 1269, his family was forced to flee Harran due to the Mongol invasions that were devastating the region[^4]. The family relocated to Damascus, which was then under Mamluk rule and served as a major center of Islamic learning. This displacement during his formative years likely influenced his later strong stance against the Mongols and his controversial fatwa declaring them apostates despite their nominal conversion to Islam[^5].

In Damascus, Ibn Taymiyyah received an exceptional education under his father, Shihab ad-Din Abd al-Halim, who was himself a respected Hanbali scholar and preacher at the Umayyad Mosque[^6]. Young Ibn Taymiyyah proved to be a prodigious student with an extraordinary memory, reportedly memorizing the entire Quran by age seven and mastering numerous hadith collections before reaching adolescence[^7]. Beyond his father, he studied under numerous prominent scholars of various Islamic disciplines, including hadith, Quranic exegesis, theology, and jurisprudence.

His education was comprehensive and multifaceted, encompassing not only traditional Islamic sciences but also logic, philosophy, and the works of earlier theological schools that he would later criticize[^8]. By his late teens, Ibn Taymiyyah had already begun teaching and issuing legal opinions (fatwas), demonstrating a precocious mastery of Islamic scholarship that impressed his contemporaries[^9]. When his father died in 1282, the twenty-one-year-old Ibn Taymiyyah succeeded him as professor of Hanbali jurisprudence, taking over his father's teaching position at the Sukkariyya Madrasa in Damascus[^10].

## Rise to Prominence

Ibn Taymiyyah's rise to prominence in the Islamic scholarly world was meteoric, driven by his exceptional intellect, prolific writing, and willingness to challenge established authorities. By his mid-twenties, he had already established himself as one of Damascus's leading religious scholars, attracting students from across the Islamic world[^11]. His reputation was built not only on his mastery of traditional Islamic sciences but also on his bold willingness to issue controversial opinions that challenged both scholarly consensus and political authorities.

The scholar's public profile increased dramatically during the Mongol invasions of Syria in the early 14th century. Unlike many scholars who fled or remained silent, Ibn Taymiyyah actively participated in the military defense of Damascus and issued fatwas supporting jihad against the Mongol invaders[^12]. In 1300, he accompanied the Mamluk army to the Battle of Marj al-Saffar near Damascus, and his presence reportedly boosted the morale of Muslim fighters[^13]. His most controversial contribution was his fatwa declaring the Mongol Ilkhanate to be apostates despite their conversion to Islam, arguing that their continued use of Mongol law (Yassa) rather than Sharia made them legitimate targets of jihad[^14].

This period established Ibn Taymiyyah as not merely a scholar but a public intellectual willing to engage with the pressing political and military issues of his day. His activism extended beyond military matters to include vocal criticism of popular religious practices he deemed innovations (bid'ah), including visitation of saints' tombs, seeking intercession through deceased holy figures, and various Sufi practices[^15]. These positions brought him into direct conflict with both Sufi orders and Ash'ari theologians who represented the dominant theological school in the Mamluk realm[^16].

Ibn Taymiyyah's combative style and uncompromising positions made him simultaneously popular among certain segments of the population and deeply threatening to religious and political establishments. His public debates and written refutations of other scholars were characterized by sharp polemics and unsparing criticism[^17]. He engaged in major theological controversies, including disputes over the nature of God's attributes, the status of reason versus revelation, and the proper interpretation of Islamic law. His insistence on a literalist reading of sacred texts and rejection of metaphorical interpretation (ta'wil) put him at odds with the prevailing Ash'ari theological school[^18].

By the early 1300s, Ibn Taymiyyah had become one of the most influential and controversial figures in Syrian religious life, with devoted followers and powerful enemies. His rise was marked by repeated cycles of popularity, controversy, trial, and imprisonment that would characterize his entire career[^19]. Despite—or perhaps because of—constant opposition from religious authorities, his influence continued to grow, and his writings circulated widely throughout the Islamic world.

---

## Major Achievements

Ibn Taymiyyah's intellectual output was extraordinarily prolific, with modern editions of his collected works comprising over 400 volumes[^20]. His major achievement lies in the development of a comprehensive theological and legal system that sought to return Islam to what he considered its pure, original form based on the Quran, Sunnah, and the practice of the first three generations of Muslims (al-Salaf al-Salih)[^21]. This approach, often called Salafism, has had profound and lasting influence on Islamic thought extending to the present day.

In theology, Ibn Taymiyyah developed a distinctive position that rejected both the rationalism of the Mu'tazilites and the scholastic theology (kalam) of the Ash'aris, which had become mainstream in Sunni Islam[^22]. He argued for a literalist understanding of divine attributes, maintaining that God's attributes mentioned in the Quran and hadith should be affirmed without asking "how" (bila kayf) they exist, but also without metaphorical interpretation[^23]. This position placed him in the middle between anthropomorphism and pure abstraction, though critics often accused him of the former.

His legal methodology emphasized ijtihad (independent reasoning) directly from primary sources rather than blind adherence to established madhab (school) doctrines, even though he himself was trained in the Hanbali school[^24]. Ibn Taymiyyah argued that the "gates of ijtihad" were not closed and that qualified scholars should derive rulings directly from the Quran and Sunnah rather than merely following earlier opinions[^25]. This approach challenged the authority structure of medieval Islamic scholarship and opened possibilities for legal reform and reinterpretation.

Among his most significant works are "Minhaj al-Sunnah al-Nabawiyyah" (The Way of the Prophetic Sunnah), a massive refutation of Shi'i theological positions; "Al-Siyasa al-Shar'iyya" (Governance According to Sharia), outlining Islamic political theory; and "Dar' Ta'arud al-Aql wa al-Naql" (Averting the Conflict between Reason and Revelation), addressing the relationship between rational and scriptural knowledge[^26]. His "Fatawa" collections encompass legal opinions on virtually every aspect of Islamic life and remain widely consulted today[^27].

Ibn Taymiyyah made important contributions to Islamic philosophy by critically engaging with Greek philosophy and its incorporation into Islamic thought through figures like Ibn Sina (Avicenna) and al-Farabi[^28]. He rejected much of the Neo-Platonic framework that had influenced earlier Muslim philosophers, arguing that it contradicted Islamic revelation and led to dangerous theological errors[^29]. His critique of philosophical theology influenced later Islamic intellectual trends toward greater emphasis on scriptural sources.

In political theory, Ibn Taymiyyah developed influential ideas about the relationship between religion and state, arguing that implementing Sharia was the primary obligation of Muslim rulers and that rulers who failed to do so lost their legitimacy[^30]. His fatwa against the Mongols established a precedent for declaring nominally Muslim rulers as apostates if they failed to implement Islamic law properly, a concept later appropriated by modern extremist movements[^31].

His scholarly methodology emphasized the critical examination of hadith chains and texts, and he made significant contributions to hadith sciences[^32]. Ibn Taymiyyah was willing to declare certain widely-accepted hadiths as weak or fabricated if they failed to meet rigorous authentication standards, demonstrating an independent critical approach[^33]. This methodological rigor influenced subsequent hadith scholarship and the development of more systematic approaches to hadith criticism.

## Controversies and Criticisms

Ibn Taymiyyah's career was marked by constant controversy, resulting in multiple trials and imprisonments that collectively spanned more than six years of his life[^34]. His first major imprisonment came in 1306 when he was confined in Damascus for his controversial theological positions on divine attributes, particularly his literalist interpretation that critics claimed amounted to anthropomorphism[^35]. This imprisonment lasted about a year and a half before he was released due to popular pressure and intervention by Mamluk amirs who supported him[^36].

His most sustained conflict involved his strident opposition to tomb visitation and seeking intercession through saints or the Prophet Muhammad. In 1310, Ibn Taymiyyah was summoned to Cairo to face charges related to his book "Ziyarat al-Qubur" (Visiting Graves), in which he declared that traveling specifically to visit the Prophet's tomb was forbidden[^37]. This position outraged many Muslims for whom pilgrimage to the Prophet's mosque in Medina was a cherished practice. Though initially cleared, he was imprisoned again in 1320 for refusing to retract these views[^38].

Ibn Taymiyyah's attacks on Sufism and Sufi practices created numerous powerful enemies. He condemned popular Sufi practices such as seeking blessing from saints' relics, celebration of the Prophet's birthday (mawlid), and various forms of mystical devotion he considered innovations[^39]. His criticism extended to prominent Sufi figures, including Ibn Arabi, whose doctrine of "unity of being" (wahdat al-wujud) Ibn Taymiyyah denounced as heretical pantheism[^40]. These positions alienated not only Sufi orders but also the many scholars and common people who revered Sufi masters.

His confrontational style and harsh polemics against other scholars created a climate of constant conflict. Ibn Taymiyyah did not hesitate to accuse prominent contemporary and historical scholars of deviation, innovation, or even unbelief when he believed they contradicted correct Islamic doctrine[^41]. This approach made him enemies among the religious establishment, particularly Ash'ari and Maturidi theologians who represented the mainstream of Sunni theological thought[^42].

In 1326, Ibn Taymiyyah was imprisoned in the Damascus Citadel for issuing a fatwa forbidding certain types of oaths of divorce that were commonly practiced[^43]. The Shafi'i and Maliki judges of Damascus obtained a ruling from Cairo prohibiting him from issuing fatwas on this matter, but he refused to comply, leading to his final imprisonment[^44]. During this confinement, authorities confiscated his writing materials to prevent him from continuing to write and disseminate his opinions, causing him great distress[^45].

Critics, both medieval and modern, have accused Ibn Taymiyyah of religious extremism and intolerance. His takfir (excommunication) of the Mongols and his harsh positions on various Muslim groups have been cited as precedents by modern extremist movements, though scholars debate whether this represents a fair reading of his positions[^46]. His views on jihad and the permissibility of rebellion against unjust Muslim rulers have been selectively appropriated by militant groups, leading some to hold Ibn Taymiyyah's thought partially responsible for contemporary Islamic extremism[^47].

His positions on religious minorities, particularly Christians and Jews, reflected medieval attitudes but included harsh statements about forcible conversion and restrictions that modern readers find troubling[^48]. While he acknowledged the protected status (dhimmi) of People of the Book under Islamic law, his writings included strong polemics against Christianity and Judaism[^49].

## Personal Life

Despite his public prominence and constant controversies, Ibn Taymiyyah maintained a relatively ascetic personal life characterized by simplicity and devotion to scholarship and worship[^50]. He never married, a notable choice that distinguished him from most Islamic scholars of his era, and devoted his entire life to study, teaching, and religious activism[^51]. Contemporary accounts describe him as living frugally, owning few possessions, and spending most of his income on books and supporting students[^52].

His students and contemporaries described Ibn Taymiyyah as possessing an extraordinary memory and intellectual capacity, able to cite extensive passages from hundreds of books and recall complex chains of transmission for hadith[^53]. He reportedly had a photographic memory that allowed him to quote extensively from works he had read years earlier[^54]. His work habits were intense; he would often write for extended periods, producing pages of complex theological and legal arguments at remarkable speed[^55].

Contemporary biographical accounts portray Ibn Taymiyyah as personally courageous and physically brave, willing to put himself in danger for his convictions[^56]. During the Mongol invasions, he personally participated in military campaigns and encouraged resistance when many other scholars fled to safety[^57]. His willingness to face powerful opponents, including Mamluk governors and chief judges, despite the personal cost demonstrated a fearless commitment to what he believed was truth[^58].

However, his personality also included traits that created difficulties in his relationships with other scholars. Contemporary accounts suggest he could be harsh and uncompromising in debate, unwilling to make diplomatic concessions even when they might have served his interests[^59]. His absolute certainty in his positions and willingness to condemn those who disagreed as innovators or deviants created numerous conflicts that might have been avoided with more diplomatic approach[^60].

Despite his controversies and imprisonments, Ibn Taymiyyah maintained a loyal circle of devoted students who preserved and transmitted his works. The most famous was Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya (1292-1350), who became a major scholar in his own right and shared his teacher's imprisonments while systematizing and popularizing Ibn Taymiyyah's ideas[^61]. Other students included al-Dhahabi, the renowned hadith scholar and historian, who studied under Ibn Taymiyyah while maintaining some critical distance from his more controversial positions[^62].

Accounts from his time in prison reveal a man who maintained his scholarly productivity even in confinement. During his various imprisonments, Ibn Taymiyyah continued to write, producing some of his most important works while incarcerated[^63]. When his writing materials were confiscated during his final imprisonment, contemporaries reported that he fell into depression, highlighting the central role that writing and teaching played in his life[^64].

He died in the Damascus Citadel on September 26, 1328 (20th of Dhu al-Qa'dah, 728 AH), while still imprisoned. Despite his controversial status, his funeral attracted an enormous crowd reported to number between 100,000 to 500,000 people, demonstrating his popular following even as authorities had condemned him[^65]. He was buried in Damascus's Sufi cemetery, an ironic resting place for someone who had spent much of his career criticizing Sufi practices[^66].

## Legacy

Ibn Taymiyyah's legacy has proven to be one of the most enduring and controversial in Islamic intellectual history, with his influence extending far beyond his medieval context to shape contemporary Islamic movements worldwide. For several centuries after his death, his works remained relatively marginalized within mainstream Sunni scholarship, which continued to be dominated by the Ash'ari theological school and the traditional madhab system he had challenged[^67]. However, his ideas experienced a major revival in the 18th century through Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab, founder of the Wahhabist movement in Arabia[^68].

The Wahhabi movement drew heavily on Ibn Taymiyyah's theology and his condemnation of practices it deemed innovations, including tomb visitation, saint veneration, and various Sufi practices[^69]. When the Saudi state adopted Wahhabism as its official religious doctrine, Ibn Taymiyyah's ideas gained state sponsorship and widespread dissemination throughout the Muslim world[^70]. This alliance between Wahhabi theology and Saudi political power transformed Ibn Taymiyyah from a controversial medieval figure into a foundational authority for a major contemporary Islamic current.

In the 20th and 21st centuries, Ibn Taymiyyah has been claimed as an intellectual forefather by various Islamic reform and revival movements. Salafi movements across the Muslim world regard him as a key authority, appreciating his emphasis on returning to scriptural sources and rejecting later innovations[^71]. His methodology of ijtihad and rejection of blind adherence to madhab doctrines has appealed to modernist reformers seeking to adapt Islam to contemporary contexts[^72].

However, Ibn Taymiyyah's legacy has been most controversial regarding his appropriation by extremist and militant movements. His fatwa declaring the Mongol rulers apostates despite their profession of Islam has been cited as precedent by groups like al-Qaeda and ISIS to justify violence against Muslim governments they deem insufficiently Islamic[^73]. His concept of "offensive jihad" and positions on rebellion against unjust Muslim rulers have been selectively quoted to support militant ideologies[^74]. This has led to extensive debate among scholars about whether such appropriations represent accurate readings of Ibn Taymiyyah's positions or distortions taken out of context[^75].

Contemporary mainstream Islamic scholars are divided on Ibn Taymiyyah's legacy. Traditional Sunni scholars, particularly those from Ash'ari and Sufi backgrounds, often criticize him as an extremist whose positions contradicted the mainstream of Islamic thought and practice[^76]. The Al-Azhar institution in Egypt, the premier center of Sunni learning, has generally maintained a critical stance toward Ibn Taymiyyah's theology[^77]. In 2010, a conference of international Islamic scholars in Jordan issued the "Amman Message," which implicitly criticized Takfiri ideology often associated with Ibn Taymiyyah's influence[^78].

Conversely, Salafi and reformist scholars defend Ibn Taymiyyah as a mujaddid (renewer) who courageously challenged corruption and innovation in Islamic practice and returned Muslims to authentic sources[^79]. They argue that his positions have been misunderstood and misappropriated by extremists, and that his actual teachings, properly understood, represent a balanced and orthodox Islam[^80]. This scholarly community has undertaken extensive work publishing and commenting on his voluminous writings, making his works more accessible than ever before[^81].

Ibn Taymiyyah's influence on Islamic legal methodology has been significant, particularly his emphasis on the continued validity of ijtihad and direct engagement with primary sources. His critique of blind following (taqlid) influenced modern Islamic reform movements that sought to bypass medieval scholarly traditions and engage directly with the Quran and hadith[^82]. This has had both progressive and conservative implications, enabling both reformist reinterpretations and literalist fundamentalism[^83].

His extensive writings on theology, jurisprudence, and philosophy continue to be studied, debated, and published throughout the Islamic world. Modern critical editions of his works have made his thought more accessible to contemporary scholars, while translations into various languages have extended his influence globally[^84]. Academic study of Ibn Taymiyyah has expanded significantly in recent decades, with scholars examining his thought in historical context and debating his contemporary relevance[^85].

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Supporters and admirers of Ibn Taymiyyah, both historical and contemporary, regard him as one of Islam's greatest scholars and a courageous reformer who stood against corruption and innovation in religious practice. His student Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya wrote extensively praising his teacher's knowledge, piety, and courage, describing him as the mujaddid (renewer) of the 8th Islamic century[^86]. Contemporary biographers celebrated his encyclopedic knowledge, noting that he had mastered not only Islamic sciences but also the writings of philosophers, theologians, and various religious sects in order to refute them effectively[^87].

Modern Salafi scholars view Ibn Taymiyyah as a key figure in the authentic Islamic tradition, praising his return to the methodology of the early Muslims (Salaf) and his rejection of later innovations that they believe corrupted Islamic practice[^88]. Sheikh Muhammad Nasiruddin al-Albani, the influential 20th-century hadith scholar, frequently cited Ibn Taymiyyah's works and praised his critical methodology in hadith sciences[^89]. Salafi scholars argue that Ibn Taymiyyah's positions on theology, particularly his literalist approach to divine attributes, represented the true position of early Muslims before it was obscured by later philosophical theology[^90].

Academic scholars have recognized Ibn Taymiyyah's significance as an original thinker who challenged dominant paradigms of his time. The French orientalist Henri Laoust, who wrote extensively on Ibn Taymiyyah, acknowledged him as "one of the most powerful personalities of Islam" whose thought represented an important alternative to Ash'ari scholasticism[^91]. Contemporary scholar Jon Hoover has argued that Ibn Taymiyyah's theological system was more sophisticated than critics acknowledge, representing a coherent alternative to Ash'ari theology rather than simple literalism[^92].

Defenders emphasize Ibn Taymiyyah's principled stand against injustice and his willingness to speak truth to power, even at great personal cost. His repeated imprisonments are viewed as testimony to his courage and integrity rather than evidence of extremism[^93]. Supporters note that he criticized rulers and religious authorities alike when he believed they violated Islamic principles, demonstrating independence from both political and scholarly establishments[^94].

Some scholars appreciate Ibn Taymiyyah's efforts to make Islamic knowledge more accessible by challenging the authority of established schools and encouraging direct engagement with primary sources. His emphasis on ijtihad has been credited with opening possibilities for Islamic intellectual renewal and adaptation to changing circumstances[^95]. Contemporary Islamic reformers have drawn on his precedent to argue for reinterpretation of Islamic law in light of contemporary needs while maintaining fidelity to scriptural sources[^96].

His intellectual productivity and the breadth of his scholarship are universally acknowledged, even by critics. The sheer volume and range of his writings—covering theology, jurisprudence, Quranic exegesis, hadith studies, philosophy, logic, and many other fields—demonstrate extraordinary learning and energy[^97]. His works continue to be mined for insights on various Islamic sciences, and his detailed analyses of theological and legal questions remain relevant to contemporary scholarship[^98].

### Negative Views

Critics of Ibn Taymiyyah, both medieval and modern, have condemned him as an extremist whose uncompromising positions and harsh polemics damaged Islamic unity and created precedents for religious intolerance. His contemporary opponents among Ash'ari theologians and Sufi scholars accused him of anthropomorphism in theology, innovation in jurisprudence, and divisiveness in community relations[^99]. The scholar Taqi al-Din al-Subki, a prominent contemporary, wrote refutations of Ibn Taymiyyah's positions and supported his imprisonment, viewing him as a dangerous deviant[^100].

Modern critics argue that Ibn Taymiyyah's influence has been predominantly negative, contributing to sectarianism and extremism in contemporary Islam. Scholar Khaled Abou El Fadl has written extensively criticizing Ibn Taymiyyah's legacy, arguing that his methodology of takfir (declaring Muslims as unbelievers) and his harsh attitudes toward theological differences have fueled contemporary extremism[^101]. Critics point to the frequent citation of Ibn Taymiyyah by terrorist groups like al-Qaeda and ISIS as evidence that his thought, even if misappropriated, contains dangerous elements that enable extremist interpretations[^102].

Academic scholars have criticized Ibn Taymiyyah's theological positions as inconsistent and problematic. His literalist approach to divine attributes, critics argue, ultimately collapses into the anthropomorphism he claimed to avoid, creating theological difficulties his defenders have never adequately resolved[^103]. His rejection of metaphorical interpretation (ta'wil) is seen as intellectually unsophisticated and unable to address genuine theological problems about divine transcendence[^104].

His harsh attacks on Sufism and condemnation of practices cherished by millions of Muslims have been criticized as intolerant and divisive. Critics note that his uncompromising stance on issues like tomb visitation and seeking intercession through saints disrupted communal harmony and created unnecessary conflict[^105]. Traditional Sunni scholars argue that his positions contradicted the established consensus (ijma') of the Muslim community and represented individual aberration rather than authentic tradition[^106].

Ibn Taymiyyah's political theology, particularly his positions on jihad and rebellion against Muslim rulers, has been criticized as dangerous and destabilizing. His fatwa against the Mongols, while defended by supporters as a necessary response to genuine apostasy, set a precedent that has been used to justify violence against Muslim governments and societies[^107]. Critics argue that his lowering of the bar for declaring Muslims as apostates has had catastrophic consequences for Muslim political life[^108].

Some scholars criticize his legal methodology as ultimately incoherent, claiming to follow the Salaf while actually introducing novel positions that earlier scholars had rejected. His claim to independent ijtihad is viewed by critics as arrogance that led him to contradict established scholarly consensus on numerous issues[^109]. Traditional madhab scholars argue that his approach undermined the stability and continuity provided by the established schools of law, replacing it with individualistic interpretation that lacked proper constraints[^110].

The intolerant tone of many of Ibn Taymiyyah's writings, characterized by harsh condemnations and broad accusations of innovation and unbelief against those who disagreed with him, has been criticized as unbecoming of a scholar and damaging to Islamic intellectual discourse[^111]. His polemical style, critics argue, prioritized winning arguments over seeking truth and fostered an adversarial approach to theological differences[^112].

Contemporary critics also note the selective appropriation of Ibn Taymiyyah's legacy by authoritarian regimes and conservative religious establishments to suppress dissent and enforce religious conformity. The alliance between Wahhabi theology and Saudi state power has been criticized as distorting Islam and exporting an intolerant version of the faith that marginalizes other Islamic traditions[^113].

## Famous Quotes

> "Whoever seeks guidance in other than the Book of Allah and the Sunnah of His Messenger, then Allah will lead him astray." - From his numerous fatwas emphasizing return to primary Islamic sources, reflecting his core methodology of basing religious understanding directly on scripture rather than later scholarly traditions.

> "The heart that is attached to anything other than Allah is veiled from Him by the measure of that attachment." - From his writings on spirituality and the heart, demonstrating that despite his criticism of Sufism, he addressed spiritual purification and God-consciousness.

> "There is no obedience to anyone in disobedience to Allah." - From his political writings on the limits of authority, establishing that religious obligations supersede political obedience when rulers command what contradicts Islamic law.

> "Whoever imitates a people is one of them." - Frequently cited statement about maintaining Islamic identity distinct from non-Muslims, used to prohibit imitation of non-Muslim practices and customs.

> "Knowledge is not by narrating much. Knowledge is fear of Allah, acting upon what He has revealed, and calling people to what He has sent down." - Reflecting his view that authentic Islamic knowledge must combine scriptural learning with piety and action, not mere academic accumulation.
