## Summary

Bank robbery in Fortaleza, Brazil on August 6, 2005 where thieves tunneled into the Central Bank and stole 164 million reais ($70 million). One of the largest bank robberies ever.

## How the burglary was carried out (high-level)

- Reporting on the case emphasizes that the theft relied on long-term preparation rather than a rapid, armed assault.
- The criminals dug a tunnel to reach the vault, a method designed to bypass alarms, guards, and street-level security.
- The operation is often described as involving logistical planning (tools, soil removal, ventilation, lighting) consistent with an organized crew.

## Targets and limitations

- The Central Bank facility in Fortaleza held large volumes of currency; cash-based theft creates challenges for tracing funds compared to electronic fraud.
- Large banknote quantities are physically bulky, which implies the presence of transport vehicles and staging locations.

## Investigation and recoveries

- Brazilian police investigations led to the arrest of some suspects in subsequent years, though attribution and the full network of participants have been subjects of ongoing reporting.
- In major cash robberies, authorities typically focus on:
  - tracking sudden asset purchases,
  - identifying rental properties used for staging,
  - analyzing communications between suspects,
  - tracing money laundering through businesses or intermediaries.
- Only a portion of stolen cash is commonly recovered in comparable cases, because currency can be dispersed quickly.

## Significance

- The case is frequently cited as one of the largest bank vault thefts in Brazil and a prominent example of “tunnel robbery” tactics.
- It contributed to discussions about physical security, perimeter monitoring, and the need for layered detection systems beyond the vault door itself.
