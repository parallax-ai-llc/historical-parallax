## Summary

Rotterdam is Europe's second-largest cocaine entry point after Antwerp. Drug-related violence has surged; PM Mark Rutte called the Netherlands a 'narco-state.'

## Background and Context

- **Port scale and connectivity**: The Port of Rotterdam is one of the largest ports in Europe, handling vast container volumes and providing extensive road, rail, and inland-waterway links into the European hinterland. Large-scale legitimate traffic creates opportunities for concealment and dilution of risk.
- **Regional trafficking dynamics**: Rotterdam is frequently discussed alongside nearby North Sea ports (notably Antwerp) as part of a broader logistics corridor used by transnational organized crime networks to move cocaine and other narcotics into Europe.

## How Smuggling Commonly Works (High-Level)

- **Container concealment**: Cocaine is commonly hidden in maritime containers among legitimate cargo, with traffickers relying on the sheer number of containers to reduce detection odds.
- **"Rip-on/rip-off" methods**: In some schemes, contraband is placed into a container after it has been packed by the legitimate shipper (rip-on) and later removed before delivery to the legitimate consignee (rip-off), attempting to avoid direct involvement of the cargo owner.
- **Insider facilitation**: Corruption or coercion of port-area workers and access to operational information (e.g., container location and movement timing) can enable rapid extraction and reduce exposure time.
- **Short-dwell extraction**: Quick retrieval in or near the port limits the window during which customs, terminal operators, or law enforcement can intervene.

## Drivers of Drug-Related Violence

- **Competition for shipments**: High-value cocaine consignments can trigger disputes between rival groups, including attempts to steal loads from each other.
- **Retaliation and intimidation**: Violence may be used to enforce debts, punish suspected theft, or intimidate perceived informants.
- **Collateral impacts**: Such disputes can spill beyond port areas into residential neighborhoods through threats, arson, or targeted attacks.

## Law Enforcement and Governance Responses

- **Customs inspections and targeting**: Authorities use risk-based profiling, scanning, and intelligence-led targeting to identify higher-risk containers.
- **International coordination**: Cooperation with European and international partners (including intelligence sharing and joint investigations) is often emphasized due to the cross-border nature of the supply chains.
- **Anti-corruption focus**: Measures aimed at reducing insider risk (screening, access controls, and investigation of corrupt facilitation networks) are commonly part of the response framework.

## Economic and Social Impact (High-Level)

- **Security costs**: Increased enforcement, surveillance, and compliance measures can raise operating costs for terminals and logistics providers.
- **Reputational risk**: A port repeatedly associated with large seizures and organized-crime activity can face reputational damage even while remaining a critical economic hub.
- **Community harm**: Drug-market violence and associated criminal economies can undermine local trust in institutions and increase perceived insecurity.
