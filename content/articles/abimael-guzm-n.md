## Summary

Peruvian communist revolutionary who founded and led the Shining Path (Sendero Luminoso), a Maoist insurgency that killed nearly 70,000 people. Known as 'Presidente Gonzalo,' he was captured in 1992 and died in prison.

## Ideology and Organization

Guzmán advanced a Maoist interpretation of revolution and framed armed struggle as the central path to political change in Peru. Under his leadership, Shining Path developed a highly centralized structure and a personality cult around his nom de guerre, “Presidente Gonzalo.”

## Insurgency and Impact

The conflict that followed involved insurgent attacks, assassinations, intimidation of local communities, and brutal counterinsurgency measures by state forces. Many assessments of the era emphasize that civilians bore the largest share of the violence and displacement.

## Capture, Trials, and Imprisonment

He was captured in Lima in 1992, after which the insurgency fragmented and declined. Guzmán was later tried and sentenced to life imprisonment; his continued symbolic role for remaining factions was a recurring issue in Peruvian politics and security policy.

## Legacy

In historical writing on late-20th-century Latin American insurgencies, Guzmán is often discussed as an example of ideological militancy combined with organizational ruthlessness, and as a case where state capacity and intelligence operations decisively altered the trajectory of an internal conflict.
