## Summary

Roman serial poisoner who served emperors as professional assassin. Allegedly poisoned Emperor Claudius and helped Nero murder Britannicus.

## Career Timeline

## Additional Context

- Ancient writers describe Locusta as a professional poisoner operating in the imperial court.
- Most detailed stories about her actions come from Roman historians writing after the events, so modern accounts treat some details as uncertain.

## Methods and Allegations

- Reported poisons in Roman sources include preparations derived from plants and animal toxins, administered in food or drink.
- Sources commonly associate her with the deaths of **Claudius** (reigned 41–54) and **Britannicus** (died 55), both connected to Nero's consolidation of power.
- Nero is said to have protected and rewarded her for successful poisonings, including giving her a form of training or a school for similar work.

## Downfall

- After Nero's death in 68, the new regime moved against figures tied to the prior court.
- Locusta is typically reported to have been executed under Emperor Galba (reigned 68–69).

## Legacy

- Locusta is often cited in popular histories as an example of how poison and court intrigue featured in elite Roman politics.
- Because the evidence is literary and politically charged, her story is also used as a case study in how Roman historians portrayed vice and tyranny.
