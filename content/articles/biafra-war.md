## Summary

Nigerian civil war caused by Biafra's secession that killed 1-3 million people. The Nigerian government used starvation as a weapon, blocking food shipments to Biafra.

## Background and causes (high level)

The conflict followed Nigeria’s independence (1960) and a period of acute political instability, including coups and communal violence.

Tensions between regions and ethnic groups, disputes over federalism, and fears of persecution contributed to the Eastern Region’s attempt to secede as the Republic of Biafra in 1967.

Control over oil-producing areas in the Niger Delta and questions of revenue allocation were also part of the broader political-economic context.

## Conduct of the war

The Biafra War was fought through conventional military campaigns as well as sieges and blockades that severely restricted the movement of food and medicine.

The humanitarian catastrophe in Biafra became internationally visible through images of mass malnutrition.

Debates over intent and responsibility often focus on the Nigerian government’s blockade policy, Biafra’s wartime administration and propaganda, and the difficulty of supplying civilians in an active war zone.

## Humanitarian response and international attention

The war is frequently cited as a turning point in modern humanitarianism, with large-scale involvement by international relief organizations and heightened media coverage.

The crisis also contributed to debates about humanitarian neutrality, the politicization of aid, and the limits of international intervention in sovereign civil conflicts.

## Aftermath and long-term impact

Biafra’s surrender in 1970 ended the secession, and the federal government declared a policy often summarized as “no victor, no vanquished,” though reintegration was complex and uneven.

The war’s legacy includes:

- Enduring political grievances and regional identity politics in southeastern Nigeria.
- Major demographic and social disruption from displacement and wartime deaths.
- Ongoing discussions about postwar reconstruction, memory, and accountability.

## Historical significance

The Biafra War remains one of the most studied African civil wars of the 20th century because it combines state formation challenges, identity-based conflict, and mass civilian suffering.

It is also frequently referenced in scholarship on famine, siege warfare, and how international media attention can shape foreign policy and humanitarian action.

## Notes on numbers and sources

Estimates of total deaths vary widely across sources, in part because many deaths were indirect (disease and starvation) and because wartime record-keeping was limited.
