## Summary

Felix Bloch shared the 1952 Nobel Prize for developing nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), which became the basis for MRI imaging.

## Scientific Contributions

Bloch is associated with multiple foundational ideas in 20th-century physics. In condensed-matter and solid-state physics, "Bloch's theorem" describes the wavefunctions of electrons in a periodic lattice, a key step in understanding band structure and electrical conduction in crystals.

## Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR)

Bloch shared the 1952 Nobel Prize in Physics (with Edward Purcell) for the development of methods for nuclear magnetic precision measurements. The work established core principles of NMR, including how nuclear spins respond to magnetic fields and radiofrequency pulses.

## From NMR to MRI

While medical magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) was developed later through advances in engineering and imaging techniques, it builds on NMR physics. Bloch's contributions are therefore often cited as part of the scientific lineage that ultimately enabled MRI as a clinical tool.

## Institutional Roles and Influence

Bloch spent much of his career at leading research institutions and trained or influenced generations of physicists. His name appears in several standard concepts and equations used in modern physics curricula.

## Key Terms

- **Bloch wave:** A quantum state in a periodic potential, expressed as a plane wave modulated by a lattice-periodic function.
- **Spin:** An intrinsic form of angular momentum carried by quantum particles, central to NMR phenomena.
- **Relaxation:** Processes (often labeled T1 and T2) describing how nuclear spins return to equilibrium after excitation.
