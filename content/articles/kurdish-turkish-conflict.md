## Summary

Ongoing armed conflict between Turkey and the PKK since 1984 that has killed over 40,000 people. The PKK seeks greater Kurdish autonomy in southeastern Turkey.

## Additional Context

### Parties and geography

- The conflict is primarily associated with the Turkish state and the Kurdistan Workers' Party (PKK), alongside related political and militia actors.
- It has been concentrated in southeastern Turkey, but its political and security effects have extended nationwide.
- Cross-border dimensions have periodically involved operations and militant activity linked to northern Iraq and, in later years, the Syrian civil war environment.

### Political attempts and breakdowns

- Multiple ceasefires and political initiatives have been attempted since the 1990s, with varying degrees of durability.
- The early-to-mid 2010s saw a notable peace process and negotiations, followed by a renewed escalation after the process collapsed.
- Domestic politics, regional conflicts, and security incidents have repeatedly influenced the incentives for both dialogue and confrontation.

### Human and institutional impact

- Beyond fatalities, the conflict has contributed to displacement, restrictions on civil liberties in certain periods, and long-term polarization.
- It has also shaped debates in Turkey about minority rights, decentralization, counterterrorism law, and the role of the military and security services.

### Why it remains significant

- One of the most enduring insurgency-state conflicts in the region, with implications for NATO ally security policy, migration dynamics, and regional geopolitics.
- Commonly referenced in studies of insurgency, peace processes, and the challenges of integrating armed actors into political settlements.
