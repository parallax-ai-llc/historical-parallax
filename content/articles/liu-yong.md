## Summary

Chinese gangster who ran a powerful organized crime syndicate in Shenyang with police protection. Executed after Supreme Court reinstated death sentence.

## Background (High Level)

- Liu Yong is described as a major organized-crime figure in Shenyang, Liaoning, during the late 1990s and early 2000s.
- Accounts of the case commonly emphasize the scale of his network and its influence over local businesses.

## Criminal Network and Alleged Protection

- The syndicate was associated with violence, intimidation, and control of illicit revenue streams.
- A prominent feature of the story is the allegation that elements of local law enforcement provided protection or failed to act, which raised broader concerns about corruption.

## Crackdown and Legal Proceedings

- Liu’s arrest occurred amid a crackdown on organized crime, with the case becoming a symbol of efforts to reassert state control over criminal groups.
- The court process is often noted because sentencing outcomes changed during appeals and review.

## Supreme Court Review and Execution

- The reinstatement of the death sentence by the Supreme People’s Court is frequently cited as a key moment, highlighting how higher-level judicial review can alter outcomes in capital cases.
- The execution in 2003 marked the end of a high-profile prosecution that had attracted national attention.

## Public Significance

- The case is frequently referenced in discussions about “protective umbrellas” (official protection for criminal groups) and the difficulty of dismantling entrenched local networks.
- It also appears in broader narratives about China’s periodic campaigns against organized crime and corruption.

## Key Takeaways

- Organized-crime enforcement can be complicated by corruption and informal protection networks.
- The Liu Yong case is remembered both for the scale of the syndicate and for the legal and political symbolism attached to the final sentence.
