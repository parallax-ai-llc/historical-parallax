## Summary

Wildfires destroyed the historic town of Lahaina, Maui, killing 101 people. Hawaiian Electric's downed power lines and inadequate emergency response contributed to the disaster.

## Context and Conditions

- Maui experienced unusually dry conditions in 2023, with invasive grasses providing fast-burning fuel.
- Strong winds associated with Hurricane Dora passing south of Hawaiʻi helped spread fires rapidly.
- Power distribution infrastructure and vegetation management around lines were scrutinized after the event.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- Multiple fires ignited on Maui on August 8, 2023; the Lahaina fire became the most destructive.
- Fire spread into Lahaina with limited time for evacuation as smoke and wind reduced visibility.
- Communications failures, power outages, and road congestion complicated escape and emergency operations.

## Human and Cultural Impact

- At least 101 people were killed, making it one of the deadliest U.S. wildfires in more than a century.
- Thousands of residents were displaced; many homes and local businesses were destroyed.
- Historic structures and cultural sites in Lahaina, a former capital of the Hawaiian Kingdom, were heavily damaged.

## Response and Recovery

- Federal, state, county, and volunteer organizations carried out search-and-recovery and provided shelter.
- Temporary housing, debris removal, and infrastructure restoration became multi-year recovery efforts.
- Public discussions focused on siren use, evacuation messaging, and emergency management coordination.

## Investigations and Accountability (Overview)

- Authorities opened investigations into ignition sources, emergency response decisions, and utility operations.
- Civil lawsuits were filed by survivors and families, including claims related to utility equipment and response.
- Official reports and litigation timelines continued beyond 2023 as evidence and findings developed.

## Key Takeaways (General)

- Wildfire risk can increase in island environments during drought and high-wind events.
- Early warnings, redundant communications, and evacuation route planning are critical in fast-moving fires.
- Utility hardening (vegetation clearance, shutoff protocols, grid resilience) is a recurring focus after major fires.
