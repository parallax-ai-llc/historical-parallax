## Summary

Nigerian writer and activist executed by the military government for leading protests against Shell Oil's environmental destruction in the Niger Delta. His death sparked international condemnation.

## Background and Context

Saro-Wiwa came from the Ogoni people, a minority ethnic group in Nigeria's Niger Delta, a region heavily affected by oil extraction. He was known not only for political activism but also for writing and television work, which gave him national and international visibility.

## Environmental and Human-Rights Activism

In the early 1990s he helped lead the Movement for the Survival of the Ogoni People (MOSOP), which organized campaigns calling attention to oil spills, gas flaring, and the economic marginalization of communities living in producing areas. The movement pressed for environmental remediation and a greater share of benefits for local residents.

## Trial and Execution

Saro-Wiwa and other Ogoni activists (often referred to as the "Ogoni Nine") were tried by a special tribunal under Nigeria's military government. The proceedings and verdict were widely criticized by international observers and human-rights organizations, who argued that due-process standards were not met.

On 10 November 1995, he was executed by hanging. The executions triggered diplomatic consequences, including Nigeria's suspension from the Commonwealth.

## International Reaction and Legacy

His death became a major symbol in global debates about corporate responsibility, environmental justice, and the treatment of dissidents under authoritarian regimes. Saro-Wiwa's case is frequently cited in discussions of the Niger Delta's long-running environmental crises and political conflicts.

## Key Themes

- Environmental justice in extractive economies and the distribution of costs and benefits.
- The relationship between multinational corporations, local communities, and the state.
- The use of international pressure (sanctions, suspensions, advocacy) in response to domestic human-rights cases.
