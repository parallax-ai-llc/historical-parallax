## Summary

Permanent international court established in 2002 to prosecute genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity. It has issued arrest warrants for leaders including Putin and al-Bashir.

## Founding and Legal Basis

- The ICC was created by the Rome Statute, a multilateral treaty that defines the court’s crimes, procedures, and relationship to states.
- The court is distinct from the International Court of Justice (ICJ): the ICC prosecutes individuals for international crimes, while the ICJ adjudicates disputes between states.

## Jurisdiction and Core Principles

- The ICC’s jurisdiction generally depends on territorial links, nationality links, or referrals (including from the UN Security Council).
- A central concept is “complementarity”: the ICC is intended to act when national legal systems are unwilling or unable genuinely to investigate and prosecute.

## Institutional Structure

- The court includes judicial chambers, an Office of the Prosecutor, and a Registry that manages administration, outreach, and support functions.
- Proceedings commonly involve pre-trial, trial, and appeals phases, with protections for defendants and provisions for victim participation in certain contexts.

## Practice, Impact, and Debate

- Supporters argue the ICC contributes to deterrence, accountability, and documentation of mass atrocities.
- Critics debate issues such as perceived politicization, uneven enforcement, challenges of arresting suspects, and the court’s reliance on state cooperation.

## Relationship to States

- Participation is through treaty membership; some major powers are not parties to the Rome Statute, which affects the court’s reach.
- State cooperation is crucial for arrests, evidence gathering, witness protection, and enforcement of sentences.
