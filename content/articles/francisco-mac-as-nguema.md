## Summary

First president of Equatorial Guinea who became one of Africa's most brutal dictators. His regime killed or drove into exile a third of the population. He banned use of the word 'intellectual' and destroyed the nation's economy.

## Rise to Power

- **Independence context**: Macías became president at Equatorial Guinea’s independence from Spain in 1968, during a period of rapid political transition.
- **Consolidation**: His rule is commonly described as moving quickly from post-independence politics into personalist authoritarian control.
- **Cult of personality**: Accounts of the period emphasize propaganda, loyalty tests, and a governing style centered on fear and personal authority.

## Governance and Repression

- **Human rights abuses**: Histories of Equatorial Guinea widely characterize his government as responsible for severe repression, including killings, imprisonment, and forced exile.
- **Institutional collapse**: State institutions, education, and professional life deteriorated significantly, contributing to long-term damage to administrative capacity.
- **Economic consequences**: The era is associated with economic breakdown and isolation, with many skilled workers fleeing the country.

## Overthrow, Trial, and Execution

- **1979 coup**: Macías was overthrown by Teodoro Obiang Nguema Mbasogo (his nephew), after which power shifted to a new regime.
- **Trial**: He was tried and convicted by the post-coup authorities, with the proceedings often referenced in discussions of post-authoritarian accountability in Africa.
- **Execution**: He was executed by firing squad in 1979.

## Legacy

- **National trauma**: His presidency is often remembered as a foundational trauma in Equatorial Guinea’s modern history.
- **Political continuity and change**: The transition to Obiang’s rule is debated in scholarship as both a break from Macías’s extreme violence and a continuation of authoritarian governance.
