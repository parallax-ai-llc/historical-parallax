## Summary

Marcus Ulpius Traianus, known as Trajan, was Roman Emperor from 98 to 117 CE and is remembered as the second of the "Five Good Emperors" of the Nerva-Antonine dynasty.[^1] He presided over one of the greatest military expansions in Roman history, bringing the empire to its maximum territorial extent.[^2] Honored with the title "Optimus Princeps" (the best ruler) by the Roman Senate, Trajan was renowned for his military conquests, extensive building projects, and enlightened governance.[^3] His cooperative relationship with the Senate and commitment to social welfare programs set him apart from many of his predecessors.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Trajan was born on September 18, 53 CE, in Italica, a Roman municipium in the province of Hispania Baetica (modern-day Andalusia, Spain), making him the first Roman emperor born outside of Italy.[^5] His family, the gens Ulpia, originally hailed from Tuder in the Umbria region of central Italy, though they had established themselves in Spain.[^6] His father, Marcus Ulpius Traianus, was a distinguished career soldier who rose to become governor of both Baetica and Syria, served as a commander during the Jewish War of 67-68 CE, and ultimately became a Roman senator and consul.[^7] His mother, Marcia, was a Roman noblewoman and the sister-in-law of Emperor Titus.[^8]

While documentation of Trajan's childhood remains sparse, historians believe he spent his early years in Italica before moving to Rome around age nine.[^9] It is likely that as the son of a prominent military commander, young Trajan moved frequently throughout the empire, accompanying his father to various military headquarters and provincial postings.[^10] This peripatetic upbringing exposed him to the realities of Roman military life and provincial administration from an early age, preparing him for his future role as emperor.

Trajan entered military service at a young age, following in his father's footsteps as was customary for sons of senatorial families. He served as a tribune under his father in Spain and commanded the Seventh Legion in northern Spain.[^11] In 76-77 CE, when his father was appointed governor of Syria, Trajan served there as tribunus legionis (military tribune).[^12] These early military experiences proved formative, developing both his tactical acumen and his reputation among the legions.

---

## Rise to Power

Trajan's ascent to imperial power began during the reign of Emperor Domitian. In January 89 CE, when the governor of Upper Germany, Antonius Saturninus, led a revolt against Domitian, the young Trajan commanded legions in Hispania Tarraconensis and marched swiftly to the Rhine to support the emperor.[^13] Though he arrived too late to participate in the actual suppression of the rebellion, Domitian rewarded his dedication and loyalty by appointing him praetor.[^14] This marked Trajan's entry into the highest echelons of Roman politics.

In 91 CE, despite his relatively young age, Trajan was appointed consul, likely benefiting from his father's prominence during the Flavian Dynasty.[^15] He subsequently served as governor of Germania and Pannonia, where he continued to build his military reputation and cultivate the loyalty of the legions along the crucial Rhine-Danube frontier.[^16]

The political landscape shifted dramatically on September 18, 96 CE, when Domitian was assassinated and replaced by the elderly, childless senator Nerva. Nerva's reign quickly proved unstable, particularly with regard to the Praetorian Guard and the army.[^17] After a revolt by members of the Praetorian Guard in 97 CE, Nerva recognized that his position had become untenable without the support of an heir who commanded the army's respect.[^18] Faced with this crisis, Nerva had little choice but to adopt a candidate with sufficient military experience, consular ancestry, and connections to secure his regime.[^19]

In October 97 CE, Nerva announced the adoption of Trajan as his heir and successor, claiming this decision was based entirely on Trajan's outstanding military merits.[^20] However, contemporary sources hint that the adoption may not have been entirely voluntary, suggesting it was effectively imposed on Nerva by military and political pressures.[^21] Regardless of the circumstances, when Nerva died of natural causes on January 28, 98 CE, Trajan succeeded to the imperial throne without opposition, marking the first instance of a non-Italian becoming Roman emperor.[^22]

## Major Achievements

### Military Conquests

Trajan's reign witnessed the Roman Empire's greatest territorial expansion, transforming him into one of Rome's most successful military commanders. His conquests added Dacia, Arabia, Armenia, and Mesopotamia to the empire, extending Roman control from the Atlantic Ocean to the Caspian Sea and briefly to the Persian Gulf.[^23]

**The Dacian Wars (101-106 CE)**

Trajan's most celebrated military achievement was the conquest of Dacia, a wealthy kingdom north of the Danube River (in modern-day Romania). The First Dacian War (101-102 CE) began when Trajan led a massive offensive across the Danube using a pontoon bridge constructed by his brilliant engineer, Apollodorus of Damascus.[^24] The Romans won decisive victories at Tapae and Adamclisi, and by 102 CE, the Dacian king Decebalus was forced to accept peace terms, establishing a Roman garrison at the Dacian capital of Sarmizegetusa.[^25]

When Decebalus violated the peace treaty, Trajan launched the Second Dacian War (105-106 CE). This campaign featured extraordinary engineering feats, including Trajan's Bridge across the Danube—a wooden superstructure supported by twenty massive stone pillars, spanning 1,135 meters and representing one of the greatest engineering achievements of antiquity.[^26] The Romans captured Sarmizegetusa, and Decebalus committed suicide to avoid capture.[^27] Dacia became a Roman province, with colonists from across the empire settling the new territory.[^28]

The conquest proved immensely profitable, as Dacia possessed rich gold and silver mines. Contemporary chroniclers estimated that Trajan obtained half a million pounds of gold and a million pounds of silver from the conquest, with the Dacian mines contributing an estimated 165-700 million denarii annually to the Roman economy.[^29] To celebrate this triumph, Trajan announced 123 days of public celebrations throughout the empire, featuring approximately 10,000 gladiators and 11,000 animals in spectacular games.[^30]

**The Parthian Campaign (113-117 CE)**

In his final years, Trajan embarked on an ambitious campaign against the Parthian Empire, provoked by Parthia's decision to install an unacceptable king on the Armenian throne without Roman approval.[^31] In 114 CE, Trajan invaded Armenia, annexed it as a Roman province, and killed Parthamasiris, who had been placed on the throne by Parthian king Osroes I.[^32]

The campaign continued with remarkable initial success. In 115 CE, Roman forces overran northern Mesopotamia, and in 116 CE they captured the Parthian capital of Ctesiphon before sailing down the Euphrates to the Persian Gulf.[^33] However, the conquest proved unsustainable. Revolts erupted across the Eastern Mediterranean, North Africa, and northern Mesopotamia, while a major Jewish uprising known as the Kitos War severely stretched Roman military resources.[^34] Parthian forces counterattacked, evicting Roman garrisons from Seleucia, Nisibis, and Edessa.[^35] The overall scarcity of manpower and the overextension of Roman supply lines meant the campaign was ultimately doomed.[^36]

### Architectural and Engineering Achievements

Trajan's building program ranks among the most ambitious in Roman history. Working primarily with the architect Apollodorus of Damascus, Trajan transformed Rome's urban landscape and infrastructure throughout the empire.[^37]

**Trajan's Forum and Column**

Trajan's Forum, the last and largest of the Imperial fora, was built between 105 and 112 CE using spoils from the Dacian conquest.[^38] The project required excavating over 300,000 cubic meters of soil and rock to eliminate a ridge connecting the Quirinal and Capitoline Hills.[^39] The complex included a massive rectangular piazza with porticoes, the Basilica Ulpia (one of the largest basilicas in Rome), two libraries (one for Latin and one for Greek texts), Trajan's Market (a multi-level retail and office complex considered a precursor to modern shopping malls), and Trajan's Column.[^40]

Erected in 113 CE, Trajan's Column stands approximately 38 meters high (including its pedestal) and is constructed from 20 colossal Carrara marble drums, each weighing about 32 tons.[^41] The column's exterior features a continuous spiral frieze depicting scenes from the Dacian Wars, with over 2,600 detailed figures providing an invaluable visual record of Roman military equipment, tactics, and engineering.[^42]

**Infrastructure Development**

Trajan undertook extensive public works throughout the empire: roads, bridges, aqueducts, land reclamation projects, and harbor construction.[^43] His road-building program enhanced military mobility, provincial administration, and commercial activity. The Via Traiana Nova, built after the annexation of Arabia, connected Bostra to Aila on the Red Sea.[^44] These infrastructure improvements not only facilitated governance and trade but also provided employment for the lower classes.[^45]

### Social Welfare Programs

Trajan established one of the ancient world's most sophisticated social welfare systems. Around 98 CE, he created the alimenta, a program providing financial support to children of impoverished freeborn citizens throughout Italy.[^46] The system was ingeniously funded through low-interest loans to Italian landowners, with the interest payments generating steady income to support orphans and poor children while simultaneously stimulating agricultural development.[^47] The program also received funding from Dacian War booty, estate taxes, and private philanthropy.[^48]

Beyond the alimenta, Trajan increased grain distributions to poor citizens, provided cash gifts to the populace, allowed provinces to retain gold remittances normally sent to the emperor, and reduced taxes.[^49] These policies demonstrated an early recognition of state responsibility for vulnerable citizens and contributed to social stability and loyalty to the regime.[^50]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his reputation as one of Rome's greatest emperors, Trajan's reign has attracted significant criticism from both ancient and modern historians, particularly regarding his aggressive expansionism and its long-term consequences for the empire.

### Military Overextension

The most substantial criticism concerns Trajan's Parthian campaign. Historian Moses Finley described it as a "badly miscalculated and expensive assault on Parthia," while others have characterized it as exemplifying questionable military adventurism driven by megalomaniacal desires for glory.[^51] The conquest of Mesopotamia proved an overambitious goal that overstretched Roman resources and extended supply lines beyond sustainable limits.[^52] Significantly, Trajan raised no new legions before the Parthian campaign, suggesting the overall scarcity of manpower doomed the enterprise from the start.[^53]

The rapid advances into Mesopotamia provoked widespread local uprisings, including the devastating Kitos War (115-117 CE), a major Jewish revolt that erupted across Egypt, Cyrenaica, Cyprus, and Mesopotamia.[^54] This rebellion resulted in immense bloodshed and severely strained Roman military capacity at a critical moment.[^55] A campaign of ethnic cleansing led to the near-complete extermination of Jewish communities from Cyrenaica and Cyprus.[^56] Parthian forces successfully counterattacked key Roman positions, and Trajan failed to capture the strategic city of Hatra despite repeated attempts.[^57]

### Policy Reversal

Trajan is renowned for abandoning the policy established by Augustus and generally adhered to by subsequent emperors of not extending the Roman frontiers beyond defensible limits.[^58] This reversal of Augustan restraint created expectations of continued expansion that his successor Hadrian rejected. Four generals disappointed by Hadrian's retreat from Trajan's conquests plotted to overthrow the new emperor, suggesting the destabilizing effect of Trajan's aggressive policies.[^59]

Edward Gibbon, writing in the 18th century, offered a penetrating critique: "Trajan was ambitious of fame; and as long as mankind shall continue to bestow more liberal applause on their destroyers than on their benefactors, the thirst of military glory will ever be the vice of the most exalted characters."[^60] Gibbon argued that even so great a ruler as Trajan could not free himself from the craving for glory, and that subsequent emperors would have done well to follow Augustus's example of moderation rather than pursuing further conquests.[^61]

### Long-Term Consequences

While Trajan's conquest of Dacia brought immediate wealth, many historians argue it ultimately became a drain on imperial resources. Over time, as Roman military strength declined, the exposed province proved difficult to defend.[^62] Modern assessments recognize that while Dacia and Arabia created real benefits, the Parthian conquest was "ephemeral and wasteful," with gains proving short-lived.[^63]

### Religious Policy

Trajan's correspondence with Pliny the Younger regarding Christians, while more moderate than some later persecutions, nonetheless established precedents for anti-Christian policy. Around 112 CE, Pliny, serving as governor of Bithynia-Pontus, wrote to Trajan describing his interrogation and execution of Christians who refused to recant.[^64] Trajan's response instructed that Christians should not be actively sought out, but if denounced and proven guilty, they should be punished unless they renounced Christianity and worshiped Roman gods.[^65] While this represented a pragmatic rather than systematic persecution, it legitimized the execution of Christians for religious obstinacy.[^66]

## Personal Life

Trajan married Pompeia Plotina before becoming emperor, and their union is widely regarded as one of the most harmonious marriages in Roman imperial history.[^67] Most historians agree they shared genuine affection and mutual respect, though the marriage produced no known children.[^68]

Plotina was possibly born in Nemausus (modern Nîmes, France) during Emperor Nero's reign or in the 70s CE, and was raised in Tejada la Vieja in Hispania.[^69] She proved to be a formidable and intellectual woman with interests spanning literature, mathematics, music, and charitable works.[^70] Renowned for her virtue, dignity, simplicity, and devotion to Epicurean philosophy, Plotina earned great respect during her lifetime for advocating the people's interests.[^71]

Upon entering the imperial palace, Plotina reportedly turned to address the crowd, stating that she desired always to remain the same person she was then—a declaration that captured her commitment to humility and continuity of character.[^72] Noted for her modesty, she refused to accept the title of augusta (empress) until 105 CE, seven years after Trajan became emperor.[^73]

Plotina took particular interest in the Epicurean philosophical school in Athens, and in 121 CE engaged Emperor Hadrian in correspondence to discuss the selection of a new head for the school.[^74] She played a crucial role in Hadrian's life, arranging his marriage to Vibia Sabina and becoming his friend and advocate—reportedly regarding him as a substitute for the son she never had.[^75]

Regarding Trajan's personal character, ancient sources are unanimously positive. Pliny the Younger's Panegyric celebrates him as a wise, just, and moral ruler, while Cassius Dio added that Trajan always remained dignified and fair.[^76] He was respected for leading by example rather than through fear, embodying the ethical ideal developed by Imperial Roman political writers.[^77]

## Legacy

Trajan's legacy proved remarkably enduring, shaping Roman imperial ideology for centuries and influencing how subsequent generations understood effective governance.

### Immediate Impact

Trajan's death on August 8-9, 117 CE, from a stroke and dropsy (edema) at Selinus in Cilicia marked the end of Rome's greatest territorial expansion.[^78] He had fallen seriously ill after the failed siege of Hatra, suffered a debilitating stroke at Antioch, and died while attempting to return to Rome.[^79] The city of Selinus was temporarily renamed Trajanopolis in his honor.[^80] He was deified by the Senate, and his ashes were placed beneath Trajan's Column—the only emperor to be buried within Rome's sacred pomerium.[^81]

His adopted heir Hadrian immediately reversed Trajan's expansionist policies, abandoning most of the Parthian conquests and focusing on consolidating and defending existing territories.[^82] This strategic reorientation, while pragmatic, disappointed those who had embraced Trajan's vision of endless expansion.[^83]

### Long-Term Influence

In the Later Roman Empire, particularly following the crises of the third century, Trajan, together with Augustus, became the paragon of imperial virtue. Fourth-century emperors were honored by the Senate with the acclamation "felicior Augusto, melior Traiano" (May you be more fortunate than Augustus and better than Trajan)—a formula that persisted for centuries.[^84] This traditional senatorial prayer testified to Trajan's enduring status as the standard against which all subsequent emperors were measured.[^85]

Trajan's architectural legacy remained highly visible. Trajan's Column still stands in Rome today, its detailed friezes continuing to provide invaluable historical evidence about Roman military practices and Dacian culture.[^86] Trajan's Forum served as the architectural climax of the Imperial fora, representing the apex of Roman public architecture.[^87] The Markets of Trajan, preserved in substantial form, offer modern visitors insight into ancient commercial life.[^88]

His infrastructural contributions—roads, bridges, harbors, and aqueducts—facilitated trade and administration throughout the empire for generations.[^89] The social welfare programs he established, particularly the alimenta, demonstrated the potential for state-sponsored relief systems and influenced subsequent imperial policy.[^90]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Ancient sources on Trajan's personality and accomplishments are remarkably uniform in their praise. Pliny the Younger's Panegyric, delivered in 100 CE, presents Trajan as the ideal emperor: moderate, accessible, respectful of senatorial prerogatives, and committed to the common good.[^91] Cassius Dio, writing in the early third century, portrayed Trajan as dignified, fair, and possessed of genuine virtue rather than mere pretense.[^92]

The Senate's bestowal of the title "Optimus Princeps" reflected genuine admiration for Trajan's governance style. This title, appearing on coins from 105 CE onward, signified "the best ruler" and acknowledged his role as benefactor, his return of confiscated property, and his preference for ruling through exemplary behavior rather than fear.[^93] According to the political ethics developed by Imperial Roman writers, Trajan embodied the virtuous autocrat who exercised power responsibly.[^94]

Medieval Christian writers offered surprisingly positive assessments despite Trajan's paganism and anti-Christian correspondence with Pliny. Dante Alighieri, in the Divine Comedy, placed Trajan in Purgatory rather than Hell, depicting him as a just ruler whose concern for a poor widow exemplified proper governance—an extraordinary honor for a pagan emperor.[^95]

Modern scholars have often echoed these positive assessments. Trajan's cooperative relationship with the Senate represented a marked improvement over the antagonism that characterized relations under emperors like Domitian.[^96] His administrative reforms, including the professionalization of the Roman bureaucracy, merit-based appointments, and dispatch of special governors to financially troubled provinces, enhanced governmental efficiency and accountability.[^97] His extensive building program provided both practical benefits and employment, while his social welfare initiatives demonstrated enlightened governance.[^98]

### Negative Views

Modern historical scholarship has developed more nuanced and critical perspectives on Trajan's reign, particularly regarding his military campaigns and their strategic wisdom.

Gibbon's 18th-century critique identified military ambition as Trajan's "besetting vice," arguing that what Romans considered an essential princely virtue represented a fundamental character flaw.[^99] Gibbon contended that mankind's tendency to applaud destroyers more than benefactors corrupted even the best rulers, and that Trajan's thirst for military glory exemplified this tragic pattern.[^100] However, modern historians recognize that Gibbon wrote with limited archaeological evidence and through the lens of 18th-century Enlightenment values and British imperialism, which colors his judgments.[^101]

Contemporary scholars offer more empirically grounded criticisms. Many historians argue that Trajan's Parthian campaign represented strategic overreach that overstrained imperial resources because of megalomaniacal desires for glory.[^102] The failure to raise new legions before undertaking such an ambitious campaign revealed poor strategic planning.[^103] The rapid collapse of Roman control in Mesopotamia after Trajan's death demonstrated that the conquests were unsustainable.[^104]

Some scholars question whether even the celebrated Dacian conquest was ultimately beneficial. While the immediate influx of precious metals enriched the treasury, the exposed province eventually became difficult to defend as Roman military strength declined.[^105] Critics argue that Trajan abandoned the sensible Augustan policy of establishing defensible frontiers in favor of prestige-driven expansion.[^106]

The Kitos War (115-117 CE), which erupted during Trajan's Parthian campaign, resulted in catastrophic bloodshed and the near-extermination of Jewish communities in Cyrenaica, Cyprus, and parts of Egypt.[^107] While Trajan did not directly order the ethnic cleansing that occurred, his overstretched military commitments created the conditions in which such violence could erupt and spread.[^108]

Modern assessments thus recognize Trajan's reign as mixed in its legacy: while his domestic governance, social programs, architectural achievements, and relationship with the Senate were exemplary, his late military adventures represented strategic miscalculations with serious long-term consequences for imperial stability.[^109]

## Famous Quotes

> "Use it for me, or, if I deserve it, against me." - Trajan to a captain of the guards when presenting him with a sword, establishing that imperial power must be subject to justice.[^110]

> "Were I still young, I would've crossed to India also." - Reportedly said after reaching Babylon during the Parthian campaign, revealing both his ambition and recognition of physical limitations.[^111]

> "Now be comforted, for I must carry out my duty before I go on: Justice requires it and pity holds me back." - Literary representation by Dante Alighieri in the Divine Comedy, depicting Trajan's response to a poor widow seeking justice, symbolizing the emperor's reputation for accessible justice.[^112]

The Senate's acclamation for new emperors: > "Felicior Augusto, melior Traiano" (May you be more fortunate than Augustus and better than Trajan) - Traditional senatorial prayer that endured for centuries, establishing Trajan as the permanent standard of imperial excellence.[^113]
