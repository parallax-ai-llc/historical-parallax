## Summary

French colonial war killed an estimated 1.5 million Algerians. French forces used systematic torture, napalm, and concentration camps to suppress Algerian independence.

## Background and Causes

- **Colonial rule and inequality:** Algeria was colonized by France in 1830 and integrated as departments of France, while political rights and land ownership were heavily skewed toward European settlers (pieds-noirs).[^1]
- **Rise of nationalism:** Algerian nationalist movements grew after World War II, including demands for self-determination and equal rights; repression of protests helped radicalize politics.[^1]

## Main Actors and Phases

- **FLN vs. French state:** The National Liberation Front (FLN) led the independence insurgency; France deployed large conventional forces, police units, and intelligence services.[^1]
- **Urban and rural conflict:** Fighting included guerrilla warfare in rural areas and high-profile urban operations (notably the Battle of Algiers, 1956–1957).[^1]
- **Regional and diplomatic dimension:** The conflict was shaped by Cold War politics and decolonization, with international attention at the United Nations and support networks in neighboring countries.[^1]

## Methods, Violence, and Humanitarian Impact

- **Counterinsurgency and coercion:** French counterinsurgency relied on mass arrests, interrogation, and population control measures; documented abuses included systematic torture.[^1]
- **Civilian displacement:** Large-scale relocations and "regroupment" camps disrupted communities and livelihoods.[^1]
- **Contested casualty estimates:** Death toll estimates vary widely across sources; many accounts cite hundreds of thousands of Algerian deaths, with some higher estimates also reported.[^1]

## Outcomes and Legacy

- **Independence:** The war concluded with the 1962 independence of Algeria following the Évian Accords (ceasefire in 1962).[^1]
- **Exodus and reprisals:** The end of the war triggered mass departure of many European settlers and violence affecting groups accused of collaboration, including some Harkis.[^1]
- **Political repercussions in France:** The crisis contributed to the fall of the Fourth Republic and the return of Charles de Gaulle, reshaping French politics.[^1]
- **Memory and reckoning:** The war remains a major subject of debate over responsibility, state violence, and recognition of victims in both Algeria and France.[^1]
