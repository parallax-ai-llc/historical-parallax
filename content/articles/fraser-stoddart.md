## Summary

Fraser Stoddart is a British-American chemist who shared the 2016 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for molecular machines.

## Additional Context

### Molecular machines (high level)

- “Molecular machines” refers to engineered chemical systems in which components can move relative to each other in controllable ways (for example, rings threaded on axles or switches that change configuration).
- Stoddart’s work is especially associated with mechanically interlocked molecules such as rotaxanes and catenanes, where topology enables motion without breaking covalent bonds.

### Why it mattered

- These concepts helped expand chemistry from making static structures to designing systems with function, responsiveness, and controllable dynamics.
- The broader field has potential applications in materials science, nanotechnology, and molecular-scale devices, although many applications remain experimental.

### Academic and institutional roles

- Nobel laureates in this area often built interdisciplinary programs spanning organic synthesis, supramolecular chemistry, and physical characterization.
- Stoddart’s career is frequently described as combining synthetic creativity with an emphasis on conceptual design and modular assembly.

## Selected Concepts

- **Supramolecular chemistry:** using non-covalent interactions (host–guest binding, π–π stacking, hydrogen bonding) to assemble complex systems.
- **Mechanically interlocked molecules:** structures whose components are linked by topology rather than a single covalent bond.
- **Switches and shuttles:** designs where a ring or component can move between sites in response to stimuli.
