## Background

Sextus Tarquinius is presented in Roman tradition as one of the sons of Lucius Tarquinius Superbus (Tarquinius the Proud), the final king of Rome.
He is chiefly known through later literary accounts that use the story to explain (and morally justify) Rome’s transition from monarchy to republic.

## The Lucretia Episode (Traditional Account)

In the canonical version of the legend, Sextus assaults Lucretia, a noblewoman celebrated for her virtue.
After disclosing the attack to her family, Lucretia takes her own life, and her death becomes a rallying point for political revolt.
The uprising is associated with Lucius Junius Brutus and culminates in the expulsion of the Tarquins.

## Sources and Historicity

The main surviving narratives are found in later historians and antiquarians, especially Livy and Dionysius of Halicarnassus.
Modern scholarship often treats the story as partly legendary: it preserves early Roman political memory but is shaped by moral and political themes rather than contemporary documentation.

## Legacy

The figure of Sextus Tarquinius became a lasting symbol in Roman and later Western writing of tyrannical abuse and sexual violence linked to political illegitimacy.
The Lucretia narrative also influenced later discussions of civic virtue, the ethics of resistance to tyranny, and the founding myths of republican government.

## Key People (Traditional Narrative)

- **Lucretia**: Roman matron whose death becomes the catalyst for revolt.
- **Lucius Junius Brutus**: Leading figure in the expulsion of the Tarquins and early Republic.
- **Tarquinius Superbus**: Last king of Rome and Sextus’s father in the tradition.

## Political Function of the Story

Roman writers used exemplary stories (exempla) to communicate civic values and political lessons. In that frame, the Lucretia narrative is often read as:

- a moral condemnation of royal arrogance and impunity
- a founding explanation for why Romans rejected monarchy
- a justification story that links private violence to public revolution

Even when treated as legend rather than strict history, it shaped how Romans narrated their own constitutional identity.

## Reception and Later Influence

The Lucretia story, with Sextus as its villain, appears repeatedly in later European cultural history, including:

- Renaissance and early modern discussions of republicanism and tyranny
- art and literature centered on Lucretia as a figure of chastity, honor, and political symbolism
- moral and legal debates about sexual violence and coercion

This reception is part of why Sextus Tarquinius remains known far beyond what the limited early evidence for the period would otherwise allow.

## Notes on Names and Chronology

Because the narrative is transmitted through later sources, details such as exact dates, family relationships, and sequence of events can vary across retellings. Modern summaries typically keep the account at a high level, focusing on the story’s role in the transition myth from kingship to republic.
