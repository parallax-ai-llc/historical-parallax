## Summary

Cho Ju-bin and others ran encrypted chat rooms where they blackmailed dozens of women and girls (including minors) into producing sexual content. 260,000 users paid for access.

## Context

The Nth Room case refers to a set of crimes organized through online messaging platforms, widely reported to have involved Telegram-based chat rooms and a monetized access structure. Victims were coerced through threats, doxxing, and other forms of blackmail into producing illegal content, which was then distributed to paying participants.

## How the Scheme Operated (High Level)

Public summaries commonly describe several elements:

- Recruitment and coercion of victims through online contact
- Use of encrypted or semi-private chat rooms to distribute material
- Tiered rooms and payment mechanisms for access
- Community dynamics where participants encouraged escalation and anonymity

These descriptions emphasize the role of platform features and informal digital economies in enabling large-scale abuse.

## Investigation and Prosecution

South Korean authorities investigated the networks, identified key operators, and pursued prosecutions. Cho Ju-bin (also known as "Baksa") became the most prominent defendant in widely covered trials.

The case is also associated with efforts by journalists, activists, and online communities to identify perpetrators and pressure institutions to respond more forcefully.

## Social and Legal Impact

The Nth Room case became a major public issue in South Korea, influencing debates on:

- Digital sex crimes and victim protection
- Platform responsibilities and moderation
- Stronger penalties for production, distribution, and possession
- Procedures for takedown, evidence preservation, and investigative capacity

It is frequently cited as a turning point in public awareness of technology-facilitated sexual exploitation and the scale of online participation.
