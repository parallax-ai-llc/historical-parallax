## Summary

Bosnian Serb convicted by the ICTY as the first person tried for war crimes since Nuremberg. He committed atrocities at the Omarska and Keraterm camps in Prijedor during the Bosnian War. Sentenced to 20 years.

## Why the Case Was Pivotal

The Tadić proceedings at the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY) are commonly described as a modern “test case” for international criminal justice. They helped establish practical precedents for:

- How an international tribunal conducts arrest, transfer, and trial
- The relationship between individual criminal responsibility and organized campaigns of violence
- The use of witness testimony and documentary evidence for crimes committed during conflict

## Crimes and Legal Concepts (High Level)

Public summaries of the judgment emphasize allegations of abuse and killings in detention-camp settings. The case is also often referenced for clarifying how courts analyze:

- **Crimes against humanity** as widespread or systematic attacks on civilians
- The distinction between direct perpetration and participation (aiding, abetting, or joint action)

## Legacy

As the first ICTY trial to reach judgment, the case became a touchstone in legal scholarship and for later prosecutions at the ICTY and other international courts. It is frequently cited when tracing the post–Cold War revival of war-crimes trials and the development of procedure in international tribunals.

## Impact on International Criminal Procedure

Commentary on the case often emphasizes that early ICTY trials had to build working practice for disclosure, witness protection, translation, and cross-examination in a multilingual post-conflict setting. Those procedural lessons later informed how other tribunals and hybrid courts designed rules to balance defendant rights with the safety of witnesses.
