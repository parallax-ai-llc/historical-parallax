## Summary

English pirate who pioneered the 'Pirate Round' route from the Western Atlantic to the Indian Ocean. Killed in battle against Mughal forces when a cannonball disemboweled him.

## The "Pirate Round"

The "Pirate Round" was a maritime route used by late-17th-century pirates and privateers to reach the Red Sea and Indian Ocean from the Atlantic, often by sailing around the Cape of Good Hope. It was attractive because it opened access to rich shipping lanes connected to the Mughal Empire, the Arabian Sea, and trade moving between India, the Middle East, and East Africa.

Thomas Tew is often credited in popular accounts with helping to popularize this route, demonstrating that relatively small crews could attempt high-value captures far from European naval enforcement.

## Piracy in the Indian Ocean context

Indian Ocean piracy in this era targeted merchant vessels carrying bullion, textiles, and other high-value cargo. These raids contributed to diplomatic tensions, triggered armed responses from regional powers, and eventually increased pressure on European states to suppress piracy that threatened trade.

The Mughal Empire, whose merchants and pilgrims traveled by sea, had strong incentives to respond forcefully to attacks, and clashes with pirates were not only criminal matters but also geopolitical incidents.

## Death and legacy

Tew’s death in 1695 is reported as occurring during combat in the Indian Ocean when he was struck by cannon fire. Accounts of his end became part of pirate lore, used to dramatize the risks of the trade even for successful captains.

Historically, Tew is remembered less for a long career than for his association with the early expansion of transoceanic piracy into the Indian Ocean and for the example it set for later pirate crews.
