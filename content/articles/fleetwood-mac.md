## Summary

Fleetwood Mac is a British-American rock band formed in London in July 1967 by guitarist Peter Green, drummer Mick Fleetwood, and guitarist Jeremy Spencer. Over nearly six decades, the group underwent dramatic lineup changes while maintaining commercial relevance across multiple eras of popular music. Their 1977 album *Rumours* became one of the best-selling albums in history, cementing their place as one of rock's most enduring acts.

The band's unique quality lies in its ability to reinvent itself. Beginning as a British blues outfit, Fleetwood Mac transformed into a California soft-rock powerhouse in the mid-1970s following the addition of Lindsey Buckingham and Stevie Nicks. This evolution was driven as much by personal turbulence as by musical ambition, producing some of the most emotionally raw and commercially successful records of the era.

## Early Life & Background

Fleetwood Mac was founded in London in July 1967, emerging from the British blues boom of the late 1960s. Peter Green, Mick Fleetwood, and John McVie — veterans of John Mayall's Bluesbreakers — formed the core of the original lineup alongside Jeremy Spencer. The band signed to Blue Horizon Records and released their self-titled debut in 1968, which reached number four on the UK Albums Chart. Early singles such as "Albatross" (1968) reached number one in the UK.

## Rise to Prominence

After Peter Green's departure in 1970 following a mental health crisis linked to LSD use, the band cycled through several lineups. Christine McVie joined as a full member in 1970. The pivotal shift came in 1974 when Mick Fleetwood recruited Los Angeles-based duo Lindsey Buckingham and Stevie Nicks. Their 1975 self-titled album reached number one in the United States and produced hit singles including "Rhiannon" and "Say You Love Me," selling over five million copies in the US alone.

The follow-up, *Rumours* (1977), recorded amid the romantic breakdowns of Buckingham/Nicks and the McVies' divorce, spent 31 weeks at number one on the Billboard 200. It has sold approximately 40 million copies worldwide, making it one of the top-selling albums ever recorded.

## Key Achievements

- *Rumours* (1977) won the Grammy Award for Album of the Year in 1978.
- The album produced four US top-ten singles: "Go Your Own Way," "Dreams," "The Chain," and "You Make Loving Fun."
- "Dreams" became the band's only US number-one single and returned to the top of streaming charts in 2020 following a viral TikTok video.
- The band was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1998.
- Their total album sales exceed 120 million worldwide.

## Controversies & Criticism

Internal conflict defined much of the band's history. Buckingham's acrimonious exit in 2018 — reportedly following a dispute with Nicks — led to litigation that was settled out of court. Peter Green's struggles with mental illness in the early 1970s, including a period of hospitalization, raised difficult questions about how the band and industry handled his deterioration. Critics have also noted that the band's post-*Tango in the Night* (1987) output failed to match the artistic heights of their 1970s peak.

## Legacy & Influence

Fleetwood Mac's influence spans soft rock, pop, and alternative music. Artists including Haim, Sheryl Crow, and Taylor Swift have cited the band as a primary influence. The 2020 viral resurgence of "Dreams" demonstrated the band's continued cultural pull across generations. Mick Fleetwood and John McVie remain the only constant members, maintaining the band's identity through more than 50 years of personnel changes, commercial highs, and personal lows.
