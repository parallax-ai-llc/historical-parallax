## Summary

ISIS attacked the Yazidi community in Sinjar killing 5,000 men and enslaving thousands of women and children. The UN recognized the attacks as a genocide against the Yazidi people.

## Additional Context

### Background

Sinjar (Shingal) is an area in northern Iraq with a significant Yazidi population, a religious minority that has faced repeated persecution over centuries. During the rapid territorial expansion of ISIS in 2014, Sinjar became a target as ISIS sought to consolidate control and impose its ideology through violence.

### The attack and immediate consequences

Reports from survivors and investigations describe mass killings, abductions, and forced displacement. Many Yazidis fled toward Mount Sinjar, where large groups were trapped without adequate food, water, or medical care, creating an acute humanitarian crisis.

### Captivity, enslavement, and abuses

Thousands of Yazidi women and children were abducted and subjected to forced marriage, sexual slavery, and other forms of abuse. The systematic nature of these crimes has been cited in legal and human-rights documentation as evidence of intent to destroy the Yazidi community as such.

### Humanitarian response and military developments

International and regional actors conducted emergency airdrops and facilitated evacuation corridors as fighting continued in the area. Over time, ISIS was pushed back from many parts of Iraq, but the recovery for survivors and the return of displaced communities remained slow and uneven.

### Accountability and recognition

Multiple bodies, including UN mechanisms and national courts, have treated ISIS’s actions against the Yazidis as genocide and crimes against humanity. Efforts to document mass graves, identify victims, and prosecute perpetrators have continued, alongside debates over reparations and long-term support for survivors.

### Ongoing impacts

- Long-term displacement, trauma care needs, and family reunification efforts
- Continued work to identify missing persons and preserve evidence for prosecutions
