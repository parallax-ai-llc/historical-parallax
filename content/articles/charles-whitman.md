## Summary

American mass murderer who shot and killed 14 people and wounded 31 from the University of Texas tower in Austin on August 1, 1966. He had previously killed his wife and mother. An autopsy found a brain tumor. Shot dead by police.

## Background

Whitman was a former U.S. Marine and a student at the University of Texas at Austin. In the months leading up to the attack he experienced severe stress and reported intense headaches; later commentary has discussed possible medical and psychological contributors, but no single factor fully explains the violence.

## 1966 University of Texas tower attack

On August 1, 1966, Whitman first killed his wife and his mother in separate locations, leaving notes that indicated a desire for them to be spared suffering and a request for an autopsy on himself. He then went to the University of Texas tower carrying firearms and ammunition and opened fire from an elevated position, killing and wounding people on and around campus.

The event is often cited as an early, widely televised mass shooting in the United States. It also highlighted how difficult it can be for first responders to locate and stop a shooter firing from a fortified vantage point.

## Death, autopsy, and investigation

Whitman was confronted and shot by police inside the tower. An autopsy identified a brain tumor; while it has been discussed as a potential contributing factor, investigators could not establish a definitive causal link between the tumor and his actions.

## Aftermath and legacy

The attack influenced law-enforcement thinking about rapid response, coordination, and marksman capabilities for “active shooter” incidents. The University of Texas and other institutions also reassessed campus safety practices and the security of elevated public spaces.
