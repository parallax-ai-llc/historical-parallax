## Summary

System of racial segregation laws in the southern United States that enforced white supremacy and denied basic rights to Black Americans for nearly a century.

## What "Jim Crow" Covered

Jim Crow laws were not a single statute but a broad, interlocking set of state and local rules that enforced racial hierarchy.
They operated alongside social intimidation and economic coercion, making "separate" rarely equal in practice.

## Common Mechanisms

- **Segregation mandates:** Separate schools, transport, housing, and public accommodations.
- **Voter suppression:** Poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, and violent intimidation reduced Black political participation.
- **Criminalization and forced labor:** Selective policing and convict leasing systems helped exploit Black labor after Reconstruction.

## Legal Architecture

The era is closely associated with Supreme Court decisions such as *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896), which upheld “separate but equal,” and later reversals including *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954).
Federal civil-rights legislation and enforcement, especially in the 1960s, dismantled the legal basis for segregation.

## Legacy

The consequences included persistent wealth and education gaps, residential segregation patterns, and long-term distrust of legal institutions.
Many historians treat Jim Crow as a continuation of racial control after slavery, adapted to new economic and political conditions.
