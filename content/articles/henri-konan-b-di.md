## Summary

Former President of Ivory Coast who promoted the xenophobic concept of 'Ivoirité' and was overthrown in a coup for corruption. His policies contributed to decades of ethnic conflict.

## Background and early roles

Bédié was a senior figure in Ivorian politics long before becoming president, serving in top legislative roles during the long period of one-party dominance after independence.

## Presidency (1993–1999)

- **Succession:** He became president after the death of Félix Houphouët-Boigny in 1993, inheriting a political system built on patronage networks and a strong executive.
- **Political climate:** His tenure coincided with rising debates over nationality, citizenship, and electoral eligibility in a country with large migrant communities.

## “Ivoirité” and political exclusion

“Ivoirité” is commonly described as a political doctrine that emphasized a restrictive definition of Ivorian identity.

High-level effects that are frequently cited in reporting and scholarship include:

- raising the salience of ethnic and regional identity in politics
- sharpening disputes over who qualified as “fully” Ivorian for purposes of office-holding
- deepening polarization ahead of later crises in the 2000s

## Corruption allegations and 1999 coup

Bédié’s administration faced recurring allegations of corruption and misuse of public funds. In December 1999, he was overthrown in a military coup led by General Robert Guéï, ending the country’s first post-independence civilian-to-civilian succession.

## Later political career

After being removed from office, Bédié remained an influential opposition figure for years and continued to play a role in coalition politics and party leadership.

## Legacy (high-level)

Assessments of Bédié’s legacy often focus on two themes:

1. **Institutional continuity vs. accountability:** the transition after 1993 and the durability of patronage structures.
2. **Identity politics:** how “Ivoirité” contributed to long-running arguments over citizenship and belonging.
