## Summary

Suicide bombing at Moscows Domodedovo airport on January 24, 2011 that killed 37 people. The bombing was carried out by a North Caucasus militant.

## Background

Domodedovo International Airport is one of Moscow's major airports and a high-traffic civilian transport hub. Attacks on transportation nodes are often designed to maximize casualties and psychological impact, and to demonstrate the ability of militants to strike symbolic or heavily used public spaces.

## Attack Overview

The bombing occurred in an arrivals area, where crowds can be dense and security screening may be less stringent than at departure checkpoints. Suicide bombings in such locations typically aim to exploit the concentration of passengers, greeters, and staff.

## Perpetrator and Militant Context

The incident has been linked in reporting to the North Caucasus insurgency, a long-running conflict involving militant groups and Russian security forces. In this broader context, attacks in major Russian cities have periodically been used to project violence beyond the immediate region of conflict.

## Casualties and Public Response

Fatalities and injuries from mass-casualty terrorism incidents often generate intense public scrutiny of airport security procedures, emergency response coordination, and intelligence failures. Following such attacks, governments commonly introduce heightened screening, expanded surveillance, and institutional reviews.

## Investigations and Prosecutions

Major terrorism investigations usually involve identifying accomplices, tracing logistics and financing, and mapping communications networks. Court proceedings in these cases can include multiple defendants and charges beyond the attack itself (such as weapons possession, conspiracy, or membership in banned organizations).

## Broader Significance

The Domodedovo attack is frequently cited in discussions about:

- counterterrorism policy and the balance between security and civil liberties
- security design for airports and other critical infrastructure
- the spillover effects of regional insurgencies into national capitals
