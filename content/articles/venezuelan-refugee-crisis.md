## Summary

Over 7.7 million Venezuelans have fled economic collapse and political repression under Maduro. The largest displacement crisis in Latin American history.

## Additional Context

## Drivers of displacement

Analyses commonly link the outflow to overlapping pressures: a severe economic contraction and hyperinflation, deteriorating public services (including health care), shortages of basic goods, insecurity, and political conflict. The crisis is often discussed alongside Venezuela’s governance challenges and international sanctions debates.[^1]

## Regional impact

Most Venezuelan migrants and refugees have moved to neighboring Latin American countries, especially Colombia, Peru, Ecuador, Chile, and Brazil. Host countries have periodically adjusted visa and regularization policies in response to changing migration flows and domestic politics.[^1]

## Humanitarian and protection concerns

Large-scale displacement raises protection risks such as exploitation, trafficking, lack of access to legal status, and barriers to health care and education. International agencies have framed the situation as both a refugee protection issue and a broader mixed-migration challenge.[^1]

## Policy responses

Responses have included temporary protection or regularization programs, humanitarian aid appeals, and multilateral coordination efforts. The effectiveness of these policies tends to depend on funding levels, administrative capacity, and the willingness of governments to sustain inclusive labor-market and social policies.[^1]

## Data caveats

Headline numbers (for example, totals of people who have left) are updated frequently and can vary depending on definitions, data sources, and whether they count migrants, refugees, asylum seekers, or people in irregular status.[^1]
