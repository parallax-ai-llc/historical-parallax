## Summary

Stephen Colbert is an American comedian, writer, and television host best known for hosting *The Late Show with Stephen Colbert* on CBS and for his decade-long run as the fictional conservative pundit "Stephen Colbert" on Comedy Central's *The Colbert Report* (2005–2014). He is one of the most influential figures in American late-night television and political satire, having shaped the way a generation engaged with news and media criticism.

Colbert's cultural impact extends beyond entertainment. His persona-driven satire on *The Colbert Report* contributed to mainstream discourse on media bias, political spin, and the concept of "truthiness" — a word he coined in 2005 that was named Merriam-Webster's Word of the Year. His transition to CBS marked a significant shift in the late-night landscape, consistently drawing millions of viewers and frequently ranking as the top-rated late-night program during politically charged periods.

## Early Life & Background

Stephen Tyrone Colbert was born on May 13, 1964, in Washington, D.C., and grew up in James Island, South Carolina, as the youngest of eleven children. When Colbert was ten years old, his father James and two of his brothers were killed in the crash of Eastern Air Lines Flight 212 on September 11, 1974 — a tragedy that profoundly shaped his worldview and, by his own account, his embrace of humor as a coping mechanism.

He attended Porter-Gaud School in Charleston before enrolling at Hampden-Sydney College in Virginia, later transferring to Northwestern University, where he studied theater. At Northwestern, he became involved with the improvisational comedy scene that would define his early career.

## Rise to Prominence

After graduating in 1986, Colbert moved to Chicago and trained at Second City, the legendary improvisational theater. He joined *The Daily Show with Jon Stewart* in 1997 as a correspondent, where his deadpan delivery and satirical instincts flourished. In 2005, Comedy Central gave him his own spinoff, *The Colbert Report*, in which he portrayed an egotistical, right-wing cable news anchor — a direct parody of figures like Bill O'Reilly.

*The Colbert Report* ran for nine years, earning Colbert widespread acclaim and multiple Emmy Awards. His 2006 performance at the White House Correspondents' Dinner — delivering pointed satire directly in front of President George W. Bush — became a landmark moment in American political comedy. In 2015, he succeeded David Letterman as host of *The Late Show*, debuting on CBS on September 8, 2015.

## Key Achievements

- Won multiple Emmy Awards for *The Colbert Report* and *The Late Show*
- Coined "truthiness," named Merriam-Webster's Word of the Year in 2006
- *The Late Show* became the most-watched late-night program in the U.S. during 2016–2017 and again during the Trump presidency
- Delivered the 2006 White House Correspondents' Dinner keynote address, widely regarded as one of the most daring performances in the event's history
- Co-authored several books, including *I Am America (And So Can You!)* (2007), a New York Times bestseller

## Controversies & Criticism

Colbert has faced criticism for blurring the line between satire and journalism, with some arguing his partisan comedy reinforces political polarization rather than challenging it. In 2017, a monologue referencing President Trump and Russian President Vladimir Putin prompted the FCC to investigate a potential indecency violation, though no action was ultimately taken. Some critics have also noted that his transition from sharp satirist to conventional late-night host has softened his edge.

## Legacy & Influence

Colbert's *Colbert Report* persona helped pioneer a form of meta-media satire that influenced shows like *Last Week Tonight with John Oliver* and *Full Frontal with Samantha Bee*, both hosted by former *Daily Show* alumni. His concept of "truthiness" entered the cultural lexicon as a prescient diagnosis of post-truth political culture. As host of *The Late Show*, he demonstrated that politically engaged, intellectually substantive content could thrive in mainstream network television, consistently outperforming competitors during periods of heightened civic interest.
