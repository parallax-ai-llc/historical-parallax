## Summary

Boko Haram commander who orchestrated the 2011 bombing of the UN headquarters in Abuja killing 23 people. He was reportedly killed by his own fighters.

## Background

Mamman Nur is described in open-source reporting as a senior figure associated with Boko Haram and later with the Islamic State-aligned faction often referred to as ISWAP. The broader insurgency in northeastern Nigeria emerged from a mix of ideological radicalization, local grievances, heavy-handed security responses, and regional instability around the Lake Chad Basin.

## Role in Boko Haram / ISWAP dynamics

Accounts frequently place Nur among the cadre involved in strategic planning and external-facing operations. Within Boko Haram, leadership and factional disputes have periodically reshaped the organization, including splits between groups favoring mass-casualty attacks on civilian targets and those emphasizing territorial control, taxation, and selective coercion.

## 2011 UN headquarters bombing

The bombing of the United Nations building in Abuja in 2011 was widely treated as a landmark escalation, signaling capability and intent to strike high-profile international targets in Nigeria’s capital. It also drew intensified domestic and international counterterrorism attention.

## Internal conflict and reported death

Nur’s reported death is commonly linked to internal power struggles and disciplinary actions within militant factions. In insurgent movements, such claims can be difficult to confirm independently, and competing narratives may circulate for strategic reasons; nevertheless, multiple reports describe him as having been killed by fellow fighters.
