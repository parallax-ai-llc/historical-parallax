Sudanese warlord and slave trader who conquered a vast empire in Central Africa.

## Background

- Rabih az-Zubayr (also spelled Rabih az-Zubair) was born in the mid-19th century in what is now Sudan and became associated with the commercial-military networks that expanded westward from the Nile region.
- He emerged from the orbit of Zubayr Rahma Mansur (Zubayr Pasha), a prominent trader and military leader whose forces operated across parts of the southern Sudan and surrounding regions.

## Expansion and Rule in Central Africa

- In the late 19th century Rabih led armed campaigns toward the Lake Chad basin, building a mobile army and extracting resources through raiding, taxation, and the capture of enslaved people.
- After defeating regional rivals, he established control over parts of the Bornu (Kanem–Bornu) sphere and installed a new political order centered on military force and tribute.
- His administration depended on garrisons, loyal commanders, and control of trade routes linking Central Africa to markets farther north and east.

## Military and Political Significance

- Rabih’s conquests reshaped the balance of power around Lake Chad at a moment when multiple empires and colonial expeditions were competing for influence.
- His state is often described as an armed polity that blended older Sahelian political structures with the practices of 19th-century frontier warlords.

## Conflict with French Colonial Forces

- As French expeditions pushed into the Chad region, Rabih became one of their most formidable opponents.
- The confrontation culminated in 1900 at the Battle of Kousséri, where Rabih was killed; the clash was also notable for the death of the French commander Amédée-François Lamy.

## Legacy

- Rabih is remembered for both the scale of his military achievements and the brutality of the slave-raiding economy that underpinned his power.
- His defeat helped open the way for consolidated French colonial rule in the Lake Chad basin.
