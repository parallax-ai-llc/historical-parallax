## Summary

Omar Khayyam (1048-1131) was a Persian polymath whose contributions spanned mathematics, astronomy, philosophy, and poetry. Born in Nishapur during the Seljuk period, he revolutionized algebra with his geometric solutions to cubic equations and led the reform of the Persian calendar, creating one of the most accurate calendars in history. While renowned in the Islamic world as a mathematician and astronomer, Khayyam achieved global fame through Edward FitzGerald's 1859 translation of his quatrains, the *Rubáiyát*, which portrayed him as a hedonistic skeptic. His life embodied the intellectual flourishing of medieval Persia, bridging scientific rigor with philosophical inquiry and poetic expression.

---

## Early Life and Background

Omar Khayyam was born on May 18, 1048, in Nishapur, the capital of Khorasan province in northeastern Persia (modern-day Iran). His full name, Ghiyāth al-Dīn Abū al-Fatḥ ʿUmar ibn Ibrāhīm Nīshāpūrī al-Khayyāmī, reveals his family's occupation—his surname "Khayyam" derives from *khayyām*, meaning "tentmaker," suggesting his father Ibrahim was a craftsman in this trade[^1]. However, the family's ability to provide young Omar with extensive education indicates they possessed sufficient means and valued intellectual pursuits.

Nishapur during Khayyam's youth was a thriving center of Islamic learning, housing numerous libraries, observatories, and gathering places for scholars. The city served as a crucial waystation on the Silk Road, facilitating not only trade but also the exchange of knowledge between East and West[^2]. This cosmopolitan environment exposed the young Khayyam to diverse philosophical and scientific traditions, including Greek geometry, Indian mathematics, and Islamic theological discourse.

Khayyam received his early education from local scholars in Nishapur, studying the Quran, Islamic law (fiqh), Arabic literature, and the fundamentals of mathematics and natural philosophy. Historical accounts suggest he was a precocious student with an exceptional memory and analytical mind[^3]. By his teenage years, he had mastered the works of Euclid and was delving into the complexities of Aristotelian philosophy and Ptolemaic astronomy.

A pivotal influence was the mathematician and philosopher Abu Ali al-Hasan ibn al-Haytham (Alhazen), whose works on optics and methodology Khayyam studied intensively. Additionally, he engaged with the philosophical writings of Avicenna (Ibn Sina), whose reconciliation of Aristotelian philosophy with Islamic thought would profoundly shape Khayyam's own intellectual trajectory[^4]. According to some biographical accounts, Khayyam briefly studied in Balkh and Samarkand, expanding his network of scholarly contacts and accessing different manuscript collections.

The Seljuk conquest of Persia in the mid-11th century initially brought political instability but eventually ushered in a period of relative security and patronage for the arts and sciences. Khayyam came of age during this transition, witnessing both the fragility of worldly power and the enduring value of knowledge—themes that would permeate his later poetry[^5].

## Rise to Prominence

Khayyam's ascent from provincial scholar to internationally recognized mathematician began in his early twenties with the completion of his groundbreaking treatise *Treatise on Demonstration of Problems of Algebra* (1070). This work, completed when he was just 22 years old, systematically classified cubic equations and provided geometric solutions using conic sections—an achievement that would not be surpassed until the 16th-century algebraic solutions of Italian mathematicians[^6]. The treatise demonstrated not only mathematical virtuosity but also philosophical sophistication, as Khayyam critiqued earlier approaches and outlined the epistemological foundations of algebraic reasoning.

Word of this brilliant young mathematician reached the Seljuk court, and around 1073, Khayyam was invited to Isfahan by the vizier Nizam al-Mulk, who served Sultan Malik-Shah I. Nizam al-Mulk was himself a remarkable figure—a statesman who recognized that cultural and scientific achievement enhanced the legitimacy and glory of Seljuk rule[^7]. He established a network of educational institutions (*nizamiyya* madrasas) across the empire and gathered leading scholars to the court.

In Isfahan, Khayyam was appointed to lead an ambitious project: the reform of the Persian calendar. The existing calendar had accumulated significant errors over centuries, causing the seasonal festivals, particularly the vernal equinox celebration of Nowruz, to drift from their astronomical moorings[^8]. Sultan Malik-Shah commissioned a new observatory in Isfahan, providing Khayyam with state-of-the-art astronomical instruments and a team of skilled assistants.

Over the course of several years, Khayyam and his colleagues made precise observations of the solar year, determining its length to be 365.24219858156 days—remarkably close to the modern value of 365.242190 days[^9]. The resulting Jalali calendar, named after the sultan (whose given name was Jalal al-Din), was implemented in 1079 and proved more accurate than both the Julian calendar then used in Europe and the later Gregorian reform of 1582[^10].

Khayyam's prestige at court grew considerably following this success. He received generous stipends that freed him from financial concerns and allowed him to pursue research in multiple fields. He wrote extensively on philosophy, particularly metaphysics and natural philosophy, engaging with the works of Aristotle and Avicenna. His philosophical writings demonstrated a rationalist bent and a willingness to question received wisdom, sometimes bringing him into conflict with religious authorities[^11].

However, Khayyam's position remained dependent on courtly patronage. When Nizam al-Mulk was assassinated by an Ismaili agent in 1092, followed shortly by Sultan Malik-Shah's death the same year, Khayyam lost his primary protectors. The political turmoil that ensued forced him to temporarily leave Isfahan and undertake the Hajj pilgrimage to Mecca—possibly as much for political safety as religious devotion[^12]. Though he later returned to court service under successive rulers, he never regained the same level of influence and resources.

## Major Achievements

### Mathematical Innovations

Omar Khayyam's most significant mathematical contribution was his systematic treatment of cubic equations in his *Treatise on Demonstration of Problems of Algebra*. Before Khayyam, Islamic mathematicians had developed sophisticated techniques for solving linear and quadratic equations, but cubic equations remained largely unsolved[^13]. Khayyam classified cubic equations into 25 different types (14 of which could be solved) and demonstrated that each type could be solved geometrically using the intersection of conic sections—circles, parabolas, hyperbolas, and ellipses.

This geometric approach was revolutionary in several respects. First, it provided actual solutions rather than mere approximations. Second, it demonstrated the unity of algebra and geometry, anticipating the later analytic geometry of Descartes by five centuries[^14]. Third, Khayyam explicitly argued that these equations could not be solved using compass and straightedge alone—a profound insight into the limitations of Euclidean methods that would not be rigorously proven until the 19th century[^15].

Beyond cubic equations, Khayyam made important contributions to the theory of ratios and proportions, extending the work of Euclid and the 10th-century mathematician al-Karaji. He also worked on the binomial theorem, discovering a method for extracting roots and calculating binomial coefficients that paralleled similar discoveries in China (Pascal's triangle)[^16]. His *Commentary on the Difficulties in the Postulates of Euclid's Book* tackled the parallel postulate, attempting to prove it from Euclid's other axioms—work that anticipated non-Euclidean geometry by seven centuries[^17].

### Astronomical Accomplishments

The Jalali calendar reform stands as Khayyam's most publicly visible achievement. The calendar's structure was ingenious: it consisted of a 33-year cycle containing 8 leap years arranged in a specific pattern, resulting in an average year length of 365.2424 days[^18]. This made it more accurate than the Gregorian calendar's average of 365.2425 days. The calendar aligned the new year (Nowruz) with the vernal equinox, maintaining the connection between the civil and astronomical year.

Beyond calendar reform, Khayyam made observations and calculations regarding planetary motions, star positions, and solar and lunar eclipses. His astronomical tables (*zij*) were used by subsequent generations of astronomers throughout the Islamic world[^19]. He also wrote treatises on the astrolabe and other astronomical instruments, improving their design and explaining their proper use.

Khayyam approached astronomy not merely as a computational discipline but as a philosophical inquiry into the structure of the cosmos. He grappled with the relationship between Ptolemaic mathematical astronomy and Aristotelian natural philosophy, recognizing tensions between the two that would not be resolved until the Copernican revolution[^20].

### Philosophical Works

Khayyam's philosophical writings, though less celebrated than his mathematics or poetry, reveal a sophisticated thinker engaged with the deepest questions of existence, knowledge, and ethics. His *Treatise on Being and Necessity* explores metaphysical questions about the nature of existence, necessity, and contingency within an Islamic framework influenced by Aristotelian and Neoplatonic thought[^21].

In *On the Knowledge of the Universal Principles of Existence*, Khayyam addresses epistemological questions: How can we know universal truths? What is the relationship between sense perception and rational knowledge? His answers display both rationalist confidence in human reason and awareness of its limitations[^22]. He defended philosophy against those religious scholars who saw it as a threat to faith, arguing that reason and revelation, properly understood, could not contradict each other.

Khayyam's philosophical stance has been characterized as "rational theism"—accepting the existence of God and religious law while insisting on the autonomy of philosophical and scientific inquiry[^23]. However, some of his statements, particularly those found in the *Rubáiyát*, suggest skepticism toward religious dogma and conventional piety, leading to accusations of heterodoxy during his lifetime and scholarly debate ever since.

### Literary Legacy

The *Rubáiyát* of Omar Khayyam consists of quatrains (*rubā'ī*, plural *rubā'iyāt*) in Persian that explore themes of mortality, the fleeting nature of life, the value of present experience, and skepticism toward religious certainty. The attribution of specific quatrains to Khayyam is contested—manuscripts contain anywhere from dozens to over a thousand quatrains attributed to him, and medieval Persian literary culture allowed for considerable fluidity in authorship[^24].

Edward FitzGerald's 1859 English translation transformed these Persian verses into a Victorian literary sensation. FitzGerald's *Rubáiyát* was not a literal translation but a creative reimagining that selected, combined, and adapted quatrains to create a unified philosophical and emotional narrative[^25]. The work went through multiple editions, each expanding and refining FitzGerald's vision of Khayyam as a hedonistic philosopher advocating for wine, pleasure, and skepticism in the face of mortality and religious hypocrisy.

Lines such as "A Jug of Wine, a Loaf of Bread—and Thou" and "The Moving Finger writes; and, having writ, Moves on" became proverbial in English[^26]. The *Rubáiyát* influenced the Pre-Raphaelites, the Arts and Crafts movement, and later the counterculture of the 1960s. It established Khayyam in Western imagination as a freethinking skeptic and sensualist—an image that both celebrated and distorted his historical reality.

## Controversies and Criticisms

### Religious Heterodoxy

Throughout his life and for centuries after, Omar Khayyam faced accusations of religious heterodoxy, skepticism, and even atheism. His rationalist approach to philosophy and apparent questioning of religious certainties made him suspect to orthodox religious scholars[^27]. Some quatrains attributed to him openly mock religious hypocrisy and conventional piety, suggesting that scholars who claim certainty about the afterlife know no more than anyone else.

Contemporary accounts indicate that Khayyam was investigated for heresy at least once during his lifetime. The 12th-century historian Nizami Arudi reported that Khayyam was "notorious" for his irreligious views and had to be careful about expressing his true opinions publicly[^28]. The philosopher al-Ghazali, though not mentioning Khayyam by name, criticized rationalist philosophers whose approach resembled Khayyam's, arguing that their reliance on reason undermined religious faith.

The extent of Khayyam's actual religious skepticism remains debated. Some scholars argue that the freethinking persona of the *Rubáiyát* is largely FitzGerald's Victorian creation, and that the historical Khayyam was a conventional Muslim who expressed doubts within acceptable philosophical parameters[^29]. Others contend that the quatrains, even if their exact attribution is uncertain, reflect genuine currents of skepticism in Khayyam's thought, supported by remarks in his philosophical writings about the limitations of religious knowledge[^30].

### Philosophical Esotericism

Related to questions of religious orthodoxy is the issue of esotericism in Khayyam's work. Some scholars suggest that Khayyam, like other medieval Islamic philosophers, employed a double discourse: an exoteric teaching for the masses that conformed to religious orthodoxy, and an esoteric teaching for select students that expressed more radical philosophical positions[^31].

If true, this makes interpreting Khayyam's actual beliefs difficult. His philosophical treatises often include conventional religious affirmations, but these might be protective camouflage rather than sincere belief. Critics of this interpretive approach argue that it allows scholars to project modern secular values onto medieval thinkers by dismissing their religious statements as insincere while accepting their skeptical statements as authentic[^32].

### Attribution Controversies

The question of which quatrains were actually written by Omar Khayyam has generated scholarly controversy for over a century. Medieval Persian literary culture did not have modern concepts of authorship and plagiarism; verses circulated orally and in manuscripts with fluid attribution[^33]. Popular poets might have verses attributed to them that were written by admirers, students, or later poets working in their style.

The earliest manuscripts containing *rubā'iyāt* attributed to Khayyam date from a century or more after his death. A manuscript from 1207 contains about 60 quatrains attributed to him; later manuscripts contain hundreds more[^34]. Modern scholarship has attempted to establish an authentic core of Khayyam's poetic work through philological analysis, but consensus remains elusive.

This controversy matters because the *Rubáiyát* is the primary source for the popular image of Khayyam as a hedonist and skeptic. If many of the most skeptical and wine-celebrating quatrains were actually written by later poets, the historical Khayyam might have been quite different from his literary persona[^35].

### Reception in the Islamic World

While Omar Khayyam achieved iconic status in the West following FitzGerald's translation, his reception in the Islamic world, particularly in Iran, has been more complicated. Some Iranian intellectuals and poets celebrated him as a freethinker who challenged religious dogma, particularly during periods of secularization in the 20th century[^36]. However, religious authorities and conservative scholars often viewed him suspiciously, seeing the celebration of Khayyam as an attack on Islamic values.

After the 1979 Iranian Revolution, Khayyam's legacy became contested political terrain. Some reformist intellectuals invoked him as part of an Iranian intellectual tradition of questioning and rational inquiry, while conservative religious authorities emphasized his mathematical and scientific achievements while downplaying or rejecting the heterodox quatrains[^37]. Debates about Khayyam thus became proxy debates about Iran's relationship to its pre-Islamic past, the role of Islam in national identity, and the balance between tradition and modernity.

## Personal Life

Details about Omar Khayyam's personal life are fragmentary and often contradictory, filtered through biographical accounts written decades or centuries after his death. The 12th-century historian Nizami Arudi, who claimed to have met Khayyam in his old age, provides some of the most detailed personal anecdotes, though their reliability is uncertain[^38].

Khayyam apparently never married or had children, unusual for a man of his social position in medieval Persian society. Some biographers suggest he may have married briefly in his youth, but no reliable records exist[^39]. His celibacy or lack of family life has been interpreted various ways: as evidence of ascetic philosophical dedication, as personal preference, or perhaps as social isolation resulting from his heterodox reputation.

Contemporary accounts describe Khayyam as reserved and sometimes difficult in personal relationships. He reportedly had few close friends and could be sardonic or dismissive toward those he considered intellectual inferiors[^40]. However, he maintained warm relationships with fellow scholars, particularly those who shared his philosophical interests. His friendship with the scientist and philosopher Bahmanyar ibn Marzban is documented in several sources[^41].

Nizami Arudi recounts visiting Khayyam in Nishapur late in the mathematician's life, finding him in a garden reading Avicenna's *Metaphysics*. When a severe windstorm arose, Khayyam carefully marked his place in the manuscript, stating that he would read no further as this was his last day of life—and indeed, he died that evening[^42]. While this story is likely apocryphal or embellished, it reflects how contemporaries viewed Khayyam: as a man devoted to philosophical study until his final breath.

Regarding Khayyam's relationship with wine—a constant theme in the *Rubáiyát*—historical evidence is ambiguous. The literal drinking of wine was prohibited in Islam but widely practiced among educated elites in medieval Persia, often in semi-private gatherings[^43]. Persian poetry of the period frequently used wine as both literal subject and mystical metaphor for divine intoxication or spiritual knowledge. Whether Khayyam's wine poetry reflects actual hedonism, mystical symbolism, or philosophical metaphor remains debated[^44].

One legend, recorded by multiple sources, claims that Khayyam requested to be buried in a spot where spring blossoms would fall on his grave. His tomb in Nishapur reportedly fulfilled this wish, located beneath pear and peach trees[^45]. This story, whether factual or not, captures the romantic association between Khayyam and the celebration of natural beauty and life's fleeting pleasures.

## Legacy

### Scientific and Mathematical Impact

Omar Khayyam's mathematical work influenced Islamic scholarship for centuries after his death. His classification and solution of cubic equations was studied by later mathematicians throughout the Islamic world and eventually reached Europe, where it influenced Renaissance algebraists[^46]. The Italian mathematician Gerolamo Cardano, who published algebraic solutions to cubic equations in the 16th century, may have had access to translations or summaries of Khayyam's geometric methods[^47].

Khayyam's work on the parallel postulate, attempting to prove Euclid's fifth postulate from the others, anticipated non-Euclidean geometry. His "Khayyam-Saccheri quadrilateral" (so named because the Italian mathematician Giovanni Gerolamo Saccheri later used the same construction) revealed the consequences of denying the parallel postulate, though Khayyam himself could not conceive of geometries where Euclidean assumptions did not hold[^48].

The Jalali calendar remained in use in Persia, with modifications, into the modern era. The contemporary Iranian calendar, adopted in 1925 and still in official use, is based on Khayyam's Jalali calendar with minor adjustments[^49]. This makes Khayyam's work one of the few medieval astronomical systems still in practical use today.

### Literary and Cultural Influence

FitzGerald's *Rubáiyát* became one of the most popular poems in the English language, going through hundreds of editions and translations back into dozens of languages, including Persian[^50]. The work influenced Victorian and Edwardian culture profoundly, shaping attitudes toward mortality, pleasure, and religious doubt. "Omar Khayyam clubs" formed in Britain and America, dedicated to discussing the poem and its philosophy[^51].

The *Rubáiyát* influenced writers from Thomas Hardy to Jorge Luis Borges, who wrote essays on both Khayyam and FitzGerald[^52]. Artists illustrated the text extensively, including Edmund Dulac, René Bull, and Elihu Vedder, whose 1884 illustrations became iconic[^53]. The poem's themes resonated with early 20th-century disillusionment and the questioning of Victorian certainties.

In Iran and the broader Persian-speaking world, Khayyam's status is complex. He ranks below the great Persian poets Rumi, Hafez, and Ferdowsi in traditional literary hierarchies, as the *rubā'ī* was considered a minor form compared to the *ghazal* or epic poetry[^54]. However, modern Iranian nationalism has embraced Khayyam as a symbol of Persian intellectual achievement, and his mausoleum in Nishapur, redesigned in the 1960s, is a popular pilgrimage site[^55].

### Philosophical Reception

In the history of Islamic philosophy, Khayyam occupies an ambiguous position. He was neither as influential as Avicenna nor as systematically philosophical as Averroes, and his philosophical writings remained relatively obscure until modern scholarly recovery efforts[^56]. However, recent research has revealed the sophistication of his metaphysical and epistemological thought, particularly his engagement with Aristotelian natural philosophy and his attempts to reconcile rational inquiry with religious tradition[^57].

Khayyam's philosophical stance—rationalist but ultimately supportive of religious faith—represents one response to the tension between philosophy and revelation that defined medieval Islamic intellectual life. His relative lack of influence may reflect the increasing suspicion toward rationalist philosophy after al-Ghazali's critique and the rise of more mystical and theological approaches to Islamic thought[^58].

### Modern Scientific Recognition

The modern scientific community has honored Khayyam's legacy through various namings. A crater on the Moon's far side is named after him, as is the asteroid 3095 Omarkhayyam[^59]. The Omar Khayyam Mathematics Competition, held in various countries, commemorates his mathematical achievements. Universities in Iran and elsewhere have established Omar Khayyam research centers dedicated to studying medieval Islamic science and mathematics[^60].

In the history of mathematics, Khayyam is recognized as a crucial figure in the development of algebra, connecting Greek geometric methods with the algebraic traditions of India and the Islamic world[^61]. His work represents a high point of Islamic mathematical achievement, demonstrating both technical virtuosity and philosophical sophistication about the nature of mathematical knowledge.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Modern scholars of Islamic science and mathematics regard Omar Khayyam as one of the most brilliant minds of the medieval period. Mathematician and historian Roshdi Rashed describes Khayyam's algebraic work as "a monument of mathematical thought" that "opened entirely new perspectives for algebra"[^62]. The geometric solution of cubic equations demonstrated both mathematical creativity and a deep understanding of the relationships between different mathematical domains.

Historian of science George Saliba argues that Khayyam exemplifies the "Golden Age of Islamic science," when patronage, institutional support, and cultural valorization of knowledge enabled extraordinary achievements[^63]. The Jalali calendar project demonstrates how scientific excellence could flourish when rulers recognized its practical and prestige value. Khayyam's multidisciplinary expertise—excelling in mathematics, astronomy, philosophy, and poetry—embodies the ideal of the polymath scholar that medieval Islamic culture cultivated[^64].

Literary scholars who have worked with the original Persian texts argue that FitzGerald's translation, despite its liberties, captured something essential in Khayyam's poetic voice: a direct, unadorned confrontation with mortality and a celebration of immediate experience over abstract promises[^65]. Scholar Mehdi Aminrazavi contends that the quatrains, even if not all authentically Khayyam's, express a genuine current of Persian philosophical poetry that questioned conventional pieties and explored existential themes with unusual directness[^66].

Defenders of Khayyam's religious position argue that his skepticism was directed not at faith itself but at false certainty and religious hypocrisy. Philosopher Seyyed Hossein Nasr suggests that Khayyam's critique of dogmatic religion actually reflects a deeper spiritual concern, aligned with the Sufi tradition's emphasis on direct experience over legalistic observance[^67]. On this reading, the wine imagery in the *Rubáiyát* represents mystical intoxication, and the skepticism reflects the apophatic theological tradition that emphasizes God's unknowability.

Cultural historians note Khayyam's role in building cultural bridges between East and West. The popularity of the *Rubáiyát* in Victorian Britain came at a time of increased Western engagement with Islamic culture, and despite the translation's inaccuracies, it fostered interest in Persian literature and Islamic intellectual history[^68]. Khayyam thus became a gateway figure through whom Western audiences encountered Persian culture.

### Negative Views

Critics of the popular Khayyam legend argue that FitzGerald's translation grossly distorted the historical figure, creating a Victorian fantasy of a freethinking hedonist that tells us more about 19th-century European anxieties than 11th-century Persian reality[^69]. The enormous popularity of this distorted image overshadowed Khayyam's actual achievements and created a caricature that impeded serious scholarly understanding.

Some scholars of Persian literature argue that the Western obsession with Khayyam is disproportionate to his actual importance in Persian literary tradition. The *rubā'ī* was a minor form, and many Persian literary historians consider Khayyam a second- or third-tier poet compared to masters like Rumi, Hafez, Sa'di, and Ferdowsi[^70]. The elevation of Khayyam above these greater poets reflects Western orientalist preferences for the exotic and skeptical over the spiritual and mystical dimensions of Persian poetry.

From a conservative Islamic perspective, Khayyam represents the dangers of excessive rationalism unconstrained by revelation. Medieval and modern religious scholars who criticized Khayyam argued that his philosophical approach led to skepticism, materialism, and the undermining of religious faith[^71]. Whether or not the most heterodox quatrains are authentically his, Khayyam's rationalist methodology, they argue, inevitably tends toward doubt about religious truths that transcend reason.

Historians of science have noted that despite Khayyam's brilliance, his work had limited immediate impact. His geometric solutions to cubic equations, while mathematically elegant, were not computationally practical compared to approximate numerical methods[^72]. His astronomical observations, though precise, were not systematically built upon by immediate successors. His philosophical writings remained relatively obscure until modern rediscovery. This limited influence raises questions about the actual historical significance of even brilliant work that does not generate a continuing research tradition.

Some critics argue that the modern Iranian nationalist appropriation of Khayyam is anachronistic, projecting contemporary concerns about secular modernity and Persian identity onto a medieval scholar who would not have recognized these categories[^73]. The conflicting images of Khayyam—hedonist skeptic, rational scientist, mystical Sufi, Iranian nationalist icon—reflect modern ideological needs more than historical reality.

> 
> *From FitzGerald's translation of the Rubáiyát, quatrain LXXI. Expresses the irreversibility of time and the futility of regret—one of the most famous verses in English poetry.*[^74]

> "A Book of Verses underneath the Bough, A Jug of Wine, a Loaf of Bread—and Thou Beside me singing in the Wilderness— Oh, Wilderness were Paradise enow!"
> 
> *FitzGerald's Rubáiyát, quatrain XII. Celebrates simple pleasures and present experience, becoming an iconic expression of hedonistic philosophy.*[^75]

> "Ah, make the most of what we yet may spend, Before we too into the Dust descend; Dust into Dust, and under Dust to lie, Sans Wine, sans Song, sans Singer, and—sans End!"
> 
> *FitzGerald's Rubáiyát, quatrain XXIV. Urges enjoyment of life before death's finality, reflecting carpe diem themes.*[^76]

> "Myself when young did eagerly frequent Doctor and Saint, and heard great Argument About it and about: but evermore Came out by the same Door as in I went."
> 
> *FitzGerald's Rubáiyát, quatrain XXVII. Expresses skepticism about theological and philosophical certainties, suggesting that elaborate arguments lead nowhere.*[^77]

> "Whoever thinks algebra is a trick in obtaining unknowns has thought it in vain. No attention should be paid to the fact that algebra and geometry are different in appearance. Algebras are geometric facts which are proved by propositions five and six of Book two of Elements."
