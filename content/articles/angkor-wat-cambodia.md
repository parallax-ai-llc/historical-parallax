## Summary

Angkor Wat is the largest religious monument ever constructed, covering an area of
approximately 400 acres (162 hectares) including its surrounding moat and enclosure.
Located near the city of Siem Reap in northwestern Cambodia, it was built in the early
12th century CE by the Khmer Emperor Suryavarman II as a state temple and eventual
mausoleum. Originally dedicated to the Hindu god Vishnu, it was gradually transformed into
a Buddhist temple from the late 12th century onward and has functioned as an active place
of Buddhist worship ever since — making it, remarkably, the world's oldest continuously
functioning temple complex.

The name "Angkor Wat" derives from the Sanskrit-Khmer "Nagara Wat" (नगर वाट), meaning
"City Temple" or "Temple of the Capital." The broader Angkor complex, which includes
Angkor Thom and dozens of other temple complexes, is the largest preindustrial urban
settlement ever discovered, covering approximately 1,000 square kilometers at its height.
Angkor Wat itself is the central jewel of this extraordinary civilization.

The monument is central to Cambodian national identity, appearing on the national flag
since 1863 — the only current national flag to feature an image of a building. UNESCO
inscribed the Angkor complex on the World Heritage List in 1992.

## History & Origins

The Khmer Empire, which produced Angkor Wat, was one of the most powerful civilizations of
Southeast Asia, dominating the region from roughly the 9th to the 15th century CE. Its
capital, Angkor (meaning "city" or "capital"), was established in the early 9th century by
King Jayavarman II, who declared himself a devaraja (god-king) — a monarch whose divine
nature linked him to Shiva, the Hindu deity of creation and destruction. This ideology of
the god-king, combining royal authority with divine sanction, remained central to Khmer
statecraft throughout the empire's history.

Suryavarman II, who ruled from approximately 1113 to 1150 CE, initiated the construction
of Angkor Wat. The scale and ambition of the project reflects his consolidation of power
after defeating rival claimants and extending Khmer control across much of mainland
Southeast Asia. Construction likely continued for over three decades, employing an
enormous labor force of craftsmen, laborers, and administrators.

Angkor Wat was dedicated to Vishnu rather than to Shiva, which distinguished it from most
other major Khmer temples (which were Shaivite). This may reflect Suryavarman II's
personal religious orientation or political alliances. The temple was also conceived as a
funerary monument for the king — a temple-mausoleum modeled on the mythological Mount
Meru, the cosmic home of the gods, in which the king would be identified with Vishnu after
death.

The Khmer Empire reached its greatest territorial extent under Suryavarman II and his
successors. However, in 1177, the city of Angkor was sacked by the Cham people (from what
is now Vietnam). It was restored by Jayavarman VII (r. 1181–c. 1218), a Buddhist king who
initiated an extraordinary building program that included the construction of Angkor Thom
(the last great capital of the Khmer Empire) and the famous Bayon temple with its
enigmatic stone faces.

Under Jayavarman VII, Angkor Wat itself began its transformation from a Hindu to a
Buddhist monument. Buddhist images were added alongside the existing Hindu iconography,
and the religious orientation of the complex gradually shifted. By the 15th century, when
Theravada Buddhism had become the dominant religious tradition of Cambodia, Angkor Wat was
functioning primarily as a Theravada Buddhist monastery and temple.

The Khmer capital was definitively moved southward to the region of present-day Phnom Penh
in the 15th century, following military pressure from the Ayutthaya Kingdom (Thai). Angkor
Wat itself was never abandoned — it continued to be inhabited by Buddhist monks — but the
broader Angkor complex was gradually reclaimed by the jungle.

Portuguese missionaries and traders who visited in the 16th century left accounts of
Angkor Wat, describing it as a magnificent and still-functioning religious monument. The
French naturalist Henri Mouhot's accounts of his visit in 1860 are often cited as the
event that "rediscovered" Angkor for European audiences, though again, the temple had
never actually been abandoned or forgotten by Cambodians.

French archaeologists and conservators working under the Ecole Française d'Extrême-Orient
(EFEO) undertook the first systematic archaeological study and conservation of Angkor from
the late 19th century through the mid-20th century, clearing jungle vegetation from many
of the temple complexes and establishing the scholarly foundations for understanding the
site.

## Religious Significance

In its original form, Angkor Wat was a Hindu monument dedicated to Vishnu, the preserver
deity of the Hindu trinity. The temple is oriented to the west — unusual among Khmer
temples, which are typically oriented east. This western orientation has been interpreted
variously as reflecting Vishnu's association with the west, or as indicating the funerary
function of the temple (the west being associated with death and the realm of the dead in
Hindu tradition).

The temple's entire architectural program is oriented around the symbolic representation
of Hindu cosmology. The central tower represents Mount Meru. The surrounding moat
represents the cosmic ocean. The enclosure walls represent the mountains at the edge of
the world. Moving from the outermost walls inward and upward represents a journey from the
earthly realm through the heavens to the divine summit.

The bas-reliefs of Angkor Wat constitute one of the most extensive narrative sculptural
programs in the world, covering approximately 800 meters of gallery walls at an average
height of 2 meters. The eight major panels depict scenes from Hindu mythology and Khmer
history. The most celebrated include a 49-meter panel depicting the Churning of the Ocean
of Milk (Samudra Manthan), a cosmic myth in which gods and demons churn the primordial
ocean to extract the nectar of immortality; a depiction of the Battle of Kurukshetra from
the Mahabharata; and a depiction of Yama, the god of death, judging the souls of the
departed.

Other panels depict Suryavarman II himself — shown in royal procession, receiving tribute
from subordinate rulers, and preparing for military campaigns. These historical panels are
invaluable for understanding the political history of the Khmer Empire.

The transformation of Angkor Wat into a Buddhist monument added layers of meaning rather
than erasing the Hindu substratum. Buddha images were installed in the main sanctuary,
replacing or supplementing the original Vishnu statue. Today, the main sanctuary contains
a large standing Vishnu statue (popularly called Ta Reach — "the Royal Grandfather") that
has been rededicated to Buddhist worship, with offerings of incense, flowers, and gold
leaf placed before it.

The transformation from Vaishnavism to Theravada Buddhism is reflected throughout the
monument: Buddhist monks still inhabit a portion of the complex, maintaining active
religious life within the ruins. Worshippers visit daily for prayer and meditation.

## Architecture & Description

Angkor Wat's architectural plan is a masterpiece of symbolic geometry. The outer enclosure
measures approximately 1,025 by 800 meters. This is surrounded by a moat approximately 190
meters wide — the largest water barrier associated with any temple complex in the world. A
causeway 12 meters wide and 250 meters long, flanked by serpent (naga) balustrades, leads
from the west entrance to the main temple.

The main temple itself is built on a raised platform with three rectangular galleries
stacked concentrically, each higher than the last. Four corner towers and a central tower
(the main prasat) define the composition. The five towers of Angkor Wat — four corner
towers and one central tower — have become one of the most recognizable silhouettes in
Southeast Asian art and architecture, representing the five peaks of Mount Meru.

The central tower rises to approximately 65 meters above the ground level of the third
gallery (approximately 55 meters above ground level of the surrounding platform). The
towers are built in the characteristic Khmer lotus-bud form, tapering to a pointed top
through a series of curved moldings that create a distinctive profile.

The galleries, towers, and connecting halls of Angkor Wat are built from sandstone blocks,
with laterite (a porous iron-rich rock) used for hidden structural elements. The sandstone
was quarried at Mount Kulen, approximately 40 kilometers to the northeast, and transported
via a network of canals. An estimated 5 to 10 million sandstone blocks were used in the
construction.

Every surface is carved with decorative motifs: floral scrollwork, celestial dancers
(apsaras) — over 1,700 individual apsara figures have been counted — devatas (female
divinities) in elaborate headdresses, and mythological animals. The density and quality of
this decorative carving is extraordinary; no surface is left plain.

The alignment of Angkor Wat with astronomical phenomena has been extensively studied. The
main western axis aligns with the sunset during the spring equinox. The positions of
various towers and sight lines correspond to the rising and setting positions of
significant celestial bodies, suggesting the monument served as an astronomical calendar
as well as a religious monument.

## Pilgrimage & Visitors

Angkor Wat is Cambodia's largest source of international tourism revenue and one of the
most visited archaeological sites in the world. In the years before the COVID-19 pandemic,
the broader Angkor complex received approximately 2.5 million international visitors per
year, with hundreds of thousands more domestic visitors. The site generates the majority
of ticket revenue for the APSARA National Authority, the Cambodian government body
responsible for managing the Angkor complex.

For Cambodian Buddhists, Angkor Wat is the most sacred site in the country. Significant
religious festivals draw large numbers of Cambodian pilgrims: the Khmer New Year (Chaul
Chhnam Thmey) in April, Pchum Ben (the Festival of the Ancestors) in September-October,
and the annual Angkor Wat International Half Marathon (which incorporates a pre-race
blessing ceremony at the temple) are all occasions that bring large numbers of worshippers
and pilgrims.

The sunrise viewed from the reflecting pools in front of Angkor Wat has become one of the
iconic travel experiences of Southeast Asia, drawing visitors from around the world.

A small community of Buddhist monks continues to reside in the complex, maintaining the
active religious life of the monument.

## Modern Significance

Angkor Wat's position on the Cambodian national flag makes it uniquely central to national
identity among the world's great archaeological monuments. During the decades of civil war
and the Khmer Rouge genocide (1975–1979), Angkor Wat survived largely intact — though
statues were damaged or looted, and some areas were used by Khmer Rouge forces. Since the
restoration of stability in the 1990s, Cambodia has invested enormously in the
conservation and management of the Angkor complex.

International conservation teams from France, Japan, India, Germany, the United States,
and many other countries work alongside Cambodian authorities to monitor and repair the
monument. The primary ongoing conservation challenges include structural instability of
some towers and galleries, soil erosion, fluctuating water table levels, and the physical
impact of millions of visitors on the stone surfaces.

The broader Angkor landscape — including the medieval hydraulic system that supported the
Khmer capital's large population — has been the subject of intensive remote sensing
research using LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) technology. These studies, conducted
since 2012, have dramatically expanded scholarly understanding of the scale and complexity
of the pre-Angkorian and Angkorian settlements in the region.

## Timeline

- **c. 9th century CE** — Khmer Empire founded; city of Angkor established
- **c. 1113–1150 CE** — Construction of Angkor Wat under Suryavarman II
- **1177 CE** — Angkor sacked by Cham forces
- **1181–c. 1218 CE** — Jayavarman VII restores Angkor; Angkor Thom and Bayon constructed; Buddhist transformation of Angkor Wat begins
- **14th–15th century CE** — Theravada Buddhism becomes dominant; Angkor Wat functions as Buddhist monastery
- **1431 CE** — Khmer capital moved southward following Thai military pressure
- **16th century CE** — Portuguese missionaries visit and document Angkor Wat
- **1860 CE** — Henri Mouhot's visit; European interest in Angkor Wat intensifies
- **1908 CE** — Systematic EFEO conservation of Angkor begins
- **1970–1993 CE** — Cambodian civil war and Khmer Rouge era; site suffers damage and looting
- **1992 CE** — Angkor inscribed on UNESCO World Heritage List (simultaneously placed on Danger list)
- **2004 CE** — Angkor removed from UNESCO Danger list following conservation progress
- **2012–present** — LiDAR surveys reveal full extent of Angkor's urban landscape
