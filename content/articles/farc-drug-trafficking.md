## Summary

Colombian revolutionary group FARCs involvement in drug trafficking that funded their insurgency for decades. FARC controlled significant cocaine production earning an estimated $600 million annually.

## Background

- The Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia (FARC) financed its insurgency through a mix of taxation, kidnappings, and involvement in the coca/cocaine economy.
- In many coca-growing regions, armed actors exerted territorial control that enabled them to levy “taxes” on cultivation, processing, and transport.

## How the Drug Economy Funded the Insurgency

- Early involvement is often described as “revolutionary taxation” on coca growers and laboratories operating in areas under FARC influence.
- Over time, accusations expanded to include deeper participation in protection, logistics, and direct trafficking.
- Revenue from the cocaine trade provided cash for weapons, recruitment, and sustained operations over decades.

## Consequences

- Drug-related income increased the intensity and duration of the conflict by giving armed groups access to large, renewable funding streams.
- It also complicated negotiations and public legitimacy, as insurgency goals became intertwined with criminal enterprise narratives.

## International Dimension

- U.S. and international counter-narcotics policy increasingly overlapped with counterinsurgency objectives in Colombia.
- Sanctions and indictments targeted individuals and networks alleged to be involved in trafficking and money flows.

## Post-Conflict Issues

- The 2016 peace accord created new dynamics around coca cultivation, substitution programs, and the struggle for territorial control by other armed actors.
- Debates continue about how to separate rural development, security, and drug policy in former conflict zones.
