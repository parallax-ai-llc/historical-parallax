## Summary

North Koreas system of political prison camps (kwanliso) holding an estimated 80,000-120,000 prisoners in conditions amounting to slavery. The regime also sends workers abroad as forced laborers.

## Forms of Forced Labor (High Level)

- **Detention-linked labor**: Human-rights reporting describes forced labor as a feature of detention systems, including political prison camps and other forms of incarceration.
- **State-assigned labor**: Accounts also describe coercive labor practices tied to state control of employment, surveillance, and punishment.
- **Overseas labor deployments**: North Korean workers have been sent abroad under arrangements in which the state is reported to capture a large share of wages and restrict movement.

## International Findings and Responses

- **UN reporting**: The 2014 UN Commission of Inquiry on human rights in the DPRK reported patterns consistent with crimes against humanity, drawing on witness testimony and other evidence.
- **Sanctions and compliance**: Several governments and multilateral bodies have used sanctions and labor restrictions as tools to reduce revenue streams linked to coercive labor.
- **Corporate and supply-chain risk**: Forced-labor allegations can create due-diligence obligations for firms, especially where subcontracting obscures the origin of labor.

## Evidence and Verification

- **Multiple-source triangulation**: Because direct access to sites is limited, researchers often combine satellite imagery, defector testimony, open-source documentation, and NGO investigations.
- **Uncertainty ranges**: Population estimates for detention systems often appear as ranges and may change as new information emerges.
