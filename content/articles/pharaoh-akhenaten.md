## Summary

Egyptian pharaoh who violently suppressed traditional religion and imposed monotheistic worship of Aten.

## Historical Context

- Akhenaten ruled in Egypt's 18th Dynasty during the New Kingdom period.
- He is commonly identified with Amenhotep IV early in his reign, adopting the name **Akhenaten** as part of his religious program.

## Religious Policy and Reform

- Promoted the solar disk **Aten** as the central object of state worship.
- Reduced the political and economic power of established temple institutions, especially those associated with **Amun** at Thebes.
- Changes are often described as an attempt at exclusivist or highly elevated Aten worship, though the exact degree of "monotheism" is debated by historians.

## Amarna Period and Capital Relocation

- Founded a new capital city, **Akhetaten** (modern Amarna), and shifted the royal court there.
- Patronized the distinctive "Amarna" artistic style, characterized by more intimate royal scenes and new conventions in portraiture.

## Governance and Foreign Affairs (High-Level)

- The reign is associated with the **Amarna Letters**, diplomatic correspondence that reflects Egypt's relationships with vassal states and neighboring powers.
- Several sources suggest strains in Egypt's influence in parts of the Levant during this period, though attribution and causality remain contested.

## Succession and Aftermath

- After Akhenaten's death, Egypt moved toward restoring traditional cults and political norms.
- His immediate succession sequence is complex (including figures such as **Smenkhkare** and **Neferneferuaten** in some reconstructions).
- Under **Tutankhamun**, royal policy explicitly reversed many Amarna-era religious changes.

## Legacy

- Frequently cited as one of the most radical religious innovators in ancient Egyptian history.
- Modern interpretations range from visionary reformer to political-religious disruptor, reflecting limited and uneven evidence.
- The Amarna period remains central to scholarship on Egyptian religion, kingship, diplomacy, and art.
