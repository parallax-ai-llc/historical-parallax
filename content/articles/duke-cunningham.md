## Summary

American Congressman and former Navy ace who accepted $2.4 million in bribes from defense contractors in the largest Congressional bribery scandal at the time. He created a 'bribe menu' with set prices for government contracts.

## Additional Context

- **Military background:** Cunningham was a U.S. Navy pilot and became a highly decorated aviator during the Vietnam War era before entering politics.
- **Political profile:** He served as a Republican member of the U.S. House of Representatives from California, focusing heavily on national security and defense-related issues.

## Corruption Case (High Level)

- **Nature of misconduct:** Prosecutors described a long-running scheme in which Cunningham accepted gifts and payments from defense contractors and used his position to steer or support government business.
- **"Bribe menu":** The case became widely known for documentation that treated official actions like a price list, reinforcing the perception of transactional corruption.
- **Legal outcome:** He pleaded guilty to federal charges including bribery and tax-related offenses and received a lengthy prison sentence.

## Aftermath and Significance

- **Institutional impact:** The scandal contributed to broader scrutiny of earmarks, contracting influence, and ethics enforcement in Congress.
- **Reputation:** Cunningham is frequently cited in U.S. political corruption discussions as an example of quid-pro-quo bribery tied to defense procurement.

## Key Takeaways

- **Conflict-of-interest risk:** The case is often used to illustrate how close relationships between lawmakers and contractors can undermine procurement integrity.
- **Paper trails matter:** Financial records, gifts, and documented communications were central to building the bribery case.
- **Ethics enforcement:** It remains a reference point for congressional ethics rules around gifts, travel, and outside benefits.
