## Summary

18th Street Gang, rival of MS-13, with an estimated 65,000 members across Central America and the US. Controls territory through extortion and drug trafficking.

## Additional Context

### Origins and evolution

Barrio 18 (18th Street Gang) originated in Los Angeles and is commonly described as one of the early multi-ethnic street gangs in the city. Over time, migration patterns, deportations, and transnational family networks contributed to its spread beyond the United States.

### Transnational presence in Central America

In the late 20th and early 21st centuries, the gang became deeply associated with violence and extortion dynamics in parts of Central America, particularly in El Salvador, Guatemala, and Honduras. Analysts often link this expansion to:

- post–civil war social disruption
- weak policing and limited prison capacity
- deportation-driven transfer of gang structures and norms

### Criminal activities

High-level descriptions of Barrio 18’s revenue streams commonly include:

- extortion (“rent”) imposed on small businesses and transport operators
- drug retail and local distribution
- robbery and other coercive crimes tied to territorial control

### Organization and factions

The gang is frequently characterized as a network of neighborhood-based cliques rather than a single centralized hierarchy, with varying degrees of coordination depending on location and prison influence.

### Rivalry and violence

The rivalry with MS-13 is a recurring theme in reporting and policy analysis. Inter-gang conflict, alongside state crackdowns, has contributed to cycles of homicide and retaliation in several countries.

### Government responses

Responses have ranged from “mano dura” (hardline policing and mass arrests) to negotiated truces and prison-based management strategies. The effectiveness and human-rights costs of these approaches are widely debated.

### Notes on estimates

Membership figures and geographic reach are difficult to measure precisely, and numbers reported in media and official statements can vary by year, country, and methodology.
