## Additional Context

- **5 Segonn** is one of several armed groups that have operated in and around Port-au-Prince during periods of state weakness and political instability.
- **Martissant** is a corridor in the southern part of Port-au-Prince whose control has been contested by rival gangs, affecting transport, commerce, and daily movement for residents.

## Why This Figure Matters (High-Level)

Ti Gabriel is notable in reporting primarily as a **localized power broker**: a gang leader whose influence is described in terms of **territorial control** rather than formal political office. In contexts like Port-au-Prince, territorial control can translate into practical authority over:

- local security and intimidation
- checkpoints and movement restrictions
- informal taxation/extortion of businesses and residents
- leverage in negotiations with state actors or rival groups

## Common Reporting Limitations

Public information about individual gang leaders in Haiti is often **sparse** and may be **dated**, because:

- names and aliases can change, and attribution can be inconsistent
- direct access for journalists is limited by security conditions
- primary-source documentation (court filings, verified biographies) is limited

As a result, summaries frequently rely on a small number of open sources and emphasize the best-documented claims (e.g., presence and control in a named neighborhood during a specific year).
