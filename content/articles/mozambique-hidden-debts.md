## Background

The term “Mozambique hidden debts” is used for undisclosed state-backed borrowing undertaken in the 2010s through state-controlled or state-linked companies. The loans were tied to projects presented as maritime security, tuna fishing, and associated infrastructure, and were accompanied by government guarantees that were not transparently reported at the time.

## How the Debt Was Hidden

High-level summaries generally emphasize that the borrowing was not fully disclosed to key oversight bodies and funding partners. The lack of transparency made it difficult for:

- Parliament and auditors to assess the scale of contingent liabilities.
- Donors and lenders to evaluate Mozambique’s true debt position.
- Citizens and markets to price sovereign risk accurately.

## Economic and Political Effects

After the debts became public, Mozambique experienced a severe fiscal and balance-of-payments shock. Commonly cited effects include:

- Loss of donor confidence and reduced access to concessional financing.
- Pressure on the national currency and higher borrowing costs.
- A prolonged period of austerity, restructuring talks, and constrained public investment.

## Investigations and Accountability

The hidden debts episode prompted investigations, criminal cases, and civil litigation. Because the financing involved international banks, investors, and intermediaries, proceedings and settlements spanned multiple jurisdictions.

## Legacy

The hidden debts are frequently cited in governance and development discussions as a case study in sovereign guarantee oversight, public financial management, and the systemic risk created when state-linked entities borrow off-budget.
