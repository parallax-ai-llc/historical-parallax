## Additional Context

### Scientific contribution: monoclonal antibodies

- Milstein is best known for co-developing the hybridoma technique (with Georges J. F. Köhler) that enabled the production of monoclonal antibodies: antibodies that are genetically identical and bind a single epitope.
- The method combined an antibody-producing B cell with a myeloma (cancer) cell to create a hybrid cell line that could both proliferate indefinitely and secrete a specific antibody.

### Institutions and career

- Much of his influential work was done at the Medical Research Council (MRC) Laboratory of Molecular Biology in Cambridge, a major center for molecular and immunological research in the 20th century.

### Impact

- Monoclonal antibodies became foundational tools in biomedical research (e.g., identifying proteins and cell types) and later a major class of therapeutics.
- Clinical and commercial applications include diagnostics (laboratory tests and imaging) and treatments for cancers, autoimmune diseases, and inflammatory conditions.

### Recognition

- The 1984 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine was awarded for “principles for production of monoclonal antibodies,” reflecting the technique’s broad enabling effect across biology and medicine.
