## Summary

Already covered.

Sir Walter Raleigh (c. 1552–1618) was an English courtier, soldier, writer, and promoter of overseas expansion during the reign of Elizabeth I. He became one of the best-known figures associated with early English attempts at colonization in North America, especially the Roanoke ventures of the 1580s, and later pursued exploration and privateering in the Atlantic world.

Raleigh’s career was tightly bound to royal favor: his rapid rise at court was followed by an equally dramatic fall after a secret marriage, leading to imprisonment and the loss of key offices. Under James I, he was accused of treason in the “Main Plot,” tried, and sentenced to death; the sentence was suspended for years before being carried out after a failed expedition to Guiana (Guyana) and diplomatic pressure from Spain.

He also left a substantial literary footprint, including poetry and prose, and is often discussed as a representative figure of the Elizabethan age—combining political ambition, imperial projects, and self-fashioning through writing.

## Early Life and Formation

Raleigh was born into a gentry family in Devon and was educated in an environment shaped by the religious and political upheavals of Tudor England. He moved through a mix of study, military service, and patronage networks—paths typical of ambitious young men seeking advancement in the late 16th century.

## Exploration, Privateering, and Colonization

Raleigh is commonly linked to England’s push toward Atlantic expansion:

- **Roanoke and “Virginia”:** He did not personally govern the Roanoke settlement, but he helped finance and organize voyages and became closely identified with the project in later narratives.
- **Maritime conflict:** English ventures in this era often blurred lines between exploration, trade, and sanctioned privateering during periods of tension with Spain.
- **Guiana expeditions:** Raleigh’s published account of his 1595 voyage contributed to English interest in South America, mixing observation, political argument, and the era’s expectations of riches.

## Court Politics and Downfall

At Elizabeth’s court, Raleigh gained offices and influence, but court politics were volatile. His secret marriage to one of the queen’s ladies-in-waiting was treated as a severe breach of protocol, triggering imprisonment and a sharp loss of status. Although he returned to public life, his position never fully recovered to its earlier peak.

## Imprisonment, Trial, and Execution

After 1603, Raleigh’s trial for treason became a notable example in English legal history because of disputes over evidence and procedure. The sentence was not immediately carried out, and Raleigh spent many years imprisoned. When he was later allowed to lead another voyage, the expedition’s failure and conflict with Spanish interests provided grounds for reviving the earlier sentence.

## Works and Legacy

Raleigh’s reputation has been sustained by both politics and literature:

- **Writing:** He is credited with poems and prose, and he authored *The History of the World* while imprisoned.
- **Mythmaking:** Later stories (such as symbolic courtly gestures) became part of a larger legend around Elizabethan courtiers, sometimes with uncertain historical basis.
- **Imperial memory:** He is often treated as an emblem of early English imperial ambition, and modern assessments tend to place his actions within the broader context of conquest, colonization, and conflict.
