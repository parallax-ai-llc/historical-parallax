## Summary

Montagnard resistance leader accused by Vietnam of separatism and terrorism for leading protests against government land seizures and religious persecution. He advocates for indigenous rights from exile.

## Background: Montagnards and the Central Highlands

Kok Ksor is commonly associated with political activism connected to the Montagnards (Degar), an umbrella term often used for several indigenous ethnic groups in Vietnam’s Central Highlands. Disputes in the region have included land tenure, resettlement, economic development, and the regulation of religious practice.

## Activism and Organizations

Accounts of Kok Ksor’s role frequently describe him as a representative or spokesperson for indigenous-rights claims, including appeals to international bodies and diaspora networks. Vietnamese authorities have also characterized related movements as separatist or security threats, illustrating the contested framing of activism in the Highlands.

## Protests and State Response

Protest waves in the early 2000s drew attention to grievances over land allocation and local governance. Responses involved policing, legal proceedings, and information controls, while international observers and human-rights organizations have debated the underlying causes and the proportionality of state actions.

## Exile and International Advocacy

From outside Vietnam, Kok Ksor has continued advocacy focused on indigenous rights and religious freedom. Exile politics can amplify international visibility, but they also complicate verification of events on the ground and can intensify state suspicion toward cross-border organizing.

## Legacy and Ongoing Debate

Kok Ksor’s prominence reflects broader tensions between centralized development policy and minority rights in multi-ethnic states. Interpretations differ sharply depending on whether the focus is placed on national territorial integrity or on indigenous self-determination, cultural autonomy, and protections against dispossession.
