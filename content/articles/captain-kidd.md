## Summary

Scottish privateer who became one of the most famous pirates in history. Originally hired to hunt pirates, he turned to piracy himself. His trial and execution in London were controversial, and his buried treasure became legend.

## Background and context

Kidd operated during an era when European states relied on privateering to attack enemy commerce. A privateer carried a government commission (a letter of marque) that authorized captures under wartime rules, but those rules were contested and could shift with politics.

In the late 1690s, Kidd’s backers included influential figures in England and the American colonies. The venture was framed as a hunt for pirates and hostile shipping, but the line between legitimate prize-taking and piracy was thin in practice, especially far from courts and admiralties.

## Capture of the *Quedagh Merchant*

The seizure of the *Quedagh Merchant* became central to the case against him. Kidd claimed the ship carried papers that made it a lawful prize, while opponents treated the capture as straightforward piracy. Disputes over ownership, flags, and documentation were common in Indian Ocean trade, making legal interpretation a powerful political tool.

## Arrest, trial, and execution

After returning to North America, Kidd was arrested and sent to London. He was tried for piracy and for the killing of a crewman, William Moore. His trial is often described as controversial because of evidentiary issues and the broader political need to demonstrate that authorities were serious about suppressing piracy.

Kidd was executed in 1701. His body was displayed in an iron cage (gibbet) as a warning to others, a practice used to deter maritime crime.

## Legacy

Kidd’s story helped popularize the enduring image of pirates as both criminals and romantic adventurers. Legends of hidden treasure, amplified by later literature and folklore, became a major part of his cultural afterlife even though historians treat many treasure claims with skepticism.

In later centuries, Kidd has been revisited as an example of how a privateer could become a scapegoat when political conditions changed, and how maritime law and imperial politics shaped the definition of “piracy.”
