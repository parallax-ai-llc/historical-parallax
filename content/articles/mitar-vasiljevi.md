## Summary

Bosnian Serb convicted of aiding and abetting murder and violence for his participation in the Višegrad massacres alongside Milan Lukić. He helped gather Bosniak civilians who were then burned alive.

## Context: Višegrad, 1992

- Višegrad (eastern Bosnia) was the site of systematic violence against **Bosniak civilians** during the Bosnian War.
- Multiple ICTY cases describe patterns including murder, terrorizing of civilians, forced displacement, and other acts treated as persecution.

## Conduct described in ICTY findings

- Vasiljević was convicted for **aiding and abetting** murder and violence.
- The findings described his role in facilitating attacks by helping gather or control civilians who were then subjected to lethal violence.
- His case is frequently discussed alongside prosecutions of **Milan Lukić** and other perpetrators connected to the Višegrad campaign.

## Legal outcome

- He was sentenced to **15 years**, later reduced on appeal to **10 years**.
- The appeal outcome is often cited as an example of how tribunals assess evidentiary weight and individual level of participation.

## Why it matters

- The Višegrad judgments helped establish a detailed historical record of atrocities in eastern Bosnia.
- The jurisprudence illustrates legal distinctions between:
  - direct perpetrators,
  - planners/commanders,
  - and those who materially assist crimes (aiding and abetting).

## Broader historical framing

- Višegrad became emblematic of the Bosnian War’s civilian targeting and ethnic cleansing dynamics.
- International prosecutions formed part of post-war efforts to document crimes and assign individual responsibility.
