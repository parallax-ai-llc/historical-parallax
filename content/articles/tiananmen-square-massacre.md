## Summary

Chinese military crackdown on pro-democracy protesters in Beijing on June 4, 1989. Hundreds to thousands of civilians were killed when the army opened fire. China has suppressed all memory of the event.

## Background and Protests (Spring 1989)

- The protests grew out of a broader movement involving **students, workers, and citizens** calling for political reform, accountability, and greater civil liberties.
- Demonstrations centered on **Tiananmen Square** in Beijing but also spread to other cities.
- The movement included hunger strikes, public speeches, and mass gatherings that drew national and international attention.

## Crackdown and Violence

- Authorities declared **martial law** and deployed troops and armored vehicles into Beijing.
- On and around June 4, 1989, the military used lethal force to clear protest sites and surrounding streets.
- The precise death toll remains disputed; estimates in public sources range from **hundreds to thousands**.

## Aftermath

- Many participants and sympathizers were detained, prosecuted, or otherwise punished.
- The crackdown marked a decisive turn in the Chinese government’s approach to large-scale political dissent.
- In the years that followed, political liberalization efforts slowed substantially, while economic reforms continued.

## Censorship and Memory

- Public discussion of the event has been heavily restricted in mainland China, including censorship in media, education, and online spaces.
- Commemorations have been limited or prevented, and references to the date and imagery associated with the protests are frequently removed.

## International Response (High-Level)

- The crackdown prompted widespread condemnation abroad and led to diplomatic and economic consequences, including sanctions and arms embargo policies in some countries.
- Over time, many international relationships normalized, but the event remains a major reference point in human-rights debates.
