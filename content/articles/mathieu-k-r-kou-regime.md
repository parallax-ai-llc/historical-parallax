## Summary

Kérékou's Marxist-Leninist regime in Benin renamed the country and suppressed dissent for 17 years. Political opponents were imprisoned, tortured, or killed.

## Political setting

The regime began after a military takeover that brought Mathieu Kérékou to power.
It is commonly characterized as a single-party state aligned with Marxist-Leninist ideology.
Benin was renamed the People's Republic of Benin during this period.

## Governance and repression (high level)

Authoritarian regimes typically combine security services, party structures, and patronage to maintain control.
In this case, open sources describe restrictions on political pluralism and punishment of opponents.
Detention, intimidation, and political violence are recurring themes in retrospective accounts.
Precise numbers and attribution can vary by source, especially for abuses carried out by security organs.

## Economic and social pressures

Many African socialist projects of the era faced external debt constraints and internal administrative capacity limits.
Benin experienced economic difficulty that contributed to public dissatisfaction.
When fiscal crisis deepens, regimes often become more vulnerable to mass protest and elite fragmentation.

## Transition in 1990

The 1990 political transition is widely described as a shift to multiparty democracy.
Such transitions often involve negotiated settlements and constitutional reforms rather than total rupture.
The Benin case is frequently cited in discussions of peaceful democratization in West Africa.
