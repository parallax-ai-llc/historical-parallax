## Summary

Aum Shinrikyo cult released sarin gas on five Tokyo subway trains during rush hour. 13 died and over 5,500 were injured in the worst terror attack in Japanese history.

## Background

The attack was carried out by **Aum Shinrikyo**, a Japanese doomsday cult that had pursued chemical and biological weapons.
In the early 1990s the group committed other violent acts, including the **1994 Matsumoto sarin attack**, which foreshadowed the Tokyo incident.

## The Attack (20 March 1995)

Key high-level facts commonly cited in reputable summaries include:

- Sarin nerve agent was released on multiple subway lines during the morning commute.
- The operation involved coordinated teams who carried punctured containers of liquid sarin onto trains.
- The attack targeted a dense urban transport network, maximizing exposure among commuters and first responders.

Casualties and injuries were substantial and spread across stations, trains, and nearby streets as people fled affected areas.

## Immediate Response

Japan’s emergency services and hospitals faced a complex scenario:

- Many victims arrived without a clear diagnosis, complicating triage.
- Secondary exposure risks affected some responders and medical staff.
- Public transportation disruption and misinformation increased the difficulty of coordinating care.

The incident accelerated awareness of chemical-agent hazards in civilian settings.

## Investigation, Trials, and Sentencing

Authorities investigated Aum’s leadership structure, financing, and weapons programs.
The legal aftermath included:

- Arrests of perpetrators and senior organizers.
- Long-running trials and appeals that extended for years.
- Debates over accountability within hierarchical extremist organizations.

## Legacy and Impact

The Tokyo subway sarin attack is frequently referenced in discussions of:

- Domestic terrorism and cult dynamics.
- Chemical weapons proliferation and security failures.
- Emergency preparedness for mass-casualty incidents.
- Policy changes affecting policing, intelligence coordination, and public safety planning.
