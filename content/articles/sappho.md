## Summary

Sappho (c. 630–570 BCE) was an ancient Greek lyric poet from the island of Lesbos, widely celebrated in antiquity as the "Tenth Muse" and "The Poetess."[^1][^2] Her revolutionary poetry, composed in the Aeolic dialect and set to music, focused on personal emotion, love, and desire from a distinctly female perspective—a radical departure from the epic tradition dominated by male poets.[^3][^4] Though most of her estimated 10,000 lines of poetry have been lost, the surviving fragments continue to influence literature, and her name has become synonymous with female same-sex love, giving rise to the terms "sapphic" and "lesbian."[^5][^6] Despite the fragmentary nature of her work, Sappho remains one of the most important figures in Western literary history, bridging the ancient and modern worlds through ongoing papyrus discoveries and reinterpretations.[^7]

---

## Early Life and Background

Sappho was born around 630-610 BCE on the island of Lesbos in the northeastern Aegean Sea, either in the city of Eresos or Mytilene.[^8][^9] She came from an aristocratic family of considerable wealth and social standing, though the exact names of her parents remain uncertain in the historical record.[^10] Ancient sources variously name her father as Scamandronymus, though other names including Scamander, Eunomus, and others appear in different testimonies.[^11] Her mother was most commonly identified as Cleïs (or Cleis), a name Sappho would later give to her own daughter.[^12][^13]

Sappho grew up during a vibrant period in Archaic Greece, when Lesbos was a major cultural center and home to several prominent poets and political figures.[^14] She had at least two brothers, Charaxos and Larichos, who are mentioned in the "Brothers Poem" discovered in 2014, and possibly a third brother named Eurygios.[^15][^16] Ancient sources tell of Charaxos's involvement with an Egyptian courtesan named Rhodopis, whom he allegedly ransomed for a large sum, an act that Sappho criticized in one of her poems.[^17] The younger brother Larichos reportedly served as a wine-pourer in the town hall of Mytilene, an honored position reserved for young men from noble families.[^18]

As a member of the aristocratic class, Sappho would have received an education exceptional for women of her time, likely including music, poetry, and the arts.[^19] The island of Lesbos in the seventh and sixth centuries BCE was known for its flourishing poetic tradition, and Sappho was a contemporary of other renowned Lesbian poets, including Alcaeus and the statesman-poet Pittacus, who would later become tyrant of Mytilene.[^20][^21] This rich cultural environment, combined with her family's wealth and status, provided Sappho with the resources and opportunity to develop her extraordinary poetic gifts from an early age.[^22]

The political landscape of Lesbos during Sappho's youth was turbulent, characterized by factional conflicts and struggles for power among aristocratic families.[^23] These upheavals would eventually lead to Sappho's exile from her homeland, demonstrating that even her privileged background could not insulate her from the violent political currents of the age.[^24]

## Rise to Prominence

Sappho's rise to prominence as a poet occurred during the late seventh and early sixth centuries BCE, a period that witnessed the flowering of Greek lyric poetry.[^25] Unlike her male contemporaries who often focused on martial themes, athletics, or political commentary, Sappho carved out a distinctive voice by centering her poetry on personal emotion, intimate relationships, and the interior experiences of herself and the women around her.[^26][^27] This radical shift in poetic focus—from the public to the private, from the collective to the individual—represented a profound innovation in Greek literature.[^28]

During her time in Mytilene, Sappho gathered around her a circle of women and girls, often referred to by scholars using the Greek term "thiasos," a private religious and educational community.[^29][^30] This community was apparently devoted to the worship of Aphrodite, the goddess of love and beauty, with Sappho serving as a kind of intermediary between the goddess and her companions.[^31] Much scholarly debate has surrounded the nature of this group: some ancient and modern interpreters have characterized it as a school where Sappho taught music, poetry, and the arts of refinement to young women in preparation for marriage, while others have viewed it as a more informal literary coterie or circle of friends and lovers.[^32][^33]

The political turmoil that engulfed Lesbos around 600 BCE forced Sappho into exile in Sicily, likely between 604/3 and 596/5 BCE.[^34][^35] This exile occurred during the conflicts surrounding the tyrants Myrsilus and Pittacus, as rival aristocratic factions struggled for control of Mytilene.[^36] Some sources suggest Sappho may have been exiled twice for her family's opposition to the populist tyrants.[^37] However, Pittacus eventually recalled the exiled aristocrats as part of his reconciliation policy, allowing Sappho to return to Lesbos, where she would spend most of her remaining life in Mytilene.[^38][^39]

By the time of her return from exile, Sappho's poetry had already begun to circulate beyond Lesbos, earning her recognition throughout the Greek world.[^40] Her innovative use of the first-person voice, her focus on emotional interiority, and her exploration of love—particularly erotic love between women—set her apart from all previous Greek poets.[^41][^42] Ancient sources indicate that she was composing and performing poetry into the late sixth century, possibly until around 570 BCE, suggesting a long and productive career that spanned several decades.[^43]

---

## Major Achievements

Sappho's most profound achievement was her revolutionary transformation of Greek poetry itself, earning her recognition in antiquity as one of the Nine Lyric Poets and the honorific title of "Tenth Muse," a designation attributed to Plato himself.[^44][^45][^46] Where earlier Greek poetry had relied on the invocation of Muses to provide an authoritative, impersonal narrative voice, Sappho boldly centered herself in her poems, speaking directly in the first person about her own experiences, emotions, and desires.[^47][^48] This was an audacious innovation that would fundamentally shape the development of Western lyric poetry for millennia to come.[^49]

Her technical accomplishments were equally impressive. Sappho composed in the Aeolic dialect of Greek, native to Lesbos, creating works of extraordinary linguistic precision and emotional power.[^50] She is credited with inventing or perfecting the Sapphic stanza, a metrical form consisting of three lines with the pattern of two trochees, a dactyl, and two more trochees, followed by a shorter fourth line called an Adonic (one dactyl and a trochee).[^51][^52] This verse form, believed in antiquity to have been Sappho's invention, became one of the most influential meters in Western poetry, adopted by Roman poets including Catullus and Horace and continuing to inspire poets into the modern era.[^53][^54]

Sappho's poetry was characterized by its clarity of language, sharp imagery, direct quotation, and unexpected wordplay.[^55] Her work demonstrated a remarkable ability to capture the physical manifestations of emotional states, particularly the overwhelming effects of erotic love.[^56] Fragment 31, perhaps her most famous surviving work, describes in vivid detail the physical symptoms of desire: the loss of speech, the sensation of fire beneath the skin, the dimming of sight, and the ringing in the ears.[^57][^58] This fragment became one of the most imitated poems in Western literature, translated and adapted by Roman poets including Catullus (in his poem 51) and alluded to by Lucretius, Plautus, and Virgil.[^59][^60]

In antiquity, Sappho's complete works were collected into nine books organized by meter and theme, with Book I alone containing 330 Sapphic stanzas.[^61] Ancient scholars estimated that she composed approximately 10,000 lines of poetry, making her one of the most prolific of the early Greek lyric poets.[^62] Her poetry was widely performed, studied, and admired throughout the ancient Mediterranean world, and the scholars of Hellenistic Alexandria considered her among the greatest lyric poets in the Greek language.[^63][^64]

Thematically, Sappho expanded the boundaries of what poetry could address, writing extensively about love and desire in ways that no Greek poet before her had attempted.[^65] She was the first extant Greek poet to write expressly about the feelings generated by love, exploring not just the emotional but also the physical and psychological dimensions of erotic experience.[^66] Her poetry celebrated the beauty of women, the pain of separation from loved ones, the jealousy of seeing a beloved with another, and the bittersweet memories of past intimacies.[^67] This focus on the subjective, individual, and private marked a stark contrast with the epic, liturgical, and dramatic poetry that dominated Greek literature in her era.[^68]

Beyond her innovations in form and content, Sappho achieved what few women in the ancient world could: lasting fame and recognition as a literary artist in her own right.[^69] She was referred to simply as "The Poetess," just as Homer was called "The Poet," a testament to her unparalleled status among female poets in antiquity.[^70] Her influence on subsequent poets, both ancient and modern, cannot be overstated—she established the personal lyric voice as a legitimate and powerful mode of poetic expression, creating a template that poets would follow for the next 2,600 years.[^71]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Throughout history, Sappho's life and work have been subjects of intense controversy, censorship, and moral condemnation, primarily centered on questions of her sexuality and the nature of her relationships with other women.[^72] In the ancient world, interpretations of Sappho's erotic poetry varied dramatically depending on the cultural context and the prejudices of individual commentators.[^73] While earlier Greek sources appear to have celebrated her work without significant moral anxiety, later Roman and Christian authors often viewed her with suspicion, hostility, or attempted to defend her against what they considered slanderous accusations.[^74][^75]

By the Roman Imperial period, many authors who commented on Sappho's sexuality treated the claim that she had erotic relationships with other women as nothing more than malicious slander.[^76] Ancient sources, including the 10th-century Byzantine encyclopedia called the Suda, record that Sappho was "slanderously accused" of having sexual relationships with her "female pupils," suggesting that even centuries after her death, the erotic content of her poetry remained controversial.[^77][^78] Some ancient authors attempted to heterosexualize Sappho's biography entirely, inventing stories of her marriage to a man with the absurdly suggestive name "Kerkylas of Andros"—a name that translates roughly to "Prick (of the isle) of Man," clearly a comic invention rather than historical fact.[^79][^80]

The most persistent and damaging legend attached to Sappho was the story of her suicide. According to this tale, Sappho leaped to her death from the Leucadian cliffs because of her unrequited love for Phaon, a young ferryman.[^81][^82] Modern scholars universally reject this story as apocryphal, noting that Phaon was a figure of myth and legend rather than a historical person, and that the suicide narrative appears to have been invented to provide Sappho with a heterosexual romantic history.[^83][^84] Most modern critics believe she died of old age around 570 BCE.[^85]

Christian censorship represented another major source of controversy in Sappho's reception history. Later Christian censors in Alexandria, Rome, and Constantinople condemned Sappho in harsh terms, with the second-century Christian apologist Tatian calling her "a whore who sang about her own licentiousness."[^86] Medieval tradition held that Saint Gregory of Nazianzus and Pope Gregory VII ordered her works burned, though modern scholars note that there is not a single ancient source that records anyone actually gathering collections of Sappho's poetry to be destroyed.[^87][^88] The loss of most of Sappho's work appears to have resulted from natural processes of textual transmission and changing literary tastes rather than deliberate destruction.[^89]

In modern times, the "Great Sappho Question"—as scholar André Lardinois termed it—continues to generate scholarly debate and public controversy.[^90] Early translators frequently heterosexualized her poetry: Ambrose Philips's 1711 translation of the Ode to Aphrodite portrayed the object of Sappho's desire as male, a reading followed by virtually every other translator until the 20th century.[^91] This pattern of heterosexual sanitization extended across centuries of reception, with Victorian and early modern scholars often straining to deny or explain away the homoerotic content of her verses.[^92]

The scholarly paradigm of "Sappho the schoolmistress," which dominated interpretation for much of the 19th and early 20th centuries, has itself been criticized as an attempt to desexualize Sappho's relationships with other women by recasting them in purely pedagogical terms.[^93] Critics of this approach argue that the ancient testimonia about Sappho's role as a teacher are unreliable, composed centuries after her lifetime and based on the "biographical fallacy" of reading poetry as transparent autobiography.[^94] However, other scholars have pushed back against an equally extreme skepticism, arguing that dismissing all ancient testimony leaves us unable to reconstruct any historical context for Sappho's life and work.[^95]

An additional source of ancient criticism concerned perceptions of Sappho's gender expression. Greeks and Romans of later centuries sometimes interpreted her poetic skill as stemming from a "perverted form of masculinity," viewing her literary achievements through the lens of assumptions about proper feminine behavior.[^96] This reflects broader ancient anxieties about women who achieved public recognition in traditionally male-dominated spheres, particularly in the literary arts.[^97]

## Personal Life

Reconstructing Sappho's personal life presents significant challenges, as scholars must navigate between unreliable ancient biographical traditions and the fragmentary, often ambiguous evidence of her poetry itself.[^98] What can be stated with relative confidence is that Sappho came from a wealthy aristocratic family in Lesbos and that she had at least two, and possibly three, brothers.[^99][^100]

The 2014 discovery of the "Brothers Poem" provided new insights into Sappho's familial relationships, mentioning her brothers Charaxos and Larichos by name.[^101] According to the fifth-century BCE historian Herodotus, Charaxos traveled to Egypt where he became involved with a courtesan named Rhodopis, whom he ransomed for a large sum.[^102] Sappho apparently wrote a poem reproving him for this expensive romantic entanglement, suggesting she maintained close ties with her family and felt entitled to comment on their behavior.[^103] The younger brother Larichos held the honored position of wine-pourer in Mytilene's town hall, a role typically reserved for aristocratic youths.[^104]

Whether Sappho was married remains uncertain. The Suda, a 10th-century Byzantine encyclopedia, claimed she was married to "Kerkylas of Andros," but modern scholars recognize this as a comic invention rather than historical fact—the name translates to something like "Prick from the Isle of Man," clearly a joke rather than a real person's name.[^105][^106] This fabricated marriage appears to have been created by ancient comic poets, perhaps to mock Sappho or to provide her with a conventional heterosexual biography.[^107]

Ancient sources indicate that Sappho had a daughter named Cleïs (or Cleis), who is referenced in two surviving fragments.[^108][^109] In fragment 132, Sappho describes Cleïs as "agapata" (beloved), a term that in other Greek literature is used for familial rather than sexual relationships, supporting the interpretation that Cleïs was indeed Sappho's daughter.[^110] Sappho reportedly treasured her daughter deeply, with one fragment comparing Cleïs to golden flowers and stating she would not trade her for all of Lydia.[^111]

The most significant relationships documented in Sappho's poetry are those with other women. Her verses name numerous female companions, including Anactoria, Atthis, Gongyla, and others, often describing intense emotional and physical attraction to these women.[^112] The nature of Sappho's community of women—whether it was primarily religious, educational, erotic, or some combination—remains debated.[^113] Some scholars argue it was a formal thiasos devoted to Aphrodite where young women were prepared for marriage, while others view it as a more informal circle of friends and lovers.[^114][^115]

Denys Page, a prominent classical scholar, stated that Sappho's extant fragments portray "the loves and jealousies, the pleasures and pains, of Sappho and her companions," finding "no trace of any formal or official or professional relationship between them."[^116] Similarly, David Campbell judged that while Sappho may have "presided over a literary coterie," the evidence for "a formal appointment as priestess or teacher is hard to find."[^117] This suggests that Sappho's relationships with the women in her circle may have been more personal and less institutional than the "schoolmistress" model implies.[^118]

Regarding her personality, what emerges from the poetry is a figure of remarkable emotional intensity and artistic self-awareness. Sappho wrote with supreme confidence in her own poetic abilities, predicting that "later on, even in an age unlike our own, someone will remember who we are"—a prophecy that has been fulfilled across 2,600 years.[^119] Her poems reveal someone capable of profound tenderness, fierce jealousy, playful humor, and acute observations of both human nature and the natural world.[^120] The fragments suggest a personality that was passionate, articulate, proud of her art, and unafraid to explore the full range of human emotion in her verse.[^121]

## Legacy

Sappho's legacy extends across more than two and a half millennia, profoundly shaping the development of Western literature, the concept of the lyric voice, and modern understandings of gender and sexuality.[^122] Her impact can be traced through multiple overlapping spheres: her influence on subsequent poets ancient and modern, her role as a cultural symbol, and her significance in feminist and LGBTQ+ movements.[^123]

In the ancient world, Sappho's influence was immediate and enduring. The scholars of Hellenistic Alexandria included her in the canon of Nine Lyric Poets, the highest recognition possible for a Greek poet, and she was the only woman among them.[^124][^125] Roman poets engaged extensively with her work: Catullus in the first century BCE established the themes and meters of Sappho's poetry as part of Latin literature, giving his lover the name "Lesbia" in homage to Sappho and translating Fragment 31 in his own Poem 51.[^126][^127] Other Roman poets including Lucretius, Horace, Ovid, Plautus, and Virgil all alluded to or adapted Sappho's work, embedding her poetic innovations into the foundation of Latin literature.[^128][^129]

During the Renaissance, interest in Sappho's work revived as classical texts were rediscovered and translated, though translators often struggled with or deliberately distorted the homoerotic content of her poetry.[^130] By the late 19th century, however, lesbian writers such as Michael Field and Amy Levy became interested in Sappho specifically for her sexuality, and by the turn of the 20th century she was considered a "patron saint of lesbians."[^131] The terms "lesbian," "sapphic," and "sapphist" began to be popularized as descriptions for homosexual and queer women in the 1890s, forever linking Sappho's name and homeland to female same-sex desire.[^132][^133]

In the 20th century, Sappho became a model for the "New Woman"—independent and educated women who desired social and sexual autonomy—and by the 1960s emerged as a central figure in second-wave feminism.[^134] During the feminist and gay liberation movements of the 1960s and 1970s, Sappho became a mascot and role model for queer people, with radical feminist texts such as Sidney Abott and Barbara Love's "Sappho Was a Right-on Woman" (1977) invoking her name even when the content had little direct connection to her poetry.[^135][^136] Paris in the early 20th century became such a destination for modernist lesbians that their community was known as "Paris Lesbos."[^137]

In contemporary culture, Sappho's legacy continues to evolve. Social media hashtags like #sapphic and #wlw (women who love women) are widely used across digital platforms, introducing her name to new generations.[^138] In the early 2000s, queer women began making pilgrimages to Lesvos, Sappho's home island, connecting physically with the geographic source of the word "lesbian."[^139] Modern poets including Ezra Pound, Anne Carson, Emily Dickinson, and Sylvia Plath have all shown clear influences of Sappho's technique and thematic focus, reimagining and reinterpreting her fragmentary work for contemporary audiences.[^140][^141]

Beyond literature, Sappho's influence extends into music, visual arts, and theater, where her themes and imagery continue to find new expressions.[^142] The fragmentary nature of her surviving work has itself become a kind of artistic inspiration, with the gaps and silences in her texts inviting creative responses from translators, poets, and artists who must navigate between scholarly accuracy and imaginative reconstruction.[^143]

Scholarly interest in Sappho has never been higher. Major papyrus discoveries in 2004 (the "Tithonus poem") and 2014 (the "Brothers Poem" and other fragments) generated international media coverage and demonstrated that new Sappho texts can still be recovered from ancient sources.[^144][^145] These discoveries sparked renewed academic debate about her life, her community of women, and the interpretation of her fragmentary corpus.[^146]

The enduring power of Sappho's legacy lies in her dual status as both a historical figure and a cultural symbol whose meaning shifts across different contexts and eras.[^147] She represents simultaneously the ancient Greek lyric tradition, the power of the female voice in literature, the celebration of same-sex desire, and the fragility and persistence of cultural memory itself.[^148] Her prediction that she would be remembered "even in an age unlike our own" has proven remarkably prescient—in the 21st century, Sappho remains as relevant and contested as ever, a figure whose fragmentary texts continue to generate new meanings and new debates.[^149]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Sappho's reputation among scholars and poets throughout history has been overwhelmingly positive, with many considering her one of the greatest lyric poets in any language.[^150] Plato's designation of her as the "Tenth Muse" set the tone for centuries of lavish praise from ancient authors who recognized her extraordinary gifts.[^151][^152] The third-century BCE poet Dioscorides wrote that the Muses themselves were nine in number until Sappho joined them, making ten, while another epigram attributed to Plato declared that Aphrodite's daughters were the nine Muses until Sappho came, making them ten.[^153]

Modern scholars have continued this tradition of high praise while providing more specific analysis of Sappho's innovations. Emmet Robbins described Fragment 31 as "probably the single most famous poem to come down from Antiquity," highlighting its enduring influence on Western literature.[^154] Marilyn Skinner has argued that Sappho's poetry creates "a discursive space that is distinctively female, not wholly inscribed within the discourse of patriarchy," recognizing her as offering an alternative to male-dominated poetic traditions.[^155] Jane McIntosh Snyder has called Sappho "the first female voice of the artist in the Western tradition," emphasizing that "that lyric voice of the private female poet is vitally important in the history of the Western literary tradition."[^156]

Classical scholar Gregory Nagy and others have emphasized Sappho's revolutionary use of the first-person perspective and her focus on subjective emotional experience.[^157] Where epic poetry presented collective narratives and mythological tales, Sappho centered individual feeling and personal relationships, fundamentally expanding what poetry could be and do.[^158] Her technical mastery of meter, particularly her development of the Sapphic stanza, has been recognized as a major contribution to the formal resources available to all subsequent poets.[^159]

Contemporary scholars have also celebrated the 2004 and 2014 papyrus discoveries, with M.L. West describing the 2014 findings as "the greatest [discovery] for 92 years" and Marilyn Skinner calling the 2004 Tithonus poem "the trouvaille of a lifetime."[^160][^161] These discoveries have renewed scholarly interest and demonstrated that even 2,600 years after her death, Sappho can still surprise and enlighten us with previously unknown works.[^162]

Feminist and queer scholars have particularly championed Sappho as a pioneering voice for women's experiences and same-sex desire in literature.[^163] Her unflinching exploration of erotic love between women, her celebration of female beauty, and her creation of a poetic community centered on women's relationships provide a rare ancient precedent for celebrating queer female existence.[^164] As such, she has become not just an important historical figure but an inspirational symbol for contemporary movements for gender and sexual equality.[^165]

### Negative Views

Despite Sappho's towering literary reputation, her work and life have attracted significant criticism, censure, and moral condemnation throughout history, particularly from those uncomfortable with the homoerotic content of her poetry.[^166] The second-century Christian apologist Tatian exemplified this hostile tradition, denouncing Sappho as "a whore who sang about her own licentiousness," a condemnation that reflected early Christian anxieties about pagan sexuality and female autonomy.[^167]

Medieval Christian authorities allegedly ordered Sappho's works destroyed, with tradition claiming that Saint Gregory of Nazianzus and Pope Gregory VII commanded her poetry to be burned.[^168] While modern scholars have questioned whether such organized burnings actually occurred—noting the absence of ancient sources documenting such events—the very existence of these traditions reveals the depth of moral opposition to Sappho's work among some Christian authorities.[^169] The characterization of her poetry as licentious or morally corrupting persisted for centuries, contributing to negative assessments of both her character and her literary merit.[^170]

In the Roman period, some authors interpreted Sappho's poetic excellence as stemming from what they considered a "perverted form of masculinity," suggesting that her literary achievements somehow violated proper feminine behavior.[^171] This critique reflects broader ancient anxieties about women who excelled in public, traditionally male-dominated spheres, and the assumption that exceptional female achievement required masculine qualities.[^172] These interpretations often carried a tone of moral disapproval, implying that Sappho had transgressed appropriate gender boundaries.[^173]

Modern scholarly skepticism has also produced what might be considered a different form of negative assessment. In the latter half of the 20th century, scholars increasingly questioned the reliability of Greek lyric poetry as a source of autobiographical information.[^174] Some, like Mary Lefkowitz, have argued that almost nothing can be known about the lives of early Greek poets such as Sappho, effectively dismissing most of the ancient biographical tradition as unreliable fabrication.[^175] While this scholarly skepticism aims for historical rigor rather than moral condemnation, it has the effect of denying the validity of much of what previous generations believed about Sappho's life and relationships.[^176]

Critics of the "biographical fallacy" argue that ancient testimonia about Sappho were composed centuries after her death and reflect the biases and assumptions of later authors rather than historical reality.[^177] This view suggests that we cannot reliably know whether Sappho led a thiasos, whether she had erotic relationships with other women, or indeed much of anything about her personal life beyond the fragmentary poems themselves.[^178] While this skepticism serves important scholarly purposes, it can also be seen as diminishing Sappho's significance by reducing her to an almost entirely unknown figure about whom we can make few certain claims.[^179]

Some ancient sources attempted to defend Sappho from accusations of same-sex relationships by insisting they were merely slander, effectively treating her lesbianism as something shameful that required rebuttal.[^180] The 10th-century Suda's claim that Sappho was "slanderously accused" of relationships with her female companions exemplifies this defensive posture, which treats the homoerotic interpretation of her poetry as defamatory rather than accurate.[^181] This tradition of "defending" Sappho by denying or minimizing the lesbian content of her work persists in some quarters even today.[^182]

## Famous Quotes

> "Some say cavalry and others claim infantry or a fleet of long oars is the supreme sight on the black earth. I say it is the one you love."
> — Fragment 16, expressing Sappho's prioritization of love over military glory

> "The moon has set, and the Pleiades; it is midnight, and time passes, and I sleep alone."
> — Fragment 168b (the "Midnight Poem"), one of the most famous surviving fragments, capturing loneliness and longing

> "I declare that later on, even in an age unlike our own, someone will remember who we are."
> — Fragment 147, a remarkably prescient prophecy about her enduring fame

> "When I look on you a moment, then I can speak no more, but my tongue falls silent, and at once a delicate flame courses beneath my skin, and with my eyes I see nothing, and my ears hum, and a cold sweat bathes me, and a trembling seizes me all over."
> — Fragment 31, describing the overwhelming physical effects of erotic desire

> "Love is a cunning weaver of fantasies and fables."
> — Fragment on the deceptive nature of love

> "I would not trade her [Cleïs, her daughter] for all Lydia nor lovely Lesbos."
> — Fragment expressing maternal devotion

> "You may forget but let me tell you this: someone in some future time will think of us."
> — Another version of her prophecy about lasting remembrance
