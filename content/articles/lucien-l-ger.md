## Summary

France's longest-serving prisoner who spent 41 years in prison for the murder of a boy.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2005 | Released after 41 years in prison |

## Background

- **Context**: Léger became a widely cited example in France of an extremely long custodial sentence being served largely for a single homicide, with later public debate focusing on detention length and rehabilitation.
- **Primary public source**: Most readily available English-language detail is summarized in the linked Wikipedia article; the outline below stays at a high level and avoids speculation beyond that public record.

## Case (high-level)

- He was convicted in France for the **murder of a child** (a boy).
- The case is frequently referenced in media and criminal-justice discussions because of the **duration of imprisonment** that followed.

## Imprisonment and release

- He spent **approximately 41 years** in prison before being released.
- The release in **2005** is often described as marking one of the longest periods of incarceration served in France in the modern era.

## Notes

- **Name**: The accented spelling “Léger” is commonly used; some sources may omit diacritics.
- **Why he is notable**: Not primarily for a public career, but for the historical/legal significance of his sentence length and the public attention the case received.

## Key points (at a glance)

- **Known for**: one of France’s longest-known incarcerations for a single murder conviction.
- **Public discussion**: often cited in debates about **parole**, **post-sentence supervision**, and the goals of long-term imprisonment.
- **Lifecycle**: imprisonment dominated the majority of his adult life; he died a few years after release.
