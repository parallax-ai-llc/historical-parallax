## Summary

American former intelligence analyst who leaked classified documents about the US drone assassination program to The Intercept. The documents revealed that up to 90% of drone strike victims were not intended targets. Sentenced to 45 months.

## Context and Significance

Hale's leak became part of a broader public debate about the scope, oversight, and consequences of the post-9/11 counterterrorism apparatus.

The disclosures were notable not only for what they suggested about targeting outcomes, but also for how they illustrated the bureaucratic workflow behind remote strikes: the production of intelligence, the translation of that intelligence into target nominations, and the operational execution.

## Key Points

- **Role and access:** Hale worked as an intelligence analyst and had access to internal reporting related to drone operations.
- **Public-interest framing:** Supporters described the disclosure as whistleblowing intended to highlight civilian harm and accountability gaps.
- **Government framing:** Prosecutors treated the act as an unlawful disclosure of national defense information, emphasizing deterrence and secrecy protections.

## Aftermath

The case is frequently cited in discussions of how the Espionage Act is used in leak prosecutions, including debates over intent, public-interest defenses, and the difficulty of litigating classification decisions.

It also intersects with wider scrutiny of U.S. drone policy, including questions of proportionality, identification standards, and the transparency of casualty assessments.
