## Summary

Michael Levitt is a biophysicist who shared the 2013 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for developing multiscale models for complex chemical systems.

## Scientific Contributions (High-Level)

Levitt is best known for pioneering computational approaches that connect different levels of description in chemistry and biology. The work recognized by the 2013 Nobel Prize in Chemistry emphasized multiscale modeling, where parts of a molecular system are treated with more detailed methods while other parts are treated with simpler approximations.

## Why Multiscale Modeling Matters

Many biologically important molecules (for example, proteins and nucleic acids) are too large and dynamic to simulate fully at the most computationally expensive level of theory for long timescales. Multiscale approaches allow researchers to study structure, motion, and interactions in complex systems while keeping computations tractable.

## Fields Influenced

- Structural biology and protein modeling.
- Computational chemistry and molecular dynamics.
- Methods development for combining classical and quantum treatments in simulations.

## Broader Impact

Levitt's work helped establish computational modeling as a core tool alongside experiment in modern chemical and biological research. It also contributed to the development of software and methodological standards used widely in academia and industry.

## Selected Themes

A recurring theme in Levitt's research is using simplified representations to capture essential physical behavior, then refining where precision is most needed. This pragmatic balance between accuracy and computational feasibility is a defining idea in computational modeling.
