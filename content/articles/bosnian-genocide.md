## Summary

Ethnic cleansing and genocide during the Bosnian War from 1992-1995. Over 100,000 killed and 2.2 million displaced.

## Historical Background (High Level)

The violence occurred in the context of the breakup of Yugoslavia and the outbreak of the Bosnian War (1992–1995).
The conflict involved multiple armed forces and political authorities, with civilian populations frequently targeted through forced displacement, detention, and mass violence.
The term “ethnic cleansing” is often used to describe campaigns aimed at removing communities from territory, while “genocide” is a legal term with specific criteria under international law.

## Srebrenica

The mass killing of Bosniak men and boys at Srebrenica in July 1995 is widely recognized as genocide in international jurisprudence.
Srebrenica has become a central reference point in the broader discussion of atrocities during the war, including questions of prevention, protection mandates, and accountability.
Commemoration practices and identification of victims have continued for decades, including through forensic work and DNA-based identification.

## International Justice and Accountability

The International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY) prosecuted numerous cases arising from the Bosnian War.
Judgments from international courts helped establish legal findings about crimes against humanity, war crimes, and, in certain circumstances, genocide.
Accountability processes also included domestic prosecutions and ongoing efforts to document events and preserve records.

## Humanitarian Consequences

Beyond the direct death toll, the war produced large-scale displacement, long-term trauma, and widespread destruction of homes and cultural sites.
Post-war reconstruction involved refugee return programs, transitional justice initiatives, and international assistance.
Debates over terminology and historical interpretation remain politically sensitive in the region.
