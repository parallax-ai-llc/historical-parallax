## Summary

On November 1, 1478, Pope Sixtus IV issued the papal bull *Exigit Sincerae Devotionis Affectus*, authorizing
the Catholic Monarchs of Spain — Ferdinand II of Aragon and Isabella I of Castile — to establish an
Inquisition in their kingdoms to combat heresy. Unlike the earlier Medieval Inquisition, which had been under
direct papal supervision, the Spanish Inquisition was firmly controlled by the Spanish crown, making it a
uniquely royal institution that blended theological enforcement with political statecraft. The immediate
trigger for its establishment was concern about *conversos* — Jews who had converted to Christianity, often
under pressure during earlier pogroms — who were suspected of secretly continuing to practice Judaism, a
capital heresy in the eyes of the Church and a political problem for a monarchy seeking unity in the aftermath
of the Reconquista.

The Spanish Inquisition operated for three and a half centuries, from its formal establishment in 1480 with
the appointment of its first inquisitors to its final abolition in 1834. During that time it tried somewhere
between 150,000 and 300,000 individuals, the vast majority for relatively minor infractions, and executed
between 3,000 and 5,000 people — a figure that, while devastating to individuals and communities, is
considerably lower than the myths generated by early Protestant and later Enlightenment polemicists, some of
whom claimed execution tolls of hundreds of thousands. What the Inquisition did achieve in terms of human
suffering was harder to quantify: the psychological terror of denunciation, the destruction of family
networks, the forced abandonment of cultural identity, the public humiliation of the *auto-da-fé*, and the
expulsion of entire communities — Jews in 1492, Moriscos in 1609 — from the country they had called home for
generations.

The Inquisition's cultural legacy is deeply contested. For defenders of early modern Spain, it was an
instrument of state consolidation and religious order, comparable in its methods to secular judicial practices
of the period and more restrained in its use of torture and execution than many contemporary secular courts.
For the descendants of *conversos* and Moriscos, for Jews of Sephardic heritage, and for historians of
religious persecution, it represents the systematic weaponization of faith against minority communities, with
consequences — demographic, economic, intellectual — that historians of Spain continue to weigh centuries
later. The Inquisition entered European consciousness as the symbol of Catholic intolerance and became a
cultural reference point — from Dostoevsky's "Grand Inquisitor" to Monty Python's famous sketch — that long
outlasted its historical reality.

## Historical Context

The Spanish Inquisition was born from the convergence of two long historical processes: the seven-century
Reconquista, by which Christian kingdoms gradually reconquered the Iberian Peninsula from Muslim rule, and the
complex, often violent history of the Jewish and Muslim communities who had lived alongside Christians
throughout that period. By the fifteenth century, Spain was a religiously plural society in which large
numbers of Jews and Muslims — and substantial communities of converts from both — were woven into the fabric
of urban commerce, royal administration, and cultural life. The Reconquista's completion, symbolized by the
fall of Granada in January 1492, brought this pluralism to a crisis point: Ferdinand and Isabella were
determined to forge a united kingdom under a single faith, and the *converso* question was the most pressing
obstacle.

The mass conversions of Iberian Jews had been proceeding throughout the fourteenth and early fifteenth
centuries, accelerated by pogroms in 1391 that killed thousands and forced many survivors to convert under
threat of death. By the mid-fifteenth century, Spain had a substantial *converso* population — "New
Christians" of Jewish descent who had been baptized and who, by canon law, were full members of the Church.
Many had risen to prominent positions in commerce, the professions, royal administration, and even the clergy.
The question of whether their conversions were sincere, or whether they secretly continued to practice Judaism
— maintaining Shabbat, observing dietary laws, conducting Jewish lifecycle rituals in private — was one that
their *Cristiano viejo* ("Old Christian") neighbors viewed with suspicion that was inflamed by economic
resentment and ethnic prejudice as much as genuine theological concern.

The institutional response Ferdinand and Isabella devised was administratively innovative. Previous
inquisitions in Spain had been under papal jurisdiction; the new institution would be under royal control,
with inquisitors nominated by the monarchs and accountable to a Suprema (Supreme Council of the Inquisition)
in Madrid. This gave the Spanish monarchy an unprecedented instrument of social and ideological discipline.
Pope Sixtus IV, who initially authorized the Inquisition, later became alarmed at its abuses and attempted to
reassert papal oversight, writing in 1482 that the Spanish inquisitors had been "moved by zeal for the faith
and also — which we regret to say — by avarice, hatred, and other motives" and had imprisoned, tortured, and
burned "true and faithful Christians." Ferdinand bluntly informed the Pope that his complaints were unwelcome
and that the Inquisition was a matter of royal prerogative. The Pope backed down — a telling illustration of
the institution's fundamentally political character.

## The Event

The Inquisition became formally operational in 1480, when its first inquisitors — Miguel de Morillo and Juan
de San Martín — began proceedings in Seville. The initial targets were *conversos* suspected of Judaizing, and
the early years were the bloodiest: in Seville alone, it is estimated that some 700 individuals were burned in
person and hundreds more in effigy in the early 1480s. The appointment in 1483 of Tomás de Torquemada as the
first Inquisitor General gave the institution its defining figure — a Dominican friar who became the
embodiment of religious terror in Protestant European memory, though historians note that his personal role
has been considerably exaggerated. Torquemada systematized the Inquisition's procedures, issuing a series of
instructions that formalized how suspects were to be identified, arrested, tried, and sentenced.

The procedural structure of the Inquisition was, in its own terms, more legally sophisticated than popular
mythology suggests. Suspects were not simply seized and tortured into confession; there was a defined process.
An inquisitor would arrive in a town and issue an "edict of grace" — a period during which individuals could
voluntarily confess their heresies and receive reconciliation with the Church at the cost of relatively mild
penalties. This was followed by an "edict of faith" — a public order requiring all Christians to denounce
anyone they knew to be practicing heresy. Anonymous denunciations were accepted, creating a system that was
profoundly corrosive of social trust. The accused was then arrested, their property sequestered, and they were
held in inquisitorial prison — often for months or years — while the case against them was assembled. They
were not told the names of their accusers.

The role of torture in the Inquisition's proceedings has been both exaggerated and understated. The
Inquisition did use torture — primarily the *garrucha* (strappado), the *toca* (water torture), and the
*potro* (rack) — but under formal rules: it was limited in duration, a physician had to be present,
confessions obtained solely under torture were not binding and had to be ratified the following day, and
clerics and nobles were generally exempt. Torture was used in a minority of cases — perhaps ten to fifteen
percent — and was restrained compared to the judicial torture routinely practiced in secular courts across
Europe. The purpose was to obtain confirmation of evidence already assembled, not to produce confessions from
scratch. The Inquisition's procedural constraints were real, but they operated within a system in which the
accused had no defense counsel, faced anonymous accusers, and was presumed to be hiding something by virtue of
the charge itself.

The most dramatic public manifestation of the Inquisition was the *auto-da-fé* ("act of faith") — a solemn
public ceremony in which sentences were read, penances assigned, and the unrepentant handed over to the
secular arm for execution. The term has been popularly associated with burning at the stake, but most *auto-
da-fé* sentences involved public humiliation: the wearing of the *sambenito* (a distinctive penitential
garment), flogging, confiscation of property, exile, or imprisonment. Only the unrepentant and the relapsed —
those who refused to recant or who had previously been reconciled and then returned to heresy — were handed to
the secular authorities to be burned. Even here, last-minute confessions were accepted: the penitent would be
strangled before the flames were lit rather than burned alive. This distinction was meaningful to the
Inquisition, though its comfort to the condemned is difficult to assess.

The Inquisition's purview expanded over time beyond *conversos* suspected of Judaizing. It investigated
Moriscos (Muslim converts suspected of secretly practicing Islam), Lutherans and other Protestants (from the
1550s), *alumbrados* (mystics suspected of antinomian spirituality), bigamists, sodomites, and eventually
anyone accused of witchcraft, blasphemy, or impeding the Inquisition's work. The remarkable sociologist of the
Inquisition, Henry Charles Lea, and later Henry Kamen, mapped the distribution of cases and found that the
Inquisition's activity waxed and waned dramatically over its three and a half centuries: periods of intense
activity against specific communities were followed by decades of relative dormancy. By the seventeenth and
eighteenth centuries, the Inquisition had evolved into something closer to a censorship office and a regulator
of cultural orthodoxy than the engine of mass terror of its early decades.

## Multiple Perspectives

### Spanish Catholic and Crown View: Unity, Sovereignty, and the Logic of the Reconquista

From the perspective of Ferdinand and Isabella and their theological advisors, the Inquisition was not an
instrument of persecution but of justice, order, and spiritual protection. The Reconquista had been a
centuries-long struggle to reclaim Spain for Christendom, and its completion in 1492 represented a
providential mandate to build a kingdom under God. In this framework, religious uniformity was not bigotry but
the foundation of political cohesion — a view that was, it must be said, standard across virtually every
European monarchy of the period, Protestant and Catholic alike. The sincerity of the *converso* problem was,
in their eyes, a genuine pastoral and political crisis: if baptism was meaningless — if thousands of
Christians were secretly Jews — then the sacramental economy of the Church was being mocked and the social
fabric of Christian society undermined.

The crown's insistence on controlling the Inquisition rather than leaving it to Rome was equally logical from
a Spanish royal perspective. The papacy had its own political interests; the Spanish monarchy had a kingdom to
govern. Making the inquisitors royal appointees, accountable to the Suprema rather than to Rome, gave the
crown a disciplined tool it could direct at specific communities at specific moments as political needs
required. The wealth of convicted heretics — confiscated to support the Inquisition's operations and, in the
early years, to fund the crown's military campaigns — was a secondary consideration that Ferdinand understood
and did not attempt to disguise. He famously remarked that "the preservation of the faith" and "the needs of
the treasury" were not incompatible aims. The auto-da-fé was simultaneously a theological statement and a
political spectacle: a public demonstration of the crown's power to enforce orthodoxy, attended by thousands
and presided over by royalty on important occasions.

The crown's view of the 1492 Alhambra Decree — the expulsion of all Jews who refused baptism from Spain — was
consistent with this logic. Having established the Inquisition to police the orthodoxy of baptized Christians,
the continued presence of openly Jewish communities was seen as a constant temptation and source of
backsliding for *conversos*. The estimated 100,000–200,000 Jews expelled were not, in the crown's framing,
persecuted for their faith — they were invited to convert and remain, as many did. Those who chose exile were
exercising an option. That this option meant abandoning their homes, property, and community network of
centuries, and that it exposed them to terrible dangers during the expulsion itself (many died at sea, were
robbed, enslaved, or killed in transit), was acknowledged without regret by the crown. The unity of Spain
demanded a price, and the crown was prepared to pay it in others' suffering.

### Jewish (Converso and Sephardic) View: Terror, Dispossession, and the Sephardic Diaspora

For the *converso* community — Jews who had converted to Christianity under pressure or by choice over the
preceding century — the Inquisition represented an impossible situation with no exit. They had accepted
baptism and its obligations; they were, by every legal and theological standard, Christians. Yet they faced a
system predicated on the assumption that their conversion was suspect, that any maintenance of Jewish family
custom was heresy, and that their very identity as descendants of Jews made them objects of permanent
suspicion. The crime they were accused of — secretly practicing Judaism — was one that the Inquisition's
procedures made virtually impossible to definitively disprove. A *converso* who lit candles on Friday evening,
who avoided pork, who observed Passover in any form, who recited Hebrew prayers, or who was denounced by a
neighbor with a grudge could find themselves before the inquisitors. And the Inquisition's acceptance of
anonymous denunciations meant that economic rivals, personal enemies, and envious neighbors could deploy it as
a weapon.

The terror was not only physical. The *sambenito*, the penitential garment worn by the condemned, was publicly
displayed in the local church after the *auto-da-fé*, bearing the name and offense of the condemned — a
permanent mark of dishonor extending to the entire family. The concept of *limpieza de sangre* ("purity of
blood"), which emerged in the mid-fifteenth century and became codified in the statutes of many institutions
and guilds, required proof that an applicant had no Jewish or Moorish ancestry for a specified number of
generations. This racial doctrine — and it was, in modern terms, a racial doctrine, applied to bloodline
rather than to belief — meant that the stigma of *converso* descent could pursue a family for generations even
after any connection to Jewish practice had entirely disappeared. Spain was developing, in effect, an ideology
of ethnic discrimination dressed in theological language.

The Alhambra Decree of 1492 — issued the same year Columbus sailed — destroyed communities that had existed in
Spain for over a millennium. The Sephardic Jews who refused baptism and chose exile created diaspora
communities across the Mediterranean world: in the Ottoman Empire (where Sultan Bayezid II reportedly welcomed
them, saying Ferdinand had "impoverished his own country and enriched mine"), in the Netherlands, in North
Africa, and eventually in the Americas. They carried with them the Ladino language (Judeo-Spanish), a
distinctive musical tradition, religious customs that preserved pre-Expulsion Iberian Jewish life, and a
collective memory of loss that persisted for centuries. Sephardic Jews in Istanbul and Thessaloniki were still
speaking a form of fifteenth-century Spanish and mourning the Expulsion centuries later. The demographic and
intellectual loss to Spain itself was immense: many of the *conversos* who were tried, humiliated, or driven
into exile were among the country's most educated and economically productive citizens.

### Muslim (Morisco) View: Forced Conversion, Cultural Erasure, and Final Expulsion

The Muslim experience of the Spanish Inquisition followed a similar but distinct trajectory. After the fall of
Granada in 1492, the *capitulations* negotiated for the surrender promised Muslims the right to practice their
faith, speak Arabic, and maintain their customs. These promises were honored for approximately seven years. By
1502, under pressure from the Inquisitor Cardinal Cisneros, who had ordered a mass forced baptism campaign
after a Muslim uprising in the Albaicín quarter of Granada, Isabella issued an edict requiring all Muslims in
Castile to convert to Christianity or leave. Aragón's Muslims, called *mudéjares*, were allowed to remain as
Muslims until 1526, when Charles V forced their conversion as well. Overnight, the Muslim population of Spain
became Moriscos — Christians by compulsion.

The Morisco experience under the Inquisition was shaped by a particular cultural violence: the systematic
destruction of their identity. Laws issued across the sixteenth century progressively banned the use of Arabic
in speech and writing, prohibited traditional clothing and bathing practices (the Arabic hammam was banned on
the grounds that ritual bathing was a sign of continuing Islamic practice), ordered the destruction of Arabic
books, and forbade traditional music and dance. Moriscos were forbidden to give their children Arabic names.
The Inquisition monitored compliance with these prohibitions, and the definition of what constituted evidence
of secret Islam was extraordinarily broad: abstaining from pork or wine, washing before prayer, facing east
during prayer, circumcision, refusing to eat food blessed in a Christian ceremony — any of these could serve
as the basis for an inquisitorial charge.

The culmination of the Morisco story was the expulsion of 1609–1614, ordered by Philip III on the advice of
the Duke of Lerma and Archbishop Ribera of Valencia. An estimated 300,000 Moriscos were expelled from Spain —
the largest forced migration in early modern European history before the twentieth century. The argument
offered was that after a century of Inquisition surveillance and cultural suppression, the Moriscos remained
unassimilated and potentially treasonous (there were genuine fears of a Morisco uprising in coordination with
Ottoman or North African powers). The expulsion devastated the agricultural regions of Valencia and Aragon,
where Morisco farmers and craftsmen had formed a significant portion of the workforce. Contemporary accounts
described convoys of expelled Moriscos being robbed, killed, and enslaved during the journey; many died at
sea. Their property was confiscated; their communities were obliterated. For the descendants of those
expelled, who settled across North Africa and parts of the Middle East, the *Expulsión* remained a defining
wound in cultural memory.

### Modern Historical Reassessment: Black Legend, Revisionism, and the Inquisition's True Record

The historiography of the Spanish Inquisition has been shaped by a centuries-long polemic known as the
*Leyenda Negra* — the "Black Legend" — a tradition of anti-Spanish, anti-Catholic polemicism originating in
Protestant Europe that depicted the Inquisition as a uniquely monstrous institution responsible for hundreds
of thousands of deaths and representing the quintessence of Catholic tyranny. The Black Legend was propagated
in works like the *Relación* of Juan Antonio Llorente (1818), who estimated that the Inquisition had executed
over 31,000 people, and in popular histories that drew on martyrological literature without critical
evaluation of primary sources. By the twentieth century, these numbers had inflated in popular consciousness
to extraordinary levels — a generation of Europeans and Americans assumed the Inquisition had burned hundreds
of thousands.

The revisionist scholarship of the twentieth century, based on systematic examination of inquisitorial
archives that became available to scholars, produced dramatically different numbers. Henry Charles Lea's
meticulous research, extended by Henry Kamen's landmark study *The Spanish Inquisition: A Historical Revision*
(1997), established that the total number of executions carried out by the Spanish Inquisition across its
entire three-and-a-half-century history was probably between 3,000 and 5,000 — still a grave toll, but
radically different from the mythology. Moreover, the Inquisition's procedural safeguards, while limited by
modern standards, were in some respects more protective of the accused than contemporary secular courts, which
routinely used torture without restriction and had no requirement to ratify confessions made under duress.
Kamen's work was important in cutting through Protestant polemic to reveal a more complex institutional
reality.

The revisionist scholarship has, however, sometimes been taken further than the evidence supports. The lower
execution figures do not diminish the Inquisition's capacity for social destruction: the psychological terror
of living under permanent suspicion, the network of informants that corroded community solidarity, the forced
cultural transformation of Moriscos, the expulsion of the Jewish community, the persecution of *conversos*
whose Jewish memories were private and harmed no one, and the long-term intellectual damage of an institution
that banned books, enforced orthodoxy, and drove out or silenced some of Spain's most intellectually
adventurous minds. The relationship between the Inquisition's culture of religious conformity and Spain's
relative intellectual stagnation compared to Protestant northern Europe in the seventeenth and eighteenth
centuries is debated, but the censorship of Renaissance and Enlightenment texts and the atmosphere of self-
censorship among educated Spaniards are documented realities. The Inquisition lasted until 1834 — nine years
after the end of the Napoleonic Wars, into the age of the first railways — an institution of the medieval
imagination stubbornly surviving into modernity.

## Legacy & Consequences

The Spanish Inquisition's most immediate and lasting demographic consequence was the destruction of Spain's
Jewish and Muslim communities. The Sephardic diaspora, created by the 1492 Expulsion, dispersed one of
medieval Europe's most intellectually and commercially vibrant Jewish populations across the Mediterranean
world. The Ottoman Empire benefited enormously from this migration: Sephardic physicians, merchants, scholars,
and craftsmen contributed to the cultural and economic life of Constantinople, Thessaloniki, Safed, and other
cities. Spain's loss was the Ottoman Empire's gain. The Morisco expulsion of 1609–1614 had similarly damaging
effects on Spanish agriculture and crafts, and historians have linked the demographic contraction of Valencia
and Aragon in the seventeenth century partly to the loss of Morisco labor. The intellectual impoverishment is
harder to quantify but no less real: writers, scholars, and scientists of *converso* descent who left Spain or
were silenced by the Inquisition's censorship represent an incalculable loss to Spanish cultural life.

The Inquisition's longer-term effects on Spanish intellectual culture are evident in the history of the Index
of Prohibited Books, which the Spanish Inquisition maintained and enforced with particular rigor. Works by
Erasmus, Machiavelli, and eventually many of the canonical texts of the European scientific and philosophical
revolutions were banned from circulation in Spain. The result was a growing divergence between Spanish
intellectual life and the scientific revolution unfolding in Protestant northern Europe. This is not to say
that Spain produced no significant intellectual figures in the early modern period — the theological
sophistication of Suárez, the mysticism of Teresa of Ávila and John of the Cross, and the literary genius of
Cervantes and Lope de Vega all flourished under the Inquisition's shadow — but the tradition of free inquiry
that drove the Scientific Revolution was systematically inhibited. Spain's eventual relative decline from the
heights of its sixteenth-century imperial power is a complex story, but the Inquisition's culture of
intellectual conformity is part of it.

The Inquisition's cultural memory outlasted the institution by centuries and continues to shape how its
history is deployed in religious and political argument. For secular and anti-clerical traditions, the
Inquisition became the exemplar of what organized religion does when it possesses coercive power — a permanent
argument for the separation of church and state. For Spanish nationalists and Catholic apologists, the
revisionist historiography has been used to argue that the Inquisition has been unfairly demonized and that
its record compares favorably to secular alternatives. For the descendants of those expelled — Sephardic Jews
who still preserve the keys to ancestral homes in Toledo and Córdoba, Morisco communities in North Africa who
remember the *Expulsión* in song and story — the Inquisition is not a historical controversy but a family
wound. Spain's Law 12/2015, which granted citizenship to descendants of Sephardic Jews expelled in 1492, was a
small gesture toward acknowledgment of a history that will not be reduced to a footnote.
