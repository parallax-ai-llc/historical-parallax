## Summary

Plotinus (204-270 CE) was a Greco-Roman philosopher born in Egypt who founded Neoplatonism, one of the most influential philosophical systems in Western intellectual history. His metaphysical doctrine of emanation from "the One" profoundly shaped Christian, Islamic, and Jewish theology for over a millennium. Despite refusing to have his portrait painted during his lifetime, his teachings—compiled by his student Porphyry into the *Enneads*—became foundational texts that influenced thinkers from Augustine to Renaissance philosophers, establishing contemplation and mystical union with the divine as the highest human achievement.[^1][^2]

---

## Early Life and Background

Plotinus was born around 204 or 205 CE in Lycopolis (modern-day Asyut), Egypt, during the reign of the Roman Empire.[^3] Despite his later prominence, Plotinus maintained extraordinary reticence about his personal origins. According to his student and biographer Porphyry, "Plotinus, the philosopher, seemed ashamed of being in the body," a sentiment so profound that "he could never be induced to tell of his ancestry, his parentage or his birthplace."[^4] This silence has left historians with limited information about his formative years, though it is clear he received a thorough Greek education and spoke Greek as his primary language.[^5]

The young Plotinus showed no early inclination toward philosophy. It was not until the relatively advanced age of twenty-eight, around 232 CE, that he decided to pursue philosophical studies.[^6] He traveled to Alexandria, then the intellectual center of the Greco-Roman world, where he encountered numerous teachers but found himself consistently disappointed. Each philosopher he studied under left him unsatisfied, unable to answer the deep metaphysical questions that troubled him.[^7] This frustrating search continued until a friend suggested he attend lectures by Ammonius Saccas, a self-taught Platonist philosopher about whom remarkably little is known.[^8]

Upon hearing Ammonius lecture for the first time, Plotinus reportedly exclaimed to his companion, "This is the man I was looking for!"[^9] He immediately devoted himself to studying under Ammonius and would remain his student for eleven years, from approximately 232 to 243 CE.[^10] Ammonius wrote nothing and required his students to swear not to divulge his teachings, contributing to the mysterious nature of this formative period in Plotinus's philosophical development.[^11] Nevertheless, these years of intensive study provided the foundation for Plotinus's later philosophical system, though he would eventually claim to be clarifying Plato's own doctrines rather than creating something entirely new.[^12]

## Rise to Prominence

After completing eleven years of rigorous philosophical training in Alexandria, Plotinus made an unusual decision that would nearly cost him his life. At approximately age thirty-eight, he resolved to investigate firsthand the philosophical teachings of Persian and Indian sages.[^13] To accomplish this ambitious goal, he joined the military expedition of Roman Emperor Gordian III, who was marching against Persia in 242-243 CE.[^14] This decision reflected Plotinus's genuine curiosity about Eastern philosophical traditions and his desire to integrate their wisdom with the Greek philosophical heritage he had absorbed from Ammonius Saccas.[^15]

The Persian campaign proved disastrous. Emperor Gordian III was assassinated by his own troops, leaving Plotinus stranded in hostile territory far from home.[^16] The philosopher barely escaped with his life and only with considerable difficulty made his way back to safety in Antioch.[^17] Though he never reached India or achieved his goal of studying Eastern philosophy directly, this harrowing experience marked a crucial transition in his life. At age forty, during the reign of Emperor Philip the Arab (244-249 CE), Plotinus arrived in Rome, where he would spend the next twenty-six years and establish himself as one of antiquity's most influential thinkers.[^18]

In Rome, Plotinus attracted a remarkable circle of students and admirers. His teaching method centered on reading and discussing substantial passages from the works of Plato and Aristotle, followed by intensive philosophical dialogue.[^19] The sessions were open to anyone who wished to attend, creating an informal but deeply committed intellectual community.[^20] Among his students were members of the Roman senatorial aristocracy, including Senator Castricius Firmus, Marcellus Orontius, Sabinillus, and Rogantianus—a senator who actually renounced his wealth and position to dedicate himself fully to philosophy.[^21] Women also numbered among his followers, including Gemina (in whose house he lived) and her daughter, also named Gemina, as well as Amphiclea.[^22]

For his first ten years in Rome, Plotinus taught exclusively on the philosophy of Ammonius Saccas and wrote nothing.[^23] It was only after this extended period of oral instruction that he began composing the treatises that would eventually become the *Enneads*. By 263 CE, when the brilliant young scholar Porphyry arrived from Athens to study with him, Plotinus had already written twenty-one treatises.[^24] Porphyry would become his most important student, living with him for approximately five years and eventually serving as the editor and publisher of his master's complete works.[^25]

Plotinus's growing reputation reached the highest levels of Roman society. He gained the respect and friendship of Emperor Gallienus (r. 253-268 CE) and his wife Salonina.[^26] This imperial favor emboldened Plotinus to propose an ambitious project: the restoration of a ruined city in Campania that would be renamed Platonopolis and governed according to the laws outlined in Plato's *Laws*.[^27] Plotinus intended to settle there with his philosophical community, creating a living experiment in Platonic political philosophy.[^28] The emperor initially seemed receptive to the idea, but opposition at court—prompted by jealousy and political concerns about creating a potential senatorial stronghold—ultimately killed the project.[^29] The planned Platonopolis remained one of history's most intriguing unrealized philosophical utopias.

## Major Achievements

Plotinus's supreme achievement was the creation of Neoplatonism, a comprehensive philosophical system that modern scholars recognize as one of the most influential intellectual frameworks in Western and Near-Eastern thought.[^30] At the heart of this system lies his doctrine of the three fundamental principles or "hypostases": the One (also called the Good), the Intellect (Nous), and the Soul.[^31] These are not merely abstract concepts but ultimate ontological realities that explain the structure of all existence.[^32]

The One stands at the apex of Plotinus's metaphysical hierarchy. It is absolutely transcendent, containing no division, multiplicity, or distinction whatsoever—beyond even being and non-being.[^33] The One is so utterly simple and perfect that it cannot be adequately described in language or fully grasped by thought.[^34] From this supreme principle, all reality emanates in a process that Plotinus likened to light radiating from the sun or an image appearing in a mirror—the source remains completely undiminished by what proceeds from it.[^35] This emanation is not a temporal event but an eternal, ongoing process that sustains all existence.[^36]

From the One emanates Nous (Divine Mind or Intellect), which Plotinus identified metaphorically with the Demiurge in Plato's *Timaeus*.[^37] Nous contains the eternal Forms or Ideas—the perfect, immaterial archetypes of all things in the sensible world.[^38] It represents the first level of multiplicity, where being and thought coincide in perfect contemplation of intelligible reality.[^39] The Intellect eternally contemplates both itself and the One, existing in a state of complete actualization and knowledge.[^40]

The third hypostasis, Soul, proceeds from Nous and serves as the mediating principle between the intelligible and sensible realms.[^41] Plotinus subdivided Soul into higher and lower aspects, identifying the lower soul with nature and the principle that animates the physical cosmos.[^42] From the World Soul proceed individual human souls, each containing both higher capacities oriented toward the intelligible realm and lower functions tied to bodily existence.[^43] Finally, at the lowest level of reality lies matter, the principle of formlessness and privation that receives the ordering influence of Soul.[^44]

This metaphysical framework provided Plotinus with the foundation for addressing fundamental philosophical questions. His doctrine of contemplation (theoria) elevated intellectual activity to the status of a productive principle.[^45] All beings, according to Plotinus, exist in a state of contemplation appropriate to their nature—even nature itself "contemplates" in producing the ordered cosmos.[^46] For human beings, the capacity for rational contemplation represents our true self, superior to all corporeal existence and making genuine happiness independent of physical circumstances.[^47]

Plotinus also developed a sophisticated account of the soul's return to its divine source, a process he called henosis or mystical union with the One.[^48] This ascent requires progressive purification from bodily attachments, ethical refinement through the practice of virtue, and intensive contemplative discipline.[^49] Plotinus claimed to have achieved this mystical union four times during the five years Porphyry studied with him—experiences of complete transcendence where all distinctions dissolve and the soul becomes one with the ultimate reality.[^50]

His *Enneads*, compiled and edited by Porphyry after Plotinus's death, represent the literary monument of these philosophical achievements. Porphyry organized his teacher's fifty-four treatises into six groups of nine (ennead means "nine" in Greek), though this arrangement has been criticized as somewhat arbitrary.[^51] The First Ennead addresses ethical and human topics; the Second and Third deal primarily with cosmology and physical reality; the Fourth concerns the Soul; the Fifth treats knowledge and intelligible reality; and the Sixth explores being and what transcends it, the One.[^52] Written between approximately 253 CE and shortly before his death in 270 CE, these treatises were not intended as systematic philosophical innovation but rather as clarification and defense of what Plotinus understood to be Plato's authentic teachings.[^53]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his enormous influence, Plotinus faced significant intellectual opposition during his lifetime and has remained controversial among scholars. His most sustained polemic was directed against the Gnostics, a diverse collection of religious movements that combined Platonic philosophy with cosmological dualism and salvation mythology.[^54] Plotinus devoted an entire treatise—*Against the Gnostics* (Enneads II.9)—to refuting their doctrines, and this work formed part of his longest composition, sometimes called the *Großschrift*, which Porphyry later divided into four separate treatises.[^55]

Plotinus attacked the Gnostics on multiple grounds. First, he accused them of betraying authentic Platonic philosophy by misinterpreting Plato's *Timaeus*.[^56] While the Gnostics portrayed creation as the result of a cosmic error or the work of an evil or ignorant demiurge, Plotinus insisted that the cosmos is fundamentally good, the product of contemplative emanation from the divine.[^57] Second, he condemned their disdain for the physical world and the human body, arguing that such attitudes reflected a dangerous lack of proper ethics.[^58] The Gnostics' belief that salvation could be achieved without moral training or philosophical discipline particularly alarmed him.[^59] Porphyry reports that Plotinus directed his anti-Gnostic arguments especially at former students who had been seduced by Gnostic teachings, hoping to bring them back to authentic Platonism.[^60]

The Gnostics were not Plotinus's only philosophical opponents. Though he rarely quoted other thinkers directly, careful analysis reveals that Epicureanism served as an important polemical target.[^61] Plotinus rejected the Epicurean materialist account of the soul, their atomic theory of reality, and their emphasis on bodily pleasure as the highest good.[^62] His philosophy represents in many ways a systematic alternative to materialist and empiricist approaches to explaining reality and human flourishing.

Modern scholars have identified numerous problems and tensions within Plotinus's philosophical system. The extreme transcendence he ascribes to the One creates profound difficulties for explaining how anything can be known or said about this first principle.[^63] If the One truly transcends all categories of being, thought, and language, how can we meaningfully discuss it or orient our lives toward union with it? Plotinus himself grappled repeatedly with this problem, providing testimony to his honest philosophical struggle with the limits of intellection and verbal formulation.[^64]

His doctrine of matter and evil has provoked particularly intense scholarly debate. In Ennead I.8, Plotinus famously claims that matter—specifically inferior matter, distinct from both intelligible matter and "designated" matter—is evil itself.[^65] This raises difficult questions: If matter is evil and yet proceeds from the Good through the process of emanation, how can the Good be responsible for producing evil? Scholarly opinion remains divided on whether Plotinus believed matter is derived from higher principles or represents an independent reality.[^66]

The editorial arrangement of Plotinus's works has also generated criticism. Porphyry's division into six enneads of nine treatises each has been attacked as artificial and reflecting the editor's numerical preferences rather than the philosopher's intentions.[^67] The chronological order of composition, meticulously preserved by Porphyry, offers an alternative approach but rarely helps readers form a complete picture of Plotinus's system, since he revisited themes throughout his career.[^68]

Furthermore, the interpretation of Plotinus remains intensely controversial, with no general consensus among scholars on many key issues.[^69] Debates continue regarding the relationship between Plotinus's philosophy and Plato's actual teachings, with some arguing for essential continuity and others emphasizing significant innovations.[^70] The Tübingen School has argued that Plotinus's metaphysics closely resembles the "unwritten doctrines" of Plato discussed by Aristotle and the Old Academy, suggesting Plotinus may have been more faithful to Plato than previously thought.[^71]

## Personal Life

The personal life of Plotinus presents historians with a fascinating paradox. While his philosophical teachings attracted devoted followers and shaped intellectual history for centuries, the man himself remains enigmatic, deliberately concealing details about his background, family, and private affairs. Porphyry's biography emphasizes that Plotinus "seemed ashamed of being in the body," an attitude that manifested in his absolute refusal to discuss his ancestry, parentage, or birthplace.[^72] This philosophical commitment to transcending bodily existence shaped his entire approach to personal matters.

Perhaps no incident better illustrates Plotinus's attitude toward physical embodiment than his refusal to sit for a portrait. When his friend Amelius urged him to allow a painting or sculpture to be made, Plotinus responded with a question that has become famous: "Is it not enough to carry about this image in which nature has enclosed us? Do you really think I must also consent to leave, as a desirable spectacle to posterity, an image of the image?"[^73] For Plotinus, the physical body was already merely a second-rate copy of the soul's true form; to create an image of that image would be to descend yet another level away from reality.[^74] Undeterred, Amelius secretly brought the artist Carterius to Plotinus's public lectures, where the painter observed the philosopher carefully enough to create a portrait without the subject's knowledge or consent.[^75]

Porphyry's account reveals other telling details about Plotinus's personal habits and character. He abstained from public baths, relying instead on daily massage at home—a practice that would have fatal consequences when an epidemic killed all his masseurs and he had no one to continue the treatment.[^76] Despite his otherworldly philosophical orientation, Plotinus showed genuine concern for orphaned children. Friends and students often entrusted him with the care of their orphaned sons and daughters, along with their property, and he managed these responsibilities with meticulous attention.[^77] He was said to be gentle and accessible to those who sought his counsel, showing none of the aloofness one might expect from a philosopher committed to transcending the material world.[^78]

His ascetic practices reflected his philosophical conviction that the soul must purify itself from bodily attachments to achieve its highest destiny. However, Plotinus's asceticism was not a rejection of life but rather a reorientation of desire away from transient material goods toward eternal realities.[^79] He ate sparingly, avoided meat, and maintained a disciplined lifestyle oriented toward contemplation.[^80] Yet unlike some later Christian ascetics who would be influenced by his philosophy, Plotinus did not regard the body as inherently evil—merely as a distraction from the soul's true vocation.[^81]

The philosophical community Plotinus established in Rome functioned as something between a school and a spiritual fellowship.[^82] Students were encouraged to engage in rigorous intellectual study, contemplation, and self-examination, with the goal of personal transformation rather than mere accumulation of knowledge.[^83] This community included people from diverse backgrounds—Greek and Roman aristocrats, physicians, poets, critics, and scholars from across the empire.[^84] Women participated alongside men, a practice less common in ancient philosophical schools than in Christian communities but consistent with Plotinus's view that the rational soul transcends bodily distinctions.[^85]

In his final years, Plotinus suffered from a painful and disfiguring illness. The exact nature of this disease remains uncertain—Porphyry's description has led modern scholars to debate whether it was tuberculosis, leprosy, or some other condition.[^86] He developed an intestinal complaint, his voice became hoarse, his sight dimmed, and ulcers formed on his hands and feet.[^87] Friends avoided his company due to the repulsive nature of his symptoms.[^88] He retreated to an estate in Campania that his friend Zethos had bequeathed to him, dying there in solitude in 270 CE, the second year of Emperor Claudius II's reign.[^89]

Only his physician-student Eustochius attended him at the end. According to Eustochius's account, when he arrived late at Puteoli, Plotinus's final words were: "I have been a long time waiting for you; I am striving to give back the Divine in myself to the Divine in the All."[^90] As he spoke these words, a snake reportedly crept from under his bed and disappeared into a hole in the wall, and at that moment Plotinus died.[^91] True to his philosophy until the end, his last recorded utterance expressed the central conviction of his life's work: that the soul's ultimate purpose is reunion with its divine source.[^92]

## Legacy

Plotinus's influence on subsequent intellectual history can scarcely be overstated. Though the term "Neoplatonism" was invented only in the nineteenth century, the philosophical system he established dominated Late Antiquity and profoundly shaped medieval and Renaissance thought in both Eastern and Western traditions.[^93] His impact extended across religious and cultural boundaries, influencing pagan, Christian, Jewish, and Islamic thinkers for over a millennium.[^94]

The most significant vehicle for Plotinus's influence on Christianity was Saint Augustine of Hippo (354-430 CE). Augustine encountered Plotinus's philosophy at a critical juncture in his intellectual and spiritual development, when he was struggling to conceive of God as immaterial spirit rather than as some kind of refined matter.[^95] Thanks to reading "the books of the Platonists"—which scholars identify as Latin translations of Plotinus and Porphyry—Augustine achieved a breakthrough.[^96] Plotinus enabled him to understand God and the human soul as incorporeal substances, a conception that would become foundational for Christian theology.[^97] Augustine's appropriation of Plotinian metaphysics, epistemology, and psychology ensured that Neoplatonic philosophy would be woven into the fabric of Western Christian thought.[^98]

Eastern Christianity also felt Plotinus's impact through the Cappadocian Fathers (Basil of Caesarea, Gregory of Nazianzus, and Gregory of Nyssa) and through the mysterious figure known as Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite.[^99] The latter's influential works, long misattributed to a convert of Saint Paul, transmitted Plotinian metaphysics in Christian dress, particularly the doctrine of divine transcendence and the soul's mystical ascent to God.[^100] The Christian doctrine of the Trinity—one God in three persons—shows structural similarities to Plotinus's three hypostases, though the relationship is complex and disputed.[^101]

In the Islamic world, Plotinus exercised enormous influence despite a peculiar case of mistaken identity. Substantial portions of Enneads IV-VI circulated among medieval Islamic philosophers under the title *Theology of Aristotle*, incorrectly attributed to Aristotle himself.[^102] Major Islamic thinkers including Al-Kindi (c. 801-873), Al-Farabi (c. 872-950), and Avicenna (Ibn Sina, c. 980-1037) absorbed and transformed Plotinian concepts, integrating them with Quranic theology and Aristotelian philosophy.[^103] These Islamic Neoplatonists in turn influenced European Scholasticism when their works were translated into Latin during the twelfth and thirteenth centuries.[^104]

The Renaissance witnessed a dramatic revival of interest in Plotinus through the work of Marsilio Ficino (1433-1499). Under the patronage of Cosimo de' Medici, Ficino established the Platonic Academy in Florence and dedicated himself to translating the works of Plato and the Neoplatonists into Latin.[^105] Between 1484 and 1486, he translated the complete *Enneads*, publishing them with his extensive commentary in 1492.[^106] Though Ficino had already translated Plato and his translation appeared first, Plotinus had always played a central role in Ficino's understanding of Platonism.[^107] His edition of the *Enneads* remained the standard throughout Europe for centuries and generated a flowering of Renaissance Neoplatonism that influenced art, literature, and theology.[^108]

Plotinus's legacy extends beyond his direct philosophical influence to encompass his role in defining how Plato himself was understood. Until well into the nineteenth century, Platonism was largely appropriated or rejected based on its Plotinian interpretation.[^109] Only with Friedrich Schleiermacher in the early nineteenth century did scholars begin to distinguish sharply between Plato's actual teachings and Neoplatonic interpretations of them.[^110] This distinction has generated ongoing scholarly debate about whether Plotinus faithfully transmitted Plato's authentic philosophy or significantly transformed it.[^111]

In contemporary philosophy, Plotinus continues to inspire discussions of consciousness, the nature of reality, and mystical experience. His insights into the relationship between unity and multiplicity, his sophisticated account of self-consciousness and reflexive thought, and his phenomenology of mystical union remain subjects of serious philosophical investigation.[^112] Thinkers interested in non-dualistic metaphysics, whether in Western or comparative philosophy, regularly engage with Plotinus's doctrine of the One and his account of emanation.[^113]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Modern scholarship almost universally recognizes Plotinus as a philosophical genius who created an extraordinarily comprehensive and systematic metaphysics. The Cambridge History of Later Greek and Early Medieval Philosophy describes him as "the one truly great philosopher of late antiquity," whose system represents "the most powerful and integrated intellectual product of the ancient mind after Aristotle."[^114] His ability to synthesize Platonic, Aristotelian, and Stoic elements into a coherent philosophical vision demonstrates remarkable intellectual creativity and rigor.[^115]

Contemporary scholars particularly admire Plotinus's willingness to confront the most difficult philosophical problems with honesty and sophistication. Rather than offering simplistic solutions, he repeatedly grappled with fundamental tensions and paradoxes, especially concerning how the absolutely transcendent One can be known or discussed.[^116] This intellectual courage and depth have earned him respect even from philosophers who reject his conclusions.[^117] His contributions to the philosophy of consciousness and self-awareness are now recognized as anticipating much later developments in phenomenology and philosophy of mind.[^118]

Plotinus's influence on the development of Christian mystical theology receives widespread scholarly appreciation. His articulation of the soul's ascent to God through purification, illumination, and union provided a conceptual framework that Christian mystics from Dionysius to Meister Eckhart to John of the Cross would adapt and develop.[^119] The contemplative traditions of Eastern Christianity, in particular, owe an enormous debt to Plotinian metaphysics and spiritual psychology.[^120]

Defenders of Plotinus emphasize that his philosophy offers resources for addressing perennial human concerns: the search for meaning, the desire for transcendence, the need to reconcile unity and diversity in our experience of reality.[^121] In an age dominated by materialist and reductionist approaches, some contemporary thinkers find in Plotinus a sophisticated alternative that takes seriously the reality of consciousness, value, and spiritual experience.[^122] His vision of reality as fundamentally intelligible and good, accessible through contemplation and rational insight, continues to inspire those seeking a philosophically grounded spirituality.[^123]

### Negative Views

Critics of Plotinus have raised substantial objections to both his philosophical system and its historical influence. The most fundamental criticism concerns the coherence of his doctrine of the One. If the One truly transcends all categories, including being itself, how can we meaningfully claim that it exists or serves as the source of reality?[^124] Some philosophers argue that Plotinus's solution—invoking mystical experience and negative theology—amounts to abandoning rational discourse precisely where it is most needed.[^125]

His theory of emanation has also faced sustained criticism. The process by which multiplicity emerges from absolute unity, and imperfection from perfection, remains philosophically obscure despite Plotinus's various analogies.[^126] Critics contend that his metaphors (light from the sun, images in mirrors) do not adequately explain the metaphysical mechanism they are supposed to illuminate.[^127] If the One is absolutely perfect and self-sufficient, what could possibly cause or necessitate emanation? Plotinus's answer—that the One's very perfection results in overflow—strikes some critics as either question-begging or incoherent.[^128]

The relationship between Plotinus's philosophy and Plato's actual teachings has generated scholarly controversy. Some argue that Plotinus systematically distorted Plato by reading later metaphysical doctrines into texts that do not support such interpretations.[^129] The "unwritten doctrines" to which the Tübingen School appeals remain speculative reconstructions rather than established historical facts.[^130] Friedrich Schleiermacher and his successors maintained that authentic Plato must be separated from Neoplatonic interpretation, and many contemporary Plato scholars agree.[^131]

Plotinus's influence on Christianity has also received critical assessment. Some scholars argue that the Neoplatonic framework distorted essential Christian doctrines, introducing an otherworldly, anti-material bias foreign to biblical thought.[^132] The sharp Plotinian distinction between the intelligible and sensible realms, and the corresponding devaluation of bodily existence, arguably conflicts with Christian affirmations of creation's goodness, the incarnation, and bodily resurrection.[^133] While Augustine successfully integrated many Plotinian elements into Christian theology, some argue this synthesis came at a cost, importing problematic assumptions about matter, body, and the relationship between time and eternity.[^134]

From a contemporary philosophical perspective, Plotinus's system rests on metaphysical assumptions that many modern thinkers reject. His confident assertions about the structure of ultimate reality, the existence of eternal Forms, and the possibility of mystical union with the divine strike empiricist and naturalist philosophers as unfounded speculation.[^135] The lack of any empirical method for adjudicating disputes about the nature of the One or the process of emanation makes Plotinian metaphysics appear arbitrary to those who demand publicly verifiable evidence.[^136]

Finally, some critics contend that Plotinus's philosophical ideal—withdrawal from political and social engagement into contemplation of eternal realities—reflects and reinforces aristocratic privilege.[^137] His ethics presuppose the leisure and education available only to a small elite, while offering little guidance for addressing practical questions of justice, social organization, or political responsibility.[^138] The failed Platonopolis project might be seen as evidence that Plotinian philosophy, despite its intellectual brilliance, lacks adequate resources for constructive political thought.[^139]

## Famous Quotes

> "Knowledge has three degrees—opinion, science, illumination. The means or instrument of the first is sense; of the second, dialectic; of the third, intuition."[^140]

This statement encapsulates Plotinus's epistemological hierarchy, placing mystical intuition above both sensory perception and rational argument as the highest form of knowledge.

> "Withdraw into yourself and look."[^141]

One of Plotinus's most frequently repeated exhortations, this reflects his conviction that genuine self-knowledge requires introspective contemplation rather than outward observation.

> "Knowing ourselves, we are beautiful; in self-ignorance, we are ugly."[^142]

This paradoxical claim connects aesthetic and moral value with self-awareness, suggesting that beauty is fundamentally a spiritual rather than physical quality.

> "Mankind is poised midway between the gods and the beasts."[^143]

Here Plotinus articulates the human condition as ontologically intermediate, capable of ascending toward divine contemplation or descending into mere animal existence.

> "I have been a long time waiting for you; I am striving to give back the Divine in myself to the Divine in the All."[^144]

Plotinus's reported last words to Eustochius perfectly express his philosophy's central goal: the soul's return to its divine source through henosis or mystical union.

> "The stars are like letters that inscribe themselves at every moment in the sky. Everything in the world is full of signs. All events are coordinated."[^145]

This statement reflects Plotinus's vision of a cosmos thoroughly organized by divine intelligence, where everything possesses meaning and purpose.

> "We must close our eyes and invoke a new manner of seeing...a wakefulness that is the birthright of us all, though few put it to use."[^146]

Plotinus here describes the contemplative discipline required for philosophical insight—a turning away from sensory distraction toward intellectual vision.
