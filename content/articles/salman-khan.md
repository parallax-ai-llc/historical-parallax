## Summary

Salman Khan is one of Bollywood's most bankable and recognizable stars, with a career spanning over three decades and a filmography exceeding 100 films. Born on December 27, 1965, in Indore, India, he rose from supporting roles in the late 1980s to become a cultural phenomenon whose films routinely gross billions of rupees at the box office. His persona — part action hero, part romantic lead — has made him a dominant force in Hindi cinema.

Khan commands one of the largest fan followings in the world, with hundreds of millions of social media followers and a devoted base known as "Bhai-jaan" fans. Beyond acting, he has built a multimedia empire encompassing film production, television hosting, and philanthropy through his Being Human Foundation. His influence extends well beyond India, reaching South Asian diaspora communities globally and shaping popular culture across the subcontinent.

Despite significant professional achievements, Khan's career has been shadowed by legal controversies that have kept him in the public eye for reasons beyond entertainment. His complex public image — generous philanthropist and convicted poacher — encapsulates the contradictions that make him one of Indian cinema's most debated figures.

## Early Life & Background

Salman Khan was born Abdul Rashid Salim Salman Khan in Indore, Madhya Pradesh, to screenwriter Salim Khan and his first wife Sushila Charak (later Salma Khan). He grew up in Mumbai in a film industry household; his father co-wrote the iconic screenplays for *Sholay* (1975) and *Deewaar* (1975). He attended St. Stanley's School and later St. Xavier's College in Mumbai, though he did not complete his degree. His upbringing amid the creative environment of Bollywood's golden age profoundly shaped his ambitions.

## Rise to Prominence

Khan made his acting debut in a minor role in *Biwi Ho To Aisi* (1988) before gaining wider attention in *Maine Pyar Kiya* (1989), a romantic blockbuster that established him as a leading man. His shirtless scene in *Maine Pyar Kiya* became iconic, setting a template for his future image. Through the 1990s he alternated between romantic roles and action films, with *Hum Aapke Hain Koun..!* (1994) becoming one of the highest-grossing Bollywood films of its era.

His career stalled in the early 2000s amid legal troubles, but he engineered a dramatic comeback with *Wanted* (2009), reinventing himself as an action star. The subsequent "Eid releases" — a tradition of launching his films on the Eid al-Fitr holiday — became a reliable commercial formula. Films like *Dabangg* (2010), *Ek Tha Tiger* (2012), *Bajrangi Bhaijaan* (2015), and *Tiger Zinda Hai* (2017) each crossed ₹2–3 billion at the box office.

## Key Achievements

- *Maine Pyar Kiya* (1989) earned him a Filmfare Award for Best Male Debut.
- *Bajrangi Bhaijaan* (2015) grossed over ₹9.69 billion worldwide, becoming one of the highest-grossing Indian films at the time.
- Hosted the reality television series *Bigg Boss* continuously from 2010, making it one of India's longest-running and highest-rated reality shows.
- His Being Human Foundation has supported education and healthcare for underprivileged communities across India since 2007.
- Received the Rajiv Gandhi Award and multiple National and Filmfare Awards across his career.

## Controversies & Criticism

Khan has faced serious legal troubles throughout his career. In 1998, he was accused of poaching blackbuck antelope, a protected species, during the filming of *Hum Saath-Saath Hain* in Rajasthan. In 2018, a court convicted him and sentenced him to five years in prison, though he was released on bail within days and later acquitted by the Rajasthan High Court in 2023. In 2002, he was charged with culpable homicide after his vehicle allegedly struck sleeping pavement dwellers in Mumbai, killing one person; that conviction was overturned on appeal in 2015. Critics have pointed to his legal history as evidence of preferential treatment for celebrities in India's justice system.

## Legacy & Influence

Salman Khan's three-decade longevity in an industry notorious for short career arcs is itself a testament to his cultural staying power. He helped define the Bollywood mass entertainer format — high-octane action, melodrama, and crowd-pleasing spectacle — that dominated Indian cinema in the 2010s. His philanthropic work through Being Human has brought genuine material benefit to thousands of beneficiaries, complicating any simple narrative about his public persona. Regardless of legal controversies, he remains one of the highest-paid and most-followed entertainers in Asia, and his Eid releases continue to be cinematic events of national scale.
