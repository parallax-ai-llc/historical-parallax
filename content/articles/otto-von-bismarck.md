## Summary

Otto von Bismarck (1815-1898) was the architect of German unification and the first Chancellor of the German Empire, serving from 1871 to 1890. Known as the "Iron Chancellor," he masterfully employed Realpolitik—a pragmatic approach to diplomacy that prioritized practical outcomes over ideological considerations—to unite the German states under Prussian leadership through a series of carefully orchestrated wars. His domestic policies included pioneering social welfare legislation that created the world's first modern social insurance system, while simultaneously suppressing political opponents through the Kulturkampf against Catholics and laws against socialists. Bismarck's complex legacy encompasses both his remarkable diplomatic achievements in maintaining European peace for two decades and his authoritarian methods that laid problematic foundations for future German governance.

## Early Life and Background

Otto Eduard Leopold von Bismarck was born on April 1, 1815, at Schönhausen, in the Kingdom of Prussia, to a Junker family—the Prussian landed nobility that formed the backbone of the kingdom's military and administrative elite[^1]. His father, Ferdinand von Bismarck, was a relatively unremarkable country squire who managed the family estates, while his mother, Wilhelmine Mencken, came from a family of distinguished academics and civil servants, providing young Otto with connections to more educated and cultured circles than typical Junker families enjoyed[^2].

Bismarck's childhood was shaped by the tensions between these two worlds: the conservative, martial values of the Prussian Junker class and the more liberal, intellectual influences of his mother's family. He received an excellent education, first at the Plamann School in Berlin, then at the Friedrich-Wilhelm Gymnasium, and finally at the prestigious Gray Cloister secondary school[^3]. Unlike many of his Junker contemporaries who pursued military careers, Bismarck studied law at the Universities of Göttingen and Berlin between 1832 and 1835, though he proved more interested in dueling, drinking, and socializing than in serious academic pursuits[^4].

After completing his studies, Bismarck briefly entered the Prussian civil service but found bureaucratic work tedious and constraining. He resigned in 1839 to help manage the family estates in Pomerania, where he spent nearly a decade as a country gentleman, earning a reputation as a wild and undisciplined young man—the "mad Junker" who engaged in excessive drinking and womanizing[^5]. This period, however, proved transformative. In 1847, Bismarck experienced a religious awakening influenced by his future wife, Johanna von Puttkamer, and her pietistic circle, which brought greater stability to his personal life[^6]. That same year, he entered politics as a representative to the Prussian United Diet, where he quickly established himself as an ultra-conservative defender of royal absolutism and aristocratic privilege, vehemently opposing the liberal and democratic movements that would sweep Europe in 1848[^7].

## Rise to Power

Bismarck's political ascent began during the revolutionary upheavals of 1848-1849, when liberal movements across Europe challenged traditional monarchies. While many conservatives wavered, Bismarck emerged as an uncompromising defender of royal authority, advocating for military suppression of the revolutionaries and earning a reputation as a reactionary hard-liner[^8]. His staunch royalism brought him to the attention of the Prussian court, and in 1851, King Frederick William IV appointed him as Prussia's representative to the German Confederation's Diet in Frankfurt, a position that would prove pivotal to his development as a statesman[^9].

During his eight years at Frankfurt (1851-1859), Bismarck's political philosophy underwent a crucial evolution. Initially expecting to work cooperatively with Austria, Prussia's fellow great German power, he instead became convinced that Austria was Prussia's primary rival for leadership of the German states[^10]. He observed that Austria consistently used its influence to constrain Prussian ambitions and maintain its own dominance in German affairs. This realization led Bismarck to abandon traditional conservative solidarity with Austria in favor of a more ruthlessly pragmatic approach: Prussia must challenge Austrian supremacy if it was to achieve greatness[^11].

Bismarck's diplomatic skills were further honed through subsequent appointments as ambassador to Russia (1859-1862) and France (1862), where he cultivated relationships with European powers and developed his understanding of the complex balance of power that governed continental politics[^12]. In September 1862, King Wilhelm I faced a severe constitutional crisis with the Prussian Parliament, which refused to approve his proposed military reforms and budget. In desperation, the king appointed Bismarck as Minister President and Foreign Minister, gambling that this formidable conservative could break the parliamentary deadlock[^13].

Bismarck's solution to the constitutional crisis demonstrated the autocratic methods that would characterize his rule. Invoking a controversial interpretation of the Prussian constitution, he argued that when the government and parliament could not agree on a budget, the government was entitled to proceed with the previous year's budget and continue collecting taxes[^14]. For four years, from 1862 to 1866, Bismarck governed without parliamentary approval, using the military reforms to strengthen the Prussian army in defiance of liberal opposition. This period of unconstitutional rule established Bismarck's reputation for ruthless effectiveness: he would achieve his goals by whatever means necessary, constitutional niceties notwithstanding[^15].

## Major Achievements

### Unification of Germany

Bismarck's supreme achievement was the unification of Germany under Prussian leadership, accomplished through a masterful combination of diplomacy and warfare between 1864 and 1871. He pursued this goal through three carefully calculated wars, each designed to isolate and defeat a specific obstacle to unification while avoiding intervention by other European powers[^16].

The Danish War of 1864 served as the opening gambit. When Denmark attempted to incorporate the duchies of Schleswig and Holstein more fully into the Danish kingdom, Bismarck maneuvered Austria into joining Prussia in a war against Denmark[^17]. The swift Prussian-Austrian victory resolved nothing, however, as Bismarck had intended—the subsequent dispute over how to administer the conquered duchies provided the pretext for his next move. Through deliberate provocation and diplomatic manipulation, Bismarck engineered the Austro-Prussian War of 1866, having first secured French neutrality and Italian alliance[^18]. The Prussian army, reformed and equipped with superior breech-loading rifles, devastated the Austrian forces at the Battle of Königgrätz in July 1866, achieving victory in just seven weeks[^19].

Bismarck's genius showed itself not only in winning wars but in making peace. Despite pressure from King Wilhelm I and the Prussian military to march on Vienna, Bismarck insisted on a lenient peace with Austria, understanding that Prussia would need Austrian neutrality for the final stage of unification[^20]. The Treaty of Prague dissolved the German Confederation, excluded Austria from German affairs, and created the North German Confederation under Prussian leadership—a crucial step toward full unification[^21].

The final act came with the Franco-Prussian War of 1870-1871. Bismarck skillfully exploited tensions over the Spanish succession to provoke French Emperor Napoleon III into declaring war on Prussia, ensuring that the south German states would rally to Prussia's defense against French aggression[^22]. The famous Ems Dispatch, which Bismarck edited to make it appear that the Prussian king had insulted the French ambassador, served as the match that lit this carefully prepared fire[^23]. Once again, Prussian military superiority proved decisive: French forces were crushed at Sedan, Napoleon III was captured, and Paris was besieged. On January 18, 1871, in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles, King Wilhelm I was proclaimed German Emperor, and Bismarck achieved his goal of a unified German Empire[^24].

### Diplomatic Achievements

Following unification, Bismarck's foreign policy focused on preserving peace and German security through an intricate system of alliances known as the Bismarckian system. Understanding that Germany's position in the center of Europe made it vulnerable to encirclement, and that France would remain a revanchist threat seeking to recover Alsace-Lorraine, Bismarck worked to diplomatically isolate France while maintaining friendly relations with the other great powers[^25].

The centerpiece of this system was the Dreikaiserbund (Three Emperors' League) of 1873, linking Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Russia in a conservative monarchist alliance[^26]. When Balkan tensions between Austria and Russia threatened this arrangement, Bismarck served as the "honest broker" at the Congress of Berlin in 1878, mediating a settlement that preserved European peace while enhancing German prestige[^27]. He subsequently developed the Dual Alliance with Austria-Hungary in 1879, expanded it into the Triple Alliance with Italy in 1882, and maintained the Reinsurance Treaty with Russia from 1887 to 1890[^28].

This diplomatic juggling act—keeping France isolated while maintaining simultaneous alliances with rival powers Austria-Hungary and Russia—represented a remarkable achievement that preserved European peace for two decades. Bismarck famously stated that he always tried to keep "at least two of the five balls in the air"—meaning that Germany should maintain partnerships with at least two of Europe's other four great powers at all times[^29]. His success in this endeavor made him one of history's most skilled practitioners of balance-of-power diplomacy.

### Social Legislation

Bismarck's domestic achievements extended beyond political unification to include pioneering social welfare legislation that established the framework for the modern welfare state. Between 1883 and 1889, his government enacted comprehensive social insurance programs covering health insurance (1883), accident insurance (1884), and old age and disability insurance (1889)[^30]. These programs were the first of their kind in the world and became models that other nations would eventually follow.

Bismarck's motives for this social legislation were complex and pragmatic rather than humanitarian. He sought to combat the growing appeal of socialism among German workers by demonstrating that the existing system could address their economic insecurity more effectively than revolutionary alternatives[^31]. "Whoever has a pension for his old age is far more content and far easier to handle than one who has no such prospect," he candidly explained[^32]. By giving workers a stake in the existing order, Bismarck hoped to immunize them against socialist ideology while simultaneously strengthening the German state and economy.

Despite these calculating origins, the social insurance system represented a genuine achievement that significantly improved the lives of millions of Germans. The health insurance program covered medical treatment and provided sick pay, the accident insurance program protected workers injured on the job, and the pension system offered security in old age[^33]. These programs established the principle that the state bore responsibility for its citizens' social welfare, a revolutionary concept that would profoundly influence political development throughout the industrialized world in the twentieth century.

## Controversies and Criticisms

### The Kulturkampf

One of Bismarck's most controversial domestic policies was the Kulturkampf ("culture struggle"), a sustained campaign against the Catholic Church in Germany from 1871 to 1878. Bismarck viewed German Catholics' loyalty to the Pope as a threat to national unity and saw the newly formed Catholic Center Party as a dangerous political opponent that could potentially ally with foreign Catholic powers against the Protestant-dominated German Empire[^34]. He launched a series of legislative measures designed to bring the Catholic Church under state control: the May Laws of 1873 placed education of clergy under state supervision, expelled Jesuits from Germany, required civil marriage, and abolished religious orders[^35].

The Kulturkampf proved to be a significant political miscalculation. Rather than weakening Catholic political organization, Bismarck's aggressive policies strengthened Catholic solidarity and increased support for the Center Party, which grew from 58 seats in the 1871 Reichstag to 91 seats in 1874[^36]. The campaign also created martyrs among imprisoned bishops and expelled clergy, generated sympathy even among many Protestants who opposed heavy-handed state interference in religious affairs, and complicated Germany's diplomatic relations with Catholic powers[^37]. By 1878, Bismarck began retreating from the Kulturkampf, and most of the anti-Catholic legislation was gradually repealed during the 1880s, though full reconciliation was not achieved until after his dismissal in 1890[^38].

### Anti-Socialist Laws

Bismarck's treatment of the Social Democratic Party demonstrated his authoritarian approach to political opposition. After two assassination attempts on Kaiser Wilhelm I in 1878 (neither actually perpetrated by socialists), Bismarck seized the opportunity to push through the Anti-Socialist Laws, which banned Social Democratic organizations, meetings, and publications, though socialist candidates could still run for Reichstag seats as individuals[^39]. These repressive measures remained in force from 1878 to 1890, creating a period of persecution during which socialist leaders were imprisoned, newspapers were suppressed, and thousands of activists fled into exile[^40].

The anti-socialist campaign, like the Kulturkampf, ultimately backfired. Despite twelve years of repression, the Social Democratic Party grew stronger: its share of the popular vote increased from 7.6% in 1878 to 19.7% in 1890, making it the largest party by vote share[^41]. Bismarck's combination of repression and social reform—the "carrot and stick" approach—failed to prevent the growth of a powerful socialist movement that would play a major role in German politics after his departure. The episode revealed the limitations of authoritarian methods in dealing with mass political movements driven by genuine economic grievances and ideological commitment.

### Colonial Policy

Bismarck's colonial ventures, though limited compared to those of Britain or France, exemplified the exploitative nature of European imperialism and created problematic legacies. Initially skeptical of colonial acquisitions, which he viewed as expensive distractions from European affairs, Bismarck reversed course in 1884-1885 and claimed territories in Africa (Togoland, Cameroon, German Southwest Africa, German East Africa) and the Pacific (German New Guinea)[^42]. These acquisitions were motivated largely by domestic political considerations—appealing to nationalist sentiment and commercial interests—rather than strategic calculation[^43].

German colonial rule was notably brutal, particularly in German Southwest Africa (modern Namibia), where the Herero and Nama genocides of 1904-1908 resulted in the deaths of approximately 80% of the Herero population and 50% of the Nama population[^44]. While these atrocities occurred after Bismarck's time, his establishment of German colonial presence created the framework for this violence. The colonial episode also contributed to international tensions: Bismarck's organization of the Berlin Conference of 1884-1885, which established rules for European colonization of Africa, legitimized the "Scramble for Africa" that would devastate the continent[^45].

### Authoritarian Governance

Bismarck's entire approach to governance was fundamentally authoritarian and anti-democratic. He manipulated constitutional provisions to rule without parliamentary approval during the constitutional crisis of 1862-1866, and even after accepting constitutional government, he worked constantly to limit parliamentary power and maintain executive dominance[^46]. His use of press manipulation, political persecution, and emergency legislation to suppress opposition created patterns of authoritarian rule that would prove problematic for Germany's democratic development.

The German Empire's constitution, largely designed by Bismarck, embodied these authoritarian principles. While it included a Reichstag elected by universal male suffrage, real power remained with the Kaiser and his appointed Chancellor, who was responsible to the monarch rather than to parliament[^47]. This constitutional structure prevented Germany from developing the parliamentary democracy that evolved in Britain and France, instead establishing a hybrid system that combined democratic forms with autocratic substance. Historians have argued that this "special path" (Sonderweg) of German political development, initiated by Bismarck, contributed to the instability that would later produce the Nazi regime[^48].

## Personal Life

Bismarck married Johanna von Puttkamer in 1847, a union that brought stability to his previously dissolute lifestyle and connected him to influential pietistic Protestant circles. Johanna was deeply religious, quiet, and conventional—seemingly an unlikely match for the tempestuous Bismarck—but the marriage proved remarkably successful and enduring[^49]. She managed the family estates while Bismarck pursued his political career, and he remained devoted to her until her death in 1894, which devastated him during his final years[^50]. The couple had three children: Marie (born 1848), Herbert (born 1849), and Wilhelm (born 1852)[^51].

Despite his public image as the "Iron Chancellor," Bismarck was emotionally volatile in private life, prone to tears, rages, and psychosomatic illnesses during periods of stress. He suffered from chronic health problems including insomnia, neuralgia, and digestive issues, which he attempted to treat through excessive eating and drinking—habits that contributed to severe obesity in his later years[^52]. His personal physician noted that Bismarck consumed enormous quantities of food at single meals and drank heavily, preferences that exacerbated his health problems but which he seemed unable to control[^53].

Bismarck's personality combined remarkable intellectual gifts with significant character flaws. He possessed extraordinary strategic vision, phenomenal memory, and formidable debating skills, along with the ability to read political situations with uncanny accuracy[^54]. However, he was also vindictive toward opponents, manipulative in personal relationships, and unable to tolerate criticism or contradiction. His feuds were legendary and prolonged; he never forgave those he believed had wronged him and worked systematically to destroy their careers[^55]. This combination of genius and pettiness, vision and vindictiveness, made him both extraordinarily effective and ultimately unable to build institutions that could function without his personal control.

In retirement after his forced resignation in 1890, Bismarck became increasingly bitter, publishing memoirs and giving interviews that attacked Kaiser Wilhelm II and his successors. He lived at his estates in Friedrichsruh and Varzin, where he received a steady stream of visitors and admirers while brooding over his dismissal[^56]. His final years were marked by declining health, the death of his beloved wife Johanna in 1894, and growing disillusionment as he watched his carefully constructed foreign policy system dismantled by the new government. He died on July 30, 1898, at the age of 83, having witnessed the beginning of Germany's transformation from the cautious, diplomatically adroit power he had created into the aggressive, ambitious state that would lead Europe into World War I[^57].

## Legacy

Bismarck's legacy remains deeply contested, with assessments ranging from celebration of his diplomatic genius and nation-building achievements to condemnation of his authoritarian methods and their long-term consequences for German political development. His immediate impact on European history was profound: he transformed the map of Europe by creating a unified German Empire, established a new balance of power that maintained continental peace for decades, and pioneered social welfare policies that influenced governance worldwide[^58].

The unified Germany that Bismarck created became Europe's dominant power, with the largest population and economy on the continent. This fundamentally altered European politics, ending the period of French dominance that had existed since the seventeenth century and creating a new center of power that would shape international relations for the next seventy years[^59]. Bismarck's diplomatic system, maintaining peace through a complex web of alliances while isolating France, represented a remarkable achievement in statecraft that many historians regard as one of the most skillful exercises in balance-of-power politics ever conducted[^60].

His social welfare innovations established Germany as a pioneer in addressing the challenges of industrial capitalism. The social insurance programs Bismarck created—health insurance, accident insurance, and old-age pensions—became models that influenced policy development throughout Europe and eventually in the United States[^61]. These programs demonstrated that conservative governments could address working-class economic insecurity through state intervention, establishing a middle path between laissez-faire capitalism and socialism that would become the foundation of the modern welfare state[^62].

However, Bismarck's legacy also includes significant negative elements that have generated enduring criticism. His authoritarian governing style and the semi-democratic constitutional structure he created for Germany prevented the development of genuine parliamentary democracy and ministerial responsibility[^63]. This "Bismarckian system" of government, which concentrated power in the executive while limiting parliamentary influence, established patterns that persisted in German politics long after his death and contributed to the constitutional weaknesses that would eventually facilitate the Nazi seizure of power[^64].

His methods—manipulation, repression of opposition, rule without parliamentary consent—set troubling precedents for German political culture. The glorification of Bismarck as the strong leader who unified Germany through "iron and blood" created a cult of authoritarian leadership that would prove dangerous in the hands of less scrupulous successors[^65]. Additionally, his failure to prepare for a succession or to build institutions capable of functioning without his personal direction meant that the carefully balanced system he created quickly unraveled after his dismissal in 1890, contributing to the aggressive foreign policy that would lead to World War I[^66].

In contemporary Germany, Bismarck's legacy remains complex and contested. Streets, squares, and monuments bearing his name testify to his enduring presence in German historical consciousness, while ongoing debates about his role in German history reflect continuing disagreement about how to assess his achievements and failures[^67]. He represents both the brilliance of German statecraft in the nineteenth century and the authoritarian traditions that would later prove so destructive, making him an appropriate symbol for the contradictions and complexities of modern German history[^68].

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Admirers of Bismarck emphasize his extraordinary diplomatic skill, political acumen, and achievements in nation-building that rank him among history's greatest statesmen. The historian A.J.P. Taylor, while acknowledging Bismarck's flaws, argued that "Bismarck fought 'necessary' wars and killed thousands; the idealists of the twentieth century fight 'just' wars and kill millions," suggesting that Bismarck's pragmatic, limited approach to warfare compared favorably to the ideologically driven total wars that would follow[^69]. This perspective views Bismarck as a master of Realpolitik who understood the limits of power and carefully calibrated Germany's ambitions to match its capabilities[^70].

Scholars have particularly praised Bismarck's diplomatic achievements in the two decades following German unification. Historian Gordon Craig wrote that Bismarck's alliance system represented "a tour de force of diplomatic skill" that successfully managed the contradictory imperatives of maintaining peace while securing German interests[^71]. His ability to simultaneously maintain alliances with Austria-Hungary and Russia, traditional rivals with conflicting interests in the Balkans, demonstrated a level of diplomatic virtuosity that few statesmen have matched[^72]. The fact that Europe avoided major war for forty-three years between 1871 and 1914—the longest period of great-power peace in modern European history until that point—is often attributed largely to Bismarck's diplomatic management[^73].

Defenders also point to Bismarck's social legislation as evidence of his progressive vision and willingness to adapt conservative principles to modern conditions. While acknowledging that his motives were primarily political rather than humanitarian, scholars like Otto Pflanze have argued that the social insurance programs nevertheless represented genuine innovation that addressed real problems and improved millions of lives[^74]. This perspective emphasizes that Bismarck, unlike rigid reactionaries, recognized that industrial capitalism created new social problems requiring state intervention, and that his social welfare system demonstrated how conservative governments could adapt to modern economic conditions[^75].

Some historians argue that criticisms of Bismarck's authoritarian methods reflect anachronistic application of democratic standards to a nineteenth-century context. According to this view, Bismarck operated within the political norms of his time, when constitutional monarchy was the standard form of government in Europe and full democracy was nowhere established[^76]. His manipulation of parliamentary procedures and suppression of opposition, while objectionable by modern standards, were not unusual for his era, and judging him by twenty-first-century democratic norms distorts historical understanding[^77]. These scholars suggest that Bismarck should be assessed based on whether his methods were effective for achieving his goals and serving German interests as understood at the time, rather than whether they conform to contemporary democratic values[^78].

### Negative Views

Critics of Bismarck argue that his authoritarian methods and the political system he created inflicted lasting damage on German political development and contributed to the catastrophes of the twentieth century. Historian Fritz Fischer and his followers have emphasized that Bismarckian political culture—characterized by executive dominance, military influence, weak parliamentarism, and intolerance of opposition—established patterns that persisted into the Weimar Republic and facilitated the Nazi rise to power[^79]. According to this interpretation, Bismarck bears indirect but significant responsibility for later German disasters because the political system he created prevented Germany from developing democratic political culture and institutions[^80].

The Sonderweg (special path) thesis, developed by scholars including Hans-Ulrich Wehler, argues that Bismarck's unification of Germany "from above" through military force rather than popular democratic movement established Germany on a distinctive path of development that diverged from Western liberal democracies[^81]. By preserving aristocratic privilege, limiting parliamentary power, and preventing genuine democratization, Bismarck created a modernized but fundamentally illiberal political order that combined economic modernization with political backwardness[^82]. This distorted development, critics argue, left Germany vulnerable to authoritarian appeals and unable to successfully navigate the challenges of the twentieth century[^83].

Scholars have also criticized Bismarck's excessive reliance on his personal skills and his failure to build sustainable institutions. Historian Jonathan Steinberg notes that Bismarck "made himself indispensable" but "left no system that could work without him," comparing him to "a juggler who insisted that no one else could keep the balls in the air"[^84]. When Wilhelm II dismissed Bismarck in 1890, the alliance system quickly deteriorated because it had been based on Bismarck's personal relationships and diplomatic skill rather than sustainable institutional arrangements[^85]. This institutional failure suggests that Bismarck's statecraft, while brilliant, was ultimately ephemeral because it could not outlast its creator[^86].

Modern critics also emphasize Bismarck's responsibility for establishing Germany's colonial empire and for the broader militaristic political culture that would lead to World War I. While Bismarck himself pursued relatively cautious foreign policy after 1871, his glorification of military solutions—his famous declaration that great questions would be decided by "iron and blood" rather than speeches and majority votes—helped create a political culture that valorized military force over diplomatic negotiation[^87]. The widespread German belief that Bismarck had solved Germany's problems through three successful wars contributed to the dangerous conviction that military solutions were superior to diplomatic compromises, a belief that would prove catastrophic in 1914[^88].

Finally, critics point to Bismarck's domestic repression and political intolerance as morally indefensible regardless of their effectiveness. The persecution of Catholics during the Kulturkampf, the suppression of Social Democrats through the Anti-Socialist Laws, and the general contempt for parliamentary democracy and civil liberties that characterized Bismarckian governance represent fundamental violations of human rights and dignity[^89]. From this perspective, Bismarck's achievements in unification and diplomacy cannot excuse or balance his authoritarian methods, and celebrating him as a great statesman implicitly endorses the principle that effectiveness justifies any means—a dangerous ethical position[^90].

## Famous Quotes

> "Politics is the art of the possible, the attainable — the art of the next best." - This quote encapsulates Bismarck's pragmatic, realistic approach to statecraft, emphasizing practical achievement over ideological purity[^91].

> "The great questions of the day will not be settled by means of speeches and majority decisions but by iron and blood." - Delivered to the Prussian Parliament in 1862, this statement became Bismarck's most famous declaration, articulating his belief that military force rather than parliamentary debate would determine Germany's future[^92].

> "When you want to fool the world, tell the truth." - This cynical observation reflects Bismarck's manipulative approach to diplomacy and his understanding that honesty could be a form of deception when people expected duplicity[^93].

> "A statesman must wait until he hears the steps of God sounding through events, then leap up and grasp the hem of His garment." - This metaphorical statement reveals Bismarck's opportunistic approach to politics, emphasizing timing and the ability to seize favorable moments[^94].

> "Preventive war is like committing suicide for fear of death." - Despite his reputation for militarism, Bismarck opposed preventive warfare after 1871, recognizing that Germany's position required careful preservation of peace rather than aggressive adventurism[^95].

> "People never lie so much as after a hunt, during a war, or before an election." - This sardonic observation demonstrates Bismarck's cynicism about human nature and political discourse[^96].
