## Background and Context

- Argentina’s armed forces seized power in 1976 and ruled through a military junta during the period commonly referred to as the **National Reorganization Process**.
- The dictatorship’s counterinsurgency campaign involved widespread human-rights abuses, including illegal detention, torture, forced disappearances, and the theft of infants born to detained women.

## Presidency (1982–1983)

- Bignone became the de facto president in the aftermath of Argentina’s defeat in the **Falklands/Malvinas War**, during the regime’s final phase.
- His administration oversaw steps toward a return to civilian government, leading to national elections in 1983.

## Prosecutions and Convictions (high level)

- In Argentina’s democratic era, Bignone was convicted in multiple cases concerning crimes committed during the dictatorship.
- A prominent set of convictions addressed the **systematic appropriation of babies** born to political prisoners, a practice documented by human-rights organizations.

## Legacy

- Bignone is often cited as a central figure in the junta’s last attempt to manage a transition out of power.
- His later convictions are frequently referenced in discussions of Argentina’s long-running legal and political reckoning with state terrorism.
