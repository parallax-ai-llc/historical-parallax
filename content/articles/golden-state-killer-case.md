## Summary

Joseph DeAngelo identified as the Golden State Killer through genealogical DNA analysis 40 years after his crimes. A former police officer, he committed 13 murders and 50 rapes.

## Background

The "Golden State Killer" label is often used as an umbrella term for a series of violent crimes in California linked to one offender.
The suspect was also known by earlier case names, including the "East Area Rapist" and the "Original Night Stalker," reflecting different phases of the crime spree and investigative jurisdictions.
The offenses spanned multiple counties and typically involved stalking, burglary, sexual assault, and—later—homicide.

## Investigation and Identification

For decades, investigators relied on traditional leads, eyewitness reports, and crime-scene evidence.
A turning point came from the use of DNA evidence collected from crime scenes and later compared through genetic genealogy techniques.
In this approach, investigators look for partial DNA matches that can indicate relatives, then build family trees to narrow down candidates.
After follow-up investigative work, Joseph James DeAngelo was identified as a suspect and arrested in April 2018.

## Legal Proceedings and Outcome

After his arrest, prosecutors consolidated charges where possible and relied on DNA evidence, historical case files, and survivor testimony.
In 2020, DeAngelo pleaded guilty in a deal that resolved multiple counts and avoided a trial.
He received a sentence of life in prison without the possibility of parole.

## Significance

The case became a widely cited example of how genetic genealogy can be used to identify suspects in long-unsolved violent crimes.
It also prompted public discussion about privacy, consent, and the use of consumer genealogy databases in criminal investigations.
For survivors and affected communities, the identification and sentencing represented a major milestone after decades of uncertainty.
