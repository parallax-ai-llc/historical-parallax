## Summary

Colombian criminal gang that operated from the El Vaticano neighborhood in Medellín, carrying out contract killings for various drug cartels. They were among the most feared sicario (hitman) groups in Colombia.

## Background and Context

El Vaticano Gang is typically described as part of the broader ecosystem of armed groups and criminal structures that emerged in Medellín during the late 20th century, when drug trafficking revenues and territorial disputes fueled demand for contract violence. The group name is associated with the El Vaticano sector, and reporting often frames them as a “sicario” organization rather than a vertically integrated cartel.

## Operations and Modus Operandi

Accounts generally emphasize:

- **Contract killings and enforcement** carried out on behalf of larger trafficking organizations or local power brokers.
- **Territorial control** in specific neighborhoods, with violence used to maintain influence and deter rivals.
- **Recruitment dynamics** that relied on local networks and the availability of armed young men during periods of intense urban conflict.

## Law-Enforcement Response

Colombian authorities periodically targeted contract-killing networks through raids, arrests, and investigations tied to homicides and organized-crime conspiracies. In many narratives, pressure from security operations and shifts in Medellín’s criminal landscape reduced the prominence of specific groups, while successor structures and rebranded factions continued to appear.

## Legacy

The gang is frequently cited in discussions of Medellín’s history of urban violence as an example of how localized hitman crews could become feared actors when connected to cartel-level financing and protection. The label also illustrates how neighborhood-based identities sometimes became part of the mythology and media portrayal of the city’s criminal underworld.
