## Summary

Saudi women's rights activist imprisoned and reportedly tortured for campaigning for the right to drive.

## Background

- Loujain al-Hathloul is a Saudi activist associated with campaigns to expand women's rights, including opposition to the male guardianship system and support for women's freedom of movement.[^1]
- Her activism gained international attention during a period of highly visible reforms in Saudi Arabia, including the eventual lifting of the ban on women driving in 2018.[^1]

## Advocacy and activism

- Known for public advocacy on women's autonomy (travel, driving, and broader civil rights) and for engagement on social media and in public campaigns.[^1]
- Her case became emblematic of a wider pattern reported by human rights organizations: simultaneous legal reforms alongside arrests of some prominent activists.[^2][^3]

## Detention, trial, and release

- In 2018, al-Hathloul was detained amid arrests of several women's rights activists in Saudi Arabia.[^1][^2]
- Multiple outlets and rights groups reported allegations of mistreatment and torture during detention; Saudi authorities have disputed some allegations.[^1][^2]
- She was later prosecuted under Saudi legal processes; reporting around the case notes restrictions on public transparency and the use of specialized courts for certain charges.[^1][^3]
- In 2021, she was released after nearly three years in detention, but public reporting described post-release restrictions such as travel bans and/or limits on speech or activism, depending on the period.[^1][^3]

## Significance

- Often cited in discussions of "reform vs. repression" in Saudi Arabia: reforms (such as women driving) occurring alongside punitive actions against activists who had advocated similar changes.[^2][^3]
- Her case has been referenced by international human rights organizations and commentators as a benchmark for evaluating Saudi Arabia's treatment of civil society and dissent.[^2][^3]
