## Summary

Ugandan cult leader and warlord known as Joseph Kony who leads the Lord's Resistance Army and is wanted by the ICC for war crimes. He abducted over 30,000 children as soldiers and sex slaves.

## Background and Context

The Lord's Resistance Army (LRA) emerged in the late 1980s in northern Uganda amid political upheaval and local grievances following years of conflict. The group combined claims of spiritual authority with armed insurgency, drawing recruits through coercion and fear.

## Allegations and ICC Case

International attention intensified as reports documented systematic abductions, forced recruitment of children, and attacks on civilian communities. In 2005, the International Criminal Court issued arrest warrants related to alleged crimes against humanity and war crimes, including murder, enslavement, sexual slavery, and the conscription of children.

## Regional Dimension

As military pressure increased in Uganda, LRA activity shifted across borders into parts of South Sudan, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, and the Central African Republic. This cross-border footprint complicated pursuit efforts and contributed to a prolonged humanitarian and security challenge for multiple states.

## Impact and Legacy

Kony became a symbol of the use of child soldiers and the difficulty of apprehending militia leaders operating in remote terrain. Advocacy efforts, including the widely viewed 2012 "Kony 2012" campaign, helped raise awareness but also prompted debate about simplification, policy effectiveness, and the role of viral activism.
