## Background

Benjamín Arellano Félix is commonly associated with the Arellano Félix Organization (AFO), also known as the Tijuana Cartel, a major trafficking group that operated along the U.S.–Mexico border.

The organization’s power historically derived from control of key border crossings and routes used to move cocaine and other narcotics into the United States.

## Notable activities (high level)

Public reporting on the Tijuana Cartel during the 1990s and early 2000s often describes:

- A focus on controlling border “plazas” and charging fees to other traffickers.
- Violent competition with rival organizations for trafficking corridors.
- Use of corruption and intimidation to reduce law-enforcement pressure.

## Arrest, extradition, and sentencing

Arellano Félix was arrested in 2002, ending his period of leadership as a fugitive figure within the organization.

He was later extradited to the United States and prosecuted in U.S. federal court.

He received a 25-year federal prison sentence, reflecting convictions or admissions related to drug trafficking and associated criminal conduct.

## Historical significance

The Arellano Félix Organization is frequently cited in analyses of late-20th-century Mexican organized crime as an example of how border control, violence, and corruption combined to create durable trafficking networks.

Crackdowns and internal fragmentation in subsequent years contributed to shifts in power among Mexico’s major trafficking groups.
