## Summary

President of Sierra Leone twice overthrown during the civil war. His government oversaw the Kamajor militia which committed its own atrocities while fighting the RUF.

## Background

Ahmad Tejan Kabbah was a Sierra Leonean politician and diplomat who also worked for the United Nations before returning to domestic politics.[^1] He became a central figure in Sierra Leone’s late-1990s transition toward elected government during the civil war period.[^1]

## Presidency and Civil War Context

Kabbah was elected president in 1996, amid the ongoing Sierra Leone Civil War.[^1] In 1997, he was removed from office in a military coup led by the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (AFRC), which aligned with the Revolutionary United Front (RUF). He was later restored to power following regional military intervention led by ECOMOG.[^1]

Civil war-era governance is often evaluated in the context of multiple armed actors committing abuses. Reporting on the period includes accusations and documentation of atrocities by the RUF and also by pro-government militias such as the Kamajors/Civil Defence Forces (CDF) operating during the conflict.[^1]

## Governance and Postwar Period

After the conflict, Sierra Leone undertook disarmament and postwar reconstruction efforts, alongside transitional justice processes that included a Truth and Reconciliation Commission and the Special Court for Sierra Leone.[^1] Kabbah’s administrations are commonly discussed in relation to the challenges of rebuilding state institutions, improving security, and managing accountability for wartime abuses.[^1]

## Legacy and Notes

Kabbah is often characterized as a leader operating under extreme constraints—attempting to sustain an elected government during a period of state collapse and mass violence.[^1] Assessments of his legacy frequently balance the symbolic importance of elected civilian rule against critiques about governance, corruption, and the conduct of allied forces during the war.[^1]
