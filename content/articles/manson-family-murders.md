## Summary

Charles Manson's followers murdered actress Sharon Tate and six others over two nights in Los Angeles. Manson believed the murders would trigger a race war he called 'Helter Skelter.'

## Background and Context

- **Manson Family** refers to a quasi-communal group led by **Charles Manson** in Southern California in the late 1960s.
- In Manson’s later statements and prosecutors’ theory at trial, the killings were linked to an apocalyptic, racially charged narrative Manson called **“Helter Skelter.”**
- The murders occurred amid wider public anxiety in the U.S. at the end of the 1960s, and they became a defining symbol of the era’s darker turn.

## Key Events (Aug 1969)

- **Night 1 (Aug 8–9, 1969):** Attacks at **10050 Cielo Drive** in Los Angeles (then associated with Sharon Tate and Roman Polanski).
- **Night 2 (Aug 9–10, 1969):** Attacks at **3301 Waverly Drive** in Los Feliz (the home of **Leno and Rosemary LaBianca**).

## Victims

The seven victims most commonly referenced across the two nights are:

- **Sharon Tate** (actor)
- **Jay Sebring** (celebrity hairstylist)
- **Abigail Folger** (socialite / heiress)
- **Wojciech Frykowski** (writer / friend of Polanski)
- **Steven Parent** (visitor at the Cielo Drive property)
- **Leno LaBianca**
- **Rosemary LaBianca**

## Perpetrators (as established in the cases)

Individuals associated with the Manson Family who were convicted for roles in the murders include:

- **Charles Manson** (convicted of first-degree murder and conspiracy in connection with the killings)
- **Charles “Tex” Watson**
- **Susan Atkins**
- **Patricia Krenwinkel**
- **Leslie Van Houten** (convicted in relation to the LaBianca murders)
- **Linda Kasabian** testified for the prosecution and was granted immunity for her testimony.

## Investigation and Prosecution (high level)

- Early investigation of the scenes did not immediately connect the two nights of killings.
- Law enforcement ultimately linked the murders to members of the Manson Family; the case became one of the most high-profile criminal prosecutions in U.S. history.
- The prosecution argued that Manson orchestrated the violence through direction and influence, even when he was not physically present at the killings.

## Trial Outcomes and Sentences

- In 1971, Manson and several co-defendants received **death sentences**.
- After California’s death penalty was invalidated in 1972, those sentences were **commuted to life imprisonment**.

## Cultural Impact

- The murders had an outsized cultural footprint in American media, true crime writing, and film/television.
- They are frequently cited as a turning point in public perceptions of the late-1960s counterculture.
