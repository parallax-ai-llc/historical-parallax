## Summary

Libyan militia leader who controlled armed groups in Misrata after the fall of Gaddafi. His forces were accused of human rights abuses against detained regime loyalists.

## Additional Context

### Post-2011 militia landscape

After the collapse of the Gaddafi government in 2011, Libya’s security environment fragmented. In many cities, revolutionary brigades and local armed groups filled the vacuum left by weakened state institutions, often operating with shifting alliances and varying degrees of coordination with formal authorities.

### Detention and human-rights concerns

Reporting on post-conflict Libya has repeatedly highlighted problems in the treatment of detainees, including arbitrary detention, lack of due process, and allegations of abuse in unofficial or semi-official facilities. References to Elgadi typically appear in this wider discussion of militia control over prisons and security functions in and around Misrata.

### Relevance

Elgadi is primarily notable as an example of how localized armed leadership emerged during the civil war and persisted afterward, complicating efforts to build unified national security forces and establish consistent legal oversight.
