## Summary

Jet Li (born Li Lianjie on April 26, 1963, in Beijing, China) is one of the most celebrated martial artists and action film stars in cinematic history. A five-time national wushu champion in China, he transitioned from competitive martial arts into a globally recognized acting career spanning Chinese, Hong Kong, and Hollywood cinema.

His films have grossed billions of dollars worldwide, and he remains a defining figure in the popularization of Chinese martial arts cinema in the West. Beyond entertainment, Li has dedicated significant personal resources to humanitarian causes, founding the One Foundation in 2007 to promote disaster relief and philanthropy in China.

---

## Early Life & Background

Li Lianjie was born on April 26, 1963, in Beijing, China, the youngest of five children. His father died when he was two years old, leaving the family in modest circumstances. At age eight, he began training in wushu at the Beijing Sports and Exercise School under coach Wu Bin, who recognized his extraordinary natural talent.

His discipline and physical gifts were apparent almost immediately. By the time he was a teenager, he had already attracted national attention as a competitive martial artist, representing the pinnacle of Chinese state-sponsored athletic training during the 1970s.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Li won the Chinese National Wushu Championships five consecutive times between 1974 and 1979, becoming a national sports hero. In 1974, at just eleven years old, he performed a wushu demonstration before President Richard Nixon during Nixon's visit to China.

His film career began with *Shaolin Temple* (1982), one of the first Chinese films shot on location at the actual Shaolin Monastery in Henan Province. The film was a massive commercial success across Asia and launched Li as a major film star. Throughout the 1980s and early 1990s, he starred in a string of successful Hong Kong action films, most notably the *Once Upon a Time in China* series (1991–1997), in which he portrayed the legendary folk hero Wong Fei-hung. These films cemented his status as the defining martial arts star of his generation.

His Hollywood breakthrough came with *Lethal Weapon 4* (1998), in which he played the villain. Despite limited screen time, his performance drew widespread praise and led to starring roles in *Romeo Must Die* (2000), *Kiss of the Dragon* (2001), and *Fearless* (2006), the latter of which he announced as his final wushu epic.

## Key Achievements

- Five-time Chinese National Wushu Champion (1974–1979)
- Star of over 50 films across a career spanning four decades
- *Hero* (2002), directed by Zhang Yimou, grossed over $177 million worldwide
- Appeared in *The Expendables* franchise (2010–2014) alongside Sylvester Stallone and Arnold Schwarzenegger
- Founded the One Foundation in 2007, which raised over $60 million for Sichuan earthquake relief in 2008

---

## Controversies & Criticism

Li has faced criticism for his Hollywood career choices, with some critics arguing that many of his American films underutilized his abilities and reduced him to supporting roles despite his star power in Asia. His decision to take villain roles in early Hollywood productions was viewed by some Chinese audiences as a concession to Western stereotyping.

In 2018, Li's health became a public concern when photographs showing significant physical changes sparked widespread media speculation about his condition. He later confirmed he had been battling hyperthyroidism and spinal problems for years, conditions that required ongoing treatment and limited his physical activities.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Jet Li's impact on global martial arts cinema is difficult to overstate. Alongside Bruce Lee and Jackie Chan, he is widely credited with bringing Chinese martial arts films to mainstream Western audiences. His portrayal of Wong Fei-hung in the *Once Upon a Time in China* series revitalized the hero genre in Hong Kong cinema.

His philanthropic work through the One Foundation established a model for celebrity-driven charity in China and has influenced how public figures engage with disaster relief in the country. Li became a naturalized citizen of Singapore in 2009, and his career trajectory from state athlete to global film star to humanitarian reflects the sweeping changes in Chinese society over the past five decades.
