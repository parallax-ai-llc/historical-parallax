## Summary

Japanese serial killer who murdered 10 people in Shizuoka prefecture using poison between 1906 and 1924.

## Background

Sataro Fukiage’s crimes are generally discussed within early 20th-century Japanese criminal history, a period when sensational murder cases were widely covered and investigative practice was becoming more formalized.

## Modus Operandi

Reporting on the case emphasizes poisoning as the principal method. Poisoning cases often drew attention because they could be difficult to detect, required forensic confirmation, and frequently involved victims within the perpetrator’s social or economic circle.

## Investigation and Trial

- The murders attributed to Fukiage spanned many years, which is one reason the case is commonly labeled “serial” in later summaries.
- Following arrest and prosecution, the case proceeded through the Japanese criminal justice system of the era, culminating in a death sentence.

## Significance

The case is sometimes referenced in discussions of how long-running patterns of violence can remain obscured when individual incidents appear unrelated, and how later consolidation of evidence can reframe a sequence of deaths as a single criminal campaign.

## Public and Historical Memory

In modern compilations of Japanese crime history, Fukiage is typically treated as a cautionary example of poison-related homicide and the challenges of linking dispersed incidents over time.

The limited surviving biographical detail is often secondary to the case summary itself.
