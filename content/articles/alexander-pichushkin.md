## Summary
Alexander Pichushkin, 'The Chessboard Killer,' was convicted of 48 murders in Moscow between 1992 and 2006. He aimed to fill all 64 squares of a chessboard with victims, targeting elderly homeless men in Bitsa Park with vodka and a hammer. He was sentenced to life imprisonment in 2007.

## Early Life
Born April 9, 1974, near Moscow. As a child he suffered a head injury from a swing. Raised by his grandfather, who encouraged his chess interest. After his grandfather's death, he became isolated and resentful. [^1]

## Criminal Career
His first murder was in 1992 at age 18. He resumed killing in 2001, luring elderly homeless men to Bitsa Park with free vodka, then striking them with a hammer. He marked each victim on a chessboard and aimed for 64 kills. [^2]

## Capture / Downfall
His final victim, Marina Moskalyova, left a note saying she was meeting Pichushkin. Security cameras captured them entering the park. Arrested June 16, 2006, he confessed to 63 murders. Convicted of 48 murders in October 2007 and sentenced to life. [^3]

## Positive Perspectives
- Led to improvements in Moscow serial crime investigation [^4]
- Prompted security cameras in public parks
- Drew attention to homeless population vulnerability

## Negative Perspectives
- Convicted of 48 murders, claimed 63 [^5]
- Targeted homeless and elderly men
- Treated murder as a game
- Operated for years without detection
- Showed no remorse
