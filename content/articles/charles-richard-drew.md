## Summary

Charles Richard Drew was an American surgeon and medical researcher who helped pioneer large-scale blood storage and blood-bank practices, including the separation and preservation of plasma.

## Background

- Drew was trained in medicine and surgery and became a leading figure in transfusion-related research in the early 20th century.
- His work is commonly associated with the development of methods that made blood products easier to store, transport, and distribute at scale.

## Plasma and Blood Banking

- Drew’s research helped demonstrate how plasma could be processed and preserved, supporting longer storage times than whole blood in certain conditions.
- Large-scale blood banking requires not only medical technique but also logistics: donor recruitment, collection protocols, testing, refrigeration, and distribution.

## World War II-Era Programs

- Drew played a prominent role in early large blood-collection efforts linked to wartime needs, which accelerated institutional investment in transfusion infrastructure.
- These efforts helped standardize procedures that later became foundational for civilian blood-bank systems.

## Segregation Controversy

- Drew publicly criticized policies that segregated blood by race, arguing that such practices were not scientifically justified.
- The controversy is frequently cited in discussions of how social prejudice can shape medical policy even when it conflicts with scientific evidence.

## Legacy

- Drew is remembered as a major contributor to modern transfusion medicine and to the institutional model of blood banks.
- Many hospitals, schools, and awards have been named in his honor.
