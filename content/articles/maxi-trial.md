## Summary

Landmark Italian trial of 475 mafiosi that began in 1986 in Palermo. Led by judges Falcone and Borsellino, it resulted in 360 convictions and was the largest criminal trial in history.

## Background

The Maxi Trial was organized against the Sicilian Mafia (Cosa Nostra) and represented a shift from fragmented prosecutions to a coordinated, large-scale judicial strategy. It relied on extensive investigative work, including testimony from cooperating witnesses (pentiti) and the aggregation of many related criminal charges into a single proceeding.

## Key Figures and Methods

The case is closely associated with anti-mafia magistrates Giovanni Falcone and Paolo Borsellino and the broader “anti-mafia pool” approach. The trial used a specially constructed, high-security courtroom (often described as a bunker) reflecting the threat environment faced by judges, prosecutors, and witnesses.

## Outcomes and Significance

Beyond the conviction count, the Maxi Trial is frequently cited for:

- Demonstrating that Mafia structures could be described and prosecuted as an integrated criminal organization.
- Establishing precedents for large, complex organized-crime cases in Italy.
- Triggering both political support and violent backlash, as anti-mafia prosecutions intensified.

## Legacy

The trial’s long-term legacy is tied to Italy’s subsequent anti-mafia legal framework and public consciousness. It also remains a reference point in international organized-crime enforcement as an early example of scaling prosecutions to match the complexity of networked criminal groups.

## Further Notes

The Supreme Court’s later confirmation of many convictions is often cited as crucial, because it demonstrated that the outcomes could survive lengthy appeals and political pressure.
