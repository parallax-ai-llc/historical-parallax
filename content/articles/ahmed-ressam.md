## Summary

Algerian terrorist known as the 'Millennium Bomber' who was arrested at the US-Canada border in December 1999 with explosives intended to bomb Los Angeles International Airport. He became a major cooperating witness.

## Background

Ahmed Ressam was an Algerian national associated in open sources with jihadist networks that operated in the 1990s, including training and logistical support connected to al-Qaeda-linked figures. His case became a prominent example of late-1990s counterterrorism investigations spanning North America and Afghanistan.

## The 1999 Border Arrest ("Millennium Bomber" Case)

Ressam was stopped in December 1999 while attempting to enter the United States from Canada near Port Angeles, Washington. Authorities found explosives and related materials, and prosecutors alleged the intent was to attack Los Angeles International Airport (LAX) during the millennium holiday period.

Key elements often highlighted in summaries include:

- the role of border inspection and questioning in triggering the search
- the quantity and type of bomb-making materials seized
- the timing, which made the case a major national-security story at the turn of the year

## Cooperation and Sentencing History

After conviction, Ressam provided cooperation to U.S. authorities that became part of wider terrorism prosecutions. Later proceedings reflected disputes over the extent and value of that cooperation, and his sentence was ultimately increased.

## Historical Significance

The Ressam case is frequently referenced in discussions of:

- aviation and airport security risk assessments before 9/11
- intelligence collection on transnational militant travel routes
- the tradeoffs and reliability challenges involved in using cooperating witnesses in terrorism cases
