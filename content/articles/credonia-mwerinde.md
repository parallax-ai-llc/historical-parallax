## Summary

Ugandan cult leader who co-led the Movement for the Restoration of the Ten Commandments of God. The cult was responsible for the deaths of at least 778 followers in Uganda in 2000—one of the largest mass murders in African history.

## Background and Role in the Movement

The Movement for the Restoration of the Ten Commandments of God emerged in Uganda in the late 1980s as an apocalyptic religious group that emphasized strict moral rules and end-times expectations. Credonia Mwerinde is commonly described as one of the movement’s key leaders, alongside Joseph Kibwetere and others, and was associated with the group’s internal discipline and organization.

## 2000 Mass Deaths (Uganda)

In March 2000, the movement became internationally known after a large number of followers died in a major incident at a church in Kanungu District. Subsequent investigations reported additional bodies and mass graves at other properties linked to the movement, contributing to death toll estimates in the hundreds.

High-level points that appear in reporting and investigations include:

- The group reportedly required followers to surrender property and cut ties with relatives.
- The movement’s leadership was alleged to have controlled daily life, finances, and access to outsiders.
- After the Kanungu incident, authorities reported more deaths at locations connected to the movement.

## Legacy

The 2000 events are frequently cited in discussions of modern mass-casualty cult tragedies and new religious movements in East Africa. Mwerinde’s ultimate fate has been reported as unclear in many summaries, with some accounts describing her as missing after the events.
