## Summary

Australian bushranger Andrew George Scott who staged armed holdups and bank robberies. His love letters to a male companion are among the earliest documented gay love letters in Australian history.

## Early Life and Persona

Andrew George Scott was born in Ireland and later emigrated to Australia. He cultivated an image mixing religiosity, literacy, and theatrical self-presentation, adopting the persona “Captain Moonlite” and at times presenting himself as a clergyman.

## Notable Incidents

Accounts commonly describe a sequence of armed robberies and standoffs associated with Scott and his small group. In an era when newspapers closely followed bushranging, the mix of crime, drama, and personal mythology helped turn Moonlite into a widely reported figure.

## Trial and Execution

Following the final incidents attributed to his gang, Scott was tried and ultimately executed by hanging in Sydney in 1880. Contemporary reporting and later historians have debated details of his actions and motivations, but the legal outcome ended his brief but prominent criminal career.

## Letters and Historical Significance

Scott’s letters to James Nesbitt (often identified as his companion) have been cited as some of the earliest openly romantic correspondence between men in Australian historical records. These documents have contributed to later reassessments of Moonlite as both a criminal figure and a subject in LGBTQ+ history.

## Cultural Legacy

Captain Moonlite has appeared in biographies, historical studies, and creative works, reflecting enduring interest in bushranging and the way personal narratives can reshape the memory of nineteenth-century outlaws.
