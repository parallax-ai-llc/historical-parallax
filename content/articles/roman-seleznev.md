## Summary

Russian hacker and son of a Russian MP who stole credit card data from over 500 businesses, causing $170 million in losses. Arrested in the Maldives by US Secret Service. Sentenced to 27 years—the longest ever for hacking at the time.

## Background and Context

- Seleznev became notable not only for cybercrime activity but also for the political visibility of his family connections in Russia.
- Prosecutors framed the case as an example of transnational cybercrime where victims, infrastructure, and perpetrators spanned multiple jurisdictions.

## Modus Operandi (High-Level)

- Allegations centered on large-scale theft and trafficking of payment-card data, typically via compromised point-of-sale systems and subsequent resale.
- Investigators tied online handles and marketplaces to the operation through a mixture of digital forensics, undercover purchases, and infrastructure link analysis.

## Arrest and Jurisdiction

- The arrest in the Maldives and transfer to the United States became a focal point in later legal arguments about jurisdiction, due process, and the location of the defendant at the moment of seizure.
- U.S. authorities pursued the case in federal court, emphasizing the impact on U.S.-based merchants and financial networks.

## Sentencing and Impact

- The 27-year sentence was widely reported as among the longest for hacking-related convictions at the time, signaling a tougher U.S. stance on major cyber-enabled financial crime.
- The case is often cited in discussions about deterrence, international cooperation, and the practical difficulties of prosecuting suspects who operate outside the victim country.

## Why This Case Still Gets Mentioned

- It illustrates how cybercrime investigations can combine technical attribution with traditional investigative work.
- It highlights the role of international travel as a point of vulnerability for suspects who otherwise remain beyond reach of certain legal systems.
- It provides a benchmark for sentencing severity in high-loss, large-victim-count cybercrime cases.
