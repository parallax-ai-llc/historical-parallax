## Summary
On February 25, 2005, armed robbers stole €72 million worth of diamonds from a KLM cargo shipment at Amsterdam's Schiphol Airport. It was one of the largest diamond heists in history. Several suspects were arrested.

## How airport cargo heists typically work (context)
High-value gems and precious metals are often transported as cargo, and large hub airports can concentrate both **valuable shipments** and **complex subcontracting**, creating opportunities for organized theft. Analyses of major airport heists commonly highlight several recurring elements:

- **Disguise and impersonation:** Robbers may present as authorized personnel (security, handlers, or airline staff) to reduce scrutiny.
- **Timing and logistics:** Knowing when a shipment is transferred between custody points can be as important as force.
- **Insider risk:** Even limited insider knowledge—schedules, container identifiers, or access procedures—can materially lower the difficulty of theft.

## Reported operational features
Public accounts of the Schiphol diamond heist described an armed team that intercepted or accessed a diamond shipment within the airport’s cargo system and rapidly removed it. Reporting often framed the incident as a “professional” robbery because it appeared to exploit procedures and timing rather than relying on prolonged violence.

## Aftermath and security implications
The heist contributed to ongoing attention on:

- **Chain-of-custody controls** for high-value cargo
- **Segregation of duties** (so no single role can access shipment details and physical access)
- **Surveillance and access logging** in cargo zones

Even when arrests occur, recovery of stolen gems can be difficult because diamonds can be cut, mixed, or sold through multiple intermediaries.
