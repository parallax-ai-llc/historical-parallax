## Summary

Iranian serial killer known as the 'Spider Killer' who murdered 16 sex workers in Mashhad between 2000 and 2001. He claimed he was 'cleansing' the city of moral corruption. His case divided Iranian public opinion.

## Case overview (high-level)

Hanaei’s crimes targeted a highly marginalized population, and the case became widely known in Iran and internationally due to both the number of victims and the moral rhetoric he used to justify his actions. Media and public discussion often focused on the social vulnerability of the victims and the question of how authorities responded during the period when the killings were ongoing.

## Investigation and arrest

Accounts describe an investigation that intensified as the pattern became clearer and as public pressure increased. His arrest in 2001 ended the killing spree, after which the case moved quickly into the criminal justice process.

## Trial and public reaction

The case provoked polarized reactions, with some commentary emphasizing criminality and victim protection, and other commentary reflecting broader debates over morality, social control, and the treatment of sex workers. This division is frequently noted in retrospective coverage as part of why the case remained prominent beyond the immediate criminal proceedings.

## Legacy and wider context

The Hanaei case is often referenced in discussions of:

- Violence against marginalized groups.
- The social conditions that increase vulnerability to predation.
- How moral narratives can be weaponized to rationalize criminal behavior.
