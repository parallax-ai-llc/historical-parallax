## Summary

Head of Vichy France government who enthusiastically collaborated with Nazi Germany.

## Background

- Born in Châteldon, Puy-de-Dôme, and trained as a lawyer before entering politics.
- Built an early reputation on the French left, including a period as a socialist deputy, before shifting toward more pragmatic, centrist power-brokering.

## Political Career (Pre-Vichy)

- Served multiple times as a cabinet minister during the French Third Republic in the 1920s and early 1930s.
- Became Prime Minister of France (1931–1932 and 1935–1936), associated with attempts at economic stabilization during the Great Depression.
- Pursued diplomacy aimed at reducing tensions in Europe, including engagement with Italy and the Soviet Union, though these efforts were controversial and politically fragile.

## Role in Vichy France

- After France’s defeat in 1940, Laval became one of the central figures in the Vichy regime under Marshal Philippe Pétain.
- Served as head of government (effectively prime minister) and was a key architect of collaboration with Nazi Germany.
- Framed collaboration as a strategy to preserve limited French sovereignty, but in practice advanced policies aligned with German demands.

## Collaboration Policies and Consequences

- Promoted the Service du travail obligatoire (STO), which compelled French labor for work in Germany.
- Played a major role in anti-Jewish measures and the deportation of Jews from France, including the deportation of foreign and, increasingly, French Jews.
- Collaboration deepened Vichy’s complicity in wartime repression, policing, and political persecution.

## Trial, Execution, and Legacy

- Captured after the liberation of France and tried for treason and collaboration in 1945.
- Convicted and executed by firing squad; his trial and conduct remain subjects of historical debate.
- In French memory, Laval is commonly cited as a symbol of Vichy collaboration and its moral and political collapse.
