## Summary

Röntgen's discovery of X-rays in 1895 revolutionized medicine. The first X-ray of his wife's hand amazed the world. He received the first Nobel Prize in Physics in 1901.

## Scientific Context

X-rays emerged from late-19th-century research into electricity and gases, especially experiments with cathode rays in vacuum tubes. Röntgen’s work showed that a previously unrecognized form of radiation could pass through many materials while being absorbed by denser substances like bone.

## Early Medical Adoption

Within months of the announcement, clinicians began using X-ray imaging to locate fractures and foreign objects. The speed of adoption reflected both the immediate practical value and the relative simplicity of producing images once the apparatus was assembled.

## Safety and Standardization

Early users did not fully understand radiation risks, and harmful exposure was common before safety practices matured. Over time, shielding, dose awareness, and standardized procedures became central to making X-ray technology routine and safe in medicine.

## Long-Term Impact

X-ray imaging helped establish diagnostic radiology as a core medical discipline and also influenced physics, crystallography, and later technologies that depend on understanding electromagnetic radiation.

## Terminology and Naming

Röntgen initially referred to the phenomenon as "X" rays to indicate an unknown type of radiation. The name persists as a reminder that the discovery emerged from experiment before a full theoretical explanation was available.
