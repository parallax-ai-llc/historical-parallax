## Summary

Bosnian Serb security officer convicted of aiding and abetting genocide for his role in guarding and executing Srebrenica prisoners. He was chief of security for the Zvornik Brigade.

## Role and Context

Nikolić’s role as a brigade security officer placed him within the chain of custody and control over detainees in the context of the Srebrenica massacre. In tribunal narratives, “security” functions in this setting often include:

- Managing detention sites and prisoner transport
- Organizing guards and coordinating movement of captives
- Communicating with other military or police units involved in operations

## Tribunal Significance

Cases arising from Srebrenica became central to the international legal record on genocide in Bosnia and Herzegovina. Nikolić’s conviction is frequently discussed in terms of **aiding and abetting** liability—how a participant who facilitates crimes can be held responsible even when not alleged to be the top political or military decision-maker.

## Legacy

The proceedings contributed to documentation of events, survivor testimony, and historical record-keeping. They are often referenced in studies of international criminal law, including standards of proof, command structures, and the evidentiary reconstruction of mass-atrocity logistics.

## Why Srebrenica Cases Are Widely Cited

Beyond their immediate legal outcomes, Srebrenica-related judgments have been used to explain how international courts reason from dispersed evidence—orders, movements of units, detention conditions, and survivor accounts—into a coherent account of intent and responsibility. This makes them frequently assigned material in courses on genocide studies and transitional justice.

They also demonstrate how “logistics” (transport, guarding, detention) can be legally central in mass-atrocity prosecutions.
