## Summary

51-day standoff between the ATF/FBI and David Koreshs Branch Davidians at the Mount Carmel compound near Waco, Texas in 1993. It ended with a fire that killed 76 people including 25 children.

## Additional Context

### Key actors

- **Branch Davidians**: A religious group led at the time by **David Koresh** (born Vernon Howell).
- **ATF**: The Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives, which initiated the raid.
- **FBI**: Took over the standoff after the initial raid and managed negotiations and tactical operations.

### Timeline overview

The initial ATF attempt to execute search and arrest warrants on February 28, 1993 led to a firefight and casualties on both sides. The event transitioned into a prolonged standoff at the Mount Carmel Center near Waco, Texas, with negotiations continuing for weeks.

On April 19, 1993, the FBI launched an operation involving armored vehicles and the introduction of CS gas into the buildings. A fire ultimately consumed the compound; the cause and responsibility for the fire have been disputed in public debate.

### Aftermath and investigations

The siege led to criminal prosecutions of surviving Branch Davidians and multiple investigations and hearings, including federal reviews of law-enforcement actions. The episode became a major reference point in U.S. discussions about federal power, religious freedom, and the conduct of tactical law enforcement.

### Cultural and political impact

Waco has been cited by various extremist and anti-government movements as a grievance narrative, and it influenced later public scrutiny of federal agencies' use-of-force policies.
