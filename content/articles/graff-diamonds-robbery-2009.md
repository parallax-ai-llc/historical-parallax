## Summary
On August 6, 2009, two armed men stole £40 million worth of jewelry from the Graff Diamonds store in London's New Bond Street. It was one of the largest jewelry heists in British history. The robbers were disguised with prosthetic makeup.

## Investigation and Prosecution
Public reporting described a fast-moving investigation that combined CCTV review, forensic work, and tracing of vehicles and communications linked to the suspects. In widely cited accounts, the use of realistic prosthetic makeup became a notable feature of the case and prompted renewed attention to identity verification and in-store security procedures.

## Security Lessons
The robbery is often referenced in discussions of high-end retail risk because it involved a targeted selection of high-value items, rapid execution, and escape planning. Industry responses emphasized layered controls such as controlled entry, staff training for coercion scenarios, and improved coordination with nearby police patrols.

## Recovery and Ongoing Interest
As with many large jewelry thefts, public sources have noted that full recovery of stolen items can be difficult due to the ease of dismantling, recutting, or reselling gems through illicit channels. The case remains a frequent reference point in media and criminology for how disguise, intimidation, and timing can defeat single-point security measures.
This has made it a recurring example in training materials for luxury retail security and investigations.
