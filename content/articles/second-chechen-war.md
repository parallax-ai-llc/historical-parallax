## Summary

Russia's war to reclaim Chechnya killed an estimated 25,000-50,000 civilians. Russian forces committed widespread atrocities including filtration camps, extrajudicial executions, and torture.

## Background and Triggers

- The war followed the unstable post-Soviet period and the aftermath of the First Chechen War (1994–1996), during which Chechnya had de facto autonomy.
- Renewed fighting in 1999 was associated with:
  - incursions into neighboring Dagestan by militant groups
  - a series of apartment bombings in Russia (attribution and responsibility have been politically contentious)

## Major Phases (High-Level)

- Russia re-entered Chechnya with large-scale conventional forces, retaking Grozny after intense fighting.
- The conflict later shifted toward counterinsurgency operations, including sweeps and raids in Chechnya and surrounding regions.

## Human Rights Allegations

- International observers and human rights groups reported patterns of abuse, including:
  - filtration/detention operations
  - torture and enforced disappearances
  - extrajudicial killings
  - collective punishment and civilian harm

## Political Outcome

- Moscow installed and supported a pro-Kremlin Chechen leadership; Ramzan Kadyrov later became a central figure in Chechen governance.
- Although Russia declared an end to the "counterterrorism operation" in 2009, violence and insurgency-related incidents continued in the North Caucasus.

## Legacy

- The war had long-term consequences for Russian domestic politics, security services, and the broader trajectory of authoritarian consolidation.
- It remains a heavily contested subject in historical memory and international reporting.
