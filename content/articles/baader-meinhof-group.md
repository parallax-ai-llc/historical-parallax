## Summary

German far-left terrorist organization also known as the Red Army Faction that carried out bombings, assassinations, and bank robberies from 1970 to 1998. They killed 34 people.

## Background

The Baader–Meinhof Group is a common media label for the **Red Army Faction (RAF)**, a West German militant organization that emerged out of late-1960s radical politics. The group positioned itself as anti-imperialist and anti-capitalist and used violence as a political tactic.

## Organization and Tactics

Accounts of the RAF often distinguish multiple “generations” of members over time. Operations associated with the group included:

- bombings targeting state and economic institutions
- assassinations and attempted assassinations
- armed robberies and kidnappings used to finance or leverage political demands

## German Autumn (1977)

The 1977 crisis period is widely discussed as a turning point in West German domestic security policy. It involved high-profile attacks and an intense state response, and it remains a reference point in debates about counterterrorism, civil liberties, and the limits of political violence.

## Dissolution and Aftermath

The RAF announced its dissolution in 1998. The group’s history is still analyzed in journalism and scholarship as an example of postwar European left-wing militancy and the long-run social consequences of politically motivated violence.

## Discussion Points

- The “Baader–Meinhof” label is sometimes used loosely; many sources treat RAF as the more precise name.
- Scholarship commonly situates the RAF alongside other European militant groups of the period for comparative study.
- Public memory in Germany often centers on victim impact, state legitimacy, and the boundaries of protest.
