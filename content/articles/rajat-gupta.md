## Summary

Indian-American business executive who was the first non-white head of McKinsey & Company. He was convicted of insider trading for passing Goldman Sachs board information to hedge fund manager Raj Rajaratnam. Sentenced to 2 years.

## Additional Context

- Gupta's early prominence is tied to management consulting and corporate governance, including leadership at a top global consulting firm.
- His later legal case is commonly summarized as a major example of insider trading involving high-level boardroom information.

## Corporate Governance Significance

- Board memberships at large financial institutions can provide access to market-moving, confidential information.
- Cases like this are often used to illustrate why fiduciary duties and information controls are central to public-company governance.

## Legal and Ethical Themes (High-Level)

- Insider trading enforcement: how regulators and prosecutors build cases around communications, timing, and trading patterns.
- Conflicts of interest: social and business relationships can create incentives to disclose confidential information.
- Reputational consequences: beyond sentencing, convictions can reshape an executive's standing in business and philanthropy networks.

## Why the Case Is Remembered

- It linked elite institutions (a major investment bank, a prominent consulting career) with a high-profile hedge fund insider trading investigation.
- Often cited in discussions about the integrity of boards and the boundary between informal conversation and unlawful disclosure.
