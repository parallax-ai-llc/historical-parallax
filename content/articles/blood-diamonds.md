## Summary

Trade in conflict diamonds mined in war zones to finance armed conflict. Diamonds from Sierra Leone, Angola, and DRC funded brutal civil wars killing millions.

## Definition and Scope

- "Blood diamonds" (often used interchangeably with **conflict diamonds**) refers to rough diamonds mined and sold to finance armed groups or conflicts, frequently alongside severe human rights abuses.
- The term is commonly associated with conflicts in parts of **West and Central Africa** during the 1990s and early 2000s, though concerns about illicit extraction and trade can arise in other regions as well.

## How the Trade Worked (high level)

- In many conflict settings, armed actors sought control over diamond-rich areas or over the transport routes used to move rough stones.
- Rough diamonds are small, high-value, and relatively easy to conceal, which made them attractive for sanctions evasion and illicit trade.
- Once diamonds enter a legitimate supply chain through cutting, polishing, and resale, tracing their origin becomes difficult without robust certification and audit systems.

## International Response

- The UN and various governments used sanctions, embargoes, and monitoring mechanisms aimed at disrupting the trade that financed armed groups.
- The **Kimberley Process Certification Scheme (KPCS)** was launched in the early 2000s as an industry-government initiative to reduce the flow of conflict diamonds.
- The Kimberley Process relies on member states implementing controls on rough diamond exports/imports and issuing certificates for compliant shipments.

## Criticisms and Limitations

- Critics have argued that the Kimberley Process definition of "conflict diamond" can be too narrow, potentially excluding some abuses not tied to rebel movements.
- Enforcement varies by country, and weak governance, corruption, or smuggling networks can undermine certification regimes.
- Human rights groups and investigative journalists have periodically highlighted gaps in traceability and compliance, leading to calls for stronger standards.

## Cultural and Economic Impact

- The issue reshaped consumer awareness and corporate responsibility programs in the jewelry sector.
- It also influenced policy discussions about supply-chain due diligence for other commodities linked to conflict or forced labor.

## Notes

- The human toll of conflicts associated with diamond revenues is widely recognized, but specific casualty estimates vary by conflict and by source; broad figures should be treated cautiously.
