## Summary

Japanese Red Army member who carried out the Lod Airport massacre in Israel in 1972, opening fire on passengers and throwing grenades killing 26 people. He is the sole survivor of the three attackers.

## Historical Context

Okamoto was a member of the Japanese Red Army, a far-left militant organization associated with international attacks during the late 20th century.
The 1972 Lod Airport attack (also known as the Lod Airport massacre) was a landmark case of transnational terrorism, carried out far from the perpetrators' home country.
The operation is often discussed in studies of how militant groups formed alliances across borders.

## The Lod Airport Attack

Open sources describe an assault involving firearms and grenades against civilians at the airport.
The incident killed and injured numerous people and had long-term political and security consequences.
It is frequently cited as an example of attacks targeting public spaces to maximize fear and attention.

## Legal and Diplomatic Developments

Okamoto was captured, tried, and sentenced in Israel.
His release in a 1985 prisoner exchange became part of the broader history of negotiations involving detained militants.
Subsequent reports discuss his later status abroad and the political sensitivities surrounding extradition and asylum.

## Significance

The case remains a reference point in discussions of international terrorism in the 1970s.
It also illustrates how a single high-profile attack can shape security practices and diplomatic relations for years.
