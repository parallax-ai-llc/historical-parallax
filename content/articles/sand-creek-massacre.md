## Summary

Colorado militia attacked a peaceful Cheyenne and Arapaho camp at Sand Creek killing 230 people, mostly women, children, and elderly. Soldiers mutilated bodies and took scalps.

## Background

The attack occurred during the American Civil War era amid escalating conflict on the Colorado Plains between U.S. settlers, territorial authorities, and Indigenous nations. A Cheyenne and Arapaho encampment was located along Sand Creek in the Colorado Territory, near Fort Lyon.

The village included people who believed they were under U.S. protection after earlier contacts with U.S. officials. Contemporary accounts and later investigations emphasize that many in the camp were non-combatants.

## Participants and Leadership

- **3rd Colorado Cavalry**: A U.S. volunteer unit involved in the raid.
- **Colonel John Chivington**: A principal commander associated with the attack.
- **Cheyenne and Arapaho leadership**: The camp is commonly associated in public memory with leaders such as **Black Kettle**, though the village included multiple families and bands.

## What Happened

On 29 November 1864, militia forces attacked the encampment at dawn. Reports from survivors, witnesses, and later U.S. inquiries describe indiscriminate killing and subsequent mutilation of bodies.

Casualty figures are reported differently across sources. Many summaries emphasize that the dead were largely women, children, and elderly people, which is central to why the event is widely characterized as a massacre.

## Immediate Aftermath

News of the attack prompted political controversy in the United States. U.S. congressional and military investigations criticized the action and the conduct of the troops, contributing to reputational damage and resignations among some involved officials.

The massacre also intensified cycles of retaliation and violence on the Plains, helping set the stage for later conflicts between the U.S. government and Cheyenne, Arapaho, and allied groups.

## Historical Significance

Sand Creek became a symbol of the consequences of broken assurances and frontier warfare. It remains an important reference point in discussions of:

- U.S. territorial expansion and Indigenous displacement
- Civilian protection and wartime conduct
- Memory, apology, and commemoration in U.S. history
