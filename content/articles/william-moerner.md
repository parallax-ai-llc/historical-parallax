## Summary

W.E. Moerner is an American physical chemist who shared the 2014 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for super-resolved fluorescence microscopy.

## Additional Context

- **Field**: Physical chemistry and biophysics, especially optical spectroscopy at the single-molecule level.
- **Why single molecules matter**: Measuring one fluorescent molecule at a time avoids ensemble averaging, revealing heterogeneity and dynamics that are otherwise hidden.

## Key Contributions

- **Single-molecule spectroscopy**: Moerner is widely credited with early demonstrations that individual molecules could be detected optically in solids, a foundational step toward modern single-molecule methods.
- **Super-resolution microscopy**: Single-molecule localization approaches (a family of techniques that includes methods such as PALM/STORM-style localization) enabled imaging beyond the diffraction limit by fitting the positions of individual emitters.
- **Bridge from physics to biology**: The techniques associated with his work became central tools for studying cellular structures and molecular machines at nanometer-scale precision.

## Nobel Prize Context (2014)

The 2014 Nobel Prize in Chemistry recognized advances in fluorescence microscopy that broke the classical diffraction limit, transforming optical imaging in chemistry and the life sciences.

## Related Concepts

- **Diffraction limit**: The conventional resolution bound in light microscopy that super-resolution methods circumvent.
- **Fluorophore photophysics**: Blinking, bleaching, and switching behavior used to separate emitters in time.
- **Localization precision**: The statistical accuracy of estimating a molecule’s position from its point-spread function.
