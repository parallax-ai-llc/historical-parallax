## Summary

Haitian military leader who overthrew democratically elected President Jean-Bertrand Aristide in a 1991 coup. His regime killed thousands before a US-led invasion restored democracy.

## Background

Raoul Cédras rose through the ranks of the Haitian Armed Forces (Forces Armées d’Haïti, FAd’H). By the early 1990s, the Haitian military remained a central political institution, and the transition to competitive elections created ongoing tension between elected civilian authority and entrenched security actors.

## Role in the 1991 coup

The 1991 coup removed President Jean-Bertrand Aristide, who had been elected earlier that year. Cédras became the de facto head of the military-backed authorities, and the armed forces and allied paramilitary elements played a decisive role in suppressing opponents. The period is commonly associated with widespread political violence, intimidation, and large-scale displacement.

## Domestic and international response

International pressure increased over time through diplomatic isolation and economic measures, alongside negotiation efforts aimed at restoring constitutional government. Human rights reporting during the military period documented abuses tied to security forces and aligned groups, which became a major driver of external intervention and subsequent accountability debates.

## 1994 intervention and exile

In 1994, a U.S.-led multinational operation (often described in policy terms as an intervention to restore Haiti’s elected leadership) compelled the military leadership to step aside and enabled Aristide’s return. Cédras left Haiti after the transition and lived abroad.

## Legacy

Cédras is frequently referenced in discussions of Haiti’s post–Cold War crises as an emblematic figure of military interference in democratic governance. His tenure is also invoked in debates over the dissolution of the Haitian army, the long-term impacts of political violence on Haitian civil society, and the international community’s role in shaping Haiti’s institutional trajectory.
