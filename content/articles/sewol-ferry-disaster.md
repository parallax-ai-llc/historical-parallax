## Summary

Sinking of the MV Sewol ferry on April 16, 2014 that killed 304 people, mostly high school students on a field trip. The crew abandoned ship while telling passengers to stay in their cabins.

## Background

The MV *Sewol* was a passenger ferry operating in South Korea. In the years leading up to the disaster, public discussion later focused on issues such as maritime safety practices, oversight, and how passenger vessels were operated and inspected.

## The sinking

On 16 April 2014, the ferry capsized and sank during a trip in waters near Jindo. Many passengers remained inside the vessel after being instructed to stay where they were, while rescue efforts unfolded over hours.

Key elements frequently highlighted in later accounts include:

- Confusing and delayed evacuation messaging.
- The role of the crew and bridge decisions during the emergency.
- The rapid loss of stability after the vessel began to list.

## Response and aftermath

The event became a national trauma in South Korea, with prolonged search, recovery, and identification efforts. The disaster prompted extensive public mourning and sharp criticism of institutional preparedness.

In the aftermath, South Korea saw sustained debate over:

- Emergency-response coordination among agencies.
- Regulatory accountability and enforcement culture.
- Safety reforms for passenger shipping and school travel.

## Investigations and accountability

Investigations, trials, and inquiries followed, examining responsibility across individuals and institutions. Legal outcomes and public inquiries were closely watched as part of a broader demand for answers and systemic change.

## Legacy

The Sewol disaster remains a defining reference point in South Korea for discussions about public safety, government responsibility, and crisis communication.
