## Summary

See Nanjing Massacre. Mass murder of 200,000-300,000 Chinese civilians by Japanese forces in 1937-1938.

## Naming and Terminology

"Nanking" is an older romanization of **Nanjing**, commonly used in English-language sources from the early-to-mid 20th century. In modern scholarship and media, the event is more often referred to as the **Nanjing Massacre**, though both names generally describe the same set of atrocities associated with the occupation of the city in late 1937 and early 1938.

## Overview of the Atrocity

The term refers to widespread war crimes committed during the occupation period, including:

- mass killings of civilians and prisoners,
- large-scale sexual violence,
- looting and destruction of property.

## Documentation and Sources

Historical understanding has relied on multiple types of evidence, such as:

- contemporary diaries and letters from residents and foreign observers,
- photographs and film footage (where available),
- wartime and postwar military records,
- survivor testimony and local archival materials.

Different bodies of evidence have been emphasized by different historians, contributing to ongoing disputes about interpretation and casualty estimates.

## Postwar Accountability

After World War II, war crimes proceedings in Asia addressed atrocities committed during Japan’s wartime campaigns in China, including crimes linked to the occupation of Nanjing/Nanking. These trials helped shape the postwar record, even as debates continued in later decades.

## Public Memory and Commemoration

The massacre is commemorated in China through memorials and museums and remains a recurring topic in education and public discourse. Internationally, it is also discussed in the context of wartime atrocities, historical reconciliation, and the politics of memory in East Asia.
