## Summary

Wiley Post was an American aviator who became one of the most celebrated pilots of the early 20th century, renowned for his solo circumnavigation of the globe and pioneering work in high-altitude flight. Flying despite the loss of his left eye in an oilfield accident, Post demonstrated that physical disability was no barrier to extraordinary achievement. His technical innovations in pressurized flight suits laid foundational groundwork for modern aviation and space exploration.

Post's career spanned the golden age of aviation, a period of rapid experimentation and heroic long-distance flights that captured public imagination worldwide. He combined natural flying instincts with a methodical approach to engineering challenges, making him as much an inventor as a pilot. His contributions to understanding the jet stream and stratospheric flight had lasting scientific consequences.

He died in 1935 in a plane crash near Point Barrow, Alaska, alongside humorist Will Rogers, a tragedy that shocked the nation and cut short a career still generating new discoveries.

## Early Life & Background

Wiley Hardeman Post was born on November 22, 1898, near Grand Plain, Texas, and raised in a farming family that relocated to Oklahoma during his childhood. Formal education ended early, and Post worked various jobs before gravitating toward the nascent oil industry. In 1926, an accident while working as a roughneck resulted in the loss of his left eye. The $1,800 workers' compensation settlement he received proved pivotal — he used the funds to purchase his first aircraft.

## Rise to Prominence

Post learned to fly with characteristic speed and confidence, earning a reputation as a skilled and daring pilot. He gained his first significant public exposure as a stunt pilot and later as a personal pilot for Oklahoma oilman F.C. Hall. Hall's Lockheed Vega, the *Winnie Mae*, became the aircraft with which Post would claim his greatest records.

In 1930, Post and navigator Harold Gatty entered the National Air Races, boosting his profile. The following year, on June 23–July 1, 1931, Post and Gatty flew around the world in 8 days, 15 hours, and 51 minutes, beating the previous record by nearly four days.

## Key Achievements

Post's most celebrated feat came on July 15–22, 1933, when he completed the first solo circumnavigation of the globe, covering approximately 15,596 miles in 7 days, 18 hours, and 49 minutes. He relied heavily on an early autopilot device called the Sperry gyroscope, making this as much a triumph of aviation technology as of individual skill.

Equally significant was his experimental work in high-altitude flight from 1934 onward. Recognizing that aircraft could travel faster in the low-pressure upper atmosphere, Post collaborated with the B.F. Goodrich Company to develop one of the world's first practical pressure suits. He conducted multiple flights above 30,000 feet in the *Winnie Mae*, becoming the first pilot to intentionally exploit the jet stream for speed. These flights established principles that would directly inform the development of military high-altitude aircraft and, eventually, spacesuits.

## Controversies & Criticism

Post's navigation and record claims occasionally drew scrutiny from contemporaries, with some pilots questioning the precision of his measured flight times. His habit of pushing aircraft beyond manufacturer specifications alarmed aviation authorities. The aircraft he flew with Will Rogers in 1935 was a hybrid floatplane critics considered front-heavy and ill-suited to Alaskan conditions.

## Legacy & Influence

Post's pressure suit work is widely acknowledged as a direct precursor to the suits worn by astronauts and high-altitude military pilots. His documentation of jet stream behavior contributed meaningfully to meteorology and flight planning. He was posthumously inducted into the National Aviation Hall of Fame in 1969. The *Winnie Mae* is preserved at the Smithsonian National Air and Space Museum in Washington, D.C.
