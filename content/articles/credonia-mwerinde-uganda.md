## Summary

Ugandan doomsday cult leader who engineered the deaths of over 700 members of the Movement for the Restoration of the Ten Commandments. She had the church doors nailed shut before setting the building on fire.

## Key Details

### Movement for the Restoration of the Ten Commandments of God

The Movement for the Restoration of the Ten Commandments of God was a Ugandan new religious movement that blended apocalyptic Christian themes with strict behavioral rules and communal living. Leaders reportedly demanded the surrender of money and property, enforced intense discipline, and emphasized an imminent end-time timetable.

### Lead-up to the mass deaths

Public accounts describe a period of escalating pressure on members as predicted dates passed without the promised apocalypse. In March 2000, a large gathering at a church compound ended in a catastrophic fire; investigators later found additional victims at other sites associated with the group, including bodies in pits and mass graves.

### Uncertainty and attribution

Sources frequently describe Credonia Mwerinde as a central leader, but details of command responsibility, planning, and the precise sequence of events have been contested in some reporting. The case is often framed as involving both mass murder and mass suicide dynamics, with ongoing debate about the extent of coercion and deception used against followers.

## Aftermath and Significance

The incident became one of the deadliest cult-related tragedies recorded. It is cited in discussions of religious extremism, fraud, and the challenges governments face in monitoring high-control groups while respecting freedom of belief. The case also prompted scrutiny of local oversight mechanisms, missing-person reporting, and investigative capacity.

### Commonly reported profile

In public summaries, Mwerinde is described as a former sex worker who became a prominent figure within the movement’s leadership circle. Accounts emphasize her role in recruitment and enforcement of group rules, though biographical specifics vary by source.
