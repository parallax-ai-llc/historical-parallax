## Context

Alvarado was one of the principal Spanish conquistadors operating in Mesoamerica during the early 16th century. He served under Hernán Cortés and later led campaigns in what is now Guatemala and neighboring regions, relying on alliances with some indigenous groups while waging war on others.

## Violence and the Toxcatl massacre

The massacre at the Templo Mayor during the festival of Toxcatl is often described as a turning point that intensified resistance and contributed to the wider cycle of siege and retaliation around Tenochtitlan. Contemporary and later accounts portray the attack as both strategically disastrous and morally emblematic of conquest-era brutality.

## Conquest of Guatemala

In Central America, Alvarado's campaigns are remembered for scorched-earth tactics, enslavement practices, and the severe demographic and social disruption that followed Spanish domination, including forced labor systems and the reordering of land and political authority.

## Why he is remembered

Alvarado is frequently cited as an archetype of conquistador violence because his name is attached to episodes where civilians were targeted and ceremonial spaces were violated, accelerating the collapse of existing political orders.

## Legacy

The historical memory of Alvarado differs sharply by perspective: Spanish imperial narratives once framed conquest as expansion and conversion, while indigenous histories and modern scholarship emphasize dispossession, mass death, and coercion as central features of the era.
