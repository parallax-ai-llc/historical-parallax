## Overview

Alfred Lothar Wegener (1 November 1880 – November 1930) was a German meteorologist, climatologist, geophysicist, and polar researcher. He is most widely remembered as the originator of the continental drift hypothesis, first presented in 1912, proposing that continents move over geological time.

## Continental drift hypothesis

Wegener argued that continents had once been joined and later separated and drifted apart. His reasoning drew on multiple lines of evidence available at the time, including:

- The apparent “jigsaw” fit of continental coastlines (notably South America and Africa).
- Similarities in rock formations and mountain belts across oceans.
- Fossil distributions suggesting past connections between now-separated landmasses.
- Paleoclimate indicators (such as evidence of ancient glaciation) that appeared inconsistent with present-day continental positions.

During Wegener’s lifetime, many geologists rejected the hypothesis largely because a convincing physical mechanism for continental movement was not yet established. The idea gained much broader acceptance decades later with developments such as seafloor spreading and paleomagnetism, forming part of the modern framework of plate tectonics.

## Meteorology and polar research

In addition to geology, Wegener made substantial contributions in meteorology and was involved in expeditions to Greenland focused on polar atmospheric circulation. Accounts of these expeditions note that participants collected extensive meteorological observations, overwintered on the Greenland ice sheet, and carried out early ice-core drilling efforts on Arctic glaciers.

## Legacy

Wegener’s name is commonly associated with several concepts and terms across Earth science, reflecting his cross-disciplinary impact. His role in articulating continental drift remains a prominent example of a scientific hypothesis that was initially controversial but later supported by new evidence and a unifying explanatory model.
