## Summary

Israeli spy who infiltrated the highest levels of the Syrian government in the 1960s, posing as a wealthy Syrian businessman. His intelligence was invaluable for the Six-Day War. Publicly hanged in Damascus.

## Early Life

Eli Cohen was born in Alexandria, Egypt, to a Jewish family and later immigrated to Israel. Biographical accounts describe him as multilingual and culturally adept—traits that later supported clandestine work requiring social integration and persuasive cover stories.

## Espionage Role

Cohen is widely known for operating under a false identity in Syria during the early 1960s. The core elements emphasized in most summaries include:

- **Deep-cover tradecraft**: presenting as a well-connected, wealthy expatriate with access to elite social circles.
- **Network-building**: cultivating relationships with officials and intermediaries rather than relying solely on technical collection.
- **Reporting**: providing Israel with political and military insights attributed to his proximity to decision-makers.

Because many operational details remain contested or are presented through later retellings, secondary sources often differ on the precise extent of his access and the specific intelligence he provided.

## Capture and Execution

Cohen was arrested by Syrian security services in 1965. He was tried and publicly executed in Damascus. His case became one of the most well-known examples of a high-impact intelligence penetration ending in a public diplomatic and propaganda event.

## Aftermath and Legacy

In Israel, Cohen has been memorialized as a national intelligence figure, with public commemoration and cultural portrayals spanning books, journalism, and screen adaptations. His story is also frequently cited in discussions of:

- the strategic value and risks of human intelligence (HUMINT),
- the difficulty of maintaining deep cover under tightening counterintelligence,
- how espionage cases can shape public narratives long after the operational period.
