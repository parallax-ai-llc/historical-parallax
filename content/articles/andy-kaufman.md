## Summary

Andy Kaufman was an American comedian, actor, and performance artist whose radical approach to comedy defied every convention of the form. Rather than telling jokes, he created elaborate personas, staged confrontations, and blurred the line between performance and reality in ways that baffled, provoked, and fascinated audiences in equal measure. His work remains unlike anything before or since.

Active primarily from the mid-1970s until his death in 1984, Kaufman achieved mainstream recognition through his role as Latka Gravas on the sitcom *Taxi* while simultaneously pursuing avant-garde performance art that alienated as many people as it captivated. He died of lung cancer at age 35, having spent barely a decade in the public eye, yet his influence on comedy, performance, and the concept of the anti-comedian proved enormous and enduring.

## Early Life & Background

Andrew Geoffrey Kaufman was born on January 17, 1949, in New York City and raised in Great Neck, Long Island. From childhood he exhibited an unusual inner world, performing for imaginary audiences in his bedroom and developing an instinct for unsettling his observers. He briefly attended Grahm Junior College in Boston before transferring to Maharishi International University in Fairfield, Iowa, where he studied Transcendental Meditation — a practice he maintained devotedly for the rest of his life. His early stand-up work in New York clubs in the early 1970s was already unconventional, featuring his meek "Foreign Man" character who would suddenly transform into a flawless Elvis Presley impression.

## Rise to Prominence

Kaufman appeared on the very first episode of *Saturday Night Live* in October 1975, performing his Mighty Mouse lip-sync routine to national television audiences. The bit was strange, committed, and unlike anything on American television. Producer Lorne Michaels recognized his singular quality but found him difficult to work with repeatedly. The more consequential break came with *Taxi* (1978–1983), where his childlike immigrant character Latka became a fan favorite and earned him two Golden Globe nominations. Simultaneously, he was developing his most confrontational material — prolonged silences, reading entire novels aloud to audiences, and his inter-gender wrestling act, in which he challenged women from the audience and taunted crowds with deliberate obnoxiousness.

## Key Achievements

- Appeared on the premiere episode of *Saturday Night Live* on October 11, 1975
- Starred in *Taxi* for five seasons (1978–1983), winning a Golden Globe in 1982
- Developed Tony Clifton, an abrasive lounge singer alter ego so fully realized that Kaufman occasionally hired another actor to perform as Clifton simultaneously
- Staged a months-long professional wrestling feud with Jerry "The King" Lawler in 1982, culminating in a widely viewed *Late Night with David Letterman* confrontation
- Performed a live reading of *The Great Gatsby* at Carnegie Hall in 1979

## Controversies & Criticism

Kaufman's inter-gender wrestling act, in which he declared himself "World Intergender Wrestling Champion" and belittled female challengers, drew genuine anger from audiences and critics who could not determine where performance ended and sincere misogyny began. His extended wrestling feud with Jerry Lawler involved a staged pile-driver that was presented as a real injury. *Saturday Night Live* viewers voted in 1983 to remove him from the show permanently. Many colleagues and audiences found his work exhausting or contemptible rather than funny, and that ambiguity was largely by design.

## Legacy & Influence

Kaufman's influence is pervasive in comedy and performance art. R.E.M. memorialized him in their 1992 song "Man on the Moon," which became the title of the 1999 biographical film starring Jim Carrey. His deconstruction of comedian-audience expectations prefigured the anti-comedy of performers like Andy Kaufman, Nathan Fielder, and Eric André. The question of what comedy is allowed to do — and whether a performer owes the audience anything — runs through his entire body of work and remains unresolved.
