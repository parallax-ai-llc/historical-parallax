## Summary

African American abstract painter. First woman to have solo show at the Whitney Museum. Her mosaic-like paintings inspired by nature were made in her 70s.

## Early life and education

Alma Woodsey Thomas was born in Georgia and later became closely associated with Washington, D.C., where she lived and worked for much of her adult life. Biographical accounts often emphasize her formal art education and her long career as an educator.

## Teaching career

Thomas spent decades teaching art in Washington, D.C. Her teaching work is frequently described as central to her identity, with painting becoming an even more prominent focus after retirement.

## Artistic style

She is best known for bright, patterned abstractions that many writers describe as "mosaic-like" or composed of repeated brush marks. Her work is often discussed in relation to:

- Color-based abstraction in mid-20th-century U.S. art
- Nature as a recurring source of titles and visual motifs
- The late-life emergence of a distinctive mature style

## Recognition and legacy

Thomas's growing institutional recognition has made her a reference point in discussions of Black modernism and the art worlds of Washington, D.C. Her work appears in major public collections, and later retrospectives have emphasized both her innovation and the historical barriers she faced.
