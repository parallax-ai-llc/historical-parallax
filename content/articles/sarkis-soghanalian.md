## Summary

Lebanese-American arms dealer known as the 'Merchant of Death' who supplied Saddam Hussein with weapons worth billions. He was a CIA asset who also armed factions in Lebanon and Latin America. Convicted of arms dealing.

## Background

Sarkis Soghanalian was a prominent figure in late–Cold War era arms trafficking, associated in press and court reporting with large, state-linked weapons procurement deals as well as covert or semi-covert supply networks.

## Operations and Networks

Accounts of his career typically emphasize:

- Intermediary roles between manufacturers, brokers, and state buyers.
- The use of front companies and international shipping routes common to transnational arms commerce.
- Deal-making that intersected with regional conflicts in the Middle East and parts of Latin America.

## Legal and Political Exposure

Soghanalian’s notoriety stems in part from criminal cases and public allegations that tied his activities to embargo violations or unlicensed exports. Discussion of his role as an intelligence “asset” appears in some sources; the verifiable core is that his dealings attracted sustained attention from law enforcement and journalists.

## Legacy

He is often cited in popular histories of arms dealing as an example of how private brokers could influence conflict supply chains, particularly when demand surged during war and procurement oversight was weak.

## Historical Context

His story is frequently placed alongside broader debates about export controls, end-user monitoring, and the difficulty of regulating weapons flows across multiple jurisdictions.
