## Summary

Bosnian Serb general who personally oversaw the Srebrenica genocide in July 1995, promising refugees they would be safe before ordering the massacre of 8,372 men and boys.

## Role in the Bosnian War

- Ratko Mladić served as a senior commander of the Army of Republika Srpska (VRS) during the Bosnian War.
- The Srebrenica enclave had been designated a UN “safe area,” which became central to later legal and historical assessments of what occurred there.

## Srebrenica (July 1995)

- In July 1995, Bosnian Serb forces captured Srebrenica and separated men and boys from women and children.
- The killings and mass graves that followed were later adjudicated by international courts as genocide.
- The episode remains one of the most documented mass atrocities in Europe since World War II.

## Capture and Conviction

- After years as a fugitive, Mladić was arrested in 2011 and transferred to the Hague.
- In 2017, an international tribunal convicted him and imposed a life sentence, which was later upheld on appeal.

## Legacy

- Srebrenica remains a central symbol in debates over international protection, peacekeeping mandates, and accountability for mass atrocities.
- The documentation of the killings has shaped historical memory, education, and memorialization efforts in Bosnia and internationally.

## Notes

- The Srebrenica events are closely tied to broader questions about how international institutions respond when declared “safe areas” collapse.
