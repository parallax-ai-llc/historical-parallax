## Background (Fiction)

Travis Bickle is the central character of Martin Scorsese’s film *Taxi Driver* (1976), written by Paul Schrader and portrayed by Robert De Niro. He is depicted as a socially isolated, mentally unstable Vietnam War veteran who works as a night-shift taxi driver in New York City.

## Character Arc (High-Level)

- **Isolation and insomnia:** The story frames Travis as unable to sleep and increasingly detached from ordinary social life, using night driving as a way to occupy his time.
- **Moral disgust at the city:** He repeatedly expresses revulsion toward crime, sex work, and public corruption, interpreting the city as “unclean.”
- **Fixation on a political campaign worker:** Travis develops an intense, awkward interest in Betsy, a staffer working for a presidential candidate (Charles Palantine).
- **Escalation toward violence:** After a series of social failures and growing paranoia, he begins preparing for violent action, including acquiring weapons and training.
- **Attempted political assassination (plot point):** Travis targets Palantine in an assassination attempt, but fails to carry it out.
- **Rescue of Iris (plot point):** He later attacks a brothel/pimp operation to free Iris, a teenage sex worker, resulting in multiple deaths and severe injury to himself.

## Themes and Interpretation

Scholarly and critical discussion often treats Travis as a study in alienation, masculine identity, and the psychological effects of war and urban anomie. The film’s use of voice-over diary entries and subjective cinematography invites viewers to see events through Travis’s distorted perspective rather than as an objective account.

## Cultural Impact

- The character became a widely cited example of the “antihero” in 1970s American cinema.
- Lines and imagery associated with Travis (notably the mirror monologue) are frequently referenced in popular culture.
- The film and character are often discussed in relation to depictions of vigilantism and political violence in media.
