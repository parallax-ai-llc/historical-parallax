## Summary

Mehmed II, known as "the Conqueror" (Turkish: *Fatih Sultan Mehmed*), was the seventh Sultan of the Ottoman Empire, reigning from 1444–1446 and again from 1451 until his death in 1481. He is best remembered for his conquest of Constantinople on May 29, 1453, which ended the Byzantine Empire after more than a millennium and transformed the Ottoman state into a major world power.

His reign marked a turning point in world history. By capturing Constantinople, Mehmed extinguished the last remnant of the Roman Empire, reshaped the geopolitical landscape of Europe and the Near East, and established Istanbul as the new imperial capital. He went on to expand Ottoman territory dramatically across Anatolia, the Balkans, and the Black Sea region, earning a reputation as one of the most formidable military strategists of the medieval period.

Beyond warfare, Mehmed was a patron of learning, fluent in multiple languages, and deeply engaged with philosophy and the arts. He invited scholars, artists, and theologians from across the known world to his court, positioning Constantinople as a new center of civilization bridging East and West.

## Early Life & Background

Mehmed was born on March 30, 1432, in Edirne, the Ottoman capital at the time, to Sultan Murad II and a concubine. He received a rigorous education in theology, mathematics, history, and languages — reportedly mastering Greek, Latin, Arabic, Persian, and Serbian in addition to Turkish. His early years were marked by political turbulence; he briefly ascended to the throne at age 12 in 1444 when his father abdicated, but Murad II returned to power in 1446 following a military crisis.

## Rise to Prominence

Mehmed permanently reclaimed the sultanate upon his father's death in February 1451. Almost immediately, he turned his focus to Constantinople, the heavily fortified Byzantine capital that had withstood Ottoman sieges for decades. He ordered the construction of Rumelihisarı fortress on the European shore of the Bosphorus in 1452, cutting off Byzantine supply lines. On April 6, 1453, he launched a siege with an army estimated at 60,000–80,000 troops and an artillery train that included massive bombards designed by the Hungarian engineer Urban. The city fell on May 29, 1453, after 53 days.

## Key Achievements

- **Conquest of Constantinople (1453):** The defining event of his reign, ending the Byzantine Empire and securing Ottoman dominance over the straits between Europe and Asia.
- **Balkan campaigns:** He captured Serbia (1459), the Despotate of the Morea (1460), and Trebizond (1461), consolidating control over southeastern Europe and Anatolia.
- **Legal codification:** Mehmed issued the *Kanunname*, a comprehensive legal code standardizing Ottoman administration, taxation, and court procedure.
- **Cultural patronage:** He commissioned the Topkapı Palace (begun c. 1459), restored the Hagia Sophia as a mosque, and invited Italian artists including Gentile Bellini to his court in 1479–1480.
- **Naval expansion:** He built a powerful Ottoman fleet that projected power across the Black Sea and eastern Mediterranean, capturing Venetian-held Negroponte (Euboea) in 1470.

## Controversies & Criticism

The fall of Constantinople involved significant violence, including the killing and enslavement of a substantial portion of the city's population, though Mehmed also issued orders protecting certain communities. His campaigns in the Balkans caused widespread displacement and demographic disruption. His attempted invasion of Italy — landing at Otranto in 1480 — alarmed European powers, and the garrison's massacre of the town's population remains a dark episode of his later reign. Some Ottoman historians also note his execution of grand viziers and members of his own family as part of consolidating power, practices later codified into dynastic law.

## Legacy & Influence

Mehmed's conquest of Constantinople reoriented European trade routes and is frequently cited as contributing to the conditions that prompted the Age of Exploration. He transformed Istanbul into one of the world's great cities, repopulating it through deliberate resettlement policies and constructing mosques, markets, and schools. His legal and administrative reforms shaped the Ottoman state for generations. In Turkey, he remains a national hero; the Fatih district of Istanbul is named in his honor, and May 29 is commemorated annually as the anniversary of the conquest.
