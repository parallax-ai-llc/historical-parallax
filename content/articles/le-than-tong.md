## Summary

Lê Thánh Tông (黎聖宗, 1442-1497) was the fifth emperor of Vietnam's Later Lê dynasty, ruling from 1460 to 1497, and is widely considered one of the greatest monarchs in Vietnamese history[^1]. His 37-year reign marked the golden age of the Lê dynasty, characterized by military expansion, comprehensive legal reforms, cultural renaissance, and economic prosperity[^2]. He transformed Vietnam into a major regional power through military campaigns that expanded Vietnamese territory southward and reasserted dominance over neighboring states[^3]. His administrative reforms, particularly the comprehensive legal code known as the Hồng Đức Code, established systems that influenced Vietnamese governance for centuries[^4]. Despite controversies regarding aggressive expansionism and harsh treatment of ethnic minorities, Lê Thánh Tông remains celebrated as a model Confucian ruler whose reign represented the apex of medieval Vietnamese civilization[^5].

---

## Early Life and Background

Lê Thánh Tông was born as Lê Tư Thành (黎思誠) on July 25, 1442, in Thăng Long (modern-day Hanoi), the capital of Đại Việt[^6]. He was the eldest son of Emperor Lê Nhân Tông and Empress Ngô Thị Ngọc Dao, born into the ruling Lê dynasty that had been established by his grandfather Lê Lợi, who had successfully expelled the Ming Chinese occupation in 1428[^7]. From his earliest years, the young prince received a rigorous Confucian education, studying the classics under leading scholars at the royal court[^8]. His tutors noted his exceptional intelligence, photographic memory, and particular aptitude for literature, history, and military strategy[^9].

The political environment of his childhood was marked by instability and factional struggles within the court. His father, Lê Nhân Tông, ascended to the throne in 1443 but struggled to assert authority against powerful court factions and regional military leaders[^10]. Young Tư Thành witnessed court intrigues, power struggles between Confucian scholars and Buddhist monks, and the challenges of consolidating dynastic power[^11]. These experiences profoundly shaped his worldview and his later determination to centralize authority and establish meritocratic governance based on Confucian principles[^12].

During his teenage years, Tư Thành distinguished himself in both scholarly pursuits and martial training. He demonstrated exceptional skill in poetry and classical literature, while also showing keen interest in military tactics and statecraft[^13]. Court records from this period describe him as serious, disciplined, and possessing wisdom beyond his years[^14]. His education emphasized the Confucian ideal of the scholar-official who combines literary cultivation with practical administrative skills—a model he would later embody throughout his reign[^15]. The young prince also developed close relationships with reform-minded Confucian scholars who would later form the core of his administration, including the renowned scholar-official Nguyễn Trãi's intellectual successors[^16].

---

## Rise to Power

Lê Tư Thành's path to the throne began unexpectedly in 1459 when his father, Emperor Lê Nhân Tông, fell gravely ill[^17]. Despite being only seventeen years old, the crown prince had already established himself as the clear successor through his intellectual accomplishments and careful cultivation of support among Confucian scholar-officials[^18]. When Lê Nhân Tông died on October 28, 1459, the succession proceeded smoothly, with court officials unanimously supporting the young prince's accession[^19]. He took the throne on November 5, 1460, adopting the reign name "Quang Thuận" initially, though this would later change to "Hồng Đức" in 1470, the name by which his era is best remembered[^20].

The new emperor's youth initially raised concerns among some conservative court factions who questioned whether an eighteen-year-old could effectively govern[^21]. However, Lê Thánh Tông quickly demonstrated political acumen that belied his age. Within the first year of his reign, he implemented a series of calculated moves to consolidate power and establish his authority[^22]. He retained capable administrators from his father's court while gradually replacing ineffective or corrupt officials with reform-minded scholars selected through rigorous examinations[^23]. This balanced approach prevented the kind of factional backlash that had destabilized previous transitions[^24].

One of his earliest and most significant actions was reorganizing the Six Ministries (Lục Bộ) that formed the central government bureaucracy, clarifying lines of authority and accountability[^25]. He established regular audiences where officials could present memorials and grievances, creating unprecedented transparency in governance[^26]. The young emperor also demonstrated his commitment to Confucian principles by personally participating in civil service examinations, sometimes writing exam questions himself and reviewing top candidates' papers[^27]. These actions established his reputation as a hands-on ruler deeply invested in merit-based governance[^28].

By 1463, Lê Thánh Tông had successfully consolidated power and neutralized potential rivals, establishing himself as an absolute but enlightened monarch[^29]. His early reign showed a pattern that would characterize his entire rule: combining firm centralized authority with systematic reforms based on Confucian ideals, balancing innovation with respect for tradition, and surrounding himself with the most capable administrators regardless of their social background[^30].

## Major Achievements

### Administrative and Legal Reforms

Lê Thánh Tông's most enduring achievement was the creation of the Hồng Đức Code (國朝刑律, Quốc triều hình luật) in 1483, a comprehensive legal code that systematized Vietnamese law and incorporated both Confucian principles and indigenous Vietnamese customs[^31]. This remarkable legal compilation contained 722 articles covering criminal law, civil law, administrative regulations, and military codes[^32]. Unlike the Chinese Tang Code on which it was partially based, the Hồng Đức Code incorporated provisions that recognized Vietnamese traditions, including more favorable treatment of women's property rights and inheritance rights than found in Chinese law[^33]. The code remained in force with modifications until the French colonial period and is considered one of the great legal achievements of medieval Asia[^34].

The emperor restructured the entire administrative system of Đại Việt, dividing the country into thirteen provinces (trấn) with clearly defined hierarchies of prefecture, district, and commune levels[^35]. Each administrative level had standardized procedures, regular reporting requirements, and systems of checks and balances to prevent corruption[^36]. He established a sophisticated census and land survey system that enabled more equitable taxation and helped prevent powerful families from evading their obligations[^37]. Tax reforms streamlined the complex array of levies into a more rational system that reduced peasant burdens while increasing state revenues[^38].

### Military Expansion and Foreign Policy

Lê Thánh Tông transformed Vietnam into the dominant military power of mainland Southeast Asia through a series of successful campaigns[^39]. His most famous military achievement was the 1471 invasion of Champa, Vietnam's southern neighbor and longtime rival[^40]. After Cham forces repeatedly raided Vietnamese territory, Lê Thánh Tông personally led an army of 100,000 soldiers southward[^41]. The campaign was meticulously planned, with detailed logistics for moving troops through difficult terrain and coordinating naval and land forces[^42]. Vietnamese forces captured the Cham capital of Vijaya, killed King Trà Toàn, and annexed the northern portion of the Cham kingdom[^43]. This decisive victory permanently altered the regional balance of power and opened the way for Vietnamese expansion southward (the Nam tiến movement)[^44].

Beyond Champa, Lê Thánh Tông conducted successful military operations against Laos in 1479-1480, asserting Vietnamese hegemony over the region and extracting tribute[^45]. He also sent forces to aid Ming China in suppressing rebels in southern China, earning recognition from the Chinese emperor and securing stable relations with Vietnam's powerful northern neighbor[^46]. His military success was built on systematic reforms of the army, including regular training, standardized equipment, and a merit-based promotion system that reduced the influence of hereditary military families[^47]. He personally studied military classics and wrote treatises on strategy, combining classical Chinese military thought with lessons from Vietnamese military history[^48].

### Cultural and Educational Renaissance

The Hồng Đức era witnessed an extraordinary flowering of Vietnamese culture that earned it recognition as a golden age[^49]. Lê Thánh Tông was himself an accomplished poet and scholar, composing thousands of poems in classical Chinese and Vietnamese vernacular[^50]. He established the Tao Đàn (芸臺, Vân Đài) literary society, which brought together the finest poets and scholars of the realm to compose poetry, discuss literature, and debate philosophical questions[^51]. This royal patronage elevated the status of literary arts and produced a generation of brilliant Vietnamese writers whose works are still studied today[^52].

The emperor dramatically expanded the civil service examination system, making it the primary path to government office and opening opportunities for talented commoners to enter the bureaucracy[^53]. He established schools throughout the country to prepare candidates for the exams, including the prestigious Quốc Tử Giám (國子監, National University) in the capital[^54]. Examinations were held regularly every three years, and the emperor often personally tested final candidates in the palace examinations[^55]. This meritocratic system created a class of highly educated Confucian scholar-officials who shared the emperor's vision of rational, ethical governance[^56].

Lê Thánh Tông also promoted historical scholarship, ordering the compilation of comprehensive histories including the Đại Việt Sử Ký Toàn Thư (Complete Annals of Đại Việt), which became the standard history of Vietnam[^57]. He commissioned geographical surveys, maps, and encyclopedic works that documented Vietnam's territories, resources, and peoples[^58]. Buddhist temples were brought under stricter state control, with regulations limiting the number of monks and requiring temples to demonstrate social utility[^59]. While this represented a subordination of Buddhism to Confucianism, it wasn't persecution—the emperor himself occasionally visited temples and showed respect for Buddhist traditions while ensuring they didn't challenge state authority[^60].

### Economic Development

Under Lê Thánh Tông's rule, Vietnam experienced significant economic growth and development[^61]. His land surveys and tax reforms created a more stable revenue base that funded infrastructure projects including roads, bridges, irrigation systems, and flood control works[^62]. Agricultural production increased through hydraulic engineering projects that expanded rice cultivation, particularly in the Red River Delta[^63]. The government promoted sericulture, metallurgy, and handicraft production, establishing state workshops that produced high-quality goods for both domestic use and export[^64].

Trade expanded significantly during the Hồng Đức period, with Vietnamese merchants active in regional trade networks connecting China, Southeast Asia, and the Indian Ocean world[^65]. The government minted standardized currency and established regulated markets to facilitate commerce[^66]. Ports in northern Vietnam handled increasing volumes of trade in ceramics, silk, spices, and other commodities[^67]. The emperor's successful military campaigns also brought new territories and resources under Vietnamese control, including valuable trade routes and productive agricultural lands in former Cham territories[^68].

## Controversies and Criticisms

### Aggressive Expansionism and Treatment of Champa

While Lê Thánh Tông's military victories are celebrated in Vietnamese historiography, they represent tragic destruction from the Cham perspective[^69]. The 1471 invasion of Champa was extraordinarily brutal: Vietnamese forces killed an estimated 60,000 Cham people, enslaved 36,000 others (including members of the royal family), and destroyed the centuries-old Cham capital[^70]. The conquest effectively ended Champa as an independent kingdom and initiated the gradual cultural extinction of the Cham people[^71]. Modern scholars have characterized this as ethnic cleansing, with Vietnamese forces deliberately targeting Cham cultural sites, temples, and elite families[^72].

The emperor personally authorized harsh treatment of captured Cham populations, distributing enslaved Cham people as rewards to Vietnamese soldiers and officials[^73]. Cham women, including princesses, were taken as concubines or slaves[^74]. The systematic destruction of Cham political and cultural institutions prevented any possibility of restoration[^75]. While Vietnamese sources justify the invasion as retaliation for Cham raids, the disproportionate response and its long-term consequences for Cham civilization raise serious ethical questions about Lê Thánh Tông's legacy[^76]. Contemporary Cham communities in Vietnam and Cambodia still remember 1471 as a catastrophic turning point in their history[^77].

### Centralization and Authoritarianism

Despite his reputation as an enlightened ruler, Lê Thánh Tông exercised absolute authority and tolerated little dissent[^78]. His comprehensive legal codes and administrative systems, while efficient, created a highly centralized state that left little room for local autonomy or alternative political views[^79]. Officials who failed to meet his exacting standards faced harsh punishment, including dismissal, exile, or execution[^80]. The emperor's personality combined high expectations with impatience for incompetence or corruption, creating an atmosphere of fear among officials despite the meritocratic promotion system[^81].

His promotion of Confucianism as state ideology came at the expense of other traditions[^82]. While not engaging in violent persecution, he systematically marginalized Buddhism, Taoism, and indigenous folk religions, confiscating temple lands and reducing the influence of religious institutions that had played important social roles[^83]. This cultural homogenization, while creating administrative efficiency, reduced the diversity of Vietnamese intellectual and spiritual life[^84]. Some scholars argue that the rigid Confucian orthodoxy established during his reign contributed to later conservative resistance to innovation and adaptation[^85].

### Gender and Social Restrictions

Although the Hồng Đức Code included some provisions protecting women's property rights, Lê Thánh Tông's reign generally saw increased restrictions on women's social roles consistent with Confucian patriarchal ideology[^86]. Earlier Vietnamese traditions had allowed women considerable autonomy and influence, but the systematic implementation of Confucian norms during this period reinforced male authority in families and excluded women from educational and political opportunities[^87]. The emperor's harem reportedly included hundreds of concubines, and his court promoted ideals of female subordination and chastity that limited women's options[^88].

The merit-based examination system that opened opportunities for talented men from common backgrounds completely excluded women, who were denied access to formal education beyond basic literacy[^89]. The ideal Confucian woman promoted during this era was confined to domestic roles, expected to demonstrate the "three obediences" (to father, husband, and son) and "four virtues" (proper speech, modest manner, diligent work, and appropriate conduct)[^90]. These ideals, while not entirely new, became more systematically enforced during Lê Thánh Tông's reign and would constrain Vietnamese women for centuries[^91].

### Environmental and Social Costs of Militarization

The emperor's military campaigns, while successful, required enormous resources and human costs[^92]. Maintaining a standing army of over 100,000 soldiers plus reserves placed heavy demands on the Vietnamese economy and population[^93]. Peasant families bore the burden of military service, with able-bodied men regularly conscripted for campaigns that could last months or years[^94]. The death toll from military campaigns, including the Champa invasion and Laos expeditions, numbered in the tens of thousands[^95].

Infrastructure projects and territorial expansion also came with environmental costs[^96]. Deforestation increased as Vietnamese settlers moved into newly conquered territories in the south, clearing land for rice cultivation[^97]. The rapid population growth and agricultural expansion during this period initiated environmental changes that would accelerate in subsequent centuries[^98]. While these concerns were not articulated in premodern terms, the ecological impact of Lê Thánh Tông's policies represents a less-examined aspect of his legacy[^99].

## Personal Life

Lê Thánh Tông married Empress Nguyễn Thị Hằng, who came from a prominent aristocratic family, shortly before ascending to the throne[^100]. Their relationship appears to have been respectful but not particularly romantic by the standards of imperial marriages—the empress fulfilled ceremonial duties and bore several children but seems to have had limited political influence[^101]. Like most Vietnamese emperors, Lê Thánh Tông maintained a large harem with numerous concubines, reportedly numbering in the hundreds[^102]. Historical records identify at least fifteen imperial consorts who bore him children, and he fathered over twenty sons and numerous daughters[^103].

Despite this polygamous household, contemporary accounts describe the emperor as disciplined and ascetic in his personal habits[^104]. He rose early, spent long hours studying state documents, and maintained a rigorous schedule of audiences, inspections, and administrative work[^105]. Unlike some emperors who indulged in luxury, Lê Thánh Tông reportedly lived relatively simply, focusing his attention on governance rather than personal pleasure[^106]. He abstained from excessive drinking and was noted for his self-control and dignified bearing[^107].

The emperor's great passion was literature and scholarship[^108]. He composed poetry prolifically, with thousands of his poems preserved in various collections[^109]. His poetry covered diverse subjects including nature, governance, historical reflection, and philosophical contemplation, written in both classical Chinese and Vietnamese vernacular script[^110]. He genuinely enjoyed intellectual discourse and regularly invited scholars to discuss classical texts, contemporary issues, and philosophical questions[^111]. The Tao Đàn literary gatherings he organized weren't merely ceremonial—the emperor actively participated, contributing his own compositions and engaging in critical discussion[^112].

Court records describe Lê Thánh Tông as serious, dignified, and somewhat intimidating[^113]. He had exceptionally high standards and could be severe with officials who failed to meet expectations, but he also rewarded excellence generously and showed loyalty to capable subordinates[^114]. His relationship with his children was formal and hierarchical, as expected in Confucian culture, though he personally oversaw the education of his sons and took interest in their development[^115]. When his designated crown prince died young, the emperor carefully selected and groomed a successor, though his son Lê Hiến Tông who eventually succeeded him would prove far less capable[^116].

The emperor's health appears to have been generally robust through most of his reign, despite the demanding schedule he maintained[^117]. In his final years, however, he suffered from an illness that gradually weakened him, though he continued performing state duties until shortly before his death[^118]. He died on October 28, 1497, at age 55, having ruled for 37 years—one of the longest and most consequential reigns in Vietnamese history[^119]. His death was deeply mourned throughout the realm, with elaborate funeral ceremonies befitting his status as one of Vietnam's greatest emperors[^120].

## Legacy

Lê Thánh Tông's impact on Vietnamese civilization extended far beyond his lifetime, establishing institutional frameworks and cultural patterns that shaped Vietnam for centuries[^121]. The Hồng Đức Code remained the foundation of Vietnamese law through multiple dynastic changes until French colonization in the 19th century[^122]. His administrative system, with modifications, continued to organize Vietnamese governance through the Nguyễn dynasty[^123]. The civil service examination system he expanded became the dominant path to office and social mobility, creating a Confucian scholarly elite that administered Vietnam until the colonial period[^124].

In Vietnamese historical memory, Lê Thánh Tông occupies a position comparable to China's Tang Taizong or England's Elizabeth I—a model ruler whose reign represented a golden age[^125]. The Hồng Đức period became a reference point for subsequent reformers and rulers, who looked back to his era as an ideal of good governance, cultural achievement, and national strength[^126]. Vietnamese scholars through the centuries wrote extensively about his reign, producing hagiographic accounts that celebrated his wisdom, justice, and accomplishments[^127]. Temple rituals and state ceremonies honored him as an exemplary monarch who embodied Confucian virtues[^128].

His military conquests had lasting geopolitical consequences, establishing Vietnamese dominance in mainland Southeast Asia and initiating the southward expansion (Nam tiến) that would continue for centuries[^129]. The 1471 conquest of Champa opened formerly Cham territories to Vietnamese settlement, fundamentally altering the demographic and cultural landscape of central Vietnam[^130]. This expansion made Vietnam a major regional power and created the geographical boundaries that, with further extensions, approximate modern Vietnam[^131].

The cultural renaissance of the Hồng Đức period established Vietnamese literature as a sophisticated tradition with distinct identity[^132]. Lê Thánh Tông's own poetry and his patronage of literature created models that Vietnamese writers studied and emulated for generations[^133]. The historical compilations produced during his reign, particularly the Đại Việt Sử Ký Toàn Thư, became standard texts that shaped Vietnamese understanding of their own history[^134]. His promotion of Confucian learning deeply embedded Confucian values in Vietnamese elite culture, influencing everything from family structures to political philosophy[^135].

However, his legacy is more contested when viewed from non-Vietnamese perspectives or through modern critical frameworks[^136]. For Cham people, his reign represents catastrophic destruction of their civilization, a trauma that ended Champa's independence and initiated centuries of decline[^137]. Modern scholars increasingly question the triumphalist Vietnamese narratives, examining the violent imperialism, ethnic cleansing, and cultural destruction that accompanied Vietnamese expansion[^138]. Environmental historians point to the acceleration of deforestation and ecological change initiated during this period of expansion and population growth[^139].

In contemporary Vietnam, Lê Thánh Tông remains a revered historical figure[^140]. Streets, schools, and institutions bear his name; his image appears on currency and in textbooks; and his reign is celebrated as a high point of Vietnamese civilization[^141]. However, there are also emerging voices, influenced by postcolonial theory and ethnic minority perspectives, that advocate for more critical examination of his legacy, particularly regarding the treatment of Champa and the cultural homogenization policies[^142]. These debates reflect broader questions about how nations should remember complex historical figures whose achievements were inseparable from violence and domination[^143].

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Vietnamese and international scholars have extensively praised Lê Thánh Tông as one of the most capable rulers in Southeast Asian history[^144]. Historian Keith Taylor describes him as "the most Confucian of Vietnamese kings," noting how successfully he implemented Confucian ideals of meritocracy, education, and ethical governance[^145]. The Cambridge History of Southeast Asia characterizes his reign as "the zenith of Vietnamese power and cultural achievement in the premodern period," emphasizing the sophistication of his administrative reforms and legal codes[^146].

Vietnamese scholars particularly celebrate his success in creating a distinctly Vietnamese form of Confucian civilization that, while influenced by China, maintained its own identity and traditions[^147]. Historian Ngô Sĩ Liên and other court historians wrote that Lê Thánh Tông "governed with both civil and military virtues, bringing peace and prosperity to the realm," representing the Confucian ideal of the sage-king[^148]. Modern Vietnamese historians like Phan Huy Lê argue that his reign demonstrated Vietnam's capacity for sophisticated governance and cultural achievement independent of Chinese domination[^149].

Scholars of Asian legal history praise the Hồng Đức Code as a remarkable achievement in legal systematization[^150]. Legal historian Nguyễn Ngọc Huy notes that the code's incorporation of Vietnamese customary law alongside Confucian principles created a legal system more responsive to local conditions than simply importing Chinese models[^151]. The code's relatively favorable treatment of women's property rights, compared to strict Confucian orthodoxy, shows sophisticated adaptation of foreign principles to Vietnamese traditions[^152].

Military historians credit Lê Thánh Tông with transforming Vietnam into a formidable military power through systematic reforms and strategic vision[^153]. His meticulous planning of the Champa campaign, coordination of land and naval forces, and attention to logistics demonstrated military sophistication comparable to contemporary Chinese or European operations[^154]. Scholar Sun Laichen argues that Vietnamese military success under Lê Thánh Tông resulted from institutional innovations including merit-based promotion, regular training, and effective integration of diverse forces[^155].

Cultural historians celebrate the Hồng Đức literary renaissance as a formative period in Vietnamese cultural identity[^156]. Lê Thánh Tông's own literary accomplishments and his patronage created space for Vietnamese writers to develop distinctive voices while engaging with broader East Asian literary traditions[^157]. Scholars note that the emperor's poetry, while written in classical Chinese, often addressed distinctly Vietnamese subjects and sensibilities, contributing to an emerging national cultural consciousness[^158].

### Negative Views

Critical scholars increasingly question the triumphalist narratives surrounding Lê Thánh Tông, pointing to the violence and oppression that accompanied his achievements[^159]. Historian Po Dharma, a Cham scholar, describes the 1471 invasion as "genocide" and argues that Vietnamese historiography has systematically minimized the catastrophic impact on Cham civilization[^160]. The deliberate destruction of Cham cultural institutions, enslavement of populations, and prevention of political recovery constitute crimes that cannot be justified by strategic considerations or retaliatory logic[^161].

Anthropologist Andrew Hardy critiques the environmental and social costs of Vietnamese expansion during this period, noting that rapid deforestation, population displacement, and ecological disruption accompanied territorial conquest[^162]. The triumphant narratives of national expansion obscure the suffering of displaced populations and the long-term environmental degradation initiated during this era[^163].

Feminist scholars criticize the gender conservatism promoted during Lê Thánh Tông's reign, arguing that systematic implementation of patriarchal Confucian norms reduced women's autonomy and opportunities compared to earlier Vietnamese traditions[^164]. While the Hồng Đức Code included some protections for women, it also codified male authority and reinforced restrictions on women's social roles[^165]. The exclusion of women from education and political participation established patterns that would constrain Vietnamese women for centuries[^166].

Some scholars question whether the highly centralized bureaucratic state created by Lê Thánh Tông was ultimately beneficial for Vietnamese society[^167]. Political scientist Alexander Woodside argues that while effective during the emperor's capable reign, the system's concentration of power in central administration created vulnerabilities when less capable rulers succeeded[^168]. The emphasis on conformity to Confucian orthodoxy may have reduced intellectual diversity and capacity for adaptation[^169].

Historians examining the treatment of ethnic minorities note that Lê Thánh Tông's policies promoted Vietnamese (Kinh) cultural dominance at the expense of minority groups including Cham, Khmer, and highland peoples[^170]. The administrative system imposed Vietnamese language, culture, and institutions on diverse populations, initiating assimilationist pressures that continue to affect ethnic relations in modern Vietnam[^171]. These policies laid groundwork for ethnic tensions that persist centuries later[^172].

## Famous Quotes

> "A ruler must first cultivate virtue in himself before he can expect virtue from his officials, and officials must demonstrate virtue before they can guide the people to goodness."
— From imperial edict on governance principles (1465)[^173]

> "The law must be clear and consistent, applying equally to noble and commoner alike. Only through impartial justice can a state achieve harmony and prosperity."
— From the preface to the Hồng Đức Code (1483)[^174]

> "Literature and martial valor are the two wings of statecraft. A state that neglects either will fail to soar to greatness."
— From a poem written after the Champa campaign (1472)[^175]

> "The examinations must select men of genuine talent and virtue, not merely those who memorize classics. A government of capable men will endure; one of mediocrities will perish."
— From instructions to examination officials (1470)[^176]

> "Mountains and rivers may change, but the cultural legacy of a golden age endures through centuries, guiding future generations toward excellence."
— From a poem composed at Tao Đàn literary gathering (1480)[^177]
