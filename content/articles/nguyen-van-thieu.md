## Summary

South Vietnamese president who ruled during the most intense period of the Vietnam War and was accused of massive corruption. He fled to Taiwan with gold from the national treasury as Saigon fell.

## Background and Rise

Thieu served in the Army of the Republic of Vietnam (ARVN) and rose through the officer corps during the tumultuous post-colonial period. He was part of the South Vietnamese military leadership that repeatedly intervened in politics after the 1963 overthrow of Ngo Dinh Diem.

## Presidency (1967–1975)

As president, Thieu presided over a state heavily dependent on U.S. military and economic support. His administration navigated major wartime escalations, the U.S. policy shift toward “Vietnamization,” and the 1973 Paris Peace Accords, after which large-scale U.S. combat involvement ended while fighting continued.

## Corruption Allegations and Governance

Thieu’s government faced persistent allegations of patronage, graft, and enrichment by elites during wartime, issues that undermined legitimacy and complicated efforts to build durable civilian institutions. Histories of the period often discuss how corruption and factional politics weakened South Vietnam’s ability to sustain the war effort.

## Resignation and Exile

In April 1975, amid rapid North Vietnamese advances, Thieu resigned and left the country. He spent years in exile, including time in Taiwan and later the United States, and remained a controversial figure among Vietnamese communities abroad.

## Legacy

Thieu is commonly evaluated in the context of South Vietnam’s broader strategic collapse in 1975—shaped by battlefield dynamics, political fragmentation, and changing external support. His presidency remains associated with both the high-water mark of South Vietnam’s wartime mobilization and the systemic problems that contributed to its defeat.
