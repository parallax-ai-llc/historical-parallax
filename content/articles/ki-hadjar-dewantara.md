## Summary

Ki Hadjar Dewantara (born Raden Mas Soewardi Soerjaningrat, 1889-1959) was a pioneering Indonesian educator, independence activist, and the founding father of Indonesian national education.[^1] He established the Taman Siswa school system in 1922, which provided education for indigenous commoners who were previously excluded from the colonial education system.[^2] After Indonesian independence, he served as the first Minister of Education and Culture from 1945 to 1949, laying the foundations for Indonesia's national education system.[^3] Honored as a National Hero by President Sukarno in 1959, his educational philosophy emphasizing freedom, cultural values, and student-centered learning continues to influence Indonesian education today.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Raden Mas Soewardi Soerjaningrat was born on May 2, 1889, in Pakualaman, Yogyakarta, Java, in the Dutch East Indies (now Indonesia).[^5] He was born into an aristocratic Javanese family of the Yogyakarta royal household, being the son of Kanjeng Pangeran Aria Soerjaningrat, who held the title of pangeran (prince), making young Soewardi part of the Pakualaman noble lineage and a grandson of Pakualam III.[^6] This privileged background gave him access to Dutch colonial education, a rare opportunity reserved almost exclusively for the European elite and a select few from the Javanese nobility.[^7]

For his early education, Soewardi attended the Europeesche Lagere School (ELS), equivalent to an elementary school, which was part of the Dutch colonial education system designed primarily for European children and select indigenous elites.[^8] Following this, he continued his education at Kweekschool, a teacher training school, before enrolling in STOVIA (School Tot Opleiding Van Indische Artsen), a medical school established to train native Indonesian doctors.[^9] However, he did not complete his medical education due to illness, a setback that would ultimately redirect his life's trajectory toward journalism, activism, and education reform.[^10]

Despite his incomplete medical training, Soewardi was deeply influenced by his exposure to Western education and the stark inequalities he witnessed in colonial society. The Dutch education system was explicitly discriminatory, creating separate schools for Europeans, Eurasians, and a tiny fraction of privileged indigenous elites, while the vast majority of native Indonesians remained illiterate and excluded from formal education.[^11] This early exposure to educational inequality planted the seeds of his lifelong commitment to democratizing education and would later inspire him to create an alternative educational system that served all Indonesians regardless of social class or economic status.[^12]

## Rise to Power

Ki Hadjar Dewantara's rise to prominence began not through political office but through the power of the written word and his bold activism against colonial oppression. Unable to complete his medical studies, Soewardi turned to journalism, becoming a prolific writer and columnist for several prominent newspapers including Sediotomo, Midden Java, De Expres, Oetoesan Indies, Kaoem Moeda, Tjahaja Timoer, and Poesara.[^13] He also contributed to Kebangoenan, a nationalist newspaper owned by the Dutch-educated jurist and politician Phoa Liong Gie.[^14] During his journalism career, Soewardi was recognized as a talented and accomplished writer whose style was popular, communicative, and yet deeply imbued with idealism for freedom and strong anti-colonialist sentiment.[^15]

In 1912, Soewardi became increasingly involved in the nationalist independence movement, co-founding the Indische Partij (Indies Party) on December 25, 1912, alongside Ernst Douwes Dekker (a mixed-race Dutch-Javanese activist) and Tjipto Mangoenkoesoemo (a Javanese physician and nationalist).[^16] The Indische Partij was groundbreaking as the first political party in the Dutch East Indies to explicitly advocate for Indonesian nationalism and independence, using the provocative slogan "Break away from Holland."[^17] Active in the nationalist cause, Soewardi belonged to a faction favoring direct action and the use of Western methods to diminish the power of the Dutch colonial authorities.[^18] The three activists became known as the "Tiga Serangkai" or "triad," representing a powerful voice for indigenous rights and self-determination.[^19]

Soewardi's moment of national prominence came in 1913 when the Dutch colonial government announced plans to collect donations from all residents of the Dutch East Indies—including native Indonesians—to fund celebrations commemorating the centennial of Dutch independence from France in 1813.[^20] This decision ignited critical opposition from pro-independence nationalists who saw the bitter irony of colonized people being asked to finance celebrations of their colonizer's freedom.[^21] In response, Soewardi wrote his most famous article, "Als ik eens Nederlander was" ("If I Were a Dutchman"), published in De Expres newspaper on July 13, 1913.[^22] The article fiercely criticized the colonial government's hypocrisy, arguing that if he were Dutch, he would be ashamed to celebrate Dutch independence while simultaneously denying freedom to the Indonesian people.[^23]

The colonial authorities considered Soewardi's writings to be so subversive, sensitive, and divisive that they feared they might incite a popular revolt and upset the delicate social order of the Dutch East Indies.[^24] As a consequence, Governor-General Alexander Idenburg ordered Soewardi's arrest and initially sentenced him to exile on Bangka Island.[^25] However, his colleagues Douwes Dekker and Tjipto Mangoenkoesoemo protested on his behalf, and eventually, in August 1913, all three members of the Tiga Serangkai were exiled to the Netherlands instead—a somewhat more lenient punishment that would paradoxically prove transformative for Soewardi's future educational mission.[^26] At the time of his exile, Soewardi was only 24 years old, yet he had already become one of the most prominent voices in the Indonesian independence movement.[^27]

## Major Achievements

Ki Hadjar Dewantara's most significant and enduring achievement was the founding of Taman Siswa (literally "Garden of Students"), a revolutionary educational institution established on July 3, 1922, in Yogyakarta.[^28] Taman Siswa represented a radical departure from the Dutch colonial education system, which had systematically excluded the vast majority of native Indonesians from formal schooling.[^29] During the colonial period in the early 20th century, education was made possible only for the elites—the colonial Dutch population and a handful of Javanese noblemen families—while native commoners were denied access to schools.[^30] Dewantara's founding of Taman Siswa was expressly declared as "the first national education institution," a bold statement of independence and a direct challenge to colonial educational hegemony.[^31]

The Taman Siswa school system was founded on Dewantara's innovative educational philosophy known as the "Among System," which emphasized student-centered learning, freedom of thought, and the cultivation of natural talents.[^32] In practice, the Among System was founded on Taman Siswa's philosophy and belief about the development of students' knowledge and character, allowing them to be free to explore their surroundings while maintaining order and harmony.[^33] This approach marked a profound shift from the authoritarian, rote-learning methods of colonial schools. In the Taman Siswa system, teachers were not classroom rulers but "pamong" (guides or mentors), following Dewantara's famous educational maxim "Ing ngarsa sung tuladha, ing madya mangun karsa, tut wuri handayani," which translates to "(for those) in front should set an example, (for those) in the middle should raise the spirit, and (for those) behind should give encouragement."[^34]

Instruction at Taman Siswa was carried on informally and emphasized traditional skills and values of Javanese life, particularly music, dance, and other cultural arts, while Western subjects such as language, science, and economics were also taught to help students cope with the demands of modern life.[^35] This integration of Indonesian cultural education with modern academic subjects represented Dewantara's vision of an education system that was both nationalist and progressive—rooted in local wisdom and values ignored by colonial schools, yet also preparing students for contemporary challenges.[^36] The school not only taught general subjects but created a holistic learning experience that promoted children's social and cultural growth.[^37]

The growth and expansion of Taman Siswa across colonial Indonesia was remarkable. From a single small private school that "was born quietly" in Yogyakarta in 1922, Taman Siswa expanded rapidly throughout Java and eventually to other islands.[^38] Java was particularly active in establishing Taman Siswa branches, and as a result, nearly half of all Taman Siswa schools were maintained throughout the colonial period.[^39] By the 1930s and 1940s, Taman Siswa had become home for many anti-colonial and nationalist activists and artists, functioning not merely as an educational institution but as a center of Indonesian cultural and political resistance.[^40] Many Indonesian modern artists taught at Taman Siswa, including renowned painters such as Sudjojono, Basuki Resobowo, Rusli, Alibasjah, Affandi, Trubus, and Sindusisworo.[^41] As noted by scholar Claire Holt, Taman Siswa had been crucial to the development of Indonesian modern art.[^42]

After Indonesia declared independence on August 17, 1945, Ki Hadjar Dewantara was appointed Minister of Education and Culture of the Republic of Indonesia on August 19, 1945—just two days after independence.[^43] He served as the first Indonesian Minister of Education (his post was officially called the Minister of Education, Teaching, and Culture) for two periods from 1945 to 1949.[^44] During his tenure as minister, Dewantara succeeded in laying the foundations for the development of Indonesia's national education system, transforming education from a colonial instrument of social control into a tool for nation-building, cultural preservation, and democratic citizenship.[^45] His ministerial work represented the culmination of his decades-long vision to make education accessible to all Indonesians, regardless of social or economic background.[^46]

In 1956 (or 1957, according to some sources), Dewantara received an honorary doctorate (Doctor Honoris Causa) in the field of cultural science from Universitas Gadjah Mada (UGM), Indonesia's oldest university, in recognition of his lifelong dedication to advancing education and culture.[^47] The award was presented on December 19, 1956, by UGM President (Rector) Prof. Dr. Sardjito, with President Sukarno witnessing the ceremony at Siti Hinggil in the Yogyakarta Palace.[^48]

## Controversies and Criticisms

While Ki Hadjar Dewantara is overwhelmingly celebrated in Indonesian history as a national hero and the father of Indonesian education, his career was not without controversy, particularly during the colonial period. His most significant controversy came from his radical political activism and inflammatory writings that directly challenged Dutch colonial authority. His article "Als ik eens Nederlander was" ("If I Were a Dutchman"), published on July 13, 1913, was considered by colonial authorities to be so subversive, sensitive, and divisive that they feared it might incite a popular revolt and upset the delicate social order of the Dutch East Indies.[^49] The article's sharp criticism of colonial hypocrisy and its call for Indonesian self-determination represented a direct threat to Dutch rule, leading to his arrest and exile.[^50]

The colonial government's response to the Indische Partij, which Dewantara co-founded in 1912, further illustrates the controversial nature of his early political activities. The party used the provocative slogan "Break away from Holland," which the Dutch authorities interpreted as dangerously separatist and potentially seditious.[^51] The party did not receive the broad support from native Indonesians for which its founders had hoped, and the colonial authorities saw it as a sufficient threat to ban the party in 1913, shortly after its founding.[^52] This suppression demonstrated the Dutch government's determination to silence nationalist voices and maintain colonial control.

During the 1920s and 1930s, the colonial government continued to view Taman Siswa with suspicion and hostility. The colonial authorities accused the Taman Siswa movement of harboring communist sympathies, a serious charge in the context of growing anti-communist sentiment among colonial powers.[^53] A number of laws in the 1920s and 1930s sought to prevent the spread of so-called "wilde scholen" (wild schools), which the government believed spread subversive ideas that threatened colonial stability.[^54] The Wild School Ordinance of 1932 stipulated that all teachers working in unrecognized schools should register themselves with local authorities and undergo a "quality check" by an official state representative—a clear attempt to monitor and control educational institutions like Taman Siswa that operated outside colonial supervision.[^55]

However, historical and academic sources provide relatively limited documentation of specific criticisms of Dewantara's educational methods or philosophy from scholarly or contemporary perspectives. The available literature focuses primarily on his philosophy as a form of resistance and his contributions to Indonesian education, rather than offering critical analyses of potential shortcomings or limitations of his approaches.[^56] Some modern scholars have noted that Dewantara's educational philosophy, while progressive for its time, was deeply rooted in Javanese cultural traditions and may have had limitations in addressing the diverse ethnic and cultural contexts of Indonesia's many islands and ethnic groups.[^57] Additionally, his emphasis on traditional Javanese arts and culture, while valuable for cultural preservation, could be viewed as prioritizing Javanese identity over the multicultural reality of the Indonesian archipelago.

Another area of potential controversy, though not extensively documented in available sources, concerns the sustainability and scalability of the Taman Siswa model. While the school system expanded significantly during the colonial period, it relied heavily on the dedication of volunteer teachers and community support, making it difficult to maintain consistent educational standards across all branches.[^58] The informal, student-centered approach, while philosophically appealing, may have faced practical challenges in preparing students for standardized examinations or higher education in the colonial system.

## Personal Life

Ki Hadjar Dewantara married Raden Ajeng Sutartinah in 1913, the same year he was exiled to the Netherlands.[^59] Raden Ajeng Sutartinah, who later became known as Nyi Hadjar Dewantara, was born on September 14, 1890 (in the Javanese calendar year 1390), in Yogyakarta.[^60] She was the daughter of Kanjeng Pangeran Haryo Sasraningrat, who held a position in the pakualaman court, and Raden Ajeng Mudmainah.[^61] Like her husband, Sutartinah came from an aristocratic background and received education at the Europeesche Lagere School (ELS), and at home she received lessons in reading Javanese books, batik making, and household crafts—traditional accomplishments expected of women of her social class.[^62]

Sutartinah proved to be not merely a supportive spouse but an active partner in her husband's educational mission. When she accompanied Ki Hadjar Dewantara to the Netherlands during his exile from 1913 to 1918, she seized the opportunity to further her education by studying the Fröbel method (a progressive kindergarten education approach) so she could become a teacher herself.[^63] During their years in exile, Sutartinah always accompanied and assisted her husband in all his activities, especially those related to education, establishing a partnership that would continue throughout their lives.[^64]

Ki Hadjar Dewantara's nuclear family consisted of himself, Nyi Hadjar Dewantara, and six children.[^65] Two of their children—the first a daughter and the second a son—were born in the Netherlands during their parents' exile between 1913 and 1918, while four other children, including another daughter, were born in Indonesia after the family's return in 1919.[^66] Sources mention two of their children by name: Ni Sutapi Asti and Ki Subroto Haryomataram, though complete information about all six children is limited in available historical records.[^67]

The partnership between Ki Hadjar Dewantara and his wife was essential to the success of Taman Siswa and his broader educational mission. Nyi Hadjar Dewantara played an active role in the school system, particularly in early childhood education, applying the Fröbel methods she had studied in the Netherlands.[^68] Their marriage represented a progressive model for its time, with Sutartinah serving not as a subordinate or merely decorative spouse, but as an active collaborator in the educational and nationalist cause.

After Ki Hadjar Dewantara's death on April 26, 1959, his wife demonstrated her deep commitment to preserving his legacy. Nyi Sutartinah collected approximately 3,000 items that had been used by Ki Hajar and his family—including documents, personal belongings, photographs, and educational materials—and these were placed in the Dewantara Kirti Griya Museum in Yogyakarta, which serves as a memorial to Indonesia's father of education.[^69] This museum continues to educate visitors about Dewantara's life, philosophy, and contributions to Indonesian independence and education.

While detailed accounts of Dewantara's personality traits are limited in historical sources, his writings and actions suggest a person of considerable courage, idealism, and determination. At just 24 years old, he was willing to face exile rather than remain silent about colonial injustice.[^70] His decision to adopt the name "Ki Hadjar Dewantara" around 1922 (formally from 1929 when he turned 40) represented a symbolic rejection of his aristocratic title "Raden Mas" and a commitment to egalitarian principles—"Ki Hadjar" being a more common, humble form of address that emphasized his role as a teacher rather than his noble birth.[^71]

## Legacy

Ki Hadjar Dewantara's legacy in Indonesian education and national identity remains profound and enduring more than six decades after his death. His most immediate and visible legacy is the continued existence of Taman Siswa schools throughout Indonesia, which persist to this day as an alternative educational system emphasizing Indonesian cultural values and progressive pedagogy.[^72] The educational philosophy he developed—particularly the Among System and his famous maxim "Ing ngarsa sung tuladha, ing madya mangun karsa, tut wuri handayani"—continues to influence Indonesian educational policy and practice.[^73] Today, part of this maxim, "Tut Wuri Handayani," is used as the official motto of the Indonesian Ministry of Education, Culture, Research and Technology, ensuring that his educational philosophy remains at the heart of Indonesia's national education system.[^74]

In recognition of his dedication and accomplishments in pioneering public education in Indonesia, Ki Hadjar Dewantara was declared the Father of Indonesian National Education, honored as a National Hero of Indonesia, and his birthday, May 2, was appointed as National Education Day through Presidential Decree No. 305 of 1959, dated November 28, 1959.[^75] This decree was issued by Indonesia's first president, Sukarno, on November 28, 1959—notably, several months after Dewantara's death on April 26, 1959, making the honor a posthumous recognition of his immense contributions.[^76] Every year on May 2, Indonesia celebrates National Education Day (Hari Pendidikan Nasional) with ceremonies, educational programs, and reflections on the state of Indonesian education, keeping Dewantara's memory and mission alive in public consciousness.[^77]

One of the most important and lasting legacies of Ki Hajar Dewantara is the concept of inclusivity in education. Taman Siswa opened access to education for all children, regardless of social or economic background, fundamentally democratizing education in a society that had been rigidly stratified by colonial policies.[^78] This principle of educational equality became a foundational value of independent Indonesia's education system and remains an aspiration (if not always a reality) for Indonesian education policy today.[^79] His vision that "every person becomes a teacher, every house becomes a school" ("Setiap orang menjadi guru, setiap rumah menjadi sekolah") reflected a radical reconceptualization of education as a community-wide responsibility rather than a privilege of the elite.[^80]

Dewantara's legacy extends beyond formal education to Indonesian cultural and artistic life. His emphasis on traditional Javanese music, dance, and arts as integral components of education helped preserve and promote Indonesian cultural heritage during a period when colonial education actively suppressed or marginalized indigenous culture.[^81] In 1949, Ki Hadjar and teachers from Taman Siswa helped found the Indonesia Art Academy (ASRI), which later became the Indonesian Institute of the Arts (ISI), establishing institutional structures for arts education that continue to train Indonesian artists today.[^82] His influence on Indonesian modern art was so significant that scholar Claire Holt noted Taman Siswa had been crucial to the development of the Indonesian modern art movement.[^83]

Ki Hadjar Dewantara's physical likeness has been immortalized on Indonesian currency—his portrait was featured on the 20,000 rupiah banknote denomination in 1998, making his image widely recognized throughout Indonesia and symbolically affirming his status as a foundational figure in the nation's history.[^84] The Dewantara Kirti Griya Museum in Yogyakarta, established by his wife Nyi Sutartinah after his death, houses approximately 3,000 artifacts related to his life and work, serving as both a memorial and an educational resource for understanding his contributions.[^85]

In contemporary Indonesia, Ki Hadjar Dewantara's educational philosophy continues to be studied, debated, and applied. The Ki Hajar Dewantara Award has been established to recognize excellence in education and cultural preservation, continuing his mission of honoring those who advance Indonesian education.[^86] Academic research on his philosophy remains active, with scholars examining how his ideas about culture-based education, student-centered learning, and educational freedom remain relevant to contemporary challenges in Indonesian education.[^87] His legacy in education endures and remains the foundation of Indonesia's education system today, shaping not only pedagogical approaches but also the fundamental values that underpin Indonesian national education.[^88]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Scholars and educators overwhelmingly regard Ki Hadjar Dewantara as a visionary educational reformer whose ideas were far ahead of his time. Modern academic assessments emphasize that his educational philosophy remains relevant and provides direction for the transformation of education in the modern era, especially in facing the challenges of globalization and the commodification of education.[^89] His ideas, which emphasize building national character through education grounded in cultural values, are seen as particularly important in the contemporary era of globalization, when Indonesian cultural identity faces pressure from Western cultural hegemony.[^90]

Educational scholars praise Dewantara's core conception of education as a holistic process. He believed that education is not just a process of transferring knowledge but also an effort to internalize cultural values in order to shape individuals as a whole, both spiritually and mentally.[^91] This holistic vision distinguishes Dewantara's philosophy from more narrowly instrumental or vocational approaches to education that dominated colonial schooling and continue to influence contemporary education systems worldwide.[^92] The concept of the "Among System" that he introduced is particularly celebrated for emphasizing the importance of freedom of thought for children, as well as the role of educators as facilitators who guide children in developing their natural potential rather than imposing rigid curricula.[^93]

Recent philosophical scholarship has begun to position Dewantara's educational philosophy within broader traditions of educational thought. Some scholars argue that Dewantara's philosophy of education, expressed in the slogan "Ing Ngarsa Sung Tuladha, Ing Madya Mangun Karso and Tut Wuri Handayani," typifies a specific form of "conduct pragmatism," which has been deemed underdeveloped and underexplored in modern philosophy of education.[^94] This positioning suggests that Dewantara developed an original philosophical approach that deserves recognition alongside Western educational philosophers like John Dewey, Maria Montessori, and Paulo Freire.[^95]

Dewantara's integration of international progressive educational theories with Indonesian cultural values is widely admired. In conceptualizing education, Dewantara was influenced by Tagore, Montessori, and Fröbel, yet he saw the importance of imparting local wisdom and values ignored by colonial schools, creating a synthesis that was both internationally informed and locally grounded.[^96] This integration demonstrates intellectual sophistication and cultural confidence—the ability to learn from foreign ideas without losing indigenous identity.[^97] His visit with Rabindranath Tagore in 1927, when the Nobel laureate poet visited Yogyakarta and saw firsthand how his ideas were implemented in Taman Siswa, represents a significant moment of transnational educational exchange and mutual recognition between two of Asia's great educational reformers.[^98]

Educational practitioners identify three major contributions of Ki Hadjar Dewantara's educational philosophy for Indonesian education: the application of a trilogy of leadership in education (embodied in his famous maxim), the concept of three centers of education (family, school, and community), and the "paguron" system (an informal, community-based learning approach inspired by traditional Javanese educational institutions).[^99] These contributions provide practical frameworks that continue to guide Indonesian educational policy and practice, demonstrating that Dewantara's ideas were not merely theoretical or idealistic but also pragmatic and applicable.[^100]

### Negative Views

Critical scholarly perspectives on Ki Hadjar Dewantara are relatively limited in available English-language academic literature, with most assessments being overwhelmingly positive or hagiographic. This scarcity of critical analysis may reflect his status as a national hero in Indonesia, where criticism of foundational figures can be culturally sensitive, or it may indicate that his educational philosophy has proven remarkably resilient to critique.[^101] However, some implicit criticisms and limitations can be identified in the historical record and contemporary scholarly discussions.

One potential criticism concerns the scalability and sustainability of the Taman Siswa model. While the school system expanded significantly during the colonial period and continues to operate today, it faced constant financial difficulties and relied heavily on the voluntary dedication of teachers and community support.[^102] The informal, culturally grounded approach that was Taman Siswa's strength also made it difficult to standardize educational quality across different branches, potentially creating inconsistent educational outcomes.[^103] Colonial authorities' accusations that Taman Siswa schools were "wilde scholen" (wild schools) spreading subversive ideas, while politically motivated, also reflected genuine concerns about the lack of formal oversight and standardization.[^104]

The strong emphasis on Javanese cultural traditions in Taman Siswa's curriculum could be viewed as problematic in the context of Indonesia's extraordinary ethnic and linguistic diversity. Indonesia comprises hundreds of ethnic groups with distinct languages, customs, and cultural traditions, yet Dewantara's educational philosophy was primarily rooted in Javanese culture, philosophy, and aesthetics.[^105] While this was understandable given that Taman Siswa was founded in Yogyakarta and expanded primarily in Java, critics might argue that this Javanese-centric approach potentially marginalized or inadequately addressed the educational needs and cultural identities of Indonesia's many non-Javanese populations.[^106]

Some scholars have noted that while Dewantara's educational philosophy emphasized freedom and student-centered learning, the implementation of these ideals in practice may have been constrained by traditional Javanese social hierarchies and concepts of authority. The concept of "Among" and the role of teachers as guides still operated within a cultural framework that emphasized respect for elders and deference to authority, which could potentially limit the full realization of student autonomy and critical thinking that progressive education theoretically promotes.[^107]

From a gender perspective, while Dewantara's wife Nyi Sutartinah played an important role in Taman Siswa and his educational work, the available historical record provides limited evidence about how gender equality was addressed in his educational philosophy or in the Taman Siswa curriculum. Education in the early 20th century, even progressive education, often maintained traditional gender roles, and it is unclear how thoroughly Dewantara's philosophy challenged these conventions.[^108]

Finally, some contemporary education scholars have questioned whether Dewantara's philosophy, while valuable for cultural preservation and character development, adequately prepared students for economic competition and technological advancement. In an increasingly globalized economy, some critics suggest that the emphasis on traditional arts, cultural values, and informal learning may not provide students with the technical skills, scientific knowledge, and credential recognition needed to compete in modern labor markets.[^109] However, defenders of Dewantara's philosophy would argue that this criticism misses the point—that education should prioritize human development, cultural integrity, and democratic citizenship over narrow economic utility.[^110]

## Famous Quotes

> "Ing ngarsa sung tuladha, ing madya mangun karsa, tut wuri handayani" (In front, a leader must set an example; in the middle, a leader must generate enthusiasm; from behind, a leader must provide encouragement) - This is Dewantara's most famous educational maxim, which became the motto of Indonesia's Ministry of Education.[^111]

> "Every person becomes a teacher, every house becomes a school" ("Setiap orang menjadi guru, setiap rumah menjadi sekolah") - Reflecting his vision of community-wide responsibility for education.[^112]

> "There are no stupid children. There are only teachers who don't know how to teach" ("Tidak ada anak yang bodoh. Yang ada hanya guru yang tidak tahu cara mengajar") - Emphasizing that educational failures result from inadequate teaching rather than student deficiencies.[^113]

> "When asked whether Ki Hadjar is a nationalist, radical, socialist, democrat, humanist, or traditionalist, say that I am just an ordinary Indonesian who works for the Indonesian nation in the Indonesian way." - Expressing his pragmatic, non-ideological approach to Indonesian nationalism.[^114]

> "Education and teaching in the Republic of Indonesia must be based on the culture and society of the Indonesian nation, aimed at inner happiness and safety of life." - Articulating his philosophy of culture-based education.[^115]
