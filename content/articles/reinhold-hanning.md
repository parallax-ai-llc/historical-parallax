## Summary

Former Auschwitz guard convicted at age 94 of accessory to at least 170,000 murders.

## Background

- **Early life:** Grew up in the Lippe region and attended elementary school before working in a factory.
- **Entry into Nazi organizations:** Joined the Hitler Youth as a teenager and later volunteered for the Waffen-SS.

## Role at Auschwitz

Hanning served as an SS guard at **Auschwitz** (including **Auschwitz II–Birkenau**) from **January 1942 to June 1944**. Reporting and court findings described duties that included guarding the camp perimeter and escorting deportees upon arrival, in the broader machinery of mass murder carried out at the site.

## Postwar life

After the war, Hanning was held as a **prisoner of war** and released in **1948**. He later worked in civilian jobs (including driving and sales) and eventually ran his own business before retiring.

## Investigation, trial, and conviction

- **Shift in German prosecutions:** In the 2010s, German prosecutors increasingly pursued cases where former camp personnel could be convicted as **accessories** based on service at extermination sites, without proving a specific individual killing.
- **Charges and verdict:** In **2016**, a court in **Detmold** convicted Hanning as an **accessory to 170,000 murders**, tied to the period he served at Auschwitz.
- **Sentence and outcome:** He received a **five-year** sentence, but he **did not serve prison time**; he died in **2017** while appellate proceedings were ongoing.

## Notes

- Hanning’s case is often discussed alongside other late-era Holocaust prosecutions (e.g., cases involving former Auschwitz personnel), reflecting how courts assessed responsibility for participation in the camp system.
