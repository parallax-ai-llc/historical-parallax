## Summary

US detention facility at Guantanamo Bay, Cuba that has held 780 terror suspects since 2002 without trial. Detainees were subjected to enhanced interrogation techniques including waterboarding.

## Purpose and Legal Framework

- The detention site at Guantánamo Bay was established after the September 11 attacks as part of the U.S. "war on terror," with detainees held as suspected militants.
- The legal status of detainees—often labeled "enemy combatants"—became a central controversy, especially regarding access to courts and due process.

## Conditions and Interrogation Controversies

- Guantánamo has been criticized by human-rights groups and international observers for indefinite detention, treatment of detainees, and interrogation practices.
- Separate but related post-9/11 programs (including CIA sites) were associated with coercive interrogation methods; Guantánamo is frequently discussed within that broader debate.

## Court Decisions and Policy Changes

- U.S. Supreme Court decisions addressed detainee rights and the legality of certain military commissions, shaping how the U.S. government could prosecute and detain suspects.
- Multiple administrations announced plans to reduce the detainee population or close the facility, but closure has faced legal and political obstacles.

## Ongoing Relevance

- The facility remains a symbol in debates over counterterrorism policy, executive power, and compliance with international humanitarian and human-rights law.
- The slow pace of high-profile cases and the long duration of detention for many individuals continue to draw scrutiny.
