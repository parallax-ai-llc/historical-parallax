## Summary

Mexican serial killers known as Las Poquianchis who operated a brothel network in Guanajuato where they enslaved, tortured, and murdered at least 91 women and an unknown number of clients. They are Mexico's most prolific serial killers.

## Background

Delfina González and María de Jesús González are sisters who became infamous in Mexico under the nickname **“Las Poquianchis.”** Accounts describe them as operating in the state of Guanajuato and controlling a network of brothels during the mid-20th century.

## Crimes and Victims

Open-source summaries typically describe a pattern that combined human exploitation and violence:

- Recruitment of young women—often from vulnerable circumstances—followed by coercion and confinement.
- Physical abuse and deprivation used to control victims.
- Deaths resulting from violence, neglect, and mistreatment, with uncertainty around the full victim count.

Reported numbers vary by source and by what authorities could verify at the time, which is common in cases involving hidden victims and incomplete records.

## Arrest and Investigation

The case came to police attention after an **escaped victim** reported what was happening. Subsequent investigation led to arrests and the discovery of evidence that supported charges of kidnapping, abuse, and homicide.

## Trial and Sentencing

The sisters were tried and convicted in the 1960s, receiving long prison sentences. The legal outcomes are frequently cited as a landmark case in Mexican criminal history, both because of the alleged scale of the crimes and because of the public attention the trial drew.

## Public and Cultural Impact

- The case entered Mexican true-crime history and has been referenced in books, journalism, and dramatizations.
- It is also discussed in broader conversations about trafficking, exploitation, and the vulnerability of marginalized women.
