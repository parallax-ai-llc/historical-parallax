## Summary

Sadi Carnot is considered the father of thermodynamics. He described the Carnot cycle and established that the efficiency of heat engines is limited by temperature differences.

## Core scientific contribution

Carnot’s 1824 work, *Reflections on the Motive Power of Fire*, is foundational because it asked a practical engineering question—how much useful work can be extracted from heat?—and answered it with a general principle that does not depend on the details of a particular machine.

He introduced the idealized reversible engine cycle (now called the Carnot cycle) to show that the maximum possible efficiency of a heat engine depends only on the temperatures of the heat reservoirs. This insight later became formalized as the Carnot theorem in thermodynamics.

## Why the Carnot cycle matters (high-level)

- It provides an upper bound: no real engine operating between two temperatures can be more efficient than a reversible one.
- It separates “engineering details” from “physical limits,” clarifying what improvements are possible.
- It helped shape later concepts such as entropy and the second law, even though the modern formulation came after Carnot.

## Historical impact

Carnot’s early death limited his direct output, but the conceptual framework he introduced influenced later scientists who developed thermodynamics in its modern form. His work is frequently cited in physics and engineering education as the cleanest route to understanding why heat engines face unavoidable constraints.

## Key concepts associated with Carnot

- **Reversibility:** an ideal limit used to define maximum efficiency.
- **Temperature reservoirs:** hot and cold bodies between which heat flows.
- **Efficiency bound:** depends on temperature difference, not engine design.
