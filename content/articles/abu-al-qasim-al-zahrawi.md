## Summary

Abu al-Qasim al-Zahrawi, known as Albucasis in the West, was the greatest medieval surgeon and is considered the father of surgery. His encyclopedic medical work Al-Tasrif, containing 30 volumes, described over 200 surgical instruments he designed. His texts were used as standard references in European medical schools for five centuries.

## Contributions to Surgery and Medicine

Al-Zahrawi’s *Al-Tasrif* is notable not only for its breadth but for its practical orientation: it described procedures, instruments, and case-based guidance for working physicians. The surgical volume includes detailed illustrations of instruments, helping standardize techniques across generations.

Key topics associated with his writings include:

- Hemostasis and wound management (including cauterization techniques common to the era).
- Obstetrics and gynecology, with descriptions of childbirth-related instruments.
- Dental and oral procedures, reflecting a holistic view of surgical practice.
- The use of ligatures and sutures, and the systematic discussion of post-operative care.

## Transmission and Influence

Latin translations of *Al-Tasrif* circulated widely in medieval and early modern Europe and were cited by later surgical authors. His reputation as “Albucasis” in Western texts reflects how Andalusian medical scholarship became integrated into European curricula through translation movements.

## Legacy

Modern histories of medicine often treat al-Zahrawi as a pivotal figure in the shift toward instrument-based, procedural surgery documented in a reproducible way, bridging Greco-Roman traditions and later European surgical practice.
