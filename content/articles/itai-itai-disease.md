## Summary

Mitsui Mining's cadmium pollution of the Jinzu River caused itai-itai disease affecting thousands. The first pollution disease officially recognized by Japan; Mitsui was found liable.

## Background and Causes

- The disease name ("itai-itai", meaning "it hurts—it hurts") reflects severe bone and joint pain reported by patients.
- The best-known outbreak occurred in Toyama Prefecture along the Jinzu River basin, where contaminated irrigation water affected rice-growing communities.
- Cadmium is a toxic heavy metal; chronic exposure is strongly associated with kidney tubular dysfunction, which in turn disrupts calcium and phosphate balance.

## Health Effects (High-Level)

Commonly reported features in public health summaries include:

- Progressive kidney damage (proximal tubule impairment).
- Bone demineralization (osteomalacia/osteoporosis-like changes) leading to fractures.
- Higher vulnerability among populations with long-term dietary exposure and other risk factors.

## Accountability, Litigation, and Policy

- The itai-itai case is frequently cited in Japan's postwar environmental history as an early, high-visibility example linking industrial emissions to downstream community health impacts.
- Court findings and subsequent settlements are often discussed as part of a broader shift toward stricter pollution control, corporate liability, and victim compensation frameworks.
- The case is also used in environmental governance literature to illustrate the importance of monitoring, disclosure, and independent epidemiological investigation.

## Legacy

- Itai-itai disease is commonly grouped with other major Japanese pollution-related public health crises of the mid-20th century.
- The incident remains a reference point in discussions about heavy-metal contamination, mining externalities, and long-tail health costs.
