## Summary

US soldiers killed approximately 300 South Korean refugees at No Gun Ri, believing they might include North Korean infiltrators. The US acknowledged the incident in 2001.

## Background

- The incident occurred during the early weeks of the Korean War (1950–1953), amid widespread fear of North Korean infiltration among fleeing civilians.
- No Gun Ri is a hamlet near Yeongdong County, North Chungcheong Province, South Korea.

## What happened (high-level)

- In late July 1950, groups of South Korean refugees were gathered and moved south by US forces in the area.
- Refugees were subsequently fired upon near a railroad underpass/bridge area at No Gun Ri; estimates of deaths vary across sources and investigations.
- Later summaries often describe the death toll as on the order of a few hundred civilians.

## Aftermath and investigations

- The event remained relatively obscure in international discourse for decades.
- In 1999, an Associated Press (AP) investigative report brought renewed attention to the massacre and prompted official inquiries.
- The US Army conducted an investigation and released its findings in 2001; the US government acknowledged that civilians were killed and expressed regret, while contesting some characterizations (including intent and casualty totals).
- South Korean investigations and survivor accounts continued to play a central role in documenting the incident and pressing for recognition.

## Legacy

- No Gun Ri is widely cited in discussions of civilian harm during the Korean War and the challenges of distinguishing combatants from non-combatants in fluid front lines.
- The incident has influenced scholarship and public debate about rules of engagement, refugee control policies, and wartime accountability.

## Notes on casualty figures

- Reported casualty numbers differ by source (survivor testimony, journalistic investigations, government reports, and later historical work).
- When citing figures, it is common to specify the source and whether the number represents confirmed deaths, estimated deaths, or combined killed-and-wounded.
