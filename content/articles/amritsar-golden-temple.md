## Summary

Harmandir Sahib, popularly known as the Golden Temple, is the holiest site in Sikhism and the spiritual and cultural center of the Sikh religion worldwide. Located in the city of Amritsar in the Punjab state of India, the temple is the seat of the Sri Guru Granth Sahib — the eternal Sikh scripture that is considered the living embodiment of the ten historical Sikh Gurus.

The temple's formal name, Harmandir Sahib, means "Abode of God" in Punjabi. It sits in the center of a sacred pool (sarovar) called Amrit Sarovar — "Pool of the Nectar of Immortality" — from which the city of Amritsar takes its name. The golden exterior of the upper floors, covered in gold leaf donated by Maharaja Ranjit Singh in the early 19th century, gives the temple its popular English name and creates one of the most spectacular religious views in the world, particularly at dawn and dusk when the gilded structure reflects in the still waters of the pool.

The Golden Temple practices the principle of equality that is central to Sikhism. Its four entrances, facing all four cardinal directions, symbolize openness to people of all backgrounds, castes, religions, and nationalities. The langar (free community kitchen) attached to the temple complex serves free meals to all visitors — approximately 100,000 people daily, rising to 150,000 or more on special occasions — making it one of the largest free kitchens in the world.

## History & Origins

The city of Amritsar was founded by Guru Ram Das, the fourth Sikh Guru, who established the settlement around 1574 CE and began excavating the sacred pool. The word "Amritsar" literally means "Pool of the Nectar of Immortality." Guru Ram Das initiated the project of creating a major center for the Sikh community, and his successor Guru Arjan Dev (the fifth Guru) continued the work.

Guru Arjan Dev completed the construction of the Harmandir Sahib around 1604 CE. He designed the temple with a distinctive approach that reflected Sikh theology: whereas Hindu temples typically had a single entrance and an elevated position symbolizing the difficulty of reaching the divine, Guru Arjan Dev built his temple lower than the surrounding ground level, accessible by descending into it — symbolizing humility — with four entrances in all four directions, symbolizing that people of all backgrounds and from all directions were welcome.

Guru Arjan Dev himself installed the Adi Granth — the first compilation of Sikh scripture — in the newly completed temple in 1604, with Bhai Gurdas as the scribe and Baba Buddha Ji as the first reader. This original installation established the principle of the Guru Granth Sahib as the central sacred presence in the temple, a role it continues to occupy.

The history of the Golden Temple is marked by several periods of destruction and rebuilding. After Guru Arjan Dev's martyrdom in 1606 under the Mughal Emperor Jahangir, and throughout the 17th century as the Sikh community faced repeated persecution by Mughal authorities, the temple and its surrounding city were attacked multiple times. The Afghan invader Ahmad Shah Durrani attacked Amritsar twice in the 1750s and 1760s, desecrating and blowing up the Harmandir Sahib in 1762.

The Sikh community, then organized into the Sikh confederacy (the Sikh Misl), rebuilt the temple after each destruction. The definitive reconstruction occurred under Maharaja Ranjit Singh, who unified the Sikh states and established the Sikh Empire (1799–1849). Ranjit Singh not only rebuilt the temple but contributed gold leaf to cover the upper portions of the structure — the gold plating that gives it its current appearance. He also installed elaborate marble inlay work, with craftsmen brought from across India using the pietra dura technique. The gilding required approximately 750 kilograms of gold.

The Sikh Empire maintained control of the temple until the British annexation of the Punjab in 1849, after which control passed to committees under British oversight. After Indian independence in 1947, the temple came under the management of the Shiromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee (SGPC), established in 1920 as the apex body managing Sikh gurdwaras (temples) in the Punjab.

## Religious Significance

Harmandir Sahib is the most sacred of all gurdwaras in the Sikh faith. Its sanctity derives from several sources: its founding by the Guru himself, its role as the first home of the Guru Granth Sahib, and the centuries of reverence accumulated through countless acts of prayer, service, and sacrifice at the site.

The Guru Granth Sahib is installed in the inner sanctum of the Harmandir Sahib each morning in a ritual called Prakash — "Manifestation of Light" — and is carried in procession from the Akal Takht (the main Sikh seat of temporal authority, located across the sarovar) to the temple. In the evening, the ceremony is reversed and the scripture is processed back to the Akal Takht for the night (Sukhasan — "Resting"). Continuous recitation of the Guru Granth Sahib (akhand path) proceeds throughout the day, transmitted by loudspeakers across the complex.

The Sikh concept of darshan — "sacred seeing" — applies to the Harmandir Sahib: the act of seeing the temple, and particularly of seeing the Guru Granth Sahib within it, is itself a spiritually meritorious act. Pilgrims often make their first sight of the temple at dawn a moment of profound religious experience.

The sarovar (sacred pool) surrounding the temple is considered spiritually purifying. Bathing in the Amrit Sarovar is practiced by devout Sikhs, who believe the waters carry the spiritual energy of the Gurus and can heal the body and spirit.

## Architecture & Description

The Harmandir Sahib is a two-story structure sitting on a 67-square-meter platform in the center of the Amrit Sarovar pool. It is connected to the surrounding marble causeway (Parikrama) by a narrow walkway approximately 60 meters long, called the Guru's Bridge (Har ki Pauri or the main causeway). Pilgrims process along this causeway, often in long queues, to enter the temple.

The lower half of the exterior is white marble, inlaid with elaborate pietra dura work — floral patterns, birds, and geometric designs in semi-precious stones including lapis lazuli, carnelian, and jade — in a style influenced by Mughal architecture. The upper portions of the temple are covered in gold leaf, applied over copper, contributing to the gilded appearance that gave the temple its popular name. The total gold used in the plating is approximately 750 kilograms, donated by Maharaja Ranjit Singh.

The main dome of the Harmandir Sahib is an inverted lotus-shaped gilded dome. This form — a lotus inverted, symbolizing the descent of the divine into the world — is distinctive in Sikh sacred architecture. Four smaller domes flank the main structure at its corners.

The interior of the temple contains the sanctum where the Guru Granth Sahib is installed. The walls are decorated with gold and floral paintings. The atmosphere is one of constant devotional activity: the Guru Granth Sahib is recited, musicians (ragis) perform kirtan (devotional music from the scripture) throughout the day, and worshippers sit in reverent attendance.

The Parikrama — the marble walkway surrounding the entire pool — is approximately 150 meters square and is the primary processional route for pilgrims who circumambulate the temple. Along the Parikrama are numerous shrines, commemorative plaques, and small gurdwaras associated with Sikh history. The Akal Takht ("Eternal Throne") — the primary temporal seat of Sikh authority — is located at the western end of the Parikrama, facing the Harmandir Sahib.

## Pilgrimage & Rituals

The Golden Temple draws approximately 100,000 visitors daily under normal circumstances, rising dramatically during major Sikh festivals. It is the most-visited gurdwara in the world and one of the most-visited religious sites in India.

Pilgrims typically arrive early in the morning, often before dawn, to witness the Prakash ceremony — the bringing of the Guru Granth Sahib from the Akal Takht to the Harmandir Sahib in a ceremonial procession accompanied by kirtan and thousands of devotees. The sight of the golden temple reflected in the early morning water, with the processional lights moving across the causeway, is one of the most moving experiences available to any religious pilgrim.

Upon entering the complex, Sikh pilgrims follow a prescribed etiquette: they wash their feet in a small pool at the entrance, cover their heads, and remove their shoes. They then proceed along the Parikrama, enter the Harmandir Sahib, make their obeisance before the Guru Granth Sahib, receive the sacred sweetened pudding (karah parshad), and are free to sit in the presence of the scripture for as long as they wish.

The langar — the free community kitchen — is central to the Golden Temple experience. Operating 24 hours a day, 365 days a year, the langar prepares and serves meals to all visitors regardless of religion, caste, or nationality. The meal is simple vegetarian food: dal (lentils), sabzi (vegetables), chapati, and kheer (rice pudding). Eating together in the langar is itself a spiritual act, breaking down the social hierarchies that Sikhism explicitly rejects.

Major festivals at the Golden Temple include Gurpurab (commemorating the birth or death anniversaries of the Gurus), Baisakhi (the harvest festival marking the founding of the Khalsa by Guru Gobind Singh in 1699), and Diwali (which at Amritsar commemorates the release of the sixth Guru, Hargobind, from Mughal imprisonment).

## Modern Significance

The Golden Temple is not only a religious site but a political one. The Akal Takht, located within the complex, is the highest seat of temporal Sikh authority and issues edicts (hukamnamas) on matters of Sikh conduct and community affairs. The relationship between the Akal Takht, the SGPC, and the Indian government has been a source of ongoing political tension.

The most traumatic modern event at the Golden Temple was Operation Blue Star in June 1984, when the Indian Army, ordered by Prime Minister Indira Gandhi, stormed the temple complex to dislodge Sikh militants led by Jarnail Singh Bhindranwale who had occupied it. The military operation resulted in significant damage to the Akal Takht and other structures within the complex, and hundreds of people were killed, including many civilians and pilgrims who were trapped inside. The operation was deeply traumatic for the Sikh community worldwide and had far-reaching political consequences: Indira Gandhi was assassinated by her Sikh bodyguards two months later, on October 31, 1984, triggering anti-Sikh pogroms across India in which thousands of Sikhs were killed.

The complex has since been fully restored, and the Akal Takht was rebuilt. The events of 1984 remain a deeply sensitive subject within the Sikh community and in Indian politics.

## Timeline

- **1574 CE**: Guru Ram Das, the fourth Sikh Guru, founds Amritsar and begins excavating the sacred pool.
- **1589 CE**: Guru Arjan Dev begins construction of the Harmandir Sahib.
- **1604 CE**: The Harmandir Sahib is completed; the Adi Granth is installed.
- **1606 CE**: Guru Arjan Dev is martyred by the Mughal Emperor Jahangir.
- **1762 CE**: Ahmad Shah Durrani destroys the temple; it is subsequently rebuilt.
- **1802–1839 CE**: Maharaja Ranjit Singh's reign; he gold-plates the temple dome and adds marble inlay work.
- **1849 CE**: British annexation of the Punjab; the temple comes under British-supervised management.
- **1920 CE**: The Shiromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee (SGPC) is established.
- **1947 CE**: Indian independence; the partition of Punjab displaces millions and reshapes the region.
- **June 1984 CE**: Operation Blue Star — Indian Army storms the Golden Temple complex.
- **October 1984 CE**: Indira Gandhi is assassinated; anti-Sikh pogroms follow.
- **Present**: The Golden Temple continues as the holiest Sikh pilgrimage site, receiving millions of visitors annually.
