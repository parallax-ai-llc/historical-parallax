## Summary

South African police shot dead 34 striking platinum miners at Lonmin's Marikana mine. It was the deadliest use of force by South African police since the end of apartheid.

## Background

The Marikana Massacre occurred during a period of heightened labor conflict in South Africa’s platinum belt. Workers at **Lonmin’s Marikana mine** were engaged in a strike tied to wages, working conditions, and representation disputes.

Labor dynamics at the site were also shaped by rivalry and tensions between unions (often summarized as **NUM vs AMCU** in broad accounts), contributing to an unstable atmosphere.

## Events (High-Level)

- In the days leading up to 16 August 2012, the strike environment was marked by clashes and deaths involving multiple parties.
- On 16 August, a group of striking miners gathered at a rocky outcrop (“the koppie”).
- South African police operations escalated, and police opened fire, killing **34 miners** in the main incident, with additional casualties reported across the broader timeline of the strike.

## Why It Was Historically Significant

- It became the **deadliest police use of force** in South Africa since the transition away from apartheid.
- The event raised international attention to inequality, labor precarity, and the post-apartheid state’s relationship to extractive industry.

## Investigations and Accountability

The government established the **Farlam Commission of Inquiry** to investigate what happened, including the roles of police leadership, mine management, and other actors.

Public debate focused on questions such as:

- Whether crowd-control planning and command decisions were appropriate.
- The extent to which negotiations and de-escalation were pursued.
- The broader structural pressures on miners and their communities.

## Aftermath

- **Political consequences:** The massacre became a touchstone issue in South African politics and civil society, influencing debate around policing and labor rights.
- **Legal and financial outcomes:** Survivors and families pursued compensation and legal remedies in the years that followed.
- **Industry impact:** It intensified scrutiny of labor practices and social conditions in the mining sector.

## Legacy

Marikana is often referenced as a symbol of unresolved economic injustice in post‑apartheid South Africa and as a case study in the risks of militarized policing during industrial disputes.
