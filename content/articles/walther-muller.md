## Early life and training

Walther Müller (often written with the umlaut as Müller) was a German physicist associated with experimental instrumentation in nuclear and particle physics. He worked in the period when radiation research was rapidly expanding and dependable detectors were essential for both laboratory research and applied measurement.

## Geiger–Müller counter

The Geiger–Müller counter is a type of gas-filled radiation detector that produces a detectable electrical pulse when ionizing radiation passes through the tube. The instrument became widely used because it is relatively robust and can detect a range of ionizing radiation with appropriate configurations.

High-level characteristics of the Geiger–Müller counter include:

- A Geiger–Müller tube with electrodes in a gas-filled chamber.
- A high-voltage supply to create conditions for a discharge when ionization occurs.
- Electronics that count pulses, enabling a simple measure of activity over time.

## Scientific and practical impact

The Geiger–Müller counter became one of the most recognizable radiation detection tools. It supported:

- Laboratory measurements in nuclear and particle physics.
- Radiation safety monitoring in medical, industrial, and research settings.
- Field measurements where simpler, rugged detection equipment was valuable.

## Legacy

Müller's contribution is primarily remembered through the lasting adoption of the Geiger–Müller counter as a standard detection and educational instrument, and as a stepping stone toward more specialized detectors used in later decades.
