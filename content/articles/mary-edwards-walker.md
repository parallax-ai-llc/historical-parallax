## Summary

Mary Edwards Walker was an American physician, surgeon, and women's rights advocate who became the first and, to date, only woman to receive the Medal of Honor, the United States military's highest decoration. Born in 1832 in Oswego County, New York, she defied the social and professional barriers of her era to earn a medical degree and serve on the front lines of the Civil War. Her life embodied a radical commitment to individual liberty, from her refusal to wear conventional women's clothing to her tireless advocacy for suffrage and dress reform.

Walker's significance extends beyond medicine into the broader history of American civil rights and gender equality. She challenged institutional sexism at every turn — fighting for official military appointment, practicing medicine without institutional support, and publicly adopting men's dress as a philosophical statement. She remains a singular figure whose legacy was formally recognized when the Army reinstated her Medal of Honor in 1977, decades after it was controversially rescinded.

## Early Life & Background

Mary Edwards Walker was born on November 26, 1832, in Oswego County, New York, the youngest of five daughters of Alvah and Vesta Walker. Her parents were progressive reformers who encouraged their daughters' education and physical freedom, notably rejecting corsets and restrictive clothing for their children. Her father, a self-taught physician, introduced her to medicine from an early age.

Walker attended the Falley Seminary in Fulton, New York, before enrolling at the Syracuse Medical College, from which she graduated in 1855 — one of very few women to earn a medical degree in the antebellum United States. She married fellow physician Albert Miller the same year, notably refusing to include the word "obey" in her vows and retaining her surname. The marriage eventually ended in divorce in 1869.

## Rise to Prominence

At the outbreak of the Civil War in 1861, Walker traveled to Washington, D.C., to offer her services to the Union Army. Despite her credentials, the Army refused to commission her as a medical officer, relegating her initially to volunteer work. She served at the Patent Office Hospital in Washington and later at the Battle of Chickamauga in 1863. Her persistence was ultimately rewarded when she was appointed as a civilian contract surgeon in 1863 — the first woman to hold such a position in the U.S. Army, assigned to the 52nd Ohio Infantry.

In April 1864, she was captured by Confederate forces while crossing enemy lines to treat civilians and was held as a prisoner of war in Richmond, Virginia, for four months before being exchanged for a Confederate officer.

## Key Achievements

On January 24, 1866, President Andrew Johnson awarded Walker the Medal of Honor for her wartime service. She was the first woman to receive the decoration. In 1917, a review board rescinded her medal along with hundreds of others on the grounds that she had been a civilian contractor rather than a combat soldier. Walker refused to return it and wore it daily until her death. In 1977, President Jimmy Carter restored the honor posthumously.

Beyond the military, Walker was a prolific lecturer and activist. She advocated for women's suffrage, dress reform, and temperance, and wrote two books: *Hit* (1871) and *Unmasked, or the Science of Immorality* (1878).

## Controversies & Criticism

Walker's adoption of men's trousers and frock coats was both a philosophical stance and a source of public ridicule throughout her life. She was arrested multiple times for masquerading as a man. Her advocacy positions, particularly later in life, were considered extreme even by progressive standards — she opposed the mainstream suffrage movement's strategy at points and held unconventional views on marriage and social organization that alienated potential allies.

The 1917 revocation of her Medal of Honor reflected institutional resistance to acknowledging a woman's wartime contributions on equal terms with male soldiers.

## Legacy & Influence

Walker died on February 21, 1919, in Oswego, New York, just months before the 19th Amendment granted women the right to vote — a cause she had championed for decades. The restoration of her Medal of Honor in 1977 confirmed her place in American military and feminist history. The U.S. Postal Service issued a commemorative stamp in her honor in 1982. She is memorialized in Oswego, where a historical marker and a monument recognize her contributions. Her life has become a touchstone for discussions of gender, military service, and the limits of institutional recognition.
