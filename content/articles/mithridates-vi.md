## Additional Context

- **Realm and geography:** Mithridates VI ruled the Kingdom of Pontus on the southern coast of the Black Sea, and sought influence across Anatolia, the Bosporan Kingdom (Crimea), and parts of the Caucasus.
- **Political positioning:** He cultivated an anti-Roman image and presented himself as a champion of Greek cities in Asia Minor against Roman taxation and client-king politics.
- **Multilingual court:** Ancient sources commonly describe him as unusually capable with languages, useful for governing a multi-ethnic set of territories and allies.

## The Asiatic Vespers (massacre) — -88

- The coordinated killings of Romans and Italians in Asia Minor are often referred to as the **Asiatic Vespers**.
- Ancient figures vary widely; modern summaries frequently present a range (tens of thousands), and the exact total is uncertain, but the event is consistently treated as a major escalation that made compromise with Rome far harder.

## Mithridatic Wars (high-level overview)

- **First Mithridatic War (-89 to -85):** After conflict spread into Roman Asia and Greece, Rome sent Lucius Cornelius **Sulla**, who campaigned in Greece and then negotiated terms that preserved Mithridates’ core kingdom but imposed indemnities and concessions.
- **Second Mithridatic War (-83 to -81):** A shorter renewal of hostilities, often framed as a Roman-initiated dispute in Asia Minor rather than a full strategic war.
- **Third Mithridatic War (-73 to -63):** Fought first against Roman commander **Lucullus** and later against **Pompey**; the war ended with Mithridates’ defeat and the reorganization of territories into Roman client arrangements and provinces.

## Downfall and Death

- After losing key positions and allies, Mithridates retreated toward the Black Sea and attempted to rebuild power through the Bosporan realm.
- He faced internal revolts and the defection of family members; his final collapse is commonly tied to rebellion led by his son **Pharnaces II**.
- Accounts of his death emphasize a failed attempt at poisoning (often linked to earlier self-immunization stories), followed by being killed by a guard at his request; details vary by source tradition.

## Legacy

- **Roman expansion:** His wars became a major driver of Rome’s long-term consolidation in Anatolia and the eastern Mediterranean.
- **Propaganda and memory:** Later Roman and Greek narratives alternately depict him as a brutal tyrant, a capable strategist, or a symbol of resistance to Roman dominance.
- **“Mithridatism” (contested):** The idea that he practiced gradual dosing to build immunity to poisons is famous, but the historical specifics are debated; it is better treated as a later tradition attached to his reputation.
