## Summary

Masayoshi Son is a Japanese technology entrepreneur, investor, and billionaire founder and CEO of SoftBank Group Corp., one of the world's most influential technology conglomerates. Born to a Korean-Japanese family in 1957, Son built his fortune through early investments in companies like Yahoo! and Alibaba, transforming SoftBank from a software distributor into a global investment powerhouse. Known for his audacious vision and high-risk investment strategy, particularly through the $100 billion Vision Fund, Son has been both celebrated as a visionary technology investor and criticized for devastating losses from failed bets like WeWork. His aggressive dealmaking and futuristic predictions have made him one of the most polarizing figures in global finance and technology.

---

## Early Life and Background

Masayoshi Son was born on August 11, 1957, in Tosu, Saga Prefecture, Japan, to Korean-Japanese parents who were second and third-generation ethnic Koreans (Zainichi Koreans).[^1] His family lived in poverty, with his father raising pigs and brewing illegal moonshine to support the family.[^2] Son grew up facing discrimination due to his Korean heritage, which was common for Zainichi Koreans in post-war Japan. The family lived in a shack with a dirt floor, and Son has described his childhood as one of significant hardship.[^3]

Despite these challenges, Son showed exceptional academic promise from an early age. He adopted the Japanese surname "Son" (his original family name was Yasumoto), and was inspired by his entrepreneurial father and grandfather to pursue business.[^4] At age 16, Son moved to the United States to attend high school in California, where he learned English and experienced American culture. He briefly attended Holy Names University in Oakland before transferring to the University of California, Berkeley, where he studied economics and computer science.[^5]

While at Berkeley, Son demonstrated his entrepreneurial instincts by inventing a multilingual pocket translator that he sold to Sharp Corporation for approximately $1 million—a fortune that would seed his future business ventures.[^6] He also imported Space Invader arcade games from Japan to supplement his income. Son graduated from Berkeley in 1980 with a BA in Economics, having spent just three years in the program.[^7] His experiences with discrimination in Japan and his exposure to American entrepreneurial culture would profoundly shape his business philosophy and global ambitions.

## Rise to Power

After returning to Japan in 1981, the 24-year-old Son founded SoftBank (originally named "Nihon SoftBank") with just two part-time employees and an initial investment of about ¥1 million (approximately $4,000 at the time).[^8] The company initially focused on distributing computer software for personal computers, acting as a middleman between software developers and retailers during Japan's PC boom.[^9]

Son's breakthrough came through his strategic partnerships with American technology companies. He convinced major publishers to allow SoftBank to distribute their software in Japan, and the company grew rapidly through the 1980s.[^10] In 1994, SoftBank went public on the Tokyo Stock Exchange, raising significant capital that Son would use for his ambitious expansion plans.[^11]

The key turning point in Son's career came with his early internet investments in the mid-1990s. In 1995, he invested $100 million in Yahoo! when it was still a startup, eventually earning returns estimated at over $100 billion on paper before the dot-com crash.[^12] This investment established Son's reputation as a visionary who could identify transformative technologies before others. He also made strategic investments in numerous other internet companies including E*Trade, Buy.com, and Webvan.[^13]

However, Son's most legendary investment came in 2000 when he met Jack Ma, the founder of a then-unknown Chinese startup called Alibaba. After a six-minute conversation in Son's office, Son agreed to invest $20 million in Alibaba—an investment that would eventually be worth over $150 billion at its peak, making it one of the most profitable venture capital investments in history.[^14] This single decision transformed SoftBank from a Japanese telecommunications and internet company into a global technology investment powerhouse.

The early 2000s saw Son expand SoftBank's operations beyond software distribution into telecommunications, acquiring Japan Telecom in 2004 and Vodafone Japan in 2006 for approximately $15 billion, creating what would become SoftBank Mobile.[^15] He also acquired Sprint Corporation in the United States in 2013 for $21.6 billion, attempting to break into the American telecommunications market, though this investment would prove problematic.[^16]

## Major Achievements

Masayoshi Son's greatest achievement is arguably the creation of the SoftBank Vision Fund in 2017, the world's largest technology-focused venture capital fund with an initial capitalization of $100 billion.[^17] The fund represented an unprecedented concentration of capital aimed at investing in artificial intelligence, robotics, and other emerging technologies. Son raised money from sovereign wealth funds including Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund ($45 billion) and Abu Dhabi's Mubadala Investment Company ($15 billion), along with major corporations like Apple.[^18]

The Vision Fund strategy was built on Son's philosophy of the "singularity"—his belief that artificial intelligence would transform every industry within 30 years, creating massive economic value.[^19] Under this framework, the fund invested in dozens of companies including Uber, WeWork, DoorDash, Slack, Nvidia (before its AI boom), and many others, often writing checks worth hundreds of millions or billions of dollars.[^20] Son's approach was to identify market leaders in emerging sectors and provide them with capital advantages that would help them dominate their markets.

Beyond the Vision Fund, Son's investment in Alibaba remains one of the most successful venture capital bets in history. His initial $20 million investment in 2000 grew to a stake worth over $150 billion at its peak, though it has since declined due to Chinese regulatory crackdowns.[^21] SoftBank's 25% stake in Alibaba has been called "the crown jewel" of the company's portfolio and has repeatedly rescued SoftBank from financial difficulties.[^22]

Son also successfully built SoftBank Mobile into Japan's third-largest mobile carrier, breaking the duopoly of NTT DoCoMo and KDDI.[^23] He was an early adopter of offering the iPhone in Japan in 2008, when the device was still relatively unknown in the country, helping to drive the smartphone revolution in Japan.[^24] His willingness to take risks on unproven technologies and business models has been a consistent theme throughout his career.

In the robotics field, SoftBank acquired Boston Dynamics from Google in 2017 and has invested heavily in humanoid robotics, including the development of Pepper, a social humanoid robot designed for customer service applications.[^25] While these ventures have not yet proven profitable, they demonstrate Son's commitment to his vision of an AI-driven future.

Son's influence extends beyond his investments. He has been a prominent advocate for renewable energy in Japan, founding SoftBank Energy in 2011 after the Fukushima nuclear disaster and pushing for greater solar power adoption.[^26] He has also been involved in telecommunications policy, advocating for spectrum reform and competition in Japan's telecom market.[^27]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Masayoshi Son's reputation has been severely damaged by a series of catastrophic investment failures, most notably the WeWork debacle. In 2017 and 2019, the Vision Fund invested approximately $10.5 billion in WeWork, a co-working space company led by controversial CEO Adam Neumann.[^28] Son personally championed Neumann and pushed for aggressive expansion, but the company's attempted IPO in 2019 collapsed spectacularly when investors rejected its business model and governance practices.[^29] SoftBank was forced to bail out WeWork with an additional $10 billion rescue package, and the investment lost billions of dollars, becoming one of the worst venture capital investments in history.[^30]

The Vision Fund's overall performance has been heavily criticized. While some investments like DoorDash and Coupang have been successful, many others have failed or significantly underperformed. Companies like Brandless, Zume Pizza, Fair, and Katerra all collapsed or faced severe difficulties.[^31] The fund's strategy of flooding companies with capital and pushing for rapid growth at any cost has been blamed for creating unsustainable business models and contributing to a broader pattern of venture capital excess.[^32]

Son's investment in Sprint, the American mobile carrier acquired for $21.6 billion in 2013, is widely considered a failure. Sprint continued to lose market share and struggled to compete with larger rivals Verizon and AT&T.[^33] SoftBank eventually merged Sprint with T-Mobile in 2020, with SoftBank shareholders receiving only a minority stake in the combined company—a significant loss compared to the initial investment.[^34]

Critics have accused Son of having a "god complex" and making reckless, emotion-driven investment decisions based on brief meetings and personal relationships rather than rigorous analysis.[^35] His famous six-minute meeting with Jack Ma before investing in Alibaba, while ultimately successful, exemplifies a decision-making style that has led to numerous failures. Former associates have described a culture where dissent was not tolerated and where Son's instincts were treated as infallible.[^36]

The Vision Fund's relationship with Saudi Arabia's crown prince Mohammed bin Salman has also attracted controversy, particularly after the murder of journalist Jamal Khashoggi in 2018.[^37] Critics argued that SoftBank's continued acceptance of Saudi capital represented tacit support for the kingdom's human rights abuses. Son faced pressure from investors and activists to distance the company from Saudi Arabia, though the relationship has continued.[^38]

Son's management style has also been criticized. Multiple reports have described a chaotic decision-making process, with Son personally dominating investment decisions and overruling his team.[^39] The rapid turnover of senior executives at SoftBank and the Vision Fund has been noted, with critics suggesting that Son's autocratic leadership style drives away talented professionals.[^40]

In recent years, SoftBank has faced severe financial pressure. In 2020, the company reported a record $13 billion annual loss, largely due to Vision Fund write-downs.[^41] Son was forced to sell over $40 billion in assets, including large portions of Alibaba and T-Mobile stock, to pay down debt and fund share buybacks.[^42] The company's debt levels have remained a concern for credit rating agencies and investors.[^43]

## Personal Life

Masayoshi Son is married to Masami Ohno, whom he met while both were students at the University of California, Berkeley. They married in 1979 and have two daughters.[^44] Son has maintained a relatively private family life despite his public business persona. His wife has largely stayed out of the public eye, and Son rarely discusses his family in interviews.[^45]

Despite his enormous wealth (estimated at around $20-30 billion, though it fluctuates significantly with SoftBank's stock price), Son is not known for extravagant personal spending compared to other billionaires.[^46] He lives in a relatively modest home by billionaire standards and does not own private jets or yachts, preferring to focus his resources on business investments.[^47]

Son is known for his intense work ethic and ambitious vision. He famously created a "300-year business plan" for SoftBank, outlining his vision for the company's evolution over centuries.[^48] This long-term thinking reflects his belief that he is building something that will outlast him and transform human civilization. He has stated that his goal is not merely to accumulate wealth but to contribute to technological progress and human advancement.[^49]

Health issues have occasionally affected Son's business activities. In 2016, he was hospitalized for exhaustion, highlighting the intense pressure and workload he maintains.[^50] He has also been open about experiencing depression following the dot-com crash in 2000, when SoftBank's value plummeted and he lost approximately $70 billion of paper wealth in a matter of months.[^51]

Son's Korean heritage remains an important part of his identity. He has spoken openly about the discrimination he faced growing up as a Zainichi Korean in Japan and has advocated for better treatment of ethnic minorities.[^52] In 1990, he became a naturalized Japanese citizen while maintaining pride in his Korean ancestry.[^53] He has made significant investments in South Korea and maintains connections to the country of his family's origin.

Son is known for his charismatic and persuasive personality. Business partners and employees describe him as someone who can inspire tremendous loyalty and enthusiasm, painting vivid pictures of the future that convince others to take enormous risks.[^54] This same charisma, however, has been criticized for leading to poor decisions when his enthusiasm overrides careful analysis.[^55]

## Legacy

Masayoshi Son's legacy remains deeply contested and will likely be debated for decades. On one hand, he has been one of the most successful technology investors in history, with his Alibaba investment alone generating returns that few investors could ever match. His vision of mobile internet, AI-driven transformation, and technological convergence has proven largely correct, even if his execution has been flawed.[^56]

SoftBank's influence on the global technology industry is undeniable. The Vision Fund fundamentally changed venture capital by introducing unprecedented amounts of capital and pushing valuations to new heights. Whether this was positive or negative remains debatable—supporters argue it accelerated innovation and allowed startups to pursue ambitious visions, while critics contend it created unsustainable business models and inflated bubbles.[^57]

Son's impact on Japanese business culture has been significant. In a country known for conservative, consensus-driven corporate management, Son represented a radically different model: aggressive, risk-taking, globally oriented, and led by a strong individual vision.[^58] He helped internationalize Japanese business and demonstrated that Japanese companies could compete at the highest levels of global technology and finance. His success as a Zainichi Korean also challenged ethnic barriers in Japanese society.[^59]

However, the devastating losses from WeWork, Uber's underperformance, and numerous failed Vision Fund investments have tarnished Son's reputation considerably. Many analysts now view the Vision Fund era as a cautionary tale about the dangers of excessive capital, founder worship, and bubble dynamics in technology investing.[^60] The fund's poor overall returns (particularly from Vision Fund 1) have raised questions about whether Son's success was more luck than skill, particularly given how dependent SoftBank's overall success has been on the singular Alibaba bet.[^61]

The long-term viability of SoftBank itself remains uncertain. The company carries significant debt, and its stock has traded at a persistent discount to the value of its holdings, reflecting investor skepticism about Son's management and strategy.[^62] Whether Son can successfully transition SoftBank to his envisioned role as an AI investment powerhouse, or whether the company will ultimately be broken up and its parts sold off, remains to be seen.[^63]

Son's influence on the next generation of entrepreneurs and investors is complex. Many founders admire his boldness and vision, seeing him as someone willing to bet on the future when others were too cautious. His willingness to invest billions in unproven companies gave many entrepreneurs opportunities they would never have had otherwise.[^64] However, the subsequent failures have led to more skepticism about the "growth at all costs" model that Son championed.[^65]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Supporters of Masayoshi Son view him as a generational visionary who understood technology trends before almost anyone else. His early recognition of the internet's importance in the 1990s, mobile computing in the 2000s, and artificial intelligence in the 2010s demonstrated genuine foresight.[^66] Academics and business historians point to the Alibaba investment as evidence that Son could identify transformative companies at early stages—the $20 million investment in 2000 ultimately generated returns of over 7,500x at peak, an almost unprecedented achievement in venture capital.[^67]

Admirers emphasize Son's role in democratizing technology in Japan. By offering the iPhone when other carriers were skeptical, and by building competitive pressure in Japan's telecommunications market, he helped bring smartphones and affordable mobile data to millions of Japanese consumers.[^68] His investments in Indian companies like Paytm and Flipkart helped build India's startup ecosystem, and his support for Chinese entrepreneurs like Jack Ma contributed to China's technological rise.[^69]

Son's defenders argue that his failures should be viewed in the context of his overall track record. Venture capital inherently involves many failures, and even the best investors lose money on most of their bets—what matters is the overall portfolio return.[^70] By this measure, the Alibaba investment alone has generated enough profit to offset many failures. Supporters also note that some investments criticized as failures (like Uber) may yet prove successful in the long term as the companies mature.[^71]

Business scholars appreciate Son's boldness in challenging established industries and norms. His willingness to make contrarian bets and pursue ambitious visions, even in the face of skepticism, represents a valuable entrepreneurial spirit.[^72] His 300-year vision for SoftBank, while perhaps grandiose, reflects a long-term orientation that contrasts with the short-termism often criticized in modern capitalism.[^73]

Some analysts view the Vision Fund's difficulties as primarily a timing issue rather than a fundamental flaw in strategy. The fund invested heavily in 2017-2019, at the peak of a bull market, and then faced the COVID-19 pandemic and subsequent tech downturn.[^74] Given more favorable market conditions, many of the investments might have performed better. Supporters also credit Son with successfully navigating the 2020 crisis by rapidly selling assets and stabilizing SoftBank's balance sheet.[^75]

### Negative Views

Critics of Masayoshi Son portray him as a reckless gambler whose successes have been primarily due to luck rather than skill. They point out that his overall investment record, when examined holistically, shows many more failures than successes.[^76] The dot-com crash wiped out most of his late-1990s investments, Sprint was a disaster, and the Vision Fund has destroyed billions in investor capital.[^77] Critics argue that focusing on Alibaba obscures a pattern of poor decision-making.

Financial analysts have harshly criticized Son's approach to due diligence and risk management. The WeWork investment, in particular, revealed serious failures in governance and oversight—Son personally pushed for investment in a company with obvious red flags, including an erratic CEO, unsustainable unit economics, and questionable corporate governance.[^78] Critics suggest this reflects a systematic problem with Son's decision-making process, where intuition and personal relationships override careful analysis.[^79]

The Vision Fund's strategy has been accused of distorting markets and enabling irresponsible behavior by founders. By flooding companies with capital and pushing them to grow at any cost, the fund encouraged unsustainable business models that prioritized growth metrics over profitability or sound business fundamentals.[^80] Academic researchers have suggested that the Vision Fund contributed to broader dysfunction in venture capital during the late 2010s, inflating valuations and encouraging wasteful spending.[^81]

Son's management of SoftBank has been criticized for creating excessive risk through leverage and concentration. The company has repeatedly taken on large debts to fund aggressive acquisitions, leaving it vulnerable to market downturns.[^82] The heavy concentration of value in the Alibaba stake means that SoftBank's fate is largely tied to a single asset, and Son's repeated selling of Alibaba shares to fund other ventures has been questioned.[^83]

Critics also point to a pattern of Son abandoning or de-emphasizing initiatives when they prove difficult. After the Sprint failure, he merged it away rather than fixing it. After robotics investments failed to generate returns, he sold Boston Dynamics.[^84] This suggests a lack of operational discipline and follow-through, with Son preferring to chase new visions rather than executing on existing commitments.[^85]

The ethical dimensions of Son's partnerships have also attracted criticism. Beyond the Saudi Arabia relationship, critics note investments in companies with questionable business models or social impacts, suggesting that Son's focus on returns overrides ethical considerations.[^86] The labor practices of gig economy companies funded by the Vision Fund, like Uber and DoorDash, have been particularly controversial.[^87]
