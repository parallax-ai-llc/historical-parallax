## Summary

Mstislav Leopoldovich Rostropovich (1927–2007) was a Soviet-born Russian cellist and conductor widely regarded as the greatest cellist of the twentieth century. His technical mastery, emotional depth, and extraordinary musicality redefined what was possible on the instrument, inspiring dozens of major composers to write works specifically for him. Over a career spanning six decades, he performed with virtually every leading orchestra and conductor in the world.

Beyond his musical achievements, Rostropovich was a towering figure of moral courage. His outspoken support for dissident writer Alexander Solzhenitsyn in 1970 led to years of Soviet persecution and ultimately forced exile. He became as celebrated for his human rights advocacy as for his artistry, embodying the idea that the artist has a responsibility to speak truth to power.

---

## Early Life & Background

Rostropovich was born on March 27, 1927, in Baku, Azerbaijan, then part of the Soviet Union, into a deeply musical family. His father, Leopold Rostropovich, was a professional cellist and his first teacher; his mother was a pianist. The family relocated to Moscow, where Mstislav entered the Moscow Conservatory at age 16, studying cello with Semyon Kozolupov and composition with Dmitri Shostakovich and Sergei Prokofiev — relationships that would profoundly shape his artistic identity. He graduated in 1948 and won the International Competition for Young Musicians in Prague that same year.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Rostropovich's international reputation grew rapidly through the 1950s and 1960s. His profound partnerships with Shostakovich and Prokofiev yielded landmark premieres: Prokofiev's Symphony-Concerto, Op. 125 (1952) and Shostakovich's Cello Concerto No. 1 (1959) were both dedicated to and premiered by him. Benjamin Britten, who became a close friend, wrote his Cello Symphony (1963) and three cello suites for him. In total, more than 60 major works were composed for Rostropovich, an almost unparalleled distinction in the instrument's history.

His career as a conductor developed in parallel. He made his conducting debut in 1962 and served as music director of the National Symphony Orchestra in Washington, D.C., from 1977 to 1994, transforming it into an ensemble of international standing.

---

## Key Achievements

- Premiered over 60 works written expressly for him, including concertos by Shostakovich, Prokofiev, Britten, Dutilleux, and Lutosławski.
- Served as music director of the National Symphony Orchestra (Washington, D.C.) for 17 years (1977–1994).
- Won four Grammy Awards and received the Lenin Prize (1964) before his Soviet citizenship was revoked.
- Awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom (1992) and the Legion of Honour by France.
- On November 11, 1989, flew spontaneously to Berlin and performed an impromptu cello recital at the fall of the Berlin Wall.

---

## Controversies & Criticism

In 1970, Rostropovich wrote an open letter defending Solzhenitsyn's right to publish, directly challenging Soviet censorship. Soviet authorities responded by banning him from performing abroad and restricting his domestic engagements. In 1974, he and his wife, soprano Galina Vishnevskaya, were permitted to leave the USSR; in 1978, their Soviet citizenship was revoked. Though some contemporaries felt he could have been more cautious, the dominant view in retrospect is that his stand was principled and costly. His citizenship was formally restored by Mikhail Gorbachev in 1990.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Rostropovich permanently expanded the cello repertoire and elevated the instrument's prestige in the concert hall. His recordings — particularly of the Bach Cello Suites, the Dvorák Concerto, and the complete Shostakovich and Prokofiev concertos — remain reference standards. The Rostropovich International Cello Competition, held in Paris, continues to identify and launch elite young cellists. He died in Moscow on April 27, 2007, at age 80, mourned as both a supreme musician and a symbol of artistic freedom.
