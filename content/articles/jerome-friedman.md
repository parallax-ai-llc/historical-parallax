## Summary

Jerome Friedman shared the 1990 Nobel Prize for experiments proving quarks exist inside protons.

## Scientific contribution (high level)

Friedman is best known for work on deep inelastic scattering, a class of experiments in which high-energy electrons are fired at protons and neutrons.
At sufficient energy and momentum transfer, the scattering pattern reveals information about the internal structure of the nucleon.
Rather than behaving as a smooth, featureless object, the proton appeared to contain point-like constituents.

## Why the experiments mattered

Before these results, the “inside” of the proton was not directly established in a way that matched later quark-based theory.
The scattering data supported the idea that there are smaller, effectively point-like components carrying fractions of the proton’s momentum.
These constituents were connected to the quark picture of hadrons and helped cement the emerging framework of particle physics.

## Connection to theory and later developments

The interpretation of the data aligned with the parton model and, more broadly, with quantum chromodynamics (QCD) as the theory of the strong interaction.
Deep inelastic scattering became a cornerstone technique: it provided quantitative ways to map how momentum is distributed among quarks and gluons.
This line of work influenced how modern collider experiments analyze hadron structure and predict cross sections.

## Collaboration and recognition

The Nobel Prize is commonly cited as recognizing a body of experimental work carried out with collaborators (notably Henry Kendall and Richard Taylor).
The award reflects not only the result but also the experimental ingenuity required to perform precision measurements at high energies.

## Legacy

Friedman’s contributions are often summarized as:

- Providing strong experimental evidence for substructure within protons
- Helping link measurable scattering patterns to the quark/parton picture
- Establishing deep inelastic scattering as a foundational tool in high-energy physics
