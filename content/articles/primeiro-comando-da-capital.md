## Origins and Prison Base

The Primeiro Comando da Capital (PCC) is commonly described as emerging from Brazil’s prison system in the early 1990s, developing internal rules and mechanisms for coordination among incarcerated members. Its prison-based organization gave it resilience and a recruitment pipeline linked to detention networks.

## Operations (High Level)

While public discussion often focuses on street-level violence, the PCC is also associated with:

- **Drug trafficking logistics** and wholesale distribution.
- **Financial flows** and laundering through intermediaries.
- Coordination across **prisons and external networks**, enabling communication and discipline.

## 2006 São Paulo Attacks

In 2006, waves of coordinated attacks and prison uprisings were attributed to the PCC, disrupting daily life in São Paulo and drawing national attention to the group’s reach and organizational capacity.

## Expansion and Rivalries

Over time, reporting has described the PCC’s expansion beyond São Paulo into other Brazilian states and cross-border trafficking routes. Rivalries with other criminal factions have contributed to spikes in violence in certain regions.

## State Response and Policy Debate

Responses have included large-scale policing operations and prison-security measures, alongside ongoing debate about:

- Whether heavy-handed tactics strengthen prison gangs by increasing incarceration and prison overcrowding.
- How to disrupt communications and finance networks without producing broader social harm.

## Why the PCC Is Often Cited

The PCC is frequently used as a case study in how prison governance, informal rule systems, and illicit markets can combine to produce a durable criminal organization.
