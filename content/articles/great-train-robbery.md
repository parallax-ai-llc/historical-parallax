## Summary

Infamous 1963 heist where a gang of 15 robbers stopped a Royal Mail train and stole £2.6 million (£55 million today). The robbery and subsequent manhunt captivated Britain. The robbers became folk heroes.

## Background

The Great Train Robbery refers to a meticulously planned robbery of a Royal Mail train traveling from Glasgow to London.
The target was cash being transported for banking and commercial use, which made rail shipments an attractive objective for organized criminals.
The scale of the theft and the planning involved made it one of the most famous crimes in modern British history.

## The Robbery

On 8 August 1963, the gang interfered with railway signals to stop the train at a remote location.
They overpowered crew members and removed sacks of cash from the train.
During the incident, the train driver was assaulted, which later intensified public attention and legal consequences.

## Investigation and Manhunt

British police launched a major investigation, using forensic evidence, informant tips, and traditional detective work.
A key breakthrough came after the gang used a rural hideout, where investigators were able to recover physical evidence.
Many participants were arrested, tried, and sentenced to lengthy prison terms.

## Cultural Impact

The robbery became embedded in British popular culture, inspiring books, films, and long-running public fascination.
Some of the figures associated with the crime became notorious for later escapes or life stories reported in the press.
The event is frequently cited in discussions of post-war British policing, media sensationalism, and the mythology that can form around high-profile crimes.
