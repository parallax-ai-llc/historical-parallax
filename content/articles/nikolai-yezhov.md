## Summary

Soviet secret police chief who oversaw the Great Purge (1937-1938), the bloodiest period of Stalin's terror. Under the 'Yezhovshchina,' over 750,000 people were executed and 1.3 million sent to gulags. He was himself purged and executed.

## Context

Yezhov led the NKVD during the most intense phase of Stalin-era political repression. In historical writing, this period is often described in terms of institutionalized denunciations, mass arrests, coerced confessions, and quotas that turned state security into a machine for terror.

The term **Yezhovshchina** is used to describe both Yezhov’s tenure and the broader climate of violence and fear associated with it. While Yezhov was a central administrative figure, responsibility is typically analyzed across the Soviet leadership, the security apparatus, and the broader command system.

## How Historians Interpret His Role

- **Bureaucratic execution of policy**: A key theme is how paperwork, directives, and internal incentives enabled extremely large-scale abuses.
- **Agency vs. instrument**: Scholarship debates the degree to which Yezhov initiated actions versus operationalized Stalin’s preferences through the NKVD.
- **Self-consuming repression**: Yezhov’s own arrest and execution is frequently cited as an example of how purges could turn inward on the security services.

## Key Takeaways

- State terror can be driven by administrative systems as much as by battlefield violence.
- Leadership accountability is often assessed through documentation of orders, reporting chains, and the practical control of policing institutions.
