## Summary

Hundreds of Amhara civilians were hacked to death with machetes in Mai Kadra during the Tigray War. Both sides blamed each other for the massacre.

## Background (High-Level)

- Mai Kadra is a town in the Western Zone of the Tigray Region, Ethiopia, near the Sudan border.
- The killings occurred in the opening days of the Tigray War, during a period of rapid military advances and local power shifts.
- Reporting and attribution have been contested, and multiple actors have offered conflicting accounts of who organized and carried out the violence.

## What Is Commonly Reported

- Victims were largely described as civilians, with many accounts identifying them as ethnic Amhara.
- Methods reported in many summaries include machetes and other sharp weapons, with some reports also describing firearms.
- The event is frequently cited as one of the earliest and most widely publicized mass-killing episodes of the Tigray conflict.

## Investigations and Documentation

- Ethiopian authorities, international media, and human-rights organizations reported on the incident, sometimes reaching different conclusions.
- Some investigations emphasized local militia/youth involvement; others stressed the surrounding military context and subsequent reprisals.
- As with many incidents in active conflict zones, independent verification has been complicated by access constraints and propaganda.

## Aftermath and Significance

- The massacre became a major reference point in narratives about ethnic violence in the conflict.
- It was used by different sides to justify security operations, mobilize support, and frame responsibility for atrocities.
- The episode is frequently discussed in broader debates about accountability mechanisms and transitional justice in Ethiopia.

## Notes on Attribution (Uncertainty)

- Contemporary sources often disagree on perpetrators and command responsibility.
- High-level summaries should distinguish between:
  - immediate attackers (on-the-ground participants),
  - organizers/instigators (planning and coordination), and
  - broader enabling conditions (breakdown of security and rule of law during conflict).
