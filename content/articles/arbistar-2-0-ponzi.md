## Summary

Spanish cryptocurrency trading bot Ponzi scheme that defrauded 32,000 investors of over €100 million. Founder Santiago Fuentes disappeared with investor funds.

## What It Was

Arbistar 2.0 was marketed as a cryptocurrency trading “bot” and investment program that promised steady returns while claiming to use automated arbitrage strategies.[^1] In practice, authorities and reporting around the case describe a classic Ponzi-like structure where withdrawals depended on new inflows rather than verifiable trading profits.

## How the Scheme Was Marketed

- **Automation narrative:** The product was promoted as algorithmic trading that could operate continuously and remove human error.
- **Predictable profits:** Materials emphasized stability and consistency, which is a common red flag in high-volatility markets like crypto.
- **Scale and reach:** The victim count widely cited in summaries (tens of thousands of investors) suggests broad distribution, including through online communities and referral networks.[^1]

## Collapse and Immediate Aftermath

When the platform froze withdrawals in 2020, many participants were unable to access their deposits.[^1] Freezes of this kind often accelerate loss realization because they prevent orderly exits and can trigger regulatory scrutiny and criminal investigation.

## Investigation and Allegations

Spanish authorities investigated the operation as a large-scale fraud involving very large aggregate losses (reported as more than €100 million) and a high number of victims.[^1] The case is frequently referenced in discussions about investor protection in lightly regulated crypto investment products.

## Why It Matters

Arbistar 2.0 is often cited as an example of:

- **“Black box” trading claims** that are difficult for retail investors to verify.
- **Return guarantees** that can mask liquidity dependence on new deposits.
- **Operational opacity** (custody, accounting, and trade execution not independently audited).
