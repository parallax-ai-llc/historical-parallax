## Summary

Japanese general who co-planned the Mukden Incident that led to Japan's invasion of Manchuria. Oversaw widespread atrocities as War Minister. Hanged as Class A war criminal.

## Background

Itagaki was a career officer in the Imperial Japanese Army and became associated with factions that supported aggressive expansion on the Asian continent. He is often discussed alongside other Kwantung Army figures who promoted “facts on the ground” operations to force political outcomes.

## Role in Manchuria and North China

Secondary histories describe Itagaki as a key planner in the chain of actions around the 1931 Mukden Incident, which Japan used as a pretext to occupy Manchuria and establish the puppet state of Manchukuo. His positions in and around the Kwantung Army placed him near decision-making on intelligence, planning, and coordination for subsequent operations.

## Senior wartime offices

During the late 1930s, Itagaki held senior posts including War Minister. As a top political-military figure, he was part of a leadership system responsible for major strategic decisions in the Sino-Japanese War and the wider Pacific conflict. Discussions of his record commonly reference responsibility for policies and command environments in which large-scale abuses occurred.

## Postwar trial and execution

After Japan’s surrender, Itagaki was tried by the International Military Tribunal for the Far East (Tokyo Trials) as a Class A war criminal. He was convicted and executed in 1948.

## Notes on attribution

In many accounts, “responsibility” is discussed both in terms of direct planning (e.g., Mukden) and broader command responsibility for wartime conduct. Precise attribution can differ across historians and legal analyses.
