## Summary

Ghanaian military dictator who seized power and ran a corrupt government enriching himself and cronies. Executed by firing squad after Rawlings' AFRC coup.

## Rise to power (1972)

Acheampong, an officer in the Ghanaian Army, came to power through a coup d'état in 1972 that removed the civilian government of Kofi Abrefa Busia. The takeover reflected economic strain, political contention, and dissatisfaction within parts of the military.

## National Redemption Council / Supreme Military Council

His government initially operated under the National Redemption Council (NRC) and later the Supreme Military Council (SMC). The period is often characterized by:

- military-led governance and restrictions on political competition
- efforts to manage shortages and stabilize public finances
- allegations of corruption and patronage (reported by opponents and later investigations)

## Economic and social programs (high-level)

One frequently cited initiative during Acheampong's era is "Operation Feed Yourself," intended to boost domestic agricultural production. Analysts disagree on the program's overall effectiveness, but it remains part of the era's public memory.

## "Union Government" debate

Late in his rule, Acheampong promoted a "Union Government" concept that would combine civilian and military participation. The proposal became politically divisive and was linked to a referendum process that attracted controversy.

## Removal and execution (1978–1979)

Acheampong was removed from office in 1978 by fellow military officers, and Ghana continued to experience instability. Following the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (AFRC) coup led by Jerry John Rawlings in 1979, Acheampong was tried by a military tribunal and executed by firing squad.

## Notes on interpretation

Assessments of Acheampong's legacy vary. Some accounts stress economic hardship and governance failures, while others emphasize the constraints of the period and the popularity of certain nationalist policies.
