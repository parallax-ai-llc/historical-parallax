## Summary

Muhammad Yamin (24 August 1903 – 17 October 1962) was an Indonesian poet, politician, historian, and national hero who played a pivotal yet controversial role in Indonesia's independence movement and early nation-building.[^1] He is recognized as a founding father of modern Indonesian poetry, a key participant in the drafting of the 1945 Constitution, and a fervent nationalist who championed Indonesian unity.[^2] Despite his intellectual contributions and literary innovations, Yamin's legacy remains deeply contested due to his reputation for exaggerating accomplishments, divisive political behavior, and disputed historical claims, particularly regarding the authorship of Pancasila, Indonesia's foundational state philosophy.[^3] He served in multiple ministerial positions under President Sukarno and was posthumously declared a National Hero of Indonesia in 1973.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Muhammad Yamin was born on 24 August 1903 in Talawi, Sawahlunto, located on the island of Sumatra in the Dutch East Indies.[^5] He came from a distinguished Minangkabau family; his father, Tuanku Oesman Gelar Baginda Khatib (1856–1924), was a prominent ulama (Islamic scholar) and adat leader who authored manuscripts on Minangkabau customary laws, now preserved at the Royal Netherlands Institute of Southeast Asian and Caribbean Studies (KITLV).[^6] Oesman had five wives and sixteen children, many of whom became influential intellectuals and political figures in early modern Indonesian history, though the family remained somewhat fragmented politically.[^7] Yamin's mother, Siti Saadah, hailed from Solok and raised him in a scholarly environment that emphasized both Islamic education and Minangkabau cultural traditions.[^8]

Yamin received his education at Dutch colonial schools for indigenous populations, first attending a Hollandsch-Inlandsche School (HIS), then moving to an Algemene Middelbare School (AMS) in Yogyakarta.[^9] His education exposed him to European literature, particularly Dutch Romanticism, which would profoundly influence his poetic style.[^10] In 1932, Yamin obtained a law degree in Jakarta (then Batavia), positioning himself among the educated indigenous elite who would lead Indonesia's nationalist movement.[^11] His upbringing in West Sumatra—a region with strong intellectual and reformist Islamic traditions—combined with exposure to Western education, shaped his identity as both a Minangkabau cultural advocate and an Indonesian nationalist.[^12]

During his youth, Yamin was deeply influenced by the cultural and political awakening sweeping the Dutch East Indies in the early 20th century. The ethical policy implemented by the Dutch colonial government, which expanded educational opportunities for indigenous populations, created a generation of educated natives who increasingly questioned colonial rule.[^13] Yamin's early writings reflected his dual identity: he initially "worshipped" his native West Sumatra before broadening his nationalism to encompass the entire Indonesian archipelago.[^14] His early exposure to both indigenous cultural traditions and Western literary forms would become the foundation for his pioneering work in modern Indonesian literature and his later political career advocating for national unity.

## Rise to Power

Muhammad Yamin's ascent to national prominence began with his literary achievements and his active participation in the youth nationalist movement of the 1920s. In 1921, at just eighteen years old, Yamin published his debut poem "Bahasa Bangsa" (Language, Nation) in the journal *Jong Sumatra*, which emphasized the crucial link between linguistic unification and national identity.[^15] This poem marked an early departure from traditional Malay literary forms by advocating for Indonesian as a unifying national language.[^16] One year later, in 1922, Yamin published *Tanah Air* (Motherland), the first collection of modern Malay verse, introducing vivid imagery of Indonesia's landscapes and patriotic sentiment that diverged from ornate classical styles toward more direct, emotive expression influenced by European Romanticism.[^17] This groundbreaking work is regarded by scholars as a foundational text in modern Indonesian poetry, pioneering the integration of personal emotion with nationalist ideology in vernacular Indonesian rather than archaic Malay forms.[^18]

Yamin's political emergence coincided with the historic Second Congress of Indonesian Youth held in Jakarta on 27-28 October 1928. He played a leadership role in this congress, which produced the influential *Sumpah Pemuda* (Youth Pledge), a declaration that proclaimed: "One Country, One Nation, One Language."[^19] On 28 October 1928, coinciding with the Youth Pledge, Yamin released his second poetry collection *Tumpah Darahku* (My Spilled Blood), further cementing his reputation as the premier poet of Indonesian nationalism.[^20] In his poetry, Yamin extensively employed the sonnet form borrowed from Dutch literature, adapting European literary structures to Indonesian themes.[^21]

Throughout the early 1930s, Yamin remained active in nationalist circles, working in journalism and joining the editorial board of the newspaper *Panorama* alongside Liem Koen Hian, Sanusi Pane, and Amir Sjarifuddin.[^22] In mid-1936, this group launched another newspaper, *Kebangoenan* (1936–1941), which served as a platform for nationalist discourse.[^23] During this period, Yamin advocated the non-cooperative stance of the Indonesian Party (*Partai Indonesia*, PARTINDO) against the Dutch colonial administration, declining all invitations to join the civil service.[^24] However, his political career was marked by controversy; he was expelled from the nationalist organization Gerindo in 1939 for breaches of regulations, including campaigning against another Gerindo candidate in municipal elections.[^25] He subsequently established the Party of Indonesian Unity (Parpindo).[^26]

In 1939, Yamin became a member of the Volksraad, the advisory colonial parliament established by the Dutch.[^27] However, his tenure proved divisive. Within the Volksraad, Yamin split the National Fraction grouping of Indonesian members led by Mohammad Husni Thamrin by enticing non-Javanese members away, damaging nationalist unity.[^28] Thamrin referred to him as "the eternal splitter" because of his confrontational attitude.[^29] Despite these controversies, Yamin's literary reputation and his participation in key moments of nationalist organizing—particularly the 1928 Youth Congress—established him as a significant intellectual figure in the independence movement, paving the way for his involvement in constitutional drafting during the Japanese occupation and subsequent independence period.

---

## Major Achievements

Muhammad Yamin's most significant contributions occurred during the crucial period of Indonesia's transition from colonial rule to independence. In 1945, during the Japanese occupation, Yamin was appointed as one of the sixty-two founding members of the *Badan Penyelidik Usaha-Usaha Persiapan Kemerdekaan* (BPUPK), the Investigating Committee for Preparatory Work for Independence, a Japanese-sponsored body tasked with planning the new Indonesian nation.[^30] During the first plenary session of BPUPK on 29 May 1945, Yamin delivered a speech proposing five philosophical principles for the proposed nation: Nationalism (*Kebangsaan*), Humanity (*Perikemanusiaan*), Belief in God (*Ketuhanan*), Democracy (*Kerakyatan*), and Social Welfare (*Kesejahteraan Rakyat*).[^31] These principles would later be incorporated into what became known as Pancasila, Indonesia's state ideology, though the primary credit for formulating Pancasila is traditionally given to Sukarno, who delivered his famous speech outlining the philosophy on 1 June 1945, two days after Yamin's presentation.[^32]

During the recess between the two BPUPK sessions, Yamin served on the Committee of Nine (*Panitia Sembilan*), alongside Sukarno, Hatta, Maramis, Soebardjo, Wahid Hasjim, Muzakkir, Agus Salim, and Abikoesno, which reformulated Sukarno's Pancasila into a preamble for the future constitution.[^33] However, when the constitutional drafting committee was formed, Yamin was disappointed at not being appointed and refused to accept his alternative appointment to a committee on financial matters.[^34] When the draft constitution was put to a vote on 16 July 1945, Yamin criticized it and was the only BPUPK member who did not immediately accept it.[^35] Despite this, on 17 August 1945, following Indonesia's proclamation of independence, the Preparatory Committee for Indonesian Independence (PPKI) tasked a commission of seven—Sukarno, Mohammad Hatta, Soepomo, Subardjo, Otto Iskandardinata, Yamin, and Wongsonegoro—to produce the final version of the constitution.[^36]

Yamin's ministerial career spanned multiple cabinets during President Sukarno's administration. He served as Minister of Education and Culture (1953–1955) in the First Ali Sastroamidjojo Cabinet, Minister without Portfolio (1957–1959) in the Djuanda Cabinet, Minister for Social Affairs and Culture (1959–1960) in the First Working Cabinet, Minister and Deputy Director of the National Planning Board (BAPPENAS) (1960–1962) in the Second Working Cabinet, and Deputy Prime Minister, Minister of Information, and Director of BAPPENAS (1962 until his death) in the Third Working Cabinet.[^37] In 1946, Yamin served as secretariat of the Indonesia Raya committee and conducted decisive research and analysis related to the history of Indonesia's Red and White flag, establishing the historical meaning of the nation's symbols.[^38]

As a historian, Yamin produced influential works that shaped Indonesian historical consciousness, particularly his books on the Majapahit Empire and its legendary prime minister, Gajah Mada. His 1948 book *Gajah Mada* explained the glory of Majapahit as a kingdom with extensive territorial power achieved through Gajah Mada's famous Palapa Oath.[^39] Yamin, along with Sukarno, frequently cited Gajah Mada's oath and the *Nagarakretagama* as historical proof of Indonesia's past greatness and as justification for territorial unity.[^40] He also wrote *Tatanegara Majapahit* (7 volumes), an exposition of the administration of the Majapahit Empire.[^41] Yamin called the Sriwijaya Kingdom the "Unitary State I," emphasizing its importance as a precursor to Indonesian national unity.[^42] He promoted the narrative of 350 years of continuous Dutch colonization starting from the establishment of the Dutch East India Company in 1602, a figure he popularized in writings from the 1930s to evoke collective resistance and national cohesion.[^43]

Yamin's literary legacy extends beyond poetry to plays, essays, and historical novels. He translated works from authors such as William Shakespeare (*Julius Caesar*) and Rabindranath Tagore, introducing world literature to Indonesian audiences.[^44] He pioneered the sonnet form in modern Indonesian literature and is considered one of the founding fathers of modern Indonesian poetry.[^45] His innovations extended to promoting Indonesian as a literary medium, aligning with emerging youth movements that sought linguistic standardization to foster national consciousness.[^46]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his intellectual contributions, Muhammad Yamin's career was marked by significant controversies that have tarnished his historical reputation. Perhaps the most damaging controversy concerns his claim to have originated Pancasila, Indonesia's foundational state philosophy. Yamin later asserted that in his 29 May 1945 speech at the BPUPK, he had enumerated the five principles that became known as Pancasila, which would have predated Sukarno's famous 1 June 1945 speech by two days.[^47] However, Yamin's claim of authorship was questioned by several prominent founding fathers, including Vice President Mohammad Hatta, Mr. Subarjo, Mr. A. Maramis, and Prof. A.G. Pringgodigdo.[^48] Hatta specifically called Yamin "crafty" (*licik*) and denied the validity of his claims.[^49] Yamin subsequently claimed that he had produced a draft constitution very similar to the version prepared by Soepomo, but there is no documentary evidence for this assertion, and Hatta explicitly denied that Yamin had presented such a document to the BPUPK.[^50]

Yamin developed a reputation for overstating his accomplishments and being argumentative.[^51] When he served as Minister of Culture in the First Ali Sastroamidjojo Cabinet from 1953 to 1955, Yamin claimed to have led the restoration of the Borobudur temple, one of Indonesia's most important Buddhist monuments.[^52] He ordered a plaque installed on the temple claiming he played a key role in the restoration work. However, once Yamin was no longer culture minister, this plaque was removed, suggesting that his colleagues did not accept his claimed contributions.[^53] This pattern of self-aggrandizement extended to other areas; during the 1940s, Yamin visited Trowulan and found fragments of terracotta, including a piggy bank in the form of a man's face, which he controversially interpreted as the face of Gajah Mada, though this interpretation remains disputed by archaeologists.[^54]

Yamin's political behavior within the nationalist movement was deeply divisive. Within the Volksraad, he split the National Fraction grouping led by Mohammad Husni Thamrin by enticing non-Javanese members away, damaging the unity that nationalists had worked to build.[^55] Thamrin's characterization of Yamin as "the eternal splitter" reflected the frustration of nationalist leaders with his confrontational style and tendency to prioritize personal ambitions over collective solidarity.[^56] His expulsion from Gerindo in 1939 for campaigning against another party candidate further demonstrated his willingness to undermine organizational discipline.[^57]

Yamin's historical work has been criticized by later scholars for prioritizing nationalist ideology over empirical rigor. Critical historians have noted that his works from the 1950s—particularly his interpretations of Indonesian history—were "romantic, ultra-nationalistic, and some would say pre-scientific."[^58] His approach to historiography emphasized mythmaking and national pride rather than careful scholarly analysis, and subsequent generations of Indonesian historians have challenged and revised many of Yamin's historical interpretations.[^59] His promotion of the "350 years of colonization" narrative, while politically useful for mobilizing anti-colonial sentiment, simplified and distorted the complex and varied nature of Dutch presence in the archipelago.[^60]

Furthermore, during the BPUPK proceedings, Yamin suggested that the new Indonesian nation should include not only the territories of the Netherlands Indies but also the entire Malay-speaking world: Sarawak, Sabah, Malaya, and Portuguese Timor.[^61] This expansive territorial nationalism reflected an ultranationalist vision that went beyond the boundaries eventually adopted by Indonesia and could have led to regional conflicts had such ambitions been pursued. Yamin's vision of Indonesian identity "initially worshipped the region of his birth, West Sumatra, and then worshipped Indonesia, later becoming an ultranationalist,"[^62] suggesting an evolution toward increasingly extreme nationalist positions.

## Personal Life

Muhammad Yamin married Siti Sundari (also known as Raden Ajeng Sundari Merto Amodjo) in 1937.[^63] Sundari came from a noble Javanese family in Surakarta and worked as a teacher while being active in feminist causes.[^64] The marriage represented a union between Yamin's Minangkabau background and Javanese aristocratic culture, bridging two of Indonesia's most prominent ethnic groups. The couple had one child, a son named Dang Rahadian Sinayangish Yamin, commonly known as Rahadian Yamin, who later pursued a career as a noted fashion designer and model (*peragawan*).[^65] No public records indicate additional marriages or significant extramarital relationships, and Yamin's personal life remained relatively private amid his very public political and intellectual pursuits.[^66]

Coming from a large family—his father had sixteen children from five wives—Yamin was part of an extensive network of half-siblings, many of whom became influential in Indonesian society, though the family remained "veritably influential, but incohesive" politically.[^67] This familial background in a prominent Minangkabau family provided Yamin with social capital and connections throughout the Indonesian elite. His mother, Siti Saadah, who came from Solok, raised him in an environment that emphasized both Islamic scholarship and Minangkabau cultural traditions, instilling in him the intellectual foundation that would shape his later work.[^68]

Yamin's personality was complex and often difficult. Contemporary accounts describe him as intelligent but argumentative, with a tendency toward self-promotion that alienated colleagues.[^69] Mohammad Husni Thamrin's description of Yamin as "the eternal splitter" and Mohammad Hatta's characterization of him as "crafty" (*licik*) suggest that he was viewed with suspicion and frustration by other nationalist leaders.[^70] His confrontational attitude in political settings, his willingness to break organizational discipline, and his persistent claims to accomplishments that others disputed all point to a personality driven by ambition and perhaps insecurity about recognition. Despite these character flaws, Yamin maintained important friendships within the nationalist movement and continued to receive appointments to high positions under Sukarno, suggesting that his intellectual contributions were valued even if his personality was not universally appreciated.

Yamin's cultural identity remained rooted in his Minangkabau heritage even as he became a champion of pan-Indonesian nationalism. His early poetry and cultural work celebrated Sumatran themes before expanding to embrace the entire archipelago. This trajectory from regional to national identity was common among Indonesian nationalist intellectuals but appeared particularly pronounced in Yamin's case, with some observers noting his evolution "from worshipping West Sumatra to worshipping Indonesia" and eventually becoming "an ultranationalist."[^71] Yamin died in Jakarta on 17 October 1962 at the age of 59, still serving as Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Information in Sukarno's government.[^72]

## Legacy

Muhammad Yamin's legacy remains deeply contested, embodying the complexities and contradictions of Indonesia's nationalist generation. On 6 November 1973, via Presidential Decree No. 088/TK/1973, Yamin was posthumously proclaimed a National Hero of Indonesia, the highest-level title awarded in the country.[^73] This recognition acknowledged his contributions to Indonesian independence, constitutional development, and cultural nationalism. The honor placed him among the pantheon of founding fathers whose "ideas contributed to the political awakening and the surge in national pride in Indonesia."[^74] His literary innovations—particularly his pioneering use of Indonesian language for modern poetry and his introduction of European forms like the sonnet—established him as "one of the founding fathers of modern Indonesian poetry," whose works continue to be studied in Indonesian schools and universities.[^75]

Yamin's historical writings, despite their methodological limitations, played a significant role in shaping Indonesian national consciousness during the critical early years of independence. His works on Gajah Mada and the Majapahit Empire, his research on the Sriwijaya Kingdom as a precursor to Indonesian unity, and his promotion of narratives linking the archipelago's various historical kingdoms into a single national story provided Indonesians with a usable past that justified their newly independent nation.[^76] His multi-volume *Tatanegara Majapahit* and his book *Gajah Mada* (1948) became influential texts that educated Indonesians about their pre-colonial heritage, even if modern historians have challenged many of his interpretations.[^77]

However, Yamin's legacy has sparked ongoing debates among historians regarding his approach to Indonesian historiography. His work is "often characterized as prioritizing ideological nationalism over empirical rigor," and critical historians have challenged his "romantic, ultra-nationalistic, and some would say pre-scientific" methodology.[^78] The evolution of Indonesian historical scholarship since the 1960s has involved systematically revising many of Yamin's claims and interpretations, particularly his simplified narratives about continuous Dutch colonization and his ideologically driven readings of pre-colonial history.[^79] Contemporary Indonesian historians recognize Yamin's importance as a cultural figure while maintaining critical distance from his historical claims.

The controversy over Pancasila authorship continues to affect Yamin's reputation. While he is acknowledged as a participant in the constitutional discussions and a member of the Committee of Nine that refined the Preamble, the consensus among historians is that Sukarno deserves primary credit for formulating Pancasila.[^80] Yamin's persistent claims to have originated these principles, despite contradictory evidence and denials from figures like Mohammad Hatta, have led some scholars to view him as a self-promoter who exaggerated his role in critical historical moments.[^81] This aspect of his legacy serves as a cautionary tale about the complexities of historical memory and the temptation among participants in historic events to inflate their contributions.

Yamin's divisive political behavior and his reputation as "the eternal splitter" also complicate his legacy.[^82] While he contributed to nationalist discourse through journalism and literary work, his actions within the Volksraad and nationalist organizations sometimes undermined the unity he claimed to champion. This contradiction—between his powerful rhetoric of national unity and his divisive personal conduct—remains a subject of historical discussion. Nevertheless, Yamin's role in the 1928 Youth Congress and the Youth Pledge remains uncontroversially important, and this contribution alone justifies his recognition as a significant figure in Indonesian independence history.[^83]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Supporters and defenders of Muhammad Yamin emphasize his pioneering role in Indonesian literature and his genuine contributions to nationalist consciousness. As a literary figure, Yamin is credited with fundamentally transforming Indonesian poetry by introducing modern forms and themes. His 1922 collection *Tanah Air* "is regarded by scholars as a foundational text in modern Indonesian poetry, pioneering the integration of personal emotion with nationalist ideology in vernacular Indonesian rather than archaic Malay forms."[^84] Literary historians recognize that Yamin's poetry provided a crucial bridge between traditional Malay literary conventions and modern Indonesian expression, helping to establish Indonesian—then called Malay—as a viable literary language capable of expressing contemporary nationalist sentiments.[^85]

Defenders point to Yamin's significant role in the 1928 Youth Congress, where he helped lead the Second Congress of Indonesian Youth that produced the *Sumpah Pemuda* (Youth Pledge), which proclaimed "One Country, One Nation, One Language."[^86] This oath became a foundational moment in Indonesian nationalism, helping to unite diverse ethnic groups under a common Indonesian identity. Yamin's timed release of his poetry collection *Tumpah Darahku* on the same date as the Youth Pledge demonstrated his ability to synchronize cultural and political nationalism effectively.[^87] His early poem "Bahasa Bangsa" (Language, Nation), which emphasized that "Lose your language and your nation is lost too," proved prophetic in anticipating the central role language would play in Indonesian nation-building.[^88]

Scholarly assessments acknowledge that Yamin, despite methodological limitations, played a vital role in constructing a historical narrative that gave Indonesians pride in their pre-colonial heritage. His works on Gajah Mada, Majapahit, and Sriwijaya "contributed to the political awakening and the surge in national pride in Indonesia" at a time when the new nation desperately needed such narratives.[^89] Some historians argue that Yamin's "romantic, ultra-nationalistic" approach, while not meeting modern scholarly standards, was appropriate and perhaps necessary for the immediate post-independence period when nation-building required emotionally compelling stories more than nuanced academic history.[^90]

Additionally, Yamin's contributions to constitutional discussions, even if his claims about Pancasila authorship are disputed, remain significant. He was genuinely one of the sixty-two members of BPUPK and did participate in the Committee of Nine that refined the constitutional preamble.[^91] His speech on 29 May 1945 proposing five national principles, regardless of whether it influenced Sukarno's formulation, demonstrated his engagement with fundamental questions about Indonesian national identity.[^92] His participation in the commission of seven that produced the final version of the 1945 Constitution places him among the constitution's actual drafters.[^93] Supporters argue that his contested claims should not entirely overshadow his real contributions to these historic processes.

### Negative Views

Critics of Muhammad Yamin focus on his character flaws, his exaggerated claims, and his divisive political behavior. The most damaging criticism concerns his disputed claim to have originated Pancasila before Sukarno. As noted by multiple founding fathers, including Vice President Mohammad Hatta, there is no credible evidence supporting Yamin's assertion that he formulated the five principles in his 29 May 1945 speech before Sukarno's famous 1 June speech.[^94] Hatta specifically called Yamin "crafty" (*licik*) and denied that Yamin had produced the draft constitution he later claimed to have written.[^95] This controversy has led many historians to view Yamin as opportunistic and dishonest, willing to falsify his role in Indonesia's founding moments for personal glory.

Critics point to a consistent pattern of self-aggrandizement throughout Yamin's career. His claim to have led the Borobudur temple restoration—commemorated in a plaque that was removed once he left office—exemplifies his tendency to exaggerate his accomplishments.[^96] His interpretation of terracotta fragments as depicting Gajah Mada's face, disputed by archaeologists, shows his willingness to make unsupported claims in his historical work.[^97] More broadly, "although Yamin was intelligent, he had a reputation for overstating his accomplishments, and for being argumentative."[^98] This reputation, acknowledged by contemporaries and confirmed by subsequent historical research, significantly undermines confidence in all of Yamin's claims about his contributions.

Yamin's political behavior within the nationalist movement drew severe criticism from colleagues. Within the Volksraad, he "divided the National Fraction grouping of Indonesian members led by Mohammad Husni Thamrin, by enticing non-Javanese members away, damaging the unity of the nationalists."[^99] Thamrin's characterization of Yamin as "the eternal splitter" captures the frustration nationalist leaders felt with his confrontational and divisive conduct.[^100] His expulsion from Gerindo for campaigning against another party candidate demonstrated his willingness to prioritize personal ambition over organizational solidarity.[^101] Critics argue that Yamin's divisive behavior contradicted his rhetoric of national unity and damaged the nationalist cause at critical moments.

From a scholarly perspective, Yamin's historical work has been thoroughly criticized by later generations of Indonesian historians. His methodology was "romantic, ultra-nationalistic, and some would say pre-scientific," prioritizing nationalist ideology over empirical evidence.[^102] Critical historians have systematically challenged and revised many of Yamin's historical interpretations, particularly his simplified narratives about continuous Dutch colonization and his ideologically driven readings of pre-colonial kingdoms.[^103] His promotion of the "350 years of colonization" myth, while politically useful, distorted historical reality and established a misleading framework that subsequent historians have had to correct.[^104] Modern Indonesian historiography has moved decisively away from Yamin's approach, viewing his work as an example of how nationalist ideology can compromise scholarly integrity.

Finally, critics note Yamin's extremist territorial nationalism, particularly his suggestion during BPUPK proceedings that Indonesia should include Malaya, Sarawak, Sabah, and Portuguese Timor—essentially the entire Malay-speaking world.[^105] This expansive vision, which would have created regional conflicts and was ultimately rejected, reveals Yamin as "an ultranationalist" whose ambitions exceeded reasonable bounds.[^106] His evolution from Sumatran regionalist to Indonesian nationalist to expansive Malay-world nationalist suggests an ideological trajectory toward increasingly extreme positions.[^107] Critics argue that Yamin's ultranationalism, combined with his character flaws and scholarly deficiencies, makes him a problematic figure whose designation as a national hero requires critical reassessment.

## Famous Quotes

> "Remember, young people, oh unhappy Sumatra, Lose your language and your nation is lost too." - From the poem "Bahasa Bangsa" (Language, Nation), 1921, emphasizing the critical importance of linguistic unity to national identity.[^131]

> "One Country, One Nation, One Language" (*Satu Nusa, Satu Bangsa, Satu Bahasa*) - The Youth Pledge (*Sumpah Pemuda*) recited on 28 October 1928, which Yamin helped formulate and which became a foundational declaration of Indonesian national unity.[^132]
