## Summary

Powerful uncle of Kim Jong-un executed for allegedly plotting a coup. His execution, along with that of his entire family circle, demonstrated Kim Jong-un's ruthlessness.

## Political Role and Influence

- **Family connection**: Jang was married to Kim Kyong-hui, a sister of Kim Jong-il, which placed him inside the ruling family network.
- **Institutional base**: He held senior positions within the Workers' Party of Korea and the state security and economic apparatus, giving him leverage over patronage and appointments.
- **External engagement**: In reporting about the early Kim Jong-un era, Jang was often described as a key interlocutor on limited economic initiatives and dealings with foreign partners, particularly in China.

## Purge and Execution (2013)

High-level accounts describe Jang's removal as unusually public and rapid:

- He was reportedly arrested in a prominent public setting, then swiftly tried and executed.
- Official accusations framed him as a factional threat and portrayed corruption, abuse of power, and disloyalty as central charges.
- The episode signaled that status, kinship ties, and prior service offered little protection under the consolidation of Kim Jong-un's rule.

## Significance and Legacy

- **Consolidation message**: The purge is widely interpreted as a decisive demonstration of Kim Jong-un's intent to centralize authority and deter elite bargaining.
- **Elite discipline**: It reinforced incentives for senior officials to display loyalty and avoid forming independent power bases.
- **International perception**: The incident shaped external assessments of North Korean decision-making as opaque, high-risk, and prone to sudden internal shocks.
