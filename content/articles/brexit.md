## Summary

Brexit — a portmanteau of "British" and "exit" — refers to the United Kingdom's withdrawal from the European Union, a process spanning from the June 2016 referendum to the formal departure on January 31, 2020. It marked the first time a member state had ever left the EU, triggering years of political turbulence, three changes of Prime Minister, and two general elections in the UK.

The referendum result of 51.9% in favor of Leave shocked political establishments across Europe and the world. The narrow margin exposed deep divisions within the UK along generational, geographic, and class lines, with Scotland and Northern Ireland voting Remain while England and Wales voted Leave.

Brexit reshaped not only British politics but also the architecture of European integration, raising fundamental questions about sovereignty, trade, immigration, and the future of multinational unions in an era of rising nationalism.

## Background & Causes

The UK's relationship with European integration was always uneasy. Britain joined the European Economic Community in 1973 and held its first membership referendum in 1975, voting 67% to remain. Euroscepticism persisted within the Conservative Party for decades, amplified by disputes over the Maastricht Treaty (1992), the single currency, and freedom of movement.

The 2008 financial crisis deepened public frustration with establishment institutions. The rise of UKIP under Nigel Farage pushed immigration and sovereignty to the center of political debate. Under pressure from his party's right wing, Prime Minister David Cameron promised a referendum in the Conservative Party's 2015 manifesto. After winning a majority, he called the vote for June 23, 2016.

Campaigners on the Leave side argued that EU membership undermined parliamentary sovereignty, cost the UK £350 million per week (a contested figure), and prevented control over immigration. Remain campaigners warned of economic damage, reduced influence, and diplomatic isolation.

## Key Figures

**David Cameron** — Conservative PM who called the referendum and resigned after the Leave result. **Theresa May** — Cameron's successor who negotiated but failed to pass the Withdrawal Agreement through Parliament three times. **Boris Johnson** — Former Mayor of London and prominent Leave campaigner who eventually secured the deal and steered Brexit to completion. **Nigel Farage** — Leader of UKIP and later the Brexit Party, the most prominent populist voice for leaving the EU. **Michel Barnier** — The EU's chief negotiator, who led Brussels' side of the protracted withdrawal talks. **Nicola Sturgeon** — Scottish First Minister who argued Scotland's Remain vote justified a second independence referendum.

## Consequences & Impact

The immediate economic impact included a sharp depreciation of the pound sterling, which fell roughly 10–15% against the dollar and euro in the days following the referendum. Businesses facing uncertainty delayed investment; some financial firms relocated European operations to Dublin, Frankfurt, and Amsterdam.

The Withdrawal Agreement included provisions for a "backstop" arrangement for Northern Ireland — designed to prevent a hard border with the Republic of Ireland — which became the most contentious element of negotiations and a recurring cause of political crisis. The subsequent Northern Ireland Protocol, later renegotiated as the Windsor Framework (2023), remained a source of friction.

Trade with the EU declined in the months following the end of the transition period, with exporters facing new customs checks, paperwork, and regulatory barriers. UK fishing communities, who had been prominent Leave supporters, found access to EU markets significantly reduced.

Scotland's political landscape shifted further toward independence, with the SNP arguing Brexit demonstrated that Scottish interests were being overridden by English voters. Northern Ireland's status remained constitutionally ambiguous, reigniting debates about Irish unification.

## Perspectives

**Leavers** argue Brexit restored democratic sovereignty to Westminster, freed the UK from unelected EU bureaucrats, and allowed independent trade and immigration policy. They point to free trade agreements signed with Australia, Japan, and other nations as early evidence of a "Global Britain" strategy.

**Remainers** contend that Brexit caused measurable economic harm, reduced the UK's geopolitical influence, damaged the union with Scotland and Northern Ireland, and was driven by misinformation — particularly about NHS funding and immigration figures.

**European observers** largely view Brexit as a cautionary tale about the dangers of holding referendums on complex constitutional questions without adequate public deliberation, while also acknowledging that EU reform failures contributed to British disillusionment.

**Historians** note that Brexit fits a longer pattern of British ambivalence toward continental integration dating to the post-war period, and that the result reflected structural inequalities in UK society as much as any specific EU policy.
