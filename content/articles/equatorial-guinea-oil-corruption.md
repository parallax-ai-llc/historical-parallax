## Summary

Equatorial Guinea's oil revenues of $4 billion annually are controlled by the Obiang family. Most of the 1.4 million citizens live in extreme poverty despite massive national wealth.

## Additional Context

### Oil boom and “resource curse” dynamics

- Equatorial Guinea’s modern oil boom began in the 1990s, transforming fiscal revenues and GDP while also intensifying concerns about rent-seeking, patronage, and weak institutions—patterns often discussed under the “resource curse” concept.[^1][^2][^3]
- The gap between national income figures and living conditions for much of the population has been a recurring theme in reporting, alongside questions about public spending priorities and off-budget flows.[^1][^2]

### Political structure and concentration of power

- The country has been led for decades by President Teodoro Obiang Nguema Mbasogo, and many corruption allegations focus on the role of the ruling family and close associates in controlling access to state and oil-linked resources.[^2][^4]
- In such systems, discretionary control over contracts, licensing, and security institutions can make anticorruption reforms difficult without broader political change and independent oversight mechanisms.[^2]

### International enforcement and high-profile cases

- A prominent strand of the Equatorial Guinea corruption story involves foreign investigations into assets allegedly purchased abroad using misappropriated funds, including proceedings in France involving Teodoro Nguema Obiang Mangue (“Teodorín”), a senior official and the president’s son.[^1][^5]
- These cases are frequently discussed as examples of how anti–money laundering and asset-seizure regimes can be used to target proceeds of corruption even when domestic accountability is limited.[^5][^6]

### Transparency, governance, and reform debates

- Policy discussions often emphasize the importance of transparent oil revenue management, independent auditing, and public procurement standards, as well as the role of global financial centers in enabling or constraining illicit flows.[^3][^6]
- Equatorial Guinea’s experience is sometimes compared to other petroleum states where sudden commodity wealth interacted with fragile institutions and entrenched political control.[^3]
