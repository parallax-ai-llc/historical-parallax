## Summary

Jafar Panahi is one of Iran's most celebrated and internationally recognized filmmakers, known for his humanist storytelling and unflinching portrayals of marginalized voices within Iranian society. Born in 1960 in Mianeh, Iran, he rose to prominence through films that combined neorealist aesthetics with deeply personal narratives, earning major prizes at Cannes, Venice, and Berlin. His work has consistently interrogated class, gender, and authority in contemporary Iran.

Since 2010, Panahi has been subject to a 20-year ban on filmmaking, screenwriting, and leaving Iran, imposed by the Iranian government following his support for the Green Movement protests. Rather than silencing him, the ban transformed his art: he continued making films clandestinely, smuggling them out of Iran on USB drives hidden in birthday cakes, and winning additional international awards for work made under direct state prohibition. His defiance has made him a global symbol of artistic freedom and resistance.

## Early Life & Background

Panahi was born on July 11, 1960, in Mianeh, a city in northwestern Iran's East Azerbaijan province. He developed an early interest in cinema and studied at the College of Cinema and Television in Tehran, graduating in the late 1970s. His education coincided with the tumultuous period of the Iranian Revolution (1979), which profoundly shaped his political consciousness. He served in the military during the Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988), an experience that reinforced his awareness of human vulnerability and state power.

## Rise to Prominence

After working as an assistant director, Panahi gained widespread attention with his debut feature *The White Balloon* (1995), scripted by Abbas Kiarostami. The film won the Caméra d'Or at Cannes and established Panahi's signature approach: naturalistic performances, non-professional actors, and narratives centered on ordinary people navigating everyday injustice. His follow-up, *The Mirror* (1997), won the Golden Leopard at Locarno, and *The Circle* (2000) took the Golden Lion at Venice, cementing his status as a world-class filmmaker.

## Key Achievements

- **The White Balloon** (1995): Caméra d'Or, Cannes Film Festival
- **The Circle** (2000): Golden Lion, Venice Film Festival
- **Offside** (2006): Silver Bear, Berlin International Film Festival
- **This Is Not a Film** (2011): Made under house arrest, smuggled to Cannes on a USB drive inside a cake; widely screened internationally
- **Closed Curtain** (2013): Silver Bear for Best Screenplay, Berlin
- **Taxi** (2015): Golden Bear, Berlin — shot entirely inside a Tehran taxi cab, won the festival's top prize
- **No Bears** (2022): Special Jury Prize, Venice Film Festival; made while Panahi was out on bail, shortly before he was imprisoned

## Controversies & Criticism

In 2010, Iranian authorities arrested Panahi and sentenced him to six years in prison and a 20-year ban on directing, writing scripts, giving interviews, or traveling abroad. The charges related to his support of opposition candidates and alleged plans to make a film about the 2009 post-election protests. The sentence drew condemnation from filmmakers and human rights organizations worldwide. In July 2022, he was imprisoned to serve the original sentence. He was released in February 2023 after a hunger strike. Critics of his government argue the prosecutions are politically motivated censorship; Iranian authorities maintain they are lawful proceedings.

## Legacy & Influence

Panahi's career has demonstrated that artistic integrity can endure and even flourish under political repression. His clandestine films have expanded the vocabulary of what cinema can be — shot on iPhones, inside taxis, within the walls of a house — while maintaining rigorous humanism. He has influenced a generation of filmmakers working under authoritarian constraints across the world. Alongside Abbas Kiarostami and Mohsen Makhmalbaf, he is considered a defining figure of Iranian cinema's international golden era.
