## Summary

Rwandan finance minister who used his position to direct genocide by distributing weapons and inciting killings. He personally participated in massacres in Kibuye Prefecture.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1994 | Distributed weapons and directed killings as finance minister |
| 2001 | Arrested in Belgium |
| 2004 | Convicted of genocide by ICTR |
| 2004 | Sentenced to life imprisonment |

## Additional Context

- **Role in government**: Ndindabahizi served as Rwanda’s Minister of Finance during the 1994 genocide, and prosecutors argued that he used authority and resources to facilitate violence.
- **International tribunal**: His case was tried at the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR), one of the key international courts established to prosecute those responsible for the genocide.

## Why the Case Matters

- The prosecution of senior officials is often cited as part of the broader effort to establish individual criminal responsibility for mass atrocities.
- ICTR proceedings contributed to the development of international criminal law and documentation of events in Rwanda.

## Key Takeaways

- The Ndindabahizi conviction is commonly referenced in discussions of state capacity, command responsibility, and the use of administrative power to enable mass violence.

## Additional Notes

- International prosecutions of genocide often rely on extensive witness testimony, documentary evidence, and institutional records to establish both actions and intent.
- ICTR judgments are also used as historical documentation, alongside other sources, to reconstruct how violence was organized and carried out.

## Key Takeaways (International Justice)

- The case is part of a broader pattern of pursuing accountability not only for direct perpetrators but also for officials alleged to have enabled violence through policy, logistics, and public authority.
