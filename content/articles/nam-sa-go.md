## Summary

Nam Sa-go was a Joseon Dynasty scholar known for his geomantic (pungsu) predictions and his systematic approach to studying geographical features for site selection. While geomancy is not modern science, his careful observation and classification of landforms represented an early systematic approach to Korean geographical analysis.

## Context: Pungsu (Korean Geomancy)

- Pungsu (풍수) is a traditional Korean system for interpreting landscapes, often used historically for siting graves, homes, and important buildings.
- It draws on ideas similar to Chinese feng shui, emphasizing mountains, water flows, orientation, and the perceived "vitality" of terrain.
- From a modern scientific perspective, pungsu is not an empirical discipline; however, historical practitioners sometimes relied on careful observation of hydrology and landforms.

## Interpreting Nam Sa-go

- Many historical figures associated with divination and geomancy accumulate layers of legend over time.
- When reading claims about specific predictions or writings, it is important to distinguish between contemporaneous records and later attributions.

## Why This Matters Historically

- Regardless of metaphysical claims, the tradition reflects how pre-modern societies made high-stakes decisions (burials, settlement, and state projects) using the conceptual tools available to them.
- It also provides a window into early Korean geographic thinking: classification of terrain, attention to microclimates, and practical concerns like drainage and exposure.
