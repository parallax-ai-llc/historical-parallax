## Summary

Bolivian dictator whose 'Cocaine Coup' was financed by drug traffickers and assisted by Nazi war criminal Klaus Barbie. Sentenced to 30 years for human rights crimes.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1980 | Seized power in cocaine-funded coup |

## Context and Significance

- García Meza’s 1980 takeover is widely remembered as a military coup intertwined with narcotrafficking networks, which is why it is often described as the “Cocaine Coup.”
- His brief rule sits within a broader period of political instability in Bolivia marked by repeated coups and short-lived governments.

## Rule and Human Rights Record

Accounts of the period emphasize repression of political opponents, intimidation of civil society, and serious human rights abuses attributed to state forces.

## Fall, Exile, and Prosecution

- After losing power, García Meza spent years outside Bolivia and later faced prosecution.
- His conviction and long sentence are frequently cited as a notable example of a former head of state being held legally accountable for crimes committed while in office.

## Legacy

Histories of late–Cold War Latin America often mention the García Meza regime when discussing the overlap between authoritarian security states, transnational criminal finance, and impunity.
