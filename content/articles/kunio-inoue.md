## Summary

Japanese yakuza boss who headed the Kobe Yamaguchi-gumi, a splinter faction that broke from the main Yamaguchi-gumi in 2015. The split led to violent street clashes between the rival factions.

## Additional Context

### Yakuza organizational setting

- The Yamaguchi-gumi has historically been Japan's largest yakuza syndicate, with a federated structure of affiliated groups.
- Inoue became prominent within this ecosystem and later led a major breakaway faction, the Kobe Yamaguchi-gumi.
- The 2015 split is often discussed as a turning point that fragmented a long-dominant criminal organization into competing blocs.

### Conflict dynamics after the split

- Rivalry between factions contributed to a period of heightened tension, including sporadic violence and a heavy police response.
- Authorities increased surveillance and enforcement pressure, and Japan expanded measures aimed at reducing yakuza influence in business and public life.
- The episode is frequently used as an example of how internal succession disputes can reshape organized-crime landscapes.

### Broader significance

- Highlights the interaction between organized crime, policing, and legal constraints in modern Japan.
- Demonstrates how factionalization can both increase short-term violence and weaken a group over time through attrition and law-enforcement targeting.

### Notes

- Japanese media and police statements often frame major yakuza splits in terms of succession and governance disputes rather than ideology.
- Assessments of membership size and finances vary because official counts depend on self-reporting, arrests, and surveillance.
