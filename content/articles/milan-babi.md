## Summary

Croatian Serb political leader who was president of the self-proclaimed Republic of Serbian Krajina. He pleaded guilty at the ICTY to persecution as a crime against humanity. Committed suicide in detention.

## Political Role During the Yugoslav Wars

Babić emerged as a prominent political figure among Croatian Serbs during the breakup of Yugoslavia. He held leadership roles in the self-proclaimed Republic of Serbian Krajina (RSK), a separatist entity that existed during the Croatian War of Independence.

## Governance and Conflict Context

The RSK was tied to broader dynamics of ethnic nationalism, armed conflict, and competing sovereignty claims in the early 1990s. In this period, local and regional authorities, paramilitary forces, and regular military units were all implicated in campaigns of violence and displacement.

## ICTY Proceedings

At the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY), Babić pleaded guilty to persecution as a crime against humanity. His case is often referenced in discussions of:

- the use of plea agreements at international tribunals;
- establishing factual records about patterns of violence;
- cooperation with prosecutors in larger cases.

## Cooperation and Testimony

Open sources report that Babić provided cooperation and testimony relevant to other ICTY proceedings concerning senior political and military leaders. Such cooperation was part of the tribunal’s broader strategy to document command structures and link policy-level decisions to crimes on the ground.

## Death in Detention

In 2006, Babić died by suicide while in UN detention. His death highlighted the psychological and security challenges associated with holding high-profile defendants during lengthy international legal processes.

## Legacy

Babić’s guilty plea and sentencing are commonly cited in historical accounts of the Yugoslav wars and in legal scholarship on international criminal justice.
