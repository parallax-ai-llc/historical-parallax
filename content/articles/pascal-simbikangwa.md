## Summary

Former Rwandan intelligence chief convicted of genocide and complicity in crimes against humanity by a French court. He was the first person tried in France for the Rwandan genocide.

## Legal Context (France)

France has pursued a number of prosecutions connected to the 1994 Rwandan genocide, relying on domestic statutes that allow French courts to try certain international crimes when jurisdictional conditions are met (for example, when suspects are present on French territory). Simbikangwa's case became notable in part because it was framed as an early test of France's capacity to conduct complex genocide trials.[^1]

## Trial and Conviction (high level)

According to widely cited accounts, Simbikangwa was convicted in France for genocide and related crimes against humanity. Reporting on the case often emphasizes the evidentiary challenges in decades-old mass-atrocity prosecutions, including witness testimony, archival material, and chain-of-command questions.[^1]

## Historical Significance

The case is frequently referenced in discussions of:

- International criminal accountability beyond international tribunals
- The role of national courts in addressing mass atrocities
- Long timelines between events, investigations, and final judgments
