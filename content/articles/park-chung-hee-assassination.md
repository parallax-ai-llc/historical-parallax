## Summary

South Korean President Park Chung-hee was assassinated by his own KCIA director Kim Jae-gyu during a private dinner. The assassination ended 18 years of authoritarian rule.

## Key Figures

- **Park Chung-hee**: President of South Korea (1963–1979), whose rule combined rapid industrialization with political repression.
- **Kim Jae-gyu**: Director of the Korean Central Intelligence Agency (KCIA) who shot Park on 1979-10-26.
- **Cha Ji-cheol**: Head of the Presidential Security Service, often described as influential in court politics and security decisions.
- **Choi Kyu-hah**: Prime Minister who became acting president after the assassination.
- **Chun Doo-hwan**: Army general who rose to dominance in the ensuing power vacuum.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- The assassination occurred during a private dinner meeting attended by senior security and intelligence officials.
- Park was shot by Kim Jae-gyu, ending Park’s long presidency and triggering an immediate national security and political crisis.
- Authorities moved quickly to detain key participants and stabilize command structures, but factional conflict intensified behind the scenes.

## Immediate Aftermath

- **Leadership transition**: The constitutional succession process elevated Choi Kyu-hah, while real influence increasingly shifted toward military actors.
- **Investigations and trials**: Kim Jae-gyu and others were prosecuted; the legal proceedings became part of the wider struggle over how the event should be interpreted.
- **Political opening and backlash**: Public expectations for liberalization (“Seoul Spring”) rose briefly, before being constrained by security crackdowns and elite power consolidation.

## Historical Significance

- The assassination marked the end of South Korea’s **Third and Fourth Republic** era under Park and accelerated a turbulent transition period.
- It is frequently cited as a turning point that reshaped civil–military relations and the trajectory of democratization movements.
- Debates persist over motives and responsibility, but the broad consequence is clear: **a rapid reconfiguration of power** that culminated in a new phase of military-led governance.
