## Context: the Derg and the Red Terror

The Derg was a military junta that ruled Ethiopia after the 1974 revolution and became associated with political repression and mass violence.

The period commonly called the “Red Terror” refers to campaigns of arrests, executions, and intimidation directed at perceived political opponents.

## Prosecution of Derg officials

After the fall of the Derg in 1991, Ethiopian courts pursued large-scale prosecutions of former officials.

These proceedings were notable for their scope and duration, and they are often cited as one of the most extensive domestic war-crimes efforts conducted on the African continent.

## Convictions and sentencing

In public summaries, Berhan Hailu is described as a Derg-era official convicted in connection with crimes against humanity and/or genocide charges tied to the Red Terror.

Such convictions generally reflect findings that senior officials bore responsibility through command authority, policy roles, or participation in state security structures.

## Historical significance

The Derg trials are frequently discussed in transitional-justice literature as a case study in:

- Domestic accountability for mass atrocities.
- The challenges of evidentiary reconstruction after regime change.
- The balance between political stabilization and legal reckoning.
