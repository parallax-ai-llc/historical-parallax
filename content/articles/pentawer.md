## Summary

Egyptian prince who conspired to murder his father Ramesses III and was forced to commit suicide.

## Context: The “Harem Conspiracy”

Pentawer is primarily known from the court records surrounding the so‑called Harem Conspiracy during the reign of **Ramesses III** (20th Dynasty, late New Kingdom). In these accounts:

- He is described as a **royal son**.
- The conspiracy is commonly associated with **Tiye** (a secondary wife of Ramesses III), with the alleged aim of **replacing the reigning king** and elevating Pentawer.

## Trial and Aftermath

Ancient Egyptian documents describe a formal legal process following the alleged plot.

- The proceedings are preserved in a set of judicial texts often grouped under the **Turin Judicial Papyrus** tradition (with related papyri and fragments).
- Multiple officials and household members were accused, including figures connected to the royal harem and court.
- Punishments vary by participant in the surviving record; Pentawer is one of the individuals described as being **forced to take his own life**.

## Historical Significance

Pentawer’s case is frequently cited because it provides a rare, detailed glimpse into:

- Palace politics in the late 20th Dynasty.
- How a high-level conspiracy was investigated and prosecuted.
- The use of judicial commissions and written records to formalize verdicts.

## Notes on Identification (Modern Scholarship)

Some modern studies have proposed a possible link between Pentawer and an unidentified royal mummy sometimes labeled **“Unknown Man E.”** This identification is **not universally accepted** and depends on interpreting a mix of archaeological context, imaging findings, and genetic analyses reported in the literature.
