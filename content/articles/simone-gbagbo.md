## Summary

Former first lady of Ivory Coast convicted by an Ivorian court of crimes against the state during the 2010-11 post-election crisis. She organized pro-Gbagbo militias that killed hundreds.

## Political Role

Simone Gbagbo was a prominent political figure in her own right as well as the spouse of President Laurent Gbagbo. Sources describe her as influential within pro-Gbagbo networks during a period marked by civil conflict, contested elections, and factional violence.

## 2010–2011 Post-Election Crisis (Context)

Côte d’Ivoire’s 2010 presidential election produced a disputed outcome and competing claims to power. The crisis escalated into violence involving security forces, militias, and armed groups aligned with different political camps. International actors, including the United Nations and regional organizations, became heavily involved in mediation and stabilization efforts.

## Criminal Proceedings and Legal Controversy

Public reporting on Simone Gbagbo often centers on questions of accountability and jurisdiction:

- **Domestic prosecution**: she was tried and convicted in Côte d’Ivoire for crimes connected to the crisis.
- **International Criminal Court (ICC)**: the ICC issued allegations/charges related to crimes against humanity, and Ivorian authorities faced pressure regarding transfer and cooperation.
- **Debate**: critics argued that domestic proceedings were either insufficient or politicized, while others emphasized sovereignty and local judicial capacity.

## Amnesty and Later Developments

In 2018, she received an amnesty, part of broader political steps aimed at reconciliation after years of instability. Amnesty and pardons in post-conflict settings are frequently contested because they may reduce immediate political tensions while also limiting formal accountability for victims.

## Legacy

Simone Gbagbo remains a polarizing figure in Ivorian politics and international discussions of transitional justice. Her case is often cited when comparing:

- domestic trials vs. international tribunals,
- reconciliation policies vs. prosecution,
- the role of political elites in mass-violence narratives.
