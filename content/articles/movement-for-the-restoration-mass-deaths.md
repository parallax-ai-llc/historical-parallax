## Summary

Mass killing of over 700 members of the Movement for the Restoration of the Ten Commandments of God in Kanungu, Uganda on March 17, 2000. Members were locked in a church that was set ablaze.

## Background

The Movement for the Restoration of the Ten Commandments of God was a Ugandan religious movement often described as an apocalyptic sect. It drew followers in the 1990s amid social and economic strain in the Great Lakes region, and it emphasized strict moral rules and end-times expectations.

## Events in 2000

The deaths in Kanungu in March 2000 are commonly summarized as a large-scale lethal incident involving fire at a church-like building used by the group. Accounts frequently note that victims were found inside the structure when it burned, and subsequent investigations reported additional bodies at other sites, including mass graves.

Because evidence emerged over time and some leadership figures were unaccounted for, the incident has been discussed in terms of competing explanations, including:

- Mass suicide in an apocalyptic context.
- Coordinated killing of followers by leaders or factions.

## Aftermath and Investigation

Ugandan authorities investigated the incident and related sites. The discovery of bodies beyond the initial fire location increased public attention and raised questions about planning, coercion, and criminal responsibility.

The case is frequently referenced in discussions of how isolated communities can be vulnerable to manipulation, as well as the difficulty of investigating mass-fatality events where records are limited and key individuals may be missing.

## Legacy

The Kanungu deaths remain one of the most widely cited mass-fatality incidents associated with a new religious movement in modern African history, and it is often used as a cautionary example in studies of charismatic authority, group isolation, and apocalyptic belief systems.
