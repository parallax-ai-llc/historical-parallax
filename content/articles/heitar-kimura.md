## Summary

Japanese general convicted for atrocities committed during the Burma campaign including murder and mistreatment of POWs on the Burma Railway. Hanged as Class A war criminal.

## Additional Context

- Kimura was a career officer in the Imperial Japanese Army, serving in artillery and staff roles before rising to general rank.
- He is associated with Japan's wartime command structure in Southeast Asia during World War II, particularly in Burma.

## Burma Campaign and Burma Railway (High-Level)

- The Burma campaign involved harsh logistics, disease, and sustained fighting, conditions that intensified violence toward civilians and prisoners.
- The Burma Railway project is remembered for extreme brutality and high mortality among forced laborers and Allied prisoners of war.
- Command responsibility in such theaters is often assessed not only by direct orders but also by what commanders knew (or should have known) and failed to prevent or punish.

## Trial and Execution

- Kimura was tried by the International Military Tribunal for the Far East (Tokyo Trials) and convicted.
- He was sentenced to death and executed by hanging at Sugamo Prison in 1948.

## Why He Matters (High-Level)

- Kimura's conviction is frequently cited in discussions of command responsibility for war crimes.
- His case illustrates how senior military leaders could be held legally accountable for systemic abuse occurring under their authority, even when carried out by subordinate units.

## Notes

- In many summaries, his wartime role is discussed alongside other senior commanders prosecuted after World War II to show how international tribunals attempted to define individual liability for state violence.
