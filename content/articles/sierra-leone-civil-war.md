## Summary

Brutal civil war from 1991-2002 fueled by blood diamonds that killed 50,000 people. The RUF rebels were notorious for amputating limbs and using child soldiers.

## Additional Context

### Origins and Drivers

The war is commonly linked to a mix of weak state institutions, corruption, regional spillover from neighboring conflicts, and competition over valuable natural resources (especially diamonds). The phrase “blood diamonds” became a shorthand for how armed groups financed operations through illicit extraction and trade.

### Major Armed Actors

The Revolutionary United Front (RUF) was the best-known insurgent group, but the conflict involved shifting alliances among government forces, militia groups, and regional actors. The fluidity of loyalties and command structures made negotiated settlements fragile.

### International and Regional Intervention

Regional forces intervened at different stages, and the United Nations deployed a large peacekeeping mission (UNAMSIL). The United Kingdom also intervened militarily in 2000, an action widely credited with stabilizing the capital and reinforcing the peace process.

### Human Rights Abuses and Accountability

The conflict featured widespread atrocities, including violence against civilians and the recruitment and use of child soldiers. After the war, transitional justice efforts included the Special Court for Sierra Leone, which prosecuted leading figures for war crimes and related offenses.

### End of War and Aftermath

The war was declared over in 2002 following disarmament and elections. Postwar Sierra Leone faced long-term challenges including demobilization and reintegration, rebuilding governance, and addressing trauma and social fragmentation caused by the conflict.
