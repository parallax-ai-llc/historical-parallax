## Summary

William Tecumseh Sherman (1820-1891) was one of the most influential and controversial military commanders in American history. As a general in the Union Army during the Civil War, he pioneered the concept of "total war" through his devastating March to the Sea campaign, which destroyed Confederate infrastructure and civilian morale. His military innovations earned him recognition as "the first modern general" by military historians, yet his scorched-earth tactics remain deeply controversial. Sherman's legacy is defined by both his strategic brilliance and the harshness of his methods, making him a figure of admiration and condemnation depending on historical perspective.[^1][^2]

---

## Early Life and Background

William Tecumseh Sherman was born on February 8, 1820, in Lancaster, Ohio, the sixth of eleven children born to Charles Robert Sherman and Mary Hoyt Sherman. His father, Charles, served as a justice of the Ohio Supreme Court, providing the family with social prominence in frontier Ohio. According to Sherman's own memoirs, he received his distinctive middle name because his father "caught a fancy for the great chief of the Shawnees, 'Tecumseh,'" the legendary Native American leader who had fought against American expansion.[^3][^4]

Sherman's comfortable childhood was shattered in 1829 when his father died suddenly, leaving the family in financial hardship. The nine-year-old boy was taken in by Thomas Ewing, a prominent Republican politician, attorney, and family friend who would later serve as U.S. Senator and Secretary of the Interior. Sherman was raised alongside Ewing's own children, including Ellen Boyle Ewing, who would later become his wife. Despite the kindness of the Ewing family, Sherman later reflected on the pain of being separated from his biological siblings and the sense of displacement that marked his youth.[^5][^6]

Living in the Ewing household provided Sherman with educational opportunities and political connections that would prove invaluable. Thomas Ewing's influence secured Sherman an appointment to the United States Military Academy at West Point in 1836. Sherman excelled academically at the academy, graduating sixth in the class of 1840. His performance demonstrated his intellectual capabilities and attention to detail, traits that would define his military career. During his time at West Point, Sherman developed friendships with cadets who would later become both his allies and adversaries during the Civil War, including George H. Thomas, who fought for the Union, and several officers who would join the Confederacy.[^7][^8]

After graduation, Sherman was commissioned as a second lieutenant and assigned to the Third Artillery in Florida, where he participated in the final stages of the Second Seminole War. However, he saw limited combat action during this period, spending much of his early military career at various garrison posts throughout the South. This experience gave Sherman intimate knowledge of Southern geography, culture, and society—knowledge that would prove crucial during the Civil War. Sherman's early military service was marked by frustration at the slow pace of peacetime promotion and the tedium of garrison life, sentiments common among ambitious young officers of his generation.[^9][^10]

## Rise to Power

Sherman's path to prominence was neither linear nor assured. In 1850, he married Ellen Boyle Ewing, his foster sister, in a Washington, D.C. ceremony attended by President Zachary Taylor and Vice President Millard Fillmore, reflecting the political connections of the Ewing family. Ellen was a devout Catholic, and the couple would raise their eight children in that faith, though Sherman himself remained skeptical of organized religion throughout his life.[^11][^12]

Frustrated by limited advancement opportunities in the peacetime army, Sherman resigned his commission in 1853 to pursue civilian business ventures. He moved to San Francisco to work as a banker during the California Gold Rush, but his bank failed during the Panic of 1857. Sherman then attempted to practice law in Leavenworth, Kansas, but found himself unsuited to the profession. In 1859, he accepted the position of superintendent of the Louisiana State Seminary of Learning and Military Academy (now Louisiana State University), where he proved to be an effective administrator and developed genuine affection for the South and its people.[^13][^14]

When Louisiana seceded from the Union in January 1861, Sherman resigned his position despite being offered a senior command in the Confederate Army. His loyalty to the Union was unwavering, though he respected many Southerners personally and understood their perspective. Sherman headed north and was commissioned as a colonel of the 13th United States Infantry. His performance at the First Battle of Bull Run (Manassas) in July 1861 impressed President Abraham Lincoln, who promoted him to brigadier general on August 7, 1861.[^15][^16]

Sherman's early Civil War service was marked by a serious setback. In Kentucky in late 1861, Sherman suffered what would now be recognized as a nervous breakdown, expressing pessimism about Union prospects and making exaggerated estimates of Confederate strength. Newspapers reported that Sherman had become insane, and he was temporarily relieved of command. This crisis might have ended his career, but Sherman received crucial support from two sources: his wife Ellen and General Ulysses S. Grant. Sherman recovered and was reassigned to Grant's command, beginning a partnership that would change the course of the war.[^17][^18]

At the Battle of Shiloh in April 1862, Sherman commanded a division under Grant and fought with distinction despite being wounded and having three horses shot from under him. His performance redeemed his reputation and demonstrated his courage under fire. Sherman became one of Grant's most trusted subordinates, and the two developed a working relationship based on mutual respect and complementary skills. Sherman later famously said, "Grant stood by me when I was crazy, and I stood by him when he was drunk, and now we stand by each other."[^19][^20]

When Grant was promoted to general-in-chief of all Union armies in March 1864, Sherman assumed command of the Military Division of the Mississippi, making him responsible for Union operations in the Western Theater. This promotion placed Sherman in position to execute the strategic vision that would make him famous—and infamous.[^21]

## Major Achievements

Sherman's greatest military achievements came during the final two years of the Civil War, when he commanded Union forces in a series of campaigns that devastated the Confederacy's ability to wage war. His strategic approach emphasized mobility, logistics, and psychological warfare, pioneering tactics that would influence military thinking for generations.[^22]

### The Atlanta Campaign

In May 1864, Sherman launched his campaign to capture Atlanta, the South's industrial and transportation hub. Commanding three armies totaling approximately 100,000 men under George Henry Thomas, James B. McPherson, and John M. Schofield, Sherman faced Confederate forces under General Joseph E. Johnston. Rather than engage in costly frontal assaults, Sherman consistently used his superior numbers to outflank Confederate positions, forcing Johnston to retreat southward. This strategy minimized Union casualties while steadily advancing toward Atlanta.[^23][^24]

Confederate President Jefferson Davis, frustrated by Johnston's defensive strategy, replaced him with the aggressive General John Bell Hood in July 1864. Hood launched a series of attacks against Sherman's forces, but these offensives resulted in heavy Confederate casualties without stopping the Union advance. After a lengthy siege and the destruction of Atlanta's rail connections, Hood evacuated the city on September 1, 1864. Sherman's forces occupied Atlanta the following day.[^25]

The capture of Atlanta had profound military and political consequences. It secured Abraham Lincoln's reelection in November 1864, as Northern voters had been losing faith in the war effort before Sherman's victory. The fall of Atlanta demonstrated that the Confederacy could be defeated and gave the Union the will to continue the fight. Sherman's success in the Atlanta Campaign established him as one of the Union's premier commanders.[^26][^27]

### Sherman's March to the Sea

Sherman's most famous—and controversial—achievement began on November 15, 1864, when he led approximately 62,000 troops out of Atlanta toward the Atlantic coast. Sherman had proposed an audacious plan: to march through Georgia without supply lines, living off the land while destroying everything of military value. Grant and Lincoln approved the strategy, understanding that breaking the South's economic infrastructure and civilian morale was essential to ending the war.[^28][^29]

During the march, Sherman's army covered 285 miles in about a month, from Atlanta to Savannah. His forces were divided into two wings that moved on parallel routes, creating a path of destruction 60 miles wide. Union soldiers destroyed railroads, burned warehouses, confiscated livestock and food supplies, and demolished anything that could support the Confederate war effort. Sherman's orders prohibited violence against civilians, but property destruction was systematic and extensive. The psychological impact was immense, as the Union Army marched virtually unopposed through the heart of the Confederacy.[^30][^31]

On December 21, 1864, Sherman's forces captured Savannah without a siege, and he famously telegraphed President Lincoln: "I beg to present you as a Christmas gift the City of Savannah, with one hundred and fifty guns and plenty of ammunition, also about twenty-five thousand bales of cotton." The March to the Sea demonstrated that the Confederacy could no longer protect its own territory and shattered Southern morale. Military historians consider it an early example of total war—warfare aimed not just at enemy armies but at the economic and psychological foundations of the enemy's ability to fight.[^32][^33]

### The Carolinas Campaign

In early 1865, Sherman turned north and marched through South Carolina and North Carolina. His troops were even more destructive in South Carolina, which they blamed for starting the war through secession. The capital, Columbia, was partially burned, though whether this resulted from Sherman's orders or accidental fires remains disputed. Sherman's Carolinas Campaign further demonstrated the Confederacy's helplessness and hastened the war's end.[^34][^35]

On April 26, 1865, Sherman accepted the surrender of General Joseph E. Johnston and all Confederate forces in Georgia, Florida, and the Carolinas—the largest surrender of Confederate troops during the war. Sherman initially offered generous terms that included political provisions, but these were rejected by the federal government. Sherman then negotiated a purely military surrender similar to Grant's terms at Appomattox.[^36]

### Post-War Military Career

After the war, Sherman continued to serve in the U.S. Army with distinction. When Grant became President in 1869, Sherman was promoted to Commanding General of the United States Army, a position he held until his retirement in 1883. During this period, Sherman oversaw the Indian Wars in the West and the Army's modernization efforts. His administrative skills and strategic thinking shaped the post-Civil War Army, though his approach to Native American policy remains controversial.[^37][^38]

Sherman published his memoirs in 1875, which became one of the most important primary sources for understanding the Civil War from a Union military perspective. The book was commercially successful and established Sherman as a major literary figure as well as a military leader.[^39]

## Controversies and Criticisms

William Tecumseh Sherman remains one of the most controversial figures in American history, with debates about his legacy continuing more than a century after his death. His tactics during the Civil War, particularly his employment of total war, have generated fierce criticism alongside military praise.[^40]

### Total War and Civilian Suffering

Sherman's deliberate targeting of civilian economic resources represented a departure from traditional 19th-century military practice, which generally sought to distinguish between combatants and non-combatants. Sherman explicitly rejected this distinction, writing that "We are not only fighting armies, but a hostile people, and must make old and young, rich and poor, feel the hard hand of war." He believed that breaking civilian morale was essential to Union victory and that harsh measures would ultimately shorten the war and save lives.[^41][^42]

Critics argue that Sherman's campaigns caused immense suffering among Southern civilians, particularly women, children, and enslaved people. While his orders prohibited violence against persons, the destruction of homes, food supplies, and livestock left many civilians destitute. Some scholars have argued that Sherman's tactics anticipated the total wars of the 20th century, including the strategic bombing campaigns of World War II.[^43][^44]

### War Crimes Allegations

Some Confederate sympathizers and Southern historians have accused Sherman of war crimes, pointing to the widespread destruction his armies caused and allegations of looting and assault. Confederate organizations have documented claims that Sherman's troops committed atrocities against civilians, though many of these claims are disputed or exaggerated.[^45][^46]

Most mainstream historians reject the characterization of Sherman as a war criminal. They note that casualty figures among Southern civilians during Sherman's campaigns were remarkably low, suggesting his troops generally followed orders to avoid violence against persons. The destruction was primarily directed at property with military or economic value. Furthermore, the concept of "war crimes" with specific legal definitions did not exist during the Civil War era; these terms only came into common usage after World War I.[^47][^48]

Historian Wesley Moody has argued that later commentators filtered Sherman's actions through 20th-century concepts of warfare, contributing to exaggerations of his atrocities. Brian Holden-Reid and other modern scholars have challenged negative portrayals, arguing that Sherman's hard-war strategy was a legitimate military approach designed to end a brutal conflict more quickly.[^49]

### Initial Surrender Terms Controversy

Sherman's first surrender agreement with Confederate General Joseph E. Johnston in April 1865 included political provisions that went beyond purely military matters, effectively offering amnesty and recognizing existing Southern state governments. The federal government swiftly rejected these terms, and Secretary of War Edwin Stanton publicly criticized Sherman, suggesting his judgment was impaired. Sherman was deeply angered by this criticism and his relationship with Stanton remained hostile. The incident demonstrated Sherman's political naivety and his tendency to exceed his authority.[^50]

### Treatment of Native Americans

During his post-war service as Commanding General of the Army, Sherman oversaw military operations against Native American tribes in the West. He supported policies of forced relocation and expressed views about Native Americans that reflected the racist attitudes common in his era. Sherman advocated aggressive military action against tribes that resisted U.S. expansion, and his approach to Native American policy is now widely condemned as part of a genocidal campaign against indigenous peoples.[^51]

### Southern Historical Memory

In the former Confederacy, particularly in Georgia and the Carolinas, Sherman's name became synonymous with Northern aggression and devastation. "Lost Cause" historiography portrayed Sherman as a war criminal who waged war against innocent civilians. This negative portrayal influenced Southern historical memory for generations and contributed to regional antagonism that persisted long after the war ended. Monuments and historical markers throughout the South still reflect this perspective, presenting Sherman as a villain who destroyed Southern civilization.[^52]

## Personal Life

William Tecumseh Sherman's personal life was marked by deep family bonds, religious tensions, periods of mental struggle, and complex relationships that shaped his character beyond his military achievements.[^53]

Sherman married Ellen Boyle Ewing on May 1, 1850, in a ceremony that reflected the Ewing family's political prominence. Ellen was four years and eight months younger than Sherman and had been raised alongside him in her father's household. Their relationship was affectionate but complicated by fundamental differences. Ellen was a devout Catholic who hoped Sherman would convert, while Sherman remained skeptical of organized religion throughout his life, though he respected Ellen's faith. This religious difference created ongoing tension but did not undermine their essential partnership.[^54][^55]

The Shermans had eight children: Maria Boyle Ewing ("Minnie"), Mary Elizabeth ("Lizzie"), William T. ("Willy") Jr., Thomas Ewing, Eleanor Mary ("Elly"), Rachel Ewing, Charles Celestine, and Philemon Tecumseh ("Cumpy"). Tragically, two children died during the Civil War years. Willie, Sherman's favorite son, died of typhoid fever in October 1863 while visiting his father's camp in Mississippi. The boy's death devastated Sherman and was one of the most painful experiences of his life. His son Charles also died during this period. Sherman was a devoted father when military service permitted, and his surviving children remained important to him throughout his life.[^56][^57]

Sherman's personality combined intellectual brilliance with nervous energy and emotional volatility. He spoke rapidly, often moving from topic to topic, and had difficulty sitting still. Modern psychologists have speculated that Sherman might have suffered from what would now be diagnosed as anxiety disorder or bipolar disorder, particularly given his 1861 breakdown in Kentucky. Throughout his life, Sherman experienced periods of deep pessimism alternating with intense energy and confidence. He was acutely sensitive to criticism, particularly from newspapers, which he despised for their inaccuracies and sensationalism.[^58]

Despite his military reputation, Sherman was uncomfortable with violence and bloodshed. He frequently expressed his hatred of war, writing "I am tired and sick of war. Its glory is all moonshine." This sentiment was genuine; Sherman viewed war as a terrible necessity, not as a glorious adventure. His famous statement "War is hell" reflected his authentic understanding of warfare's brutality, not mere rhetoric.[^59][^60]

Ellen Sherman died in 1888, plunging Sherman into depression. His children and friends kept him active during this period, helping to blunt the sadness, but Sherman never fully recovered from the loss of his wife. He died three years later, on February 14, 1891, in New York City, and was buried in Calvary Cemetery in St. Louis, Missouri, near Ellen.[^61]

## Legacy

William Tecumseh Sherman's legacy is extraordinarily complex, encompassing military innovation, moral controversy, and lasting influence on warfare and American historical memory. His impact extends far beyond the Civil War era, shaping military doctrine and strategic thinking into the modern age.[^62]

### Military Innovation and Influence

British military historian B.H. Liddell Hart ranked Sherman as "the first modern general" and one of the most important strategists in military history. Liddell Hart argued that Sherman understood warfare in ways that anticipated 20th-century conflict, particularly the importance of mobility, logistics, and psychological warfare. Sherman's March to the Sea demonstrated that a modern army could sustain itself by living off enemy territory while simultaneously destroying the enemy's economic infrastructure and civilian will to resist.[^63][^64]

Sherman's influence extended to military theorists and practitioners worldwide. Heinz Guderian, the German general who created and led the panzer forces in World War II, stated that he derived techniques from Liddell Hart's writings, which themselves drew heavily on studying Sherman's operations. Sherman's emphasis on operational mobility and deep penetration into enemy territory influenced the development of armored warfare and combined arms tactics in the 20th century.[^65]

Military scholars emphasize Sherman's understanding that modern democratic warfare depends on civilian morale and economic capacity as much as military strength. His campaigns deliberately targeted these factors, recognizing that breaking the Southern people's will to fight was as important as defeating Confederate armies. This insight, while controversial, proved strategically sound and influenced subsequent military thinking about the nature of modern war.[^66]

### Continuing Controversy

Sherman's reputation remains deeply divisive. In the South, particularly in Georgia and the Carolinas, his name is still associated with destruction and trauma. The Lost Cause mythology portrayed Sherman as a war criminal who waged barbaric warfare against civilians, and this interpretation influenced Southern historical memory for generations. Even today, debates about Sherman's tactics and legacy can provoke strong emotional reactions.[^67]

Conversely, many Northern and academic historians view Sherman as a brilliant strategist who employed harsh but effective methods to end a devastating war. They argue that his total war approach actually minimized overall casualties by shortening the conflict, and that property destruction, while severe, caused relatively few civilian deaths. From this perspective, Sherman's willingness to wage hard war demonstrated moral courage and strategic vision rather than cruelty.[^68]

### Scholarly Reassessment

Modern scholarship has generally moved toward more balanced assessments of Sherman's legacy. Historians like Wesley Moody have challenged exaggerated accounts of atrocities, while scholars such as John F. Marszalek, Victor Davis Hanson, and Brian Holden-Reid have emphasized Sherman's principled approach to warfare within the constraints of his era. These scholars argue that Sherman's methods, while harsh, adhered to legitimate military objectives and avoided gratuitous violence.[^69][^70]

At the same time, contemporary historians acknowledge the immense suffering Sherman's campaigns caused and the problematic aspects of his legacy, including his role in Native American policy. The scholarly consensus recognizes Sherman as a complex figure whose military brilliance cannot be separated from the moral questions his tactics raise about the limits of acceptable warfare.[^71]

### Civil Rights and Reconstruction

Sherman's views on race and Reconstruction evolved over time. During the war, he was primarily focused on military objectives rather than emancipation, and he expressed conventional racist attitudes of his era. However, near the end of his life in 1888, Sherman published an essay defending the full civil rights of Black citizens in the former Confederacy, calling upon the South to "let the negro vote, and count his vote honestly." This evolution suggests a capacity for growth, though Sherman never became a strong advocate for racial equality.[^72]

### Cultural Impact

Sherman's legacy permeates American culture. His name remains synonymous with total war and scorched-earth tactics. The phrase "War is hell," attributed to Sherman, has entered common usage as a succinct expression of warfare's brutal reality. His memoirs remain essential reading for understanding the Civil War, and countless books, articles, and documentaries have examined his life and campaigns.[^73]

The debate over Sherman reflects broader American divisions about the Civil War's meaning and legacy. How one views Sherman often correlates with how one interprets the war itself—as a noble crusade against slavery, a tragic conflict between Americans, or a Northern invasion of Southern sovereignty. Sherman's legacy thus serves as a lens through which Americans continue to grapple with their most divisive historical event.[^74]

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Admirers of William Tecumseh Sherman emphasize his strategic brilliance, operational innovation, and pragmatic approach to ending the Civil War. Military historians, particularly those focused on the evolution of modern warfare, consistently rank Sherman among history's greatest commanders.[^75]

B.H. Liddell Hart's influential assessment positioned Sherman as a pioneering military thinker whose operations anticipated modern mechanized warfare. Liddell Hart argued that Sherman understood the principle of the "indirect approach"—achieving victory by disrupting enemy logistics, communications, and morale rather than seeking decisive battle. This approach, Liddell Hart contended, was more effective and ultimately more humane than the frontal assaults favored by many Civil War commanders, which produced horrific casualties with limited strategic results.[^76]

Victor Davis Hanson, the military historian and classicist, has praised Sherman's clear-eyed understanding of war's nature and his willingness to pursue victory through whatever legal means necessary. Hanson argues that Sherman's hard war shortened the conflict and prevented the emergence of a prolonged guerrilla insurgency that could have killed far more people. From this perspective, Sherman's harsh tactics were a form of moral realism—recognizing that half-measures would only prolong suffering.[^77]

John F. Marszalek, Sherman's most comprehensive biographer, presents him as a complex, often misunderstood figure whose actions must be understood within their historical context. Marszalek emphasizes Sherman's genuine hatred of war, his concern for his soldiers' welfare, and his belief that harsh measures would ultimately save lives by ending the conflict decisively. Marszalek's work humanizes Sherman while acknowledging his flaws and the legitimate criticisms of his methods.[^78]

Military professionals have consistently recognized Sherman's contributions to logistics and operational planning. His ability to move large armies across difficult terrain while maintaining supply lines (or deliberately operating without them during the March to the Sea) demonstrated exceptional organizational skills. His coordination of multiple army corps moving on separate routes required sophisticated planning and communication that impressed contemporary observers and later military theorists.[^79]

Defenders of Sherman also note that his campaigns, despite their destructive nature, were characterized by remarkably low civilian casualties. His orders explicitly prohibited violence against persons, and while these orders were not perfectly enforced, the overall discipline of Union forces during the March to the Sea was notable given the circumstances. This suggests Sherman maintained ethical boundaries even while waging total war against property and economic resources.[^80]

### Negative Views

Critics of Sherman focus on the immense suffering his campaigns caused, the moral problems inherent in total war, and the long-term damage to North-South relations. These critiques come from multiple perspectives, including Lost Cause sympathizers, anti-war advocates, and scholars concerned with the ethics of warfare.[^81]

John B. Walters's "Merchant of Terror: General Sherman and Total War" (1973) presented Sherman as responsible for "a mode of warfare which transgressed all ethical rules and showed an utter disregard for human rights and dignity." Walters argued that Sherman's deliberate targeting of civilian economic resources crossed moral boundaries and established dangerous precedents for future conflicts. This interpretation views Sherman as a pioneer of the total warfare that characterized 20th-century conflicts, including the strategic bombing campaigns of World War II.[^82]

James Reston Jr., in his 1984 work, drew explicit connections between Sherman's tactics and later American military campaigns, arguing that Sherman planted "the seed for the Agent Orange and Agent Blue programs of food deprivation in Vietnam." This critique positions Sherman's legacy within a broader pattern of American military willingness to inflict suffering on civilian populations in pursuit of strategic objectives.[^83]

Confederate heritage organizations and Lost Cause historians have maintained that Sherman was essentially a war criminal who terrorized innocent civilians. These critiques, while often historically problematic and motivated by Confederate apologetics, reflect genuine trauma experienced by Southern civilians during Sherman's campaigns. The destruction of homes, confiscation of food, and economic devastation caused real hardship that affected generations.[^84]

Some modern commentators sympathetic to the Confederate cause have characterized Sherman's approach as proto-totalitarian, arguing that it foreshadowed the mass violence and civilian targeting of 20th-century conflicts. They contend that Sherman's willingness to wage war against civilian populations represented a moral failing that cannot be justified by military necessity or strategic effectiveness.[^85]

Anti-war critics focus on Sherman's famous statements about war's nature, arguing that his acknowledgment that "war is hell" should have led him to reject total war tactics rather than embrace them. From this perspective, Sherman's intellectual understanding of war's cruelty makes his willingness to inflict that cruelty on civilians more morally problematic, not less.[^86]

Scholars concerned with the ethics of warfare note that Sherman's campaigns established precedents that later military leaders used to justify increasingly destructive tactics. While Sherman's property destruction may have been restrained by 20th-century standards, critics argue that he helped normalize the concept that civilian economic resources are legitimate military targets, opening the door to progressively more extensive targeting of civilian populations.[^87]

## Famous Quotes

> "War is cruelty, and you cannot refine it; and those who brought war into our country deserve all the curses and maledictions a people can pour out." - From letter to Atlanta city officials, September 1864, explaining his evacuation order[^88]

> "There is many a boy here today who looks on war as all glory, but, boys, it is all hell." - Address at Michigan Military Academy, June 19, 1879, origin of the famous "War is hell" quote[^89]

> "I confess, without shame, that I am sick and tired of fighting—its glory is all moonshine; even success the most brilliant is over dead and mangled bodies, with the anguish and lamentations of distant families, appealing to me for sons, husbands, and fathers ... it is only those who have neither fired a shot nor heard the shrieks and groans of the wounded who cry aloud for blood, more vengeance, more desolation." - Letter written in 1864, expressing his authentic views on warfare[^90]

> "Grant stood by me when I was crazy, and I stood by him when he was drunk, and now we stand by each other." - Reflecting on his partnership with Ulysses S. Grant[^91]

> "We are not only fighting armies, but a hostile people, and must make old and young, rich and poor, feel the hard hand of war." - Justifying his total war strategy[^92]

> "I beg to present you as a Christmas gift the City of Savannah, with one hundred and fifty heavy guns and plenty of ammunition, also about twenty-five thousand bales of cotton." - Telegram to President Abraham Lincoln, December 22, 1864[^93]

> "I hereby state, and mean all I say, that I never have been and never will be a candidate for President; that if nominated by either party, I should peremptorily decline; and even if unanimously elected I should decline to serve." - Refusing political pressure to run for President in 1884[^94]
