## Summary

Mas Oyama founded Kyokushin karate and was famous for fighting bulls barehanded. He built one of the world's largest martial arts organizations.

## Kyokushin karate

Kyokushin is typically described as a full-contact style emphasizing physical conditioning, powerful strikes, and a culture of rigorous training. The organization built around Oyama contributed to the international spread of Japanese karate after World War II.

A hallmark of Kyokushin competition is its ruleset, which historically emphasized continuous fighting and durability. While details vary across organizations and eras, the broader identity is strongly tied to realism and toughness.

## Public persona and stories

Oyama's reputation includes dramatic demonstrations and stories intended to showcase strength and fighting spirit, including accounts of bouts with bulls. Such stories have become part of martial arts folklore, and are often discussed alongside the broader tradition of public demonstrations used to promote schools and styles.

## Organizational legacy

As Kyokushin grew, it developed a global network of dojos and tournaments. After Oyama's death, Kyokushin organizations experienced splits and reorganizations, a common pattern in large martial arts lineages when central leadership transitions.

## Influence

Many notable fighters in kickboxing and mixed striking sports trained in Kyokushin or were influenced by its conditioning and sparring culture. In modern martial arts discussion, Oyama's legacy is often framed as bridging traditional dojo practice with competitive, full-contact formats.

## Enduring identity

Kyokushin remains a recognizable global brand within karate, and "Osu" culture and hard training are often cited as defining elements of the community associated with Oyama.
