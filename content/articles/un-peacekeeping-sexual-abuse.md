## Summary

UN peacekeepers have committed thousands of sexual exploitation and abuse incidents across missions, including in Congo, Haiti, and CAR. Perpetrators are rarely held accountable.

## Terminology

The UN commonly uses the term **sexual exploitation and abuse (SEA)** to describe prohibited sexual conduct by UN personnel, including peacekeepers and civilian staff, in the context of UN operations.

## Why accountability is complex

Peacekeeping forces are contributed by member states. In many cases, criminal jurisdiction over uniformed personnel remains with the troop-contributing country, which can complicate investigations and prosecution.

## Reporting and documentation

Cases have been documented in multiple missions and regions over time. Reporting is affected by stigma, fear of retaliation, limited local legal capacity, and the practical barriers victims face in accessing UN mechanisms.

## Institutional responses

The UN has repeatedly announced reforms and policies aimed at prevention and enforcement, often described under a "zero tolerance" framework.
These measures have included vetting and training, conduct and discipline units, repatriation of contingents, and efforts to improve transparency around allegations.

## Victim assistance and criticism

Advocacy groups have argued that support for victims has been inconsistent and that compensation and remedies can be difficult to obtain.
The issue continues to shape debates about peacekeeping mandates, oversight, and how international organizations enforce standards across diverse national contingents.

## High-level takeaways

- Misconduct allegations can undermine local trust and operational legitimacy.
- Prevention requires both field-level enforcement and follow-through by member states.
- Transparency about outcomes is a recurring demand from journalists and civil-society monitors.
