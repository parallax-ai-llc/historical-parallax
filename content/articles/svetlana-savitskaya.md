## Summary

Svetlana Yevgenyevna Savitskaya is a Soviet cosmonaut and aviator who became the second woman in space and the first woman to perform a spacewalk. Flying aboard Soyuz T-7 in August 1982, she broke a 19-year gap since Valentina Tereshkova's 1963 flight and demonstrated that women could serve as fully capable mission specialists in the Soviet space program. Her second mission in July 1984 aboard Soyuz T-12 culminated in a 3-hour, 35-minute extravehicular activity (EVA), pre-empting NASA's plans to have an American woman conduct the first female spacewalk.

Beyond spaceflight, Savitskaya was an exceptional test pilot and aerobatics champion, setting over 18 world records in aviation. Her career bridged elite military aviation and the cosmonaut corps, making her one of the most accomplished aviators of the 20th century. She later entered Soviet and Russian politics, serving multiple terms in the State Duma.

## Early Life & Background

Svetlana Savitskaya was born on August 8, 1948, in Moscow, to Yevgeny Savitsky, a prominent Soviet Air Marshal and World War II ace. Growing up in a military aviation household gave her early exposure to aircraft and flying culture. She began parachute jumping at age 16 and pursued aviation with singular focus, enrolling in flight training programs while completing her formal education.

She graduated from the Moscow Aviation Institute and later earned qualifications as a test pilot from the Moscow Aviation Institute's flight school. Her academic and technical formation was deeply rooted in Soviet aerospace engineering, providing the theoretical foundation for her later piloting and cosmonaut careers.

## Rise to Prominence

Savitskaya made her name first in competitive aerobatics and high-altitude aviation. In 1970, she won the World Aerobatics Championship, becoming the absolute world champion. Throughout the early 1970s she set multiple FAI-recognized records in jet aircraft, including altitude, speed, and time-to-climb records in various aircraft classes — accumulating more than 18 world records in total.

She was selected for cosmonaut training in 1980 as part of a group intended to reassert Soviet leadership in crewed spaceflight amid growing American competition. Her test pilot credentials, logging over 1,500 hours across more than 20 aircraft types, made her a technically strong candidate.

## Key Achievements

Her first spaceflight, Soyuz T-7 (August 19–27, 1982), made her the second woman in space. She docked with the Salyut 7 space station and conducted scientific experiments over nearly eight days.

Her second mission, Soyuz T-12 (July 17–29, 1984), was the more historic. On July 25, 1984, Savitskaya and Vladimir Dzhanibekov conducted an EVA during which she used a hand-held electron beam welding tool to perform cutting, welding, and soldering tasks on the exterior of Salyut 7 — the first such manual fabrication work performed in open space. Her spacewalk lasted 3 hours and 35 minutes, making her the first woman to walk in space, beating NASA astronaut Kathryn Sullivan by roughly three months.

## Controversies & Criticism

Western observers and some historians have noted that the timing of Soviet female spaceflights appeared tied to propaganda objectives rather than purely merit-based selection, particularly given the long gap between Tereshkova's 1963 flight and Savitskaya's 1982 mission. Savitskaya herself has acknowledged the political dimensions of Soviet space scheduling without dismissing her own qualifications.

Her post-cosmonaut political career, including membership in the Communist Party of the Russian Federation and nationalistic public statements, has drawn criticism from liberal commentators in Russia and abroad.

## Legacy & Influence

Savitskaya's spacewalk directly accelerated NASA's timeline for female EVA participation and forced a broader reckoning with gender integration in spaceflight operations worldwide. She demonstrated that women could perform complex manual technical tasks in the extreme environment of open space, dismantling a key remaining argument against full female participation in crewed missions.

Her aviation records and aerobatics championship remain part of the historical benchmark for 20th-century Soviet sport aviation. She was awarded the Hero of the Soviet Union twice — in 1982 and 1984 — one of very few individuals to receive the honor for spaceflight achievements.
