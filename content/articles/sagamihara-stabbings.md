## Summary

Satoshi Uematsu broke into a care facility for disabled people and stabbed 19 residents to death. The deadliest mass killing in Japan since WWII, motivated by hatred of the disabled.

| Year | Event |

## Key Facts

- **Date:** 2016-07-26 (overnight hours)
- **Location:** Tsukui Yamayuri-en, a residential care facility for people with disabilities in Sagamihara, Kanagawa Prefecture, Japan
- **Victims:** 19 people killed; dozens injured
- **Perpetrator:** Satoshi Uematsu, a former employee of the facility
- **Weapons:** Primarily knives
- **Motive (as stated by the perpetrator):** Hatred toward people with disabilities and the belief they should not live

## Background

- The facility housed adults with intellectual and other disabilities.
- Uematsu had previously worked at the facility and was familiar with the building and routines.
- Months before the attack, he delivered a letter to political officials expressing extremist views about people with disabilities.

## What Happened

- Uematsu entered the facility at night, restrained staff, and moved room to room attacking residents.
- After the attack, he left the scene and turned himself in to police.
- The incident was widely described as Japan’s deadliest mass killing since World War II.

## Investigation and Legal Proceedings

- Uematsu was arrested shortly after surrendering.
- Prosecutors treated the incident as a mass murder case; reporting highlighted extensive evidence and a direct admission.
- In 2020, a Japanese court convicted Uematsu and sentenced him to death.

## Impact and Aftermath

- The killings prompted renewed discussion in Japan about disability rights, institutional care, and stigma.
- Disability advocacy groups emphasized the harm caused by dehumanizing language and social exclusion.
- The case is frequently referenced in debates about hate-motivated violence and the protection of vulnerable communities.
