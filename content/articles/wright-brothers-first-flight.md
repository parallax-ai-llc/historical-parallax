## Summary

On December 17, 1903, Wilbur and Orville Wright made the first sustained, controlled, powered heavier-than-air flight at Kitty Hawk, North Carolina. The longest flight lasted 59 seconds over 852 feet. Their achievement launched the age of aviation.

## Background

The Wright brothers approached flight as an engineering problem: stability and control mattered as much as power. Their early work with kites and gliders helped them test wing shapes, lift, and practical control surfaces before attempting powered flight.

## What Made the 1903 Flights Significant

- **Controlled flight**: The flights demonstrated sustained, controlled operation rather than short hops.
- **Heavier-than-air power**: The Flyer combined an engine with airframe and propellers designed as a system.
- **Repeatable trials**: Multiple attempts in one day provided clearer evidence than a single isolated moment.

## Immediate Aftermath

After the 1903 tests, the brothers continued iterating on design and piloting techniques, refining control and reliability. Subsequent work in 1904–1905 is often emphasized as crucial for turning a breakthrough into a practical airplane.

## Legacy

The Kitty Hawk flights became a cornerstone narrative in aviation history, influencing how engineers and institutions evaluated flight: controllability, documentation, and systematic experimentation became central to later aircraft development.

## Sources and Evidence

Accounts of the 1903 flights are often discussed alongside physical artifacts (such as photographs and surviving components) and contemporaneous reporting. The episode also illustrates how early technological milestones can be contested or reinterpreted depending on standards of proof and definitions of "first" achievement.
