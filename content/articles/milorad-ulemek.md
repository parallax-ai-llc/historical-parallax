## Summary

Serbian former special forces commander known as 'Legija' who masterminded the assassination of Prime Minister Zoran Đinđić in 2003. He led the organized crime unit behind the killing. Sentenced to 40 years.

## Background

- Known by the nickname **"Legija"**.
- Served in armed formations during the Yugoslav wars and later rose within Serbian special police and security structures.
- Became associated with the **Special Operations Unit (JSO / "Red Berets")**, an elite unit linked to Serbia’s state security apparatus during the era.

## Role in Serbian security milieu and organized crime narratives

- Post-2000 Serbia saw repeated claims and investigations about overlaps between parts of the security apparatus and criminal networks.
- In public reporting and in court proceedings, Ulemek’s name became tied to this broader theme: elite units, wartime veterans, and organized crime operating in a politically turbulent transition.

## Assassination of Zoran Đinđić (2003)

- Prime Minister **Zoran Đinđić** was shot and killed in Belgrade in March 2003.
- Serbian courts convicted Ulemek for organizing and coordinating the plot, with the attack treated as a direct strike on the post-Milošević reform project.
- The killing triggered a major crackdown on organized crime and rogue networks inside and around security services.

## Legal outcomes (Serbia)

- Ulemek received long prison sentences, including a **40-year** sentence tied to the Đinđić murder case.
- He was also convicted in other high-profile cases connected to political violence from the late 1990s and early 2000s, which are often discussed in the same historical arc as the Đinđić assassination.

## Historical significance

- His biography is frequently cited in scholarship and journalism as an example of how wartime paramilitary experience, intelligence-linked units, and criminal groups could intersect in the region.
- The Đinđić case remains one of the defining political-criminal cases in modern Serbian history.
