## Summary

Afghan ruler who invaded India repeatedly and carried out devastating raids. His forces massacred tens of thousands at the Third Battle of Panipat and plundered Hindu temples.

## Background

Ahmad Shah Abdali (also known as Ahmad Shah Durrani) emerged from the Abdali/Durrani Pashtun tribal confederation during the collapse of Persian and Mughal power in the region. In 1747 he was selected by Afghan tribal leaders and established what became known as the Durrani Empire, with centers of power in Kandahar and later Kabul.

## Expansion and Major Campaigns (High Level)

His reign is commonly characterized by repeated military campaigns west and east of Afghanistan that reshaped regional politics:

- **Khurasan and Persia:** Campaigns sought influence over territories contested after Nader Shah’s death.
- **Punjab and North India:** Repeated invasions aimed at tribute, control of strategic cities, and influence over declining Mughal authority.
- **Northwest frontier:** Fighting with local powers (including Sikh groups in Punjab) became a recurring feature of his later years.

## Governance and Rule

While often remembered for conquest, he also built institutions associated with state formation:

- Consolidation of authority through alliances with tribal elites.
- Revenue extraction and administration tied to military patronage.
- Use of strategic marriages, appointments, and negotiated settlements to stabilize rule in key regions.

## Legacy

Ahmad Shah is widely regarded in Afghan historiography as a founder of a modern Afghan polity due to the scale and durability of the Durrani state. At the same time, many communities in areas affected by his campaigns remember his invasions primarily through the lens of **war, mass violence, and plunder**, especially during raids in North India.

## Additional Notes

- Names and titles vary across sources (Abdali vs. Durrani) and reflect shifting tribal and dynastic identities.
- Accounts of casualties and specific atrocities differ by historian and contemporary chronicle, so aggregate figures should be treated cautiously.
