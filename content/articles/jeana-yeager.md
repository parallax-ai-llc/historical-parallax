## Summary

Jeana Yeager (born May 18, 1952) is an American aviator best known as co-pilot of the Voyager aircraft during the first nonstop, non-refueled circumnavigation of the globe in December 1986. Alongside pilot Dick Rutan and designer Burt Rutan, she helped accomplish one of the most demanding feats in aviation history, completing a 9-day flight that pushed both human endurance and aircraft engineering to their limits.

Her achievement came after years of meticulous preparation, technical training, and record-setting flights that established her credentials as one of the most capable aviators of her generation. Yeager's contributions to the Voyager project extended well beyond piloting — she was instrumental in planning, record attempts, and the grueling physical demands of the mission itself.

## Early Life & Background

Jeana Yeager was born on May 18, 1952, in Fort Worth, Texas. She developed an early interest in mechanical and technical pursuits and worked as a drafter and helicopter pilot before transitioning into fixed-wing aviation. Her background in precision work and her aptitude for engineering made her a natural fit for highly technical aviation projects. She earned her pilot's license and accumulated extensive flight hours before becoming involved with the Voyager project in the early 1980s.

## Rise to Prominence

Yeager came to prominence through her association with designer Burt Rutan and pilot Dick Rutan, whom she met in the early 1980s. The three formed the core team behind the Voyager project — an audacious plan to fly around the world without stopping or refueling. To prepare, Yeager and Dick Rutan set several world records in distance flying during the mid-1980s, demonstrating their capacity for long-duration flight and validating Voyager's design. She was co-pilot and a key project manager throughout the aircraft's construction and test program, logging thousands of hours of preparation before the record attempt.

## Key Achievements

On December 14, 1986, Voyager departed Edwards Air Force Base in California with Yeager and Dick Rutan aboard. Over nine days, three minutes, and 44 seconds, they flew 24,986 miles (40,212 kilometers) — completing the first nonstop, non-refueled circumnavigation of the Earth. The aircraft landed on December 23, 1986, with fuel almost entirely exhausted. The mission required both pilots to endure extreme sleep deprivation, cramped conditions inside a cockpit measuring roughly 7.5 feet long, and severe turbulence over the Pacific and Indian Oceans. In 1987, Yeager and Dick Rutan were awarded the Presidential Citizens Medal by President Ronald Reagan. Yeager also co-authored the book *Voyager* (1987) with Dick Rutan, detailing the journey.

## Controversies & Criticism

The Voyager project generated relatively little controversy, though the personal and professional relationship between Yeager and Dick Rutan — who had been romantically involved — created documented interpersonal tensions during the mission. Accounts from both principals acknowledged friction during the flight under extreme stress, though both credited mutual professionalism with the mission's success.

## Legacy & Influence

Yeager's accomplishment stands as a landmark in aviation history, closing one of the last great frontiers of atmospheric flight. The Voyager aircraft is permanently displayed at the Smithsonian National Air and Space Museum in Washington, D.C., alongside the Wright Flyer and Spirit of St. Louis. Her mission helped inspire a generation of aviators and aerospace engineers and demonstrated what purpose-built, fuel-efficient composite aircraft could achieve. Yeager remains a celebrated figure in general aviation and a symbol of what meticulous preparation and endurance can accomplish.
