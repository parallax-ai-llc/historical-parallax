## Summary

The Western Wall — known in Hebrew as the Kotel ha-Maaravi, and sometimes called the Wailing Wall — is a remnant of the ancient retaining wall that formed the western boundary of the expanded Temple Mount platform constructed by Herod the Great beginning around 19 BCE. It is the holiest site at which Jews are permitted to pray, the nearest accessible point to the Holy of Holies of the ancient Temple, and one of the most potent symbols of Jewish identity, faith, and historical memory.

The Wall is not part of the Temple itself but rather a remnant of the massive outer retaining wall that supported the enlarged Temple Mount platform. Nevertheless, because it is the closest accessible relic of the Temple complex, it has been the focal point of Jewish prayer and longing for nearly two thousand years. The site is located in the Jewish Quarter of the Old City of Jerusalem, operated by the Israeli Ministry of Religious Affairs, and accessible to visitors of all faiths.

Each year, millions of people visit the Wall — Jews to pray and observe lifecycle events, and visitors of all backgrounds to bear witness to one of the world's most emotionally charged religious sites. It is customary to insert small notes containing prayers and wishes into the crevices between the Wall's ancient stones, a practice that results in hundreds of thousands of written prayers being removed and reverently buried twice a year to make room for new ones.

## History & Origins

The Western Wall's history is inseparable from the history of the Temple Mount and the two Temples that once stood upon it. The First Temple, built by King Solomon around 957 BCE, was the dwelling place of the Ark of the Covenant and the center of Israelite worship. It was destroyed by the Babylonian King Nebuchadnezzar II in 586 BCE, and much of the Jewish population was taken into exile in Babylon.

The Return from Babylon and the construction of the Second Temple began around 538 BCE under the decree of Cyrus the Great of Persia. The Second Temple was completed around 516 BCE and served as the spiritual center of Judaism for over 500 years, though it was profaned by the Seleucid King Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BCE — an event commemorated in the Jewish festival of Hanukkah.

The most significant transformation of the Temple Mount came under Herod the Great, the Roman client king of Judea who ruled from 37 to 4 BCE. Herod undertook a massive expansion of the Temple and its surrounding platform, effectively doubling or tripling the area of the Temple Mount. To create a level platform on the sloping hill, enormous retaining walls were constructed on all four sides, filled in with dirt and stone. The western retaining wall of this Herodian expansion is the Western Wall that stands today.

Herod's Temple was one of the great architectural achievements of the ancient world. First-century Jewish historian Josephus described it in superlatives, and even the Roman author Pliny the Elder noted its magnificence. Herod's construction project employed tens of thousands of workers for decades and was not fully completed until 63 CE — just seven years before its destruction.

The destruction came in 70 CE, when Roman legions under the general Titus besieged and captured Jerusalem after a prolonged Jewish revolt. The Temple was burned and the city was razed. The Arch of Titus in Rome, still standing today, depicts Roman soldiers carrying away the Temple's sacred vessels — including the great golden menorah — in triumph. This catastrophe, known in Jewish tradition as the Churban (Destruction), fundamentally reshaped Judaism, which transformed from a Temple-centered, sacrificial religion to the rabbinic, synagogue-based religion it remains today.

After 70 CE, Jews were forbidden from Jerusalem under Roman rule. After the Bar Kokhba revolt of 132–135 CE, they were expelled from the city entirely and permitted to enter only once a year, on the anniversary of the Temple's destruction. Despite these restrictions, Jews continued to regard the site with the deepest reverence.

The specific history of Jewish prayer at the Western Wall is somewhat obscure in the early medieval period. The 4th-century Christian Bordeaux Pilgrim described seeing Jews lamenting at a "perforated stone" near the Temple site on the anniversary of the destruction. Over subsequent centuries, various accounts confirm Jewish presence and prayer at or near the Wall.

In the Ottoman period, from the 16th century onward, the Western Wall was formally designated as a Jewish prayer site. Sultan Suleiman the Magnificent is credited with designating the area in front of the Wall for Jewish worship. A narrow alley along the Wall's face — only a few meters wide — served as the Jewish prayer space for centuries, hemmed in by buildings of the Moroccan Quarter (Mughrabi Quarter) on the western side.

The political and religious significance of the Wall intensified in the modern era. In 1929, a dispute over Jewish practices at the Wall — specifically the erection of a screen to separate male and female worshippers — triggered the Western Wall riots, in which Arab mobs attacked Jewish communities across Palestine, killing 133 Jews. The British Mandatory authorities subsequently convened an international commission that reaffirmed Arab ownership of the Wall while guaranteeing Jewish access.

The most dramatic moment in the modern history of the Wall came during the Six-Day War of June 1967, when Israeli paratroopers broke through to the Wall and an emotional gathering took place as the site came under Israeli control for the first time in history. The images of Israeli soldiers weeping at the Wall became iconic symbols of the Jewish state's establishment of sovereignty over its holiest site. Within days, Israel demolished the Mughrabi Quarter and created the large open plaza that exists today.

## Religious Significance

In Jewish theology, the Temple Mount is the most sacred space on Earth — the site where God's presence (the Shekhinah) was most fully manifest, where the sacrificial service that maintained the cosmic order was performed, and where, on Yom Kippur, the High Priest would enter the Holy of Holies — the innermost sanctuary — and pronounce the divine name of God. The destruction of the Temple and the cessation of this sacrificial order represents a catastrophic rupture that Jewish tradition describes as the withdrawal of God's Shekhinah.

The Western Wall is the holiest site at which Jewish prayer is currently possible. The precise location of the Holy of Holies within the Temple Mount is unknown, but it is believed to lie somewhere in the area now occupied by the Dome of the Rock. Jewish law regarding ritual purity (tumah) complicates or in the view of many authorities prohibits ascending the Temple Mount, making the Western Wall the practical focal point of prayer.

A Midrashic teaching holds that the Western Wall will never be destroyed, that the Shekhinah (divine presence) still rests upon it, and that a person's prayer at the Wall is particularly direct. This theological significance is expressed in the many liturgical poems (kinot) mourning the Temple's destruction that are recited on Tisha B'Av, the fast day commemorating the Temple's fall.

The Wall is a site for prayer on all occasions — daily, Shabbat, holidays — as well as lifecycle events. Bar and Bat Mitzvahs are celebrated at the Wall. Soldiers take oaths of allegiance there. It is common for political and military leaders to visit the Wall at significant moments. The practice of inserting written prayers (kvitlach) into the Wall's crevices has become universal among visitors, both Jewish and non-Jewish.

## Architecture & Description

The visible portion of the Western Wall rises approximately 19 meters above the current plaza level, though excavations have revealed that the Wall extends much further below ground — the base courses of Herodian stonework lie 21 meters below the current ground level. The Wall's total height, from the base to the original Temple Mount surface, was approximately 32 meters.

The Wall is composed of distinctive large limestone blocks, the largest of which — known as Herod's stone — is located in the Northern Wall and measures approximately 13.6 meters long, 3.3 meters tall, and is estimated to weigh over 500 tons. The massive scale of these stones reflects Herodian engineering at its most ambitious: the stones were quarried, transported, and placed with extraordinary precision, without mortar, using a technique called dry stone construction.

The Herodian courses — large, carefully drafted stones with distinctive margins around their faces — are visible in the lower sections of the Wall. Above these original courses, additional layers were added in subsequent periods: Umayyad, Crusader, and Ottoman additions are identifiable by their different stone sizes, shapes, and tooling marks.

The Western Wall Plaza, created after the demolition of the Mughrabi Quarter in 1967, is a large open area approximately 60 meters deep and 70 meters wide. It is divided by a mechitza (partition) into separate men's and women's prayer sections, a division that has itself been a source of controversy (see below). The plaza is paved and has been landscaped to accommodate large crowds.

To the north of the open plaza, the Western Wall Tunnel extends underground for 488 meters along the full length of the Herodian Western Wall, revealing the lower courses of the Wall that have been buried under centuries of accumulated debris. The tunnel was excavated by Israeli archaeologists beginning in 1967 and opened to the public in 1996. Its 1996 opening was accompanied by violent clashes, which resulted in over 80 deaths.

## Pilgrimage & Rituals

The Western Wall is open for prayer and visitation 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. It receives approximately three million visitors annually, making it one of the most visited sites in Israel and the Middle East.

The practice of prayer at the Wall follows traditional Jewish forms: the standing Amidah prayer faces the Wall (i.e., toward the Temple Mount), and worshippers may remain for as long as they wish. On Friday evenings (the onset of Shabbat), thousands gather for communal prayer that transforms the plaza into a joyful scene of song and dance.

High holidays and festivals draw particularly large crowds. On Tisha B'Av, the atmosphere is somber and mournful, with worshippers sitting low (as a sign of mourning), reading the Book of Lamentations, and reciting kinot. On the intervening Shabbatot of Passover and Sukkot, the priestly blessing (duchaning) is performed, with descendants of the ancient priestly class (Kohanim) gathering to bless the congregation.

## Modern Significance

Since 1967, the Western Wall has been administered by the Israeli government through the Rabbi of the Western Wall, a government-appointed position. The site is treated as an Orthodox Jewish synagogue, with gender-separated prayer areas governed by Orthodox standards — a policy that has been the subject of intense controversy.

The Women of the Wall, a pluralistic Jewish women's prayer group, has since 1988 sought the right to pray at the main women's section of the Wall wearing prayer shawls and reading from the Torah — practices traditionally restricted to men in Orthodox Judaism. Their monthly prayer gatherings have frequently been met with resistance, and the Israeli legal system has repeatedly adjudicated the matter without achieving a final resolution.

A 2016 government agreement to create a dedicated egalitarian prayer section south of the main prayer plaza — the "Ezrat Yisrael" section near Robinson's Arch — appeared to offer a compromise, but the agreement was subsequently suspended following pressure from the ultra-Orthodox parties in the Israeli government coalition, reigniting the controversy.

The Western Wall also carries significance in the context of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. Palestinian authorities and Arab governments reject Israeli sovereignty over East Jerusalem, including the Old City and the Western Wall. The status of Jerusalem's holy sites is one of the most intractable issues in peace negotiations.
