## Summary

Apollonius of Perga was an ancient Greek mathematician known as 'The Great Geometer.' His treatise 'Conics' defined and systematized the study of conic sections (ellipses, parabolas, and hyperbolas). These curves later proved fundamental in physics — Kepler used ellipses for planetary orbits and Galileo used parabolas for projectile motion. His work influenced mathematics for over 2,000 years.

## Contributions

Apollonius is best known for giving conic sections much of the terminology and systematic treatment that later mathematicians used. In *Conics*, he studied properties of ellipses, parabolas, and hyperbolas using geometric methods, including relationships involving diameters, tangents, and chords.

## Influence on Later Science

Although written in the language of classical geometry, the conceptual framework of conics became foundational for later analytic geometry and for early modern physics. The shapes described by Apollonius appear in orbital mechanics and optics, and the study of tangents and intersections foreshadows later coordinate-based approaches.

## Surviving Works and Transmission

Of the eight books of *Conics*, several survived in Greek and others through later transmission. The work circulated through late antiquity and the medieval period, including through Arabic scholarship, and re-entered early modern European mathematics via translation and commentary.

## Related Topics

Apollonius is also associated with problems and results in classical geometry beyond conics, and his name appears in later mathematical literature as a reference point for rigorous geometric construction and proof.
