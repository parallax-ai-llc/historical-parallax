## Summary

In August 1991, hardline Communist Party officials attempted to seize power from Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev. For three days, the world held its breath as tanks rolled into Moscow and the fate of the Soviet Union hung in the balance. What happened next would change history: ordinary citizens, led by a defiant Boris Yeltsin standing atop a tank, faced down the coup plotters—and won.[^1]

The coup failed spectacularly. Within 72 hours, the conspirators were arrested, Gorbachev was freed from house arrest, and the Communist Party that had ruled for seven decades was on its knees. But the greatest irony was this: the coup intended to save the Soviet Union instead destroyed it. By December 1991, the USSR had dissolved, and 15 independent nations emerged from its ruins.[^2]

The August Coup was one of history's great turning points—the moment when the Cold War definitively ended, when empire gave way to democracy (however imperfect), and when one man standing on a tank became the symbol of people power against authoritarianism.[^3]

## Historical Background

### Gorbachev's Reforms

By 1991, the Soviet Union was in crisis, largely due to reforms initiated by its own leader:

**Glasnost (Openness):**
- Press freedoms unprecedented in Soviet history
- Criticism of government tolerated
- Truth about Stalinist crimes revealed

**Perestroika (Restructuring):**
- Economic reforms to address stagnation
- Limited market mechanisms introduced
- State control loosened

**New political landscape:**
- 1989: First semi-competitive elections
- 1990: Communist Party's monopoly legally abolished
- Non-communist governments in Eastern Europe
- Baltic states demanding independence[^4]

### The Hardliners' Fear

Conservative forces within the Party, military, and KGB watched with horror as:

- **Eastern Europe fell:** Poland, Hungary, Czechoslovakia, East Germany—all communist governments collapsed in 1989
- **Nationalism spread:** Baltic states, Ukraine, Georgia demanded sovereignty
- **The economy collapsed:** Shortages, inflation, chaos
- **The Party weakened:** Gorbachev marginalized hardliners

For them, Gorbachev was destroying everything the Soviet Union represented. Something had to be done.[^5]

### The New Union Treaty

The immediate trigger was the **New Union Treaty**, scheduled for signing on August 20, 1991:

- Would transform USSR into a loose confederation
- Republics would gain significant autonomy
- Central government power greatly reduced
- **Hardliners' view:** This meant the end of the Soviet Union

They decided to act the day before the signing.[^6]

## The Coup

### Day 0: August 18 (Sunday)

**Gorbachev's Dacha, Crimea:**
- Gorbachev was vacationing at Foros, Crimea
- At 4:50 PM, representatives of the plotters arrived
- They demanded he resign or transfer power to Vice President Yanayev
- **Gorbachev refused:** "You'll destroy yourselves and the country"
- His communications were cut; he was placed under house arrest
- His phone lines, including the nuclear codes communication, disabled[^7]

### The Conspirators: State Committee on the State of Emergency (GKChP)

| Name | Position |
|------|----------|
| Gennady Yanayev | Vice President; nominal coup leader |
| Vladimir Kryuchkov | KGB Chairman |
| Dmitry Yazov | Defense Minister |
| Boris Pugo | Interior Minister |
| Valentin Pavlov | Prime Minister |
| Oleg Baklanov | Deputy Defense Council Chairman |
| Vasily Starodubtsev | Farmers' Union head |
| Alexander Tizyakov | State Enterprises head |

**The Gang of Eight:** These men believed they were saving the Soviet Union.[^8]

### Day 1: August 19 (Monday)

**6:00 AM:**
- State of Emergency declared
- Yanayev announced on TV that Gorbachev was "ill" and unable to perform duties
- Political activities banned
- Media censorship imposed
- Curfew declared in Moscow

**Morning:**
- Tanks and troops entered Moscow
- Armored vehicles surrounded key buildings
- Taman and Kantemirovskaya divisions deployed
- Estimated 4,000 troops, 362 tanks in Moscow

**But something unexpected happened:** The military moved slowly, hesitantly. Orders were unclear. Many officers were uncomfortable. And in the Russian White House (parliament building), resistance was forming.[^9]

### Yeltsin's Defiance

**Boris Yeltsin**, President of the Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic, was the coup's most dangerous opponent.

**Morning, August 19:**
- Yeltsin arrived at the White House
- Issued decree declaring the coup illegal and unconstitutional
- Called for general strike
- Demanded Gorbachev's return

**12:00 PM:** The Iconic Moment
- Yeltsin emerged from the White House
- Climbed onto Tank No. 110 of the Taman Division
- Delivered a speech to the crowd:
  > "Citizens of Russia... We are dealing with a right-wing, reactionary, anti-constitutional coup... The current use of force is absolutely illegal."

**The image circled the world:** A leader standing on a tank, defying the coup—democracy versus authoritarianism in one photograph.[^10]

### Popular Resistance

**Tens of thousands of Muscovites** gathered to defend the White House:

- Barricades constructed from trolleybuses, construction materials
- Human chains formed
- Food and supplies brought by citizens
- Rain fell; people stayed
- Veterans, students, babushkas, intellectuals stood together

**International support:**
- US President George H.W. Bush called Yeltsin, pledged support
- Western governments condemned the coup
- International pressure mounted[^11]

### Day 2: August 20 (Tuesday)

**The coup was already wavering:**

- Resignations from generals and officials
- Many military units refused to participate
- Some soldiers openly sided with defenders
- Tank crews gave ammunition to protesters

**Press conference disaster:**
- Yanayev appeared before cameras at 5:00 PM
- His hands visibly trembled
- He slurred words, appeared drunk
- Projecting weakness, not strength
- **"He looked like he was at his own funeral"**

**The night of August 20-21:**
- Three protesters died in confrontation with armored vehicles
  - Dmitry Komar
  - Ilya Krichevsky
  - Vladimir Usov
- Only three casualties of the entire coup
- Military assault on White House never came[^12]

### Day 3: August 21 (Wednesday)

**5:00 AM:**
- Troops began withdrawing from Moscow
- It was over

**The conspirators fled:**
- Some attempted to fly to Crimea to negotiate with Gorbachev
- Yanayev, Pavlov remained in Moscow
- All were arrested over the following hours

**Gorbachev freed:**
- Around 2:00 AM August 22, Gorbachev's communications restored
- He flew back to Moscow
- Arrived exhausted, but free[^13]

## Why the Coup Failed

The August Coup failed for multiple, interconnected reasons:

### 1. Incompetent Execution
- Plotters were indecisive bureaucrats, not revolutionary leaders
- No plan to arrest Yeltsin (they didn't think he was important)
- Military orders were vague and contradictory
- No attempt to seize television stations until too late

### 2. Military Reluctance
- Many officers refused to fire on citizens
- Younger officers sympathetic to reform
- No one wanted another Tiananmen Square
- Several units defected to Yeltsin

### 3. Popular Resistance
- Tens of thousands surrounded White House
- General strike called (though limited)
- Coup lacked public legitimacy
- Soviet citizens had changed—they no longer feared the state as before

### 4. International Pressure
- Western governments immediately condemned coup
- Economic consequences implied
- Pressure on wavering officials

### 5. Gorbachev's Reforms Had Worked
- Glasnost meant information flowed
- Independent media reported truth
- People knew their rights
- Fear of state had diminished[^14]

## Aftermath

### Immediate Consequences

**For Gorbachev:**
- Returned to Moscow, but fatally weakened
- His authority was broken
- Yeltsin was now the real power
- Gorbachev resigned December 25, 1991

**For the Communist Party:**
- Boris Yeltsin suspended CPSU in Russia on August 24
- Party property seized
- Gorbachev resigned as General Secretary August 24
- The Party that had ruled since 1917 was finished

**For the Soviet Union:**
- Republics accelerated independence declarations
- Ukraine declared independence August 24
- Other republics followed
- **December 8:** Russia, Ukraine, Belarus declared USSR dissolved
- **December 25:** Gorbachev resigned; Soviet flag lowered over Kremlin
- **December 26:** USSR officially ceased to exist[^15]

### The End of the Soviet Union

The coup's greatest irony: it was meant to save the Soviet Union, but it destroyed it.

- The hardliners proved the center was hollow
- Yeltsin emerged as dominant figure
- Republics concluded Moscow could not be trusted
- Independence became unstoppable
- A 74-year experiment ended[^16]

## Historical Assessment

### Was the Coup Justified?

**Coup Plotters' Perspective:**
- The Soviet Union was being destroyed
- Chaos would lead to catastrophe
- Strong measures were necessary
- They were patriots, not traitors

**Democratic Perspective:**
- The coup was illegal and unconstitutional
- The people had the right to self-determination
- Reform, however imperfect, was better than repression
- The plotters represented a discredited system

### What If It Had Succeeded?

Historians speculate:
- Likely renewed repression, not Stalinist terror
- Economic crisis would have continued or worsened
- Delayed but not prevented Soviet dissolution
- Cold War tensions might have resumed
- Eastern Europe's freedom at risk

### Yeltsin's Mixed Legacy

The hero of August 1991 would become a controversial figure:
- Presided over economic "shock therapy" and oligarch capitalism
- Bombed his own parliament in 1993
- Waged brutal Chechen wars
- Enabled Putin's rise
- But: in August 1991, he stood for democracy against tanks[^17]

---

## Legacy

### For Russia

**Positive:**
- End of Communist dictatorship
- Democratic possibility (however brief)
- Freedom of speech, religion, movement
- Integration with world economy

**Negative:**
- Economic collapse of 1990s
- Rise of oligarchs
- Chaos enabled authoritarian nostalgia
- Putin's restoration of state control

### For the World

**Cold War ended:**
- Nuclear threat dramatically reduced
- Former enemies became partners (briefly)
- Germany reunified
- NATO expanded eastward

**New nations born:**
- 15 independent states from USSR
- Some democratic (Baltics)
- Some authoritarian (Central Asia)
- Some in between (Ukraine, Georgia)

### Symbolic Legacy

The image of Yeltsin on the tank became:
- Symbol of people power against authoritarianism
- Proof that nonviolent resistance could defeat military force
- Inspiration for future democratic movements
- But also: cautionary tale of how quickly democratic hope can fade[^18]
