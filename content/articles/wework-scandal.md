## Summary

WeWork's failed $47 billion IPO exposed CEO Adam Neumann's self-dealing, including charging the company for his own trademarked 'We' brand. Company eventually filed for bankruptcy.

## Background

WeWork is a coworking and flexible office-space company founded in 2010 by Adam Neumann and Miguel McKelvey.

Before attempting to go public, WeWork raised large amounts of venture funding, including major investment from SoftBank’s Vision Fund, and reached private-market valuations that were widely reported to be as high as US$47 billion.

## What made it a “scandal”

The controversy centered on corporate governance and related-party transactions disclosed in public offering documents and media reporting around the attempted IPO.

Key themes commonly cited include:

- Concentrated founder control and unusually founder-friendly governance provisions.
- Related-party dealings and perceived conflicts of interest.
- Aggressive growth narratives and large operating losses.
- Questions about valuation methodology and disclosures.

## IPO collapse and leadership changes

In 2019, WeWork’s IPO process unraveled after investors scrutinized disclosures and the company faced widening criticism.

Adam Neumann ultimately resigned as chief executive, and the IPO was canceled.

## Aftermath

WeWork later became a public company through a SPAC transaction.

In November 2023, it filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection, and the business was restructured with a smaller footprint relative to its peak expansion.
