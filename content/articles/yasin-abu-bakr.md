## Background

Yasin Abu Bakr was a Trinidadian radical Islamist leader and militant associated with the **Jamaat al Muslimeen**, an Afro-Trinidadian Muslim group in Trinidad and Tobago.

In many sources he is also identified by the name **Lennox Philip**, reflecting his birth name prior to his conversion to Islam and adoption of the name by which he became publicly known.

## Jamaat al Muslimeen

Jamaat al Muslimeen became one of the most prominent Islamist organizations in Trinidad and Tobago, and Abu Bakr’s leadership made him a central figure in national debates about extremism, security, and the relationship between religion and politics.

Accounts of his formation and influences often mention time spent outside Trinidad and Tobago (including study abroad) as well as connections in the broader international Islamist milieu during the late 20th century.

## The 1990 coup attempt (high level)

Abu Bakr is best known for orchestrating the **1990 attempted coup d’état** in Trinidad and Tobago.

- Armed members of the Jamaat al Muslimeen attacked key state sites, including the national parliament (often referred to as the **Red House**) and a state broadcasting facility.
- During the operation, the **Prime Minister A. N. R. Robinson** and other officials were held hostage for several days.
- The event produced a prolonged national crisis and remains one of the most consequential episodes in the country’s modern political history.

## Aftermath

Following the collapse of the coup attempt, Abu Bakr and members of his group faced arrest and detention. The episode has been repeatedly revisited in public discussion, legal analysis, and historical writing, both for its immediate violence and for the long-running questions it raised about accountability, amnesties, and the limits of state authority during emergencies.

## Personal life and later years

Public reporting describes Abu Bakr as having a large family and a public profile that continued well after 1990.

Some sources also state that he **died in 2021**, which would place his life and influence within the broader arc of post–Cold War Islamist militancy and its local expressions in the Caribbean.

## Legacy

Abu Bakr’s legacy is primarily tied to:

- The durability of the **1990 coup attempt** as a national reference point for political violence.
- Ongoing debate about the social and political conditions that allowed an armed movement to challenge the state.
- The ways Trinidad and Tobago’s institutions responded to the crisis, shaping later approaches to internal security and extremism.
