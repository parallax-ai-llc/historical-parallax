## Summary

Persian ruler who sacked Delhi and stole the Peacock Throne and Koh-i-Noor diamond. His later reign was marked by increasing paranoia, cruelty, and mass blinding of subjects.

## Rise to Power

- Nader Shah rose from a military background during a period of instability following Safavid decline and external invasions.
- He rebuilt a large field army and used rapid campaigns to reassert control over Iran and adjacent regions.

## Major Campaigns (High Level)

- He fought wars against neighboring powers, including campaigns in the Caucasus, Central Asia, and against the Ottoman Empire.
- The 1739 invasion of the Mughal Empire culminated in the sack of Delhi and enormous material loot, which had long-term symbolic and political consequences.

## Governance and Reputation

- Contemporary and later accounts portray his reign as a mix of military effectiveness and harsh rule.
- Late in his reign, chronic rebellion and fiscal pressure contributed to more coercive policies and violent reprisals.

## Aftermath

- His assassination triggered political fragmentation, and the Afsharid state did not long survive him as a coherent empire.
- Nader Shah is often discussed as an example of a conqueror whose empire depended heavily on continual military success.

## Historiography

- Assessments of Nader Shah differ: some emphasize state restoration and military modernization, while others foreground coercion and civilian suffering.
- Many narratives rely on court chronicles and later histories that can be partisan or moralizing.
