## Summary

Runaway train carrying crude oil derailed and exploded in Lac-Megantic, Quebec on July 6, 2013, killing 47 people and destroying the town center.

## Background

The derailment occurred on the Montreal, Maine & Atlantic Railway (MMA) line, where unit trains of crude oil from the Bakken formation were being moved through small Quebec communities.

## Contributing Factors (High-Level)

Investigations and public reporting have cited a combination of factors, including securement practices for unattended trains, brake effectiveness, and the characteristics of the volatile crude oil being transported.

## Immediate Impact and Response

The derailment and resulting explosions destroyed a large portion of Lac-Megantic’s downtown core.
Emergency services from the region responded, evacuations were conducted, and extensive environmental cleanup followed due to fuel contamination.

## Legal and Regulatory Aftermath

The disaster led to major scrutiny of rail safety practices in Canada and the United States, including rules around train securement, crew requirements, and the classification/handling of flammable liquids.
MMA entered bankruptcy proceedings in the years following the incident.

## Long-Term Significance

The Lac-Megantic disaster is often cited as one of the deadliest rail disasters in modern Canadian history and a turning point in policy debates over crude-by-rail transportation.
