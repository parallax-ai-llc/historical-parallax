## Summary

Berenice Abbott (1898–1991) was an American photographer whose documentary precision and artistic vision shaped the medium across six decades. She is best known for her sweeping photographic survey of New York City in the 1930s, which captured the metropolis in a state of dramatic transformation between the old and the modern. Her work sits at the intersection of art and social record, combining rigorous technical craft with a humanist's eye for place and change.

Abbott's significance extends beyond her own imagery. She was instrumental in rescuing and promoting the photographic legacy of Eugène Atget, the French photographer whose thousands of glass-plate images of Paris might otherwise have been lost. As an educator and writer, she also helped establish photography as a legitimate fine art discipline in American institutions, influencing generations of photographers who followed.

---

## Early Life & Background

Born on July 17, 1898, in Springfield, Ohio, Abbott grew up in difficult circumstances after her parents separated. She studied at Ohio State University briefly before relocating to New York City around 1918, drawn by its bohemian cultural scene. By 1921 she had moved to Paris, where she initially pursued sculpture. She fell into photography almost by accident, accepting a job as a darkroom assistant to Man Ray around 1923. The medium took hold quickly; within two years she had opened her own portrait studio in Paris and was photographing some of the most prominent intellectual and artistic figures of the era, including James Joyce, Jean Cocteau, and André Gide.

---

## Rise to Prominence

Abbott's Paris years established her technical foundations and gave her access to a remarkable social world. In 1925 she met Eugène Atget, then elderly and largely unknown, and immediately recognized the importance of his systematic documentation of disappearing Paris. When Atget died in 1927, Abbott acquired his archive of roughly 8,000 glass-plate negatives and prints. She spent subsequent decades printing, promoting, and publishing his work, ensuring its preservation and eventual recognition.

Returning permanently to New York in 1929, Abbott was struck by how radically the city had changed since she had left. She conceived a large-scale documentary project, "Changing New York," which she pursued through the 1930s with financial support from the Federal Art Project, part of the New Deal's Works Progress Administration, from 1935 to 1939. The project produced around 300 final prints.

---

## Key Achievements

"Changing New York" remains Abbott's central achievement. Published as a book in 1939, it documented skyscrapers alongside tenements, elevated railways beside pushcart markets — the collision of eras in a single city. Images such as *Nightview, New York* (1932) and *El at Columbus Avenue and Broadway* (1929) have become canonical records of Depression-era urban life.

In the 1940s and 1950s Abbott turned to scientific photography, collaborating with Physical Science Study Committee at MIT to create images illustrating principles of physics for educational purposes. This work produced strikingly beautiful photographs that made abstract concepts visually legible.

She also authored several influential books, including *A Guide to Better Photography* (1941) and *The View Camera Made Simple* (1948), as well as a major monograph on Atget published in 1964.

---

## Controversies & Criticism

Abbott's decades-long stewardship of Atget's archive, while widely praised, occasionally drew scrutiny regarding ownership and the terms under which prints were sold and distributed before the archive was acquired by the Museum of Modern Art in 1968. Some critics also noted that her shift toward scientific and educational photography in the 1950s pulled her away from the documentary tradition where her reputation was strongest, resulting in work that received less sustained critical attention. Her outspoken, direct personality generated friction in some professional circles, though it also earned deep respect from students and peers.

---

## Legacy & Influence

Abbott's influence on American photography is substantial. Her New York survey helped define what urban documentary photography could accomplish as both art and social record. Her championing of Atget introduced European documentary traditions to American audiences. As a teacher at the New School for Social Research from 1935 to 1958, she trained a generation of photographers in technical rigor and documentary ethics.

She spent her final years in Monson, Maine, where she died on December 9, 1991, at age 93. Her work is held in major collections including the Museum of Modern Art, the Smithsonian American Art Museum, and the New York Public Library. Abbott is consistently cited as a foundational figure in the history of photography.
