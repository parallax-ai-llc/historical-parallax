## Summary

Amitabh Bachchan is an Indian actor widely regarded as one of the greatest and most influential figures in the history of cinema. Born on October 11, 1942, in Allahabad, India, he has appeared in over 200 films across a career spanning more than five decades. His commanding screen presence, distinctive baritone voice, and ability to embody both intense dramatic and action roles made him the defining star of Hindi cinema from the 1970s onward.

Bachchan's cultural impact extends far beyond entertainment. He transformed the archetype of the Hindi film hero, replacing the romantic ideal with the image of the "Angry Young Man" — a working-class protagonist raging against systemic injustice. French director François Truffaut called the 1970s Indian film industry a "one-man industry," referring specifically to Bachchan's dominance. His influence reshaped Bollywood's narrative sensibility and continues to inform Indian popular culture today.

## Early Life & Background

Amitabh Harivansh Rai Bachchan was born in Allahabad (now Prayagraj), Uttar Pradesh, to the celebrated Hindi poet Harivansh Rai Bachchan and social activist Teji Bachchan. He was educated at Sherwood College in Nainital and later graduated from Kirori Mal College, University of Delhi, with a degree in science. Before entering films, he briefly worked as a freight broker with the shipping firm Bird and Company in Kolkata.

His upbringing in a literary household and exposure to progressive intellectual circles shaped his articulate public persona. He relocated to Mumbai in the late 1960s to pursue an acting career, facing early rejections — including from All India Radio, which deemed his voice unsuitable for broadcast.

## Rise to Prominence

Bachchan made his film debut in Saat Hindustani (1969). His early career produced modest results until Zanjeer (1973) — a crime drama in which he played a brooding, incorruptible police officer — established him as a leading man of a new kind. The film launched a series of roles scripted by Salim–Javed that codified the "Angry Young Man" persona.

The mid-1970s saw an extraordinary run of hits: Deewaar (1975), Sholay (1975), and Trishul (1978) cemented his superstar status. Sholay, in particular, became one of the highest-grossing Indian films of all time and remains a cultural landmark. Through the late 1970s and 1980s, Bachchan dominated the box office with few rivals.

## Key Achievements

- Won four National Film Awards for Best Actor, for Agneepath (1990), Black (2005), Paa (2009), and Gulabo Sitabo (2020).
- Received the Padma Shri (1984), Padma Bhushan (2001), and Padma Vibhushan (2015) — three of India's highest civilian honors.
- Awarded the Dadasaheb Phalke Award in 2018, Indian cinema's highest lifetime recognition.
- Named by BBC online polls as "Star of the Millennium" in 1999.
- Has hosted the television quiz show Kaun Banega Crorepati (India's Who Wants to Be a Millionaire) since 2000, running for over 15 seasons.

## Controversies & Criticism

Bachchan faced significant scrutiny during the 1980s for his close association with then-Prime Minister Rajiv Gandhi and his brief, turbulent stint as a Member of Parliament for Allahabad (1984–1987), which he resigned citing incompatibility with political life. He was named in the Bofors arms deal controversy, though no charges were filed against him.

In 2017, the Panama Papers and subsequent Pandora Papers leaks listed his name in connection with offshore financial entities, drawing public criticism. Bachchan denied wrongdoing, and no legal action resulted. His social media statements during communal tensions in India have occasionally attracted both support and criticism for perceived ambiguity.

## Legacy & Influence

Bachchan fundamentally altered the grammar of Hindi film stardom. His collaborations with directors Yash Chopra, Ramesh Sippy, and Manmohan Desai produced films that defined a generation's relationship with cinema. Later-career performances in Black (2005) and Piku (2015) demonstrated his range and adaptability, earning him new audiences decades into his career.

His wax figure has been displayed at Madame Tussauds in London, New York, Hong Kong, Bangkok, Delhi, and Sydney. Younger actors including Shah Rukh Khan and Ranveer Singh have cited him as a primary influence. The "Bachchan phenomenon" is studied in academic contexts as a case study in star power, national identity, and the politics of popular culture.
