## Summary

Two separate mass killings by Los Zetas in San Fernando, Tamaulipas. In August 2010, 72 migrants were massacred, and in 2011, 193 people were killed after being pulled from buses.

## Background

San Fernando is a municipality in Tamaulipas, a northeastern Mexican state that has been contested by organized criminal groups.
During the late 2000s and early 2010s, the region was affected by cartel fragmentation, competition over routes, and aggressive efforts to control territory.
Migrants traveling north through Mexico were especially vulnerable to extortion, kidnapping, and forced recruitment.

## 2010 migrant killings

The 2010 incident drew international attention because the victims were foreign nationals traveling through Mexico.
Reporting and official accounts described the event as part of broader criminal predation against migrants, including demands for ransom and coercion.
The case became a symbol of the security crisis and the humanitarian risks on migration corridors.

## 2011 bus passenger abductions and graves

The 2011 killings were associated with people taken from buses on highways and later found in mass graves.
The discovery of multiple graves highlighted both the scale of violence and the difficulty of timely detection in rural areas.
These events contributed to public pressure for investigations and accountability.

## Aftermath and investigations

Mexican authorities pursued arrests and prosecutions connected to Los Zetas and to local collaborators.
Like many mass-atrocity investigations, the process involved identification of remains, coordination with victims’ families, and forensic work.
The cases are often referenced in discussions of impunity, victim support, and security-sector capacity in Mexico.
