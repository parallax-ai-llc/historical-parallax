## Summary

American who was executed with husband Julius for espionage. Her role remains debated; recent evidence suggests she was less involved than her husband. Their sons spent decades trying to clear her name.

## Background

- Rosenberg grew up in New York City and became involved in labor and left-wing political circles during the Depression era.
- The Rosenberg case unfolded during the early Cold War, when U.S. authorities prioritized counterintelligence and prosecutions related to Soviet espionage.

## The Rosenberg Espionage Case (High-Level)

- Prosecutors alleged that a network passed U.S. military and atomic-related information to the Soviet Union.
- Ethel’s husband, Julius Rosenberg, was portrayed as a central organizer; Ethel was accused of participating and supporting the conspiracy.
- The trial and sentencing became a global flashpoint, with arguments about evidence, proportionality of punishment, and political context.

## Debate and Reassessment

- Historians and commentators have continued to debate Ethel Rosenberg’s level of involvement.
- Later disclosures and archival material intensified scrutiny over witness testimony, prosecutorial tactics, and the degree to which the case reflected the politics of the era.

## Legacy

- The executions remain among the most famous espionage-related cases in U.S. history.
- The case influenced public discourse on civil liberties, anti-communism, and the boundaries of national-security prosecutions.
- It continues to appear in scholarship and popular culture as a lens on fear, loyalty, and state power in the early Cold War.
