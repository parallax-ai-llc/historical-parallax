## Summary

British brigadier who ordered the Jallianwala Bagh massacre in Amritsar, killing at least 379 unarmed civilians.

## Context and Early Life

- Reginald Edward Harry Dyer was an officer in the British Indian Army who served primarily in British India.[^1]
- By 1919 he held the rank of brigadier-general and was involved in security operations in Punjab during a period of intense political unrest following World War I.[^1]

## Jallianwala Bagh Massacre (Amritsar, 13 April 1919)

- The incident occurred at Jallianwala Bagh, a walled public garden in Amritsar, where a large crowd had gathered.[^1]
- Dyer brought troops to the site and ordered them to fire into the crowd without warning, an action widely described as a massacre.[^1]
- Contemporary and later estimates of fatalities vary; the commonly cited official figure is at least 379 killed, with many more wounded.[^1]
- The event became a major turning point in Indian political life, intensifying opposition to British rule and becoming a lasting symbol of colonial violence.[^1]

## Aftermath and Official Response

- The killings prompted major controversy in Britain and India and led to formal inquiries into the events in Punjab.[^1]
- Dyer was relieved of his command and effectively ended his active military career after the official response condemned his actions, even as some supporters attempted to portray him as having acted to restore order.[^1]

## Legacy

- The Jallianwala Bagh massacre remains one of the most infamous episodes of British colonial rule in India and is commemorated at the Jallianwala Bagh memorial site in Amritsar.[^1]
- Historians and public discussions often treat Dyer as a central figure in debates over imperial policy, accountability, and state violence in colonial settings.[^1]
