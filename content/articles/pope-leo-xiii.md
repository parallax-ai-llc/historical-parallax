## Summary

Pope Leo XIII (born Gioacchino Vincenzo Raffaele Luigi Pecci) served as the head of the Catholic Church from 1878 until his death in 1903, making his twenty-five year reign the third longest pontificate of his era.[^1] Elected at age 68 and initially considered a transitional figure due to his frail health, Leo surprised the world by living to 93 and fundamentally reshaping Catholic engagement with the modern world.[^2] He is best remembered as the "Social Pope" for his groundbreaking 1891 encyclical Rerum Novarum, which established Catholic social teaching and defended workers' rights during the Industrial Revolution.[^3] A brilliant diplomat and prolific writer who issued 88 encyclicals, Leo sought to reconcile Catholic doctrine with contemporary thought while firmly opposing what he viewed as the errors of modernism, socialism, and liberalism.[^4]

---

## Early Life and Background

Gioacchino Vincenzo Raffaele Luigi Pecci was born on March 2, 1810, in Carpineto Romano, a small town in the Papal States southeast of Rome.[^5] He was the sixth of seven children born to Count Domenico Ludovico Pecci and Anna Francesca Prosperi-Buzzi, a family of minor Italian nobility known for their deep Catholic piety.[^6] The Pecci family's noble status was sometimes questioned, and when young Gioacchino sought admission to the prestigious Accademia dei Nobili in Rome, he faced opposition and was compelled to write a detailed history proving that the Pecci of Carpineto were indeed a branch of the noble Pecci family of Siena.[^7]

At the age of eight, Vincenzo and his brother Giuseppe were sent to the Jesuit school in Viterbo, where they remained for six years from 1818 to 1824.[^8] During this formative period, he gained exceptional facility in Latin and Italian, laying the foundation for his later intellectual pursuits.[^9] In 1824, when their mother fell gravely ill, the brothers were summoned to Rome, where they remained with their father and attended the Jesuit Collegium Romanum.[^10]

Vincenzo's education continued at the Academia dei Nobili, where he studied primarily diplomacy and law, subjects that would prove invaluable during his later career as a papal diplomat.[^11] His academic excellence was evident as he progressed through his theological studies. In 1832, he obtained his doctorate in theology, after which he gained admission to the prestigious Academy of Noble Ecclesiastics and embarked upon studies in canon and civil law at the Sapienza University in Rome.[^12] By 1836, he had earned doctorates in theology, civil law, and canon law, demonstrating his remarkable intellectual capacity.[^13] On December 31, 1837, Vincenzo Pecci was ordained to the priesthood by Cardinal Odeschalchi, the Vicar of Rome, marking the beginning of a clerical career that would ultimately lead him to the Chair of Saint Peter.[^14]

---

## Rise to Power

Following his ordination in 1837, Father Pecci embarked on a distinguished career in the service of the Holy See, initially serving as a governor in various provinces of the Papal States before transitioning into Vatican diplomacy.[^15] His early appointments included progressively important positions as provincial administrator (legate) in Benevento, Spoleto, and finally Perugia, each posting larger and more significant than the last.[^16] These administrative roles provided him with invaluable experience in governance and demonstrated his diplomatic skill and administrative competence.

In 1846, responding to the wishes of the Perugians and recognizing the needs of that city and district, Pope Gregory XVI appointed Monsignor Pecci as Bishop of Perugia, retaining for him the honorary title of archbishop.[^17] During his thirty-year tenure as bishop of Perugia, Cardinal Pecci proved himself an excellent and dedicated shepherd, outspoken in his promotion of Thomistic philosophy and even founding the Academy of St. Thomas Aquinas to advance this intellectual tradition.[^18] His time in Perugia was marked by pastoral dedication, intellectual vigor, and a growing reputation as a capable leader within the Church hierarchy.

Pope Pius IX elevated Pecci to the cardinalate in 1853, recognizing his abilities and service to the Church.[^19] For the next quarter-century, Cardinal Pecci remained in Perugia, somewhat removed from the center of ecclesiastical power in Rome. This changed dramatically in August 1877, when Pope Pius IX appointed him Camerlengo (Chamberlain) of the Holy Roman Church following the death of Cardinal Filippo de Angelis, a position that required him to reside in Rome and placed him at the heart of papal administration.[^20]

When Pope Pius IX died on February 7, 1878, after the longest pontificate in history, a conclave of 64 cardinal electors gathered from February 18-20, 1878, to choose his successor.[^21] The conclave met in the Apostolic Palace in the Vatican—a historic first, as previous conclaves had been held in the Quirinal Palace, which was now the residence of the Italian king following the capture of Rome.[^22] On the third ballot, the cardinals elected Cardinal Gioacchino Pecci, who took the name Leo XIII in honor of Pope Leo XII.[^23] He was crowned in the Sistine Chapel on March 3, 1878.[^24] At 68 years old and in delicate health, many anticipated a brief transitional pontificate, but Leo would defy all expectations and reign for twenty-five years.[^25]

## Major Achievements

Pope Leo XIII's pontificate was marked by extraordinary intellectual productivity and diplomatic achievement. He issued 88 encyclicals during his twenty-five year reign, addressing nearly every major theological, social, and political question facing the Church and the world.[^26] His writings shaped Catholic thought for generations and demonstrated a sophisticated engagement with modernity that departed from his predecessor's more confrontational approach.

**Rerum Novarum and Catholic Social Teaching**

Leo XIII's most enduring achievement was the promulgation of Rerum Novarum ("Of New Things") on May 15, 1891, an encyclical that established the foundations of modern Catholic social teaching.[^27] Addressing "the condition of the working classes" during the Industrial Revolution, Leo outlined a comprehensive vision that rejected both laissez-faire capitalism and socialism.[^28] The encyclical affirmed workers' rights to a living wage, safe working conditions, reasonable hours, and the freedom to form trade unions and strike when necessary.[^29] Simultaneously, it defended the right to private property and the dignity of labor itself.[^30]

Rerum Novarum proved so influential that successive popes wrote anniversary encyclicals celebrating its legacy, including Quadragesimo Anno, Octogesima Adveniens, and Centesimus Annus.[^31] The document moved social issues to the center of the Church's mission and earned Leo the titles "The Social Pope" and "The Pope of the Workers."[^32] Its impact extended beyond Catholicism—moral theologian Father John Ryan credited Rerum Novarum with shaping his economic views, and President Franklin Roosevelt acknowledged Father Ryan as a key influence on New Deal programs.[^33]

**Thomistic Revival and Intellectual Leadership**

On August 4, 1879, Leo issued Aeterni Patris, an encyclical calling for "Christian philosophy to be restored according to the spirit of St. Thomas Aquinas."[^34] This document represented a decisive turning point in Catholic philosophical and theological thought, revitalizing Thomism and ending the doctrinal pluralism that had characterized nineteenth-century Catholicism.[^35] Following this encyclical, Leo created the Pontifical Academy of St. Thomas Aquinas on October 15, 1879, and ordered the publication of a critical "Leonine edition" of Aquinas's complete works.[^36]

The Thomistic revival initiated by Aeterni Patris established neo-scholasticism as the dominant Catholic philosophical system and provided a framework for addressing modern errors through classical metaphysics and epistemology.[^37] This intellectual project aimed to demonstrate that faith and reason could enter into fruitful dialogue and that Catholic thought could engage contemporary philosophy without compromising essential doctrines.

**Biblical Scholarship and Education**

In Providentissimus Deus (November 18, 1893), Leo gave the first formal papal authorization for the use of critical methods in biblical scholarship.[^38] While strongly defending divine inspiration and scriptural inerrancy, the encyclical opened the door for Catholic theologians to employ modern historical and textual methods in studying Scripture.[^39] This balanced approach encouraged judicious acceptance of contemporary scientific and historical discoveries while protecting against rationalist excesses.[^40] In 1902, Leo established the Pontifical Biblical Commission to adapt Catholic biblical studies to modern scholarship and defend Scripture against attacks.[^41]

Leo demonstrated his commitment to scholarship by taking the unprecedented step of opening the Vatican Archives to researchers of all faiths in 1883.[^42] This ecumenical gesture gave scholars access to invaluable historical documents and facilitated serious historical research. He personally supported German historian Ludwig von Pastor's comprehensive twenty-volume study of the papacy, fostering rigorous historical analysis.[^43]

**Diplomatic Achievements**

Leo proved himself a masterful diplomat, improving the Church's relations with numerous nations despite the difficult circumstances following the loss of the Papal States. He successfully negotiated an end to the Kulturkampf in Germany, the anti-Catholic campaign under Otto von Bismarck that had severely restricted Church activities.[^44] Through patient diplomacy, compromises were reached in 1880 and 1883, with formal revision of the restrictive May Laws occurring in 1887.[^45]

Leo pursued a controversial rapprochement with France between 1887 and 1893, pressing French Catholics to accept the Third Republic through his ralliement policy, articulated most clearly in the 1892 encyclical Au Milieu des Sollicitudes.[^46] He also improved relations with Russia, Britain, and other nations, demonstrating remarkable diplomatic skill.[^47] In 1878, he restored the Scottish Catholic hierarchy, and in 1879, elevated the distinguished theologian John Henry Newman to cardinal.[^48] In 1886, he established a Catholic hierarchy in British India, expanding the Church's institutional presence globally.[^49]

**Marian Devotion and the Rosary**

Leo XIII holds the record for papal writings on the Holy Rosary, publishing twelve encyclicals and several letters on this devotion throughout his pontificate.[^50] He dedicated the month of October to the rosary and declared the feast of the Queen of the Holy Rosary in 1883.[^51] In his rosary encyclicals, including Octobri Mense, Leo became the first pope to fully embrace the concept of Mary as mediatrix, describing the Virgin Mary as mediating all graces.[^52] These teachings earned him the affectionate title "The Rosary Pope" and established devotional practices that continue in Catholic life today.[^53]

## Controversies and Criticisms

Despite his many achievements, Pope Leo XIII's pontificate was marked by significant controversies and criticisms from various quarters. His attempts to navigate between tradition and modernity satisfied neither progressive reformers nor staunch conservatives, leaving his legacy subject to competing interpretations.

**The "Prisoner in the Vatican" and Italian Relations**

Leo inherited from his predecessor Pius IX the status of "prisoner in the Vatican," resulting from the 1870 capture of Rome by Italian forces during the unification of Italy.[^54] Like Pius IX, Leo refused to accept the Italian Law of Guarantees, which he felt could be unilaterally reversed and failed to ensure the Church's independence from political interference.[^55] He continued the policy of forbidding Italian Catholics from voting in Italian elections or holding elected office, a stance that marginalized Catholic political participation.[^56]

More controversially, by the late 1880s, Leo actively sought to entice France, Germany, and Austria to invade Italy and seize power from the Italian monarchy, hoping to restore papal temporal authority.[^57] This futile scheme demonstrated a fundamental inability to accept the new political reality of a unified Italy with Rome as its capital. The "Roman Question" would not be resolved until the Lateran Treaty of 1929, long after Leo's death.[^58]

**Condemnation of "Americanism"**

In 1899, Leo issued the apostolic letter Testem Benevolentiae Nostrae, condemning what became known as "Americanism"—perceived tendencies among American Catholics toward excessive accommodation with modern liberal democracy and separation of church and state.[^59] Critics had alarmed Leo about supposed American Catholic emphasis on individual conscience, minimization of external spiritual direction, and adaptation of Church teaching to modern sensibilities.[^60] This condemnation strained relations with the American hierarchy and reflected Leo's deep suspicion of classical liberalism's influence within the Church, even as he sought diplomatic engagement with liberal regimes.

**Anglican Orders Controversy**

Of significant ecumenical consequence was Leo's 1896 encyclical Apostolicae Curae, which declared all Anglican ordinations "absolutely null and utterly void."[^61] After a thorough investigation involving scholars with divergent views, Leo concluded that Anglican ordinals were deficient in proper intention and sacramental form, and that the Anglican priesthood was fundamentally different from the Catholic sacrificing priesthood.[^62] The archbishops of Canterbury and York responded with Saepius Officio in 1897, but the rupture deepened existing divisions and foreclosed possibilities for reconciliation that would not be revisited until the mid-twentieth century.[^63]

**Harsh Stance Against Modern Thought**

While more diplomatically skilled than Pius IX, Leo remained fundamentally opposed to many aspects of modern thought. His encyclical Libertas Praestantissimum (1888) condemned modern concepts of liberty, distinguishing between legitimate freedom and what he considered license.[^64] In Immortale Dei (1885), he articulated a vision of church-state relations that rejected modern liberal democracy's assumptions about popular sovereignty and religious neutrality.[^65] His 1884 encyclical Humanum Genus launched an ambitious attack on Freemasonry, depicting it as a naturalistic conspiracy incompatible with Catholic faith and explicitly forbidding Catholics from joining Masonic lodges under any circumstances.[^66]

**Anti-Modernist Tendencies**

Though the full-blown Modernist Crisis erupted under his successor Pius X, Leo's 1893 encyclical Providentissimus Deus is often cited as marking the beginning of this controversy.[^67] While he opened doors for critical biblical scholarship, his suspicion of modern intellectual trends created an atmosphere of surveillance and suspicion. After Leo's death, an ultra-conservative reaction set in that stifled the very scholarly initiatives he had cautiously encouraged.[^68] His attempt to make Thomism the exclusive philosophical framework for Catholic thought, while intellectually impressive, limited doctrinal pluralism and made creative theological development more difficult.[^69]

## Personal Life

Pope Leo XIII was known for his distinctive personality traits that marked him throughout his long life. Described as "a man gifted with a superior intelligence, an energetic temperament, a keen awareness of his personal worth, and a discriminating sense for public relations," Leo combined intellectual brilliance with personal charm.[^70] Despite his deep involvement in European politics and ecclesiastical affairs, he remarkably retained "the old simple charm, the unworldly temper of mind, the grace, delicacy, and affection of early youth."[^71]

Leo was widely known for his cheerfulness and gentle sense of humor, with numerous anecdotes attesting to this endearing quality.[^72] When rumors circulated about his failing health—which happened frequently given his frail constitution—Leo would mischievously walk about briskly to dispel such speculation, demonstrating both his wit and determination.[^73] He was a tireless worker who exacted extraordinary effort from his collaborators, maintaining a methodical regularity of life that compensated for his physical fragility.[^74]

Though far from robust in health throughout his life, Leo's disciplined daily routine served him remarkably well.[^75] The cardinals who elected him in 1878 anticipated a brief pontificate given his age and delicate condition, yet he reigned for twenty-five years and lived longer than any of his known predecessors.[^76] Toward the end of his life, he used a gold-headed cane when walking, as mobility became increasingly difficult.[^77]

Leo was an intellectual deeply sympathetic to scientific progress and committed to demonstrating the Church's openness to legitimate advances in knowledge.[^78] Yet he remained fundamentally a pastor concerned with the Church's internal life and the spreading of its message throughout the world.[^79] This combination of intellectual sophistication and pastoral concern defined his approach to the papacy.

On July 6, 1903, Leo contracted pneumonia, and despite medical interventions including injections to ease his pain, the infection spread to his left lung.[^80] He died at 3:55 PM on July 20, 1903, at the age of 93, having lived through most of the nineteenth century and into the twentieth.[^81] At his death, he enjoyed vast personal prestige and had left the papacy itself greatly enhanced in global standing, even as fundamental structural questions about the Church's relationship to modernity remained unresolved.[^82]

## Legacy

Pope Leo XIII's quarter-century pontificate fundamentally reshaped Catholic engagement with the modern world, leaving a complex and multifaceted legacy that continues to influence the Church today. His impact extended far beyond the institutional boundaries of Catholicism, affecting labor movements, political theory, philosophical discourse, and interfaith relations.

**Founding Father of Catholic Social Teaching**

Leo's most enduring contribution was establishing the foundations of Catholic social doctrine through Rerum Novarum.[^83] This 1891 encyclical "has shaped Catholic social teaching ever since," spawning a body of work to which nearly every subsequent pope has contributed.[^84] The encyclical's principles—the dignity of the human person, rights and responsibilities, the dignity of work and rights of workers, the option for the poor, solidarity, and subsidiarity—became the seven pillars of Catholic social teaching.[^85]

Leo's intervention "moved social issues to the center of the Church's mission," demonstrating that Catholics must have a social conscience and actively work for justice in society.[^86] This legacy proved consequential beyond Catholic circles; the principles articulated in Rerum Novarum influenced labor legislation, social welfare policies, and economic thinking throughout the twentieth century.[^87] As one scholar noted, Leo "charted a path that rejected both socialism and laissez-faire capitalism, defending private property rights, insisting on the dignity and rights of workers, championing the importance and goodness of labor, and advocating for a living wage."[^88]

**Intellectual and Philosophical Influence**

Through Aeterni Patris and the Thomistic revival, Leo provided the Catholic Church with a coherent philosophical framework for engaging modernity.[^89] The neo-scholastic movement he initiated dominated Catholic intellectual life well into the mid-twentieth century, shaping theological education and providing analytical tools for addressing contemporary questions.[^90] While some scholars consider his emphasis on Thomism as "antiquated, restricted, and perhaps obsessively concerned," the intellectual rigor he demanded elevated Catholic philosophical discourse and demonstrated that faith could engage reason without sacrificing either.[^91]

His opening of the Vatican Archives and support for historical research established a precedent of scholarly openness that enhanced the Church's credibility among academics and intellectuals.[^92] His careful approach to biblical studies in Providentissimus Deus, balancing critical methods with doctrinal fidelity, laid groundwork for the twentieth-century flourishing of Catholic biblical scholarship.[^93]

**Diplomatic and Pastoral Achievements**

Leo significantly improved the Holy See's diplomatic standing despite the loss of temporal power, demonstrating that moral authority could transcend territorial sovereignty.[^94] His successful navigation of the Kulturkampf, his complex engagement with the French Third Republic, and his expansion of the Catholic hierarchy in various nations showed a diplomatic finesse that enhanced papal prestige globally.[^95]

His promotion of Marian devotion, particularly the rosary, established October as a month dedicated to Mary and created devotional practices that remain vibrant in Catholic life.[^96] The twelve encyclicals on the rosary earned him lasting affection as "The Rosary Pope" and deepened Marian theology, particularly regarding Mary's role as mediatrix.[^97]

**Limitations and Unfinished Business**

Despite these achievements, Leo's legacy includes significant limitations. As historians note, "it would be a mistake to assess Leo's pontificate as a radical or even a strong departure from that of his predecessors."[^98] He remained fundamentally opposed to core aspects of political modernity including democracy, religious liberty, and separation of church and state.[^99] His condemnation of Americanism, his uncompromising stance on the Roman Question, and his attacks on Freemasonry and liberalism demonstrated deep resistance to modern political and social arrangements.

When Leo died in 1903, "he enjoyed vast personal prestige; his Church was enthusiastic for the papacy; but Leo, like his predecessor, had not been able to adapt Church structure and thought to the new realities of the emergent 20th century."[^100] The fundamental questions about how Catholicism would relate to democracy, pluralism, religious freedom, and scientific modernity would not be resolved until the Second Vatican Council six decades later.

**Historical Verdict**

Contemporary scholars describe Leo as "an enigmatic figure praised as a progressive reformer by some, dismissed as a reluctant modernizer by others."[^101] His "legacy is one of cautious adaptation rather than radical transformation."[^102] The English writer Thomas Carlyle called him a great "reconciler of differences," acknowledging his ability to maintain traditional doctrine while showing awareness of the modern world.[^103]

Leo's "intellectual breadth, strength of character, and devotion to service have brought him great praise," even among those who question his theological positions.[^104] He proved himself "a brilliant man, able to steer the Church into a role in society that at once showed an awareness of the modern world but did not stray far from traditional church doctrine."[^105] This balancing act—engaging modernity without capitulating to it—defined his pontificate and established patterns that shaped Catholic responses to the twentieth century's challenges.

## Historical Assessment

### Positive Views

Admirers of Pope Leo XIII emphasize his remarkable intellectual gifts, diplomatic skill, and pastoral sensitivity in navigating the turbulent transition from the nineteenth to the twentieth century. Scholars praise him for demonstrating that the Catholic Church could engage contemporary thought without compromising essential doctrine, offering a middle path between reactionary rejection and uncritical accommodation of modernity.[^106]

His promotion of Thomistic philosophy is lauded as providing Catholics with robust intellectual tools for addressing modern philosophical errors while maintaining continuity with the Church's intellectual tradition.[^107] The opening of the Vatican Archives represented an unprecedented gesture of scholarly openness that enhanced the Church's credibility and demonstrated confidence in its historical record.[^108] His support for critical biblical scholarship, while cautious, showed a recognition that faith and legitimate historical-critical methods need not conflict.[^109]

Leo's greatest admirers celebrate him as the father of Catholic social teaching, whose vision in Rerum Novarum provided a prophetic third way between exploitative capitalism and totalitarian socialism.[^110] His insistence on workers' dignity, the right to a living wage, and the legitimacy of labor organization placed the Church firmly on the side of the vulnerable and established principles that guided Catholic social activism throughout the twentieth century.[^111] As one scholar notes, Rerum Novarum's impact extended to shaping New Deal policies in America, demonstrating the encyclical's influence beyond Catholic circles.[^112]

His diplomatic achievements—ending the Kulturkampf, engaging constructively with republican France despite ideological differences, expanding the Catholic hierarchy globally—showed sophisticated political judgment.[^113] Supporters argue that his refusal to accept the loss of the Papal States, while ultimately futile, maintained important principles about the Church's independence from state control that would bear fruit in the 1929 Lateran Treaty.[^114]

Catholic devotees particularly cherish his deep Marian piety and promotion of the rosary, which enriched Catholic spiritual life and deepened theological reflection on Mary's role in salvation history.[^115] His tireless work ethic, personal humility, gentle humor, and longevity in office demonstrated pastoral dedication that inspired the faithful.[^116] At his death, he commanded "vast personal prestige" and left the papacy significantly enhanced in global moral authority despite the loss of temporal power.[^117]

### Negative Views

Critics of Leo XIII emphasize that beneath his diplomatic sophistication and intellectual openness lay a fundamentally reactionary worldview that perpetuated the Church's alienation from modern political and intellectual developments. While more tactful than Pius IX, Leo shared his predecessor's deep hostility to liberal democracy, religious pluralism, and modern freedoms.[^118]

His encyclicals condemning liberalism (Libertas), defending traditional church-state arrangements (Immortale Dei), and attacking Freemasonry (Humanum Genus) revealed an unwillingness to accept the legitimacy of modern political arrangements based on popular sovereignty and religious neutrality.[^119] The condemnation of "Americanism" demonstrated suspicion toward the very democratic experiment that would eventually prove most hospitable to Catholic flourishing in the twentieth century.[^120]

Critics argue that Leo's opening toward biblical criticism was grudging and ultimately undermined by the anti-Modernist reaction that intensified after his death, for which his own suspicions of modern thought had laid groundwork.[^121] His declaration of Anglican orders as "absolutely null and utterly void" in Apostolicae Curae represented an ecumenical setback that foreclosed dialogue and hardened denominational divisions.[^122]

The Thomistic revival, while intellectually impressive, imposed a rigid philosophical uniformity that stifled creative theological thinking and doctrinal pluralism.[^123] Some scholars consider his learning "antiquated, restricted, and perhaps obsessively concerned with Thomism," limiting the Church's intellectual engagement with contemporary philosophy.[^124] The institutionalization of neo-scholasticism created an intellectual monoculture that persisted until Vatican II.

Most critically, historians note that despite twenty-five years in office, "Leo, like his predecessor, had not been able to adapt Church structure and thought to the new realities of the emergent 20th century."[^125] His "cautious adaptation rather than radical transformation" left fundamental questions unresolved about Catholicism's relationship to democracy, pluralism, scientific modernity, and human rights.[^126] The ralliement policy in France failed to create lasting Catholic-republican reconciliation, and his intransigence on the Roman Question perpetuated unnecessary conflict with Italy.[^127]

His refusal to allow Italian Catholics to participate in democratic politics marginalized Catholic voices and contributed to the rise of anti-clerical forces in Italian politics.[^128] The scheme to encourage foreign powers to invade Italy and restore papal temporal power showed poor judgment and unrealistic expectations.[^129] Critics argue that Leo's legacy is primarily one of missed opportunities—a brilliant mind and skilled diplomat who ultimately lacked the vision to lead the Church into genuine engagement with the modern world, leaving that task for successors generations later.[^130]

## Famous Quotes

> "Once the demands of necessity and propriety have been met, the rest that one owns belongs to the poor." - From his social teaching on economic justice[^131]

> "It is shameful and inhuman to treat men like chattels to make money by, or to regard them merely as so much muscle or physical power." - From Rerum Novarum on workers' dignity[^132]

> "The world has heard enough of the so-called 'rights of man.' Let it hear something of the rights of God." - On the relationship between human liberty and divine authority[^133]

> "Nothing emboldens the wicked so greatly as the lack of courage on the part of the good." - Calling Catholics to moral courage[^134]

> "The Rosary is the most excellent form of prayer and the most efficacious means of attaining eternal life. It is the remedy for all our evils, the root of all our blessings. There is no more excellent way of praying." - On Marian devotion[^135]

> "Catholics are born for combat." - Encouraging active engagement in defense of faith[^136]

> "The best parent and guardian of liberty amongst men is truth." - On the relationship between truth and freedom[^137]

> "It is a capital evil with respect to the question We are discussing to take for granted that the one class of society is of itself hostile to the other, as if nature had set rich and poor against each other to fight fiercely in implacable war." - From Rerum Novarum rejecting class warfare ideology[^138]
