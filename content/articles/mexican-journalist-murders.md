## Summary

Over 150 journalists have been murdered in Mexico since 2000, making it the deadliest country for reporters outside war zones. Cartels and corrupt officials target reporters with impunity.

## Background and Context

Mexico has experienced sustained violence against the press in the 21st century, with risks driven by organized crime, local corruption, and weak protection mechanisms. Killings and disappearances often affect local reporters covering crime, politics, and public security, especially in regions with cartel influence.

## Common Threat Patterns

- **Retaliation for reporting:** Investigations into trafficking networks, public procurement, police abuse, and political corruption can trigger threats.
- **Local vulnerability:** Community and regional outlets typically have fewer security resources and less national visibility.
- **Impunity:** Cases frequently go unsolved, which can reinforce a cycle of intimidation.
- **Self-censorship:** Persistent danger can lead outlets and reporters to limit coverage of certain topics.

## Impact

- **Public accountability:** Reduced investigative reporting can weaken oversight of local authorities and criminal groups.
- **Information gaps:** Communities may receive less reliable reporting on security, elections, and public spending.
- **Diaspora and displacement:** Some journalists relocate internally or seek refuge abroad due to credible threats.

## Responses and Protections (High-Level)

- **Federal and state mechanisms:** Protection programs and special prosecutor offices have been created over time, with debated effectiveness.
- **Civil society support:** NGOs and press freedom organizations provide legal aid, training, and incident documentation.
- **International attention:** Global press freedom groups and intergovernmental bodies periodically issue reports and recommendations.
