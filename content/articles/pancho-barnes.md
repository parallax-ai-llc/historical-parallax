## Summary

Florence Leontine Lowe Barnes, known universally as "Pancho Barnes," was one of the most colorful and accomplished aviators of the early twentieth century. Born into California privilege, she rejected a conventional life to become a pioneering female pilot, breaking speed records, training military pilots, and operating one of the most legendary establishments in aviation history — the Happy Bottom Riding Club near Edwards Air Force Base.

Barnes embodied the spirit of aviation's golden age, fearless at the controls and equally fierce in defending the rights of pilots and stunt flyers. Her influence extended from Hollywood film sets to the high desert of the Mojave, where her ranch became a social hub for test pilots and the men who would eventually break the sound barrier. Chuck Yeager and other aviation legends counted her among their most important friends and advocates.

## Early Life & Background

Florence Lowe was born on July 22, 1901, in San Marino, California, to a wealthy family with deep roots in early aviation — her grandfather, Thaddeus Lowe, was a Civil War balloonist and aeronaut of some renown. Despite her privileged upbringing and education at a convent school, she chafed against the expectations placed on women of her class. In 1921 she married Reverend C. Rankin Barnes, with whom she had a son, but the marriage was troubled and she increasingly sought escape in adventure.

The nickname "Pancho" was earned during an escapade in the late 1920s when she disguised herself as a man and traveled to Mexico, where companions gave her the name. The persona stuck, and she embraced it fully for the rest of her life.

## Rise to Prominence

Barnes learned to fly in 1928, quickly proving herself an exceptional pilot. In August 1930 she broke Amelia Earhart's women's air speed record, clocking approximately 196 mph in a Travel Air Mystery Ship. She went on to work as a stunt pilot in Hollywood, founding the Associated Motion Picture Pilots in 1931 to improve safety and working conditions for stunt flyers — one of the first such advocacy organizations in the industry.

During the 1930s she also raced in major air competitions, promoted aviation, and ran a small airline service. Her transition to the Mojave came during World War II, when she purchased land near what would become Muroc Army Air Field (later Edwards AFB) and established her ranch.

## Key Achievements

- Broke the women's air speed record in 1930 at approximately 196 mph
- Founded the Associated Motion Picture Pilots (1931), advocating for stunt pilot safety
- Operated the Happy Bottom Riding Club (1940s–1953), a vital social and informal support hub for test pilots at Edwards AFB
- Contributed to the culture documented in Tom Wolfe's *The Right Stuff* (1979), in which she appears as a vivid character

## Controversies & Criticism

Barnes feuded publicly and legally with the U.S. Air Force after Edwards AFB condemned and bulldozed her ranch in 1953 to expand the base. She sued the government and eventually reached a settlement, though the experience embittered her considerably. Her outspoken, unconventional behavior — including heavy drinking and frank language — made her a divisive figure in some circles, even as test pilots adored her.

## Legacy & Influence

Barnes died on March 30, 1975, in Cantil, California. She was posthumously inducted into the Women in Aviation International Pioneer Hall of Fame. Her life inspired a 1988 biographical television film, *Pancho Barnes*, starring Valerie Bertinelli. The Happy Bottom Riding Club remains a legendary symbol of the camaraderie and daring that defined Edwards AFB's golden era of flight testing, and Barnes herself endures as an icon of female independence in aviation.
