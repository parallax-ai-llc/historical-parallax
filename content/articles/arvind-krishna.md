## Summary

Arvind Krishna is an Indian-American technology executive who has served as Chairman and Chief Executive Officer of IBM since 2020. A three-decade IBM veteran with deep expertise in cloud computing, artificial intelligence, and quantum computing, Krishna has steered one of the world's oldest and most storied technology companies through a significant strategic transformation toward hybrid cloud and AI services.

His ascent to the top of IBM marked a shift in the company's direction. Under his leadership, IBM completed the landmark $34 billion acquisition of Red Hat in 2019 — the largest acquisition in the company's history — and subsequently spun off its managed infrastructure services division into a separate company called Kyndryl in 2021. These moves reflect Krishna's conviction that IBM's future lies in high-value software and consulting rather than legacy IT services.

Krishna is widely regarded as one of the most technically grounded CEOs in the enterprise technology sector. His academic background in electrical engineering and his hands-on role in developing IBM's cloud and quantum research divisions lend him credibility rare among executives at his level.

## Early Life & Background

Arvind Krishna was born in 1962 in Andhra Pradesh, India. He completed his undergraduate degree in electrical engineering at the Indian Institute of Technology Kanpur (IIT Kanpur), one of India's most prestigious technical universities. He subsequently moved to the United States, earning a Ph.D. in electrical engineering from the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign in 1990.

His formative years were shaped by a rigorous engineering education and an early immersion in computing research, laying the technical foundation that would define his corporate career.

## Rise to Prominence

Krishna joined IBM in 1990 following the completion of his doctorate. Over the next three decades, he held a series of increasingly senior research and business roles. He led IBM's cloud and cognitive software division and was a principal architect of the company's hybrid cloud strategy.

His most consequential pre-CEO contribution was championing and executing the acquisition of Red Hat, which closed in July 2019 for approximately $34 billion. The deal, the largest in IBM's history, was designed to position IBM as the dominant player in the enterprise hybrid cloud market. In April 2020, Krishna was named CEO, succeeding Ginni Rometty.

## Key Achievements

- Orchestrated the $34 billion Red Hat acquisition (2019), IBM's largest-ever deal.
- Appointed CEO of IBM in April 2020 and added the Chairman title in January 2021.
- Led the spin-off of Kyndryl (NYSE: KD) in November 2021, separating IBM's $19 billion managed infrastructure services business.
- Accelerated IBM's investment in generative AI with the launch of the watsonx platform in 2023, targeting enterprise AI adoption.
- Championed IBM's quantum computing program, expanding access through IBM Quantum Network partnerships with over 200 organizations worldwide.

## Controversies & Criticism

IBM's transformation under Krishna has not been without challenges. The company's revenue growth remained sluggish in the early years of his tenure, prompting investor skepticism about the pace of the hybrid cloud strategy's payoff. Workforce reductions accompanied the Kyndryl spin-off, affecting tens of thousands of employees. Critics have also questioned whether IBM's AI offerings can compete effectively against larger platforms from Microsoft, Google, and Amazon. Some analysts have argued that the Red Hat acquisition, while strategically sound, was priced at a significant premium that constrained IBM's financial flexibility.

## Legacy & Influence

Krishna has repositioned IBM from a broad-based IT services conglomerate into a more focused hybrid cloud and AI company, a structural shift with long-term implications for how legacy technology firms navigate digital transformation. His emphasis on open-source infrastructure — embodied by the Red Hat bet — has influenced enterprise technology strategy across the industry. His leadership of IBM's quantum computing initiative has helped normalize quantum as a practical business consideration rather than a purely academic pursuit. Whether IBM's transformation yields the sustained growth it promises will define his ultimate legacy.
