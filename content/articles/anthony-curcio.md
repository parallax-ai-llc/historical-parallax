## Summary

American bank robber who orchestrated an elaborate Brink's armored car heist using Craigslist to recruit unwitting accomplices. He had over a dozen decoys dressed identically to him appear at the robbery scene.

## The 2008 Brink's Heist

Curcio planned the robbery around distraction and misdirection. Using Craigslist, he recruited people for what they believed was a legitimate "security" or "training" job, instructing them to show up wearing the same clothing. When the robbery occurred, the look-alikes created confusion for witnesses and responding officers.

The heist targeted a Brink's armored car near a bank in Washington state, and the use of multiple decoys became the defining feature of the case.

## Investigation

Authorities traced aspects of the operation after the fact, and cooperation from an accomplice contributed to identifying Curcio as the organizer. The case is often referenced as an example of how online marketplaces can be misused for criminal logistics without recruits understanding their role.

## Sentence and Later Attention

Curcio was sentenced in federal court and served prison time. The incident has been retold in news and documentary formats as a high-planning, low-violence robbery centered on social engineering rather than sophisticated weaponry.

## Why the Case Stood Out

Unlike many robbery narratives that focus on force, this incident is often summarized as a "crowd camouflage" tactic: creating many plausible suspects to slow identification in the crucial first minutes.

The planning also highlights how criminals can outsource small tasks (assembling decoys, scouting) through public postings while maintaining personal distance from participants.
