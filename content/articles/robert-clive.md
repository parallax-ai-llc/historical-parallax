## Summary

British colonial administrator known as 'Clive of India' who established British colonial rule in Bengal. He amassed a personal fortune through corruption and manipulation. His exploitation contributed to the 1770 Bengal famine that killed 10 million people.

## Early Career

- Clive joined the British East India Company (EIC) as a young man and was initially employed in administrative roles in India before becoming involved in military operations.
- His rise occurred in the context of intense European rivalry on the subcontinent, where commercial companies operated with private armies and shifting alliances with local rulers.

## Bengal and the East India Company

- The Battle of Plassey (1757) became a turning point that enabled the EIC to exert decisive influence in Bengal, often described as a foundational moment for later British imperial expansion.
- Clive’s role is associated with political maneuvering and alliance-building, including cooperation with local elites who opposed the existing Nawab.

## Governance, Wealth, and Controversy

- After military success, Clive returned to Britain with significant wealth, contributing to allegations that EIC officials extracted fortunes through gifts, monopolies, and leverage over revenue systems.
- He later returned to India in a senior position and pursued reforms aimed at curbing some forms of company corruption and regulating private trade by officials.

## Parliamentary Inquiry and Reputation

- Clive faced scrutiny in Britain amid wider concerns about EIC governance, including parliamentary investigations into corruption and the company’s political power.
- His legacy remains contested: some accounts frame him as an architect of empire and a reformer of company administration, while others emphasize coercion, extraction, and the long-run costs of colonial rule.

## Bengal Famine (1770) Context

- The 1770 Bengal famine is widely understood to have resulted from a complex mix of climatic shocks, market dynamics, and administrative failures.
- While Clive’s career is tied to the EIC’s consolidation of revenue control, scholarly debates vary on how to allocate responsibility across individuals versus institutional systems and later policy decisions.
