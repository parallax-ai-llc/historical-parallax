## Summary

Political consulting firm that harvested personal data of 87 million Facebook users without consent to influence elections including the 2016 US presidential election and Brexit referendum.

## What Happened (High Level)

The Cambridge Analytica scandal is generally described as a **data privacy and political influence** controversy involving Facebook user data obtained via an app in a way that exceeded what many users understood or consented to. The issue became emblematic of how social platforms can enable large-scale data collection and downstream uses that are hard for individuals to see or control.

## Data Pathway (Simplified)

High-level retellings usually describe a chain like:

1. A third-party Facebook application collected information from users who installed it.
2. Under platform rules and technical settings at the time, data could also be accessed from some users’ friends.
3. The collected data was then used for profiling and political targeting work associated with Cambridge Analytica and related entities.

Exact scope, categories of data, and legal responsibilities are complex and have been discussed differently across investigations.

## Why It Mattered

The scandal became significant beyond one company because it highlighted:

- **Consent and transparency gaps:** users often did not understand how far data could travel.
- **Platform governance:** the role of API policies, enforcement, and auditing of third-party developers.
- **Political advertising and influence:** microtargeting, persuasive messaging, and the difficulty of measuring impact.
- **Trust and accountability:** questions about how technology companies respond to misuse after discovery.

## Regulatory and Institutional Responses

Public reporting commonly notes subsequent investigations and scrutiny by regulators and legislators, with outcomes including:

- hearings and reports on social-media governance
- changes to platform API access and developer policies
- privacy enforcement actions and broader debate about data protection regimes

## Broader Concepts

Readers often connect this episode to:

- the economics of data-driven advertising
- algorithmic amplification and information campaigns
- privacy law, data minimization, and purpose limitation
- corporate compliance and third-party risk management

## Notes on Impact Claims

Claims about the direct electoral impact of targeted advertising and data profiling are difficult to prove conclusively, and assessments vary. The scandal is often treated as a landmark in public awareness of modern data practices, regardless of specific causal claims.
