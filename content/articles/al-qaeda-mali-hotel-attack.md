## Summary

Jihadist attack on the Radisson Blu hotel in Bamako killing 22 people including foreign guests. Gunmen took 170 hostages before Malian and French forces ended the siege.

## Background

Mali experienced a growing jihadist insurgency in the years leading up to 2015, with militant groups operating across the Sahel and targeting state institutions, security forces, and foreign interests. Hotels and other international venues were viewed as “soft targets” because they concentrated diplomats, business travelers, aid workers, and tourists in accessible urban locations.

## The Attack (Bamako, 20 Nov 2015)

The assault took place at the Radisson Blu hotel in Bamako, Mali’s capital. Armed attackers entered the hotel and initiated a hostage situation that involved a large number of guests and staff. Malian security forces, with support from international partners including French forces, conducted an operation that ended the siege.

## Attribution and Claimed Responsibility

Public reporting on the incident linked the attack to al-Qaeda–aligned militant networks active in the region. Such claims and attributions are typically assessed through a mix of on-the-ground investigations, intercepted communications, and statements released by militant media channels.

## Victims and Impact

The attack killed dozens of people, including foreign nationals, and generated international condemnation. Beyond the immediate loss of life, the incident intensified concerns about urban terrorism in West Africa and the vulnerability of civilian spaces used by international communities.

## Aftermath

In the wake of the siege, Mali increased security measures around major hotels, diplomatic facilities, and public venues. The event also reinforced ongoing international counterterrorism involvement in the Sahel and highlighted the cross-border nature of militant activity in the region.

## Legacy

The Radisson Blu hostage crisis is frequently cited as a landmark example of Sahel-based jihadist groups projecting violence into a national capital. It remains part of broader analyses of how militant organizations select targets for symbolic value, media attention, and disruption of economic confidence.
