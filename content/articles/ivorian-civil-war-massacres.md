## Summary

Post-election violence in Ivory Coast killed over 3,000 people when Laurent Gbagbo refused to accept electoral defeat. His forces and pro-Ouattara forces committed massacres.

## Context (2010–2011 Post-Election Crisis)

- The violence followed a disputed presidential election and competing claims to legitimacy by rival political camps.
- Many analyses emphasize how polarization, hate speech, and the militarization of party-aligned youth and militia networks accelerated the crisis.
- The period is often framed as part of a longer arc of instability linked to earlier conflict, questions of citizenship/identity politics, and uneven control of territory.

## Patterns of Violence

Public documentation of the crisis often highlights:

- Mass killings in certain towns and neighborhoods during shifting front lines.
- Targeting of civilians along perceived political, ethnic, or regional lines.
- Sexual violence and other serious human rights abuses in the context of law-and-order breakdown.

## Accountability and International Justice

- Investigations and prosecutions have involved domestic Ivorian processes as well as international attention.
- The International Criminal Court (ICC) cases related to Côte d'Ivoire are frequently referenced in discussions of post-conflict accountability, evidentiary standards, and political legitimacy.

## Humanitarian and Social Impact

- Large-scale displacement and refugee flows affected neighboring states and internal host communities.
- Post-crisis reconciliation efforts have had to balance truth-telling, reparations, and political stabilization.
