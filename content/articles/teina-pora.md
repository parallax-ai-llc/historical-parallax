## Case Overview

Teina Pora is a New Zealander whose conviction for the 1992 rape and murder of Susan Burdett (Papatoetoe, Auckland) was ultimately quashed. The case became a prominent example in New Zealand of wrongful conviction concerns, including the reliability of confessions and the safeguards required during lengthy police interviews.

## Key Facts (High Level)

- **Crime (1992):** Susan Burdett, aged 39, was raped and murdered in her home; she was struck repeatedly with a softball bat.
- **Convictions (1994; again in 2000):** Pora was convicted of rape and murder, serving roughly two decades in prison.
- **Parole (2014):** Released on parole after about 20 years of imprisonment.
- **Exoneration (2015):** Convictions quashed by the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council.
- **Aftermath:** Pora later received financial compensation and a formal apology from the New Zealand government; Malcolm Rewa was later convicted of Burdett’s murder (after earlier being convicted of raping her).

## Contributing Issues Discussed in Coverage

Public reporting and later reviews frequently emphasize several broad issues that can arise in wrongful conviction cases:

- **Interview safeguards:** Prolonged interviews and the importance of legal representation.
- **Vulnerability:** Questions raised about cognitive vulnerability and comprehension at the time of key statements.
- **Evidentiary consistency:** The need to reconcile admissions with forensic and corroborating evidence.

## Significance

The Pora case is often referenced in debates about criminal justice reform, particularly around interview practices, disclosure, and protections for young or vulnerable suspects. It also illustrates how major cases can remain contested for decades, with later proceedings and re-evaluation of evidence reshaping the historical record.
