## Summary

Genocide of 8,000+ Bosniak men and boys by Bosnian Serb forces in July 1995. Worst mass killing in Europe since WWII.

## Historical Context

- The Srebrenica massacre occurred during the Bosnian War, within the wider breakup of Yugoslavia and the conflict in Bosnia and Herzegovina.
- Srebrenica had been designated a United Nations “safe area,” but Bosnian Serb forces captured the enclave in July 1995.

## What Happened

- After the takeover, large numbers of Bosniak men and boys were separated, detained, and killed in a series of mass executions.
- Many victims were later identified through forensic exhumations and DNA-based identification efforts.

## Legal Findings

- International courts, including the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY), issued judgments related to the massacre.
- Several senior political and military figures were tried and convicted for crimes connected to Srebrenica.
- The events are widely recognized in international jurisprudence as genocide.

## International Response and Aftermath

- The failure to protect the safe area became a major point of controversy and introspection for the international community.
- Srebrenica influenced later debates about peacekeeping mandates, civilian protection, and accountability for mass atrocities.

## Remembrance

- Annual commemorations and memorials focus on victims, survivors, and the continuing effort to locate and identify remains.
- The massacre remains a central reference point in European post–Cold War history and in studies of ethnic cleansing and genocide prevention.
